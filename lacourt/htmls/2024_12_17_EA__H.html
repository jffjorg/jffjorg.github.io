DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19PSCV00687    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: H

Meritage Homes of California, Inc. v. Michael Baker International, Inc.,
Case No. 19PSCV00687

TENTATIVE RULINGS ON MOTIONS IN LIMINE [FN1]


I.        Plaintiff’s Motion in Limine No. 3 to Exclude Any Testimony by Doug Martin, Mark Doty, or Michael Boeck in Opposition or Rebuttal to Meritage Unless Those Witnesses are Disclosed and Produced for Testimony During Plaintiff’s Case-in-Chief

As to witness Doug Martin, the motion is denied as moot, as MBI’s opposition suggests that only two of the three witnesses—Mark Doty and Michael Boeck—would potentially provide live testimony.

As to witnesses Mark Doty and Michael Boeck, the Court is inclined to grant the motion, in the interests of orderly trial proceedings and avoiding juror confusion that could result from having to hear both deposition testimony and live testimony from the same witness. While the Court lacks authority to order out-of-state witnesses to appear at trial, see, e.g., Amoco Chemical Co. v. Certain Underwriters at Lloyd’s of London (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 554, the Court permits MBI to make a choice: it may either elect to call the witnesses and permit them to testify in Plaintiff’s case-in-chief or it may rely solely on deposition testimony. Accordingly, if MBI elects to call Doty or Boeck as live witnesses in its case, MBI must also allow Plaintiff to call the witnesses in Plaintiff’s case-in-chief. If MBI does not elect to make the out-of-state witnesses available for live testimony, both parties may present deposition testimony.

The parties are ordered to meet and confer further on this issue prior to trial. The Court expects the parties to work together and be flexible regarding scheduling given that the witnesses are located out of state.

 

II.      Plaintiff’s Motion in Limine No. 4 to Exclude Reference to Settlement

The Court is inclined to grant the motion in large part, particularly in absence of any indication that the settlement agreement is a sliding scale agreement and/or contains a term requiring settling defendants to provided continued participation in the trial. That said, the Court will permit MBI to revisit the issue at trial to the extent that employees of Boudreau, John Lisee Pumps, and/or Pentiar provide testimony that is inconsistent with testimony offered prior to the settlement. (Diamond v. Reshko (2015) 239 Cal. App. 4th 828, 848 (2015) [“While the testimonial discrepancy itself was revealed, the jury was deprived of evidence which could have supported a conclusion that Mansouri was biased in favor of the plaintiffs now that he had settled his case with the Diamonds.”].)

Accordingly, depending on the testimony presented, the Court may permit limited references to the settlement for the sole purpose of establishing witness bias, in which case the Court would provide an appropriate limiting instruction. If MBI wishes to revisit the issue at trial, it must first raise the issue with the Court outside the presence of the jury before introducing evidence of the settlement or referencing the settlement in any way.

 

III.     Plaintiff’s (Amended) Motion in Limine No. 5 to Exclude Argument and Evidence Referring to and Concerning the “Empty Chair” Defendants and Any Alleged Proportionate Fault of the Settling Defendants

The Court defers ruling on this motion, pending the outcome of Plaintiff’s request for declaratory relief. Specifically, in Plaintiff’s trial brief, Plaintiff requests that the Court determine a number of threshold issues relating to the Master Professional Services Agreement (“MPSA”), including whether, “pursuant to sections 8.2 and 8.4 of the MPSA,” Plaintiff “need not prove negligence or ‘substantial factor’ causation to prove its right to indemnity from MBI.”

At the FSC, the parties should be prepared to address Plaintiff’s request for declaratory relief; the Court also may request supplemental briefing as to this issue following the FSC.

 

IV.     Plaintiff’s Motion in Limine No. 6 to Exclude Reference to Statements Referencing the Term “Fraud” in Describing Any Non-Settling Party’s Conduct

The Court seeks clarification as to what Plaintiff is seeking and what evidence is in dispute. The motion in limine references only “non-settling” parties but both the motion and opposition seem to contemplate reference to the settling defendants’ alleged fraudulent conduct. The Court will hear further from the parties in this regard at the time of the FSC.

 

V.       Plaintiff’s Motion in Limine No. 7 to Tom Card’s Opinions Regarding the Design of the Lift Station

The Court is inclined to deny the motion. Mr. Card’s previous testimony appears to be within the scope of the alleged failure of the sewer lift system and to the extent that Plaintiff believes the declaration is being taken out of context and/or is beyond Mr. Card’s expertise, Plaintiff is free to present those arguments at trial.


[FN 1] Defendant Michael Baker International, Inc. (“MBI”) did not file motions in limine in accordance with the Department H Case Management Conference Standing Order. To the extent that MBI intended to renew the motions in limine it had filed back in January 2023 – well before the case was assigned to this Court and other significant in the case (such as the settlement reached between Plaintiff and the other defendants) – MBI needed to re-file its motions in limine or at least alert the Court to the specific motions in limine at issue in its other trial filings. The Court will hear further from MBI in this regard at the FSC.

Case Number: 20PSCV00155    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: H

Bank of the West v. Lindsay, Case No. 20PSCV00155

ORDER ON OSC RE: ORDER FOR SALE OF DWELLING

The Order to Show Cause Re: Order for Sale of Dwelling is DENIED as untimely.


Background 

Plaintiff Bank of the West (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows: 

          On September 13, 2017, Defendant Rodney Michael Lindsay (“Defendant”) entered into a Retail Installment Sale Contract (the “Contract”) with Plaintiff’s assignor for the purchase of a 2016 Chevrolet Silverado, VIN #1GC1KVE85GF124055. Defendant failed to make payments due under the Contract. 

On February 26, 2020, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Defendant and Does 1-60 for (1) breach of retail installment sale contract, (2) money lent, (3) recovery of personal property (claim and delivery), and (4) conversion. 

On May 1, 2020, Defendant’s default was entered. On August 24, 2020, the Court entered default judgment. 

An Order to Show Cause Re: Order for Sale of Dwelling is set for October 21, 2024.

Legal Standard 

“[T]he interest of a natural person in a dwelling may not be sold under this division to enforce a money judgment except pursuant to a court order for sale…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 704.740, subd. (a).) 

“Section 704.740 is part of the homestead laws. ‘Homestead laws are designed to protect the sanctity of the family home against a loss caused by a forced sale by creditors. . . The homestead exemption ensures that insolvent debtors and their families are not rendered homeless by virtue of an involuntary sale of the residential property they occupy. Thus, the homestead law is not designed to protect creditors. . . This strong public policy requires courts to adopt a liberal construction of the law and facts to promote the beneficial purposes of the homestead legislation to benefit the debtor [and his family].’” (Wells Fargo Financial Leading, Inc. v. D&M Cabinets (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 59, 67-68, quoting Amin v. Khazindar (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 582, 585.)

Discussion

Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor applies for an order for sale of the dwelling commonly known as 937 N. Soldano Avenue, Azusa, CA 91702 (the “Property”) owned by Defendant/Judgment Debtor.

 

Service Requirements

“Upon the filing of the application by the judgment creditor, the court shall set a time and place for hearing and order the judgment debtor to show cause why an order for sale should not be made in accordance with the application. The time set for hearing shall be not later than 45 days after the application is filed or such later time as the court orders upon a showing of good cause.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 704.770, subd. (a).)

“Not later than 30 days before the time set for hearing, the judgment creditor shall do both of the following:

(1) Serve on the judgment debtor a copy of the order to show cause, a copy of the application of the judgment creditor, and a copy of the notice of the hearing in the form prescribed by the Judicial Council. Service shall be made personally or by mail.

(2) Personally serve a copy of each document listed in paragraph (1) on an occupant of the dwelling or, if there is no occupant present at the time service is attempted, post a copy of each document in a conspicuous place at the dwelling.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 704.770, subd. (b) [emphasis added].)

Here, Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor filed (mail served on April 22, 2024) an “Application for Issuance of Order for Sale of Dwelling Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 704.750(a)” on April 25, 2024.

On May 16, 2024, an “Order to Show Cause Why Order for Sale of Dwelling Should Not Issue” (“OSC”) was entered, wherein an Order to Show Cause was set for September 12, 2024. On June 12, 2024, Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor filed a “Notice of Hearing[, etc.],” which was personally served on Defendant/Judgment Debtor that day.

On August 13, 2024, Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor filed a proof of service reflecting that Defendant/Judgment Debtor was served that day by mail with the Application, OSC and Notice of Hearing. On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed another proof of service, reflecting that the Application, OSC, and Notice of Hearing had been posted on the property on August 13, 2024.

The September 12, 2024 Order to Show Cause was continued to October 21, 2024; the Court provided the parties with notice of the continued hearing.

On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor filed a “Notice of Hearing[, etc.],” advising of the new October 21, 2024 hearing date; this notice was personally served on Defendant/Judgment Debtor that day.

On September 20, 2024, a “First Amended Order to Show Cause Why Order for Sale of Dwelling Should Not Issue” (“Amended OSC”) was entered, reflecting the October 21, 2024 date.

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a proof of service reflecting that Defendant/Judgment Debtor was served on September 27, 2024 by mail with the Notice of Hearing and OSC (presumably the Amended OSC). On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed another proof of service, reflecting that the Amended OSC and Notice of Hearing had been posted on the property on September 27, 2024. The aforesaid proofs of service do not reflect service “[n]ot later than 30 days before” October 21, 2024.

With that said, on October 17, 2024, the Court continued the hearing, at Defendant/Judgment Debtor’s request, to December 17, 2024; the Court provided notice to the parties.

The Court determines that notice was adequately provided to Defendant/Judgment Debtor, based on the circumstances set forth above.

 

Timeliness of Application

“Promptly after a dwelling is levied upon…, the levying officer shall serve notice on the judgment creditor that the levy has been made and that the property will be released unless the judgment creditor complies with the requirements of this section. Service shall be made personally or by mail. Within 20 days after service of the notice, the judgment creditor shall apply to the court for an order for sale of the dwelling and shall file a copy of the application with the levying officer. If the judgment creditor does not file the copy of the application for an order for sale of the dwelling within the allowed time, the levying officer shall release the dwelling.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 704.750, subd. (a) [Emphasis added].)

Plaintiff has provided the Court with a copy of the levying officer’s “Notice to Creditor of Levy on Dwelling” dated February 6, 2024. (Olson Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. E.) The Application was filed on April 25, 2024, well in excess of 20 days from the date of the levying officer’s notice; as such, the court determines that Code of Civil Procedure § 704.750, subdivision (a) has not been satisfied.

The Court, then, denies the application without prejudice as untimely.

As an aside, the Court notes that Defendant’s October 11, 2024 filing, styled a “Response to Order to Show Cause Notice [sic] of Motion and Motion for Leave to File Amended Answer,” is construed as a response to the Order to Show Cause only and not as an independent motion.

Case Number: 24PSCV02472    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: H

 

 Zhao v. Lee, et al, Case No. 24PSCV02472

ORDER ON DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

          1.       Defendants Henry Lee and Shu Fang Li’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED, with 20 days leave to amend in part (i.e., as to the first through third causes of action) and OVERRULED in part (i.e., as to the fourth through seventh causes of action).

          2.       Defendants Henry Lee and Shu Fang Li’s Motion to Strike Portions of [Plaintiff’s] Complaint is DENIED as MOOT in part (i.e., as to Paragraph 41, 7:12-14) and otherwise DENIED.

 

I.        Background

Plaintiff Yu Zhi Zhao (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

 

          On or about March 15, 2014, Plaintiff entered into an oral agreement with Henry Lee (“Lee”) and Shu Fang Li (“Li”) (together, “Defendants”), wherein Defendants agreed to lease the property located at 2026E Shamwood Street in West Covina (the “subject property”) to Plaintiff for a three-year period in exchange for Plaintiff’s payment of $1,750.00 in monthly rent. After December 2017, the parties orally renewed the lease on an annual basis; rent was increased to $1,980.00/month beginning January 1, 2020.

 

          Beginning in August 2019, Plaintiff made approximately $70,000.00 in permitted additions to the subject property, with Defendants’ knowledge and consent. From about April to August 2021, Defendants promised Plaintiff that after the improvements were made, Plaintiff would only be responsible for the increased portions of the real estate tax and homeowner insurance premium and that they would not charge Plaintiff additional rent for the improvements. Defendants refused to give Plaintiff a written lease because they wanted Plaintiff to pay rent in cash.

 

          In or about December 2022, the permit for Plaintiff’s improvements was issued and the improvements were completed. Defendants thereafter asked Plaintiff to pay $2,900.00 in monthly rent, without any written notice in advance, instead of paying the increased portion of real estate property taxes or insurance premiums. Defendants have subsequently raised rent several times without providing Plaintiff with written notice. Plaintiff has asked Defendants to reimburse her for the cost of constructing the improvements on the subject property, but they refuse and are instead attempting to evict her.

 

On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Defendants and Does 1-25 for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Fraud, (3) Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment, (4) Retaliation in Violation of Civil Code § 1942.5, (5) Violation of 2019 Tenant Protection Act, (6) Breach of Quasi-Contract/Unjust Enrichment, and (7) Violation of the Unfair Competition Law—Business & Professions Code § 17200, et seq.

 

Pending before the Court are (1) Defendants’ demurrer to the complaint and (2) Defendants’ motion to strike portions of the complaint.

 

 

II.      Legal Standards

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).)

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

III.     Discussion

          A.       Defendants’ Demurrer      

Defendants demur, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to the first through seventh causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint, on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

 

Request for Judicial Notice 

The Court grants Plaintiffs’ Request for Judicial Notice (i.e., of the complaint filed August 6, 2024 in Case No. 24WCUD01628).

 

Merits

At the outset, the Court notes that the “Relevant Facts and Procedural History” section contained in Defendants’ demurrer contains purported facts that are extrinsic to the face of Plaintiff’s complaint and are not judicially noticeable. The Court thus declines to consider them as improper at the demurrer stage.

 

First Cause of Action (i.e., Breach of Contract)

“The elements of a breach of oral contract claim are the same as those for a breach of written contract: a contract; its performance or excuse for nonperformance; breach; and damages.” (Stockton Mortgage, Inc. v. Tope (2014) 233 Cal.App.4th 437, 453). “It is essential to the existence of a contract that there should be: 1. Parties capable of contracting; 2. Their consent; 3. A lawful object; and, 4. A sufficient cause or consideration.” (Civ. Code § 1550.)

 

Plaintiff has alleged that she has resided at the premises since 2014 and that she initially leased the premises pursuant to an oral agreement for a term of three years, which was followed by oral renewals of the lease on an annual basis. (Complaint, ¶¶ 1, 7, and 10.) Plaintiff has alleged that she made “additional constructions on the premises,” “applied [for] permit[s] for constructing these additions from the City and did all the work relating to the construction” “[b]eginning from August 2019.” (Id., ¶ 15.)

 

Plaintiff has alleged that “[f]rom about April 2021 to about August 2021, during the period when Plaintiff applied for permit for the improvement on behalf of defendants, defendants promised Plaintiff that after the improvements were made Plaintiff would only be responsible for the increased portions of the real estates tax and homeowner insurance premium, and defendants would not charge additional rents for the improvements made by Plaintiff” (Id., ¶ 17), that “[i]n or about December 2022, the permit for the improvements made by Plaintiff was issued by the City, and, the improvement was finally completed” (Id., ¶ 19), that “defendants immediately changed their words” and “asked Plaintiff to pay  monthly rent in the amount of $2900[FN1],. . . instead of paying the increased portion of real estate property taxes or insurance premium” (Id.), that defendants thereafter raised the monthly rent to $2,600.00 beginning from January 2023, to $2,700.00 beginning from June 2023 and to $2,835.00 beginning from June 2024 (Id., ¶¶ 20-22), that “Plaintiff requested that defendants reimburse her for the cost of constructing additions on the Premises but defendants refused” (Id., ¶ 23), that “the parties intended Plaintiff to reside at the Premises for the remaining of her life time” (Id., ¶ 25) and that defendants are now trying to evict her (Id.).

 

The Court agrees that it is unclear, as currently pled, how many contracts were made between Plaintiff and Defendants. The first cause of action is labeled, “Breach of Contracts.” Additionally, Plaintiff has alleged that she entered into oral agreements with Defendants from about April 2021 to about August 2021. (Complaint, ¶ 25; see also, ¶ 29, 5:15 [referring to “agreements”].) Plaintiff, however, subsequently alleges that there was an agreement. (Id.; see also, ¶¶ 28 and 29, 5:16 [referring to “agreement”].) Clarification is needed in this regard.

 

Further, Plaintiff has alleged that she made “additional constructions on the premises” “[b]eginning from August 2019” with defendants’ “knowledge and consent.” (Id., ¶ 15.) It is unclear to the Court, from the facts alleged, whether or not the cited improvements were already completed by Plaintiff before the parties entered into any of the alleged oral contracts. It is further unclear to the Court what the terms of each of the alleged oral contracts were.[FN2]

 

The Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action on this basis.

 

Second Cause of Action (i.e., Fraud)

 

“‘Promissory fraud’ is a subspecies of the action for fraud and deceit.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.) “The elements of promissory fraud . . . are: (1) a promise made regarding a material fact without any intention of performing it; (2) the existence of the intent not to perform at the time the promise was made; (3) intent to deceive or induce the promisee to enter into a transaction; (4) reasonable reliance by the promisee; (5) nonperformance by the party making the promise; and (6) resulting damage to the promise[e].” (Rossberg v. Bank of America, N.A. (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1498.) 

“Fraud must be pleaded with specificity…[t]o withstand a demurrer, the facts constituting every element of the fraud must be alleged with particularity, and the claim cannot be salvaged by references to the general policy favoring the liberal construction of pleadings.” (Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782 [emphasis in original].) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73 [quotations and citations omitted; emphasis in original].)

 

Plaintiff has alleged that “[f]rom about April 2021 to about September 2022, defendants made numerous false promises to Plaintiff, including but not limited to: if Plaintiff would make improvements to the Premises and apply for the city permit on their behalf for the improvement, Defendants would not seek rent increases from Plaintiff, and that Plaintiff would only be responsible for the increased portions of the property tax and/or insurance premium resulting from the improvements. Defendants further represented to Plaintiff that Plaintiff would be free to use the improvements before Plaintiff made the improvements.” (Complaint, ¶ 34.)

 

Plaintiff has failed to plead the aforementioned false promises with particularity (i.e., when they were made, in what form and by whom). Additionally, Plaintiff previously alleged that she had already made “additional constructions on the premises” “[b]eginning from August 2019” (Id., ¶ 15), which is well before Defendants are alleged to have made the alleged false promises in 2021-2022.

 

The Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action on this basis.

 

Third Cause of Action (i.e., Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment)

 

“[E]very lease includes a covenant of quiet possession and enjoyment.” (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1299.) “Minor inconveniences and annoyances are not actionable breaches of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment. To be actionable, the landlords act or omission must substantially interfere with a tenants right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy.” (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 589 [emphasis added].) “Breach can take many forms, including actual or constructive eviction.” (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 293.)

 

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants breached the covenant of quiet enjoyment by engaging in the following acts from January 2023-present: “a. Frequent and/or unnecessary visits the [sic] Premises for the purposes of distances of [sic] Plaintiff’s quiet enjoyment. b. Unnecessary inspection or maintenance of the Premises without notifying Plaintiff. C. Frequently visited the Premises, talked to Plaintiff’s subtenants, warned the subtenants that subtenants there were illegal and demanded the subtenants to move out. d. Frequently called Plaintiff asking for rental increases.” (Complaint, ¶ 44.)

 

The aforementioned acts, without more, do not constitute “substantial interference” with Plaintiff’s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy.[FN3]

 

The Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action on this basis.

 

Fourth Cause of Action (i.e., Retaliation in Violation of Civil Code § 1942.5)

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff has not alleged a violation of Civil Code section 1942.5(a), because Plaintiff did not plead that she made a “complaint to an appropriate agency as to the tenantability of a dwelling” and that she was “not in default as to the payment of rent.” 

Plaintiff, in turn, asserts that her fourth cause of action is predicated on subsection (d), which provides that “[n]otwithstanding subdivision (a), it is unlawful for a lessor to increase rent, . . . or threaten to do any of those acts, for the purpose of retaliating against the lessee because the lessee . . . has lawfully and peaceably exercised any rights under the law.” Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants increased rents in retaliation for Plaintiff notifying them that she would pay all future rent by check and would report her rental payment to the IRS in the future. (Complaint, ¶ 48.) Defendants have not filed a reply brief addressing subsection (d). 

The Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action.

 

Fifth Cause of Action (i.e., Violation of 2019 Tenant Protection Act) 

Under the Tenant Protection Act of 2019, an owner of residential real property must not, over the course of any 12-month period, increase the gross rental rate more than 5% plus the percentage change in the cost of living, or 10%, whichever is lower, of the lowest gross rental rate charged at any time during the 12 months prior to the effective date of the increase. (Civ. Code § 1947.12(a)(1).) Notice of any increase must be furnished in compliance with Civil Code section 827. (Civ. Code § 1947.12(e).) 

Plaintiff has alleged that she paid $1,750/month in rent from March 2014 through December 2017, $1,850.00 commencing from January 2018, and $1,900.00 commencing from January 2019. (Complaint, ¶¶ 9, 11, and 12.) Plaintiff has alleged that “[b]eginning on January 1, 2020, defendants requested Plaintiff to pay monthly rent in the amount of $1980.00” (Id., ¶ 13), that Plaintiff paid monthly rent of $1,980.00 from December 2021 to December 15, 2022 (Id., ¶ 58), that, in or about December 2022, Defendants asked Plaintiff to pay monthly rent in the amount of $2900, without any advance written notice (Id., ¶ 19), and that in January 2023, June 2023, and June 2024, Defendants raised rent to $2,600.00/month, $2,700.00/month and $2,835.00/month, respectively, without any advance written notice (Id., ¶¶ 20-22). 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff has not pled any facts indicating whether the premises “is exempt from AB1482.” (Dem., 13:2-3.) As Plaintiff notes, however, “[w]hether there is an exemption is a possible defense.” (Opp., 10:17-18.) 

Defendants next assert that Plaintiff’s allegations regarding rent charged are confusing. The Court disagrees. Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants asked Plaintiff to pay monthly rent of $2,900.00 in December 2022, but apparently began charging $2,600.00 in rent instead, effective January 2023. Defendants do not address Plaintiff’s allegations that rent was increased from $1,980.00 as of December 14, 2022 to $2,600.00 as of January 2023, without any advance written notice. 

Lastly, Defendants’ argument regarding emotional distress is more appropriately reserved for a motion to strike, inasmuch as a demurrer is not utilized to attack portions of a cause of action. 

The Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action.

 

Sixth Cause of Action (i.e., Breach of Quasi-Contract/Unjust Enrichment) 

“The elements for a claim of unjust enrichment are receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.” (Lyles v. Sangadeo-Patel (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 759, 769 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) 

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants received a benefit (namely, extensive construction improvements made by Plaintiff on the premises) at Plaintiff’s expense that they unjustly retained.

 

The Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action.

 

Seventh Cause of Action (i.e., Violation of the Unfair Competition Law—Business & Professions Code § 17200, et seq.)

“Business and Professions Code section 17200. . . establishes three varieties of unfair competition-acts or practices which are unlawful, or unfair, or fraudulent.” (Podolsky v. First Healthcare Corp. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 632, 647.) “Unlawful business acts or practices within the meaning of the UCL include anything that can properly be called a business practice and that at the same time is forbidden by law.  A practice is forbidden by law if it violates any law, civil or criminal, statutory or judicially made, federal, state or local. By extending to business acts or practices which are unlawful, the UCL permits violations of other laws to be treated as unfair competition that is independently actionable. Even if the violation of another law does not create a private right of action, if the violation constitutes unfair competition, it is actionable.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1474-1475 [quotations and citations omitted].)

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants violated Civil Code section 1942.5 and the 2019 Tenant Protection Act. (Complaint, ¶ 74.) Retaliatory eviction is an unfair business practice under Business and Professions Code section 17200. (Hernandez v. Stabach (1983) 145 Cal.App.3d 309.)

 

The Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action.

Leave to Amend

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained, with 20 days leave to amend in part (i.e., as to the first through third causes of action) and overruled in part (i.e., as to the fourth through seventh causes of action).

 

          B.       Defendants’ Motion to Strike

Defendants move to strike out the following portions of Plaintiff’s complaint:

          1.       Paragraph 41, 7:12-14 (i.e., “[t]he aforementioned conduct of defendants was done with malice, fraud and oppression, so as to justify an award of exemplary or punitive damages”);

          2.       Paragraph 55, 9:23-26 (i.e., “[t]he actions of Defendants were malicious and fraudulent, and in conscious disregard of the 2019 Tenant Protection Act (AB1482), and Plaintiff is entitled to an award of exemplary or punitive damages. Plaintiff is also entitled to recover reasonable attorney’s fees pursuant to Civil Code section 1942.5”);

 

          3.       Paragraph 67, 11:16-18 (i.e., “[t]he actions of Defendants were malicious and fraudulent, and in conscious disregard of the 2019 Tenant Protection Act (AB1482), and Plaintiff is entitled to an award of exemplary or punitive damages. Plaintiff is also entitled to recover reasonable attorney’s fees”);

          4.       Prayer, Paragraph E, 13:11-12 (i.e., “[f]or punitive and exemplary damages in an amount determined to be just to punish defendants”);

          5.       Prayer, Paragraph H, 13:16 (i.e., “[f]or Attorney’s fees herein incurred”);        

          6.       Paragraph 55, 9:25-26 (i.e., “Plaintiff is also entitled to recover reasonable attorney’s fees pursuant to Civil Code section 1942.5”);

          7.       Paragraph 67, 11:16-18 (i.e., “Plaintiff is also entitled to recover reasonable attorney’s fees”);

          8.       Paragraph 54, 9:20 (i.e., “plus emotional distress from Defendants’ pattern of intimidating behavior”); and

          9.       Paragraph 66, 11:13 (i.e., “plus emotional distress from Defendants’ pattern of intimidating behavior”).

 

Punitive Damages

At the outset, the motion is summarily denied as moot with respect to Paragraph 41, 7:12-14, based upon the ruling made on the demurrer.

Defendants assert that Plaintiff fails to allege any facts supporting a request for punitive damages (i.e., Requests Nos. 2-4 identified above). The Court disagrees. Plaintiff has alleged, in the fourth and fifth causes of action, that Defendants increased the rent on the premises dramatically and threatened to evict Plaintiff after Plaintiff spent $70,000.00 making improvements on the premises and notified Defendants that she would, inter alia, pay her future rent via check and report her rental payments to the IRS.

The Court denies the motion in this regard.

 

Attorney’s Fees

Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees pursuant to Civil Code section 1942.5 is inappropriate because Plaintiff has failed to allege retaliatory eviction. The Court disagrees, based on the ruling made on demurrer. The Court denies the motion in this regard.

 

Emotional Distress

Defendants assert that Plaintiff has not alleged any facts to support her claim for emotional distress; again, the Court disagrees, for the reasons set forth above. The Court denies the motion in this regard.

[FN2] The Court declines to entertain Defendants’ Statute of Frauds argument at this juncture, on the basis that the contract or contract(s) have not been identified in the first instance.

[FN3] Plaintiff, in opposition, references Lee’s August 6, 2024 filing of an unlawful detainer (“UD”) action. Inasmuch as the UD action was instituted after the filing of the instant action, it is not considered by the Court for the purpose of determining whether or not Plaintiff sufficiently alleged the elements of the third cause of action.
