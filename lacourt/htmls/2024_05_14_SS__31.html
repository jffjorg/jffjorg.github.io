[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT   

 

Dept. 31 

1:30 p.m.  

May 14, 2024 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Alan Sandleman (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Jagwinder Singh (“Defendant”) for damages arising from an automobile collision.  

On October 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Settlement of Entire Case providing the parties entered into a conditional settlement upon receipt of settlement funds.   

Plaintiff now moves for an order to enforce settlement agreement pursuant to CCP § 664.6.  Defendant opposes the motion, and Plaintiff filed a reply. 

At the April 30, 2024 hearing on this matter, after hearing oral argument, the Court agreed to take another look at the issues. After further consideration, the Court’s opinion has not changed. Despite Plaintiff’s arguments regarding the validity of electronic records and digital signatures, there is no written agreement before the Court to evaluate. 

 

II. MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT  

Pursuant to CCP § 664.6: “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” 

For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following: 

(1) The party. 

(2) An attorney who represents the party. 

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf. 

(CCP §664.6(b).)  

Strict compliance with the statutory requirements is necessary before a court can enforce a settlement agreement under this statute.  (Sully-Miller Contracting Co. v. Gledson/Cashman Construction, Inc. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 30, 37.)  Thus, to enforce a written settlement agreement under CCP § 664.6, the following three elements must be met: (1) the parties must have come to a meeting of the minds on all material points; (2) there must be a writing that contains the material terms of the agreement; and (3) the writing must be signed by the parties.  (Weddington Productions, Inc. v. Flick (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 793, 797-98.) 

Here, Defendant is correct that there is no evidence of a writing signed by the parties for the Court to enforce. CCP § 664.6 “require[s] the signatures of the parties seeking to enforce the agreement under [Code of Civil Procedure] section 664.6 and against whom the agreement is sought to be enforced.”  (J.B.B. Investment Partners, Ltd. v. Fair (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 974, 985.) “A procedure in which a settlement is evidenced by one writing signed by both sides minimizes the possibility of … dispute[s] and legitimizes the summary nature of the section 664.6 procedure.”  (Robertson v. Chen (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1290, 1293.) CCP § 664.6 requires strict compliance. The parties participating in mediation and agreeing to settle the dispute for a sum does not meet the statutory requirements of section 664.6, which requires a single writing containing the material terms of the agreement and signed by both parties.  

 

III. CONCLUSION 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement pursuant to CCP § 664.6 is DENIED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS 

 

Dept. 31 

1:30 p.m.  

May 14, 2024 

 

I. BACKGROUND  

Plaintiff Lymbhya Tavera Garcia (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Target Brands, Inc. (“Defendant”) for injuries Plaintiff sustained when she slipped on a pool of liquid in Defendant’s store and fell.  

On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions to strike Defendant’s Answer and enter judgment in the amount of $2 million dollars on the grounds that Defendant failed to comply with the Court’s December 22, 2023 Order. Defendant opposes the motion, and Plaintiff filed a reply. 

 

  1. Moving Argument 

Plaintiff argues that following the December 22, 2023 Order, Defendant did not serve responses within the time period ordered. On March 1, 2024, Defendant then produced Target’s policy manual through a link, and this was not attended by any response or acknowledgment to the other requests previously ordered. The issue was raised at IDC on April 9, 2024, and to date, Defendant has not served any responses in compliance with the December 22, 2023 Order.  

 

  1. Opposing Argument 

Defendant argues the delay in production is due to Defendant waiting for Plaintiff’s counsel to sign a stipulated protective order. Defendant contends this was discussed at the last IDC, and both parties understood the next steps. However, Plaintiff has not signed the protective order, which prevents Defendant from producing the documents. Defendant avers it has been cooperative in producing the documents sought and served its fourth set of supplemental responses on April 18, 2024. Defendant concedes to its tardiness, but argues that terminating sanctions are unwarranted since the documents cannot be produced absent a signed protective order. 

 

  1. Reply Argument 

Plaintiff reiterates that further responses were not served by January 22, 2024 in compliance with the December 22, 2023 Order, and that the further responses served on April 30, 2024 are incomplete and not code-compliant. Plaintiff contends there is no stipulated protective order, nor did the Court order a protective order as a condition of disclosure.   
 

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE  

Plaintiff requests the Court take judicial notice of: (1) the Court’s December 22, 2023 Order, (2) Clerk’s Certificate of Service, (3) Defendant’s Answer filed on August 12, 2020, (4) register of actions, (5) the complaint filed in Arnetta Dean v. Target Corporation, 2024CH01892 on March 11, 2024, and (6) Defendant’s motion for summary judgment. 

For requests 1-4 and 6, it is unnecessary for the Court to take judicial notice of matters on its own docket. Request 5 is denied; a complaint filed in another action has no bearing on this action.  

 

III. TERMINATING SANCTIONS  

  1. Legal Standard 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030 gives the court the discretion to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in a misuse of the discovery process.  A court may impose terminating sanctions by striking pleadings of the party engaged in misuse of discovery or entering default judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(d).)  A violation of a discovery order is sufficient for the imposition of terminating sanctions.  (Collison & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1620.)   

A terminating sanction is a "drastic measure which should be employed with caution."  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793.)  "A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction."  (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.)  While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery."  (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at 793.)  "[A] court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations."  (Ibid.)  Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information.  (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971].) 

 

  1. Discussion & Conclusion 

On December 22, 2023, the Court granted in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents (“RPDs”), set one, and ordered Defendant to provide further responses within thirty days as follows: (1) Request Nos. 19, 20, 21, 23, 32 and 36 were granted in part as to any documents not part of the “Guest Incident Investigation Kit” or directly subject to the attorney-client privilege, (2) Request No. 22 was granted only to the extent that Defendant must serve a code-compliant response, (3) Request No. 25 was granted in terms of providing supplemental responses with basic witness information of name and contact information, and (4) Request No. 37 was granted only to the extent of all security reports for all slip and fall incidents occurring anywhere in the subject Target covering 3 years prior to the incident. (Min. Order, Dec. 22, 2023.)  

Indeed, as argued by Plaintiff, a protective order was not contemplated in the Court’s December 22, 2023 Order. According to the Court’s recollection, the issues were resolved at the last IDC, and there is no basis for Defendant’s continuous delay in producing documents. No condition precedent was part of the Court’s order December 22, 2023 Order, and Defendant may not unilaterally set such conditions to avoid compliance. At this juncture, and considering the history of this issue, Defendant’s actions evince a measure of bad faith. However, the Court will not impose terminating sanctions at this time. The purpose of discovery sanctions is not for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information. (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971].) To impose terminating sanctions now does not serve a remedial purpose, and would be disproportionate to Defendant’s misconduct. (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 1215, 1223.) Pursuant to CCP § 2031.310(i), if a party fails to obey an order compelling further response, the court may impose a monetary sanction, issue sanction, evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction. Thus, the Court will first impose a monetary sanction for Defendant’s failure to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s RPDs, set one. Defendant is on notice that if it continues to delay and/or fail to comply with the Court’s December 22, 2023 Order, the Court will impose a greater sanction, which may be evidentiary/issue sanctions and/or terminating sanctions. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516 [If a lesser sanction fails to curb abuse, a greater sanction is warranted.].) 

  

III. CONCLUSION 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED. However, as provided above, the Court will impose on Defendant and its counsel of record monetary sanctions, payable to Plaintiff through counsel of record within 20 days. Plaintiff’s counsel is requested to provide their rates at the hearing. This tentative will be adjusted accordingly.

I. MOTIONS TO COMPEL 

  1. Background 

Plaintiff Jade Carrasco, by and through her Guardian Ad Litem Jesse Ronald Carrasco, (“Plaintiff Carrasco”) propounded form interrogatories, set one, and special interrogatories, set one, on defendant Joseph Radgowski (“Defendant Joseph”) on December 27, 2023. (Quiroga Decl. ¶ 7.) Responses were due on or before January 27, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 8.) To date, Defendant Joseph has not served responses. (Id. at ¶ 10.) Plaintiff Carrasco therefore seeks an order compelling Defendant Joseph to respond, without objections, to the outstanding discovery and to pay sanctions.  

Defendant Joseph is in pro per. As of May 1, 2024, no opposition has been filed. 

 

  1. Legal Standard 

For a motion to compel initial discovery responses, all a propounding party must show is that it properly served its discovery requests, that the time to respond has expired, and that the party to whom the requests were directed failed to provide a timely response. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 906.) Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a), 2031.300, subd. (a).) 

 

  1. Discussion & Conclusion  

Therefore, because the evidence shows Defendant Joseph was properly served with discovery and failed to timely respond, any objections have been waived. Plaintiff Carrasco’s unopposed motions are GRANTED.  

Defendant Joseph is ordered to serve verified responses to Plaintiff’s Carrasco’s form interrogatories, set one, and special interrogatories, set one, without objections, within thirty (30) days. (CCP § 2030.290(a),(b).) 

 
II. SANCTIONS 

Sanctions are mandatory against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response, unless a court makes certain findings.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).)¿Defendant Joseph did not file any opposition. However, sanctions may be awarded, even though no opposition was filed, pursuant to CRC 3.1348(a). Plaintiff Carrasco seeks $1,710 in monetary sanctions for each motion.  

A court has discretion to award sanctions that are “suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks” but they should not be punitive in nature or levied for the purposes of punishing an offending party. (Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545.)        

Plaintiff Carrasco is awarded $200 for each motion as attorney’s fees. Further, Plaintiff Carrasco is awarded two motion filing fees of $60, for a total of $120 as costs.   

Sanctions are imposed against Defendant Joseph. Defendant Joseph is ordered to pay sanctions to Plaintiff Carrasco, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $520, within thirty (30) days.

Dept. 31

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Abram Tavera for damages arising from a motor vehicle accident.  Trial is currently set for June 20, 2024.   

At this time, Plaintiff moves to compel the deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable and Document Production.  On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff served a Notice of Deposition of the Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) and Document Production Requests on Defendant. (Sarkesians Decl., ¶ 2.) On January 17, 2024, Defendant objected to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition due to being unilaterally set. (Id., ¶ 3.) On January 24, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed defense counsel requesting alterative dates for said deposition. (Id., ¶ 4.) On January 26, 2024, defense counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel stating that they cannot comply with the notice of deposition as “no one that fits the categories” provided.  

In opposition, Defendant objects to the deposition on the ground that the date was unilaterally selected by Plaintiff’s counsel. Further, Defendant contends that there is no such entity as “The Estate of Abraham Tavera” and that there is no PMK for a non-existent entity. 

 

 

II. DISCUSSION 

Here, objections to a notice of the deposition are very limited and may only pertain to errors or irregularities in the deposition notice itself. (CCP § 2025.410, [“Any party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with Article 2 (commencing with Section 2025.210) waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.].) Article 2 which consists of Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2025.210-2025.295 provide specific requirements that a deposition notice must satisfy. The unilateral setting of the deposition, in itself, is not a valid basis for objection. 

In terms of the contention of the non-existence of “The Estate of Abraham Tavera” (Doe 1), even in instances where an entity is not in existence for purposes of producing a person most knowledgeable or qualified, a court may direct an entity to elect or appoint an individual who can act as a person most qualified or knowledgeable for purposes of a deposition.¿ (Melendrez v. Superior Court (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1343, 1347.)¿ Here, defendant Abram Tavera (“Decedent”) has passed away, and defense counsel avers that the defense has already admitted liability for the incident’s occurrence. Due to the closure of Department 30 of Spring Street Courthouse and the recent transfer of this action to the Department 31 of Spring Street Courthouse, this Court is unaware of any discussions that may have previously been held regarding a substitution for Decedent as a party to this action. As such, the Court will work with the parties to schedule an IDC so that the Court may discuss the issues with the parties prior to ruling on the motion. In general, a pending action may still proceed against Decedent’s personal representative or successor-in-interest (CCP § 377.40). An estate cannot speak for itself, nor can counsel represent a client who is dead. There would necessarily need to be an appointed personal representative on behalf of Decedent. It would appear to this Court that Plaintiff would therefore be entitled to take the deposition of Decedent’s designated personal representative and/or successor-in-interest, regardless of whether the personal representative may or may not have any relevant information in this matter. The Court will discuss the issues with the party at the hearing and at an IDC, if needed.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION 

 

Dept. 31 

1:30 p.m.  

May 14, 2024 

 

I. BACKGROUND FACTS 

Plaintiff Juan Pablo Alcantar (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Alexander Escalante (“Defendant”) for injuries arising from a motor vehicle v. bicycle incident.  

On March 19, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion compelling Plaintiff to appear for a mental examination with neuropsychologist John T. Dunn, Ph.D., and ordered the parties to draft a stipulated protective order regarding disclosure and use of the raw test data. (Min. Order, March 19, 2024.) 

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for reconsideration of the Court’s March 19, 2024 Order on the grounds that Plaintiff will stipulate within the meaning of CCP § 2032.320. Defendant opposes the motion, and Plaintiff filed a reply.  

 

II. MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION 

A. Timeliness of Motion 

CCP § 1008(a) states:  

 

When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. 

CCP § 1008(a), thus, requires a motion for reconsideration to be filed within ten days after notice of the subject ruling. Defendant served the Notice of Ruling on April 25, 2024. The motion for reconsideration was filed on April 8, 2024. Thus, the motion filed prior to the notice service date is timely since the clock had yet to run at the time. 

 

B. Legal Standard 

CCP § 1008(a), thus, requires the Court to reconsider a prior ruling if it finds there are new or different facts, circumstances, or law than those before the Court at the time of the original ruling.  Once the Court determines the existence of new or different facts, circumstances, of law, it can either modify or affirm its prior decision. (Corns v. Miller (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 195, 202.) 

 “[A] court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon ‘new or different facts, circumstances, or law.’” (Ibid.) The burden under § 1008 “is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.” (New York Times Co. v. Sup.Ct. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213.) There is a strict requirement of diligence, so the moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence or different facts earlier.  (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.)   

C. Discussion 

In the original briefs regarding this issue of Plaintiff’s mental examination, the factual circumstance of Plaintiff offering to Defendant to stipulate to not making any psychological claim, and to maintain nothing more than garden variety emotional damages claim was raised. However, Plaintiff’s opposition at the time did not offer a CCP § 2032.320 stipulation in order for this Court to preclude the granting of the mental examination absent a showing of exceptional circumstances. As cited by both Plaintiff and Defendant, aside from showing that evidence of new or different facts exist, the moving party must also provide a satisfactory explanation for failing to produce such evidence earlier. (Pazderka v. Caballeros Dimas Alang, Inc. (1998) 62 Cal. App. 4th 658, 670.) Here, Plaintiff provides no explanation as to why a stipulation under CCP § 2032.320 was not raised earlier. The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered, and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 150.)  On its face, Plaintiff’s application for reconsideration has not met the mandatory prerequisites. 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Santos Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Housing Authority of The City of Los Angeles (“HACLA”) and Does 1 to 50 for damages arising from a workplace injury. Plaintiff alleges he was employed to perform soldering work on an elevator in Building 2 of the Jordan Downs Housing Project. While the elevator was positioned on the 6th floor, Plaintiff fell six stories in free fall when the elevator suddenly fell and crashed to the 1st floor. 

Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) sets forth seven causes of action against HACLA and Does 1 to 50 for: (1) negligence – liability for employee, (2) negligence – liability for independent contractor, (3) premises liability, (4) liability for non-delegable duty, (5) liability for dangerous condition on the property, (6) negligence as a common carrier under CA Civil Code §2100, and (5) strict liability under CA Civil Code §2101.  

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint naming Walton Construction, Inc. (“Walton”) as Doe 1. Walton now demurs to the first, second, fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action of the FAC, arguing these causes of action fail to state sufficient facts because Walton is not a public entity and not a common carrier.  

Plaintiff opposes the motion, and Walton filed a reply.  

 

II. DEMURRER 

  1. Meet and Confer 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (CCP § 430.41(a).)   

The Court finds Walton has fulfilled this requirement prior to filing the demurrer.  (Mot. Blackstone Decl. ¶¶ 3-7.) 

 

  1. Legal Standard 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings.  It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (CCP §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery.  (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-72.)  The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint; or in a superseded complaint in the same action.  (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”].) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action.  (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97,119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) 

 

  1. 1st Cause of Action – Liability for Employee 

Walton contends that the first cause of action alleges that all defendants are liable under California Government Code § 815.2, and that it is therefore not viable cause of action against it because Walton is not a public entity.  

Contrary to Walton’s assertion that Walton is alleged to be liable under Gov. Code §815.2 [Liability of Public Entities], Walton’s alleged liability as Doe 1 is not tied, in any manner, to this provision.  The FAC alleges in relevant part that Does 1-50 negligently controlled and maintained the elevator by restricting Plaintiff’s access and instructing Plaintiff to utilize the elevator to access the location of the metal beam that needed to be soldered. (FAC at ¶ 14.) As a result of Does 1-50’s control over the temporary platform and failure to stop work upon notice of a dangerous condition, Does 1-50 caused Plaintiff permanent physical and emotional injury. (Id. at ¶¶ 15-18.) In short, the FAC alleges duty, breach, causation, and damages against Does 1 to 50. Paragraph 19 of the first cause of action citing to Gov. Code §815.2 refers specifically to HACLA only.  

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.  

 

  1. 2nd Cause of Action – Liability For Independent Contractor 

Walton argues the second cause of action asserts Walton is liable under Gov. Code § 815.4 for injury caused by a tortious act or omission of an independent contractor of a public entity. 

Here, the SAC alleges that Does 1-50 and/or Does 1-50’s independent contractors negligently conducted itself with respect to control over the elevator, and realleges the factual allegations of access restriction, instructions to Plaintiff, the temporary elevator platform, and the failure to stop work upon notice of a dangerous condition resulting in Plaintiff’s physical and emotional injuries. (FAC at ¶¶ 22-27.) Similar to the first cause of action, the reference to Gov. Code § 815.4 is specific to HACLA only. (Id. at ¶ 28.) 

Accordingly, Walton’s demurrer to the second cause of action is overruled.  

 

  1. 4th Cause of Action – Liability for Non-Delegable Duty 

Walton argues that the FAC alleges it is liable under Gov. Code § 815.6 for failure to discharge a mandatory duty when it is not a public entity. Walton further contends that it is not a common carrier, but instead a licensed general contractor not engaged in transportation as a regular undertaking or engaged in transportation services. Walton contends this is not a cause of action, and that Plaintiff already asserted a claim against it for negligence in the third cause of action for premises liability. 

Again, a review of the specific allegations contained FAC provides that the allegation of the Gov. Code is related to HACLA only. (FAC ¶ 47.) The FAC alleges that all defendants hired Steel Craft Fabrication Inc. to perform soldering work, defendants retained control of the work, defendants are common carriers, and that Plaintiff suffered severe physical injuries as a result of defendants’ acts or omissions related elevator access and the temporary elevator platform. (Id. at ¶ 44-46, 48-49). The Court’s review on demurrer is limited to the face of the pleading and all matters judicially noticeable. The function of a demurrer is to test the legal sufficiency of a complaint, but not the truthfulness of the allegations.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) The Court will not consider Walton’s extrinsic facts that it is actually not a common carrier. (Gould v. Maryland Sound Industries, Inc. (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1137, 1144. [“‘[D]efendants cannot set forth allegations of fact in their demurrers which, if true, would defeat plaintiff's complaint.’”].)  

In terms of the contention that the fourth cause of action is duplicative of the third cause of action for premises liability, Walton does not provide this as a basis for the demurrer in its Notice. The Notice states Walton is demurring to the fourth cause of action for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action against it, and for uncertainty. As a general rule, the trial court may consider only the grounds stated in the notice of motion. (Gonzales v. Superior Court (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1542, 1545.) The purpose of these requirements is to cause the moving party to “sufficiently define the issues for the information and attention of the adverse party and the court.” (Hernandez v. National Dairy Products (1954) 126 Cal.App.2d 490, 493.) The Court will not consider an argument not properly noticed, nor supported in the moving papers with any authority. The contention was raised as one sentence in the moving papers, and Walton notably did not provide any case law until the reply, which prevents Plaintiff from having a meaningful opportunity to respond.   

 Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled. 

 

  1. 5th Cause of Action – Liability for Dangerous Condition of Property 

Here, Walton’s contention that the cause of action asserting Gov. Code § 835 [Dangerous Condition of Public Property] against it finally has merit. The FAC alleges that all defendants had actual and constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Gov. Code § 835 to have sufficient time prior to injury, and that Plaintiff suffered injuries as a result thereof. (FAC ¶¶ 57-58). Only HACLA is alleged to be a public entity for this Gov. Code § 835 to be applicable. (Id. at ¶ 56). There are no allegations that Walton is a public entity for this fifth cause of action to be applicable to it. Plaintiff’s opposition refers to Gov. Code §815.2 and argues that it was specifically alleged against HACLA only. However, Gov. Code §815.2 is related only to the first cause of action. The fifth cause of action alleges Gov. Code § 835, which pertains to public entity liability for a dangerous condition of its property.  

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained. 

 

  1. 6th Cause of Action – Negligence as a Common Carrier Under Civil Code §2100 

Plaintiff re-alleges the factual allegations, and asserts that all defendants were common carriers within the meaning of Civil Code §2100. (FAC ¶¶ 59-62.) Walton argues that it is not a common carrier. There is no requirement that Plaintiff must prove that Walton is a common carrier by alleging all the factors that have bearing on determining whether a party is a common carrier. As previously provided, the purpose of a demurrer is to test the legal sufficiency of the complaint, not to weigh on the truthfulness of the allegations. “No matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff's allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.” (Kerivan v. Title Ins. & Trust Co. (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 225, 229.) 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is overruled.  

 

  1. 7th Cause of Action – Strict Liability Under Civil Code §2101 

The FAC alleges that defendants utilized a “carrier persons” (elevator) in its building 2 of the Jordan Downs Housing Project, and that the elevator was not safe or fit for the purpose to which it was put, thereby causing Plaintiff injuries. (FAC ¶¶ 63-65.) Walton contends it cannot be deemed a common carrier. The Court refers to its analysis under the sixth cause of action.  

Accordingly, the demurrer to the seventh cause of action is overruled. 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, Walton’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC is SUSTAINED IN PART as to the fifth cause of action for dangerous condition of property only. The demurrer as to the first, second, fourth, sixth, and seventh causes of action are OVERRULED.  

The burden is on Plaintiff to show in what manner they can amend the complaint, and how that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349; Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.) Unless Plaintiff plans on alleging that Walton is actually a public entity for the fifth cause of action to be applicable to it, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action will be sustained without leave to amend.
