Order to Show Cause re Why Defendant Intercity Fellowship Hall’s Answer Should Not be Stricken Because it is a Corporation Not Represented by Counsel

            On March 22, 2024, the motion to be relieved as counsel for defendant Intercity Fellowship Hall (“Defendant”), filed by attorney Zachary McCready, came before the court.  The court issued an order on that date (1) noting that Defendant filed, on March 6, 2024, a “Substitution of Attorney – Civil” form, stating that Defendant is substituting in as counsel to represent itself in this action in place of counsel, (2) finding that the motion to be relieved as counsel was therefore moot, and (3) setting an Order to Show Cause re why Defendant’s answer should not be stricken because it is a corporation not represented by counsel for hearing on June 10, 2024.  (Mar. 22, 2024 Order, pp. 1:20-28, 2:5-8.)  The court further ordered plaintiff Mary Erickson (“Plaintiff”) to give notice of that ruling.

On June 10, 2024, the court issued an order finding that Plaintiff did not serve a notice of the court’s March 22, 2024 ruling on Defendant.  (June 10, 2024 Order, p. 2:5-8.)  The court therefore exercised its discretion to continue the hearing on the Order to Show Cause to July 15, 2024.  (Id. at p. 2:9-13.)  The court further ordered that any response to the Order to Show Cause shall be filed no later than nine court days before the hearing.  (Id. at p. 2:14-15.)

            First, the court finds that Plaintiff has given Defendant notice of the pending Order to Show Cause re why Defendant’s answer should not be stricken as a corporation unrepresented by counsel.  (June 10, 2024 Notice of Ruling, p. 7 [proof of service by certified mail to Defendant at 5881 Cherry Avenue, Long Beach, California]; March 6, 2024 Substitution of Attorney, MC-050, ¶ 2 [listing Defendant’s address to be 5881 Cherry Avenue, Long Beach, California].)

            Second, the court finds that Defendant is improperly representing itself in this action.

            “[U]nder a long-standing common law rule of procedure, a corporation, unlike a natural person, cannot represent itself before courts of record in propria persona, nor can it represent itself through a corporate officer, director or other employee who is not an attorney.¿ It must be represented by licensed counsel in proceedings before courts of record.”¿ (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1145.)¿¿ 

            Defendant is a corporation.  (Mot. to be Relieved as Counsel, MC-051, ¶ 4, subd. (b) [Defendant is a corporation]; Def. Answer, ¶ 5 [“Admitted that INTERCITY FELLOWSHIP HALL, is a California corporation”].)  Thus, Defendant is required to be represented by counsel in this action.  (CLD Construction, Inc., supra, 120 Cal.App.4th at p. 1145.)  However, on March 6, 2024, Defendant filed a “Substitution of Attorney” form (Judicial Council form MC-050), stating that it was substituting in to represent itself in this action in place of attorney Zachary McCready.  (Def. March 6, 2024 Sub. of Attorney, MC-050, ¶¶ 1-2.)  Defendant has not (1) filed another Substitution of Attorney form stating that it is now represented by counsel, or (2) filed a response to the Order to Show Cause or other evidence with the court establishing that it has since retained counsel.

            Thus, the court finds that Defendant is improperly representing itself in propria persona and therefore grants its own motion to strike Defendant’s answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b) [court may, at any time in its discretion, strike a pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state].)

 

            The court orders that “Defendant Intercity Fellowship Hall’s Answer to Plaintiff Mary Erickson’s Initial Complaint; Jury Trial Demanded,” filed by defendant Intercity Fellowship Hall on June 2, 2023, is stricken.

            The court orders plaintiff Mary Erickson to file a Request for Entry of Default (Judicial Council form CIV-100) as to defendant Intercity Fellowship Hall no later than 10 days from the date of this order.

            The court orders that trial, set for April 16, 2025, is vacated.

            The court orders that the final status conference, set for April 4, 2025, is vacated.

            The court sets an Order to Show Cause re entry of default and default judgment for hearing on November 4, 2024, at 8:30 a.m., in Department 53.

            The court orders plaintiff Mary Erickson to file default judgment documents required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800 (including a proposed judgment on JUD-100) no later than September 26, 2024.   

            The court orders plaintiff Mary Erickson to give notice of this ruling.

Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s ex parte application for leave to file or in the alternative ost iso motion for reconsideration of petition for injunctive relief and sanctions

 

 

 

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed “Plaintiff’s Notice and Ex Parte Application for Leave to File or in the Alternative OST ISO Motion for Reconsideration of Petition for Injunctive Relief and Sanctions,” which is set on the court’s calendar for July 15, 2024.

            First, Plaintiff filed two previous ex parte applications seeking a preliminary injunction, which were presented to and denied by the court on June 18 and July 2, 2024.  Plaintiff’s ex parte application for reconsideration of those orders is not based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a).    

Second, the court finds that plaintiff has not satisfied the requirement of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1202, subdivision (c), that an applicant for an ex parte order must make an affirmative factual showing in a declaration containing competent testimony based on personal knowledge of irreparable harm, immediate danger, or other statutory basis for granting relief ex parte.

Courts may, in ruling on a request for injunctive relief, take into consideration a plaintiff’s delay in determining the existence of imminent irreparable injury.  (O’Connell v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1481 [noting that “the urgency with which the trial court was forced to decide plaintiffs’ motion may have been, to some extent, of plaintiffs’ own making—a fact that the trial court, as a court of equity, should have taken into account in determining what weight to give plaintiffs’ claim of imminent irreparable injury”]; Lusk v. Krejci (1960) 187 Cal.App.2d 553, 556 [“Long delays in assertion of rights can be the basis of denial of mandatory injunctive relief”].)  Here, Plaintiff has filed ex parte applications for preliminary injunction based on (1) an alleged illegal lockout and wrongful eviction that occurred on December 22, 2021, and (2) an alleged violation that occurred on or about November 14, 2022.  (Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application, filed July 1, 2024, p. 2:20-26; Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application filed July 12, 2024, p. 2:19-23.)

Third, the court does not find good cause to grant Plaintiff’s request for an order shortening time on the hearing on the relief requested in the ex parte application or to prescribe shorter times for the filing and service of papers than the times specified in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b).  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (b).)

The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s ex parte application for leave for OST or in the alternative ex parte hearing of his request for reconsideration of the court’s June 18 and July 2, 2024 orders denying Plaintiff’s ex parte applications for preliminary injunction.

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion for issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions against plaintiff

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:             Defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC, joined by defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas on June 20, 2024 

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Emilio Reyes

Motion for Issue, Evidence, or Terminating Sanctions Against Plaintiff

The court considered the moving, joinder, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC’s evidentiary objections, filed on July 8, 2024, as follows:

Objection No. 1 is sustained.

            The court overrules plaintiff Emilio Reyes’s objection to defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC’s reply papers because they were filed at least five court days before the hearing on this motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

            The court notes that plaintiff Emilio Reyes has also objected to “Defendant Lorraine Ann Escobar’s Notice of Joinder and Joinder to Defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC’s Motion for Terminating, Etc., Sanctions, and Objection to Plaintiff’s Declaration; [and] Declaration of Aidan W. Butler[.]”  (Pl. Objections, p. 2, ¶ 2.)  However, the court has not received any filings from defendant Lorraine Ann Escobar joining the pending motion.  Thus, the court does not rule on this objection.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC (“McIntosh”) moves the court for an order imposing issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions against plaintiff Emilio Reyes (“Plaintiff”) on the grounds that Plaintiff (1) failed to respond or submit to authorized methods of discovery, and (2) disobeyed a court order to provide discovery by refusing to answer questions at his deposition.  Defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas (the “Los Indios Defendants”) filed a joinder to McIntosh’s motion on June 20, 2024, asserting that the bases of McIntosh’s motion apply equally to them.

If a party engages in a misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.)¿ Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 provides, in relevant part, that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery.”¿  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d), (g).)  “The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’”  (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)

First, the court denies McIntosh’s request for issue or evidence sanctions because McIntosh did not request, in its notice of motion or supporting memorandum, the imposition of specific issue or evidence sanctions that it contends are warranted and tailored to the harm caused by Plaintiff’s failure to respond to its questioning at his deposition.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subds. (b) [authorizing issue sanctions that (1) designated facts be taken as established or (2) prohibiting any party from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses], (c) [authorizing evidence sanction prohibiting party from introducing designated matters into evidence]; Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604 [court should select a sanction that is tailored to the harm caused by the withheld discovery].)

Second, the court finds that McIntosh has not justified its request for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff.  

On May 1, 2024, the court issued an order (1) granting Los Indios Defendants’ motion to compel the deposition of Plaintiff, and (2) directing Plaintiff to attend and testify in person at a deposition to be taken and recorded by counsel for Los Indios Defendants on May 8, 2024.  (May 1, 2024 Order, pp. 3:26-4:4.)  On May 2, 2024, the court issued a minute order modifying its May 1, 2024 order to order Plaintiff to appear for deposition on May 30, 2024.  (May 2, 2024 Minute Order, p. 2.)

Plaintiff appeared for his deposition on May 30, 2024.  (Sisson Decl., ¶ 2.)  Prior to deposition, counsel for the named defendants agreed that counsel for Los Indios Defendants would question Plaintiff for four hours, counsel for McIntosh would question Plaintiff for one and one-half hours, and counsel for nonmoving defendant Lorraine Escobar would question Plaintiff for the remaining one and one-half hours.  (Sisson Decl., ¶ 3.)  However, at his deposition, Plaintiff asserted that he was not given notice of McIntosh’s intent to question him and therefore refused to submit to questioning by its counsel.[1]  (Def. Appendix of Evid., Ex. Z, Pl. Dep. pp. 211:3-20 [“Because I have no proper notice I am not answering any of your questions”], 212:25-213:11.)

Plaintiff contends that he did not refuse to attend his deposition and was required only to submit to questioning by counsel for Los Indios Defendants.  The court disagrees.  Plaintiff was required to allow counsel for McIntosh (and nonmoving defendant Lorraine Escobar) to examine him at his deposition.  Although the court’s May 1, 2024 order, in ruling on the motion filed solely by the Los Indios Defendants, ordered Plaintiff to attend and testify at a deposition to be taken by counsel for Los Indios Defendants, the law requires Plaintiff to submit to examination by other parties at his deposition.  Moreover, there is no language in the court’s May 1, 2024 order that precluded McIntosh (or nonmoving defendant Lorraine Escobar) from questioning Plaintiff pursuant to such law.[2]

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2025.610 and 2025.290, any natural person, including a party to the action, may not be deposed more than once, except upon a showing of good cause, and “a deposition examination of the witness by all counsel, other than the witness’ counsel of record, shall be limited to seven hours of total testimony.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.610, subds. (a) [no party “may take a subsequent deposition of that deponent” once any party has taken the deposition of any natural person], (b) [court may grant leave to take subsequent deposition for good cause shown], 2025.290, subd. (a) [emphasis added].)  “This applies both to depositions by the party who took the first depo[sition] and to any other party served with the deposition notice.  I.e., all parties are expected to examine the deponent during the first deposition.”  (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2024) ¶ 8:478 [emphasis in original].)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.330 provides that the “[e]xamination and cross-examination of the deponent shall proceed as permitted at trial under the provisions of the Evidence Code.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.330, subd. (d); Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2024) ¶ 8:709 [“All parties present at the deposition have the right to cross-examine the deponent, just as at trial”].)  At trial, a witness is subject to the examination of all parties.  (Evid. Code, §§ 711 [“At the trial of an action, a witness can be heard only in the presence and subject to the examination of all the parties to the action, if they choose to attend and examine [emphasis added], 773, subd. (b), 776, subd. (b).)  Plaintiff was therefore required to submit to examination by counsel for all defendants, including McIntosh.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff (1) violated the court’s May 1, 2024 order to attend and testify at a deposition, and (2) failed to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (g), (d).)  The court further finds that McIntosh has demonstrated that it has been prejudiced by Plaintiff’s conduct because McIntosh did not have the opportunity to question Plaintiff regarding his ability to establish the elements of each cause of action alleged against it, the existence of an attorney-client relationship between McIntosh and Plaintiff, and Plaintiff’s federal action.  (Sisson Decl., ¶¶ 5-6.)

However, the court finds that McIntosh has not made a sufficient showing to support terminating sanctions because, based on the record presented, McIntosh has not presented argument or evidence showing (1) that Plaintiff’s actions were willful, (2) the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery, or (3) that Plaintiff’s misuse of the discovery process set forth above was preceded by a history of abuse.  (Los Defensores, Inc., supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390.)  Moreover, the court finds that the less severe remedy of directing Plaintiff to appear for a further session of his deposition is an appropriate remedy to address the prejudice suffered by McIntosh as a result of Plaintiff’s misuse of the discovery process.  (Lopez, supra, 246 Cal.App.4th at p. 604.)

The court therefore denies McIntosh’s request for terminating sanctions, without prejudice to McIntosh’s filing a new motion for appropriate monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions in the event that Plaintiff does not comply with the order to complete his deposition and to permit, and respond to, examination by counsel for McIntosh and the other defendants in this action set forth below.

Third, the court denies Los Indios Defendants’ joinder to McIntosh’s motion.  Los Indios Defendants did not present sufficient argument or evidence establishing that Plaintiff’s refusal to submit to questioning by counsel for McIntosh harmed or prejudiced them, such that the imposition of terminating sanctions in their favor and against Plaintiff is warranted.  (Lopez, supra, 246 Cal.App.4th at p. 604 [sanctions must be tailored to the harm caused by withheld discovery and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to, but denied, discovery].)  

ORDER

The court denies defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC’s motion for issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions, without prejudice to defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC’s filing a new motion for monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions if plaintiff Emilio Reyes does not comply with the court’s order set forth below.

The court orders plaintiff Emilio Reyes to attend and testify in person at a further session of deposition to allow counsel for defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC, and counsel for other defendants, to examine plaintiff Emilio Reyes for an additional three hours, on July _____, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., at the following location: ___________________________________________________________________________.

            The court orders defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC to give notice of this ruling.
