Case Number: 23STCV00547    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 30

  PETITION TO CONFIRM MINOR’S COMPROMISE  

 

 

Analysis 

 

  1. Procedural Requirements 

 

Petitioner Ignacia Nuno is the guardian ad litem of the disabled claimant Leslie Gadea. 

 

Petitioner has filed a Proposed Order with the petition, along with a Proposed Order to deposit money into a blocked account. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co., Ltd. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1605 [stating that a petition for court approval must comply with Cal. Rules of Court, rules 7.950, 7.950.5, 7.591 and 7.952].) Petitioner uses Judicial Council form MC-350 for the Petition and presents the Court with a Proposed Order Approving Compromise of the Disputed Claim. 

 

The Petition is thus procedurally proper. 

 

  1. Substantive Requirements  

 

Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor or dependent adult’s claim. (Prob. Code, §§ 3500, 3600; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.)   

 

Pursuant to Probate Code section 3600, subdivision (a)(2), a court may approve a compromise of a pending action or proceeding to which a minor or person with a disability is a party. Upon making such an Order,  

 

[t]he court . . . shall make a further order authorizing and directing that reasonable expenses, medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator, costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or person with a disability. 

 

(Prob. Code, § 3601, subd. (a).)   

 

  1. Discussion 

 

Here, the Petitioner has filled out form MC-350 and adequately satisfied the form’s requirements. After reviewing the Petition, the Court finds that the settlement is fair and reasonable. Defendants Norma Salceda, M.D., Stan Tovsky, M.D., and Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center will pay sums of $975,000, $837,500, and $1,500,000 to Claimant, respectively. (Petition ¶ 10.) Petitioner is not a Plaintiff in this action and will not receive any compensation under the settlement aside from expenses. (Id. ¶ 11.)  

 

$1,167,461.45 will be taken out of Claimant’s recovery to reimburse Medical for Claimant’s care costs. (Id. ¶ 12.) $1,084,068.39 will be taken out of Claimant’s recovery for attorney’s fees. (Id. ¶ 13.) $50,000 will be taken out of the Claimant’s recovery to reimburse Petitioner for childcare costs. (Ibid.) And $27,444.26 will be taken out of the Claimant’s recovery to reimburse litigation costs. (Ibid.) Accordingly, after paying fees and costs, Claimant will recover $983,525.90. (Id. ¶ 15.) There is no guardianship or conservatorship of the estate of claimant, and Petitioner requests a court order to invest $700,000 in a single-premium deferred annuity and to transfer $283,525.90 into a special needs trust for the benefit of the person with the disability. (Id. ¶ 18.) 

 

Counsel for Petitioner provides a declaration attesting to his reasonable attorney fees. (Attachment 13a.) Petitioner and her attorney agreed to a contingent fee agreement at a rate of 33% of the Claimant’s recovery. (Id., Kramer Decl. ¶ 37.) The requested fees are fair and reasonable given counsel’s experience and time spent on the case. 

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Petition.

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION 

 

Analysis 

 

The Hearth Project, LLC (Defendant) moves for an order compelling Nicole Greene (Plaintiff) to submit to binding arbitration and stay this action pending resolution of the arbitration. The motion is granted. 

 

Evidentiary Objections 

 

Defendant objects to the declaration of Andrew Bogard on various grounds. Those objections are overruled. Bogard sufficiently establishes foundation and personal knowledge of the events contained in the declaration to survive an objection under Evidence Code section 702. (Bogard Decl. ¶ 1.) Nor does the Court find the testimony within the declaration to be speculative. Defendant’s objections on hearsay grounds are overruled as the statements constitute party admissions. 

 

  1. Legal Standard 

 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) 

 

A proceeding to compel arbitration is in essence a suit in equity to compel specific performance of a contract. (Freeman v. State Farm Mutual Auto Insurance Co. (1975) 14 Cal.3d 473, 479.) Such enforcement may be sought by a party to the arbitration agreement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1280, subd. (e)(1).)  

 

A motion to compel arbitration requires the facts to be proven by affidavit or declaration and documentary evidence with oral testimony taken only in the court’s discretion. (Code Civ. Proc., §1290.2; Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413–414.) The motion must set forth the provisions of the written agreement and the arbitration clause verbatim, or such provisions must be attached and incorporated by reference. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330; see Condee v. Longwood Mgmt. Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218.)   

 

Once the moving party alleges that an arbitration agreement exists, the burden shifts to the responding party to prove the falsity of the purported agreement, and no evidence or authentication is required to find the arbitration agreement exists. (See Condee, supra, 88 Cal.App.4th at p. 219.) However, if the existence of the agreement is challenged, “[movant] bears the burden of proving [the arbitration agreement’s] existence by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal, supra, p. 413; see also Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1058–1060.) 

 

  1. Discussion  

 

Defendant argues that a valid arbitration agreement exists. A party moving to compel arbitration may meet its initial burden by providing a copy of the signed arbitration agreement. (Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165–166.) Here, Defendant points to the Purchase and Sale Agreement (PSA) between the parties and the arbitration provision contained therein. (Compl., Ex. A § 14.) The PSA provides that “any dispute . . . shall first be submitted to mediation and, if not settled during meditation, shall thereafter be submitted to binding arbitration.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff does not challenge the validity of the agreement. Thus, Defendant has met its initial burden.  

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant waived its right to compel arbitration. In determining whether a party has waived its right to arbitration, courts consider many factors, including: “(1) whether the party's actions are inconsistent with the right to arbitrate; (2) whether ‘the litigation machinery has been substantially invoked’ and the parties ‘were well into preparation of a lawsuit’ before the party notified the opposing party of an intent to arbitrate; (3) whether a party either requested arbitration enforcement close to the trial date or delayed for a long period before seeking a stay; (4) whether a defendant seeking arbitration filed a counterclaim without asking for a stay of the proceedings; (5) ‘whether important intervening steps [e.g., taking advantage of judicial discovery procedures not available in arbitration] had taken place’; and (6) whether the delay ‘affected, misled, or prejudiced’ the opposing party.” (St. Agnes Medical Center v. PacifiCare of California (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1187, 1196 (St. Agnes).) Proof of waiver must be by clear and convincing evidence. (Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 562, 584 (Quach).) But there is no requirement that the party opposing enforcement of the arbitration agreement demonstrate prejudice. (Id. at p. 585.) 

 

Defendant’s actions are not inconsistent with the right to arbitrate. “To establish waiver under generally applicable contract law, the party opposing enforcement of a contractual agreement must prove by clear and convincing evidence that the waiving party knew of the contractual right and intentionally relinquished or abandoned it.” (Quach, supra, 16 Cal.5th at p. 584.) This may be shown through evidence of words expressing an intent to relinquish the right or “conduct that is so inconsistent with an intent to enforce the contractual right as to lead a reasonable factfinder to conclude that the party had abandoned it.” (Ibid.) 

 

 Here, Plaintiff does not point to any words by Defendant expressing an intent to relinquish its right to arbitration. Further, the Court finds that Defendant’s conduct is consistent with an intent to enforce the arbitration right. A brief recitation of the events leading up to the present motion is required. 

 

 On December 29, 2022, Plaintiff sent Defendant notice of her claim and a demand for mediation and arbitration. (Bogard Decl. ¶ 4.) The parties initially agreed to a mediation on June 6, 2023, but were forced to reschedule to August 23, 2023, due to inclement weather. (Id. ¶ 5.) Defendant requested a one-month extension, to which Plaintiff agreed. (Id. ¶ 6.) On September 15, 2023, Defendant canceled the mediation in full, leading Plaintiff to file the present lawsuit. (Id. ¶ 7.) Defendant later agreed to reschedule a mediation for February 13, 2024, but the parties failed to reach an agreement on that date. (Id. ¶¶ 10, 13.) Plaintiff served discovery on Defendant on February 13, which Defendant responded to on May 16. (Id. ¶ 14.) On July 11, 2024, Defendant filed a cross-complaint against a third-party. (Ibid.) Defendant agreed to a deposition of its person most qualified set for July 31, 2024, but the deposition was delayed after counsel for the third-party made a surprise appearance. (Id. ¶ 15.) Defendant has refused to resume the deposition. (Id. ¶ 16.) Plaintiff served further discovery on September 10, which Defendant objected to on October 14. (Id. ¶ 17.) Defendant filed the present motion to compel arbitration the day after Plaintiff named another third-party as a defendant. (Ibid.)  

 

Defendant’s delay in seeking arbitration does not by itself constitute conduct demonstrating abandonment because Defendant sought to exercise its right to mediation. The PSA called for arbitration if mediation failed. (Compl., Ex. A. § 14.) Thus, the months of delay surrounding mediation proceedings do not show that Defendant intended to relinquish its right to arbitrate because the PSA also contemplated mediation.  

 

Nor is Defendant’s conduct after mediation concluded inconsistent with an intent to arbitrate. The PSA explicitly contemplated arbitration after unsuccessful mediation proceedings, providing that disputes, “if not settled during mediation, shall thereafter be submitted to binding arbitration.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff consistently maintained that she desired mediation. (Codog Decl. ¶¶ 7, 9–12.) Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s propounded discovery did not demonstrate a waiver of its right to arbitrate, since mediation had not yet concluded and the right to arbitrate had not yet been realized. In fact, Defendant has reiterated that its participation in discovery is not meant as a waiver of its arbitration rights. (Id. ¶ 13, Ex. 9.) Thus, Plaintiff has not demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that Defendant’s actions are so inconsistent with the right to arbitrate that they constitute waiver. Plaintiff’s similar argument that Defendant waived its right to arbitration through unnecessary delay fails for the same reason. 

 

Alternatively, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has waived its right to arbitration by invoking the “litigation machinery.” In Oregel v. PacPizza, LLC (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 342, 355–56 (Oregel), a court of appeal affirmed the denial of an order to compel arbitration on the basis that the defendant had already engaged in discovery, participated in case management conferences, and failed to raise its arbitration agreement as an affirmative defense. The present case is distinguishable. Here, Defendant did assert the arbitration agreement as an affirmative defense. (Answer ¶ 22.) Additionally, Defendant did not actively participate in discovery to the extent of the defendant in Oregel. There, the defendant not only responded to the plaintiff’s discovery requests but also propounded its own discovery. Here, Defendant has only responded to Plaintiff’s discovery while reiterating its intent to resolve the present dispute through mediation. Finally, in the present case the PSA contemplates the conclusion of mediation proceedings before arbitration. In Oregel, the defendant had no similar reason to wait to assert its arbitration right. Thus, the Court does not find that Oregel applies to the present case. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is granted. 

 

Defendant additionally requests that the present proceedings be stayed pending the outcome of arbitration. If a court orders arbitration of a controversy, the court shall stay the action or proceeding until arbitration is complete on motion by a party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.) Here, the Court has ordered arbitration. Accordingly, the proceedings are stayed pending the outcome of that arbitration.  

 

Plaintiff’s additional argument concerning the arbitrability of its claims against Roundhouse will be considered pursuant to the pending motion to compel arbitration filed by that party.  

 

Conclusion  

The motion to compel arbitration filed by Defendant is granted and the proceedings are stayed as to Defendant The Hearth Project LLC pending completion of arbitration.

Case Name: 1200 MANAGEMENT LLC vs LIRON SHVARTZ, et al.

Case # 23STCV27752

 

Analysis

 

1200 Management LLC (Plaintiff) demurs to Liron Shvartz and Black Rock Market Café, Inc.’s (Defendants) thirty-two affirmative defenses contained in their answer. Plaintiff’s demurrer is overruled.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is sustained where “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., 430.10, subd. (e).) “A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Yalung v. State (2023) 98 Cal.App.5th 71, 80.) In reviewing a complaint’s legal sufficiency, a court will treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. (Esparza v. Kaweah Delta Dist. Hospital (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 547, 552.) It is well settled that a “demurrer lies only for defects appearing on the face of the complaint[.]” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 594, 601.) “We not only treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but also give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Guclimane Co. v. Stewart Tit. Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, the complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded. (Wilner v. Sunset Life Ins. Co. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 952, 958.)

 

When ruling on a demurrer, a court may only consider the complaint’s allegations or matters which may be judicially noticed. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The Court may not consider any other extrinsic evidence or judge the credibility of the allegations pleaded or the difficulty a plaintiff may have in proving his allegations. (Ion Equipment Corporation v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.) A demurrer is properly sustained when the complaint, liberally construed, fails to state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action. (Kramer v. Intuit Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 574, 578.) Demurrer is also appropriate where the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).)

 

A party may demur to an answer when “[t]he answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (a).) The answer must include facts supporting any defenses “as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and which are alleged in the complaint.” (FBI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384.) However, in pleading a defense under the statute of limitations, it is not necessary to state specific facts showing the defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 458.) If not raised in the answer at all, matters constituting an affirmative defense are irrelevant at trial. (Carranza v. Noroian (1966) 240 Cal.App.2d 481, 488.) However, a defense that is defectively pleaded may still be allowed if the pleading gives the plaintiff sufficient notice to prepare a defense. (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240.)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) The Court may grant leave to amend an answer so long as there is no prejudice to the plaintiff. (Hong Sang Market, Inc. v. Peng (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 474, 488.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff demurs to Defendants’ affirmative defenses contained in their answer on the ground that Defendants have failed to state facts sufficient to constitute those defenses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (a).) As a threshold matter, “[e]ach ground of demurrer must be in a separate paragraph and must state whether it applies to the entire complaint, cross-complaint, or answer, or to specified causes of action or defenses.” (Rules of Court, rule 3.1320(a).) A demurrer may be disregarded if it fails to distinctly specify any of the grounds upon which objections to the answer are taken. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.60.)

 

Here, in a single paragraph, Plaintiff demurs to every affirmative defense alleged by Defendants because they fail to state sufficient facts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (a).) Thus, Plaintiff demurs to each affirmative defense separately rather than the answer as a whole, yet fails to set out the specific grounds as to each defense in a separate paragraph. Plaintiff having violated the California Rules of Court, the Court may disregard its demurrer. Moreover, while the affirmative defenses may be in some cases standard boilerplate, the Court believes in general they do allege sufficient ultimate facts to withstand demurrer.  A better, more efficient way to proceed in this case is for Plaintiff to propound discovery asking for supporting evidentiary facts.

 

Nevertheless, the Court will address the one defense for which Plaintiff offers actual argument. .Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ twentieth affirmative defense under the statute of frauds is insufficiently pled. Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s claim for breach of contract is barred by the statute of frauds. (Answer ¶ 20.) The statute of frauds applies to contracts regarding the lease of real property for longer than one year. (Civ. Code, § 1624, subd. (a)(3).) The lease at issue in the present case is for a period longer than one year. (Compl., Ex. B.) Thus, this is a potentially applicable defense. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s demurrer is overruled.

 

C.      Conclusion

 

The Court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrers to the Answer.


Case Number: 23STCV29762    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 30

 

MOTION TO SET ASIDE/VACATE DISMISSAL 

 

 

Showroom Interiors LLC (Plaintiff) moves for an order to set aside the dismissal entered by the Court on August 1, 2024, for failure to appear at a case management conference or file the required statement. The motion is unopposed. 

 

  1. Legal Standard 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b): “the court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” 

 

Mandatory relief from default or dismissal is available when fault of counsel is shown through affidavit: “the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any . . . resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)  

 

  1. Discussion 

 

Plaintiff moves for mandatory relief from dismissal within the six month window required by statute based on the fault of Plaintiff’s counsel. Where counsel submits an affidavit attesting to their fault, the Court must grant relief unless it finds that the dismissal was not in fact caused by counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) Here, Plaintiff’s counsel offers a declaration alleging that due to his own in advertent and mistaken calendaring of the hearing date for the wrong day, he did not file a timely case management statement or attend the hearing. (Tapper Decl. ¶ 2.) Plaintiff also failed to attend the hearing due to counsel’s mistake. (Ibid.) Thus, Plaintiff’s motion to vacate dismissal is accompanied by an attorney’s affidavit attesting to his mistake, and vacatur of dismissal is mandatory. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is granted. 

Case Number: 23STCV30210    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 30

 Meet and Confer: 

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2), 2030.300, subd. (b), 2033.290 subd. (b).)¿“A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each¿issue presented by the motion.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)¿However, a discovery motion need not be denied automatically based upon the reason that the moving parties failed to¿meet and confer in good faith.¿(See¿Obregon v. Superior Court¿(1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434.)¿ 

 

On November 18, 2024, Plaintiff sent Defendant a meet and confer letter requesting the production of documents. (Kintzelf Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. C.) Defendant produced a response on November 25 but it included objections and was incomplete. (Id. ¶ 5, Ex. D.) Accordingly, the Court finds that the parties have sufficiently attempted met and conferred prior to filing this motion.  

 

Analysis: 

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Zara Barani to produce all the documents demanded in Plaintiff’s September 19, 2024, deposition notice. Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $2,200. The motion is stayed pending the scheduling of an informal discovery conference. 

 

  1. Legal Standard 

 

A party may move for an order compelling further response to a request for production of documents if the demanding party deems that responses are incomplete, evasive, or contain meritless objections. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subd. (a).)  A motion to compel further responses to an inspection demand “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.” (Id., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).)¿“Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial.¿ (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588.) Additionally, “[i]f a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (a).) 

 

“For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it ‘might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.’ [Citation].¿Admissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence. [Citation]¿These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery.” (Gonzales v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)  

 

  1. Discussion 

Local Court Rules: Department 30 

 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff did not participate in an informal discovery conference prior to moving to compel further responses. Local Court rules dictate “The court will not hear a discovery motion, other than a motion to compel where there has been no response, until the parties participate in an informal discovery conference with the court.”  

 

On September 19, 2024, Plaintiff took the deposition of Defendant, but did not request the documents noticed in the deposition. (Edwards Decl. ¶ 2.) On November 25, Defendant provided documents in response to a request for documents noticed in the deposition of Defendant to Plaintiff. (Id. ¶ 4.) Rather than responding to the document production, Plaintiff filed the present motion. (Id. ¶ 5.) Plaintiff did not request or participate in an informal discovery conference prior to filing the motion. Because the present motion seeks to compel further production of documents where Defendant has already offered a response, local rules dictate that Plaintiff first participate in an informal discovery conference. Accordingly, the present motion is stayed pending participation in an IDC.  

 

The Court therefore stays the instant motion pending participation in an IDC. 


Case Number: 24STCV07883    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 30

Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted 

 

 

On June 5, 2024, Plaintiffs served requests for admissions (RFA) on Philly Auto, Inc. (Defendant). (Green Decl. ¶ 2.) Responses were due on July 9. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250.) On July 11, Defendant requested an extension until July 31. (Green Decl. ¶ 4.) Responses were never served. (Stearns Decl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiffs filed the present motion on October 25, 2024. Defendant provided responses that day. (Id. ¶ 11, Ex. 2.) Plaintiffs are not required to meet and confer with counsel before bringing the present motion under section 2033.280, because Defendants failed to timely respond.  

 

Under section 2033.280, the Court shall grant an order deeming requests for admissions admitted unless the responding party serves substantially compliant responses before the hearing on the motion. A finding of substantial compliance depends on “whether answers in a proposed response are ‘as complete and straightforward’ as reasonably possible, as explicitly required by section 2033.220.” (Katayama v. Continental Investment Group (2024) 105 Cal.App.5th 898, 907.) A party not in possession of information or knowledge necessary to respond to a request for admission must state in the answer that “a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter in the particular request has been made, and that the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable that party to admit the matter.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220, subd. (c).) 

 

Here, Defendant responded to requests 1–5 by stating that “[r]esponding party is without sufficient information to admit or deny the substance of this request.” (Stearns Decl. Ex. 1.) Defendant did not attest to having made a reasonable inquiry as required by section 2033.220. Thus, Defendant’s responses as to these requests were not in substantial compliance with section 2033.220.  Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to deem the RFAs admitted is granted.  

 

Defendant argues that requests 1–5 are outside the scope of allowable discovery in the present case. Discoverable information includes anything “that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) Here, Plaintiffs’ requests relate to Defendant’s receipt of Plaintiffs’ notice as required for Plaintiffs to bring a claim under the Consumer Legal Remedies Act (CLRA) pursuant to Civil Code section 1782. This is relevant to the present litigation because Defendant asserts a lack of proper notice as a complete defense to Plaintiffs’ CLRA claims. (Answer ¶ 19.) Thus, requests 1–5 are within the scope of discovery as they relate to the subject matter of the pending action. 

 

The Court grants Plaintiffs’ motion to deem requests for admissions admitted.
