Case Number: 20STLC10399    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 25

State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company v. Briana Paysinger

20stlc10399

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

            On December 15, 2020, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Briana Paysinger (“Defendant”) for subrogation, stemming from an automobile collision between Defendant, on the one hand, and an individual insured by Plaintiff’s automobile insurance policy, on the other hand.  (Compl. ¶ 6)  Plaintiff compensated the insured for claimed damages in the amount of $13,086.47 and filed the instant claim against Defendant for allegedly causing the damages.  (Ibid. pp. 2-3.)  On February 16, 2021, Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint.

 

            On October 6, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Settlement.

 

On February 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for Dismissal with Reservation to Vacate and Enter Judgment Upon Breach (“Stipulation”), signed by Plaintiff’s authorized representative, Defendant, and Defendant’s attorney, to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $8,634.06.  (2-1-23 Stipulation.)  On February 3, 2023, the Court dismissed the entire case without prejudice pursuant to the Stipulation and Code of Civil Procedure § 664.6.  (2-3-23 Order.)

 

On June 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment (“Motion”).

 

On July 24, 2023, the Court noted that it could not ascertain the date by which Defendant had to make the one-time payment of $4,500, thus, the Court could not determine whether Defendant had defaulted on the agreement.  (7-24-23 Minute Order.)  The Court continued the hearing on the Motion and ordered Plaintiff to file supplemental papers clarifying the date by which Defendant had to make the payment.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Supplemental Brief in support of the Motion.  On August 23, 2023, the Court again continued the hearing on the Motion as it found that the Supplemental Brief did not sufficiently address the Court’s previous Order.  (8-23-23 Minute Order.)

 

No opposition has been filed.  To date, no supplemental papers have been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

CCP § 664.6, provides a summary procedure that enables judges to enforce a settlement agreement by entering a judgment pursuant to the terms of the parties’ settlement.  In particular, the statute provides:

 

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.  If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following:

 

(1) The party.

(2) An attorney who represents the party.

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf.

 

CCP § 664.6(a)-(b) (emphasis added).

 

III.            Discussion

 

A. Retention of Jurisdiction

 

“‘[V]oluntary dismissal of an action or special proceeding terminates the court’s jurisdiction over the matter.’  (Conservatorship of Martha P. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 857, 867) [12 Cal.Rptr.3d 142.) ‘If requested by the parties,’ however, ‘the [trial] court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce [a] settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.’ (§ 664.6, italics added.)”  (Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 917.)  “‘Because of its summary nature, strict compliance with the requirements of section 664.6 is prerequisite to invoking the power of the court to impose a settlement agreement.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Sully-Miller Contracting Co. v. Gledson/Cashman Construction, Inc. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 30, 37).)

 

“A request for the trial court to retain jurisdiction under section 664.6 ‘must conform to the same three requirements which the Legislature and the courts have deemed necessary for section 664.6 enforcement of the settlement itself: the request must be made (1) during the pendency of the case, not after the case has been dismissed in its entirety, (2) by the parties themselves, and (3) either in a writing signed by the parties or orally before the court.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 440).)  “The ‘request must be express, not implied from other language, and it must be clear and unambiguous.’” (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen, supra, 97 Cal.App.4th at 440).)

 

Here, the parties signed a Stipulation for Dismissal with Reservation to Vacate and Enter Judgment Upon Breach (“Stipulation”) containing the parties’ agreement for the Court to retain jurisdiction pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §664.6 to enforce the terms of the stipulation and enter judgment in the event of default.  (2-1-23 Stipulation ¶ 3.)  Prior to the dismissal of this action, the Stipulation was signed by the parties and submitted to the Court.  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  On February 3, 2023, the Court dismissed the case without prejudice expressly stating that “it shall retain jurisdiction pursuant to CCP § 664.6.”  (2-3-23 Order.)

 

On July 24, 2023, the Court found that the Stipulation complies with § 664.6 requirements and the Court has retained jurisdiction to enter judgment pursuant to the parties’ Stipulation in this action.  (7-24-23 Minute Order.)

 

 

 

 

B. Entry of Judgment

 

The Stipulation Agreement filed on February 1, 2023, provides that Plaintiff and Defendant agreed to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $8,634.06.  (2-1-23 Stipulation ¶ 2.)  Pursuant to the Stipulation, Defendant’s insurance carrier, Farmers Insurance, would make a payment of $4,134.06 by December 1, 2022.  (Ibid.)  Subsequently, “beginning MARCH 31, 2023, a one-time payment of $4,500.00 shall be made for the settlement amount to be paid in full.”  (Ibid.)

 

The Stipulation also provides that in the event Defendant fails to make timely payments, Plaintiff will mail a letter to Defendant regarding the default.  (Ibid. at ¶ 3.)  If Defendant does not remedy the default within fourteen (14) days of receiving notice, “then plaintiff will be entitled to have any dismissal in this action set aside and judgment entered for the settlement amount, minus credit for payments received, plus any costs associated with entering the judgment not to exceed $500.”  (Ibid.)  The declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel shall be sufficient proof for judgment to be entered.  (Ibid.)

 

On June 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion alleging that Defendant has defaulted on payments and Plaintiff is seeking to set aside the dismissal and enter judgment pursuant to the Stipulation.  (Memorandum pp. 1-2.)  According to Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant’s insurance carrier made a one-time payment of $4,134.06.  (Reese Decl. ¶ 4.)  However, Defendant failed to make any payments.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  Around May 16, 2023, Plaintiff mailed Defendant a default notice; however, Defendant did not make any payments after the notice was sent.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6-7, Ex. C.)

 

Plaintiff seeks to have the dismissal set aside and judgment entered in the amount of $5,036.31 as follows: $8,634.06 (settlement amount), minus $4,134.06 (payment made by Defendant’s insurance carrier), plus $536.31 (court costs).  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  The court costs consist of a $396.31 filing fee, $80.00 service fee, and $60.00 motion filing fee.  (Ibid.)

 

On July 24, 2023, the Court noted that it could not ascertain whether Defendant failed to make a timely payment.  (7-24-23 Minute Order.)  While the Stipulation states that the one-time payment of $4,500.00 must be made “beginning March 31, 2023,” it does not state how long Defendant has to make the payment.  (Ibid.)  For this reason, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion and ordered Plaintiff to file supplemental papers clarifying the date by which Defendant had to make the one-time payment toward the settlement amount.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Supplemental Brief in support of the Motion.  Plaintiff explains that pursuant to Paragraph 3 of the Stipulation, Defendant had fourteen (14) days from March 31, 2023, to make a payment.  (Supp. Brief.)  If Defendant did not make a payment within this grace period, then Plaintiff would proceed to vacate dismissal and enter judgment.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff has not received any payments from Defendant.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 23, 2023, the Court found that Plaintiff’s explanation does not correspond to the language in the Stipulation.  (8-23-23 Minute Order.)  The Stipulation states that “beginning MARCH 31, 2023, a one-time payment of $4,500.00 shall be made for the settlement amount to be paid in full.”  (2-1-23 Stip. ¶ 2.)  “If defendant fails to make a timely payment, said failure is a material breach.”  (Ibid. at ¶ 3.)  If the event of a breach, Plaintiff will mail a letter to Defendant informing Defendant that the payment has not been received and Plaintiff will have a grace period of fourteen (14) days after receiving the letter to cure the default.  (Ibid.)  Thus, it appears that the fourteen-day grace period starts after the letter is mailed.  The Court found that Plaintiff has still not clarified the date by which the payment had to be made to avoid default and once again continued the hearing for Plaintiff to provide further clarification.  (8-23-23 Minute Order.)

 

To date, no additional papers have been filed.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion is denied without prejudice.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Plaintiff State Farm’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

            On August 23, 2023, the Court noted that there were deficiencies in the Motion and continued the hearing to allow Counsel an opportunity to correct these deficiencies.  (8-23-23 Minute Order.)  On August 25, 2023, Counsel filed supplemental papers.

            In making a motion to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with procedures set forth in Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362.  The motion must be made using mandatory forms:

 

1.     Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel directed to the client – (MC-051);

2.     Declaration “stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship” the reasons that the motion was brought (MC-052);

3.     Proposed Order (MC-053).

 

(Ibid.)  The forms must be timely filed and served on all parties who have appeared in the case.  (Ibid.)  If these documents are served on the client by mail, there must be a declaration stating either that the address where client was served is “the current residence or business address of the client” or “the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.”  (Cal. All documents were served on Plaintiff by mail on August 25, 2023, at 4806 West Concord Ave Visalia, CA 93277.  (8-25-23 MC-051 p. 3; 8-25-23 MC-052 p. 3; 8-25-23 MC-053 p. 3.)  Counsel indicates that he has been unable to confirm Plaintiff’s address despite mailing the moving papers to client’s last known address, return receipt requested, calling client’s last known telephone number, and sending emails.  (8-25-23 MC-052 ¶ 3b(2).)

 

            The Court finds that Counsel has satisfied all of the requirements for the instant Motion.

 

            According, Counsel Abbott’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel as to Plaintiff Sunshine Logistics Enterprises is granted.

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Counsel Brett T. Abbott’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel as to Plaintiff Sunshine Logistics Enterprises is GRANTED, and the Order will be signed at the hearing.  “After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.”  (Cal. Case Number: 21STLC09127    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 25

Comforting Home Hospice Inc., et al. v. David C. Perkins

21stlc09127

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On December 30, 2021, Plaintiffs Comforting Home Hospice Inc. (“Hospice”) and Alexander Everest (“Everest”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendant David C. Perkins (“Perkins”) for violation of Business & Professions Code § 7031, breach of contract, fraudulent concealment, and negligent misrepresentation.

 

Defendant, in propria person, filed an Answer on April 7, 2022.  Defendant also filed a Cross-Complaint against Hospice and Everest for (1) common count – work labor and services and (2) breach of oral contract.  Hospice and Everest filed a joint Answer to the Cross-Complaint on May 13, 2022.

 

On February 9, 2023, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants filed a Motion for Summary Adjudication of Plaintiff’s first cause of action for violation of Business & Professions Code § 7031.  The Court granted the Motion on May 4, 2023.  (5-4-23 Minute Order.)

 

            On August 3, 2023, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants filed the instant Motion for Order Dismissing the Pending Cross-Complaint Pursuant to Court’s Findings on Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment, dated May 4, 2023.  (“Motion”).  On August 28, 2023, Defendant filed Notice of Non-Opposition to Motion.  No opposition has been filed.

 

On August 16, 2023, Plaintiff Hospice filed a Notice of Withdrawal from the action.

 

On August 18, 2023, the Court granted Everest’s Ex-Parte Application with respect to the request to continue trial, but denied the Application with respect to the request to shorten time for hearing on the 09/25/2023 Motion to Dismiss Cross-Complaint.  (8-18-23 Minute Order.)

 

            On September 18, 2023, Everest filed a Notice of Non-Opposition to the instant Motion.  To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Everest moves to dismiss the Cross-Complaint filed by Defendant/Cross-Complainant Perkins on the ground that there are no triable issues of fact to be determined at trial as the Court determined all issues in its May 4, 2023, ruling on Everest’s Motion for Summary Adjudication.  (Mot. pp. 1-2.)  The Court ruled that the claims made in the Cross-Complaint were for the work performed by Perkins as an unlicensed contractor in violation of Business & Professions Code § 7031.  (Ibid.)  Accordingly, no such claims can be made under § 7031.  (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiffs submitted a Proposed Order for the Court to sign.  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  The Court signed the Order but removed the following statements:

 

1)     Pursuant to B&P Section 7031(a), Defendant Perkins may not bring or maintain any action or collect any compensation for work where a license is required.

2)     Defendant's Cross-complaint for $10,657.18 is denied and hereby dismissed with prejudice.

 

(Ibid.)  Everest argues that the removal of these statements “contradicts the Court’s findings with regard to B&P section 7031 which found that Defendant Perkins is an unlicensed contractor and operating in violation of B&P § 7031.”  (Ibid.)  Given that the claims in the Cross-Complaint are for the same services as those found to violate § 7031, the Cross-Complaint must be dismissed.  (Ibid. at pp. 4-5.)

 

Business and Professions Code § 7031(a) states:

Except as provided in subdivision (e), no person engaged in the business or acting in the capacity of a contractor, may bring or maintain any action, or recover in law or equity in any action, in any court of this state for the collection of compensation for the performance of any act or contract where a license is required by this chapter without alleging that they were a duly licensed contractor at all times during the performance of that act or contract regardless of the merits of the cause of action brought by the person, except that this prohibition shall not apply to contractors who are each individually licensed under this chapter but who fail to comply with Section 7029.

            Although § 7031(a) does not permit an unlicensed contractor to bring or maintain an action in law or equity, nothing in this section gives the Court authority to dismiss an action brought in violation of § 7031, pursuant to a motion to dismiss.  Moreover, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant has not presented any other legal basis that confers authority on the Court to dismiss such an action.

            For this reason, the Court denies Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s Motion for Order Dismissing the Pending Cross-Complaint.

III.            Conclusion

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

The Motion for Order Dismissing the Pending Cross-Complaint, filed by Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Alexander Everest, is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

Case Number: 22STLC00803    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 25

Home Depot U.S.A., Inc. v. Laura Elizabeth Christine

22stlc00803

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On February 7, 2022, Plaintiff Home Depot U.S.A., Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “Home Depot”) filed an action against Defendant Laura Elizabeth Christine (“Defendant” or “Christine”), for 1) breach of contract, 2) open book account, 3) restitution, 4) account stated, and 5) quantum meruit.

 

On October 11, 2022, pursuant to Plaintiff’s request, the Court entered default against Defendant.  (10-11-22 Request for Default.)  On November 16, 2022, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Request for Default Judgment and Judgment was entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $18,495.84.  (11-16-22 Default Judgment.)

 

            On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Amend Judgment by Default to Correct Clerical Error (“Motion”).

 

            No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 473(d) provides that, “the court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed.”  A court has the inherent power to correct clerical errors in its records so as to make these records reflect the true facts.  (In re Candelario (1970) 3 Cal. 3d 702, 705.)  “The test which distinguishes clerical error from possible judicial error is simply whether the challenged portion of the judgment was entered inadvertently (which is clerical error) versus advertently (which might be judicial error, but is not clerical error).  Unless the challenged portion of the judgment was entered inadvertently, it cannot be changed post judgment under the guise of correction of clerical error.”  Tokio Marine & Fire Ins. Corp. v. Western Pacific Roofing Corp. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 110, 117 (internal citation omitted).  “An amendment that substantially modifies the original judgment or materially alters the rights of the parties, may not be made by the court under its authority to correct clerical error, therefore, unless the record clearly demonstrates that the error was not the result of the exercise of judicial discretion.”  (In re Candelario, 3 Cal. 3d. at 705.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks to amend a portion of the judgment which omitted the prejudgment interest amount.  (Mot. p. 2.)

 

In her declaration in support of the Motion, Plaintiff’s counsel states that, after default was entered against Defendant on October 11, 2022, Plaintiff submitted a Request for Entry of Default Judgment with supporting evidence.  (Ferrentino Decl. ¶ 3, Exs. A-C.)  Plaintiff’s Request for Entry of Default Judgment included “‘10% pre-judgment interest starting 9/4/2020.’”  (Ibid. at ¶ 4, Ex. B.)  Given that Plaintiff could not predict when judgment would be entered, a certain sum was not included in the proposed judgment; however, Plaintiff did provide the pre-judgment interest percentage and start date to enable the Court to calculate the final amount.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. B.)  On November 16, 2022, Plaintiff received the Judgment, which included $17,405.87 in damages, pre-judgment interest at the rate of 10% per annum, and $1,089.97 in costs.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. D.)  However, there was no amount of interest specified from September 4, 2020, to November 16, 2022, the date of entry of Judgment.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)  Counsel states that this may be a mere “clerical oversight/error” and requests that the Court correct the error by entering a sum of $3,829.27 in interest.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  Plaintiff has submitted a Proposed Amended Judgment which includes $17,405.87 in damages, $3,829.27 in prejudgment interest, and $1,089.97 in costs, for a total judgment of $22,325.11.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10, Ex. E.)

The Court finds that the omission of the pre-judgment interest amount is a clerical error and does not reflect the total sum of judgment.  Moreover, Plaintiff has accurately calculated prejudgment interest between the dates noted in the Court’s judgment.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Motion to Amend Judgment by Default to Correct Clerical Error.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Plaintiff Home Depot U.S.A., Inc.’s Motion to Amend Judgment by Default to Correct Clerical Error is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Kaylee Dickerson v. Dollar Tree Stores, Inc.

22stlc05275

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

            On August 9, 2022, minor Plaintiff Kaylee Dickerson, through her parent Ahamad Dickerson (“Ahamad”), filed an action against Dollar Tree Stores, Inc. (“Dollar Tree” or “Defendant”) for premises liability.  Ahamad was appointed guardian ad litem for minor Plaintiff Kaylee on August 10, 2022.  (8-10-22 Application and Order.)

 

            On September 27, 2022, Petitioner Ahamad filed an Expedited Petition to Approve Compromise of a Disputed Claim on behalf of minor Claimant Kaylee Dickerson.

            On October 25, 2022, Petitioner filed another Expedited Petition on behalf of minor Claimant Kaylee Dickerson.  On December 2, 2022, the Court noted several deficiencies in the Expedited Petition and set a hearing for January 5, 2023.  (12-2-22 Minute Order.)  On January 5, 2023, the Court found that Petitioner had not filed any supplemental papers correcting the deficiencies noted in the Court’s previous Order and denied the Expedited Petition.  (1-5-23 Minute Order.)

 

On May 10, 2023, Petitioner filed the instant Expedited Petition to Approve Minor’s Compromise on behalf of minor Claimant Kaylee Dickerson (“Petition”).  On June 2, 2023, the Court noted that the Petition contained several deficiencies and set a hearing for July 18, 2023, to allow Petitioner an opportunity to correct these deficiencies.  (6-2-23 Minute Order.)

 

On July 10, 2023, Petitioner filed a Supplemental Declaration and an amended MC-351, Proposed Order.

 

On July 18, 2023, the Court did not adopt its tentative ruling and continued the hearing on the Expedited Petition.  (7-18-23 Minute Order.)  The Court ordered Petitioner to file a supplemental declaration addressing the issues noted in the Court’s Order.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 22, 2023, Petitioner informed the Court that he had attempted to file a supplemental declaration but it was rejected.  (8-22-23 Minute Order.)  Thus, the Court continued the hearing once again to allow Petitioner another opportunity to file the supplemental papers.  (Ibid.)

 

Petitioner filed supplemental papers on August 21, 2023.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

            Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim.  (Prob. Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; Code of Civ. Proc. § 372.)  “‘[W]ithout trial court approval of the proposed compromise of the ward’s claim, the settlement cannot be valid.  [Citation.] [¶] Nor is the settlement binding [on the minor] until it is endorsed by the trial court.’”  (Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.)  A minor, like Claimant, “shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 372(a)(1).) Alternatively, the petitioner may file a declaration demonstrating that he or she has a right to compromise the minor’s claim under Cal. Probate Code § 3500.

 

            Regarding the substance of the Petition, to obtain court approval of the settlement of a minor’s claims, the petitioner must file a complete and “verified petition for approval of the settlement and must disclose ‘all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise.’ [Citations.]”  (Barnes v. Western Heritage Ins. Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 249, 256; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.)  (Italics added.)

 

Under Probate Code § 3505, if a petition is unopposed, the Court must issue a decision on the petition at the conclusion of the hearing.

 

III.            Discussion

 

Under California Rules of Court, rule 7.950.5, an expedited petition for minor’s compromise without a scheduled hearing is permitted as long as Petitioner uses the required Judicial Council forms and meets certain conditions.  The conditions are:

 

(1) Petitioner is represented by an attorney authorized to practice in the courts of this state;

(2) The claim is not for wrongful death;

(3) Settlement proceeds will not be placed in a trust;

(4) There are no unresolved liens to be satisfied from the proceeds of the compromise;

(5) Petitioner's attorney did not become involved at the request of Defendant or insurance carrier connected with the Petition;

(6) Petitioner's attorney is not employed by nor associated with a Defendant or insurance carrier in connection with the Petition;

(7) If an action has been filed on the claim, (A) all Defendants have appeared and are participating in the compromise OR (B) the Court has determined the settlement to be in good faith.

(8) The settlement, exclusive of interests and costs, is $50,000 or less OR (A) the amount payable is the insurance policy limits AND all proposed contributing parties would be substantially unable to use assets other than the insurance policy limits.

(9) The court does not otherwise order.

 

Having reviewed the Expedited Petition filed on May 10, 2023, the Court set a hearing on the Petition to allow Petitioner an opportunity to correct the deficiencies noted in the Court’s Order.  (6-2-23 Minute Order.)  On July 10, 2023, six (6) court dates before the scheduled hearing, Petitioner filed supplemental papers.  Although, Petitioner did not file the supplemental papers in compliance with the June 2, 2023, Order, the Court, in its discretion and for the purpose of judicial efficiency, accepted the late-filed papers.  (7-18-23 Minute Order; Cal. Rules of Court, 3.1300(d).)  The Court found that Petitioner satisfactorily addressed the deficiencies previously noted.  (7-18-23 Minute Order.)

 

However, the Court continued the hearing and ordered Petitioner to address the following:

1. How will the settlement funds be distributed?

2. If the monies will be deposited in a blocked account, then Counsel should provide the name and address of the bank where the settlement funds will be deposited at, and submit a proposed order to deposit (MC-355) and (proposed) Order Granting Expedited Petition (MC-351).

 

(Ibid.)

 

            On August 22, 2023, the Court once again continued the hearing on the Petition, as Petitioner informed the Court that his supplemental papers had been rejected.  (8-22-23 Minute Order.)

 

            Having reviewed the supplemental papers filed on August 21, 2023, the Court makes the following findings.

 

            Petitioner’s counsel explains that the settlement amount will be distributed as follows: $3,000 to the minor Claimant through his father and guardian ad litem, $3,000 to Petitioner’s counsel, and $3,000 to the medical provider Chiropractic Elevated.  (8-21-23 Udeochu Decl. ¶ 1.)  These funds will be deposited in Petitioner’s personal bank account at a US Bank branch, with the address and account number provided in counsel’s declaration.  (Ibid. at ¶ 2.)  Petitioner has also filed a Proposed Order, MC-351, and Order to Deposit Funds in Blocked Account, MC-355.

 

            The Court finds that all of the requirements of the instant Petition have been satisfied.

 

            Accordingly, the Expedited Petition for Approval of Minor’s Compromise, filed on behalf of minor Claimant Kaylee Dickerson, is GRANTED.

Sandra Cohen v. Xsiavosh Asrabadi, et al.

23stcp01751

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On May 22, 2023, Petitioner Sandra Cohen (“Petitioner” or “Cohen”) filed the instant Petition to Compel Arbitration (“Petition”) seeking an order compelling Respondents Xsiavosh Asrabadi (“Asrabadi”) and Bahreh Qmarsi (“Qmarsi”) (collectively “Respondents”) to arbitrate a controversy pursuant to a California Residential Purchase Agreement (“Agreement”) entered into by Petitioner and Respondents.

 

On August 16, 2023, the Court rejected Petitioner’s Requests for Entry of Default/Judgment against Respondents.  (8-16-23 Notices of Rejection.)

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §1281.2, generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting¿rulings on common issues.

 

When seeking to compel arbitration, the initial burden lies with the moving party to demonstrate the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by prepondernace of evidence.  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841-42; Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021), 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 164-65.)  It is sufficient for the moving party to produce a copy of the arbitration agreement or set forth the agreement’s provisions.  (Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  The burden then shifts to the opposing party to prove by a preponderance of evidence any defense to enforcement of the contract or the arbitration clause.  (Ruiz, 232 Cal.App.4th at 842; Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  Subsequently, the moving party must establish with the preponderance of admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties.  (Ibid.)  The trial court then weighs all the evidence submitted and uses its discretion to make a final determination.  (Ibid.)  “California law, ‘like [federal law], reflects a strong policy favoring arbitration agreements and requires close judicial scrutiny of waiver claims.’”  (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 31.)

 

If the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until arbitration is completed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Around August 8, 2020, Petitioner and Respondents entered into a California Residential Purchase Agreement (“Agreement”).  (Pet. ¶ 4, Ex. A.)  Pursuant to the Agreement, paragraph 22(B), “the parties agreed that any dispute or claim in law or equity arising between them out of the Agreement or any resulting transaction, which is not first settled through mediation, shall be decided by neutral, binding arbitration.”  (Ibid.)

 

In September 2020, a dispute arose between Petitioner, as seller of the Property, and Respondents, as potential buyers of the Property, because Respondents attempted to cancel the Agreement and demand the release of the buyer’s deposit.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  Around September 30, 2020 and January 3, 2023, Petitioner sent a letter demanding mediation pursuant to paragraph 21 of the Agreement.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  Respondents did not respond to the letter.  (Ibid.)  Around March 31, 2023, Petitioner again demanded mediation and informed Respondents that she would move to compel arbitration of the dispute if they did not respond by April 7, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 9.)  Respondents did not respond to Petitioner’s communications.  (Ibid.)

 

Petitioner requests that the Court order Respondents to first mediate the dispute, and if that does not resolve the controversy, to compel arbitration of the dispute.  (Ibid. at p. 3.)

 

The Court finds that Petitioner has presented the Agreement, entered into by Petitioner and Respondents, which contains a provision for arbitration of disputes arising out of the Agreement.  (Pet. ¶ 4, Ex. A.)  The provision states that “[t]he Parties agree to mediate any dispute or claim arising between them out of this Agreement, or any resulting transaction, before resorting to arbitration or court action.”  (Ibid. at ¶ 22A.)  Petitioner has shown that it has attempted to mediate the dispute; however, Respondents have not been responsive to Petitioner’s demands.  Thus, the Court finds that Petitioner is entitled to a Court order compelling arbitration of the dispute.

 

However, the Court finds the Petition unclear regarding the form of relief requested.  Petitioner has filed a Petition to Compel Arbitration based on Code of Civil Procedure §1281.2; however, at the end of the Petition, “Petitioner prays 1. That the court order Respondents to first mediate the controversy in question, and if mediation does not resolve the matter, then a binding arbitration hearing shall be conducted, all in accordance with the terms of the Agreement.”  (Pet. p. 3.)

 

Given the lack of clarity, the Court continues the hearing on the Petition.  Petitioner is ordered to file supplemental papers clarifying whether she is moving for an order compelling arbitration or whether she wishes to proceed with mediation.  Petitioner is also ordered to file the letters sent to Respondents demanding mediation of the controversy.

 

IV.            Conclusion

 

For the reasons discussed above,

 

The hearing on the Petition to Compel Arbitration, filed by Petitioner Sandra Cohen, is CONTINUED to ______________at ____ a.m. in Department 25 at the SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.  Petitioner is ordered to file supplemental papers addressing the issues discussed herein at least 16 court days before the next scheduled hearing.  Failure to do so may result in the Petition being placed off calendar or denied.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice. 

Case Number: 23STLC01331    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 25

Felix Rodriguez v. YNG LLC, et al.

23stlc01331

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On February 23, 2023, Plaintiff Felix Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants YNG, LLC (“YNG”), Vinisha Foods Corporation (“Vinisha Foods”), and Binoy Kothari (“Kothari”) for an alleged violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act.

 

On March 28, 2023, Defendants YNG, Vinisha Foods, and Kothari filed a joint Answer to the Complaint.  On the same day, Defendant Kothari filed an Application for Stay.  On April 14, 2023, the proceedings were stayed for 90 days and an Early Evaluation Conference was scheduled for May 24, 2023.  (4-14-23 Notice of Stay.)  The Court lifted the stay on May 24, 2023.  (5-24-23 Minute Order.)

 

On August 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel YNG LLC’s Further Answers to Interrogatories.  On September 19, 2023, the Court placed the Motion off calendar and denied Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.  (9-19-23 Minute Order.)

 

On August 2, 2023, Plaintiff also filed the instant Motion to Compel Vinisha Foods Corporation’s Further Answers to Interrogatories (“Motion”).  Defendant Vinisha Foods filed an Opposition on September 12, 2023.  No reply has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.300 provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that” the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete; (2)¿an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §¿2030.300(a).)  Notice of the motions must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response.  (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2030.300(c).)  The motion must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration “showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2016.040, 2030.300(b).)  California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(2)).

 

III.            Discussion

 

A.     Motion to Compel Further Responses

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Vinisha Foods “to serve further full and complete answers to the interrogatories numbered one through five in Plaintiff’s first set of Special Interrogatories.”  (Mot. p. 1.)  Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1000.  (Mot. p. 1.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel asserts that he served Defendant Vinisha Foods with Special Interrogatories (Set One) on March 31, 2023.  (Mehrban Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Defendant served responses to the Interrogatories.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4, Ex. B.)  Plaintiff’s counsel sent a follow-up email regarding the responses.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. C.)  No further answers have been submitted.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)

 

Defendant Vinisha Foods opposes the Motion.  Defense counsel explains that he received the Special Interrogatories via email on July 4, 2023 and did not receive any copies of the Special Interrogatories prior to that date.  (Chowdhary Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 2.)  Plaintiff’s counsel requested responses by July 14, 2023; defense counsel sent the responses on July 14, 2023, via email.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 2-3, Ex. 2.)  On July 17, 2023, defense counsel received a meet and confer email from Plaintiff’s counsel regarding Defendant’s responses and a threat of sanctions if Defendant did not provide supplemental responses by July 31, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4, Ex. 2.)  On July 28, 2023, defense counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that he had not received the original discovery requests sent on March 31, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. 2.)  He also informed Plaintiff’s counsel that the meet and confer letter did not address Defendant’s specific objections and he requested to continue the meet and confer process via a telephone discussion, offering specific dates of availability.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6-8.)  Plaintiff’s counsel replied to the email stating that he had already presented Plaintiff’s position through his meet and confer letter.  (Ibid. at ¶ 9.)  On August 24, 2023, defense counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that he had not received the notice of the instant Motion and requested service of all documents via electronic transmission.  (Ibid. at ¶ 10, Ex. 3.)  On August 28, 2023, Defendant served further responses to the discovery requests.  (Ibid. at ¶ 11, Ex. 1.)  Defense counsel conveyed his availability to discuss the supplemental responses and on August 30, 2023, forwarded verification of the responses.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 12-13, Exs. 3-4.)  He requested that Plaintiff take the Motion off calendar; Plaintiff’s counsel acknowledged receipt of the supplemental responses but refused to take the Motion off calendar.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 13-14, Ex. 4.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant has demonstrated that supplemental responses were served on Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel has acknowledged receipt of these supplemental responses.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Answers to Interrogatories from Vinisha Foods is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.

 

B.     Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d)).

 

Furthermore, § 2030.300(d) authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions if a party “unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

Plaintiff requests $1000 in sanctions for Defendant’s failure to serve further responses to the instant discovery request.  (Mehrban Decl. ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff’s counsel states that his hourly rate is $500.00 per hour, and he has “spent and anticipate[s] spending a cumulative total of no less than two hours” in preparing the Motion and Separate Statement, reviewing Defendant’s Opposition, and appearing at the hearing.  (Ibid.)

 

Here, the Motion is placed off calendar as moot.  The Court also finds that the imposition of sanctions would be unjust due to Defendant’s attempts to meet and confer and provide compliant responses to the discovery request and Plaintiff’s refusal to engage in a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution prior to the filing of the instant Motion.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is denied.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

The Motion to Compel Defendant Vinisha Foods Corporation’s Further Answers to Interrogatories, filed by Plaintiff Felix Rodriguez, is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.  Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23STLC03090    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 25

Jason Alan v. AllianceOne Receivables Management, Inc.

23stlc03090

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background & Discussion

 

On May 9, 2023, Plaintiff Jason Alan (“Plaintiff”) filed an action for violations of the Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, Civil Code § 1788, et seq., and the Unfair Competition Law, Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq., against Defendant AllianceOne Receivables Management, Inc. (“Defendant”).

 

Defendant filed the instant Demurrer ( “Demurrer”) on June 29, 2023.  Plaintiff filed an Opposition on July 26, 2023, and Defendant filed a Reply on August 28, 2023.

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff seeks injunctive relief.  (Compl. p. 8 – Prayer for Relief.)  However, limited jurisdiction courts, such as this one, do not have the authority to issue permanent injunctive relief.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 86(a)(8).)  Thus, this Court has no jurisdiction over this action.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is PLACED OFF CALENDAR.  The Court, on its own motion, also RECLASSIFIES this action to unlimited jurisdiction court, as permitted by Code of Civil Procedure § 403.040(a).

 

II.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Defendant AllianceOne Receivables Management, Inc.’s Demurrer is PLACED OFF CALENDAR.

 

The action is RECLASSIFIED as an unlimited jurisdiction case and is transferred to the transfer/reclassification desk for reassignment.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
