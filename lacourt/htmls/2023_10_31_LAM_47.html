DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV15786    Hearing Date: October 31, 2023    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 31, 2023                   TRIAL DATE: January 23, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         James Weldon Schott v. Miguel A. Melgoza

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV15786           

 

(1)   MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(2)   MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(3)   MOTION FOR ORDER DEEMING THE TRUTH OF MATTERS IN REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1)-(3) Plaintiff James Weldon Schott

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 10/26/23

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/24/20: Complaint filed.

·         09/27/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for partition of a parcel of real property and for fraud, conversion, and breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant induced him to loan money for the purchase of the property to operate it as a dispensary.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel responses to form interrogatories and requests for production and for an order deeming the truth of matters in requests for admissions. Plaintiff also requests sanctions.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Deeming the Truth of Matters in Requests for Admission Propounded to Defendant is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $2,180 against Defendant only. Payment is to be made within 30 days of this order.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories & Requests for Production

 

Plaintiff moves to compel responses to form interrogatories and requests for production.

 

Legal Standard

 

When a party to whom an inspection demand or an interrogatory is directed fails to respond, a party making the request may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(b) [interrogatories]; 2031.300(b) [requests for production].) A party who fails to provide timely responses waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(a); 2031.300(a).) For a motion to compel initial responses, no meet and confer is required. All that must be shown is that discovery requests were properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response has been served. (Leach v. Sup. Ct. (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-06.)

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff served form interrogatories and requests for production via email on Defendants’ counsel on April 10, 2023. (Declaration of Matthew T. Ward ISO Mot. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s counsel granted multiple extensions, with the final deadline set for June 28, 2023. (Id. ¶¶ 3-5, Exhs. B-D.) To date, no responses have been received. (¶ 10.) Plaintiffs are therefore entitled to an order compelling responses to the discovery requests.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request sanctions against Defendants and their counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $1,560 for each motion, for a total of $3,120.

 

            Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subd. (a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

            Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories or requests for production against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(c); 2030.300(d); 2031.300 (c); 2031.310(h).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Id.)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subdivision (a) states: “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Here, Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendant and his counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $1,560 on each of the two motions to compel, for a total of $3,120. This request is based on 2.5 hours of attorney time incurred on each motion at a rate of $500 per hour, plus an additional half hour anticipated, plus $60 in filing fees for each motion. (See Ward Decl. ¶¶ 11-13.) In light of the substantial similarity between the two motions, the Court finds that awarding the full amount of attorney’s fees would be inappropriately duplicative. Moreover, the Court observes that Defendant’s counsel has filed a motion to be relieved as counsel, with an included declaration which states, under penalty of perjury, that Defendant has stopped communicating with him entirely. (See Declaration ISO Mot. to be Relieved as Counsel filed October 25, 2023.) It thus appears that the culpability for Defendant’s failure to respond rests more properly with Defendant himself than with Defendant’s counsel. The Court will therefore instead award a total of $1,620 in attorney’s fees on these motions against Defendant only, representing a total of 3 hours of attorney time at an hourly rate of $500, plus $120 in filing fees for the two motions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,620 against Defendant only.

 

Motion for Order Deeming Truth of Matters in Requests for Admissions

 

            Plaintiff also moves for an order deeming the truth of matters in requests for admissions propounded to Defendant

 

Legal Standard

 

When a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to respond, the party propounding the requests may move for an order that the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) “The court shall make this order [deem the requests admitted], unless it finds that the party to whom the request for admissions have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with section 2033.220.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff served requests for admissions on Defendant via email on April 10, 2023. (Declaration of Matthew T. Ward ISO Mot. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s counsel granted multiple extensions, with the final deadline set for June 28, 2023. (Id. ¶¶ 3-5, Exhs. B-D.) To date, no responses have been received. (¶ 10.) Plaintiffs are therefore entitled to an order compelling responses to the discovery requests.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against Defendant and his counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $1,560.

 

Failure to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process for which sanctions may be imposed. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2023.010(d); 2023.030(a).) Sanctions are mandatory on the party or attorney or both whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated a motion to deem requests for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)

 

            Here, Plaintiff request sanctions against Plaintiff only in the amount of $1,560, representing 2.5 hours of attorney time at a rate of $500 per hour plus an additional anticipated half hour, plus $60 in filing fees. (See Ward Decl. ¶¶ 11-13.) For the reasons stated above in connection with the motions to compel, the Court declines to award the full amount of sanctions or to impose sanctions against Defendant’s counsel. The Court will therefore award sanctions in the amount of $560 against Defendant only in connection with this motion.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Deeming the Truth of Matters in Requests for Admission Propounded to Defendant is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $560 against Defendant only.

 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Deeming the Truth of Matters in Requests for Admission Propounded to Defendant is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $2,180 against Defendant only. Payment is to be made within 30 days of this order.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 31, 2023                               JUDGMENT: July 6, 2023

                                                          

CASE:                         Eduardo Yanez Guzman v. RHC Automotive, Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV33808           

 

MOTION TO TAX COSTS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant FCA US LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Eduardo Yanez Guzman

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         09/03/20: Complaint filed.

·         05/10/23: Jury verdict rendered.

·         05/17/23: Dismissal of RHC Automotive Inc., d/b/a Nissani Bros. Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram

·         07/06/23: Judgment entered.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on September 3, 2020. Plaintiff purchased a new 2016 Dodge Ram 2500 on January 23, 2016, which was delivered with serious defects in the transmission and electrical systems as well as the brakes and the engine. A jury verdict was rendered on May 10, 2023, in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant FCA US LLC. Judgment was entered on the verdict on July 6, 2023.

 

Plaintiff moves for attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $278,941.82 as the prevailing party.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED in the amount of $196,713.12 in attorney’s fees.

 

            This ruling is without prejudice to Plaintiff’s right to seek costs via a procedurally proper Memorandum of Costs.

 

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves for attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $278,941.82 as the prevailing party.

 

Defendant moves to tax costs claimed in the memorandum of costs.

 

Defendant’s Evidentiary Objections

 

            Defendant raises numerous evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Roger Kirnos offered in support of the motion. The Court rules on these objections as follows:

 

            Objection No. 1: OVERRULED. As supervisor and managing partner, the training, skills, experience, and hourly rates of subordinate attorneys are within the declarant’s personal knowledge, and these statements do not lack foundation. The statements are not hearsay and are relevant on their face.

 

As the remainder of the evidence to which Defendant objects is not material to the Court’s ruling, the Court declines to rule on the balance of Defendant’s objections.

           

Entitlement to Fees

 

            Plaintiff seeks an award of attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses pursuant to a judgment entered in Plaintiff’s favor. Plaintiff brought claims for violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civ. Code § 1790 et seq.) and for fraudulent omission.

 

Civil Code section 1794(d) states:

 

If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.

 

            A jury trial in this action commenced on May 1, 2023, concluding with a verdict in Plaintiff’s favor on both causes of action asserted in the operative Complaint. (See Plaintiff’s Exh. D.) Judgment was entered on July 6, 2023, designating Plaintiff the prevailing party in this action and awarding Plaintiff $190,198.63 in damages and prejudgment interest in the amount of $25,459.72. (Id.) Defendant does not dispute that Plaintiff is the prevailing party in this action and is entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses.

 

//

 

Reasonableness of Fees

 

            Plaintiff requests a total fee award of $230,858.25, based on a total of $153,905.50 in fees actually incurred by Plaintiff’s counsel to date, plus an additional $76,952.75 resulting from a 1.5x multiplier, plus $3,465.00 in anticipated fees to prepare a reply brief and attend the hearing on this motion. (Declaration of Roger Kirnos ISO Mot. ¶ 2, Exh. A.)

 

            Reasonable attorney’s fees are allowable costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc § 1033.5(a)(10), (c)(5)(B).) In actions that are based on a contract, “where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract… shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.” (Civil Code § 1717(a) [emphasis added].) A recovery of attorney’s fees is authorized even in noncontractual or tort actions if the contractual provision for fee recovery is worded broadly enough. (See Code Civ. Proc § 10211; Maynard v. BTI Group, Inc. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 984, 993 [agreement to award fees based on outcome of “any dispute” encompasses all claims, “whether in contract, tort or otherwise]; Lockton v. O'Rourke (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1051, 1076; Lerner v. Ward (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 155, 160.)

Reasonable attorney’s fees shall be fixed by the Court and shall be an element of the costs of suit. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(c)(5)(B).) Reasonable attorney’s fees are ordinarily determined by the Court pursuant to the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096; Margolin v. Regional Planning Com. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004 [“California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys' fee award.”].) “[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award….”  (Ibid.) In setting the hourly rate for a fee award, courts are entitled to consider the “fees customarily charged by that attorney and others in the community for similar work.” (Bihun v. AT&T Info. Sys., Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 976, 997 [affirming rate of $450 per hour], overruled on other grounds by Lakin v. Watkins Associated Indus. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 644, 664.)  The burden is on the party seeking attorney’s fees to prove the reasonableness of the fees. (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 615.) 

The Court has broad discretion in determining the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award, which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.” (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1393-1394.)  The Court need not explain its calculation of the amount of attorney’s fees awarded in detail; identifying the factors considered in arriving at the amount will suffice. (Ventura v. ABM Indus. Inc. (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 274-275.)

1.      Base Fee Requests

Plaintiff has provided an itemized list of the attorney time billed in connection with this case. (Kirnos Decl. Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s counsel billed a total of 335.6 hours of attorney time at hourly rates ranging from $200 to $595 per hour. (Id.) Attorney Kirnos attests to the skills, training, experience, and hourly rates of the various attorneys that performed work on this case as their supervisor and Managing Partner of Knight Law Group, LLP, as well as his own skills and experience, and to the veracity of the time entries. (Kirnos Decl. ¶¶ 30-45.) The Court also observes that, notwithstanding the length of time spent on this case, that Plaintiff’s counsel has exercised billing judgment, assigning research and drafting tasks to associates and junior partners, and reserving more strategic and editing pursuits to senior partners charging higher rates. (See generally Exh. A.)

Although Defendant identifies numerous entries that it contends are ““duplicative,” or “excessive time,” “unreasonably inflated,” or are mere “paralegal work,” Defendant offers no evidence supporting these contentions beyond a conclusory assertion that they are so. Defendant’s contentions that many of Plaintiff’s documents and motions are “boilerplate” is similarly unsupported, as Plaintiff’s admission that his counsel uses templates to perform more efficient work does not render the filings for which they were used mere “boilerplate.”

            Based on the foregoing, the Court concludes that Plaintiff’s fees of $157,370.50 were reasonably incurred and appropriate for use as the lodestar for Plaintiff’s fee award, including the hours spent through the filing of the fee motion plus the $3,465 expended on preparing the reply papers and appearing at the hearing on the motion.  

 

2.      Fee Multiplier

 

            Plaintiff requests that the lodestar be enhanced by a multiplier of 1.5x, which would result in an additional award of $76,952.75.  Plaintiff contends that this multiplier is reasonable considering the contingent nature of this action, Plaintiff’s counsel’s experience and knowledge, the difficulty of this litigation, and the favorable result achieved for Plaintiff. Multipliers for successful representation on a contingency basis have frequently been awarded. (See, e.g., Santana v. FCA US LLC (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 334, 352.)

 

            Defendant argues that the Court should decline to award a positive multiplier because this case was a straightforward Song-Beverly Action with no real novelty or difficulty. Defendant’s contention is belied by the record in this case which reflects three years of litigation, substantive motion practice, motions in limine, and a jury trial resulting in a decisive verdict for Plaintiff. In the Court’s view, prosecuting such a case through motions practice and pursuing a jury trial to a final verdict shows legal representation that embraces risk despite the contingent character of receiving fees, such that a multiplier on the fee award is proper. That said, the Court is persuaded that a 1.5x fee multiplier is somewhat inflated relative to the procedural history of this litigation and the total award to Plaintiff. Further, the experience of Plaintiff’s counsel and the difficulty of the case are already reflected in the lodestar computation based on the hourse expended and counsel’s hourly rates.  Because the lodestar does not recognize the risk taken in bringing this contingent matter to trial or the excellent result achieved, the Court will therefore award a reduced 1.25x multiplier, or $39,342.62, added onto the fees actually incurred.

 

            The Court therefore concludes that an award of $196,713.12 is proper in this case.

 

Costs

 

            Plaintiff also seeks an award of costs in the amount of $10,291.19.

 

            A prevailing party on a Song-Beverly claim may also seek to recover costs reasonably incurred, as well as attorney’s fees. (Civ. Code § 1794(d).) Once a request for costs is properly challenged, the burden shifts to the propounding party to demonstrate why such costs are recoverable and proper. (Whatley-Miller v. Cooper (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1103, 1113.) Costs are normally sought by way of a memorandum of costs pursuant to California Rule of Court 3.1700(a).

 

            Here, Plaintiff served and filed a memorandum of costs on August 1, 2023. A motion to tax costs is currently pending and scheduled for hearing on November 16, 2023. The Court therefore declines to consider the issue of costs on this motion.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED in the amount of $196,713.12in attorney’s fees.

 

            This ruling is without prejudice to Plaintiff’s right to seek costs via a procedurally proper Memorandum of Costs.
