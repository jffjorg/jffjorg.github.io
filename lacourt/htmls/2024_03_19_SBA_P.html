DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV003726    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: P

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA
COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES
LINDSEY MENCO, an individual;
Plaintiff,
v.
PHILLIP’S DRAPERIES AND CURTAINS,
INC., a California Corporation; SSV
PROPERTIES, a California Corporation; and
DOES 1 through 30, inclusive,
Defendants.
Case Number 23TRCV003726
[Tentative] Ruling
Date of Hearing: March 19, 2024
Dept. P
Judge David K. Reinert

SUMMARY OF RULING
Defendant Mechoshade System LLC (“Mechoshade”) has demurred to Plaintiff’s
five causes of action for strict products liability (manufacturing, design defect and failure
to warn), products liability negligence, and general negligence pursuant to CCP 430.10(e)
on the ground that they fail to meet the fact-pleading standard of CCP § 425.10(a)(1).

The Court finds that Plaintiff adequately pled her causes of action. Therefore, the
demurrer is OVERRULED.

BACKGROUND
Plaintiff filed her original complaint November 9, 2023, naming, inter alia, 15
“John Doe” defendants who allegedly “manufactured, designed, assembled, compounded,
tested or failed to test, inspected or failed to inspect, packaged, labeled, fabricated,
constructed, analyzed, distributed, merchandised, recommended, advertised, promoted,
marketed, and/or sold” the product at issue, a set of window blinds. (Compl. ¶¶ 20, 26, 32,
54; see also ¶ 41.)

Plaintiff’s claim arises from an injury she allegedly suffered on November 17,
2021, when a valence from a set of window blinds detached from the wall of an office in
El Segundo and struck her head.

On December 1, 2024, Plaintiff amended her complaint to substitute Mechoshade
for Doe No. 1. Mechoshade was served December 14, 2023.

Mechoshade brought this motion on February 6, 2024 . Plaintiff filed an opposition
on March 5, 2024. Mechoshade replied on March 7, 2024

DISCUSSION
Page 2

I. Legal Standard

A complaint for damages must contain a “statement of the facts constituting the
cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10.)

Under the demurrer statute, the party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint
has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on several grounds, including that
the pleading “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ.
Proc., § 430.010, subd. (e).)

“In determining whether or not the complaint is sufficient, as against the demurrer
upon the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the rule
is that if, upon a consideration of all the facts stated, it appears that the plaintiff is entitled
to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants, the complaint will be held
good.” (Matteson v. Wagoner (1905) 147 Cal. 739, 742.) No matter the likelihood of the
plaintiff’s ability to prove them, “a general demurrer admits the truth of all material factual
allegations in the complaint.” (Alcorn v. Anbro Engineering, Inc. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 493,
496.)

“To the extent there are factual issues in dispute . . . this court must assume the
truth not only of all facts properly pled, but also of those facts that may be implied or
inferred from those expressly alleged in the complaint.” (City of Atascadero v. Merrill
Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.) “[Q]uestions of fact
may be resolved [at the pleadings stage] only when there is only one legitimate inference
to be drawn from the allegations of the complaint.” (Tracfone Wireless, Inc. v. County of
Los Angeles (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1359, 1368.)

Here, Defendant argues that Plaintiff have not pled facts sufficient to constitute
their fifth cause of action for fraudulent inducement-concealment because it (1) is barred
by the applicable statute of limitations under Code of Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e), (2) fails to
state facts relevant to the elements of the claim under Code of Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e), and
(3) fails to allege a transactional relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose.

A. Strict Liability Causes of Action

Mechoshade asserts that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for strict liability based on
manufacturing defect, second cause of action for strict liability based on design defect, and
third cause of action based on failure to warn are insufficiently pleaded because Plaintiff
did not specifically describe the manufacturing defect, design defect, or failure to warn.
(Demurrer, 1:11-13.) Mechoshade claims that the Complaint “offers no facts, only legal
conclusions.” (Demurrer, 3:20-21.) In the complaint, Plaintiff described the product as the
“window blinds”, specifically “the large and heavy metal vallance that was part of” it.
(Compl., ¶12.) The only allegation of a product failure is that the valence “unexpectedly
detached and fell” when plaintiff’s coworker tried to operate the blinds. (Id.) The other
allegations of facts relate to Plaintiff’s injuries.

Mechoshade provides a table comparing Plaintiff’s allegations to relevant
California Civil Jury Instructions (CACI) for each cause of action. (Demurrer, 3:20-5:23.)

Page 3

There is substantial overlap between the two. Plaintiff argues that her claims are properly
pled precisely because they match the jury instructions, which lay out the “essential factual
elements” of the claims. (Opp’n, 5:3-8.) In reply, Mechoshade responds that Plaintiff cites
no authority for this proposition, because there is none. If all that was needed was a
recitation of the elements without any actual acts, there would be no reason to go through
even that formality, because stating the name of the cause of action would provide the exact
same information. (Reply, 4:12-24.)

“What is important is that the complaint as a whole contain sufficient facts to
apprise the defendant of the basis upon which the plaintiff is seeking relief” (Doheny Park
Terrace Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Truck Ins. Exchange (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th
1076, 1098-99.) A complaint will be upheld if it provides the defendant with “notice of the
issues sufficient to enable preparation of a defense.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42
Cal. 4th 531, 549-500.) To plead sufficient facts to establish strict liability in tort, a plaintiff
need allege that he was: 1) injured by the product; 2) because of a defect in its design and
manufacture; 3) of which the plaintiff was not aware; 4) while it was being used in the
manner in which it was intended. (See Alvarez v. Felker Mfg. Co. (1964) 230 Cal.App.2d
987, 1005.)

Here, the “legal conclusions” pointed to by defendant, while rooted in the relevant
jury instruction, are not impermissible. (See 4 Witkin Cal. Procedure (6th Ed. 2021) §398
(“Generally, allegations that an act, instrument, claim or demand is ‘illegal,’ ‘unlawful,’
‘unauthorized,’ ‘void,’ ‘wrongful,’ ‘without right,’ or ‘fraudulent,’ etc. are legal
conclusions.”).)

The court finds that Plaintiff has provided sufficient facts to state causes of action.
Accordingly, Mechoshade’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first three causes of action is overruled.

B. Negligence Causes of Action

Mechoshade asserts that Plaintiff’s negligence claims are insufficiently pleaded
because she did “not identify a single act or omission of Mechoshade alleged to be
negligent, only listing the elements of a negligence claim familiar to any first-year law
student.” (Demurrer, 5:24-28.) Plaintiff alleges that the valence fell when her coworker
operated the blinds. Mechoshade argues that this allegation alone cannot meet the causation
element of a negligence claim. “Just like a car accident is typically the result of negligent
driving but only rarely because of a defect in the car itself, when artwork, a shelf , or a hook
falls off the wall, the inference is it was not hung properly. One does not assume the object
itself was wrongly manufactured.” (Reply, 3:15-18.) “In the ordinary personal injury
lawsuit, in which the complaint’s factual recitations show plainly the connection between
cause and effect, it suffices to pleat causation succinctly and generally.” (Bockrath v.
Aldrich Chem. Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78.) Only when “the pleaded facts of
negligence and injury do not naturally give rise to an inference of causation [must] the
plaintiff…plead specific facts affording an inference [that] one caused the others. (Id.)
Taking all of Plaintiff’s allegations as true, as is proper in considering a demurrer, and
construing the complaint in the light most favorable to the pleading party, the Complaint
is sufficient to allege negligence as to Defendant Mechoshade.

Page 4

Accordingly, Mechoshade’s demurrer to Plaintiff two negligence causes of action
is overruled.

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’ Amended Complaint is OVERRULED

BACKGROUND

On October 10, 202, Plaintiff was injured when she tripped over a tree well in front of 6220 W. 87th Street, Westchester, CA. (Complaint p.4.)  Plaintiff alleges that she complied with “a claims statute.” (Id. at ¶ 9.)  In her complaint, Plaintiff alleges two causes of action; for Premises Liability and General Negligence respectively.  Under the premises liability claim, plaintiff asserts that the City is liable for 1) negligence, 2) willful failure to warn, and 3) dangerous condition of public property. (Id. at p. 4.)

Plaintiff’s counsel sent a Claim for Damages to Defendant which was received on April 12, 2023. (Decl. Hayes, Exh. B.)  That claim stated a date of loss as October 3, 2022. (Id.)  The claim was returned with no action having been taken because it was not filed within six months of the purported date of loss. (Id. at Exh. C.)  Plaintiff’s counsel then sent a request for relief to file a late claim to the Defendant, dated June 14, 2023. (Id. at Exh. D.)  Thereafter, on July 12, 2023, Defendant sent a letter to Plaintiff alerting Plaintiff that the claim had been denied. (Id. at Exhibit F.)  On the same date, Plaintiff sent a second letter alerting Plaintiff that her “Application for Leave to Present a Late Claim” had been denied. (Id. at Exhibit E.)

Defendant asserts that the demur must be sustained without leave to amend because Plaintiff failed to comply with the requisite claims statutes.  In the alternative, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s “general negligence” and “willful failure to warn” causes of action are not available under the Government Code.  Plaintiff argues in opposition that

 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Before a suit for damages may be filed against a public entity, a claimant must present a timely written claim to the public entity and the claim must have been acted upon by the board or deemed rejected.  Gov. Code §945.4; Munoz v. State of Cal. (1995) 33 Cal. App. 4th 1767, 1777.  A claim relating to a cause of action for injury to person or personal property must be presented to the public entity not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.  Gov. Code §911.2.  Munoz, supra, 33 Cal. App. 4th at 1777.  If the claim is denied, any suit predicated on that claim must be brought against the public entity within six months of the notice of notice of the denial. (Gov. Code §945.6(a)(1).)

If a claimant fails to present a claim within the statutory period, he may apply “within a reasonable time not to exceed one year after the accrual of the cause of action” to the public entity for leave to present a late claim.  (Gov. Code §911.4.)  If the public entity denies leave to present a late claim, a claimant may petition the Court under Government Code §946.6 for relief from the requirements of Government Code §945.4.  (Munoz, supra, 33 Cal. App. 4th at 1777.)  That petition, made pursuant to Government Code §946.6, must be “filed within six months after the application to the board is denied or deemed to be denied pursuant to Section 911.6.”  (Gov. Code §946.6(b); see also City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (1993) 14 Cal. App. 4th 621, 627.)  This requirement is mandatory.  (Lineaweaver v. S. Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1983) 139 Cal. App. 3d 738, 741.)

“[A] public entity may be estopped from asserting the limitations of claims statute where its agents or employees have prevented or deterred the filing of a timely claim by some affirmative act.” (John R. v. Oakland Unified School Dist. (1989) 48 Cal.3d 438, 445.)  “[A]ctual fraud or the intent to mislead is not essential.” (Id.)

             A public entity may be sued. (Gov. Code §945.)  However, under the Government Claims Act, “there is no such thing as common law tort liability for public entities; a public entity is not liable for an injury except as otherwise provided by statute.” Quigley v. Garden Valley Fire Protection Dist. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 798, 803.

 

DISCUSSION

            The question presented in this motion is whether, by sending two letters, one denying Plaintiff’s claim outright, one denying Plaintiff’s request to file a late claim, Plaintiff can rely on Section 945.6(a)(1) and file her suit in court or whether she must first have filed for relief from an untimely claim under Section 946.6.  In this case, the court finds that, by both denying the underlying claim (which it asserts it had no duty to consider) and denying the request to grant relief to file a late claim, the City created a situation in which Plaintiff could not know how to respond.  Only the City knew whether it would assert reliance upon the denial of the underlying claim or denial of the request to submit a late claim to defend the action. (See Sofranek v. County of Merced (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1251.)  Therefore, by filing her suit within six months of being given notice of the City’s denial of her claim, the Plaintiff has complied with the Government Claims Act.

            The next question presented is whether the Plaintiff can bring suit against the City for claims based in the common law.  The court finds that she cannot. (See Quigley, supra, 7 Cal.5th at 803.)

 

CONCLUSION

Therefore, defendant’s demur to Plaintiff’s cause of action for Premises Liability due to a dangerous condition of public property is OVERRRULED.  Defendant’s demur to all other counts and causes of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.
