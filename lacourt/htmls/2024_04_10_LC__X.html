DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV00411    Hearing Date: April 10, 2024    Dept: X

   Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

 

 

HEARING DATE:     April 10, 2024                                     TRIAL DATE: May 7, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         MIKAYEL ISRAYELYAN, an individual, v. KRIKOR BARDAKJIAN, an individual; 485 CLIFF DR, LLC, a California limited liability company, and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive.  

 

CASE NO.:                 22AHCV00411

 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Mikayel Israyelyan

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      No response filed.

 

SERVICE:                              Filed January 18, 2024

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

             Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his claim for quiet title.

 

BACKGROUND

 

             This case arises out of Plaintiff Mikayel Israyelyan’s (“Plaintiffs”) claim that Defendant Krikor Bardakjian (“Defendant”) improperly transferred property held by 485 Cliff Dr, LLC to himself. Plaintiff alleges that he and Defendant were both 50 percent members of 485 Cliff Dr, LLC and worked to purchase, invest in, and renovate property located at 485 Cliff Drive, Pasadena, California, 91107 (“Subject Property”). Plaintiff filed this complaint on June 24, 2022, alleging two causes of action for (1) quiet title and (2) declaratory relief.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to his claim for quiet title is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s interest in the Subject Property is determined to be 50 percent.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “A party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code of Civil Procedure section 473c subd. (a)(1).) “The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code of Civil Procedures section 473c subd. (c).)

 

 “[W]here the plaintiff has also moved for summary judgment—or, as in this case, summary adjudication—that party has the burden of showing there is no defense to a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a).) That burden can be met if the plaintiff “has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) If the plaintiff meets this burden, it is up to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (S.B.C.C., Inc. v. St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 383, 388.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his cause of action for quiet title of the Subject Property.

 

“To prevail on a quiet title claim, a plaintiff must establish title to the property in dispute. [Citation.] ‘The plaintiff may recover only upon the strength of his or her own title ... and not upon the weakness of the defendant's title. Where the plaintiff relies on a paper title alone he must trace his title (1) to the government; or (2) to grantor in possession at the time of the conveyance to the plaintiff; or (3) to a source common to the chains of title of plaintiff and defendant.’ ” (Thompson v. Ioane (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1180, 1195.) “As a plaintiff moving for summary judgment on a quiet title claim, [the plaintiff] bore the burden to make out a prima facie case of ownership.” (Ibid.)

 

“In order to quiet title, a plaintiff's complaint ‘must include’ all of the following as elements of the claim: (1) a description of the property that is the subject of the action; (2) the title of the plaintiff as to which a determination is sought and the basis of the title; (3) ‘[t]he adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought’; (4) the date as of which the determination is sought; and (5) a prayer for the determination of plaintiff's title ‘against the adverse claims.’ ” (Weeden v. Hoffman (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 269, 294.)

 

Plaintiff argues that he was wrongfully denied his 50 percent interest in the Subject Property when Defendant transferred the property from 485 Cliff Dr, LLC to himself. Plaintiff provides that in 2018, Defendant approached him with the idea to purchase the Subject Property to renovate and “flip” it. (Israyelyan Decl. ¶¶ 3-4.) Plaintiff provides that on July 6, 2018, the parties formed 485 Cliff Dr, LLC to purchase the Subject Property. (Id. ¶ 5.) On August 28, 2018, 485 Cliff Dr, LLC purchased the Subject Property. (Id. ¶ 6, Exhibits B and C.)  The parties brought in a third-party investor, Manuel Tchaboukian, who provided additional funds for membership interest in 485 Cliff. Dr, LLC. (Id. ¶ 9.) The parties ultimately purchased Tchaboukian’s membership interest in the business. (Id. ¶ 11.)

 

Plaintiff provides that at relevant times, he and Defendant were both 50 percent members of 485 Cliff Dr, LLC, including as listed on the organization’s 2020 and 2021 tax returns. (Israyelyan Decl. ¶¶ 13, 16, and 17.) Plaintiff provides that on April 16, 2021, Defendant transferred the Subject Property from 485 Cliff Dr, LLC to himself by grant deed without Plaintiff’s consent. (Id. ¶ 19, Exhibit L.)

 

Plaintiff has demonstrated that he was a 50 percent member of 485 Cliff Dr, LLC, who was the owner of the Subject Property. Plaintiff also established that Defendant transferred the Subject Property from 485 Cliff Dr, LLC to himself without Plaintiff’s permission. Plaintiff also provides that he did not receive compensation or reimbursement for purchase or renovation of the property. (Israyelyan Decl. ¶ 23.) Further, Defendants have failed to oppose the present motion and have failed to raise a triable issue of material fact.

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to his claim for quiet title is granted. Plaintiff’s interest in the Subject Property is determined to be 50 percent.

 

Plaintiff also moves for a judgment on “further relief as is just and proper.” (Motion at p. 9:7.) Plaintiff makes various damage claims regarding his investments and the value of the property. However, Plaintiff does not establish, for purposes of this motion, what remedy he is seeking or that he is entitled to a specific remedy.

 

“A cause of action to quiet title is clearly not a tort claim, and it does not seek to hold a defendant liable for damages. Rather, ‘actions to quiet title, like true declaratory relief actions, are generally equitable in nature.’ [Citation.] ‘A quiet title action is a statutory action that seeks to declare the rights of the parties in realty. [Citations.] “ ‘ “The object of the action is to finally settle and determine, as between the parties, all conflicting claims to the property in controversy, and to decree to each such interest or estate therein as he may be entitled to.” ’ ” [Citation.] The purpose of a quiet title action is to determine any adverse claim to the property that the defendant may assert, and to declare and define any interest held by the defendant, “ ‘so that the plaintiff may have a decree finally adjudicating the extent of his own interest in the property in controversy.’ ” ’ ” (Weeden, supra, 70 Cal.App.5th at p. 291.)

 

Thus, at this current juncture, Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to the remedies is denied.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to his claim for quiet title is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s interest in the Subject Property is determined to be 50 percent.

CASE:                         LUCKY STAR KITCHEN, INC. a California corporation, v. LI LIU, an individual; TIAN YOU FENG, INC., a California corporation; YUXI LIU, an individual; YAN CHEN, an individual; and DOES 1-50, inclusive, 

 

CASE NO.:                 23AHCV01075

 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Lucky Star Kitchen, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      Defendants Tian You Feng, Inc., Li Liu, and

 

SERVICE:                              Filed February 14 and 15, 2024

 

OPPOSITION:                       Filed March 27, 2024

 

REPLY:                                   Filed April 3, 2024

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

             Plaintiff moves for an order compelling Defendants to provide further responses to its requests for the production of documents and form interrogatories.

 

BACKGROUND

 

             This case arises out of Plaintiff Lucky Star Kitchen, Inc.’s (“Plaintiff”) claim that Defendants Li Liu, Tian You Feng, Inc., Yuxi Liu, and Yan Chen engaged in the fraudulent transfer of assets. Plaintiff filed this complaint on May 11, 2023, alleging one cause of action for violation of the Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses is DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions are denied.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

On receipt of a response to discovery requests, the party requesting may move for an order compelling further responses for interrogatories (Code Civ. Proc. 2030.300), requests for admission (Cod. Civ. Proc. section 2033.290), and request for production (Code Civ. Proc. section 2031.310). “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or any specific later date to which the requesting party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the requesting party waives any right to compel further response to the requests for admission.” (Code Civ. Proc. section 2033.290, subd. (c).

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiff moves for an order compelling further responses to its discovery requests. Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses from Li Liu as to its requests for the production of documents and further responses from Yan Chen as its form interrogatories. Plaintiff provides that it served its discovery requests on Defendants on August 4, 2023. (Qi Decl. at p. 2:10-12.) Plaintiff provides that Defendant provided deficient responses on October 4, 2023. (Id. at p. 2:13.) Defendant provided supplemental responses on November 14, 2023. (Id. at p. 2:19.)

 

            Plaintiff provides that the parties agreed to extend the deadline to file a motion to compel further responses 60 days starting from January 10, 2024. (Qi Decl. at p. 2:22-24.) However, in the attached correspondence, Defendants’ counsel provides “I agree you have 60 days to bring a Motion to Compel from today’s date for any outstanding discovery.” (Exhibit 5b at p. 2.) This email was sent on December 5, 2023. (Ibid.) The present motions were filed on February 14, and 15, 2024. Under the parties’ agreement to extend the deadline, Plaintiff’s motions are untimely.

 

CONCLUSION

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses is DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions are denied.
