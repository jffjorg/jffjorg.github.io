DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 21STCV28160    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:   May 9, 2024                                       

 

CASE NAME:           Wang v. Liu

 

CASE NO.:                21STCV28160

 

MOTION TO QUASH ORDER FOR APPEARANCE AND EXAMINATION, AND SUBPOENA DUCES TECUM

 

MOVING PARTY:  Nonparty Liang Ly LLP

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Yongkang Wang

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      Order quashing Plaintiff Yongkang Wang’s Order for Appearance and Examination of Jason Liang and Deposition Subpoenas for Production of Business Records of non-party’s counsel, Liang Ly LLP;

2.      Alternatively, protective order limiting the scope of both the order for examination and subpoena;

3.      Order for attorney’s fees in the amount of $9,060.00.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Liang Ly LLP’s Motion to Quash Order for Appearance and Examination, and Subpoena Duces Tecum is GRANTED.

2.      The moving party and Plaintiff’s requests for attorney’s fees are both DENIED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On July 30, 2021, Plaintiff Yongkang Wang (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Defendant Ashley Liu (Defendant) and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive for: (1) Fraud by Intentional Misrepresentation; (2) Promissory Fraud; (3) Conversion; (4) Breach of Oral Agreement; (5) Breach of Written Agreement; (6) Unjust Enrichment; and (7) Business & Professions Code §17200.

 

Plaintiff alleges Defendant made false statements of material fact to Plaintiff to induce Plaintiff to enter into a currency exchange agreement and remit $1.53 million to Defendant.

 

On March 15, 2024, Liang Ly LLP (Liang LLP) brought the instant motion to quash order for appearance and examination, and subpoena duce tecum. On April 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On May 2, 2024, Liang LLP filed a reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1985.3(g) states in relevant part, “[a]ny consumer whose personal records are sought by a subpoena duces tecum and who is a party to the civil action in which this subpoena duces tecum is served may, prior to the date for production, bring a motion under Section 1987.1 to quash or modify the subpoena duces tecum. Notice of the bringing of that motion shall be given to the witness and deposition officer at least five days prior to production. The failure to provide notice to the deposition officer shall not invalidate the motion to quash or modify the subpoena duces tecum but may be raised by the deposition officer as an affirmative defense in any action for liability for improper release of records.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3(g).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 708.200, states in pertinent part, “[i]n any proceeding under this article, the court may, on motion of the person to be examined or on its own motion, make such protective orders as justice may require.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.200.)

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Liang LLP argues the subpoena and order for appearance and examination are improper on their face because they are facially defective and overbroad. Specifically, Liang LLP argues Plaintiff has been misinformed about Defendant being the CEO of A-May Investment and having a controlling interest because non-party and non-judgment debtor, A-May Investment has no CEO but co-managed by Defendant and Hongmei Jin. Liang LLP also asserts Ms. Jin and Defendant are currently in a lawsuit Case No. 19STCV17961, where Defendant is alleged to have misappropriated project funds, which shows that Defendant does not have a controlling interest. Similarly, Liang LLP asserts Plaintiff’s own declaration admits that Liang LLP, which is counsel for A-May Investment does not possess or control any asset belonging to Defendant. Likewise, Liang LLP contends Plaintiff failed to tender the required fees for mileage from the third party’s place of residence to the place of examination per Code of Civil Procedure section 708.120(f). Furthermore, Liang LLP argues Plaintiff’s subpoena broadly seeks bank statements of all trust accounts Liang LLP holds on behalf of A-May Investment LLC since January 2020 and all documents relating to any transfer over $500 made to or from those trust accounts starting January 2020, where A-May Investment does not hold any property belonging to Defendant, Liang LLP is a third-party who is not a judgment debtor, and Jason Liang has already informed Plaintiff’s counsel that Liang LLP does not hold any special trust account that matches Plaintiff’s description. Moreover, Liang LLP contends the subpoena seeks records protected from disclosure by the attorney-client privilege and protected from disclosure by third-party’s right to privacy. Additionally, Liang LLP contends the subpoena seeks records not relevant or reasonably calculated to lead to discovery of admissible evidence and was not issued for a legitimate purpose. Alternatively, Liang LLP requests the Court enter a protective order limiting the scope of both the order for examination and the subpoena.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the order for examination and subpoena seeks only trust account bank records of A-May Investment LLC, a business which Defendant has a financial interest. Specifically, Plaintiff asserts Liang LLP concedes Defendant is a co-managing member of A-May Investment and the most recent statement of information filed with the Secretary of State on April 21, 2022, listed Defendant as A-May Investment’s sole member and CEO. Similarly, Plaintiff contends Ms. Jin’s complaint alleges A-May Investment is the Alter-Ego of Defendant and under Defendant’s sole domination and control. Likewise, Plaintiff argues the present motion fails to establish that Mr. Liang and Liang LLP is not a third  person within the meaning of sections 708.120(a) and 708.130 for the purpose of a post-judgment enforcement proceeding. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends the bank records are not subject to attorney-client privilege because the primary purpose of any communications between Liang LLP and A-May Investment related to A-May Investment’s trust account is not for legal advice but rather the business purpose of fulfilling the Liang LLP’s duty as custodian of the funds. Plaintiff also argues that Mr. Liang waived his right to challenge the service of the notice on grounds that it failed to tender mileage fees because he appeared at the hearing date on the notice and his office address is within one mile of the Court. Finally, Plaintiff contends the order for appearance and subpoena are not overbroad because they only seek bank records of A-May Investment’s trust account which holds the net sales proceeds of the Long Beach Project.

 

In reply, Liang LLP argues the order for examination cannot be enforced because Plaintiff concedes that he had failed to tender the required witness fee at the time he served the notice and Mr. Liang appeared for the hearing under the threat of Plaintiff’s counsel that a bench warrant would be issued if he did not appear. Liang LLP reiterates that A-May Investment is not a judgment debtor and its property does not belong to Defendant; the order for examination and subpoena seek to invade the attorney-client privilege; and the order for examination and subpoena are overbroad and improper because Plaintiff applied for a third-party judgment debtor’s examination per section 708.120 not section 708.130, which permits a more expansive inquiry.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 708.120(a), “[u]pon ex parte application by a judgment creditor who has a money judgment and proof by the judgment creditor by affidavit or otherwise to the satisfaction of the proper court that a third person has possession or control of property in which the judgment debtor has an interest or is indebted to the judgment debtor in an amount exceeding two hundred fifty dollars ($250), the court shall make an order directing the third person to appear before the court, or before a referee appointed by the court, at a time and place specified in the order, to answer concerning such property or debt. The affidavit in support of the judgment creditor’s application may be based on the affiant’s information and belief.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.120(a).)

 

In Fox Johns Lazar Pekin & Wexler, APC v. Superior Court (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1210, the court held that “the purpose of section 708.120 is to provide a tool that allows a judgment creditor to find property or money that is owed to the judgment debtor. To this end, it allows the judgment creditor to obtain an order to examine a third party who it believes possesses the judgment debtor's property or owes the judgment debtor a debt over $250. The judgment creditor must prove to the court's satisfaction that either one of these circumstances exists before the court will issue an order requiring the party to appear for an examination. Further, the statute clearly provides that the third party is to answer questions regarding the subject property or debt. Section 708.120 does not allow for a more expansive examination.” (Fox Johns Lazar Pekin & Wexler, APC v. Superior Court (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1221 (Fox Johns).) As such, “it is not the mechanism to allow a judgment creditor to discover the identity of a law firm's clients, review the law firm's billings of other clients, or otherwise obtain information about entities that may be the alter ego of the judgment debtor.” (Id.) Furthermore, Fox Johns concluded “a subpoena must be tethered to a case” after finding that the subpoena sought documents relating to the law firm’s billing invoices to clients in the action, who paid the invoices, and documents sufficient to identify the firm’s clients. (Id. at 1222.)

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure 708.120(f), “[a]n order made pursuant to subdivision (a) is not effective unless, at the time it is served on the third person, the person serving the order tenders to the third person fees for the mileage necessary to be traveled from the third person’s residence to the place of examination. The mileage fees shall be in the same amount generally provided for witnesses when legally required to attend civil proceedings in the court where the examination proceeding is to be conducted.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.120(f).)

 

The Court finds that the order for appearance and examination is proper because it seeks documents from Mr. Liang and Liang LLP upon information and belief that they hold a special trust account on behalf of A-May Investment LLC, which Defendant holds an interest in and serves as a CEO. (Yan Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 2.) This information is relevant to the case at bar, i.e., enforcement of post-judgment proceedings. Furthermore, it does not appear that the information is sought to discovery entities that may be alter egos of Defendant. However, the subpoena does appear to be overly broad. Although the subpoena seeks bank records from any trust account for A-May Investment since January 2020, it also seeks all documents relating to any transfer of over $500 made to and from the A-May Investment trust accounts held by Liang LLP starting from January 2020, which could contain billing invoices evidencing transfers made to and from the purported trust accounts. In turn, potentially revealed billing invoices would show who paid the invoices and identify the firm’s clients. As such, the subpoena should be limited to documents evidencing any transfer of over $500 made to and from the A-May Investment trust accounts held by Liang LLP starting from January 2020 excluding billing invoices, who paid the invoices, and documents that would identify the firm’s clients. Nevertheless, the order for appearance and examination, and subpoena are not effective per Code of Civil Procedure section 708.120(f) because Plaintiff did not tender mileage fees necessary for Mr. Liang’s travel from his residence to the place of examination.

 

Therefore, the order should be quashed and re-served in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 708.120(f).

 

Sanctions

 

“Except as specified in subdivision (c), in making an order pursuant to motion made under subdivision (c) of Section 1987 or under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2(a).)

 

Here, Liang LLP’s seeks $9,060.00 in attorney’s fees for (1) 6.5 hours drafting this motion; (2) 2.5 hours drafting a reply to the opposition; (3) 1.0 hour preparing for and attending the hearing on the motion at $900.00 per hour; and (4) $60.00 in costs for having to bring the motion. (Schein Decl., ¶¶ 10-11.) While Liang LLP’s counsel has shown that his billing rate is reasonable given years of practice, educational background, and positions he has held in major law firms, the Court finds that the motion was not opposed in bad faith. Furthermore, one of the requirements of the subpoena although overbroad was not oppressive. Thus, sanctions are not warranted at this time. Similarly, the Court finds that the motion was not made in bad faith, so Plaintiff’s counsel request for sanctions is also unwarranted at this time.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

1.      Liang Ly LLP’s Motion to Quash Order for Appearance and Examination, and Subpoena Duces Tecum is GRANTED.

2.      The moving party and Plaintiff’s requests for attorney’s fees are both DENIED.

Moving party is to give notice.
