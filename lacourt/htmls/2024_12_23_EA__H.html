DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22PSCV00441    Hearing Date: December 23, 2024    Dept: H

Hernandez v. Rubio, Case No. 22PSCV00441

ORDER RE: NON-PARTY MARTINEZ’S DEPOSITION

The Court CONTINUES the hearings on (1) Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Non-Party Martinez to Comply with Deposition Subpoena and (2) Non-Party Witness Martinez’s Motion for Protective Order to February 19, 2025, at 9:00 a.m. so counsel for Plaintiff Hernandez and counsel for Non-Party Martinez can engage in a meaningful meet-and-confer session regarding a deposition date and the 293 document requests.


I.        Background 

Plaintiff Roger Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

          Plaintiff is a former California State Assemblyperson, a former candidate for U.S. Congress, and the ex-husband of Defendant California State Senator Susan Rubio (“S. Rubio”). Defendant Blanca E. Rubio (“B. Rubio”) is S. Rubio’s sister and a California State Assemblyperson. Defendant Maria Morales (“Morales”) is a former El Monte City Council member. Defendant Alma Martinez (“Martinez”) is the City Manager of El Monte. S. Rubio filed a request for a three-year domestic violence restraining order against Plaintiff on April 13, 2016, which the court granted on July 1, 2016. The restraining order was renewed by S. Rubio on January 17, 2020 for an additional five years. Beginning in or about April 2017, Plaintiff began his post-elected-office professional life as a governmental consultant. Plaintiff’s clients, however, would terminate his services upon being informed—either directly or indirectly by S. Rubio, B. Rubio, Morales, and/or Martinez—that the city councils of El Monte, West Covina, and Baldwin Park would refuse to grant any permits to, or otherwise “do any business” with, any of Plaintiff’s clients. 

On October 27, 2022, an “Order Granting Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint (Anti-SLAPP)” was entered. 

On November 30, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action against S. Rubio, B. Rubio, Morales, Martinez, and Does 1-50 for: (1) intentional interference with contractual relations and (2) intentional interferences with prospective economic relations. 

On December 22, 2022, Plaintiff filed a “Notice of Appeal” of the Court’s October 27, 2022 order. 

On July 6, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Morales and Martinez from the First Amended Complaint, with prejudice. 

On October 4, 2023, the Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal filed December 22, 2022; on December 11, 2023, remittitur was issued. 

A Mandatory Settlement Conference is set for April 24, 2025. 

The Final Status Conference is set for July 8, 2025. Jury Trial is set for July 22, 2025. 

Pending before the Court are: (1) Plaintiff’s motion to compel non-party witness Martinez to comply with a deposition subpoena and (2) non-party Martinez’s motion for a protective order precluding or limiting in scope her deposition as City Manager for the City of El Monte.

II.      Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Deposition Subpoena 

          A.       Legal Standard 

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness of the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms of conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a).) “The following persons may make a motion pursuant to subdivision (a): (1) A party. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (b).) 

“If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (a).) “This motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (b).)

“A written notice and all moving papers supporting a motion to compel an answer to a deposition question or to compel production of a document or tangible thing from a nonparty deponent must be personally served on the nonparty deponent unless the nonparty deponent agrees to accept service by mail or electronic service at an address or electronic service address specified on the deposition record.” (Cal. Rules of Court rule 3.1346).

          B.       Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order, per Code of Civil Procedure sections 1987.1 and 2025.480, for an order compelling non-party witness Martinez[FN1] to comply with the “Deposition Subpoena for Personal Appearance and Production of Documents and Things” served on her on July 17, 2024. Plaintiff also seeks an order requiring Martinez to pay $4,035.00 in attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in connection with this motion.

The proof of service accompanying the motion does not reflect that personal service was effectuated on Martinez as required by California Rules of Court rule 3.1346. However, because Martinez filed an opposition, the Court elects to proceed to the merits of the motion. 

Plaintiff’s counsel Ross Hollenkamp (“Hollenkamp”) represents, and/or the accompanying exhibits reflect, as follows: 

          On July 16, 2024, Martinez was personally served with a “Deposition Subpoena for Personal Appearance and Production of Documents and Things,” which included, as Attachment 3, a set of Requests for Production of Documents that included 293 individual requests. (Hollenkamp Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.) On July 17, 2024, Plaintiff mail-served a “Notice of Taking Deposition of Non-Party Alma Martinez and Demand for Production of Documents and Things,” which attached the subpoena, to counsel for S. Rubio and B. Rubio as well as Martinez’s former counsel of record. 

          On July 19, 2024, Martinez served an “Objection to Deposition of Alma Martinez” (“Objection”), advising that she would not appear for deposition “for the reasons stated in her counsel’s meet and confer of July 19, 2024” and objecting to the 293 document requests collectively on the following six grounds:

          “1.      Objection. This request seeks to invade the attorney client privilege, the attorney client doctrine, privacy rights of public employees under Govt Code 6254, and privacy rights of third parties.

          2.       You are deposing Ms. Martinez solely in her capacity as an APEX witness and on matters that have nothing to do with this case, or on matters addressed by the ANTI SLAPP statute.

          3.       The term “lawsuit” is not defined as I understand that many portions of the Complaint were stricken by the Court, so it is not clear what claims or allegations remain at issue.

          4.       The one issue in the case as I understand is the bid plaintiff made and the fact that he was not awarded the bid per the dates specified in the lawsuit to which I raised the ANTI SLAPP defense. Your subpoena is asking for more recent matters and recent attempts to bid on work by the plaintiff, that have nothing to do with this case.

          5.       A 296-document request[FN2] is burdensome, harassing, and oppressive, and not relevant to the subject matter of this lawsuit.

          6.       Ms. Martinez is not the custodian of city records.” (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. C.) 

          Hollenkamp and Martinez’s counsel Nancy Doumanian (“Doumanian”) thereafter met and conferred “via telephone and email.” (Id., ¶ 7.) On July 31, 2024, an assistant to Doumanian advised that Doumanian would produce Martinez for deposition, but that the deposition would be limited to three unspecified topics “directly related to the case and w[ould] not cover any outside matters”; that same day, another of Doumanian’s assistants, Aileen Ramos (“Ramos”), advised that she had reached out to Martinez about scheduling her deposition and offered Martinez the following dates: August 14, 2024 (represented to be the earliest date available before Doumanian began trial), October 29, 2024 (represented to be the earliest date available after Doumanian was set to end trial), and November 12, 2024. (Id., ¶ 7, Exh. D.) On August 1, 2024, Ramos advised that Martinez said she was available for deposition on November 12, 2024. (Id.) 

          On August 5, 2024, Hollenkamp advised Doumanian that November 12, 2024 was not an acceptable deposition date and requested that Doumanian confirm Martinez’s availability for August 14, 2024. (Id.) Hollenkamp also proposed the following alternative dates: August 12, 13, 19, 21, 26-30, or September 3, 2024. (Id.) Hollenkamp further advised that the deposition would remain on calendar as noticed for August 8, 2024, until one of the above alternative dates was confirmed. (Id.) That same day, Ramos advised Hollenkamp that Martinez was unavailable August 14, 2024, but that she would check her availability for August 27 and 29, 2024, as those dates had “just opened up” for Doumanian. (Id.) Ramos further advised that Doumanian was starting a trial on September 5, 2024 that was expected to continue until October 11 or 14, 2024, and that, as such, Doumanian’s availability was “severely limited” until the first week in November. (Id.) Ramos advised later that day that Martinez was not available for either of the abovementioned August dates but could accommodate November 5, 2024 instead of November 12, 2024. (Id.) On August 6, 2024, Hollenkamp advised Doumanian and Ramos that November 5 was not an acceptable alternative date, that Martinez could sit for deposition on August 14, 27, or 29, that if Martinez did not agree to any of these other dates the deposition would proceed on August 8, and that if Martinez did not appear on August 8, he would take a “Certificate of Non-Appearance” and file a motion to compel compliance. (Id.)

          Hollenkamp proceeded with taking the deposition as noticed and had a “Certificate of Non-Appearance” taken on August 8, 2024. (Id., ¶ 7, Exh. E.) On September 30, 2024, Hollenkamp asked Doumanian whether she and Martinez were available for Martinez to be deposed on October 18 or October 21-25. (Id., ¶ 9, Exh. F.) Hollenkamp also requested that Doumanian provide alternative dates for the week of October 14, 2024 if none of the above dates worked. (Id.) On October 3, 2024, Ramos advised that none of these dates worked for Doumanian but advised that Martinez was available for deposition on November 5. (Id.) On October 4, 2024, Hollenkamp advised Doumanian that if she failed to confirm her and her client’s availability on the proposed dates, by the close of business October 7, he would proceed with a motion. (Id.) This motion followed on October 8, 2024. 

It does not appear to the Court that a good faith meet-and-confer session was conducted prior to the filing of the instant motion. Martinez is the City Manager of El Monte; as such, it is not surprising to the Court that her availability for deposition is limited. Doumanian, a sole practitioner, immediately communicated through her office to Hollenkamp that she had limited upcoming availability due to a lengthy scheduled trial.[FN3] While the Court appreciates Plaintiff’s frustration that an earlier date could not be arranged for Martinez’s deposition, the Court does not believe that Plaintiff’s decision to proceed with taking a “Certificate of Non-Appearance” on August 8, 2024 was appropriate under the circumstances. 

Additionally, the Court does not see any evidence in the record that a good faith meet-and-confer discussion occurred with respect to the 293 document production requests. Although Hollenkamp attests that telephonic and email meet-and-confer discussions were conducted after he received Martinez’s Objection, after which time it was agreed that Martinez would appear for her deposition, Hollenkamp’s declaration is devoid of any details regarding a discussion of any particular document production. There is also no written meet-and-confer correspondence regarding any particular document production. 

Hollenkamp has submitted a second declaration, concurrently with Plaintiff’s reply brief, in which he advises the Court that, although he and Doumanian subsequently agreed that Martinez would be deposed on December 6, 2024, attorney Allen Secretov (“Secretov”), counsel for S. Rubio and B. Rubio, “hijacked” the proceedings by insufficiently noticing (i.e., on December 2, 2024, at 4:56 p.m.; see 12:24-28, Exhs. H and I) and conducting (despite receiving Plaintiff’s objections; see 13:1-6; Exhs. J and K) a testimony-only, remote deposition of Martinez on that date. (Hollenkamp Reply Decl., 6:21-7:1, 8:8-16 and 9:24-26.) Hollenkamp advises that he “took the Dec. 6 date off-calendar (and thus also did not re-notice the deposition for that date)” because he deduced, from Doumanian’s filing of the “Motion for Protective Order” on November 14, 2024, that Martinez had changed her position and would not voluntarily sit for any deposition. (Id., 7:2-12.) Hollenkamp did not attend the December 6 deposition. 

Under the circumstances, the Court is inclined to continue the hearing to February 19, 2025, at 9:00 a.m. The Court notes that Plaintiff noticed Martinez’s deposition in her capacity as “El Monte City Manager,” whereas S. Rubio and B. Rubio noticed (and deposed) Martinez in her personal capacity. The fact that Secretov conducted a testimony-only deposition of Martinez on December 6, 2024 does not preclude Hollenkamp from deposing Martinez as “El Monte City Manager.” 

Counsel for Martinez and Plaintiff are instructed to conduct a good faith meet-and-confer session regarding the 293 document production requests and to prepare and file a Joint Status Report outlining their meet-and-confer efforts and identifying which specific production requests, if any, are no longer at issue and which remain disputed and the nature of the dispute no later than nine court days before the continued hearing date. As part of the meet-and-confer session, counsel should also confer about deposition dates. At the hearing on February 19, 2025, Martinez’s counsel should be prepared to present at least three available deposition dates between February 20, 2025 and April 5, 2025; if the parties cannot reach agreement as to a particular date, the Court will set a deposition date at the February 19, 2025 hearing. The Court reserves on the issue of sanctions at this juncture. 

III.     Martinez’s Motion for a Protection Order

          A.       Legal Standard 

“(a) Every court shall have then power to do all of the following:

(1) To preserve and enforce order in its immediate presence. . .

(3) To provide for the orderly conduct of proceedings before it, or its officers. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a).) 

“The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions:

(1) That the deposition not be taken at all.

(2) That the deposition be taken at a different time. . .

(5) That the deposition be taken only on certain specified terms and conditions. . .

(9) That certain matters not be inquired into,

(10) That the scope of the examination be limited to certain matters.

(11) That all or certain of the writings or tangible things designated in the deposition notice not be produced, inspected, copies, tested, or sampled . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b).) 

          B.       Discussion

Martinez moves the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure sections 128(a) and 2025.420(b)(1), for orders (1) precluding her deposition as City Manager for the City of El Monte, (2) limiting the duration and scope of her deposition as City Manager for the City of El Monte, and (3) precluding production of documents or limiting production of documents to 10 items as City Manager for the City of El Monte. 

The Court is inclined to continue the hearing on the motion to February 19, 2025, at 9:00 a.m., for the reasons identified above.

Case Number: 24PSCV03718    Hearing Date: December 23, 2024    Dept: H

Blue Sky Utility 2021 XIII LLC, et al. v. Bright Power, Inc., Case No. 24PSCV03718

ORDER RE: EXPUNGEMENT OF LIEN

I.        Background

Plaintiffs Blue Sky Utility 2021 XIII LLC (“Blue Sky XIII”) and Blue Sky Utility LLC (“Blue Sky”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

          ROIC Diamond Hills Plaza, LLC (the “Landlord”) is the owner or reputed owner or lessee of the real property commonly known as Diamond Hills Plaza and located at 2711-2899 S. Diamond Bar Blvd. in Diamond Bar (the “Subject Property”). Plaintiffs and Landlord entered into a Site Lease Agreement (the “Lease”) dated May 6, 2021, wherein Plaintiffs leased certain premises as set forth in said Lease to own and operate a solar system at Diamond Hills Plaza.

          On March 10, 2022, Blue Sky XIII entered into a written engineering, procurement, and construction contract with Bright Power, Inc. (“BPI”), in which BPI promised to engineer, design, construct, and install a solar voltaic facility at Diamond Hills Plaza, Project ID 21-54 (the “Contract”). The scope of the work was subsequently modified and reduced by 55.6%. Plaintiffs paid BPI for the work. On September 23, 2024, BPI recorded a verified claim of lien in the amount of $1,862,616.25 (the “Claim of Lien”) on the subject property. BPI refuses to release the Claim of Lien, which places Plaintiffs at risk of an Event of Default under the Lease.

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a verified complaint, asserting causes of action against BPI and Does 1-150 for (1) mandatory and permanent injunction and (2) declaratory relief to expunge mechanic’s lien.

An Order to Show Cause Re: Expungement of Lien is set for December 23, 2024.

A Case Management Conference is set for April 16, 2025.

 

II.      Discussion

The Court has ordered BPI to show cause (1) why the Court should not declare invalid and unenforceable the Claim of Mechanic’s Lien recorded by BPI on November 15, 2024 and thereby ordered released and expunged from the subject property and (2) why the Court should not permanently enjoin BPI from recording a Claim of Mechanic’s Lien on the subject property.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

As to Plaintiffs’ Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”) filed November 25, 2024, the Court rules as follows: Granted as to Exhibit 1 (i.e., “Claim of Mechanic’s Lien” recorded September 23, 2024); Granted as to Exhibit 2 (i.e., “Mechanic’s Lien Release” recorded November 14, 2024); Granted as to Exhibit 3 (i.e., “Claim of Mechanic’s Lien” recorded November 15, 2024); and Granted as to Exhibits 4-6 (i.e., as to the declarations of Allon Raveh, Michael Medina, and Deborah Wilson filed November 8, 2024 concurrently with Plaintiffs’ Ex Parte Application #1).

As to Plaintiffs’ RJN filed December 13, 2024, the Court rules as follows: Granted (i.e., as to BPI’s complaint filed April 5, 2024 in case styled Bright Power Inc. v. Blue Sky Utilities LLC, et al., Case No., 24CV000547, without exhibits).

 

Evidentiary Objections

Turning to Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections, the Court rules as follows: (1) as to the Declaration of Martin Glickfeld, the Court sustains the objections as to Nos. 1-3, and (2) as to the Declaration of Brian Peterson, the Court overrules the objections as to Nos. 1-3, 5, and 6 and sustains the objections as to Nos. 4 and 7.

 

Merits

Plaintiff seeks relief pursuant to the Court’s equitable powers and Connolly Development, Inc. v. Superior Court (1976) 17 Cal. 3d 803, 823 [“Even after the lien has been recorded,. . . the owner in many instances could seek a mandatory injunction ordering the claimant to release the lien. . . the owner need not wait until the claimant dues to enforce the lien; the imposition of that lien, and the owner’s denial of its validity, comprise a controversy sufficient to permit an immediate suit for declaratory relief. . . by filing an action for injunctive or declaratory relief, the owner or lender can obtain a hearing either before imposition of the lien or within a reasonable period thereafter”], Lambert v. Superior Court (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 383, 387 [“If a claimant has not yet imposed a lien or, having imposed it, has not yet sued to foreclose it, an owner's speediest remedy is through proceedings for injunctive or declaratory relief”], and RGC Gaslamp LLC v. Ehmcke Sheet Metal Co., Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 413, 423 [“after recordation but before foreclosure proceedings commence, the owner may seek declaratory or injunctive relief challenging the validity of the lien”].

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 526, “an injunction may be granted in the following cases:

(1) When it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually.

(2) When it appears by the complaint or affidavits that the commission or continuance of some act during the litigation would produce waste, or great or irreparable injury, to a party to the action.

(3) When it appears, during the litigation, that a party to the action is doing, or threatens, or is about to do, or is procuring or suffering to be done, some act in violation of the rights of another party to the action respecting the subject of the action, and tending to render the judgment ineffectual.

(4) When pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief.

(5) Where it would be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount of compensation which would afford adequate relief.

(6) Where the restraint is necessary to prevent a multiplicity of judicial proceedings. . .”

BPI agrees that the issues “relating to the manner in which [a mechanic’s] lien was perfected” is an equitable issue to be decided by the court (Selby Constructors v. McCarthy (1979) 91 Cal.App.3d 517, 527) and that “the issue presently before the court is properly determined by the court pursuant to the court’s equitable powers” (Resp., 5:21-22).

Plaintiffs have proffered the following evidence in their Application and Verified Complaint:

          The Landlord is the owner or reputed owner or lessee of the real property commonly known as Diamond Hills Plaza and located at the Subject Property. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 3; Raveh Decl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiffs, as Lessees, and Landlord entered into a Site Lease Agreement (“Lease”) dated May 6, 2021, wherein Plaintiffs leased certain premises as set forth in said Lease to own and operate a solar system at Diamond Hills Plaza (“Plaza”). (Verified Complaint, ¶ 4; Raveh Decl., ¶ 2).

          On March 10, 2022, Blue Sky XIII and BPI entered into the Contract, in which BPI promised to engineer, design, construct, and install a solar voltaic facility at Diamond Hills Plaza. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 5, Exh. A; Raveh Decl., ¶ 3.) BPI was paid by Plaintiffs for the work at the Subject Property. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 5; Medina Decl., ¶ 2.)

          The last date that BPI performed work on the solar system at the Subject Property was April 24, 2024. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 6.) The solar system at the Subject Property was occupied and used by putting it into revenue service on April 24, 2024. (Id., ¶ 7; Medina Decl., ¶ 3.)

          On May 22, 2024, BPI executed a “Form of Final Completion Certificate;” this was executed by Blue Sky XIII on June 7 and 21, 2024. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 8, Exh. B; Raveh Decl., ¶ 4.) On August 22, 2024, BPI sent correspondence addressed to Blue Sky XIII with an enclosed BPI accounting spreadsheet and invoices which reflected that the “Final Completion Milestone” for the solar system at the subject property was achieved on May 21, 2024. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 9; Medina Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. E.)

          On September 23, 2024, BPI recorded a verified claim of lien in the amount of $1,862,616.25 (the “September 23, 2024 Claim of Lien”) on the subject property. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 10, Exh. C; Nov. 25, 2024 RJN, Exh. 1.)

          In an October 21, 2024 letter to Plaintiffs, the Landlord stated that the September 23, 2024 Claim of Lien was a breach of the Lease and that failure to remove same   within 30 days would constitute an Event of Default by Plaintiffs under the Lease. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 12; Raveh Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. D.) Landlord sent another letter, dated November 8, 2024, to Plaintiffs, reiterating this position. (Wilson Decl., ¶3; Exh. 2.)

          On November 14, 2024, BPI recorded a “Mechanic’s Lien Release” in connection with the September 23, 2024 Claim of Lien. (Nov. 25, 2024 RJN, Exh. 2); however, the next day, BPI recorded another lien (the “November 15, 2024 Claim of Lien”), which asserts a claim identical to that stated in the September 23, 2024 Claim of Lien. (Id., Exh. 3.) 

Plaintiff contends that the November 15, 2024 Claim of Lien is untimely and invalid and that BPI’s “inchoate right” to a mechanic’s lien on the subject property was extinguished by the “Mechanic’s Lien Release” recorded by BPI on November 14, 2024. (App., 1:10-12.)

As to the first ground, the Court agrees that the November 15, 2024 Claim of Lien is untimely and invalid. Civil Code section 8412 provides that “[a] direct contractor may not enforce a lien unless the contractor records a claim of lien after the contractor completes the direct contract, and before the earlier of the following times: (a) Ninety days after completion of the work of improvement[,] (b) Sixty days after the owner records a notice of completion or cessation.”

Pursuant to Civil Code section 8180, “completion of a work of improvement” happens “upon the occurrence” of any one of four enumerated events, including “[a]ctual completion of the work of improvement,” “[o]ccupation of use by the owner accompanied by cessation of labor,” “[c]essation of labor for a continuous period of 60 days,” or “[r]ecordation of a notice of cessation after cessation of labor for a continuous period of 30 days.” 

Again, on May 22, 2024, BPI executed a “Form of Final Completion Certificate;” this was executed by Blue Sky XIII on June 7 and 21, 2024. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 8, Exh. B; Raveh Decl., ¶ 4.) Further, on August 22, 2024, BPI sent correspondence addressed to Blue Sky XIII with an enclosed BPI accounting spreadsheet and invoices reflecting that the “Final Completion Milestone” for the solar system at the subject property was achieved on May 21, 2024. (Verified Complaint, ¶ 9; Medina Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. E.) May 21, 2024 is 178 days before November 15, 2024. 

BPI also included a handwritten “Completion Date: 6/21/2024” on its September 23, 2024 Claim of Lien. (Nov. 25, 2024 RJN, Exh. 1.) 

BPI now asserts that “[t]he last day of work on the project was July 2, 2024 and no work has been done since.” (Opp., 8:11.) BPI’s statement, however, is supported entirely by Peterson’s declaration, which is devoid of any facts regarding the work performed by BPI on July 2, 2024.

BPI further contends that the November 15, 2024 Claim of Lien is not untimely because the contract was terminated on October 10, 2024, when Blue Sky Utility filed a lawsuit against BPI in case styled Blue Sky Utility LLC, et al. v. Bright Power, Inc., Case No. 24CV001768. (Opp., 3:14-18 and 7:23-24.) BPI improperly conflates the filing of a lawsuit for breach of contract with the termination of a contract. At any rate, under BPI’s logic that the filing of a lawsuit is a termination of the contract and equal to “completion” of the contract under the mechanic’s lien statutes, Plaintiffs point out that “completion” would have occurred even earlier on April 5, 2024, when BPI sued Plaintiffs in case styled Bright Power Inc. v. Blue Sky Utilities LLC, et al., Case No. 24CV000547.

With respect to the second ground, “when a release states that the claim of lien has been fully satisfied. . . the claimant’s inchoate right to a lien, upon which any claim of lien is based, is extinguished, because then the underlying obligation has been paid according to the express terms of the release. (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1444.) The “Mechanic’s Lien Release” recorded on November 14, 2024 provides as follows:

          “The Mechanic’s Lien claimed by Bright Power, Inc. against ROIC Diamond Hills Plaza, LLC, upon the following described real property in the City of Diamond Bar, County of Los Angeles, State of California, as described as 2711 S Diamond Bar Blvd., Diamond Bar, CA 91765 is hereby fully released, the claim thereunder having been fully paid and satisfied, and that certain Notice of Mechanic’s Lien for $1,862,616.25 for engineering, designing, constructing, furnishing, and installing renewable energy system facilities recorded as Instrument No. 20240647578 on 9/23/202, of Official Records of Los Angeles County, California is hereby satisfied and released.” (Nov. 25, 2024 RJN, Exh. 2.)

BPI argues that language contained in the “Mechanic’s Lien Release” recorded November 14, 2024 that BPI’s lien claim was “fully paid and satisfied” and is hereby “satisfied and released” was made in error and has since been corrected by the recording of an Amended Release. (Peterson Decl., ¶ 15, Exh. A; Dec. 13, 2024 Supp. Glickfeld Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A.) BPI, however, has not provided the Court with authority that it may undo the effect of the November 14, 2024 release by simply recording an amended release that purports to “supersede and replace” the November 14, 2024 release.

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have suffered and will continue to suffer irreparable harm and are under imminent risk of default on the Lease as a direct result of BPI’s recording of the September 23, 2024 and November 15, 2024 Claims of Liens and that, as such, Plaintiffs are entitled to immediate relief in the form of an order expunging the November 15, 2024 Claim of Lien.

Further, the Court is inclined to impose a permanent injunction restraining BPI from recording a claim of mechanic’s lien on the subject property, based on BPI’s action in recording the November 15, 2024 Claim of Lien (the day after representing to the Court that the September 23, 2024 Claim of Lien was being released). The Court will hear further from the parties in this regard at the hearing, including as to whether a permanent injunction (as opposed to a preliminary injunction) is appropriate at this juncture, but it appears to the Court that there is no further evidence to present or develop such that a permanent injunction is appropriate.
