DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02741    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: W

LONNI LZ INC. vs GENERAL MOTORS, LLC

 

PLAINTIFF LONNI LZ INC’S MOTION FOR TERMINATING, ISSUE, EVIDENCE AND/OR MONETARY SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,750.00

 

Date of Hearing:          June 7, 2024                                        Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         23VECV02741

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc.

Responding Party:       Defendant General Motors LLC   

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc. filed a complaint against Defendant General Motors LLC on June 23, 2023 for Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Plaintiff alleges on October 16, 2022, Plaintiff purchased a 2023 Chevrolet Suburban. However, the subject vehicle has suffered from nonconformities to warranty to, including, but not limited to, the engine, pistons, check engine light, faulty valve cover gasket, and other defects.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc.’s Motion for Terminating, Issue, Evidence and/or Monetary Sanctions in the Amount of $2,750.00 is DENIED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits evidentiary objections to the declaration of Cameron Major submitted by Defendant General Motors LLC in support of its opposition to Plaintiff’s motion.

 

The court OVERRULES Plaintiff’s objections Nos. 1-3.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves this court for an order for evidence, issue, terminating, and/or monetary sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2023.010, 2023.030(a)-(d), and 2025.450(h) against Defendant General Motors, LLC preventing GM from calling witnesses at trial whom it refuses to produce at deposition, or an order that Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act claims have been proven and limiting the issues at trial to the question of damages, or striking GM’s answer and rendering judgment by default against GM based upon the persistent failure to comply with authorized methods of discovery as well as this Court’s February 26, 2024, Order. Plaintiff also moves for an order of monetary sanctions of $2,750.00 for having to bring the instant motion as well as an additional $500.00 a day in which GM violated this Court’s Order.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 empowers the Court to impose terminating, issue, and evidentiary sanctions in the event an individual engages in conduct which constitutes a “misuse of the discovery process”.  (CCP § 2023.030(b), (c), (d).)  A “misuse of the discovery process” includes failure to respond to an authorized method of discovery or disobeying a court order to provide discovery.  (CCP § 2023.010(d), (g).)  

 

Terminating sanctions should not be ordered lightly, but are justified where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and there is evidence that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules.  (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)  Lack of diligence may be deemed willful where the party understood its obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply.  (Deyo v. Killbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 787; Fred Howland Co. v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1966) 244 Cal.App.2d 605, 610-611.)  The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful.  (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 788; Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250; Evid. Code, §§ 500, 605.)

 

On February 26, 2024, this court granted Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant General Motors, LLC’s Person(s) Most Qualified, with Production of Documents and ordered GM’s Person Most Qualified (“PMQ”) to appear for deposition on or before April 26, 2024, and to produce all requested documents. On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff served its Third Amended Notice of GM’s PMQ deposition. (Hunt Decl. ¶7.) On March 20, 2024, GM wrote to Plaintiff and stated that it could produce a PMQ witness for twelve (12) separate cases between the parties on April 26, 2024. (Hunt Decl. ¶8.) However, on March 26, 2024, GM served Plaintiff with objections to the Third Amended Notice. (Hunt Decl. ¶10.) Upon receiving the objections, Plaintiff informed Defendant they had a court order for the deposition and produced a Fourth Amended Notice for April 23, 2024. (Hunt Decl. ¶¶11, 12.) GM again served objections. (Hunt Decl. ¶13.) Although not for the instant matter, the deposition did take place on April 23, 2024 for two other cases. (Major Decl. ¶6.) Plaintiff served a Fifth Amended Notice, however, GM did not object or appear in response to the Fifth Amended Notice. (Hunt Decl. ¶15.) Plaintiff then filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions.

 

Plaintiff argues this court should impose evidence, issue, or terminating sanctions as GM has demonstrated a deplorable pattern and practice of obstructive delay tactics without substantial justification and a conscious disregard for the discovery process; judicial process; this court and its orders; and every effort to move this case forward.

 

In opposition, GM argues Plaintiff has failed to meet and confer in good faith, repeatedly ignoring GM’s efforts to identify a date so that GM’s PMQ could be deposed on or before the court-ordered deadline. Despite the repeated threats of Plaintiff’s counsel; despite Plaintiff’s serving Third and Fourth Amended Deposition Notice unilaterally scheduling the deposition for dates that GM had specifically said would not work; and despite GM’s good faith efforts to work on mutually agreeable dates after entry of the Court’s Order, Plaintiff filed this Motion on April 30th, GM remains willing to provide its PMQ for deposition on the witness’s first available date.

 

The court finds evidentiary, issue, and/or terminating sanctions not warranted considering both parties have created this issue before the court. The court further finds monetary sanctions not warranted at this time. The court reminds the parties that discovery is supposed to be self-executing. Defendant cannot ignore its statutory obligation to comply with a properly noticed PMK deposition by stringing Plaintiff along then playing victim. However, Plaintiff must fully exhaust the meet and confer process rather than rushing to court, filing serial motions, and failing to consider the burden on this court to hear unnecessary and repetitive motions. The parties must work together in good faith before returning to the court with easily-resolvable discovery disputes like this one.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc.’s Motion for Terminating, Issue, Evidence and/or Monetary Sanctions in the Amount of $2,750.00 is DENIED.

 


Case Number: 23VECV03774    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: W

ISMAIL AKEL vs FARIDEH JAVAHERIPOUR, et al.

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS SERVED UPON PLAINTIFF, ISMAIL AKEL ADMITTED AND FOR SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,110.00

 

Date of Hearing:        June 7, 2024                                      Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03774

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Farideh Javaheripour  

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff Ismail Akel filed a complaint against Defendant Farideh Javaheripour and Ramin Javahery for Violation of Los Angeles Municipal Code sect. 151.10; Violation of Business and Professions Code 17200; Common Counts: Money Had and Received; Fraud; and Violation of Cal Civil Code 1947.11. Plaintiff alleges they entered into a lease agreement with Defendants to rent a property owned by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges Defendant represented that the Property was legal to be rented; however, upon taking possession and steps to arrange for US mail delivery to the Property, Plaintiff discovered the Property was an illegal structure.

 

On February 8, 2024, Farideh Javaheripour filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff for breach of written agreement and common counts.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Served upon Plaintiff Ismail Akel Admitted is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Farideh Javaheripour move the court for an order compelling Plaintiff Akel to have the genuineness of the documents and truth of matters specified in Plaintiff’s First Set of Request for Admissions be deemed admitted. Defendant also requests monetary sanctions entered against Plaintiff.

 

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the truth of any matters specified in the requests and the genuineness of any documents be deemed admitted, as well as a monetary sanction. (CCP §2033.280(b).) The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants that party relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (CCP §2033.280(a)(1)-(2).) The court shall grant the motion “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response … in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (CCP §2033.280(c); St. Mary v. Sup. Ct. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 777-78.)

 

On March 7, 2024, Defendant served on Plaintiff the first set of discovery requests which included the Request for Admissions. (Kritzer Decl. ¶2, Exh. 1.) Responses were due by April 8, 2024. (Kritzer Decl. ¶4.) As of the date of this filing, Defendant has not received any responses despite several conversations between counsel. (Kritzer Decl. ¶4.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff Akel has failed to timely respond to Defendant’s properly served discovery request. The court therefore GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Served Upon Plaintiff Akel.  

 

Sanctions

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (CCP §2033.280(c).) 

 

Defendant requests $1,110.00 in sanctions for 1 hour preparing the instant motion, another two hours preparing any reply and attending the hearing on the motion, and the $60 filing fee. Counsel’s hourly rate is $350.00.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the amount of $760.00.
