Case Number: 23STCP04228    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         October 2, 2024

Case Name:                             Robert K. Holmes v. Marcus Kellum

Case No.:                                23STCP04228

Motion:                                   Amended Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award

Moving Party:                         Petitioner Robert K. Holmes   

Responding Party:                   Unopposed  

Notice:                                    IMPROPER

 

Tentative Ruling:                    The hearing on the Amended Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award is CONTINUED to Monday, October 31, 2024 at 10:00 AM, so that Petitioner can provide Respondent with notice of the continued hearing date.  

                                               

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 22, 2023, Arbitrator Mary J. Dederick (the “Arbitrator”) issued an arbitration award (the “Award”) in the amount of $17,588.00 for Petitioner Robert K. Holmes (“Petitioner”) and against Respondent Marcus Kellum (“Respondent”).

 

On November 17, 2023, Petitioner filed a Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award (the “Petition”).  

 

On March 22, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the Petition to June 6, 2024, to allow Petitioner to correct numerous deficiencies in the Petition. (03/22/24 Minute Oder at p. 6.) The Court indicated that Petitioner failed to attach a copy of the attorney-client agreement and did not set forth the substance of the arbitration provision in accordance with CCP § 1285.4(a), or otherwise deny the existence of an attorney-client agreement. (03/22/24 Minute Order at p. 4.) The Court also noted that Petitioner did not provide a proof of service indicating that both the Petition and notice of the hearing were served on Respondent, and therefore Petitioner did not satisfy CCP § 1290.4. (03/22/24 Minute Order at p. 5.)

 

On June 6, 2024, after hearing, the Court continued the hearing on the Petition to July 23, 2024, so that Petitioner could cure the service defect. (06/06/24 Minute Order at pp. 1-2.) Petitioner was ordered to give notice. (06/06/24 Minute Order at p. 2.)

 

On June 7, 2024, the Court issued a nunc pro tunc order, which indicated, in part, that the hearing on the Petition was continued from June 6, 2024 to September 11, 2024, and that Petitioner represented that he intended to submit an Application for Service by Publication as well as an Application and proposed Order to Post. (06/07/24 Minute Order.)[1]

 

On June 7, 2024, the Clerk’s Office mailed the Court’s nunc pro tunc order to Petitioner.

 

On June 18, 2024, Petitioner filed an Application for Publication, which was rejected on June 21, 2024, on the grounds that Petitioner needed to provide facts and evidence to substantiate the need to serve by publication. (See 06/21/24 Notice of Rejection of Electronic Filing.)

 

On July 23, 2024, due to the Court’s emergency closure and connectivity issues, the Court, on its own motion, continued the hearing on the Petition to August 27, 2024 at 10:00 AM. ANALYSIS

 

I.          Confirming the Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award    

A.                Legal Standard

“Any party to an arbitration award in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct, or vacate the award.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1285.) “Once a petition to confirm an award is filed, the superior court must select one of only four courses of action: It may confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.” EHM Productions, Inc. v Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc. (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063. “[I]t is the general rule that, with narrow exceptions, an arbitrator’s decision cannot be reviewed for errors of fact or law.” Ibid.  

“A petition under this chapter shall: (a) Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of the agreement. (b) Set forth the names of the arbitrators. (c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1285.4.) The petition must also “name as respondents all parties to the arbitration and may name as respondents any other persons bound by the arbitration award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.) “A response to a petition under this chapter may request the court to dismiss the petition or to confirm, correct or vacate the award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.2.)

Code Civ. Proc § 1283.6 provides that “[t]he neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.” California Rules of Court, rule 3.825(b)(1) provides that the arbitrator must file the award with the clerk, with proof of service on each party to the arbitration. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.825(b)(1).) “A party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by filing and serving a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served on the petitioner.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.)

Code of Civil Procedure § 1290.4, the statute governing proper service of the Amended Petition states, in pertinent part:

“(a) A copy of the petition and written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.

(b) If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision: ¶ (1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action; ¶ (2) Service outside this State shall be made by mailing a copy of the petition and notice of hearing and other papers by registered or certified mail. Personal service is the equivalent of such service by mail. Proof of service by mail shall be made by affidavit showing such mailing together with the return receipt of the United States Post Office bearing the signature of the person on whom service was made.”

B.        Discussion  

Petitioner has satisfied the requirements of CCP § 1285.4. Petitioner has attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate. (Amended Petition, p. 2; Amended Petition at Ex. A.) Petitioner has stated the Arbitrator’s name, Mary J. Dederick, and attaches a copy of the Award. (Amended Petition, p. 2 at ¶ 4; Attachment 6(c).) The Award provides that the Petitioner is to be awarded the amount totaling $17,588.20 representing the difference between the $22,588.20 in fees and/or costs which should have been charged in the matter, and $5,000.00 paid by Respondent. (Amended Petition at Attachment 6(a) at p. 9:2-17.) Petitioner has now attached a copy of the attorney-client agreement. (Amended Petition at Ex. A.) Petitioner has therefore complied with Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4.

Here, included with the Amended Petition is a copy of the proof of service indicating that the Arbitrator served the Award by mail on both Petitioner and Respondent on October 2, 2023. The Amended Petition was filed on August 1, 2024, which is more than ten days after the award was served on Petitioner.

Petitioner, however, has not complied with CCP § 1290.4. Despite being ordered by the Court to provide notice of the continued hearing on the Petition, Petitioner did not file a proof of service showing effectuation of such notice. (See 07/23/24 Minute Order.)

Moreover, the Cout cannot vacate or correct the Award unless Respondent has been given reasonable notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.4, subd. (b)(2).) Here, the Amended Petition is unopposed; however, the Court notes that Petitioner has not filed a proof of service showing that notice of the continued hearing was provided to Respondent. The Proof of Service filed with the Court is defective since it states that “Demadn for Production and Inspection of Documents and Tangible Things Set One” was served on August 27, 2024. 

All moving and supporting papers must be filed at least 16 court days before the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

Based on the foregoing, the Court cannot grant the Amended Petition as the Court must ensure that Respondent is afforded proper notice.

II.        Conclusion

           

            The Court therefore CONTINUES the hearing on the Amended Petition to Monday, October 31, 2024 at 10:00 AM so that Petitioner can provide proper notice of the hearing date on the Amended Petition.

BACKGROUND

 

On September 6, 2023, Plaintiff Croft Plaza Apartments Homes LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this unlawful detainer action against Defendant William Mochon (“Defendant”).

 

Defendant filed his answer on October 6, 2023 stating that the action should be dismissed as Defendant will vacate the property by November 1, 2023.

 

On December 27, 2023, the Court found that possession was no longer at issue and the case was reassigned to an Independent Calendar Court.

 

On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment, which was denied without prejudice on July 10, 2024 due to untimely notice.

 

On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for summary judgment.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on the basis that Defendant owes monthly rent to Plaintiff, vacated the subject property, and did not assert any affirmative defenses.

 

OPPOSITION

 

             No opposition has been filed.

 

REPLY

 

             No reply has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Legal Standard

In unlawful detainer actions, Code of Civil Procedure § 1170.7 provides that “A motion for summary judgment may be made at any time after the answer is filed upon giving five days notice. Summary judgment shall be granted or denied on the same basis as a motion under Section 437c.” 

Here, the issue of possession has become moot, and the case has become an “ordinary civil action” in which the landlord may seek damages. (Motion, 5:18-22; Fish Construction Co. v. Moselle Coach Works, Inc. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 645, 658.) As this is no longer an unlawful detainer case, but an ordinary civil action, there is insufficient notice. CCP section 437c(a)(2) provides that “[n]otice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(2); see Cuff v. Grossmont Union High School Dist. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 582, 596.) If the notice is served by mail to an address within the State of California, the 75-day period is increased by 5 days. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(2).) The Court lacks power to change the notice and filing periods of section 437c. (See McMahon v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 112, 115-18; see also Urshan v. Musicians’ Credit Union (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 758, 765-66 (discussing McMahon and reversing judgment for failure to provide notice by the statutorily required minimum time period); Hernandez v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 285, 299.) Parties may stipulate to waive the statutorily mandated minimum notice period. (See Credit Suisse First Boston Mortgage Capital v. Danning, Gill, Diamond & Kollitz (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1290, 1301; Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1014.)

The case qualifies for § 1179a trial precedence only when possession is in issue (CCP § 1179a). Thus, if the tenant has permanently vacated (returned possession to the landlord) after commencement of the unlawful detainer, the action is not triable on the “summary” calendar and not entitled to precedence under CCP § 1179a; it becomes an “ordinary civil action.” (Civ.C. § 1952.)

This is supported by the rationales in Fish Construction Co., supra, and Lynch, supra, which Plaintiff cites to in its moving papers, as the summary procedures in an unlawful detainer action has been removed and the action is proceeding as an ordinary civil action for rent. Accordingly, the time period for a motion for summary judgment on the issue of the rent owed and not possession would follow the ordinary civil time period rather than that of an unlawful detainer action.

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(p)(1), a plaintiff or cross-complainant has the initial burden on summary judgment or adjudication of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action. Once that burden is met, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.

 

On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party’s supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion. (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21; Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)

 

Furthermore, on summary judgment, the issues are defined by the pleadings. (See Physicians Comm. For Responsible Medicine v. McDonald’s Corp. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 554, 568 [“the issues framed by the pleadings are the only issues a motion for summary judgment must address.”]; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., ITD., v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472; Bostrom v. County of San Bernardino (1995) 35 Cal. App. 4th 1654, 1663; 580 Folsom Assocs. v. Prometheus Dev. Co. (1990) 223 Cal. App. 3d 1, 14, 18 [burden to address theories “reasonably contemplated by the opponent’s pleading”]; Melican v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2007) 151 Cal. App. 4th 168, 182 [not requiring party to negate an unstated claim].)

 

Courts cannot decide summary judgment or adjudication motions based upon credibility. (AARTS Prods., Inc. v. Crocker Nat’l Bank (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 1061, 1064 [“[T]he role of the court in summary judgment proceedings is not to weigh the evidence, but to determine whether there exists a triable issue of material fact.”]; Habitat Trust for Wildlife, Inc. v. City of Rancho Cucamonga (2009) 175 Cal. App. 4th 1306, 1342.) That said, the affidavits and declarations of the moving party are strictly construed and those of the opponent are liberally constructed. (Howe v. Pioneer Mfg Co. (1968) 262 Cal. App. 2d 330, 336-37.)

II.        Discussion

Plaintiff argues that there are no disputed material facts in the case. Plaintiff is asserting a single cause of action against Defendant for monetary damages. The case initially involved unlawful detainer but transformed into a civil action for damages once Defendant vacated the property, rendering the issue of possession moot. An unlawful detainer is effectively ended if, prior to trial, the issue of possession has become moot because the tenant has delivered possession to the landlord. In such event, the case becomes an “ordinary civil action” in which the landlord may seek damages under Civ. Code § 1951.2, as well as any other appropriate relief. (Civ. Code § 1952.3(a)]; Fish Const. Co. v. Moselle Coach Works, Inc. (1983) 148 CA3d 654, 658.)

Plaintiff argues that defenses related to the unlawful detainer notice are not applicable in a civil action for monetary damages. In Lynch & Freytag v. Cooper (1990) 218 Cal.3d 603, the landlord sued in unlawful detainer, for possession, rent, and attorney fees, but the case was converted from unlawful detainer to a civil action after the issue of possession was withdrawn. At trial, the landlord was awarded rent and attorney’s fees, and the Court of Appeal affirmed, finding that the conversion was proper as the tenant was not prejudiced in any way. Similarly, here, Plaintiff states that Defendant relinquished possession during the unlawful detainer proceedings, causing the case to transform into an ordinary civil action under Civil Code § 1952.3, where Plaintiff is seeking only damages under Civil Code § 1951.2. Consequently, any defects in the three-day notice to pay rent or quit, or its service, are not valid defenses since the service of the pay or quit notice was withdrawn. Plaintiff further asserts that it has satisfied the burden by establishing standing as the owner of the property, allowing it to prosecute this action as the real party in interest.

Additionally, Plaintiff states it has demonstrated that Defendant had a rental agreement, failed to pay the monthly rent of $3,000 due in July 2023, and vacated the premises in November 2023.  The evidence before the court establishes the following material facts.  Plaintiff is owner of the of the residential real property located at 832 N. Croft Avenue, #106, Los
Angeles, California 90069. (Wilson Decl. ¶2, Ex. 1). Defendant William Mochon occupied the Premises pursuant to a written rental agreement. (Wilson Decl. ¶3, Ex. 1). The monthly rent for premises was $3,000 due on the 1st of the month. (Wilson Decl. at ¶4, Ex.1). Defendant failed to pay the monthly rent that came due for July 2023. (Wilson Decl. at ¶5). Defendant vacated the Premises on 11/16/2023 and did not leave a forwarding address. (Wilson Decl. at ¶6).  The reasonable value of the rent for the Premises is $100/day. (Wilson Decl. at ¶7).

Additionally, Plaintiff argues that affirmative defenses in an unlawful detainer action must be supported by factual allegations, and it is the defendant’s responsibility to establish these defenses by a preponderance of the evidence, as per Westernland Office Service v. Cerbantes (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 724, 742. Although detailed factual allegations are not required, the defendant must provide enough information to give the plaintiff notice to prepare for the defense. Here, Defendant failed to raise any affirmative defenses or provide factual support.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated there are no triable issues of material fact in the case, and that Plaintiff has established its case in chief and is entitled to monetary damages. The Court finds that defenses related to the unlawful detainer notice are inapplicable in this civil action for monetary damages. The case law cited by Plaintiff, including Lynch & Freytag v. Cooper, supports this position, confirming that the withdrawal of the unlawful detainer issue does not prejudice Defendant and that the conversion to a civil action was proper. Plaintiff has established its standing as the property owner, giving it the legal capacity to prosecute the case as the real party in interest. Furthermore, Plaintiff has provided uncontested evidence that Defendant had a rental agreement, failed to pay the $3,000 rent due in July 2023, and vacated the premises in November 2023, and Defendant has not produced any evidence to dispute these material facts.

Furthermore, proper notice was provided pursuant to CCP section 437c(a)(2).

Given these findings, the Court grants summary judgment in favor of Plaintiff.
