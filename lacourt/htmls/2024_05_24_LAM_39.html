DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV28289    Hearing Date: May 24, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

May 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER

19STCV28189

MOTION

Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant BDB Properties, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff SoCal Lien Solutions

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff SoCal Lien Solutions (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant BDB Properties, Inc. (“Defendant”) to collect sums due for construction work.  In its order of September 2, 2022, the Court entered judgment by default against Defendant.  Defendant moves to set aside the default judgment Plaintiff took against Defendant.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.  The Court previously considered this motion and, in its order of January 17, 2024, continued the matter for further briefing.

DISCUSSION

“There are four grounds which a court, utilizing its equity capacity may rely upon to provide relief from a default. Those areas are (1) void judgment, (2) extrinsic fraud, (3) constructive service, and (4) extrinsic mistake.”  (Stiles v. Wallis (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 1143, 1147.)  The Court may set aside the default judgment as void if Defendant did not have “actual or constructive notice” of the proceedings.  (Lovato v. Santa Fe Internat. Corp. (1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 549, 553.)

Plaintiff served Defendant via the Secretary of State.  A plaintiff may serve summons on a corporation via the Secretary of State if the plaintiff obtains a court order based on a factual showing that the corporate defendant has failed to file with the Secretary of State the annual statement of names and addresses of corporate officers and agents for service of process required by Corporations Code section 1502, or that the designated officers or agents cannot be found after diligent search.  (Corps. Code, § 1702, subd. (a).) 

In its order of June 8, 2022, the Court granted Plaintiff’s application to serve Defendant via the Secretary of State.  Plaintiff served the Secretary of State on June 20, 2022.  (See Notice of Lodging Record of Service of Process, filed December 28, 2023.)  Defendant thereafter had until August 1, 2022 to respond.  However, the return from the Secretary of State shows that the Secretary of State inexplicably did not serve Defendant until October 24, 2022, after Plaintiff took judgment by default against Defendant.  See Notice of Lodging Record of Service of Process, filed December 28, 2023.)  It therefore appears that Defendant did not receive actual or constructive notice of this action in time to respond.  The Court, therefore, sets aside the default and default judgment as void. 

Defendant also seeks relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5.  A defendant must seek relief under section 473.5 within two years after entry of default judgment or 180 days after service of written notice that default or default judgment has been entered, whichever comes first.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).)  Plaintiff served Defendant with written notice of the entry of judgment on September 6, 2022.  Plaintiff had until March 10, 2023 to seek relief under section 473.5.  Plaintiff’s motion for relief under section 473.5 is, therefore, untimely.

Plaintiff argues that Defendant cannot seek relief based on Koski v. U-Haul Co. (1963) 212 Cal.App.2d 640.  In that case, the Court held, “service upon the Secretary of State is permissible . . . , and when such service is properly made, it is personal service and relief is not available to the corporation by motion under section 473a of the Code of Civil Procedure.”  (Id. at p. 645.)  Here, however, Defendant shows that the Secretary of State did not complete service.  Pursuant to Corporations Code section 1702, “the Secretary of State shall give notice of the service of the process to the corporation at its principal office, by forwarding to such office, by registered mail with request for return receipt, the copy of the process . . . .”  (Corp. Code, § 1702, subd. (b), emphasis added.)  Further, “[t]he certificate of the Secretary of State, under the Secretary of State's official seal, certifying to . . . the giving of notice thereof to the corporation and the forwarding of such process pursuant to this section, shall be competent and prima facie evidence of the matters stated therein.”  (Id. at subd. (c).)  While Plaintiff contends the statutory scheme for service of process via the Secretary of State does not require the Secretary of State to serve Defendant to complete service of process, these provisions make it clear the Secretary of State must give Defendant notice of the proceedings by certified mail for service to be effective.  Defendant has shown the Secretary of State did not timely do so.  As such, Defendant has shown it did not receive proper notice of this action.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The motion is granted.  The Court vacates the default and default judgment taken against Defendant.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of such.


Case Number: 23STCV25848    Hearing Date: May 24, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

May 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV25848

MOTIONS

Demurrer to Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendants RF Renovo Management Company, LLC, Daniel Rosen, Kevin Werner, and Brendan Ochinero

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Megan Blair O’Brien

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Megan Blair O’Brien (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants RF Renovo Management Company, LLC (“RF Renovo”), Daniel Rosen (“Rosen”), Kevin Werner (“Werner”) and Brendan Ochinero (“Ochinero,” collectively, “Defendants”) for employment claims.  Defendants demur to the second, fifth, seventh, eighth, tenth, eleventh, twelfth, and thirteenth causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint.  Defendants also move to strike the punitive damages allegations from the complaint.

 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Plaintiff’s second cause of action is for hostile work environment harassment against RF  Renovo and Rosen.  (See Complaint, ¶ 62.)  To state a claim for hostile work environment harassment, Plaintiff must allege that RF Renovo and Rosen engaged in severe or pervasive harassment that unreasonably interfered with Plaintiff’s work performance.  (See Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 877.)  Here, Plaintiff alleges, “Rosen routinely made comments to [Plaintiff] that were charged with gender-based animus and gender stereotyping, including: Warning O’Brien that ‘a more quiet way up the leader board will go a long way;’ After a meeting, in response to [Plaintiff]’s request for access to a lead generator to which male employees had access and about which they were contemporaneously talking, yelling at [Plaintiff], ‘you speak when you’re spoken to!’; telling [Plaintiff] in team meetings that her successes were only a result of luck; commanding [Plaintiff], ‘you don’t do anything until I tell you to do it!’; Frequently screaming at [Plaintiff] while sparing male employees from such hostile behavior; [¶] Rosen repeatedly suggested to [Plaintiff] that she needed ‘mental help’ or similar from June 2022 forward;  [¶] [RF Renovo] and Rosen failed to send [Plaintiff] a single referral during her first ten months on the job, while they routinely provided multiple weekly leads to their male regional directors and loan officers during the same time period; [¶] Rosen refused to grant [Plaintiff] access to vital industry resources that [RF Renovo] provided to male employees, including ‘Black Knight,’ a lead generating tool; [¶]  Rosen barred [Plaintiff] from obtaining a California Broker’s License, a credential that Rosen knew another executive had recommended to [Plaintiff] that she obtain, and that Rosen allowed Tony Hayeslip, a California-based employee, who was ultimately given [Plaintiff’s] business, to obtain, and that Brendan Ochinero, another California regional director who is a man, had already obtained.  [¶]  Rosen told [Plaintiff] that she needed to boost her marketing and prospecting efforts, while he simultaneously cut her meetings with the marketing team, denied her access to team members who are vital [and] thwarted her efforts at marketing herself online, refused her access to the marketing tool that was available to men in her position, and created a flyer with [Plaintiff’s] likeness and list of credentials and an email address whose contacts were purposely funneled to Tony Hayeslip and Brendan Ochinero, to whom Rosen later gave [Plaintiff]’s promised and established territory. [Plaintiff’s] male counterparts were not subject to such actions; [¶] Rosen ignored [Plaintiff]’s efforts to reach him so that he could approve deals, worth several million dollars, that she was near closing, interfering with prospective profits and damaging her business relationships.  When pressed, Rosen explained that he simply was ‘having a bad day,’ and, on another instance, that he ‘did not want to deal’ with [Plaintiff] at the time.”  (Complaint, ¶ 20.)

  Plaintiff further alleges, “On one occasion, Werner told [P]laintiff that his residence of Chicago had more stringent COVID-related policies in place ‘because we have a Black dyke as a mayor.’  [¶] In a group meeting, Rosen openly mocked an Afghan-American woman employee’s name: ‘What kind of parents would name you Horia?!’  In another meeting, Rosen told the same employee: ‘How can I put this?  They're not bad locations, but I wouldn't feel comfortable living there.  You probably would though.’  [¶] On May 10, 2022, Werner, Rosen, O’Brien, and other [RF Renovo] employees went out on the town to socialize after a day of training.  Throughout the evening, which began with a happy hour and progressed through other bars, a male lender approached woman after woman after woman, to the visible amusement of other male [RF Renovo] employees.  Later that night, an employee told the group, including the lender, that a busy two-story McDonald’s restaurant they were passing was known locally as ‘Club McDonald’s’ because it was filled most nights with underage girls who could not yet get into bars.  The next morning, sitting around a packed conference room table inside Company headquarters, the lender and other male employees loudly celebrated the revelation that the lender had visited and left the McDonald’s with two young women who had attended a Justin Bieber concert earlier that night.  Upon hearing this news, Rosen rose from his chair and gave the lender a celebratory high-five, laughing, and gleefully congratulating him on the accomplishment.  [¶] In a mid-August group meeting, Rosen told the remaining sales team the Company was no longer going to use recruiters to find new employees, as they had done in hiring a then recently departed woman employee.  Rosen made clear that he did not want to repeat such hires.  He urged the employees present in the meeting to look within their former companies to ‘find other guys that fit into this culture.’  All but two of the twenty meeting attendees were men.  ‘Look around you,’ Rosen instructed.  ‘Make sure they fit into our group. Make sure they look like the guy next to you.’  [Plaintiff] reasonably believed that the comment was discriminatory in nature, as both [P]laintiff and the recently terminated woman employee were hired through the recruiting process.  [¶] Rosen scolded other women employees and diminished their professional accomplishments in meetings.”  (Complaint, ¶ 21.) 

The court cannot say, as a matter of law, Plaintiff has not pled conduct amounting to severe or pervasive harassment.  Therefore, for pleading purposes, Plaintiff’s allegations suffice, and the court overrules the demurrer to the second cause of action.

Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is for retaliation under Labor Code section 1102.5 against RF Renovo, Rosen and Werner.  Rosen and Werner demur to the fifth cause of action.  By its terms, Labor Code section 1102.5 provides for an action against an employer.  (See Mathews v. Happy Valley Conference Center, Inc. (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 236, 266-267.)  As Plaintiff concedes, Rosen and Warner were not Plaintiff’s employer; RF Renovo employed Plaintiff.  (Complaint, ¶ 13.)  Accordingly, the court sustains the demurrer to the fifth cause of action against Rosen and Werner without leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action is under Labor Code section 1197.5, which is the Equal Pay Act.  Pursuant to the statute, an employer cannot discriminate in wages based on the sex, race, or ethnicity of employees, if the wages are “for substantially similar work, when viewed as a composite of skill, effort, and responsibility, and performed under similar working conditions.”  (Lab. Code, § 1197.5(a), (b).)  Plaintiff alleges, “[RF Renovo] failed to pay [Plaintiff] her wages due on November 30, 2022 and December 15, 2022, failed to pay [Plaintiff] the full amount of her incentive compensation throughout 2022, and intentionally conspired to divert [Plaintiff]’s business and exclusive geographic area to its favored male lenders for the purpose of depriving O’Brien of her income and terminating her.”  (Complaint, ¶ 56.)  Plaintiff does not allege that RF Renovo failed to pay Plaintiff the same amount as it paid other employees who performed substantially similar work as Plaintiff.  As such, Plaintiff fails to state a claim under Labor Code 1197.5.  The court sustains the demurrer to the seventh cause of action with leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action is for conversion against Defendants.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants “wrongfully appropriated and used [Plaintiff’s] book of business and business reputation at little or no cost to them.”  (Complaint, ¶ 60.)  “[T]here can be a conversion of intangible rights represented by special instruments . . . .”  (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 212.)  Here, however, Plaintiff claims Defendants misused interests that do not have a readily ascertainable value.  These are not proper subjects for a claim of conversion. 

In opposition, Plaintiff cites Palm Springs-La Quinta Development Co. v. Kieberk Corp. (1941) 46 Cal.App.2d 234 (Palm Springs-La Quinta).  In that case, the court upheld a claim for conversion “for destroying and appropriating tangible personal property consisting of a cabinet of lead cards containing the names and valuable information regarding prospective and actual purchasers of real property, contrary to the express terms of a written contract.”  (Id. at p. 240.)  The court distinguished a claim for conversion of “good-will of a business in a specified district . . . ,” which would not be actionable.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff’s allegations Defendants misappropriated Plaintiff’s business reputation and book of business are more akin to the goodwill of a business than to the tangible personal property at issue in Palm Springs-La Quinta Development Co. v. Kieberk Corp. (1941) 46 Cal.App.2d 234.  If Plaintiff contends Defendants misappropriated tangible personal property, Plaintiff must so allege.  The court sustains the demurrer to the eighth cause of action with leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s tenth cause of action is for unjust enrichment against Defendants.  Unjust enrichment is ““a general principle,”” not a cause of action.  (Melchior v. New Line Productions, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal. App. 4th 779, 793.) The court sustains the demurrer to the tenth cause of action without leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s eleventh cause of action is under Business and Professions Code section 17200 against Defendants.  Plaintiff must allege a claim under Business and Professions Code section 17200 “with reasonable particularity . . . .”  (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)  Plaintiff alleges, “Defendants engaged in unlawful, unfair, and deceptive business practices when they misappropriated [Plaintiff’s] book of business, used her likeness and labor without just compensation, and damaged her business reputation and relationships with her clients.”  (Complaint, ¶ 74.)  Plaintiff’s allegations are not sufficiently specific.  Plaintiff must allege with specificity which of Defendants’ actions she contends violated Business and Professions Code section 17200.  Accordingly, the court sustains the demurrer to the eleventh cause of action with leave to amend. 

Plaintiff’s twelfth cause of action is for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage against Defendants.  To state a cause of action in that regard, Plaintiff must allege an economic relationship exists between Plaintiff and a third party with the probability of future economic benefit to Plaintiff, Defendants’ knowledge of the relationship, Defendants’ intentional acts to disrupt the relationship that result in actual disruption of the relationship, and resulting harm to Plaintiff.  (Salma v. Capon (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 1275, 1290.)  Here, Plaintiff alleges that she “was in an economic relationship with her business contacts and prospective clients that likely would have resulted in economic benefit to [Plaintiff].  Defendants knew of those relationships but nevertheless engaged in wrongful conduct, including conversion of [Plaintiff’s] book of business, that they intended to disrupt those relationships or knew that disruption of those relationships were certain or substantially certain to occur.”  (Complaint, ¶¶ 76-77.)  Plaintiff does not adequately allege the nature of the relationship that Plaintiff had with her business contacts and prospective clients.  “[A]n essential element of the tort of intentional interference with prospective business advantage is the existence of a business relationship with which the tortfeasor interfered.  Although this need not be a contractual relationship, an existing relationship is required.”  (Roth v. Rhodes (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 530, 546, internal citation omitted.)  A mere hope of future business with Plaintiff’s business contacts and prospective clients is not actionable.  The court, therefore, sustains the demurrer to the twelfth cause of action with leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s thirteenth cause of action is for fraud in the inducement against RF Renovo and Werner.  A party must allege fraud with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.)  Fraud in the inducement is a claim that a party intended to enter into a contract, but fraud induced the party’s consent to that contract.  (Brown v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2008) 168 Cal. App. 4th 938, 958.)  Plaintiff alleges, to induce her to work for RF Renovo, Werner, as RF Renovo’s chief executive officer, promised her she “would serve as the exclusive managing director of L.A., and that anyone in the L.A. lending team would report to her in subordinate positions;  [¶] [RF Renovo] would funnel leads and referrals to [Plaintiff], to expand her book of business and, as explicitly stated in its offer letter, back her ‘with capital, team support, marketing air cover, and technology, to help maximize the success of [her] business;’ [¶] [RF Renovo] funds its own loans, because it is financially secure; [¶] [and] neither Werner nor his businesses had ‘ever been sued,’ a claim offered as evidence of the integrity and trustworthiness of him and his business operations.”  (Complaint, ¶ 16.) 

To be actionable as fraud, a misrepresentation must be of fact.  (Civ. Code, § 1710, subd. (1).)  Werner’s alleged statements of plans for Plaintiff’s work with RF Renovo are not statements of fact.  While Werner’s alleged statements that RF Renovo and Werner had never been sued and RF Renovo funds its own loans are statements of fact, Plaintiff does not allege how those statements were false, or how those statements induced Plaintiff to work for RF Renovo.  The court, therefore, sustains the demurrer to the thirteenth cause of action with leave to amend.

The Court likewise grants the motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages with leave to amend.  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to  Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Plaintiff does not allege any specific facts that constitute malicious conduct, which could support an award of punitive damages.  (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶¶ 83-84.)  Accordingly, to the extent it is not moot, the Court grants the motion to strike with leave to amend.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The demurrer by Rosen and Werner to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action in the complaint is sustained without leave to amend.  The demurrer to the second cause of action in Plaintiff’s complaint is overruled.  Defendants’ demurrer is otherwise sustained with leave to amend.

To the extent it is not moot, Defendants’ motion to strike is granted with leave to amend. 

Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 10 days of notice of this order. 

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of such.


Case Number: BC396756    Hearing Date: May 24, 2024    Dept: 39

CASE NUMBER

MOTION

Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant Florian Hamm

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Pricaspian Development Corporation

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Pricaspian Development Corporation (“Plaintiff”) obtained a default judgment against Defendant Florian Hamm (“Defendant”).  The court entered the default judgment on January 6, 2012.  Defendant moves to set aside the default judgment Plaintiff took against Defendant.  Plaintiff opposes the motion. 

DISCUSSION

“There are four grounds which a court, utilizing its equity capacity may rely upon to provide relief from a default. Those areas are (1) void judgment, (2) extrinsic fraud, (3) constructive service, and (4) extrinsic mistake.”  (Stiles v. Wallis (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 1143, 1147.)  The Court may set aside the default judgment as void if Defendant did not have “actual or constructive notice” of the proceedings.  (Lovato v. Santa Fe Internat. Corp. (1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 549, 553.)

Here, Plaintiff served Defendant by publication.  Defendant contends service was improper.  The default judgment is void if Plaintiff did not properly serve Defendant.  (Gibble v. Car-Lene Research, Inc. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 295, 313.)  Unlike other service methods, a plaintiff must obtain a court order prior to serving a defendant via publication. Strict compliance with the statutory requirements is required.  (County of Riverside v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 443, 450.)  To obtain an order for publication, the plaintiff must show reasonable diligence in serving the defendant by some other manner.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.50, subd. (a).)  The plaintiff must establish reasonable diligence via the testimony of an individual with personal knowledge of the efforts to serve the defendant.  (Olvera v. Olvera (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 32, 42.)  Likewise, the plaintiff must establish that a cause of action exists against the defendant via the testimony of an individual with personal knowledge of the underlying facts.  (Harris v. Cavasso (1977) 68 Cal.App.3d 723, 726.)

In determining whether a judgment is void for lack of proper service, the Court considers only “the summons, with the affidavit or proof of service . . . .”  (Kremerman v. White (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 358, 370-371.)  Here, Defendant’s arguments that Plaintiff should have located Defendant to serve him, that Plaintiff did not select the newspaper most likely to provide actual notice to Defendant, and that Plaintiff failed to prove damages exceed the record before the court and rely on extrinsic evidence.  As such, Defendant was required to raise this argument within two years.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, sub. (a); Rogers v. Silverman (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1114, 1123.)  Defendant failed to do so, and, therefore, those are not valid grounds to grant defendant’s motion. 

However, Defendant also argues the declaration in support of Plaintiff’s application to serve Defendant by publication was defective, because it does not state a cause of action against Defendant.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 415.50, subd. (a).)  The declaration must state the facts of the cause of action against Defendant based on personal knowledge.  (Harris v. Cavasso (1977) 68 Cal.App.3d 723, 726.)  In support of Plaintiff’s application to serve Defendant by publication, Plaintiff advanced only the declaration of his counsel, R. Jeffrey Neer (“Counsel”).  (See Exhibit A to Motion to Set Aside and Vacate Default and Default Judgment.)  Counsel failed to address the underlying facts of Plaintiff’s claim.  Further, Counsel lacked personal knowledge of the underlying facts.  As such, Plaintiff’s application to serve Defendant by publication was defective.  The court grants the motion to set aside the default and default judgment as void.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The motion is granted.  The court vacates the default and default judgment taken against Defendant.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of such.
