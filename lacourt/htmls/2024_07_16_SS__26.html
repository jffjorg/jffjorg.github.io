Case Number: 21STLC05178    Hearing Date: July 16, 2024    Dept: 26

Westlake Services, LLC v. JV Auto-Sales, et al.

MOTION FOR WAGE GARNISHMENT ORDER

AGAINST JUDGMENT DEBTOR’S SPOUSE

(CCP § 706.120 et seq.)

 


REPLY: None filed as of July 12, 2024.

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Judgment Creditor Westlake Services, LLC’s Motion Attaching the Wage Earnings and Other Assets of Rotha Valdovinos is DENIED.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff American Contractors Indemnity Company (“Judgment Creditor”) filed the instant action for breach of contract against Defendant Cristian Valdovinos Aka Cris Valdovinos Villalobos Aka Cristian A. Villalobos, Aka Christian Valdovinos Aka Cristian A. Valdovinos Villalobos (“Judgment Debtor”) and others on July 14, 2021. On December 14, 2022, default judgment was entered in favor Judgment Creditor and against Judgment Debtor. Judgment Creditor filed the instant Motion Attaching the Wage Earnings and Other Assets of Rotha Valdovinos on March 5, 2024. The Motion initially came for hearing on May 14, 2024 and was continued for further briefing. (Minute Order, 05/14/24.) Judgment Creditor filed and served a supplemental declaration on June 28, 2024. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

Discussion

 

“An earnings withholding order may not be issued against the earnings of the spouse of the judgment debtor except by court order upon noticed motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 706.109.) Family Code section 903, subdivision (a) provides that in the case of a contract, a debt is incurred “at the time the contract is made” and subdivision (b) provides that in the case of a tort, a debt is incurred “at the time the tort occurs.” (Fam. Code, § 903, subds. (a)-(b).) Family Code section 910, subdivision (a) provides that “[e]xcept as otherwise expressly provided by statute, the community estate is liable for a debt incurred by either spouse before or during marriage, regardless of which spouse had the management and control of the property and regardless of whether one or both spouses are parties to the debt or to a judgment for the debt.” (Fam. Code, § 910, subd. (a).)

 

Judgment Creditor contends that Judgment Debtor is married to Rotha Valdovinos (“Rotha”). Motion, Frischer Decl., ¶2 and Exhs. 1-2.) This is demonstrated by copies of a grant deed from the Fresno County Recorder and a property detail report showing a transfer of property to Judgment Debtor and Rotha as husband and wife in October 2023. (Ibid.) The Court finds this evidence demonstrates the spousal relationship between Judgment Debtor and Rotha prior to entry of the judgment in this action, such that an earnings withholding order may be issued against Rotha’s earnings.

 

The supplemental declaration states that Rotha is employed as a packer by Pactiv Corporation of Fresno, California. (Motion, Supp. Frischer Decl., Exh. 2.) In support of this contention, Judgment Creditor attaches a copy of Rotha’s First California Federal Credit Union Membership Application & Account Agreement, listing Pactiv Corporation as their employer. (Ibid.) That Application and Account Agreement, however, was completed by Rotha on April 30, 2021, more than three years ago. Indeed, it was completed before this action was filed or default judgment was entered against Judgment Debtor. The evidence of Rotha’s employment, therefore, is not sufficiently timely to support the issuance of an earnings withholding order under Code of Civil Procedure section 706.109.

 

Conclusion

 

Judgment Creditor Westlake Services, LLC’s Motion Attaching the Wage Earnings and Other Assets of Rotha Valdovinos is DENIED.

Case Number: 22STLC08613    Hearing Date: July 16, 2024    Dept: 26

Nissanoff v. Oscar Health Plan of Cal., et al.

MOTIONS TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES

(CCP § 2030.300)

 


 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Jonathan Nissanoff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, is DENIED AS TO NOS. 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11-14, 18-19 AND GRANTED AS TO NOS. 4, 6, 8, 10, 15-17, AND 20. DEFENDANT IS TO SERVE FURTHER RESPONSES TO NOS. 4, 6, 8, 10, 15-17, AND 20 WITHIN 20 DAYS’ NOTICE OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Jonathan Nissanoff (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for common counts and breach of contract against Defendant Oscar Health Plan of California (“Defendant”) on December 27, 2022. Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint on February 14, 2023.

 

Plaintiff filed a “Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses” on February 20, 2024. At the hearing on March 21, 2024, the Court noted that the motion improperly combined multiple motions into one and ordered that they be filed separately. Plaintiff filed the instant (1) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One; and (2) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, on March 26, 2024. The Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, was granted in part on May 23, 2024. (Minute Order, 05/23/24.) The Motion was initially set for hearing on June 4, 2024 but was continued to allow for consideration of Defendant’s opposition papers. Plaintiff filed a reply on May 30, 2024.

 

Discussion

 

Notice of the motion to compel further must be given “within 45 days of service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or any specific later date to which the requesting party and the responding party have agreed in writing,” otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (c).) Defendant’s discovery responses were served electronically on June 13, 2023. (Motion, Nissanoff Decl., ¶5.) Notice of the Motions was initially timely filed and served on July 23, 2023. (Id. at Exh. 4.)

 

Also, the Motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b)(1); 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).) The supporting declarations demonstrate an effort by Plaintiff to obtain further responses from Defendant after service of the original and supplemental responses. (Id. at Exh. 3; Reply, Exh. 2.) The Court finds that Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts were in good faith, starting with a letter regarding the interrogatories on June 16, 2023 and continuing over the course of many emails with Defendant’s attorneys. (Reply, Exhs. 1-2.)

 

Finally, Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345 requires all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a).) Alternatively, “the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(3).) The Motion is accompanied by a separate statement. (Separate Statement, filed 03/26/24.)

 

Special Interrogatories, Set One

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2-20. Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300 states in relevant part:

 

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

 

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

. . . .

 

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).)

 

Interrogatory No. 2 asks Defendant to describe how it takes into account each of the factors under Title 28 of the California Code of Regulations, Section 1300.71, subdivision (a)(3)(B) (“the Gould factors”) in determining the reasonable and customary value of out-of-network physician’s emergency medical services.

 

Defendant objected to the request as vague as to time and scope, ambiguous as to the terms “emergency medical services” and “noncontracted physician”, and seeking proprietary, confidential, and trade secret information. It then responds that it calculates the allowable amounts in accordance with applicable laws and regulations.

 

If a timely motion to compel responses has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify its objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (Stendell) (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255 [citing Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221].) The Court also agrees that Defendant’s purported response that it calculates the allowable amounts in accordance with applicable laws and regulations is non-responsive. As Plaintiff points out, this does not provide any factual information regarding how Defendant takes into account the Gould factors when determining the usual, customary, and reasonable (“UCR”) rate. Furthermore, Defendant’s objection that certain terms are ambiguous is belied by its own use of those terms in its response. Third, Defendant’s objection that the information sought is propriety, privileged or confidential was addressed in the Court’s ruling on Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production, Set One. As the Court explained, Defendant offers no evidence to show that the information sought falls within the definition of a trade secret and the parties have a stipulated protective order in place.

 

Defendant’s opposition also argues that the interrogatory contains various subparts, as prohibited by Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.060. A subpart is something that is part of a larger part. The interrogatory does include subpart by asking Defendant to explain how it takes into account each of the Gould factors. Responding as to each of the factors is a smaller part of the larger question of how Defendant takes into accounts all the Gould factors. Plaintiff argues that the interrogatory is simply asking one thing: how does Defendant account for the Gould factors in determining the UCR rate for emergency medical services? However, it also requires Defendant to respond by addressing each factor listed. The interrogatory, therefore, violates the prohibition on special interrogatories with subparts. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 2 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 3 asks Defendant to describe material changes it made to the payment methodology for out-of-network emergency services from 2017 to the present, including, not limited to the timing of those changes, the nature of the changes to the methodology, and the rationale for such changes. This interrogatory is compound in that it is comprised of two or more separate questions. First, the interrogatory asks Defendant to describe the material changes it made to the payment methodology. While the response to that question may include the nature of the material changes, the timing and rationale for those changes are separate from the changes themselves. Therefore, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 3 is denied for being impermissibly compound.

 

Interrogatory No. 4 asks Defendant to describe material changes it made to the payment methodology for out-of-network emergency services due to the No Surprises Act, 42 USC, § 300gg-111, et. seq. In response, Defendant objects that the interrogatory is ambiguous as to time and scope, ambiguous as to the terms "changes [] made to YOUR payment methodology” and “out of network emergency medical services,” and seeks information that is proprietary, privileged, or confidential. As discussed above, Defendant’s objections regarding ambiguity and confidential material are without merit. Nor does Defendant’s opposition separate statement address any of these objections. The separate statement makes alternative arguments, regarding the relevance of the information sought and that this interrogatory is duplicative of Special Interrogatory, No. 2. The Court already addressed this relevance objection in its order regarding the Request for Production of Documents. Nor does it find this interrogatory duplicative of the one above. This interrogatory seeks more specific information about material changes to Defendant’s payment methodology. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 4 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 5 asks Defendant to describe its methodology for paying out-of-network physicians for post-stabilization services rendered in the hospital setting. It goes on to state that if Defendant’s methodology varies based on whether the hospital at which such physicians’ services are rendered is in-network or out-of-network, Defendant should explain how and why such variations occur. The Court agrees that this interrogatory is compound in that it contains two distinct questions. The request to compel a further response to Special Interrogatory No. 5 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 6 asks Defendant to describe its methodology for determining the UCR rate for post-stabilization services rendered by out-of-network physicians. Based on the same relevance objections and arguments discussed above, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 6 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 7 asks Defendant to describe how it accounts for the Gould factors when determining how an out-of-network physician is paid for post-stabilization services. As with Special Interrogatory No. 1, requiring Defendant to respond with respect to each of the Gould factors means the interrogatory includes subparts. The request to compel a further response to Special Interrogatory No. 7 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 8 asks Defendant to describe the databases, data sources, or statistically credible information it uses as a part of its UCR rate methodology. Defendant’s initial objections based on ambiguity and confidentiality are deemed without merit, as discussed above. The opposition now argues that the interrogatory is “hopeless overbroad” because it asks Defendant to identify and describe any document it used in the payment methodology. The interrogatory does not ask Defendant to identify every such document. Rather, it asks that Defendant describe categories of information. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 8 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 9 asks Defendant to describe material changes it made to the payment methodology for out-of-network post-stabilization services from 2017 to the present. As with Special Interrogatory No. 3, this interrogatory contains subparts regarding the timing and rationale for the changes. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 9 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 10 asks Defendant to describe material changes it made to the payment methodology for out-of-network post-stabilization services due to the No Surprises Act, 42 USC, § 300gg-111, et. seq. For the reasons set forth with respect to Special Interrogatory No. 4, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 10 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 11 asks Defendant to identify all professional claims it paid for emergency medical services within 4-and-a-half years of the date this action was filed. An additional objection by Defendant to this interrogatory is that it requires the preparation of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary, in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230. This statute does not prohibit an interrogatory that requires such a preparation, however. Rather, it states: “If an answer to an interrogatory would necessitate the preparation or the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of or from the documents of the party to whom the interrogatory is directed, and if the burden or expense of preparing or making it would be substantially the same for the party propounding the interrogatory as for the responding party, it is a sufficient answer to that interrogatory to refer to this section and to specify the writings from which the answer may be derived or ascertained.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230.) Defendant has not shown that the burden of preparing such a compilation, etc. would be the same for it as for Plaintiff, nor has Defendant specified the writings from which the answer may be ascertained. This objection, therefore, is also without merit.

 

Defendant also objects in its opposition that this interrogatory contains subparts and the Court agrees. Plaintiff asks not only for the professional claims paid by Defendant for emergency services over the last four-and-a-half years, but also the date of service, all diagnosis and/or billing codes, the payor type, the billed charges, the allowed amount, and the patient responsibility for each claim. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 11 is therefore denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 12 asks Defendant to identify all professional claims it paid for post-stabilization services within 4-and-a half years of the date this action was filed. Based on the same objections and arguments discussed above, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 12 is denied on the same basis as Interrogatory No. 11.

 

Interrogatory No. 13 asks Defendant to describe its methodology for receiving, reviewing, and responding to requests for the authorization of post-stabilization care made pursuant to Health & Safety Code section 1262.8. It also asks Defendant to explain how its methodology varies based on whether a provider’s authorization request is made via telephone, fax, or electronic or other means; the hours during which staff and personnel are available to review, approve and/or deny the request; and the means by which YOU communicate the decision to approve or deny the request are conveyed to the provider. This requests is both compound and contains subparts. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 13 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 14 asks Defendant to describe its policies and procedures for transfer or repatriation of patients after a request for authorization under Health & Safety Code section 1262.8, including any changes from 2017 to the present. Again, this request contains subparts relating to transfer and repatriation, and is compound with respect to changes to the identified policies and procedures. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 14 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 15 asks Defendant to describe its policies and procedures with respect to reimbursement of noncontracted post-stabilization services when it does not notify the provider within 30 minutes of the request for authorization. Defendant again relies on objections that the interrogatory is ambiguous, seeks confidential information, requires the preparation of a compilation, and is irrelevant to this action. Based on the same objections and arguments discussed above, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 15 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 16 asks Defendant to describe its policies and procedures to pay the reasonable charges associated with the transfer of the patient after their emergency medical condition has been stabilized. As before, Defendant relies on objections that the interrogatory is ambiguous, seeks confidential information, requires the preparation of a compilation, and is irrelevant to this action

Therefore, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 16 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 17 asks Defendant to describe its policies and procedures to pay for all the immediately required medically necessary care rendered to the patient prior to transfer after the emergency medical condition has been stabilized. For the same reasons set forth with respect to Interrogatory Nos. 15 and 156 Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 17 is granted.

 

Interrogatory No. 18 asks Defendant to identify any physicians, physician groups, or other providers for the provision of emergency medical services in California with which it contracts, to identify all such contracts, including the contracted parties, the effective date(s), and payment methodologies set forth therein. This interrogatory is impermissibly compound. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 18 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 19 asks Defendant to identify any physicians, physician groups, or other providers for the provision of post-stabilization services in California with which it contracts. Like Interrogatory No. 18, this interrogatory is compound. Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 19 is denied.

 

Interrogatory No. 20 asks Defendant to identify all its written policies and procedures that relate to the handling and resolution of out-of-network provider grievances. Defendant again relies on objections that the interrogatory is ambiguous, seeks confidential information, requires the preparation of a compilation, and is irrelevant to this action. Based on the discussion above, Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s further responses to Interrogatory No. 20 is granted.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Jonathan Nissanoff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, is DENIED AS TO NOS. 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11-14, 18-19 AND GRANTED AS TO NOS. 4, 6, 8, 10, 15-17, AND 20. DEFENDANT IS TO SERVE FURTHER RESPONSES TO NOS. 4, 6, 8, 10, 15-17, AND 20 WITHIN 20 DAYS’ NOTICE OF THIS ORDER.

Case Number: 23STCP04499    Hearing Date: July 16, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Roberts v. Caraccioli, et al.

PETITION TO CONFIRM ARBITRATION AWARD

(CCP § 1285, et seq.)

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Petitioner Jordan Roberts’ Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is GRANTED IN THE AMOUNT OF $18,532.05 PRINCIPAL AND INTEREST CALCULATED AT SEVEN PERCENT PER ANNUM FROM MAY 13, 2023 UNTIL ENTRY OF JUDGMENT. PETITIONER IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On December 14, 2023, Petitioner Jordan Roberts (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award against Respondent Carlos Caraccioli (“Respondent”). The Petition was initially set for hearing on April 18, 2024 but continued the day before to May 14, 2024 with an order from the Court for Petitioner to give notice to all parties. (Notice Re: Continuance, 04/17/24.) At the hearing on May 14, 2024, the Court noted the lack of any proof of service of the Petition and hearing date and continued the matter to July 16, 2024. (Minute Order, 07/16/24.) On June 25, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of personal service.

 

Discussion

 

Confirmation of the arbitration award is sought under Code of Civil Procedure section 1285, et seq., which states “[a]ny party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.  The petition shall name as respondent all parties to the arbitration and may name as respondents any other persons bound by the arbitration award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.) “If a petition or response under this chapter is duly served and filed, the court shall confirm the award as made, whether rendered in this state or another state, unless in accordance with this chapter it corrects the award and confirms it as corrected, vacates the award or dismisses the proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.)

 

A response to a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award that seeks to vacate or correct the award must be served and filed no later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the respondent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1288.2.)

 

Discussion

 

Service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4 requires that the Petition and Notice of Hearing be served on Respondent “in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice” or “[i]f the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made . . . [s]ervice within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4, subds. (a), (b).)

 

Petitioner has not shown that the parties’ arbitration agreement provides for the manner of service of the Petition and the Notice of Hearing. It follows that the Petition and the Notice of Hearing must be served in the manner provided by law for service of a summons, which is set forth in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 to 415.95. Petitioner has now filed proof of service of the Petition and the Notice of Continued Hearing Date by personal delivery to Respondent on June 22, 2024. Therefore, the Court finds that service of the Petition and the Notice of Hearing conforms to Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4. 

 

Service of the Arbitration Award and Timing of Service of Petition (CCP §§ 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 requires that “[t]he neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1283.6.) This requirement may be satisfied by service by the arbitrator, or upon proper service of the Award with the Petition. (See Murry v. Civil Service Employees Ins. Co. (1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 796, 799-800.) The arbitration award is accompanied by a proof of service by certified mail dated April 13, 2023. (Pet., Attachment 8(c), p. 11.)

 

Also, a party seeking a court judgment confirming an arbitration award must file and serve the petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.) Petitioner has now demonstrated compliance with Code of Civil Procedure sections 1288 and 1288.4.

 

Confirmation of the Arbitration Award

 

An arbitration award is not directly enforceable until it is confirmed by a court and judgment is entered.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1287.6; Jones v. Kvistad (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 836, 840.) The court must confirm the award as made, unless it corrects or vacates the award, or dismisses the proceeding.  (CCP § 1286; Valsan Partners Limited Partnership v. Calcor Space Facility, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 809, 818.) Code of Civil Procedure, section 1285.4 states a petition under this chapter shall:

 

a)      Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement.

 

b)      Set forth the names of the arbitrators.

 

c)      Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4.) The Petition complies with the above requirements. It sets forth the nature of the arbitration agreement and the name of the arbitrator. (Pet., ¶4.) A copy of the arbitration award is also attached. (Id. at Attachment 8(c).) Substantively, the Petition demonstrates that Petitioner is entitled to an award of $18,532.05 from Respondent. (Id. at Attachment 8(c), p. 10.)

 

Attorney’s Fees, Costs and Interest

 

The interest sought by Petitioner, seven percent per annum from 30 days after the award was issued (May 13, 2023), is appropriate pursuant to Civil Code section 3289, subdivision (b).

 

Conclusion

 

Petitioner Jordan Roberts’ Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is GRANTED IN THE AMOUNT OF $18,532.05 PRINCIPAL AND INTEREST CALCULATED AT SEVEN PERCENT PER ANNUM FROM MAY 13, 2023 (30 DAYS AFTER THE AWARD WAS ISSUED) UNTIL ENTRY OF JUDGMENT. PETITIONER IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(CCP §§ 2031.300, 2023.010)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant City of Whittier’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF MARTHA DELIA ANDA IS TO SERVE VERIFIED RESPONSES TO THE DISCOVERY REQUESTS, WITHOUT OBJECTIONS, WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER. ANALYSIS:

 

On April 11, 2024, Defendant City of Whittier (“Defendant Whittier”) served Request for Production of Documents, Set One, on Plaintiff Martha Delia Anda (“Plaintiff”). (Motion, Verska Decl., Exh. A.) Despite a meet and confer effort, Plaintiff has not served responses to the discovery. (Id. at ¶¶4-9.) Defendant Whittier filed the instant Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions, on May 30, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

Based on Plaintiff’s failure to serve initial responses to the discovery, the Motion to Compel Responses to the Request for Production of Documents, is granted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) There is no requirement for a prior meet and confer effort before a motion to compel initial responses can be filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) Furthermore, the motion can be brought at any time after the responding party fails to provide the responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) Pursuant to a lodestar calculation, sanctions are awarded against Plaintiff in the amount of $245.00 based on one hour of attorney time billed at $245.00 per hour. (Motion, Verska Decl., ¶11.)

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant City of Whittier’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. Ovsepian v. Lopez, et al.

DEMURRER

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Cynthia Lopez’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On December 18, 2023, Plaintiff Oganes John Ovsepian (“Plaintiff”), in propria persona, filed the Complaint in this action against Defendant Cynthia Lopez (“Defendant”). On June 11, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint, also in propria persona. No opposition to the Demurrer has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

The Demurrer is not accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. (Demurrer, Lopez Decl.) However, failure to comply with the meet and confer requirement is not grounds to overrule the Demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3)(B)(4).) Defendant is admonished that failing to meet and confer in the future may result in the Demurrer being placed off calendar.

 

Defendant demurs on the grounds that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action. (Notice of Demurrer, p. 2:10-11 [citing Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e)].) The Courts finds that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action. The Complaint is set forth on Judicial Council Form PLD-C-001 and indicates that Plaintiff asserts a single cause of action for breach of contract. (Compl., ¶8.) However, beyond the name of the cause of action, no factual allegations are included in the Complaint. The form complaint specifically requires that such allegations be attached but no additional pages are attached. (See ibid.) Nor has Plaintiff filed any opposition to the instant Demurrer demonstrating that the Complaint includes sufficient allegations or can be amended to do so. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Therefore, the Demurrer to the Complaint is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Cynthia Lopez’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

Ortiz v. Smith, et al.

DEMURRER

(CCP § 430.10, et seq.)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Housing Authority of the City of Los Angeles’ Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

BASED ON THE RELIEF SOUGHT IN THE COMPLAINT, THIS ACTION IS RECLASSIFIED AS AN UNLIMITED CIVIL CASE AND TRANSFERRED TO THE RECLASSIFICATION/TRANSFER DESK FOR REASSIGNMENT TO AN INDEPENDENT CALENDAR COURT. PLAINTIFFS KIMBERLY ORTIZ AND MARIA GLORIA ANDRADE ARE ORDERED TO PAY THE RECLASSIFICATION FEE WITHIN TEN (10) DAYS.

 

ALL HEARING DATES IN DEPARTMENT 26 OF THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE ARE VACATED. 

 

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiffs Kinberly Ortiz and Mana Gloria Andrade (“Plaintiffs”), in propria persona, filed the Complaint in this action against Defendants Kito Robinson Smith (“Defendant Smith”) and Housing Authority of the City of Los Angeles (“Defendant HACLA”). Defendant HACLA filed a Notice of Appearance on May 14, 2024, then the instant Demurrer to the Complaint on June 11, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

The Demurrer is not accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. Defendant is admonished that failing to meet and confer in the future may result in the Demurrer being placed off calendar.

 

The Demurrer is brought on three separate grounds: the Complaint (1) fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; (2) is uncertain; and (3) is moot. (Demurrer, p. 1:18-20.) Special demurrers, however, are not permitted in the limited jurisdiction court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) Therefore, the Court will not rule on the demurrer for uncertainty. Nor does the Demurrer cite authority for a demurrer based on mootness. The memorandum of points and authorities relies on Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, which does not state a party may object by demurrer on the grounds that a pleading is moot. The Demurrer also cites two cases regarding mootness. Parkford Owners for a Better Community v. County of Placer (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 714, 721, however, concerned a motion for judgment on the pleadings, not a demurrer. Center for Environmental Science, Accuracy & Reliability v. Department of Water Resources (Cal. Ct. App., Feb. 1, 2021, No. C080967) 2021 WL 320814, is an unpublished case that cannot be cited as legal authority. Therefore, no authority is presented to sustain the Demurrer on the grounds that the Complaint is moot. The Court accordingly denies Defendant HACLA’s request for judicial notice, which is directed to the mootness argument.

 

Defendant HACLA makes a minimal argument that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action because it is “completely incomprehensible and of no legal merit whatever.” (Demurrer, p. 2:13-20.) The Complaint purports to seek injunctive relief to prevent Plaintiffs’ eviction from the premises at 1343 E. 114th Street, Apartment 657, Los Angeles, California. (Compl., p. 1-2.) No other argument is provided in the Demurrer regarding the sufficiency of Plaintiffs’ allegations for injunctive relief. Therefore, the Demurrer for failure to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action is overruled.

 

The Court, now having now ruled on the Demurrer to the Complaint, finds it lacks jurisdiction to issue a judgment in this action. The limited jurisdiction court lacks jurisdiction to issue the injunctive relief sought in the Petition. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 85, 86.)

Therefore, the case is reclassified as an unlimited civil case and transferred to the Reclassification/Transfer Desk for collection of fees and reassignment of the case to an Independent Calendar Court. Plaintiffs are ordered to pay the reclassification fee within ten (10) days.

 

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Housing Authority of the City of Los Angeles’ Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

BASED ON THE RELIEF SOUGHT IN THE COMPLAINT, THIS ACTION IS RECLASSIFIED AS AN UNLIMITED CIVIL CASE AND TRANSFERRED TO THE RECLASSIFICATION/TRANSFER DESK FOR REASSIGNMENT TO AN INDEPENDENT CALENDAR COURT. PLAINTIFFS KIMBERLY ORTIZ AND MARIA GLORIA ANDRADE ARE ORDERED TO PAY THE RECLASSIFICATION FEE WITHIN TEN (10) DAYS.

 

ALL HEARING DATES IN DEPARTMENT 26 OF THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE ARE VACATED.
