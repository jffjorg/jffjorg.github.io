DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 20STCV46963    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:   September 9, 2024                                         

 

CASE NAME:           City of Los Angeles v. Yunhee Min, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                20STCV46963

 

MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT

 

MOVING PARTY:  Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant City of Los Angeles, Acting by and through the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): None as of August 29, 2024

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      An Order determining Good Faith Settlement between Plaintiff and Defendant Brandon Min.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On December 8, 2020, Plaintiff City of Los Angeles, Acting by and through the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Defendant Yunhee Min with three causes of action for: (1) Account Stated, (2) Book Account, and (3) Goods and Services Rendered. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant owed $59,134.92 for water, sewer, electric and/or solid waste/resource services.

 

On May 28, 2021, Defendant filed an Answer and a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendants City of Los Angeles, Chris Min, Brandon Min, and Jeffrey Siegel as Administrator of the Estate of Byung Ok Min with four causes of action for: (1) Damages and Other Relief Pursuant to Civil Code § 1798.93, (2) Equitable Indemnity, (3) Contribution, and (4) Violations of Penal Code § 530.5. According to the Cross-Complaint, Cross-Defendants fraudulently opened an account in Cross-Complainant’s name with the LADWP. Cross-Complainant also alleges that she only has a 15% ownership interest in the Property.

 

On July 1, 2021, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant City of Los Angeles filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

 

On July 26, 2021, Cross-Defendant Chris Sung Hoon Min filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿877.6, subdivision¿(a)(1),¿provides, in relevant part, that, on noticed motion, “[a]ny¿party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint¿tortfeasors¿or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff¿. . . and one or more alleged¿tortfeasors¿or co-obligors¿. . . .”¿¿“A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint¿tortfeasor¿or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling¿tortfeasor¿or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”¿¿(Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 877.6, subd.¿(c).)¿¿Although a determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.¿¿(Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 877, subd.¿(a).)¿ 

 

“The party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof on that issue.”¿¿(Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 877.6, subd.¿(d).)¿ 

 

In¿City of Grand View Terrace v. Superior Court¿(1987)¿192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261, the court provided the following guidance regarding a motion for a good faith settlement determination:¿ 

 

This court notes that of the hundreds of motions for good faith determination presented for trial court approval each year, the overwhelming majority are unopposed and granted summarily by the trial court.¿ At the time of filing in many cases, the moving party does not know if a contest will develop.¿ If each motion required a full recital by declaration or affidavit setting forth a complete factual response to all of the¿Tech-Bilt¿factors, literally thousands of attorney hours would be consumed and inch-thick motions would have to be read and considered by¿trial courts in an exercise which would waste valuable judicial and legal time and clients’ resources. . .. That is to say, when no one objects, the barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient.¿ 

If the good faith settlement is contested, section 877.6, subdivision (d), sets forth a workable ground rule for the hearing by placing the burden of proving the lack of good faith on the contesting party.¿¿Once there is a showing made by the settlor of the settlement, the burden of proof on the issue of good faith shifts to the¿nonsettlor¿who asserts that the settlement was not made in good faith.¿¿If contested, declarations by the¿nonsettlor¿should be filed which in many cases could require the moving party to file responsive¿counterdeclarations¿to negate the lack of good faith asserted by the¿nonsettling¿contesting party.¿ 

(192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1260-1261¿[citation omitted].)¿ 

 

In¿Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates¿(1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499, the California Supreme Court identified the following nonexclusive factors courts are to consider in determining if a settlement is in good faith under section 877.6: “a rough approximation of plaintiffs’¿total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial.¿¿Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants,¿as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of¿nonsettling¿defendants.”¿ Not every factor will apply in each case. (Dole Food Co., Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (Shell Oil Co.) (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 894, 909.)

 

The evaluation of whether a settlement was made in good faith is required to “be made¿on the basis of¿information available at the time of settlement.”¿¿(Tech-Bilt, Inc.,¿supra,¿38¿Cal.3d at¿p.¿499.)¿¿“‘[A] defendant’s settlement figure must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant’s liability to be.’ [Citation.]”¿¿(Ibid.)¿ 

 

“The party asserting the lack of good faith, who has the burden of proof on that issue (§ 877.6,¿subd. (d)), should be permitted to demonstrate, if he can, that the settlement is so far¿‘out of the ballpark’¿in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of¿the statute.¿¿Such a demonstration would establish that the proposed settlement was not a¿‘settlement made in good faith’¿within the terms of section 877.6.”¿¿(Id.¿at pp. 499-500.)¿ 

 

“[A] court not only looks at the alleged tortfeasor's potential liability to the plaintiff, but¿it must also consider the culpability of the tortfeasor vis-à-vis other parties alleged to be responsible for the same injury.¿ Potential liability for indemnity to a nonsettling defendant is an important consideration for the trial court in determining whether to approve a settlement by an alleged tortfeasor.¿ [Citation.]”¿ (TSI Seismic Tenant Space, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 159, 166.)¿ 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff City of LA contends the settlement with Defendant Brandon Min is in good faith because it satisfies the Tech-Bilt factors and, importantly, is relative to their proportionate share of the Property.

 

“The party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof on that issue.”¿¿(Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 877.6,¿subd.¿(d).)¿Here, no party filed an opposition.  

  

To determine whether settlement has been in good faith, courts look at certain factors. In¿Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates¿(1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499, the California Supreme Court identified the following nonexclusive factors courts are to consider in determining if a settlement is in good faith under section 877.6: “a rough approximation of plaintiffs’¿total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial.¿¿Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants,¿as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of¿nonsettling¿defendants.” 

 

Factor 1: Proportionate Liability

 

Defendant Brandon Min is settling the LADWP bill for his proportionate share of the Property and LADWP has agreed to settle Brandon Min’s Cross-Complaint against it for $24,000. The settlement is not suspicious because Defendant Brandon Min is settling for his proportionate share of liability.

 

Accordingly, this factor weighs in favor of finding good faith.

 

Factor 2: Amount Paid in Settlement

 

As above, Defendant Brandon Min is settling with LADWP for his proportionate share of the bill. (Declaration of A. Voltaire Lazaro (Lazaro Decl. ¶¶ 6, 8.)

 

Accordingly, this factor weighs in favor of finding good faith.

 

Factor 3: Recognition Settlor Should Pay Less in Settlement

 

Based on the facts of this case, this factor does not apply because Defendant Brandon Min is settling for his proportionate share of LADWP’s bill.

 

Factor 4: Allocation of Settlement Proceeds Among Plaintiffs.

 

This factor does not apply because there is only one Plaintiff. 

 

Factor 5: Settlor’s Financial Condition and Insurance Policy Limits

 

This factor does not apply because Defendant Brandon Min’s financial condition or insurance coverage is not at issue here.

 

Factor 6: Evidence of Collusion, Fraud, or Tortious Conduct Between Settlor and Plaintiffs Aimed at Making the Nonsettling Parties Pay More than Their Fair Share

 

Here, there is no evidence of fraud or collusion. First, there is no evidence the settlement was not negotiated at arm’s length and in good faith. Defendant Brandon Min is paying his proportion of the bill. (Lazaro Decl. ¶ 6, Exhibit 1.)

 

Accordingly, the Tech-Bilt factors have been satisfied.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

1.      Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

Moving party is to give notice.

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

Meet and Confer¿ 

¿¿ 

Prior to filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to satisfy their meet and confer obligations pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. §430.41, and demonstrate that they so satisfied their meet and confer obligation by submitting a declaration pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. §430.41(a)(2) & (3). ¿The meet and confer requirement also applies to motions to strike. (CCP § 435.5.)¿Here, the parties met and conferred via telephone on May 22, 2024. (Sarkissian Decl. ¶¶4-5.) Thus, this requirement is met.

 

Demurrer¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿(Hahn v. Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. …. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿ 

¿ 

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true. (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.) Courts also consider exhibits attached to the complaint and incorporated by reference. (See Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94 (Frantz).)¿ 

 

Motion to Strike¿ 

¿ 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id.¿§¿437.)¿“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)¿¿ 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Demurrer

 

Statute of Frauds

 

As an initial matter, Moving Parties contend that Plaintiff’s claim is barred by the statute of frauds. (Civ. Code § 1624.) However, as alleged in the FAC, Plaintiff and Defendant Chahayed executed an oral contract for the sale of his business. Not only is this outside the statute of frauds but the parties subsequently memorialized this oral contract in writing with the Escrow Instructions attached as Exhibit 1 to the FAC. Indeed, Moving Parties acknowledge the Escrow Instructions.

 

Accordingly, the court finds that the statute of frauds does not apply.[1]

 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Moving Parties contend that this claim fails because there was no contract.[2] Plaintiff argues that he sufficiently alleged an oral contract.

 

“To establish a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must plead and prove (1) the existence of the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) resulting damages to the plaintiff. [Citation.]” (Maxwell v. Dolezal (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 93, 97-98.) “A written contract may be pleaded either by its terms – set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference – or by its legal effect. [Citation.] In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms.’” (Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 993.)  

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, Plaintiff sufficiently alleged a claim for breach of contract against Defendant Chahayed.[3] First, needed to allege the existence of a contract, including: parties capable of contracting, consent, lawful object, and consideration. (Civ. Code § 1550.) Plaintiff did so by Plaintiff alleging an oral contract between himself and Defendant Chahayed for the purchase of his business (Simi Oil) for $75,000. (FAC ¶ 30; see also FAC, Exhibit 1.) Second, Plaintiff alleges he fully performed by obtaining a new lease from Defendant BM which was the only contingency of the oral contract. (FAC ¶ 31.) Third, Plaintiff sufficiently alleged Defendant Chahayed breached the contract by refusing to sign the new lease, filing an FBN Statement prematurely identifying himself as owner of Simi Oil, and refusing to pay the $75,000.00 to acquire Simi Oil. (FAC ¶ 32.) Fourth, Plaintiff alleges damages by the breach because Defendant Chahayed allegedly stole Chung’s business from him without paying.[4] (FAC ¶¶ 32, 33.)

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Moving Parties’ demurrer to the first cause of action.

 

Second Cause of Action – Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Failr Dealing

 

The arguments are virtually the same as above.[5]

 

The elements of a claim for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing are: (1) a contractual relationship; (2) plaintiff fulfilled their contractual obligations; (3) any conditions precedent to defendant’s performance occurred; (4) defendant unfairly interfered with plaintiff’s right to receive the benefits of the contract; and (5) plaintiff was harmed by defendant’s conduct. (Waller v. Truck Ins. Exchange, Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 1, 36.)  

 

Every contract contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing that neither party will do anything to interfere with the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement. (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1393.) It is an “implied-in-law” contractual term whose breach “will always result in a breach of the contract.” (Id. at 1393-1394.) To assert a claim, a plaintiff must show that the defendant’s conduct “demonstrates a failure or refusal to discharge contractual responsibilities . . . by a conscious and deliberate act, which unfairly frustrates the agreed common purposes and disappoints the reasonable expectations of the other party . . . .” (Id. at p. 1395.) The conduct may also be a breach of a consensual contract term. (Id.) “If there exists a contractual relationship between the parties, . . .  the implied covenant is limited to assuring compliance with the express terms of the contract . . . .” (Racine & Laramie, Ltd. v. Department of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1026, 1032.)

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, Plaintiff have insufficiently alleged a claim for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing. First, as noted above, Plaintiff sufficiently alleged a contractual relationship with Defendant Chahayed. (FAC ¶ 30.) Second, Plaintiff alleges he performed his obligations. (FAC ¶¶ 31, 36.) Plaintiff’s obtaining the new lease was also the condition precedent to Defendant Chahayed’s performance. (FAC ¶ 31.) Third, Plaintiff alleges Defendant Chahayed unfairly interfered by refusing to sign the new lease that Plaintiff obtained for him. (FAC ¶ 37.) As noted in Careau & Co., refusing to sign the lease as alleged here is a “conscious and deliberate act” not to discharge his contractual duties. Fourth, Plaintiff alleges harm from this conduct. (FAC ¶ 38.)

 

However, Plaintiff failed to allege a “special relationship” with Defendant Chahayed that warrants a tort claim. (Careao & Co., supra,at p. 1395-1401 [discussing evolution of tortious breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing outside of the insurance context and ultimate arrival are requirement for “special relationship” based on several factors.]) Here, as in Careao & Co., Plaintiff and Defendant Chahayed allegedly entered into an oral contract and Plaintiff has not alleged any “indicia of unequal bargaining here, no adhesive agreements, no indication that one party had any particular advantage over the other.” (Careao & Co., supra, 222 Cal.App.3d at 1400.) There are no facts that “ordinary contract damages” would not be adequate to make Plaintiff whole. (Id.) The court is not convinced that Plaintiff can do so.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Parties’ demurrer to the second cause of action.

 

Sixth Cause of Action – Civil Conspiracy

 

Moving Parties contend that Plaintiff insufficiently alleged this claim. Plaintiff argues he did because it relates to the third and fourth causes of action against the Landlord Defendants’ interference with contract and interference with prospective economic advantage.

 

“The elements of an action for civil conspiracy are (1) formation and operation of the conspiracy and (2) damage resulting to plaintiff (3) from a wrongful act done in furtherance of the common design.” (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1136.) “There is no separate tort of civil conspiracy and no action for conspiracy to commit a tort unless the underlying tort is committed and damage results therefrom.” (Id.) If that act is committed, then “each member may be held responsible as a joint tortfeasor, regardless of whether or not he directly participated in the act.” (Richard B. LeVine, Inc. v. Higashi (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 566, 574.)

 

First, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Nickerson “persuaded CHAHAYED not to sign the New Lease until CHUNG had been evicted from SIMI OIL” and that each Defendants knew about Plaintiff’s contract for the sale of Simi Oil to Defendant Chahayed. (FAC ¶¶ 1, 2, 15, 18, 23, 24, 25, 42, 43, 47, 48.) Second, Plaintiff alleges that the Defendants agreed to interfere with this contract. (FAC ¶ 23, 24, 25, 59.) Third, Plaintiff alleges damages from the wrongful act.[6] (FAC ¶ 60.) To the extent that Moving Parties seek additional facts supporting this claim, they are still to be determined through discovery. (See Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216 [less specificity required when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy.]) 

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Moving Parties’ demurrer to the Sixth Cause of Action.

 

Seventh Cause of Action – Aid and Abetting

 

The arguments here are essentially the same as for the sixth cause of action.

 

For liability under a theory of aiding and abetting, Plaintiff must show that the person “(a) knows the other's conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act or (b) gives substantial assistance to the other in accomplishing a tortious result and the person's own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.’ ” (Richard B. LeVine, supra, 131 Cal.App.4th at p. 574.) As with civil conspiracy, there must be an underlying wrongful act. (Id. at 575.)

 

The analysis here is unchanged in this cause of action as for the sixth cause of action for Defendant Chahayed. However, Plaintiff has not alleged facts implicated Defendant Simi Oil.

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Moving Parties’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action as to Defendant Chahayed and SUSTAINS the demurrer as to Defendant Simi Oil.

 

Eighth Cause of Action – Unfair Competition

 

Moving Parties contend that Plaintiff did not allege what unfair competition took place. Plaintiff did not address this in his opposition.[7]

 

Unfair competition is any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practices or act and unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading advertising. (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.)¿ A plaintiff needs to identify statutory, regulatory, or decisional law that the defendant has violated.¿ (Bernardo v. Planned Parenthood Federation of America (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 322, 352.)¿Unfair competition “borrows” violations of other laws and authorizes a separate action pursuant to unfair competition. (See Farmers Ins. Exch. v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 377, 383.) Unfair conduct in unfair competition actions must be violative of public policy and “tethered to specific constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provisions.”¿ (Scripps Clinic v. Superior Court (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 917, 940.) As a statutory cause of action, allegations of unfair business practices must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation. (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.) The statutory violations must be specifically delineated and said violations must relate to a business activity.¿ (Ibid.)¿

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, the court agrees that Plaintiff insufficiently alleged a claim for unfair competition. The court does not see factual allegations supporting this cause of action with reasonable particularity, if at all.[8]

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Parties’ demurrer to the eighth cause of action.

 

Tenth Cause of Action – Fraud

 

Moving Parties contend Plaintiff failed to sufficiently allege this claim.[9] Plaintiff argues he did.

 

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469; Perlas v. GMAC Mort., LLC (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 429, 434 [requiring plaintiffs who claim fraud against a corporation to allege the names of the persons who made the misrepresentations, their authority to speak for the corporation, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.]) Fraud allegations need not be liberally construed, general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient, and every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged fully, factually and specifically. (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal. App. 3d 1324, 1331.)¿ 

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, Plaintiff sufficiently alleged a claim for fraud against Defendant Chahayed.[10] First, Plaintiff identifies the alleged misrepresentations. (FAC ¶¶ 76a, 76b, 76d.) Second, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Chahayed knew these representations were false and that he never intended to pay $75,000.00 to Plaintiff for Simi Oil. (FAC ¶¶ 77, 77a, 77b.) Third, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Chahayed intended for Plaintiff to rely on the representations. (FAC ¶ 79.) Fourth, Plaintiff alleges that he reasonably believed Defendant Chahayed. (FAC ¶ 80.) Fifth, Plaintiff alleges resulting damages of $100,000.[11] (FAC ¶¶ 81, 82.) However, there are no factual allegations as to Defendant Simi Oil.

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Moving Parties’ demurrer to the tenth cause of action as to Defendant Chahayed and SUSTAINS the demurrer as to Defendant Simi Oil.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Punitive Damages

 

Moving Parties request the court to strike the following language from the FAC:

 

Punitive Damages Allegations:

1.      Paragraph 39: “In doing the things herein alleged, CHAHAYED acted willfully, oppressively, fraudulently and maliciously, and with a conscious disregard of the rights of CHUNG and, accordingly, CHUNG is entitled to recovery of punitive damages in an amount to be determined by the trier of fact.”

2.      Paragraphs 59-61: “On information and belief, ALL DEFENDANTS knowingly and willingly conspired and agreed among themselves to tortously interfere with Oral Contract to which CHUNG was a party, to interfere with CHUNG’s prospective economic advantage. As a direct and proximate result, CHUNG has suffered damages in a sum to be proven at trial. In conspiring to engage in the tortious conduct described above, ALL DEFENDANTS acted with malice and oppression warranting the award of punitive damages and exemplary damages in an amount to be proven at trial.”

3.      Paragraphs 63-64: “Each of the ALL DEFENDANTS at all times owed a duty to CHUNG to refrain from interfering with CHUNG’s Oral Contract and prospective economic advantage. Each of the ALL DEFENDANTS provided substantial assistance or encouragement to one or more other DEFENDANT in breaching their duties to CHUNG, which was a substantial factor in causing CHUNG to suffer damages in an amount to be proven at trial. In Intentionally aiding and abetting other Defendants in committing the breaches of their duty alleged above, ALL DEFENDANTS acted with malice and oppression warranting the award of punitive damages and exemplary damages in an amount to be proven at trial.”

4.      Paragraphs 66-68: “ALL DEFENDANTS’ acts offended established public policy and were immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous and substantially injurious to CHUNG and consumers. ALL DEFENDANTS engaged in unfair business practices whose harm to CHUNG outweighs any possible benefits. ALL DEFENDANTS’ acts constituted unlawful, unfair, and fraudulent business practices in violation of California Business and Professions Code §§ 17200 through 17208. ALL DEFENDANTS’ unlawful, unfair, and fraudulent business practices have caused great and irreparable injury to CHUNG and his reputation and CHUNG continues to suffer monetary damages and injury to his reputation and goodwill by reason of such unfair competition.”

5.      Paragraph 77 (77a-b): “The above representations by NICKERSON and CHAHAYED were false. The true facts were: 77a. CHAHAYED never intended to pay $75,000.00 to CHUNG for SIMI OIL. He intended to pay nothing for Simi Oil. Indeed, he criminally filed the FBN Statement even before the Escrow opened, registering and publishing the FBN Statement he was the owner of Simi Oil when he was not. He planned to steal SIMI OIL by conspiring with NICKERSON to lead on CHUNG until Chung’s lease expired on November 30, 2022. And after CHUNG was evicted, he planned to sign New Lease for SIMI OIL without having to pay $75,000.00 to CHUNG. 77b. CHAHAYED never intended to sign the New Lease issued in his name for SIMI OIL. HE never intended to close the Escrow.”

6.      Paragraphs 81-83: “CHUNG was harmed of at least $100,000.00; and CHUNG’s reliance on NICKERSON and CHAHAYED’s representation was a substantial factor in causing his harm. NICKERSON and CHAHAYED acted with malice and oppression warranting the award of punitive damages and exemplary damages in an amount to be proven at trial.”

7.      Prayer item C: “punitive damages.”

Moving Parties contend that Plaintiff insufficiently alleged a claim for punitive damages. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

To obtain punitive damages, a plaintiff must plead sufficient facts in support of punitive damages.¿ (See¿Hilliard v. A.H. Robins Co.¿(1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-92.)¿ In addition,¿punitive damages are allowed only where “it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”¿ (Civ. Code, § 3294(a).)¿ Courts have viewed despicable conduct as conduct “so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (Scott v. Phoenix Schools, Inc., (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 702, 715.) Further, Civil Code § 3294(c) provides the definition of malice, oppression, and fraud. Malice is “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Ibid.) Oppression is “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” (Ibid.) Fraud is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”¿(Ibid.)¿ 

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, Plaintiff sufficiently alleged a claim warranting punitive damages. Notably, Plaintiff alleged fraud by Defendant Chahayed who induced Plaintiff to enter a contract he never intended to perform. (See, e.g., FAC ¶¶ 1, 2.) Working in tandem with Plaintiff’s landlord, Nickerson, who allegedly sought to wrongfully evict Plaintiff for reporting hazardous conditions is conduct that “would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” Whether Plaintiff can ultimately prove he is entitled to punitive damages is a question for the future, not at the pleading stage.

 

Accordingly, the court DENIES Moving Parties’ motion to strike punitive damages.

(CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).)

 

“A party may not recover attorney fees unless expressly authorized by statute or contract. [Citations.] In the absence of a statute authorizing the recovery of attorney fees, the parties may agree on whether and how to allocate attorney fees.” (Hom v. Litigation Privilege

 

Moving Parties seek to strike the following portion of the FAC:

 

Litigation Privilege:

1.      Paragraph 24: “And the despicable plan worked to perfection. CHAHAYED refused to sign the New Lease wanting to get SIMI OIL for free. As part of the conspiracy, on December 22, 2022, NICKERSON sent a Notice of Termination of Tenancy to CHUNG on December 22, 2022. A true and correct copy of the Notice is attached as Exhibit 5. NICKERSON made it clear that if CHUNG did not vacate the SIMI OIL, NICKERSON would file an unlawful detainer action against him to evict him and recover attorneys’ fees. CHUNG had been constructively evicted and left the SIMI OIL on December 31, 2022. In his hurry, he left tools, machines, and/or equipment (“Personal Property”) used at SIMI OIL. He had not removed the Personal Property from the SIMI OIL because it was part of the sale of SIMI OIL to CHAHAYED.”

The litigation privilege is codified at Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b), and provides that “a privileged publication or broadcast is one made . . . [¶] [i]n any . . . judicial proceeding . . . .”¿¿ 

¿ 

“’The principal purpose of [Civil Code] section [47, subdivision (b)] is to afford litigants and witnesses . . .¿ the utmost freedom of access to the courts without fear of being harassed

subsequently by derivative tort actions.’ [Citation.] Additionally, the privilege promotes effective judicial proceedings by encouraging ‘“open channels of communication and the presentation of evidence”’¿without the external threat of liability [citation]¿and ‘by encouraging attorneys to zealously protect their clients' interests.’ [Citation.]” (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 321-322.)¿ 

¿ 

The litigation privilege is “absolute,” and “it bars all tort causes of action except a claim of malicious prosecution.” (Hagberg v. California Federal Bank (2004) 32 Cal.4th 350, 360.) It applies not only to statements made before a court but broadly “to any communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that have some connection or logical relation to the action.” (Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205, 212 (Silberg).) Simply put, “communications in connection with matters related to a lawsuit are privileged under Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b).” (Sylmar Air Conditioning v. Pueblo Contracting Services (2004) 122 Cal.App.4th 1049, 1058.)¿ 

 

Moving Parties contend these allegations must be stricken because they are protected by the litigation privilege. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

The court disagrees for three reasons. First, Plaintiff’s claim is not based on the Notice of Termination of Tenancy, rather, the Notice of Termination of Tenancy is illustrative of Defendant Nickerson’s alleged bad acts. Second, the Notice of Termination of Tenancy is not part of any judicial proceeding and the court is unaware of authority indicating that a threatened unlawful detainer action that never comes to fruition is still protected by the litigation privilege. Finally, the court is unsure how Moving Parties have standing to raise this particular argument since the paragraph in question really pertains to Defendant Nickerson, not to Defendant Chahayed or Defendant Simi Oil.

 

Accordingly, the court DENIES Moving Parties’ motion to strike allegations under the litigation privilege.

 

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend should be liberally granted if there is a reasonable possibility an amendment could cure the defect.¿ (County of Santa Clara v. Superior Court (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 1018,1035.)¿ The Plaintiff has the burden of demonstrating that leave to amend should be granted, and that the defects can be cured by amendment. (“Plaintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349).¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiff has requested leave to amend. The court is skeptical that Plaintiff can amend concerning Defendant Simi Oil. However, out of an abundance of caution, the court will allow Plaintiff’s an opportunity to amend the FAC to allege factual allegations pertaining to Defendant Simi Oil and to otherwise allege facts that address the deficiencies noted above.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS leave to amend.
