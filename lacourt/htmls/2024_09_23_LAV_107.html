[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS ADMITTED

 

Dept. 107

9:00 a.m.

September 23, 2024

 

I.                BACKGROUND

On December 7, 2023, Plaintiffs Kelvin Garcia and Mark Pascua filed a complaint against Defendants Valerio RCFE Inc., Kittridge RCFE LLC, and Francis Martir, alleging causes of action for (1) willful misclassification; (2) failure to pay minimum wages; (3) failure to pay wages; (4) failure to pay overtime; (5) failure to authorize or permit meal breaks; (6) failure to authorize or permit rest breaks; (7) failure to furnish complete and accurate wage statements; (8) failure to indemnify for losses and expenditures; (9) failure to provide timely copies of personnel file and payroll records; (10) waiting time penalties; (11) constructive discharge in violation of public policy;  (12) whistleblower retaliation; and (13) discrimination.

On August 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this motion to deem requests for admissions admitted. On September 10, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition. On September 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply.

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

            “If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply: (b) The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted…. The Court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2033.210, 2033.220, and 2033.230. (2) the party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect…. (c) The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. (Code Civ. Proc. Section 2033.280.) 

 

Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with a motion to deem matters specified in a request for admissions as true.  (Code Civ. Proc. Section 2033.280(c).) 

              

III.           DISCUSSION

 

On June 27, 2024, Plaintiffs served Defendants by email at William@bailey.law, with Request for Admissions, Set One. (Simonian Decl., ¶ 3; Exhs. 1-3.) Responses were due by July 30, 2024. (Id., ¶ 4.) No responses were provided, and Plaintiffs sent a meet and confer letter on August 2, 2024. (Id., ¶ 5; Exh. 4.)

In opposition, Defendants argue that without obtaining consent from Defendants, Plaintiffs attempted service of the discovery by email. Defendants argue that absent consent, service by email of the discovery requests was improper. Defendants also argue that service of this motion by email was improper for the same reason. On August 2, 2024, Defendants served Plaintiffs with an E-Service Notice of Compliance with CCP § 1010.6, indicating they accept service at the following email addresses: spornbida@bailey.law; and service@bailey.law.

 In reply, Plaintiff provides that on August 2, 2024, Defendants’ counsel indicated that he “errored and did not notice that [Plaintiffs] sent discovery requests” and that he would get the discovery responses by August 16, 2024. (Simonian Reply Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. 1.)  No responses were provided as of the date of the reply. (Simonian Decl. Reply, ¶ 7.)

Electronic service is a permitted method of serving discovery requests. (Code Civ. Proc., sections 2016.050, 1013(g), 1011(c), and 1010.6.) Code Civ. Proc., section 1010.6(b)(2) provides that “a person represented by counsel, who has appeared in an action or proceeding, shall accept electronic service of a notice or document that may be served by mail, express mail, overnight delivery, or facsimile transmission.” 

 

Here, Defendants are represented by counsel and have appeared. They shall accept electronic service.

 

The court notes that Code Civ. Proc., section 1010.6(b)(3) provides that “before first serving a represented person electronically, the person effecting service shall confirm the appropriate electronic service address for the counsel being served.” However, by what means that confirmation should take place is not stated. In any event, it appears that Plaintiff had previously communicated by email with defense counsel at his email address, William@bailey.law. (See Simonian Reply Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. 1.) As such, this may be construed as confirmation.

Defendants cite to California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 2.251(b) which is entitled Electronic service by express consent, and states:

 (1) A party or other person indicates that the party or other person agrees to accept electronic service by:

(A)  Serving a notice on all parties and other persons that the party or other person accepts electronic service and filing the notice with the court. The notice must include the electronic service address at which the party or other person agrees to accept service; or

(B)  Manifesting affirmative consent through electronic means with the court or the court's electronic filing service provider, and concurrently providing the party's electronic service address with that consent for the purpose of receiving electronic service. A party or other person may manifest affirmative consent by serving notice of consent to all parties and other persons and either:

(i)  Agreeing to the terms of service with an electronic filing service provider, which clearly states that agreement constitutes consent to receive electronic service; or

(ii)  Filing Consent to Electronic Service and Notice of Electronic Service Address (form EFS-005-CV).

 

However, CRC Rule 2.251(b) is only one condition of electronic service. CRC Rule 2.251(c), entitled Electronic service required by local rule or court order, provides: “a party that is required to file documents electronically must also accept service of documents electronically…”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.251(c)(3).) Los Angeles Superior Court (“LASC”), Local Rules, rule 3.4(a) requires that all documents in the instant action be filed electronically. As such, because Defendants are required to file documents electronically under the local court rule, they must also accept discovery electronically.  Thus, electronic service of the discovery on defense counsel was proper.

 

Lastly, Defendants argue that Plaintiff failed to properly serve the motion for the same reason. Again, the court finds electronic service was proper. Even if it was not, any service issue as to the motion was waived by the opposition which addresses the merits, and because Defendants failed to assert any prejudice.

 

            In sum, as the discovery request for admissions was properly served, and Defendants failed to timely respond, the motion to deem requests for admissions is granted. The truth of the matters in Plaintiffs’ request for admissions, set one, served on Defendants is deemed admitted.

 

Sanctions in connection with this motion are mandatory. Thus, Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions is granted, however, the amount sought is not reasonable for this straightforward motion. As such, the court imposes sanctions in the amount of $718.91 ($650 per hour, for one hour, plus $68.91 filing fee), against Defendants and counsel of record William R. Bailey, jointly and severally, to be paid within 30 days of this order.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion to have matters in requests for admissions deemed admitted is GRANTED. The truth of the matters in Plaintiffs’ request for admissions, set one, served on Defendants is deemed admitted.

            Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions is GRANTED. Defendants and counsel of record William R. Bailey are ordered to pay Plaintiffs’ counsel monetary sanctions in the amount of $718.91, jointly and severally, within 30 days of this order.

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice.

I.                BACKGROUND

On July 8, 2024, Plaintiff Steven Higgins filed this unlawful detainer complaint against Defendants Kambiz Razaghi, Naznin Mojahed, and Tereza Teymourian.

On September 5, 2024, Defendants Razaghi and Mojahed filed this motion to quash service of summons and complaint. No opposition has been filed. (Defendant Tereza Teymourian filed a motion to quash on September 12,2024 which is set for October 9, 2024.)

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A defendant . . . may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:  (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her. . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10(a).)  “[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.]”  (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.)  “[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper” but only if it “complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.”  (Id. at 1441-1442.)  When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.”  (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.)  “A court lacks jurisdiction over a party if there has not been proper service of process.”  (Ruttenberg v. Ruttenberg (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 801, 808.) 

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

Defendants move to quash service of summons, arguing that Plaintiff did not properly serve them as the complaint was taped to their front door.

Here, no proof of service on the summons or Complaint has been filed. Thus, there is no assertion by Plaintiff that service has been properly effectuated.

Therefore, the motion challenging service of the summons and complaint is premature, as Plaintiff has not yet effected service of the summons and complaint on Defendant.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion to quash is denied as premature.

Defendants are ordered to give notice.
