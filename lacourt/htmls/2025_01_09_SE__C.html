Case Number: 20STCV49018    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: C

GAMEZ, ET AL. v. COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 20STCV49018

HEARING:  01/09/25

 

#9

 

I.                Defendants County of Los Angeles, Leticia Garcia, Francine Jimenez, and Gabriela Alcala’s Motion for Summary Judgment of Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is deemed to be a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings. The Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED with 30 days leave to amend.

 

II.              Cross-Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is DENIED. Cross-Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication is DENIED.

 

Opposing Party(s) to give notice.

 

Background

 

This wrongful death action was filed by Plaintiffs MARIA GAMEZ; K.G., a minor through her Guardian ad Litem, Maria Gamez; and A.G., a minor through his Guardian ad Litem Maria Gamez (collectively “Plaintiffs”) against Defendants COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES (“County”); LETICIA GARCIA; GABRIA ALCALA; FRANCINE JIMINEZ; GERALDO GAMEZ; TOMAS GAMEZ; MARIA GUADAULUPE GAMEZ and Does 1 to 100 on December 22, 2020.

On August 26, 2021, Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). The FAC alleges the following relevant facts: The County took custody of James Gamez (“J.G.”) and his two siblings, K.G. and A.G., from their mother and placed them with his paternal aunt and uncle. (FAC, ¶ 35.) During the 2019 to 2020 school year, J.G. experienced increasingly severe asthma, and the school called J.G.’s home. (FAC, ¶ 37.) But the school was unaware that J.G. had been placed in foster care; thus, it notified Decedent’s mother. (FAC, ¶ 37.) The school advised J.G.’s mother to notify the foster parents that they needed to take him to a doctor. (FAC, ¶ 37.) J.G.’s mother contacted both the County of Los Angeles and the foster parents. (FAC, ¶ 37.) The County advised the mother to contact the foster parents. (FAC, ¶ 38.) The foster parents disregarded the information. (FAC, ¶ 38.)

“[J.G.] was a nine year old male with a past medical history of chronic asthma, but very much active and alive. As a result of abuse and neglect by his foster parents, TOMAS and GUADALUPE, including failing to obtain proper medical treatment, and the COUNTY and DCFS failing to heed the warnings of not only his mother, MARIA, but also the warnings of his school’s administration, his condition worsened because of their medical neglect and other abuse.” (FAC ¶29.) “JAMES came home from a family party at his father, GERALDO’s, house around 11:30 p.m. on or about November 16, 2019. His father, GERALDO, and the foster parents, TOMAS and GUADALUPE disregarded [J.G.’s] asthma condition and smoked around him. [J.G.] indicated he was not feeling well, and eventually stated to his minor sister, K.G., that he did not feel well. His biological father, GERALDO, and his foster parents, TOMAS and GUADALUPE, ignored his urgent medical situation. [J.G.] told his sister KG that he was not feeling good and starting shaking and coughing. Their biological father, GERALDO, and the foster parents, TOMAS and GUADALUPE still ignoring the worsening medical situation, KG was forced to assist [J.G.] with his inhaler medication. However, KG noted that [J.G.] worsened and had seizure-like activity with his eyes rolling back, he turned pale and he syncopized and went unconscious. Their biological father, GERALDO, and the foster parents, TOMAS and GUADALUPE, still ignoring the pleas for help, KG was forced to call 911 herself. At some point, [J.G.] stopped breathing, and KG attempted CPR. When the fire department arrived on the scene they found [J.G.] supine on the ground in the bedroom. Legal Standard

 

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing no triable material fact issues.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  If the moving party meets this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a converse prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) 

 

A defendant’s motion for summary judgment ‘necessarily includes a test of the sufficiency of the complaint.  Motions for summary judgment in such situations have otherwise been allowed as being in legal effect motions for judgment on the pleadings. Thus, if the reviewing court finds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action as a matter of law, it need not reach the question whether plaintiff's opposition to the summary judgment motion raises a triable issue of fact. (Hansra v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 630, 638.)

Collateral Estoppel

 

Defendants argue collateral estoppel bars the issue of improper removal of the Gamez children because the dependency, or juvenile, court adjudicated the same issues on August 5, 2019.

 

It is the remedies available to the plaintiff in a forum of limited jurisdiction that determine whether collateral estoppel bars a subsequent claim in a different forum. (See, e.g. Cunningham v. United States (Fed. Cir. 2014) 748 F.3d 1172, 1180.) Juvenile court protects abused or neglected children and those at substantial risk and provides permanent, stable homes if those children cannot be returned home within a prescribed period. (Sara M. v. Super. Ct. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 998, 1008.) Its role is not to award damages.

 

Here, Plaintiffs seek monetary damages.

 

Thus, collateral estoppel does not bar the issue of improper removal of the Gamez children.  

 

          Public Entity and Public Employee Liability

 

A public entity is not liable for an injury, except as otherwise provided by statute. (Gov. Code, § 815, subd. (a).) Civil Code section 1714, which imposes a general duty of care on all persons, is an insufficient statutory basis for imposing direct liability on public agencies. (Eastburn v Regional Fire Protection Auth. (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1180.) No negligence cause of action may be asserted directly against a public entity, but under Government Code section 815.2, subdivision (a), vicarious liability may be imposed on public entities for the “tortious acts and omissions of their employees.” (See Comment to Gov. Code, § 815.2.) A public employee is liable for any injury caused by act or omission “to the same extent as a private person,” except as provided by statute. (Gov. Code, § 820, subd. (a).) Public employees have an array of immunities, including the immunity for discretionary acts (Gov. Code, § 820.2). 

 

Separately, Government Code section 815.6 contains a three-pronged test for determining whether liability may be imposed on a public entity: (1) an enactment must impose a mandatory, not discretionary, duty; (2) the enactment must intend to protect against the kind of risk of injury suffered by the party asserting section 815.6 as a basis for liability; and (3) breach of the mandatory duty must be a proximate cause of the injury suffered. If the public entity establishes that it exercised reasonable diligence to discharge the duty, it is not liable. (Gov. Code, § 815.6.) Whether an enactment is intended to impose a mandatory duty is a question of law for the court. (County of L. A. v. Super. Ct. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 627, 639.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to investigate or otherwise respond to the reported instances of child abuse and/or neglect; Defendants failed to properly conduct an assessment and develop a case plan; Defendants failed to conduct a basic evaluation of risks to determine whether an emergency existed; and Defendants failed to control the conduct of the father and the foster parents. (FAC, ¶¶ 56, 64, 65, 66.) Further, Plaintiffs allege the following statutes and regulations create mandatory duties: Welfare and Institutions Code section 328; Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501, subdivision (d); Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501, subdivision (f); Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501.1, subdivision (d); Welfare and Institutions Code section 16504, subdivision (a); DSS Regulations 31-101, 31-105, 31-110, 31-115, 31-120, 31-125, 31-128, 31-201, 31-205, 31-206. (FAC, ¶¶ 56, 64, 65, 66.) Plaintiffs also allege that as mandated reporters under Penal Code sections 11165.7(a)(15) and (18), Defendants did not report suspected child abuse.

 

The Court will first address whether the statutes and regulations impose mandatory duties as opposed to discretionary duties. In doing so, the Court finds instructive County of L.A. v. Super. Ct., supra, 102 Cal.App.4th 627 and Becerra v. County of Santa Cruz (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1450.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code section 328, subdivision (a)(2) states, “The social worker shall interview any child four years of age or older who is the subject of an investigation to ascertain the child’s view of the home environment.” This refers to the investigation to determine whether child welfare services should be offered to the family and whether proceedings in the juvenile court should commence. (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 328, subd. (a).) Thus, it does not apply to the purported wrongdoings alleged here: the actual placement and monitoring and supervision of the placement.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501, subdivision (f) states, “County welfare departments shall respond to any report of imminent danger of a child immediately.” Subdivision (d) states, “This chapter shall not be construed to affect duties that are delegated to probation officers pursuant to Sections 601 and 654.” What constitutes “imminent” danger lies at the discretion of the social worker. Thus, subdivision (f) imposes no mandatory duty. Separately, subdivision (d) imposes no duty.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501.1, subdivision (d) discusses the requirements of a case plan. This by itself does not impose a mandatory duty on the social workers.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code section 16504, subdivision (a) states, “Any child reported to the county child welfare services department to be endangered by abuse, neglect, or exploitation shall be eligible for initial intake and evaluation of risk services. Each county child welfare services department shall maintain and operate a 24-hour response system. An immediate in-person response shall be made by a county child welfare services department social worker in emergency situations in accordance with regulations of the department. The person making any initial response to a request for child welfare services shall consider providing appropriate social services to maintain the child safely in his or her own home. However, an in-person response is not required when the county child welfare services department, based upon an evaluation of risk, determines that an in-person response is not appropriate. An evaluation of risk includes collateral contacts, a review of previous referrals, and other relevant information.” The first part of this subdivision describes legislative goals, not mandatory duties. The latter part is about the initial response, so it does not apply to the situation presented in this case: the placement and monitoring and supervision of the placement. Thus, the statute imposes no mandatory duties.

 

DSS Regulation 31-101 states that the social worker responding to a referral shall be skilled in emergency response. (FAC, ¶ 59.) This is not a mandatory duty; rather, it is a legislative goal.

 

DSS Regulation 31-105 states that a social worker shall immediately initiate and complete the Emergency Response Protocol process to determine whether an in-person investigation is required. (FAC, ¶ 60.) Furthermore, the social worker shall record all available and appropriate information on the Emergency Response Protocol form or an approved substitute. (FAC, ¶ 60.) This describes the process for the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-110 is about in-person investigations, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-115 is about in-person immediate investigations, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-120 provides for in-person investigations within 10 calendar days, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-125 is about investigation requirements, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-128 is about law enforcement assistance, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-201 is about assessment and case planning, which is part of the process of determining what a child needs for his or her welfare once the State of California decides to intervene. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-205 is about assessment documentation, which is part of the process of the discretionary act of intervening for the child’s welfare. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-206 is about case plan documentation, which is part of the process of the discretionary act of intervening for the child’s welfare. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

Under the Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act, a mandated reporter shall make a report to an agency, specified in Section 11165.9, whenever the mandated reporter, in the mandated reporter's professional capacity or within the scope of the mandated reporter's employment, has knowledge of or observes a child whom the mandated reporter knows, or reasonably suspects has been the victim of child abuse or neglect. (Pen. Code, § 11166.) But this is a discretionary act because the mandated reporter must determine whether the child is a victim of child abuse or neglect.

 

Thus, none of the statutes and regulations Plaintiffs allege impose a mandatory duty.  To the extent that they are both mandatory and discretionary in nature, Plaintiffs’ allegations are directed at how Defendants exercised their discretion.  This is insufficient to state a cause of action for negligence. (County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 627 (county and social worker are immune from liability for the discretionary acts of the social worker in placing and supervising minor); Becerra v. County of Santa Cruz (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1450 (same).) 

 

Because the negligence theory was not adequately pleaded, Defendants have no burden to show that Plaintiff’s negligence claim cannot be established.  The Court deems the motion for summary judgment to be a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Hansra v. Super. Ct. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 630, 638-639.) Defendants therefore have no obligation to adduce evidence to negate the negligence theory. 

 

Because this issue is dispositive, the Court will not adjudicate Defendants’ causation arguments.

 

Thus, the motion for judgment on the pleadings is granted as to the negligence cause of action.

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow the court to determine whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and if not, to allow an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843 (Atlantic Richfield).) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c) requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and “all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence” and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the moving party must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) A cause of action cannot be established if the undisputed facts presented by defendant prove the contrary of plaintiff's allegations¿as a matter of law. (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1597.) To show a complete defense, the defendant must present admissible evidence of¿each essential element¿of the defense upon which it bears the burden of proof at trial.¿(Anderson v. Metalclad Insulation Corp.¿(1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 284, 289.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the moving party has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162.) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Id. at pg. 166.) 

 

Discussion

 

Collateral Estoppel

 

Cross-Defendants argue that collateral estoppel bars the issue of improper removal of the Gamez children because the dependency, or juvenile, court adjudicated the same issues on August 5, 2019.

 

It is the remedies available to the plaintiff in a forum of limited jurisdiction that determine whether collateral estoppel bars a subsequent claim in a different forum. (See, e.g. Cunningham v. United States (Fed. Cir. 2014) 748 F.3d 1172, 1180.) Juvenile court protects abused or neglected children and those at substantial risk and provides permanent, stable homes if those children cannot be returned home within a prescribed period. (Sara M. v. Super. Ct. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 998, 1008.) Its role is not to award damages.

 

Here, Cross-Complainant seeks monetary damages.

 

Thus, collateral estoppel does not bar the issue of improper removal of the Gamez children.

 

First and Second Causes of Action: Violation of Civil Rights under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 and Monell Liability

 

Cross-Defendants argue causation and assert that it applies to Government Code section 815.2, not any other cause of action. Yet Cross-Defendants move for summary judgment as to the entire cross-complaint. Even though Cross-Defendants are unclear as to whether they are also moving for summary judgment and adjudication as to the alleged violations of 42 U.S.C. § 1983, the Court will adjudicate Cross-Defendants’ causation arguments as it applies to the two alleged violations of 42 U.S.C. § 1983. Cross-Defendants argue that state law immunity applies, but state law immunity does not apply to 42 U.S.C. § 1983 actions brought in either state or federal court.¿(Martinez v. Calif.¿(1980) 444 U.S. 277, 284 n8.)

 

General tort principles of proximate cause apply to 42 U.S.C. § 1983 actions. (Martinez v. Calif., supra, 444 U.S. 277 at pg. 285.)

 

As framed by the pleadings, Cross-Defendants breached their duties to Cross-Complainants and decedent by wrongfully removing decedent from the custody of his parents and by placing and maintaining him in placements depriving him of essential minimally adequate living conditions suitable for J.G.’s chronic asthma and allergy to cats and dogs and/or violations of civil and personal rights. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 42.) Further, Cross-Defendants refused to place decedent in the care of other relatives who had no cats and dogs and were willing to take in decedent. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 43.) As a direct and proximate result of Cross-Defendants’ acts, decedent suffered injuries, including loss of his own life and other damages. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 51.) As to the second 42 U.S.C. § 1983 cause of action, the gist of the allegations is the same.

 

Cross-Defendants argue that the actual and proximate cause of J.G.’s injuries was an asthma attack that was not timely or properly treated, first by his father and later by foster parents. Cross-Defendants do not attribute J.G.’s injuries to any conduct by the county.  

 

The pleadings serve as the “outer measure of materiality” in a summary judgment motion, and the motion may not be granted or denied on issues not raised by the pleadings. (Laabs v. City of Victorville¿(2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1258.) Here, Cross-Defendants do not directly address Cross-Complainant’s causation allegations but assert their own theory about why J.G. died. On this ground, the Court denies summary judgment and summary adjudication.

 

Further, causation is ordinarily a question of fact which cannot be resolved by summary judgment. (Kaney v. Custance (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 201, 212.) The issue of causation may be decided as a question of law only if, under undisputed facts, there is no room for a reasonable difference of opinion. (Ibid.)

 

As discussed above, there is a difference of opinion about why J.G. died. Thus, causation is not an issue that can be resolved by summary judgment.

 

Therefore, summary judgment and summary adjudication as to the first and second causes of action are DENIED. 

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Negligence

 

The Court addresses the fifth cause of action of breach of duties under special relationship, which is a type of negligence.

 

A public entity is not liable for an injury, except as otherwise provided by statute. (Gov. Code, § 815, subd. (a).) Civil Code section 1714, which imposes a general duty of care on all persons, is an insufficient statutory basis for imposing direct liability on public agencies. (Eastburn v Regional Fire Protection Auth. (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1180.) 

 

No negligence cause of action may be asserted directly against a public entity, but under Government Code section 815.2, subdivision (a), vicarious liability may be imposed on public entities for the “tortious acts and omissions of their employees.” (See Comment to Gov. Code, § 815.2.) A public employee is liable for any injury caused by act or omission “to the same extent as a private person,” except as provided by statute. (Gov. Code, § 820, subd. (a).) But public employees have an array of immunities, including the immunity for discretionary acts. (Gov. Code, § 820.2). 

 

Cross-Complainants allege that Cross-Defendants breached their duty of care to J.G. by failing and neglecting to fulfill or perform their duties to control, care for, and protect the health, safety, and well-being of J.G. while he was in foster care. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 85.) As a result, J.G. lived with a cat and dog despite his allergic reactions to them and his chronic asthma, which led to the escalation of his asthma attack and his death. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 86.) His chronic asthma could have and would have been discovered if Cross-Defendants had exercised due care, including examining medical records, communicating with his family, adhering to the county’s procedures and policies, conducting in-person interviews, and engaging in other methods of supervising, caring, and overseeing dependent minors. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 86.)

 

Under in Becerra v. County of Santa Cruz (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1450, 1462, the determination to place a child in a particular foster home is a discretionary act, immune from liability according to Government Code section 820.2. Similarly, the determination to intervene and discontinue a child’s placement is a discretionary act, immune from liability. (Id. at pg. 1466.) However, immunity does not extend to injuries inflicted by the execution of a County policies or customs.. (See Alicia T. V. County of Los Angeles (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 869, 881-882.) Here, the Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges throughout the Cross-Complaint that DCFS failed to conduct adequate safety and risk assessments of foster parents, consistent with their prevalent custom and practices. (See, e.g., Cross-Compl. ¶17.)

 

Moreover, Plaintiff presents evidence of the following:

 

·       Leticia Garcia did not do anything to become acquainted with what steps were necessary to treat J.G.’s asthma. (Nguyen Decl., ¶7, Ex. 5, Leticia Garcia Deposition, p. 183:10-13.)

·       Leticia Garcia did not take any steps to make sure that the foster parents were aware of what was needed to treat J.G.’s asthma. (Id., p. 184:3-6.)

·       Leticia Garcia did not know that pets triggered J.G.’s asthma. (Id., p. 184:7-12.)

·       Gabriela Alcala did not know J.G. had a nebulizer and she did not have any conversations with anyone about a nebulizer. (Nguyen Decl., ¶9, Ex. 7., Gabriela Alcala Deposition, p. 112:10-13.)

·       Gabriela Alcala did not check with the foster parents to see if they would be willing to administer medication. (Id., p. 113:15-18.)

·       Gabriele Alcala did not train the foster parents on how to use the nebulizer. (Id., p. 113:19-22.)

·       Gabriela Alcala did not know what triggered J.G.’s asthma. (Id., p. 145:17-19.)

 

The Court determines that this evidence is sufficient to raise a triable issue of material fact as to the fifth cause of action for Breach of Duties under Special Relationship. 

 

Accordingly, summary adjudication as to the fifth cause of action is DENIED. 

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Breach of Mandatory Duties

 

Government Code section 815.6 contains a three-pronged test for determining whether liability may be imposed on a public entity: (1) an enactment must impose a mandatory, not discretionary, duty; (2) the enactment must intend to protect against the kind of risk of injury suffered by the party asserting section 815.6 as a basis for liability; and (3) breach of the mandatory duty must be a proximate cause of the injury suffered. If the public entity establishes that it exercised reasonable diligence to discharge the duty, it is not liable. (Gov. Code, § 815.6.) Whether an enactment is intended to impose a mandatory duty is a question of law for the court. (County of L. A. v. Super. Ct. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 627, 639.)

 

Cross-Complainant alleges that the following statutes and regulations create mandatory duties: Welfare and Institutions Code section 328; Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501, subdivision (d); Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501, subdivision (f); Welfare and Institutions Code section 16504, subdivision (a); DSS Regulations 31-101, 31-105, 31-110, 31-115, 31-120, 31-125, 31-135, 31-137, 31-201, 31-205, 31-210, 31-215, 31-225, 31-230, 31-310, 31-320, 31-330, 31-401, 31-405, 31-420, 31-445, and 31-501. (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 71-72.) Cross-Complainants also allege that as mandated reporters under Penal Code sections 11166, et seq., Cross-Defendants did not report suspected child abuse. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 72.)

 

The Court will address whether the statutes and regulations impose mandatory duties as opposed to discretionary duties. In doing so, the Court finds instructive County of L.A. v. Super. Ct., supra, 102 Cal.App.4th 627 and Becerra v. County of Santa Cruz (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1450.

 

As it applies to the instant action, Welfare and Institutions Code section 328 concerns whether child welfare services should be offered to the family. Thus, it does not apply to the purported wrongdoings alleged here: the actual placement and monitoring and supervision of the placement.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code section 16501, subdivision (f) states, “County welfare departments shall respond to any report of imminent danger of a child immediately.” Subdivision (d) states, “This chapter shall not be construed to affect duties that are delegated to probation officers pursuant to Sections 601 and 654.” What constitutes “imminent” danger lies at the discretion of the social worker. Thus, subdivision (f) imposes no mandatory duty. Separately, subdivision (d) imposes no duty.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code section 16504, subdivision (a) states, “Any child reported to the county child welfare services department to be endangered by abuse, neglect, or exploitation shall be eligible for initial intake and evaluation of risk services. Each county child welfare services department shall maintain and operate a 24-hour response system. An immediate in-person response shall be made by a county child welfare services department social worker in emergency situations in accordance with regulations of the department. The person making any initial response to a request for child welfare services shall consider providing appropriate social services to maintain the child safely in his or her own home. However, an in-person response is not required when the county child welfare services department, based upon an evaluation of risk, determines that an in-person response is not appropriate. An evaluation of risk includes collateral contacts, a review of previous referrals, and other relevant information.” The first part of this subdivision describes legislative goals, not mandatory duties. The latter part is about the initial response, so it does not apply to the situation presented in this case: the placement and monitoring and supervision of the placement. Thus, the statute imposes no mandatory duties.

 

DSS Regulation 31-101 states that the social worker responding to a referral shall be skilled in emergency response. (FAC, ¶ 59.) This is not a mandatory duty; rather, it is a legislative goal.

 

DSS Regulation 31-105 states that a social worker shall immediately initiate and complete the Emergency Response Protocol process to determine whether an in-person investigation is required. (FAC, ¶ 60.) Furthermore, the social worker shall record all available and appropriate information on the Emergency Response Protocol form or an approved substitute. (FAC, ¶ 60.) This describes the process for the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-110 is about in-person investigations, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-115 is about in-person immediate investigations, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-120 requires in-person investigations within 10 calendar days, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-125 is about investigation requirements, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-128 is about law enforcement assistance, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-135 is about ensuring there is authority to remove a child from the home, which is part of the discretionary act of removing a child from a home. It does not impose a mandatory duty. But a part of DSS Regulation 31-135 is about requiring documenting in the case record pre-placement preventive efforts, and Cross-Complainants assert that Cross-Defendants violated it. This is a mandatory, not discretionary duty, because it amounts only to obedience of orders. (See Scott v. County of L.A. (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 125, 141-142.)

 

DSS Regulation 31-137 sets forth guidelines for transitional care prior to placement. This does not impose a mandatory duty. 

 

DSS Regulation 31-201 is about assessment and case planning, which is part of the process of determining what a child needs for his or her welfare once the State of California decides to intervene. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-205 is about assessment documentation, which is part of the process of the discretionary act of intervening for the child’s welfare. It does not impose a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-210 and 31-215 are about case plan time frames, and Cross-Complainants allege Cross-Defendants violated the regulations by not completing a case plan within 30 days of removal. This, too, is a mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-225 and 31-230 are about case plan documentations. While there are some parts of the two regulations that are discretionary, there are also mandatory components. DSS Regulation 31-225.2 states, “The case plan update shall be considered complete only if all of the elements specified in Section 31-225 have been documented and the social worker’s supervisor has signed and dated the case plan update.” DSS Regulation 31-230.1.11 states, “At a minimum, the social worker shall complete a case plan update in conjunction with each status review hearing,” and at least once every six months. Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendants failed to update case plan documentation.

 

DSS Regulation 31-310 sets forth social worker responsibilities. Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendants failed to monitor J.G.’s health and medical condition and provide services appropriate to those needs; and failed to assist his parents and foster parents to understand agency procedures and orders of the court. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 72.) These are discretionary acts.

 

DSS Regulation 31-320 and 31-330 sets forth social worker requirements for contact with the child and out-of-home care provider, respectively. Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendants failed to adequately conduct face-to-face contacts with J.G. and the caregiver. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 72.) While the regulations contain mandatory duties, Cross-Complainant’s allegations are about discretionary duties.

 

DSS Regulation 31-401 and 31-445 are about requirements for placement, including with extended family members. Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendants violated those regulations by placing J.G. in inadequately trained foster home incapable of meeting his needs and not fulfilling social worker responsibilities for placement. Although the regulations set forth mandatory duties, Cross-Complainants allegations are about discretionary duties.

 

DSS Regulation 31-405 sets forth guidelines for social worker responsibilities for placement. They do not impose mandatory duties. Further, Cross-Complainant merely alleges that Cross-Defendants failed to fulfill the social worker responsibilities for placement and does not allege breach of any mandatory duty.

 

DSS Regulation 31-420 sets forth foster care placement guidelines. These are discretionary and do not impose a mandatory duty. Further, Cross-Complainant does not allege any mandatory duty was breached.

 

DSS Regulation 31-501 sets forth child abuse and neglect reporting requirements. These are discretionary, not mandatory, duties because there needs to be a determination of what is child abuse and neglect.

 

Under the Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act, a mandated reporter shall make a report to an agency, specified in Section 11165.9, whenever the mandated reporter, in the mandated reporter's professional capacity or within the scope of the mandated reporter's employment, has knowledge of or observes a child whom the mandated reporter knows, or reasonably suspects has been the victim of child abuse or neglect. (Pen. Code, § 11166.) But this is a discretionary act because the mandated reporter must determine whether the child is a victim of child abuse or neglect.

 

Next, the Court will consider Cross-Defendants’ arguments.

 

Cross-Defendants argue that they satisfied their mandatory duties. But Cross-Defendants do not argue that they satisfied their mandatory duties under DSS Regulations 31-135, 31-210, 31-215, 31-225, and 31-230.  Thus, the Court determines that the fourth cause of action in the Cross-Complaint is adequately pleaded, and Cross-Defendants have not met their burden to show that there are no triable issues of material fact as to breach of mandatory duties.

 

The motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth cause of action for Breach of Mandatory Duties is DENIED. 

 

Third Cause of Action: Wrongful Death

 

The elements of a cause of action for wrongful death are a tort and resulting death.” (Lopez v. City of L.A. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 675, 685.) Cross-Complainants plead negligence to support the wrongful death claim.

 

Given the Court’s analysis above, summary adjudication as to the third cause of action for wrongful death is DENIED. 

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Survival Action

 

Cross-Defendants also do not separately or independently argue the survival action. But considering the allegations, the Court finds that opposing parties had adequate notice of the issues. (Folberg v. Clara G.R. Kinney Co., supra, 104 Cal.App.3d 136 at pgs. 139-140.)

 

Because the Court denied summary adjudication as to the first, fourth and fifth causes of action, summary adjudication as to the survival action is DENIED.

Case Number: 23NWCV03903    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: C

Estate of Lori Braza, et al. vs City of Bellflower, et al.

Case No.: 23NWCV03903

Hearing Date: January 9, 2025

 

#5

Tentative Ruling

Defendant City of Bellflower’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.  Defendant to answer within 10 days. 

Plaintiffs to give notice.

 

Background

This is a personal injury case.  In a Complaint filed on December 1, 2023, Plaintiffs Estate of Lori Braza and Julia Braza as Successor-in-Interest to Decedent Lori Braza (“Plaintiffs”) allege that on or about December 20, 2022, “Decedent LORI BRAZA was lawfully walking on Cedar St. near 8536-8522 Cedar St. when she tripped on an uneven, broken, raised and/or deteriorating portion of the pavement and/or sidewalk. This caused Plaintiff to fall and sustain serious injuries and damages.” (Complaint, pp. 6-8.) Plaintiffs assert causes of action for General Negligence and Premises Liability against City of Bellflower, County of Los Angeles, State of California, California Department of Transportation, and Cedar Private Homes. 

Defendant City of Bellflower (“the City”) demurs to the First and Second Causes of Action on the grounds that Plaintiffs failed to comply with the mandatory provisions of the Government Claims Act before filing the instant lawsuit. 

Evidentiary Objections

The Court rules as follows upon the objections made by Plaintiffs:

Objection Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 are OVERRULED.

Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”).  

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

Discussion

Lori Braza died on July 27, 2023.  The City argues that Plaintiffs Estate of Lori Braza and Julia Braza, as Successor-in-Interest of Decedent Lori Braza, were each required to file their own wrongful death claim with the City on or before January 29, 2024, which was six months after the death of Lori Braza. 

 

The California Tort Claims Act (Act) (Gov.Code, § 810 et seq.) governs actions against public entities and public employees. The timeliness of such actions is governed by the specific statute of limitations set forth in the Government Code, not the statute of limitations applicable to private defendants. (County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1267.) Under the Act, no person may sue a public entity or public employee for “money or damages” unless a timely written claim has been presented to and denied by the public entity. (Gov.Code, § 945.4.) A claim pertaining to a cause of action for personal injury must be filed within six months after the cause of action accrues. (County of Los Angeles, supra, 127 Cal.App.4th at 1267.) “The general rule for defining the accrual of a cause of action sets the date as the time ‘when, under the substantive law, the wrongful act is done,’ or the wrongful result occurs, and the consequent ‘liability arises.’ [Citation.] In other words, it sets the date as the time when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements.” (Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 397.)

 

Failure to allege facts in the complaint demonstrating compliance with the prelitigation governmental claims presentation requirements subjects the complaint to a general demurrer. (State of Calif. v. Sup. Ct. (Bodde) (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1239.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs have adequately pleaded compliance with the California Tort Claims Act.  The Complaint alleges that the City was served with a claim for damages on June 9, 2023. (Complaint, Attachment page, Second Cause of Action.) This date is within six-months of the date of injury on December 20, 2022. 

 

Survival causes of action are governed by CCP §377.30 which provides: “A cause of action that survives the death of the person entitled to commence an action or proceeding passes to the decedent’s successor in interest … and an action may be commenced by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” Therefore, this lawsuit, which survives the death of Lori Braza, is properly commenced by her Successor-in-Interest, Julia Braza. 

 

The City argues that Plaintiffs were required to file separate wrongful death claims within 6 months of Lori Braza’s death.  This argument presumes that Plaintiffs are seeking damages for wrongful death.  They are not.  Unlike a wrongful death action, a survival action is a cause of action that existed while the decedent is alive and survives the decedent. The decedent's personal representative or successor in interest may commence that action. (Grant v. McAuliffe (1953) 41 Cal.2d 859, 864; Quiroz v. Seventh Avenue Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1264 [“Unlike a cause of action for wrongful death, a survivor cause of action is not a new cause of action that vests in the heirs on the death of the decedent. It is instead a separate and distinct cause of action which belonged to the decedent before death but, by statute, survives that event.”] Here, because Plaintiffs do not allege a cause of action for wrongful death, they need not allege that a wrongful death claim was filed with the City in compliance with the California Tort Claims Act.

 

At the hearing on November 12, 2024, the City requested a continuance in order to provide supplemental briefing about Plaintiffs’ recent request to file a late wrongful death claim.  The City contended that the request to file a late claim contains allegations which contradict the Complaint.  However, even if the Court were to take judicial notice of Plaintiffs’ request to file a late claim, the Court may not take judicial notice of the truth of the allegations contained therein, absent an exception to the hearsay rule. (Richtek USA, Inc. v. uPI Semiconductor Corp. (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 651, 659-60.) Thus, a continuance was not granted for this purpose. 

 

The City also asserted that Plaintiffs have not filed a statement in compliance with CCP § 377.32.  This point was well-taken.  The Court, on its own motion, continued Defendant City of Bellflower’s Demurrer to January 9, 2025 and ordered Plaintiff Julia Braza as Successor-in-Interest to Lori Braza to file and serve a statement in compliance with CCP § 377.32 no later than December 12, 2024. 

 

On December 11, 2024, Lori Braza’s spouse, Arnold Catain Braza, filed a declaration stating, among other things, that he and Lori Braza had been separated for many years before her death, but they did not obtain a legal divorce. (Arnold Braza Decl., ¶3.) Arnold Braza relinquishes any right to act as successor in interest to Lori Braza and consents to the appointment of Julie Braza (the daughter or Arnold and Lori Braza) as the successor in interest to Lori Braza. (Id., ¶¶5, 7.) On December 11, 2024, Julie Braza filed her own declaration in compliance with CCP § 377.32.

 

The City raises various objections to the declarations of Arnold and Julie Braza, including the argument that Julie Braza does not claim to be the personal representative of Decedent’s estate.  However, for reasons discussed above, Julie Braza need not make such an allegation because the operative complaint does not assert a claim for wrongful death. 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.  Defendant to answer within 20 days.

CONTRERAS v. ORTEGA

HEARING:  01/09/25

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#3

 

Defendant CAMACHO TAPIA, INC.’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Court Clerk to give notice.

 

 

No Opposition(s) filed as of January 6, 2025.

 

This personal injury action was filed by Plaintiff FRANK CONTRERAS (“Plaintiff”) against Defendants IRENE ORTEGA; VICTOR ORTEGA; and MARTHA VASQUEZ (collectively “Defendants”) on May 4, 2023.

 

On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff’s Complaint was dismissed without prejudice because as of that date, Defendants had not been served with process of the Complaint. (See 11/15/23 M.O.)

 

Plaintiff now moves to set aside the dismissal under CCP §473(b).

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted….” (emphasis added.) (CCP §473(b).)

 

Here, there is no copy of any proposed pleading/First Amended Complaint attached to or lodged in conjunction with the subject Motion.

 

The Motion is DENIED without prejudice due to Plaintiff’s failure to attach a copy of a pleading proposed to be filed therein as required by CCP §473(b). The terms of the code are mandatory; compliance is not optional.
