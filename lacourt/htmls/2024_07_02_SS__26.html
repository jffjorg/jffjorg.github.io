Case Number: 21STLC05836    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Llorens v. Lee, et al.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

(CCP § 437c)

TENTATIVE RULING: 

 

Defendant Rachel Nicole Lee’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. MOVING DEFENDANT IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Luelle Llorens (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence against Defendant Rachel Nicole Lee (“Defendant”) on August 9, 2021. Defendant filed an answer on May 4, 2022. On June 27, 2022, the Court granted Plaintiff’s counsel’s motion to be relieved. (Minute Order, 06/27/22.)

 

On May 3, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, and Request for Sanctions, and Motion to Compel Responses to Demand for Inspection, and Request for Sanctions. (Minute Order, 05/03/23.) The Court denied Defendant’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions on January 4, 2024.

 

Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment on April 15, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

The Complaint alleges causes of action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence based on a motor vehicle accident that occurred on August 15, 2019 on the SR-90 westbound near Lincoln Boulevard in the City of Marina del Rey, Los Angeles, California. (Compl., ¶¶MV-1.) Specifically, the Complaint alleges Defendant was not paying attention and failed to adequately and properly drive at a safe speed, when operating the subject motor vehicle, thereby causing Plaintiff damage and serious injuries. (Id. at ¶GN-1.)

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment on the Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c. A defendant seeking summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established; or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) Plaintiff is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until Defendant meets its initial moving burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839-840.)

 

Defendant’s Initial Burden of Proof

 

The Motion is brought on the grounds that Plaintiff has admitted facts showing conclusively that (1) Defendant’s alleged negligence was not a substantial factor in Plaintiff’s injuries; and (2) Plaintiff suffered no damages related to the accident. Therefore, Plaintiff cannot establish the elements of causation or damages. (See County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.) In support of the Motion, Defendant presents the following facts. On November 2, 2022, Defendant served Requests for Admission, Set One, on Plaintiff. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 2; Blamires Decl., Exh. A.) Following Plaintiff’s failure to serve responses, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted on August 30, 2023. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 3-5; Blamires Decl., ¶¶3-5 and Exh. B.) Via the admissions, Plaintiff admits that Defendant was not a substantial factor in causing her alleged injuries, that Plaintiff was not injured in the subject accident, that Plaintiff did not receive medical treatment as a result of the accident, that Plaintiff did not lose any income as a result of the subject accident, that Plaintiff will not lose any future income as a result of the subject accident, and that Plaintiff did not suffer any loss of earning capacity as a result of the subject accident. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 6-11; Blamires Decl., Exh. A, Request Nos. 1-6.) This evidence of Plaintiff’s admissions, as established by the Court’s August 30, 2023 order, carries Defendant’s initial burden of proof that Plaintiff cannot establish the elements of causation and damages.

 

The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate the existence of a triable issue of material fact. Plaintiff, however, has not filed any opposition to the instant Motion.

 

Conclusion

 

Therefore, Defendant Rachel Nicole Lee’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. DEFENDANT IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

Case Number: 23AHCV01818    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Palmer v. Torres, et al.

MOTION TO SUBSTITUTE SUCCESSOR IN INTEREST

(CCP § 377.10, et seq.)


TENTATIVE RULING

 

Non-party Bobby Marcellus Gould’s Motion to Substitute Bobby Marcellus Gould, an Individual and as Successor In Interest To Decedent Simone Palmer, is DENIED.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

The instant action was commenced by Plaintiff Simone Palmer (“Palmer”), Ahnesti Johnson, and Knowledge Johnson (“minor Plaintiffs”) on August 10, 2023. The action for motor vehicle negligence is brought against Defendants Hortencia Reyes Torres and Samantha Reyes Torres (“Defendants”). On May 9, 2024, Moving Party Bobby Marcus Gould (“Gould”) filed the instant Motion to Substitute Bobby Marcellus Gould, an Individual and as Successor In Interest To Decedent Simone Palmer. The Motion was initially heard on June 12, 2024 and continued to allow Gould to file supplemental papers. (Minute Order, 06/12/24.) No additional papers, nor opposition has been filed to date.

 

 

 

Discussion

 

Gould moves to be substituted into this action, following the death of Palmer, as her surviving husband and the successor in interest of the Estate of Simone Palmer. Code of Civil Procedure, section 377.31 states, “[o]n motion after the death of a person who commenced an action or proceeding, the court shall allow a pending action or proceeding that does not abate to be continued by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.31.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 377.11 defines “successor-in-interest” as “the beneficiary of the decedent’s estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.11.)

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 377.32:

 

(a) The person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest under this article, shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating all of the following:

 

(1) The decedent’s name.

(2) The date and place of the decedent's death.

(3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent's estate.”

(4) If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent's cause of action to the successor in interest.

(5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof:

 

(A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.”

(B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.”

 

(6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.”

 

(7) “The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.”

 

(b) Where more than one person executes the affidavit or declaration under this section, the statements required by subdivision (a) shall be modified as appropriate to reflect that fact.

 

(c) A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32.)

 

Discussion

 

The Motion is supported by the declarations of Gould and of Palmer’s attorney, to which is attached a certified copy of the death certificate. (Motion, Gould Decl., Exh. B.) The declarations set forth some but not all of the information required by Code of Civil Procedure, section 377.32.

 

Gould’s declaration states Palmer’s name, and that “no proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate.” (Id. at ¶¶1, 5.) There is no statement, however, of Palmer’s date and place of death. Gould also declares that there has been no administration of Palmer’s estate. (Id. at ¶¶5-6.) Finally, Gould declares he is Palmer’s successor in interest, as set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 377.11. (Id. at ¶6.) Section 377.11 defines successor in interest as the “beneficiary of the decedent’s estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.11.) Neither the declaration of Gould nor Palmer’s counsel, however, set forth supporting facts to show that Gould is the beneficiary of Palmer’s estate or that he succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of a cause of action.

 

The supporting declarations, therefore, do not comply with the statutory requirements set forth above.

 

Conclusion

 

Non-party Bobby Marcellus Gould’s Motion to Substitute Bobby Marcellus Gould, an Individual and as Successor In Interest To Decedent Simone Palmer, is DENIED.

Case Number: 23STLC02523    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Hurtado v. Counts, et al.

VACATE DEFAULT AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT

(CCP §§ 473(b))


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Catarina Counts’ Motion to Vacate Entry of Default is GRANTED ON CONDITION THAT A COPY OF THE PROPOSED RESPONSIVE PLEADING IS FILED BY THE TIME OF THE HEARING.

 

                                                                                                                               

ANALYSIS:

 

On April 18, 2023, Plaintiff Valerie Hurtado (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for motor vehicle negligence against Defendants Catarina Counts (“Defendant Counts”) and Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“Defendant Metro”) (erroneously sued as “Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transit Authority”). Following Defendant Count’s failure to file a responsive pleading, the Court entered their default on September 25, 2023. Defendants then filed answers to the Complaint on November 17, 2023.

 

Defendant Counts filed the instant Motion to Vacate Default on March 22, 2024. The Motion initially came for hearing on April 22, 2024, at which time the Court continued the matter to allow Defendant to file a supplemental brief. (Minute Order, 04/22/24.) At the hearing on April 22, 2024, the matter was continued again to allow for Defendant’s supplemental briefing. Supplemental declarations were filed on June 7, 2024, which the Court did not see in time for the hearing on June 11, 2024. At the June 11, 2024 hearing, the Court continued the matter to allow Plaintiff to file a supplemental brief within ten days. 

 

Discussion

 

The Court already has stricken Defendant Counts’ answer filed on November 17, 2023 pursuant to its inherent authority under Code of Civil Procedure section 436, subdivision (b). The answer was filed while Defendant Counts was in default and not permitted to file a responsive pleading. (Sporn v. Home Depot USA, Inc. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 1294, 1301 [holding that entry of default cuts off the defendant's right to take further affirmative steps such as filing a pleading or motion].)

 

The Motion to Vacate is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), which states that an application for relief must be made within a reasonable time, no more than six  months after entry of the order from which relief is sought, and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect of the moving party or its attorney. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) The motion must also be accompanied by a copy of the moving defendant’s proposed pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) This can be corrected if the defendant submits a proposed responsive pleading by the hearing date. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403.)

 

Where the statute requires that “ ‘ “the application must be made within a ‘reasonable time’ … what is a reasonable time in any case depends upon the circumstances of that particular case.” While in “the determination of that question, a large discretion is necessarily confided to [the trial] court” ... there must be some showing—some evidence—as the basis for the exercise of such discretion.’ ” (Caldwell v. Methodist Hospital (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1521, 1524 [citing Carrasco v. Craft (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 796, 805].)

 

Defendant Counts now provides a declaration that states that upon being served with the Summons and Complaint on July 31, 2023, she reached out to her supervisor at Defendant Metro. (Motion, Supp. Counts Decl., ¶3.) The supervisor stated that since Defendant Metro was named as a defendant, its legal department would have a copy of the lawsuit and would timely respond on behalf of Defendant Counts. (Ibid.) Defendant Metro, however, was not served with the lawsuit until October 26, 2023. (Motion, Supp. Yehoshua Decl., ¶4.) This was after the date for Defendant Counts to respond, at which point Plaintiff had already taken her default. (Id. at ¶¶3-4.) Defendant Counts also reiterates that she only first discovered the entry of default on March 21, 2024, the day before hearing the instant Motion. (Motion, Supp. Counts Decl., ¶4.) Defense counsel also points out that the answer filed on behalf of Defendant Counts on November 17, 2023 was accepted by the Court and that Plaintiff propounded discovery on Defendant Counts on January 30, 2024. (Id. at ¶¶6-8.) As a result, defense counsel did not discover the entry of default until later reviewing the docket. (Id. at ¶9.)

 

This additional evidence demonstrates that Defendant Counts sought relief shortly after learning of the entry of default and that her failure to timely respond to the Complaint was due to mistakenly believing that Defendant Metro would handle the defense of the action. This mistake was reasonable in light of Defendant Counts’ conversation with her supervisor. The Court notes that the Motion is not accompanied by a copy of Defendant Counts’ proposed answer, which must be submitted by the time of the hearing.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Catarina Counts’ Motion to Vacate Entry of Default is GRANTED ON CONDITION THAT A COPY OF THE PROPOSED RESPONSIVE PLEADING IS FILED BY THE TIME OF THE HEARING.

Case Number: 23STLC02679    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Taing v. Worldwide ATM, Inc., et al.

MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO REQUEST TO INSPECT

WRITINGS TO ENFORCE ORDER; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(CCP §§ 680.230, 680.270, 708.030)

TENTATIVE RULING:  

           

Plaintiff Richard Heng Taing dba USA Donuts’ Motion to Compel Responses to Request to Inspect Writings to Enforce Order Dated 11-20-23, and Request for Sanctions is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On April 24, 2023, Plaintiff Richard Heng Taing DBA USA Donuts (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendant Worldwide ATM, Inc. (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer and cross-complaint on June 28, 2024 and July 5, 2023, respectively. On November 20, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Special and Form Interrogatories and awarded sanctions of $1,043.61 against Defendant. (Minute Order, 11/20/23.) The sanctions were to be paid within 20 days’ service of the order. (Ibid.)

 

On March 5, 2024, the Court granted defense counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel. (Minute Order, 03/05/24.) On May 7, 2024, at the order to show cause regarding legal representation for Defendant, there was no appearance by Defendant and the Court struck its answer. (Minute Order, 05/07/24.)

 

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Responses to Request to Inspect Writings to Enforce Order Dated 11-20-23, and Request for Sanctions on May 22, 2023. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

The Motion is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 680.230, 680.270, and 708.030 on the grounds that any court order requiring the payment of money is treated as a money judgment.

 

Monetary sanction orders are enforceable through the execution of judgment laws. (Weil & Brown, Cal.Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial 3 (1994) ¶¶ 9:344.21, 9:344.22, p. 9(1)–92.) These orders have the force and effect of a money judgment, and are immediately enforceable through execution, except to the extent the trial court may order a stay of the sanction. (See §§ 680.230, 680.270, 699.510, subd. (a); Jones v. Otero, supra, 156 Cal.App.3d at p. 759, 203 Cal.Rptr. 90.)

 

(Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615.) Enforcement of judgment laws provide that the judgment creditor may demand that the judgment debtor produce and permit the inspection of a document if the demand requests information to aid in the enforcement of the money judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.030, subd. (a).) The judgment debtor must respond and comply with the demand within 30 days of its service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.030, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260.) Plaintiff contends it served Defendant with the demand for inspection on March 4, 2024. (Motion, Gentino Decl., Exh. 1.) The attached inspection demand, however, does not include a proof of service showing the manner and location of its service. Accordingly, the Court cannot make a finding regarding the date and propriety of service of the inspection demand, or the date on which Defendant’s responses were due.

 

The Court additionally notes that service of the instant Motion is questionable. Defendant is currently an unrepresented party over whom electronic service is dictated by Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (c)(1). Under this statute, an unrepresented party may consent to receive electronic service by either serving a notice on all parties and filing the notice with the court or by “[m]anifesting affirmative consent through electronic means with the court or the court's electronic filing service provider, and concurrently providing the party’s electronic address with that consent for the purpose of receiving electronic service.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (c)(3).) Defendant is currently in a limbo state as an unrepresented corporation but is still entitled to proper notice of the instant Motion.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff Richard Heng Taing dba USA Donuts’ Motion to Compel Responses to Request to Inspect Writings to Enforce Order Dated 11-20-23, and Request for Sanctions is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

Case Number: 23STLC04715    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Melson v. Gonzalez, Jr., et al.

MOTION FOR TERMINATING AND MONETARY SANCTIONS

(CCP § 2023.010, et seq.)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Douglas Melson’s Motion to Strike Answer and Enter Default; Request for Sanctions is GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART. DEFENDANT MARIO GONZALEZ, JR.’S ANSWER FILED ON NOVEMBER 27, 2023 IS STRICKEN AND DEFENDANT IS TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $310.00 TO PLAINTIFF’S COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT IS DENIED.

 

ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE ENTRY OF DEFAULT/DEFAULT JUDGMENT IS SET FOR SEPTEMBER 10, 2024 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On July 26, 2023, Plaintiff Douglas Melson (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Mario Gonzalez, Jr. (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer on November 27, 2023. On February 26, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Defendant’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One. (Minute Order 02/26/24.) The Court also deemed Requests for Admission, Set One, admitted against Defendant. (Ibid.) Defendant was ordered to serve verified responses and pay sanctions within 20 days’ service of the order. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Terminating and Monetary Sanctions against Defendant on May 6, 2024, which seeks an order striking Defendant’s answer and entering default. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Legal Standard

 

Where a party willfully disobeys a discovery order, courts have the discretion to impose terminating, issue, evidence or monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010, subds. (d), (g); R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.) The court should look to the totality of the circumstances in determining whether terminating sanctions are appropriate. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.) “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.” (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.) “The court may impose a terminating sanction by one of the following orders:

 

(1)   An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

 

(2)   An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

 

(3)   An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

 

(4)   An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).)

 

 

Discussion

 

On February 26, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Defendant’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One. (Motion, Piazza, Jr. Decl., ¶12 and Exh. A.) Defendant was ordered to serve verified responses and pay sanctions within 20 days’ service of the order. (Ibid.) Notice of the ruling was mailed to Defendant the next day. (Id. at ¶13 and Exh. B.) As of the filing of this motion, Defendant has not served responses as ordered. (Id. at ¶15.) Plaintiff now moves for terminating sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 by striking Defendant’s answer and entering default.

 

The Court finds that terminating sanctions are warranted for Defendant’s non-compliance with the discovery order. Despite notice of the Court’s ruling, Defendant failed to serve responses as ordered. Nor has Defendant filed an opposition to this noticed motion. This demonstrates that compliance with the Court’s orders cannot be achieved through lesser sanctions. Although terminating sanctions are a harsh penalty, “[t]he court [is] not required to allow a pattern of abuse to continue ad infinitum.” (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 280.) However, it is not clear whether Plaintiff seeks entry of default against Defendant, or default judgment. The former is not provided for in the moving statute, and the latter is not supported by any evidence. The terminating sanction, therefore, is limited to striking Defendant’s answer.

 

Finally, Plaintiff is sanctioned for the fees incurred bringing this action in the amount of $310.00 based on one hour of attorney time billed at $250.00 and $60.00 in filing fees. (Motion, Piazza, Jr. Decl., ¶16.)

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Douglas Melson’s Motion to Strike Answer and Enter Default; Request for Sanctions is GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART. DEFENDANT MARIO GONZALEZ, JR.’S ANSWER FILED ON NOVEMBER 27, 2023 IS STRICKEN AND DEFENDANT IS TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $310.00 TO PLAINTIFF’S COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT IS DENIED.

 

ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE ENTRY OF DEFAULT/DEFAULT JUDGMENT IS SET FOR SEPTEMBER 10, 2024 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

 

 

Moving party to give notice.
