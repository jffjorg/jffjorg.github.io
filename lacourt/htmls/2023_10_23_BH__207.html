DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SMCV01733    Hearing Date: October 23, 2023    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

October 23, 2023

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV01733

MOTIONS

Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Tent 123

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

MOTION

 

            Plaintiff Tent 123 (“Plaintiff”) filed an unlawful detainer complaint on October 4, 2022.  Defendant Fethi Sahman (“Defendant”) vacated the premises in December 2022. 

 

            Plaintiff now moves for leave to file a first amended complaint for damages only.  Defendant has not opposed the motion for leave to amend. 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Amendments to Pleadings: General Provisions

 

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

            To wit, without notice to the other party the Court has wide discretion to allow either party (i) to add or strike the name of a party or (ii) to correct a mistake in the name of a party or a mistake in any other respect “in furtherance of justice” and “on any terms as may be proper.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see also Marriage of Liss (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1429.) Alternatively, after notice to the other party, the Court has wide discretion to allow either party to amend pleadings “upon any terms as may be just.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1). Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure section 576 states “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”

 

            Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, leave to amend is liberally granted. (Berman vs. Bromberg (1986) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945; Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489 [this has been an established policy in California since 1901] (citing Frost v. Whitter (1901) 132 Cal. 421, 424; Thomas v. Bruza (1957) 151 Cal.App.2d 150, 155).) The Court of Appeal in Morgan v. Superior Court held “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530, citations omitted.) Moreover, “it is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [opposing party did not establish harm by the delay in moving to amend the complaint].)

 

            “The court may grant leave to amend the pleadings at any stage of the action.” (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 6:636 (hereafter Weil & Brown).) Denial of a motion to amend is rarely justified if the motion is timely made and granting the motion will not prejudice the opposing party. (Id. at ¶ 6:639, citations omitted.) However, if the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend. (Id. at ¶ 6:655, citations omitted. Absent prejudice, any claimed delay alone is not grounds for denial. “If the delay in seeking the amendment has not misled or prejudiced the other side, the liberal policy of allowing amendments prevails. Indeed, it is an abuse of discretion to deny leave in such a case even if sought as late as the time of trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:653 (citing Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565).) “Prejudice exists where the amendment would result in a delay of trial, along with loss of critical evidence, added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc. . . . But the fact that the amendment involves a change in legal theory which would make admissible evidence damaging to the opposing party is not the kind of prejudice the court will consider.” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:656, citations omitted.)

 

            “Even if some prejudice is shown, the judge may still permit the amendment but impose conditions, as the Court is authorized to grant leave ‘on such terms as may be proper.’” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:663, citation omitted.) For example, the court may cause the party seeking the amendment to pay the costs and fees incurred in preparing for trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:664 (citing Fuller v. Vista Del Arroyo Hotel, 42 Cal.App.2d 400, 404).)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324: Procedural Requirements

 

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must:

 

“(1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleadings, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

(2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

(3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”

 

            In addition, under Rule 3.1324(b), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must be accompanied by a separate declaration that specifies the following:

 

“(1) the effect of the amendment;

(2) why the amendment is necessary and proper;

(3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

(4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s motion fails to comply with Rule 3.1324(b).  Plaintiff has not provided any declaration in support of the motion. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend without prejudice for failure to comply with Rule 3.1324(b).

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Amendments to Pleadings: General Provisions

 

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

            To wit, without notice to the other party the Court has wide discretion to allow either party (i) to add or strike the name of a party or (ii) to correct a mistake in the name of a party or a mistake in any other respect “in furtherance of justice” and “on any terms as may be proper.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see also Marriage of Liss (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1429.) Alternatively, after notice to the other party, the Court has wide discretion to allow either party to amend pleadings “upon any terms as may be just.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1). Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure section 576 states “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”

 

            Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, leave to amend is liberally granted. (Berman vs. Bromberg (1986) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945; Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489 [this has been an established policy in California since 1901] (citing Frost v. Whitter (1901) 132 Cal. 421, 424; Thomas v. Bruza (1957) 151 Cal.App.2d 150, 155).) The Court of Appeal in Morgan v. Superior Court held “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530, citations omitted.) Moreover, “it is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [opposing party did not establish harm by the delay in moving to amend the complaint].)

 

            “The court may grant leave to amend the pleadings at any stage of the action.” (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 6:636 (hereafter Weil & Brown).) Denial of a motion to amend is rarely justified if the motion is timely made and granting the motion will not prejudice the opposing party. (Id. at ¶ 6:639, citations omitted.) However, if the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend. (Id. at ¶ 6:655, citations omitted. Absent prejudice, any claimed delay alone is not grounds for denial. “If the delay in seeking the amendment has not misled or prejudiced the other side, the liberal policy of allowing amendments prevails. Indeed, it is an abuse of discretion to deny leave in such a case even if sought as late as the time of trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:653 (citing Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565).) “Prejudice exists where the amendment would result in a delay of trial, along with loss of critical evidence, added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc. . . . But the fact that the amendment involves a change in legal theory which would make admissible evidence damaging to the opposing party is not the kind of prejudice the court will consider.” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:656, citations omitted.)

 

            “Even if some prejudice is shown, the judge may still permit the amendment but impose conditions, as the Court is authorized to grant leave ‘on such terms as may be proper.’” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:663, citation omitted.) For example, the court may cause the party seeking the amendment to pay the costs and fees incurred in preparing for trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:664 (citing Fuller v. Vista Del Arroyo Hotel, 42 Cal.App.2d 400, 404).)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324: Procedural Requirements

 

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must:

 

“(1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleadings, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

(2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

(3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”

 

            In addition, under Rule 3.1324(b), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must be accompanied by a separate declaration that specifies the following:

 

“(1) the effect of the amendment;

(2) why the amendment is necessary and proper;

(3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

(4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Procedural Requirements   

 

Plaintiff’s declaration accompanying the motion indicates, in relevant part:

 

2.  That this amendment will allow Plaintiff to assert all her allegations and Defendants for the proper adjudication of the case otherwise she would be seriously prejudiced.

 

3.  That amendment is proper because Plaintiff did not have all her allegations or Defendants and or facts delineated at the time of the filing of the Complaint and/or First Amended Complaint.

 

4.  That Plaintiff solidified who and what were the defendants and the causes of action and damages recently as she was waiting for a decision from the Appellate Division, Court of Appeal, CA Supreme Court and U.S. Supreme Court on a related case, due to her continued illness and limited legal resources.

 

(Ortiz Decl. ¶¶ 2-4.) 

 

In light of the protracted procedural history of this action and the Unlawful Detainer action, Plaintiff’s statement that she “solidified who and what were the defendants and the causes of action and damages recently as she was waiting for a decision from the Appellate Division, Court of Appeal, CA Supreme Court and U.S. Supreme Court on a related case, due to her continued illness and limited legal resources” is too vague to inform the court when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were first discovered.  Thus, Plaintiff has not satisfied the procedural requirements of Rule 3.1324(b).

 

Similarly, with respect to the procedural requirements of Rule 3.1324(a), the motion indicates “The proposed amendments fall into four categories: (1) non-substantive clerical corrections; (2) addition of new legal claims to clarify a previously existing cause of action; (3) the addition of new causes of action based on facts already pleaded; and (4) additions to Plaintiff’s prayer based on the newly proposed causes of action and mistakes in the previous complaint.”  (Motion at pp. 7-8.)

 

Plaintiff did file a declaration on February 6, 2023 with a proposed second amended complaint, and Plaintiff did electronically lodge a “Second Amended Complaint” with the Court on March 21, 2023, but these do not comport with the rule.  Moreover, the proposed Second Amended Complaint reasserts all the causes of action of the First Amended Complaint, including those that were either stricken and/or dismissed with prejudice by way of the anti-SLAPP motion, the demurrer, and/or the motion to strike.  The proposed Second Amended Complaint corrects the numbering, such that there are no longer two separate “Tenth” causes of action; changes the Fourteenth (now Fifteenth) cause of action from Intentional and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress to just Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and adds a Twentieth cause of action for “Malicious Institution of Civil Proceeding.” 

 

            The proposed Second Amended Complaint that Plaintiff lodged does not appear to add any additional defendants, and adds only one new cause of action.  Therefore, it appears Plaintiff seeks leave to file a different version of the Second Amended Complaint that has not been provided to the Court.  The motion also does not indicate “where, by page, paragraph, and line number,” the additional allegations and/or deletions are located.

 

            Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to satisfy the procedural requirements of moving for leave to amend.

 

            Substance of Motion

 

            Plaintiff contends on Reply (without evidentiary support) that she was unable to timely file a second amended complaint, pursuant to the Court’s December 6, 2022 Minute Order, because when she attempted to timely file it, it did not actually file for some reason.[2]

 

But even if Plaintiff’s assertions were supported by competent evidence, the version of the Second Amended Complaint Plaintiff lodged with the Court in March 2023 vastly exceeds the scope of the leave Plaintiff was granted on December 6, 2022.  Following Defendants’ successful anti-SLAPP motion, demurrer, and subsequent motion to strike, the only cause of action remaining in the complaint is the Twelfth cause of action, and the Twelfth cause of action remains only with respect to Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendants demanded rent in excess of the maximum lawful rent permitted by section 1809 of the Santa Monica Charter Amendment.  All other causes of action have been stricken or dismissed from the complaint with prejudice.

 

            The Court granted Plaintiff leave to amend the allegations to attempt to revive her claim of punitive damages under the twelfth cause of action and to revive her request for injunctive relief, both of which the Court struck without prejudice.  Plaintiff was not permitted to re-assert the other eighteen causes of action that were stricken and/or dismissed with prejudice.  Nor was Plaintiff permitted to add a cause of action for “Malicious Institution of Civil Proceeding.” 

 

            Therefore, even if the Court were to excuse the delay, the proposed Second Amended Complaint Plaintiff filed in March 2023 is and was wholly improper. 

 

            Now Plaintiff seeks further leave of the Court to not only re-assert claims already dismissed with prejudice, but also to add new, unspecified defendants and causes of action.  Plaintiff does not specify who she seeks to name as a defendant, what new causes of action she seeks to add, what the factual bases for those newly added parties and causes of action are, when or how Plaintiff discovered those facts, or why Plaintiff could not have added those facts, claims, and/or defendants earlier in the litigation.  All Plaintiff indicates in this regard is that “Plaintiff solidified who and what were the defendants and the causes of action and damages recently as she was waiting for a decision from the Appellate Division, Court of Appeal, CA Supreme Court and U.S. Supreme Court on a related case, due to her continued illness and limited legal resources.”  (Ortiz Decl. ¶ 4.)  Therefore, Plaintiff has not met her burden to establish a sufficient factual or legal basis for the Court to grant leave to amend.

 

Further, Defendants will be prejudiced.  Despite Plaintiff’s contention that “This is simply not the ‘eve of trial’ type scenario” (Motion at p. 12) the request is being made on the eve of trial.  Trial is currently scheduled for the same day as the hearing on this motion.  Plaintiff cannot, at this very late stage of the litigation, reassert causes of action that were already dismissed with prejudice, or add new unspecified defendants and causes of action without severely prejudicing Defendants.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Because Plaintiff has not satisfied the procedural requirements of moving for leave to amend, Plaintiff untimely lodged a version of the second amended complaint that far exceeded the scope of the leave granted by the Court’s December 6, 2022 Order, the proposed amendments improperly seek to revive causes of action that have been stricken and/or dismissed with prejudice, and Defendants will be prejudiced by the proposed amendments, given that Plaintiff seeks to add new defendants and causes of action on the eve of trial, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend the complaint.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332(c), provides:

[…]

 

(6) A party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; or

Factors the Court considers in ruling on a motion for continuance include:

 

(1)  The proximity of the trial date;

 

(2)  Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party;

 

(3)  The length of the continuance requested;

 

(4)  The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance;

(7)  The court's calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials;

 

(8)  Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial;

 

(9)  Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance;

 

(10)  Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and

Plaintiff contends that a continuance is warranted because (1) the Clerk has not yet filed the proposed second amended complaint; (2) “there is a Petition for Review of the previous Court’s ruling pending at the California Supreme Court (and a forthcoming writ at the Court of Appeal); and (3) “the hospitalization and illness of Plaintiff which precluded her from doing her discovery.”  (Motion at p. 2.)

 

Proposed Second Amended Complaint

 

The fact that the clerk has not yet filed the proposed second amended complaint does not constitute good cause to continue the trial.  As explained in the Court’s concurrent order on Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint, the proposed second amended complaint Plaintiff received by the Court in March 2023 exceeds the scope of the leave granted by the Court’s December 6, 2022 order by improperly seeking to revive causes of action that have already been stricken and/or dismissed with prejudice, and attempting to add an additional cause of action.  Therefore, the proposed second amended complaint cannot properly be filed.

 

Defendants argue in opposition that Plaintiff’s complaint should be dismissed with prejudice because of Plaintiff’s failure to timely file an amended complaint in conformance with the Court’s December 6, 2022 Order.  The Court disagrees.  Aspects of Plaintiff’s Twelfth cause of action remain active and are not dependent upon any amendments.  That Plaintiff either chose to or failed to timely amend to revive previously stricken requests for punitive damages, treble damages, disgorgement, and injunctive relief does not impact what remains of the twelfth cause of action.  Therefore, the Court finds that dismissal of the Twelfth cause of action is inappropriate.

 

Petitions for Review/Writs

 

Unlike an appeal, which is taken by right, and which automatically stays further trial court proceedings under Code of Civil Procedure section 916, “a writ is an extraordinary, discretionary remedy” which does not automatically stay proceedings in the trial court.  (In re Brandy R. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 607, 609-610.)  Thus, a pending writ is not a basis to continue the trial.

 

Moreover, there is no pending writ.  Plaintiff contends a writ will be “forthcoming.”  (Motion at p. 7.)  Thus, the forthcoming writ does not form a basis to continue the trial.

 

With respect to petitions for review, “A petition for review must be served and filed within 10 days after the Court of Appeal decision is final in that court.”  (California Rules of Court, rule 8.500(e)(1).)  “Any answer to the petition must be served and filed within 20 days after the petition is filed” and “[a]ny reply to the answer must be served and filed within 10 days after the answer is filed.”  (Rule 8.500(e)(4)-(5).)  “The petition must also be served on the superior court clerk[.]”  (Rule 8.500(f)(1).) 

 

Here, the appellate court ruled on the Anti-SLAPP motion on August 22, 2022.  Thus, Plaintiff’s petition was due by September 1, 2022, Defendants’ answer by September 21, 2022, and Plaintiff’s reply by October 1, 2022.  Now, over a year later, Plaintiff has not filed any notice with the court regarding any petition for review with the California Supreme Court.

 

On Reply, Plaintiff clarifies “Plaintiff filed a Writ of Certiorari (not a Petition for Review as stated by Defendants) with the U.S. Supreme Court but the Court wanted it bifurcated and it is still in someone’s desk.  The U S [sic] Supreme Court has yet to file the Writ, thus it is not in their docket; thus it does not appear when you search it – as of right now.”  (Reply at p. 6.) 

 

Therefore, there is no petition for review or writ of certiorari to warrant a stay or continuance.

 

Plaintiff’s Illness and Hospitalization

 

Plaintiff’s declaration in support of the motion indicates “That I was seriously ill and hospitalized while waiting for the filing of the Amended Complaint by the Clerk and am now recovering from a serious illness for the last months (and previously).”  (Ortiz Decl. ¶ 4.)  With regard to Plaintiff’s attempt to have the Clerk file the proposed second amended complaint, Plaintiff indicates, “The last request to the Clerk was last Friday, September 15, 2023.”  Thus, Plaintiff asserts that from at least January 2023 through September 2023, she has been seriously ill and hospitalized, and therefore unable to conduct discovery.

 

Although Plaintiff’s declaration does not elaborate on the details of the discovery Plaintiff was unable to obtain, a lengthy and serious illness and hospitalization could form the basis of an excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts, pursuant to Rule 3.1332(c)(6). 

 

Moreover, the trial is currently scheduled for the same day as the hearing date on this motion and Plaintiff’s concurrent Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint, and the parties still have vastly different expectations with respect to the appropriate scope of this case.  Plaintiff seeks to litigate 18 causes of action that were previously stricken and/or dismissed with prejudice, whereas Defendants request that the Court dismiss the case in its entirety with prejudice.  Thus, it appears neither party is currently prepared to try the Twelfth cause of action.

 

Therefore, although the trial date is imminent, because there is only a single cause of action remaining in this action, the Court will grant a short continuance to enable the parties to prepare for trial as to the twelfth cause of action.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Although the Court finds that the clerk’s refusal to file Plaintiff’s improper proposed second amended complaint, and the pending and/or forthcoming writs do not create a significant, unanticipated change in the case to warrant a continuance, Plaintiff’s lengthy illness and hospitalization, combined with the parties’ starkly contrasting expectations with respect to the proper scope of the trial warrant a short continuance.

 

As such, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for a short continuance, to permit the parties to prepare for trial on the Twelfth cause of action remaining in this action.  Consequently, the Court will set a Trial Setting Conference on November 20, 2023 at 8:30 A.M. in Department 2307.  All parties shall be prepared to advise the Court when they will be ready to proceed with a trial on the remaining cause of action, and whether the trial will be a jury or bench trial with an attendant time estimate for the trial. 

 

 The Clerk of the Court shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling.
