DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV32363    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Tuesday, November 5, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   22STCV32363

CASE NAME:                        Boyle Flats Food & Beverage, Inc., et al. v. Alex Koons

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Daleyn Accountancy, Inc. 

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants Boyle Flats Food & Beverage, Inc. and Ted Yenawine

TRIAL DATE:                        Not set.

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer to Fourth Amended Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        22 October 2024

REPLY:                                  28 October 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Defendant Daleyn’s demurrer is sustained in its entirety without leave to amend.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

 

This is an action arising out of Plaintiff Tad Yenawine’s (“Yenawine”) and Defendant Alex Koons (“Koons”) formation of a California entity, Plaintiff Boyle Flats Food and Beverage, Inc. dba Purgatory Pizza (“BFFB”). Plaintiffs and Defendant operated a restaurant business thus called “Purgatory Pizza.” The Complaint alleges Defendant took monies from Plaintiff BFFB’s bank accounts for expenses to open his own pizza restaurant in 2020. The Complaint alleges Defendant applied for and received an Emergency Incident Disaster Loan (“EIDL”) in BFFB’s name which was transferred to Defendant’s own account and no payments were made by Defendant for the EIDL afterwards. The Complaint also alleges Defendant abandoned his duties as manager of Purgatory Pizza and continued to pay himself his salary.¿¿ 

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges four causes of action: (1) breach of contract; (2) conversion; (3) fraud; and (4) breach of fiduciary duty.¿¿ 

 

On June 23, 2021, Petitioner filed a Request for Dismissal without prejudice as to his claim for writ of mandate.¿¿ 

 

 On October 23, 2023, Defendant Coons filed the Third Amended Cross-Complaint (“TACC”) alleging four causes of action: (1) Dissolution of Corporation, (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty – Derivative, (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty – Individual, and (4) Failure to Reimburse Business Expenses.

 

On February 28, 2024, the court sustained Defendant Daleyn’s demurrer to the First Amended Complaint with leave to amend.

 

On May 28, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleging eight causes of action against Defendant Koons and Defendant Daleyn Accountancy, Inc. (“Daleyn”); and Does 2 to 10. The TAC alleges:

 

1)     Breach of Contract – against Defendant Koons;

2)     Conversion - against Defendant Koons;

3)     Fraud - against Defendant Koons;

4)     Breach of Fiduciary Duty;

5)     Breach of Contract – against Defendant Daleyn;

6)     Conversion – against Defendant Daleyn;

7)     Fraud – against Defendant Daleyn; and

8)     Breach of Fiduciary Duty - against Defendant Daleyn;

 

Defendant Daleyn’s demurrer was sustained with leave to amend.

 

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Fourth Amended Complaint (“4AC”) alleging the same eight causes of action as the TAC.

 

Defendant Daleyn now demurs to the 4AC. Plaintiff opposes the demurrer. The matter is now before the court.

 

Demurrer[1]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.¿ (CCP, § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) In evaluating a demurrer, the court accepts the complainant’s properly pled facts as true and ignores contentions, deductions, and conclusory statements. (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1976) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Moreover, the court does not consider whether a plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations or the possible difficulty in making such proof. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿ The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

II.        Discussion

 

On or about 2020, Defendant Daleyn was contracted to provide BFFB with accounting and business advisory services. (4AC, ¶¶ 47, 48, Ex. 2.) Defendant Daleyn became aware that Defendant Koons was taking money from BFFB to fund his new business venture while also proving Koons with accounting and business advisory services. (4AC, ¶ 50.) Plaintiffs Yenawine and BFFB now bring claims against Daleyn for breach of contract, conversion, fraud, and breach of fiduciary duty. Defendant Daleyn now demurs to all the causes of action alleged against it.

 

A.        Yenawine’s Standing  to Bring Action on Behalf of BFFB

 

Defendant Daleyn alleges that Yenawine lacks standing to bring an action on behalf of BFFB. Under Anmaco, Inc. v. Bohlken (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th (Anmaco), Plaintiff Yenawine lacks standing to bring an action on behalf of Plaintiff BFFB. As previously noted by this court in its February 28, 2024, ruling, the Anmaco Court found that because a corporate president has no greater power than any other director, the president had no power to initiate a lawsuit in the name of the corporation against a co-director and equal shareholder. (Id. at p. 900.)

 

Pressing the corporation into litigation as a plaintiff is inappropriate where the other shareholder-director could claim equal authority to bring suit in the corporate name. This is particularly obvious in the instant case where Bohlken is not only an equal director and shareholder, but is also Chief Executive Officer of the company. The proper vehicle for such a suit, when the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, is a shareholders’ derivative action. 

 

(Id. at p. 900.)  

 

Plaintiffs assert they have standing to bring the action under Corporations Code §§ 207, 315, 316(c), 506, and BFFB’s corporate Bylaws and resolutions (4AC, ¶ 50.) The court finds that Corporations Code § 207 outlines the powers of a corporation, section 315 pertains to rules on corporate loans, section 316(c) allows creditors and shareholders to bring suit against directors under the four specific circumstances outlined in section 316(a), and section 506 allows shareholders to bring suit for unauthorized distribution of dividends. The 4AC fails to allege with requisite particularity how each of the above sections gives Plaintiff Yenawine standing to bring an action on behalf of BFFB. Consequently, the court fails to find that the Corporations Code gives Plaintiff Yenawine authority to bring suit on behalf of BFFB.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that under Corp. Code § 310(a), Koons could not vote on matters in which he had a financial interest. However, the 4AC is devoid of allegations that Koons could not vote on a board resolution because he had a financial interest in the resolution and that, in the event there is a deadlock between the Board of Directors, the vote of one Director is sufficient to pass a resolution. Facts not alleged in the pleadings are presumed to not exist. (Schick v. Lerner (1987) 193 Cal.App.3d 1321, 1327.)

 

However, the 4AC also alleges that after BFFB’s bylaws were adopted, BFFB’s board of director voted to approve a resolution that authorized officers of BFFB to  take any action  “as may in the judgment of the officer so acting be necessary or desirable in connection with, or in furtherance of, any of the foregoing resolutions, and the execution and delivery of any such document, agreement or instrument or the taking of any such action shall be conclusive evidence of such officer’s authority hereunder to so act.” (4AC, ¶¶ 8, 9., Ex. 1, § IV, at p. AK000003-4.)

 

The court may take judicial notice of the existence of BFFB’s bylaws. “Although the existence of a document may be judicially noticeable, the truth of statements contained in the document and its proper interpretation are not subject to judicial notice if those matters are reasonably disputable.” (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113.) Defendant Daleyn asserts that it would be improper to interpret the written consent provision in the bylaws (§ IV) as allowing officers to take any action that may in the judgment of the officer to be necessary or desirable because each officer of the company would continually overrule each other and initiate and then dismiss lawsuits, leaving a feedback loop of destructive litigation disabling the corporation in their wake.

 

On its face, the bylaws allow an officer to take an action if it is necessary or in furtherance of “the forgoing resolutions” and “execution and delivery” of “all documents, agreements and instruments to effectuate any of the foregoing resolutions.” (TACC, Ex. 1, § IV.) Therefore, a condition precedent to an officer taking action is that the action be in furtherance of the board resolution contemplated by Exhibit 1.

 

The 4AC alleges that because the bylaws provide that BFFB’s President should enforce the board’s action, the BFFB board passed resolutions authorizing and directing Yenawine to take any actions Yenawine saw fit. (4AC, ¶ 9, Ex. 1 (B)(§IV)(C) at p. AK000026-27.) However, the 4AC fails to allege that BFFB’s board specifically passed a resolution that allowed Yenawine to initiate this action against Daleyn. BFFB’s bylaws state that the powers of the President are limited to effecting the order and resolution of the Board of Directors, meaning both Yenawine and Koons need to pass such resolution. (Id.)

 

DIRECTORS.

 

(A) NUMBER OF DIRECTORS. The authorized number of Directors of the Corporation shall be, initially, two (the “Board of Directors”) until changed by an amendment to these Bylaws duly adopted in accordance with these Bylaws by the vote or written consent of the initial Directors, Alex Koons, and Tad Yenawine, individuals authorized to perform the duties presented under these Bylaws.

 

(4AC, Ex. 1(B) Art. III(A) at p. AK000022.)

 

IV.       OFFICERS

 

[ . . . ]

 

(C) PRESIDENT. The President shall be the chief executive officer of the Corporation, and subject to the direction of the Board of Directors, shall have general supervision over the business and affairs of the Corporation. The President shall see that all orders and resolutions of the Board of Directors are carried out, and shall perform any other duties as the Board of Directors, once duly authorized, may assign. The President shall have the duties and authority of a chief executive officer and preside at all shareholders'’ meetings and meetings of the board. The President shall initially be Tad Yenawine.

 

(4AC, Ex. 1(B) Art. IV(C) at pp. AK000026-27)

 

Therefore, for Yenawine to have authority as President of BFFB to bring suit, there must be a resolution by BFFB’s board of directors that would lead Yenawine to think such action was necessary or in furtherance such a resolution. The Anmaco Court was clear that when the bylaws of the corporation do not permit the President to act without board approval “[t]he president does not have the power to enter into contracts or execute other instruments or render the corporation ‘liable for any purposes or any amount’ without Board authorization” including the “power to institute litigation in the corporate name against the other 50 percent shareholder.” (Anmaco, supra, 13 Cal.App.4th at p. 898.) 

 

“The California position with reference to the authority of corporate officers has been strict. Even the president, apart from acts done in the course of ordinary business, or in pursuance of apparent authority depends upon the powers conferred upon him, either by statute, corporate charter, by-laws or the directors. The mere fact that he is president, without more, does not necessarily give him greater power than any other director.” (Sealand Inv. Corp. v. Emprise Inc. (1961) 190 Cal.App.2d 305, 321–322; see also O'Flaherty v. Belgum (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1044, 1062.) Here, the 4AC fails to state when the board of director passed a resolution that led Yenawine to believe it was necessary or in furtherance of a board resolution to bring suit on behalf of BFFB.

 

Consequently, the court agrees that Yenawine fails to allege facts to show that it has standing to bring suit on behalf of BFFB. Moreover, Yenawine fails to show that he can bring suit against Defendant Daleyn for torts that belong to him individually rather than as BFFB’s President. In Murphy v. BDO Seidman (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 687, the Court of Appeal extended liability for breach of intentional misrepresentation to anyone who would reasonably rely on the misrepresentations of the auditor and who the auditor intended such individually. (Id., at p. 694.) The 4AC fails to allege that Daleyn intended Yenawine to rely on its misrepresentations. The 4AC fails to show facts to show that Daleyn is personally liable to Yenawine for any torts committed against Yenawine in his individual capacity rather than in his capacity as President of BFFB.

Defendant Daleyn’s demurrer to the First Amended Complaint and Third Amended Complaint have been sustained. This is Daleyn’s third demurrer to Plaintiff’s pleadings. “In response to a demurrer and prior to the case being at issue, a complaint . . .  shall not be amended more than three times, absent an offer to the trial court as to such additional facts to be pleaded that there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured to state a cause of action.” (CCP, § 430.31(e)(1).)

 

Unless the Plaintiffs can show that a resolution was passed that led Yenawine to believe he could bring suit or that there is case law showing that a board of director(s) can act unilaterally when the board is deadlocked, the court sees no reason why leave to amend to should be granted. Unless Plaintiff Yenawine can show that he can bring actions against Daleyn in his individual capacity, the court sees no reason why leave to amend should be granted.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to 4AC is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Daleyn’s demurrer is sustained in its entirety without leave to amend.

LEGAL STANDARDS

 

A.        Demurrer 

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP, § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.”¿(C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)¿For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded.¿ (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.)¿A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.”¿(Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿ 

 

B.        Motion to Strike 

 

¿Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP, § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP, § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

 

C.        Leave to Amend 

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿¿¿

II.        Discussion

 

A.        Summary of Allegations in SACC

(TACC, ¶¶ 4, 16.) Larisa Vinnitskaya (“Vinnitskaya”) is the Chief Executive Officer of IMA. (Id., ¶ 5.) Cross-complainant Aram Sharnazyan (“Sharnazyan”) owns and operates A&G Management Services, Inc. (“A&G”). (Id., ¶¶ 2, 17.) On or about September 25, 2019, IMA and A&G entered into an agreement (the “Agreement”) wherein A&G would be the exclusive manager and operator of the Business. (Id., ¶ 18, Ex. 1.)  

 

In June 2020, A&G and Baranovskiy formed a partnership that allowed Baranovskiy to run the day-to-day operations of the A&G. (TACC, ¶ 21.) Baranovskiy retained his associates Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun as 11 personnel to manage the Business's day-to-day operations. (Id., ¶ 22.) By July 4, 2023, the partnership between IMA and A&G was terminated due to alleged nonpayment of taxes and failing to pay the reduced monthly fee that A&G asserts IMA accepted. (Id., ¶¶ 32-35.)

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainants A&G and Aram Sharnazyan have filed a TACC against Cross-Defendants on the basis that they made misrepresentations to A&G and interfered with A&G’s contractual and economic relationships.

 

B.        Second and Third Causes of Action – Negligent and Intentional Misrepresentation 

 

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage. [Citations.] The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true. [Citations.] Each element of a fraud count must be pleaded with particularity so as to apprise the defendant of the specific grounds for the charge and enable the court to determine whether there is any basis for the cause of action, although less specificity is required if the defendant would likely have greater knowledge of the facts than the plaintiff. [Citation.]”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230–231.) Negligent misrepresentation requires that the party owe a duty to disclose. (See Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 841; Levine v. Blue Shield of California (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1117, 1136.)  

 

The second and third causes of action allege that “Baranovskiy, orally represented to Cross-Complainants that he and his associates would operate the Business in a diligent and honest manner on behalf of Cross-Complaints and for Cross-Complainants' benefit, including but not limited to properly documenting transactions/sales, retaining all cash from transaction/sales in the cash register, depositing monies into the correct account(s) as instructed by Cross-Complainants.” (TACC, ¶¶ 22, 55, 68, 76.) This allegation does not amount to a misrepresentation because it is an opinion regarding a future event about how A&G would be operated. “The law is well established that actionable misrepresentations must pertain to past or existing material facts. [Citation.] Statements or predictions regarding future events are deemed to be mere opinions which are not actionable. [Citation.]” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)

 

Next, Cross-Complainant alleges that in March 2023, A&G’s accountant, Emilio Amaran, discovered that Cross-Defendants were committing theft, fraud, and embezzlement. (TACC, ¶ 25.) Baranovskiy, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and/or Bohun would claim to sell accessories and apparel on A&G’s software but were in fact selling cannabis products. (Id., ¶¶ 27, 26.) When confronted by A&G, Baranovskiy could not provide proof of any apparel or accessories. (Ibid.) Afterwards “Baranovskiy, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and/or Bohun shortly 20 thereafter devised a new fraudulent scheme” wherein they would input a 50% discount on sales while charging customers full price and embezzling the additional cash paid. (Id., ¶ 28.) Moreover, Cross-Defendant Baranovskiy, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and/or Bohun would deposit cash from the register, include monies from their scheme, into the ATM and when the ATM cassettes were full, they would each deposit the case into a bank account belonging to Beranovskiy or Nixpro. (Id., ¶ 62.) In June 2023, A&G discovered the fraudulent scheme and terminated the partnership with Baranovskiy. (Id., ¶ 30.)

 

The court finds that the TACC sufficiently alleges representations regarding embezzlement of A&G’s products by misrepresenting the products sold and their price on Meadow, the point-of-sale system software utilized by A&G. (TACC, ¶ 26.) As a whole, the TACC alleges that the Cross-Defendants knew that the Meadow transactions were not accurate because of their engagement in the embezzlement scheme but intentionally or negligently misrepresented the products sold or the price of said products on Meadow, A&G relied on the representations by continuing its business relationship with Baranovskiy and Nixpro, such that had A&G known about the misrepresentations sooner, it would have terminated its relationship with Baranovskiy and his associates, the other Cross-Defendants, much earlier.

 

While Cross-Defendants assert that under the Los Angeles Municipal Code § 1-5.06(a), A&G cannot accept money for the cannabis in its own name and the monies paid belong to IMA, the fact that the TACC alleges that Cross-Defendants misrepresented the point of sale, is a sufficient allegation of a material misrepresentation to sustain a claim for negligent and intentional misrepresentation.

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the second and third cause of action is overruled.

 

C.        Demurrer to Fourth Cause of Action – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Cross-Defendants assert that the fourth cause of action fails because the TACC does not allege a breach of contract claim against them. “‘The prerequisite for any action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is the existence of a contractual relationship between the parties, since the covenant is an implied term in the contract.’” (Molecular Analytical Systems v. Ciphergen Biosystems, Inc. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 696, 711–712 citing Smith v. City and County of San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49.)

 

In their opposition, Cross-Complainants assert that they voluntarily withdraw the fourth cause of action, hence making the demurrer to the fourth cause of action moot. As the Cross-Complainant has not moved to dismiss the fourth cause of action, the court sustains the demurrer to the fourth cause of action without leave to amend.

 

D.        Fifth, Sixth, Seventh Causes of Action - Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations, and Negligent Interference with Economic Relations 

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations are ‘(1) the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of that contract; (3) the defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4)actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.’ ” (Redfearn v. Trader Joe's Co. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 989, 997, citing Reeves v. Hanlon (2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1148.)¿ 

 

To state a claim for the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage (IIPEA), the claimant must allege: (1) an economic relationship between the claimant and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the claimant; (2) the defendant or cross-defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant or cross-defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the claimant proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.¿(Marsh v. Anesthesia Services Medical Group, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 480, 504.) 

 

The tort of negligent interference with a prospective economic advantage (“NIPEA”) differs in that the defendant's act disrupting the economic relationship does not need to be intentional. (Venhaus v. Shultz (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1078.)“The tort of negligent interference with economic relationship arises only when the defendant owes the plaintiff a duty of care.” (Stolz v. Wong Communications Limited Partnership (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1811, 1825 [italics original].)

 

“[T]he same wrongful act may constitute both a breach of contract and an invasion of an interest protected by the law of torts.”¿ (North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 774.) However, “conduct amounting to a breach of contract becomes tortious only when it also violates a duty independent of the contract arising from principles of tort law.”¿ (Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 551 [italics added]; see also Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 515 [‘ “An omission to perform a contract obligation is never a tort, unless that omission is also an omission of a legal duty.” ’ [internal quotation marks and citations omitted].)

 

The fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action similarly allege that IMA and A&G entered into an Agreement or had an economic relationship wherein A&G would be the exclusive manager and operator of the cannabis business. (TACC, ¶¶ 110, 120, 121, 132, 133.) “Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun knew of the Agreement between IMA and A&G as they were hired to operate the Business on A&G's behalf.” (Id., ¶ 112, 122, 134.) The TACC alleges that “IMA received proceeds from Baranovskiy through the embezzlement and benefitted from Baranovskiy’s schemes” and “Cross-Defendants named herein ultimately acted together to deceive, defraud, and conspire against Cross-Complainants by converting the Business's funds for their own gains and to fraudulently induce the termination of the Agreement.” (Id., ¶¶ 113, 114, 125, 136, 137.) “IMA terminated the Agreement with A&G free to [be free] form a business relationship with Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun.” (Id., ¶¶ 116, 127, 139.)

Cross-Defendants assert that the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action fail because Cross-Complainants fail to allege facts to support the allegation that Cross-Defendants’ conduct was a substantial factor in causing IMA to terminate the Agreement. “[A]n actor's conduct ‘is not a substantial factor in bringing about harm to another if the harm would have been sustained’ in the absence of the actor's conduct.” (Jenni Rivera Enterprises, LLC v. Latin World Entertainment Holdings, Inc. (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 766, 792.) The TACC alleges that IMA terminated the contract “due to nonpayment of Taxes and the Reduced monthly fee” not because A&G discovered the Cross-Defendants’ embezzlement scheme and cut off IMA’s benefit from the scheme. (TACC, ¶ 34.) Accordingly, the court finds that the TACC fails to allege facts to show that Cross-Defendants’ conduct caused a disruption in IMA and A&G’s contractual relationship. The fact that Cross-Defendants benefitted from IMA’s termination of the contract is insufficient to show that Cross-Defendants’ conduct caused IMA to terminate the business relationship.

 

Moreover, both interference with contract and interference with prospective economic relations are “Torts [that] enable a party to a contract to sue a stranger to that contract for interfering with the contract and both require an intentional act.” (PMC, Inc. v. Saban Entertainment, Inc. (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 579, 595 disapproved on other grounds by Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134.) “The tort of intentional interference with contractual relations is committed only by “strangers—interlopers who have no legitimate interest in the scope or course of the contract's performance.” (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 514.) Here, the TACC repeatedly alleges that Cross-Defendants are agents of IMA. (TACC, ¶¶ 13, 39, 64, 66, 85, 87.) If Baranovsky, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohan were agents of IMA, then they are not strangers to the contract between IMA and A&G and cannot be held liable for assisting IMA in breaching its own contract.

 

Cross-Complainants’ opposition asserts that there is also a second contractual relationship between A&G and Baranovskiy. (TACC, ¶¶ 16-18, 19-21.) While the TACC does allege a business relationship between A&G and Baranovskiy, the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action are devoid of the factual elements necessary to support claims for tortious interferences by the other Cross-Defendants.

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Motions to Strike

 

A.        Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Strike:

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants seek to strike the following from the TACC:

 

·       All allegations relying on claims of conversion, conspiracy, and embezzlement throughout the TACC, specifically at ¶¶ 25, 28, 36, 39-40, 58, 61, 64, 79, 82, 85, 96, 100-101, 113, 114-116, 124, 125-128, 136, 137-139, and 144, on the grounds that the Court has sustained Demurrers to causes of action for Conversion, Violation of Penal Code § 496(c) and Conspiracy without leave to amend;

 

·       TACC, ¶ 70 at pp. 15:6; ¶ 91 at p. 18:26, and ¶ 106 at p. 21:9: “Cross-Complainant reasonably relied on Cross-Defendants’ representations.”

 

·       TACC ¶ 108 at p. 21:13-17: “The conduct described in the preceding paragraphs was willful, intentional, and fraudulent, as those terms are defined in Civil Code § 3294. Accordingly, Cross-Complainants are entitled to punitive and exemplary damages against Cross-Defendants in such amounts as shall be necessary and appropriate to punish Cross-Defendants for their acts, practices, misrepresentations, and concealment, and to deter them, and anyone else, from ever attempting to commit similar acts.”

 

The court denies the request to strike claims relying on claims of conversion, conspiracy, and embezzlement because they are used to support Cross-Complainants’ claims of negligent and intentional misrepresentation. Matter which is essential to a cause of action should not be stricken and it is error to do so. (Clements v. T.R. Bechtel Co. (1954) 43 Cal.2d. 227, 242.)

Where a motion to strike is so broad as to include relevant matters, the motion should be denied as the proper pleading challenge by an opponent in such a case is a special demurrer to the cause of action. (Triodyne, Inc. v. Superior Court (1966) 240 Cal.App.2d 536, 542.)

Moreover, as the demurrer to the second and third causes of action has been overruled, the court similarly denies the request to strike the allegation that the Cross-Complainants relied on the Cross-Defendants’ representations.

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civ. Code § 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.)  The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Ibid.)  

“Malice” is defined in Civ. Code § 3294 (c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.”  (Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 847, 891.) Fraud means “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294(c) (3).)

The court denies the Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ request to strike punitive damages from the TACC because the TACC sufficiently alleges that individual Cross-Defendants Baranovsky, , Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohan engaged in fraudulent conduct to deprive Cross-Complainant of their property. (TACC, ¶¶ 28, 39, 58, 61, 64, 79, 82.)

Therefore, the motion to strike is denied.
