DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV00982    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: P

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. P

 

RIGOBERTO PEREZ, et al.

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

24TRCV00982

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR COMPANY, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          July 31, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Rigoberto Perez, et al.

Demurrer with Motion to Strike

           

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

RULING

The motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED without leave to amend. The motion to strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.  

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 20, 2024, plaintiffs Rigoberto Perez and Laura Perez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc. (“Defendant”), alleging the following causes of action: (1) violation of subdivision (d) of Civil Code § 1793.2; (2) violation of subdivision (b) of Civil Code § 1793.2; (3) violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code § 1793.2; (4) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability; and (5) fraudulent inducement – concealment.

            The complaint alleges that, on August 8, 2021, Plaintiff purchased a 2021 Honda Pilot which was manufactured and or distributed by Defendant. (Compl. ¶ 6.) A written warranty accompanied the sale of the subject vehicle. (Id. at ¶ 7, Exh. A.) It is further alleged that the subject vehicle possessed a “sensing defect” that were covered by the warranty and substantially impaired the subject vehicle’s use, value and safety. (Id. at ¶¶ 11-12. 57.) Defendant is alleged to have concealed this “sensing defect” prior to Plaintiff’s purchase of the subject vehicle. (Id. at ¶ 64.)

            Now, Defendants move to dismiss the fifth cause of action in the complaint as well as the prayer for punitive damages. Plaintiff opposes.

 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

A.    Judgment on the Pleadings

      “The grounds for a motion for judgment on the pleadings must appear on the face of the complaint or from a matter of which the court may take judicial notice.” (Richardson-Tunnell v. School Ins. Program for Employees (2007) 157 Cal.App. 4th 1056, 1061, disapproved on other grounds by Quigley v. Garden Valley Fire Prot. Dist. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 798, 815 n.8. Accord  Bufil v. Dollar Financial Group, Inc. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1202,  disapproved on other grounds by Noel v. Thrifty Payless, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 955, 986; Saltarelli & Steponovich v. Douglas (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1, 5.)

      A motion for judgment on the pleadings involves the same type of procedures that apply to a general demurrer. (Richardson-Tunnell v. School Ins. Program for Employees (2007) 157 Cal.App. 4th 1056, 1061,  disapproved on other grounds by  Quigley v. Garden Valley Fire Prot. Dist. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 798, 815 n.8; Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal. App. 4th 1057, 1064.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether properly pled factual allegations, assumed to be true and liberally construed, are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Stone Street Capital, LLC v. Cal. State Lottery Com'n (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 109, 116; Fire Ins. Exchange v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 446, 452-53.) A motion for judgment on the pleadings does not lie as to only part of a cause of action. (Fire Ins. Exch. v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) “[A] motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the office of a general, not a special, demurrer...” Fabbro v. Dardi & Co. (1949) 93 Cal. App. 2d 247, 252.)

 

B.     Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

 

DISCUSSION

            As a preliminary matter, the Court finds that the Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 430.41 and 435.5. (Hernandez Decls. at ¶ 2.)

A.    MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

Defendant argues that the fifth cause of action is subject to a motion for judgment on the pleadings because it has not been pleaded with the requisite specificity. (MJOP at pp. 16-19.) In particular, Defendant argues that the sufficient facts have not been alleged that Defendant had exclusive knowledge of the alleged defect or that the parties had a direct transaction to trigger a duty to disclose the alleged defect.

To state a claim for fraudulent inducement-concealment, a plaintiff must allege: (1) the defendant “concealed or suppressed a material fact,” (2) the defendant was “under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff,” (3) the defendant “intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff,” (4) the plaintiff was “unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact,” and (5) “as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310–11.)  

Absent a fiduciary relationship between the parties, a duty to disclose can arise in only three circumstances: (1) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of the material fact; (2) the defendant actively concealed the material fact; or (3) the defendant made partial representations while also suppressing the material fact. (Bigler-Engler, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at 311; LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) Such circumstances “presuppose[ ] the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise.” (LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 337.) The California Supreme Court “has described the necessary relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose as a ‘transaction’ between the plaintiff and defendant ….” (Bigler-Engler, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at p. 311; Warner Construction Corp. v. City of Los Angeles (1970) 2 Cal.3d 285, 294 [“In transactions which do not involve fiduciary or confidential relations”]; Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1187–89 [rejecting concealment claim where plaintiffs “were not involved in a transaction with the parties they claim defrauded them”]; LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 337 [“such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties”].) 

Defendant contends that it did not have exclusive knowledge of the alleged defect because it is alleged that this alleged defect was publicly disclosed through the NHTSA and there was no direct transaction between the parties. (Demurrer at pg. 6.) In this regard, Defendant argues that sufficient facts have not been alleged to suggest that Defendant’s authorized dealerships are its agents for the purpose of selling cares. (Id. at pg. 7.) Moreover, Defendant asserts that the express warranty does not create a duty to disclose because it is not part of the sale contract. (Id. at pg. 6, relying on Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324 611, 619-620.)

In opposition, Plaintiff maintains that the fifth cause of action has been sufficiently alleged and reasons that a transaction relationship is not required under California law for the manufacturer to have a duty to disclose. Regardless, Plaintiff asserts that for pleading purposes, her allegations are sufficient to establish a transactional relationship. (Opposition at pg. 7, relying on Dhital v. Nissan North America (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828.)

The plaintiffs in Dhital sued Nissan for Song-Beverly violations. They alleged the transmission was faulty and unreliable, and that it placed them at risk for an accident though they did not allege they had actually been injured. The pleading included a claim for fraudulent inducement. Plaintiffs alleged that Nissan concealed defects it had a duty to disclose in order to induce the purchase. The trial court sustained Nissan’s demurrer to the fraudulent inducement cause of action on the basis that it was barred by the economic loss doctrine. Within its decision reversing the lower court’s decision, the appellate court found that:

 

“[a]t the pleading stage (and in the absence of a more developed argument by Nissan on this point), we conclude Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient. Plaintiff alleged that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan’s authorized dealerships are its agents for purposes of the sale of Nissan vehicles to consumers. In light of these allegations, we decline to hold Plaintiff’s claim is barred on the ground there was no relationship requiring Nissan to disclose known defects.”

 

(Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at pp. 727-729.)

However, other courts have called into question Dhital’s presumption of a transactional relationship between the parties.  Antonov v. General Motors LLC (C.D. Cal., Jan. 19, 2024, No. 823CV01593FWSMJR) 2024 WL 217825, at *11 (“[I]n Dhital the California Court of Appeal conducted limited substantive analysis of the plaintiffs' transactional relationship allegations due to the defendant's failure to fully brief the argument” making the conclusion that there was a transaction unconvincing.”)  Defendant here has set forth a satisfactory argument as to why it should not be summarily presumed that an agency relationship existed between Defendant and the dealership that sold the subject vehicle to Plaintiff. “[A] dealer or retailer may in a layman's view be an agent of the [manufacturer], but he is not an agent in the legal sense of that relationship.” (Avalon Painting Co. v. Alert Lumber Co. (1965) 234 Cal.App.2d 178, 184; see also Herremans v. BMW of N. Am., LLC, No. (C.D.Cal. 2014) 2014 WL 5017843, at *6 [“[t]he relationship between automobile manufacturers and their dealers has been examined by a host of courts throughout the country, all of which have agreed that dealers are not ‘agents’ of manufacturers.”].) Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege that Defendant’s authorized dealerships are its agents. Critically, “fraud must be pleaded specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Thus, without sufficiently alleging the existence of a transactional relationship, the complaint fails to sufficiently allege the existence of a duty.

Further, Plaintiff’s own complaint undermines the assertion that the information regarding the “sensing defect” was solely in the possession of Defendant.  In Paragraph 63 of the complaint, Plaintiff sets forth a series of complaints about the “sensing defects” in Honda vehicles which were publicly available between March 9, 2017 and April 17, 2020, all of which were posted prior to Plaintiff’s purchase of the subject vehicle.

Accordingly, because the complaint does not sufficiently allege a direct transaction between the parties, the fifth cause of action for fraudulent concealment has not been sufficiently alleged. Because Plaintiff’s own complaint makes clear that information regarding the existence of the “sensing defect” was available to the public prior to Plaintiff’s purchase of the vehicle, the court finds that there is no possibility of successful amendment.  For the sake of judicial economy, the court declines to address Defendant’s remaining argument regarding preemption.

B.     MOTION TO STRIKE

Because Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings has been granted as to the fifth cause of action, and the motion to strike attacks the punitive damages that would flow only from that cause of action, it is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED and the motion to strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.
