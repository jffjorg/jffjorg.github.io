DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV00960    Hearing Date: November 25, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV00960

MOTION

Motion to Reopen Discovery

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Philip K. Anthony, Michael Cobo, Nicole Khoshnoud, Deepwater, Inc., and HM&R, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff U.S. Legal Support, Inc.

 

MOTION

Plaintiff U.S. Legal Support, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed this shareholder derivative action regarding DecisionQuest, LLC (“DecisionQuest”) against Defendants Philip K. Anthony (“Anthony”), Michael Cobo, Nicole Khoshnoud, Deepwater, Inc., and HM&R, LLC (collectively, “Defendants”).  Defendants seek leave to reopen fact discovery for the purpose of propounding written discovery on Plaintiff.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

In determining whether to re-open discovery, the Court must consider the necessity of and reasons for the additional discovery, the diligence or lack thereof by the party seeking to reopen discovery in attempting to complete discovery prior to the cutoff, whether permitting the discovery will prevent the case from going forward on the trial date or will otherwise prejudice any party, and any past continuances of the trial date.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (b).)

Defendants rely on the declaration of their counsel, David P. Crochetiere (“Counsel”).  Counsel fails to address whether Defendants were diligent in seeking to complete the discovery at issue prior to the cutoff date.  Instead, Counsel states Plaintiff “all but abandoned its discovery efforts after October 2022 . . . .”  (Declaration of David P. Crochetiere, ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff’s discovery is irrelevant to whether Defendants were diligent in seeking discovery. 

Defendants also rely on the declaration of Brain A. Sun (“Sun”), who is Anthony’s counsel in criminal proceedings.  Sun states Anthony knew the United States Attorney’s Office for the Central District of California initiated proceedings to investigate Anthony by October 26, 2022, and he learned in March 2024 the investigation was ongoing and had expanded to include potential crimes relating to DecisionQuest’s employee stock ownership plan.  (Declaration of Brian A. Sun, ¶ 8.)  This does not explain Defendants’ delay in pursuing discovery.  Indeed, Sun concedes he suspected Plaintiff had disclosed confidential information and referred Defendants for criminal prosecution in December 2022.  (Id., ¶ 6.)  Defendants, therefore, had ample opportunity to conduct discovery prior to the discovery cut-off date of June 14, 2024.  Even assuming Defendants learned of new information in March 2024, Defendants could have propounded discovery before the cut-off date. 

Accordingly, the Court denies the motion as being without evidentiary support.

Defendants are to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 22STCV13023    Hearing Date: November 25, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV13023

MOTION

Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Van Nuys-L, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTIES

Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky

 

MOTION

Defendant Van Nuys-L, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky (“Plaintiff”) to further respond to Special Interrogatories, set three (“SROG”) and Requests for Admissions, set three (“RFA”).  Plaintiff opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories or requests for admissions unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, 2033.290.)

In response to Defendant’s SROG at issue, Plaintiff asserted objections.  As Defendant timely filed this motion to compel further responses, Plaintiff has the burden to justify his objections.  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

In SROG Nos. 36, 37, and 38, Defendant seeks information about payments Plaintiff received from the Employment Development Department (EDD).  Plaintiff objects such information is privileged.  Plaintiff is incorrect.  Information about Plaintiff’s submissions to the Employment Development Department is not discoverable.  (Richards v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 635, 638-639.)  However, information about payments Plaintiff received does not fall within the privilege.  Plaintiff must respond to SROG Nos. 36, 37, and 38.  In SROG No. 39, Defendant asks Plaintiff about the information Plaintiff submitted to the EDD.  That information is privileged.  Plaintiff need not further respond to SROG No. 39.

In SROG Nos. 44, 45, 46, Defendant asks Plaintiff about any applicable disability insurance policies Plaintiff has.  Plaintiff cites no applicable privilege that would justify Plaintiff’s complete failure to respond to these requests.  Instead, Plaintiff argues the information Defendant seeks is irrelevant.  A party may obtain discovery on any matter that “appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence . . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)  This rule is “applied liberally in favor of discovery . . . , and (contrary to popular belief), fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases.” (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546, emphasis deleted.)  Plaintiff must respond.

In SROG No. 52, Defendant asks, “Please state at what point (i.e. 12 months, 24 months) YOUR leave of absence provided by DEFENDANT would have become indefinite?”  (See Separate Statement in Support of Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Defendant’s Special Interrogatories, Set Three, p. 22.)  Plaintiff fails to justify his objections to the SROG.  The SROG is not so ambiguous as to be unintelligible.  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783, superseded by statute on another ground as stated in Guzman v. General Motors Corp. (1984) 154 Cal.App.3d 438, 444.)  Plaintiff contends responding to SROG No. 52 would require Plaintiff to reveal attorney work product.  The party resisting the discovery based on absolute work product protection must make a preliminary showing disclosure would reveal Defendant’s counsel’s “impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030, subd. (a).)  Plaintiff fails to do so.  Plaintiff must respond to SROG No. 52.

Defendant moves to compel further responses to RFA Nos. 39, 40, 41, and 42, which ask Plaintiff to admit facts pertaining to his claim with the EDD.  Plaintiff asserted only objections to the RFA.  Plaintiff must provide an answer that is “as complete and straightforward” as possible, and must “[a]dmit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true . . . or as reasonably and clearly qualified by” Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220, subds. (a), (b)(1).)   Plaintiff’s objections do not warrant Plaintiff’s wholesale refusal to respond to the RFA.  As set forth above, the information Plaintiff submitted to the EDD is not discoverable.  However, the privilege that covers the information Plaintiff submitted to the EDD does not cover any questions regarding Plaintiff’s disability claim.  If Plaintiff could not respond to the RFA as written, Plaintiff was required to qualify his responses.  Therefore, Plaintiff must further respond to the RFA at issue.

The Court notes in its notice concerning concludes Plaintiff’s refusal to provide adequate responses to the discovery is an abuse of the discovery process and awards sanctions.  The Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel of record, Berenji Law Firm, APC, in the amount of $2,850, which represents six hours of attorney time to prepare the motions and attend the hearing at $475 per hour.  Plaintiff and his counsel of record, jointly and severally, are to pay sanctions in the amount of $2,850 to Defendant, by and through counsel, within 30 days of notice of this ruling.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Steve Martinez and Martin Martinez Lopez

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Steve Martinez and Martin Martinez Lopez (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.  Defendant demurs to the third cause of action in Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint.  Plaintiffs oppose the demurrer. 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Plaintiffs’ third cause of action is brought under Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3), which provides, “Every manufacturer of consumer goods sold in this state and for which the manufacturer has made an express warranty shall: [¶] Make available to authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period.”  In support of this claim, Plaintiffs allege they purchased a Honda CR-V vehicle from Defendant, which included a written warranty (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 9, 10), and “Defendant failed to make available to its authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period” (id., ¶ 34).  Plaintiffs’ allegations suffice for pleading purposes. 

The level of specificity for which Defendants appear to argue is not warranted at this stage of the litigation.  Defendant will have the opportunity to obtain further information regarding Plaintiffs’ claim though discovery.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.  Defendant is to answer the complaint within 20 days. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
