DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30920    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Thursday, September 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   21STCV30920

CASE NAME:                        Raul Raygoza v. General Motors, LLC

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant General Motors LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Raul Raygoza

TRIAL DATE:                        Post settlement  

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion to Tax Costs

OPPOSITION:                        27 August 2024

REPLY:                                  3 September 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Defendant GM’s motion to strike costs is denied. Plaintiff to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On August 20, 2021, Raul Raygoza (“Plaintiff”) filed a lemon law action against General Motors LLC (“Defendant” or “GM”) and Does 1 to 10 for (1) Violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(d); (2) Violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(b); (3) Violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(A)(3); (4) Breach of Express Written Warranty (Civ. Code §§ 1791.2(a), 1794); and (5) Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civ. Code §§ 1791.1, 1794, 1795.5).

 

On December 4, 2023, a Notice of Settlement was filed. On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees was granted in the sum of $20,982.00.

 

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a memorandum of costs for $1,615.71.

 

Defendant GM now moves to strike costs. Plaintiff opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.


 

motion to strike costs

 

I.                Legal Standard

 

CCP § 1033.5 sets forth the costs recoverable by the prevailing party. To recover a cost, it must be reasonably necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount. (Perko’s Enterprises, Inc. v. RRNS Enterprises (l992) 4 Cal.App.4th 238, 244.) " If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-74.) “On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ibid.) 

 

The losing party may dispute any or all the items in the prevailing party’s memorandum of costs by a motion to strike or tax costs. (CRC, rule 3.1700(b).) A motion to strike challenges the entire costs memorandum, whereas a motion to tax challenges particular items or amounts. 

 

II.        Discussion

 

Defendant GM seeks to strike the memorandum of costs on the grounds it is untimely and seeks unreasonable costs.

 

A.        Timeliness of Memorandum of Costs

 

Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a) states:

 

A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.5 or the date of service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first. The memorandum of costs must be verified by a statement of the party, attorney, or agent that to the best of his or her knowledge the items of cost are correct and were necessarily incurred in the case.

Defendant GM asserts that because a CCP § 998 settlement is the functional equivalent of a dismissal, Plaintiff had 15 days from then to file a memorandum of costs. “[T]he term ‘judgment’ in section 998 is meant to include its functional equivalents, such as dismissal of a case with prejudice.” (Madrigal v. Hyundai Motor America (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 385, 400, review granted].)

 

The Court disagrees for three reasons.  First, Madrigal does not stand for the proposition that the 15-day deadline to file a memorandum of costs begins to accrue at the time a party accepts a 998 Offer. In DeSaulles v. Community Hospital of Monterey Peninsula (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1140, the California Supreme Court explained that for purposes of enforcing a settlement, a 998 Offer was the equivalent of a dismissal and made no assertion that 998 settlements start the accrual of the 15 day deadline under CRC, rule 3.1700(a).  “‘[A]s between the parties thereto and for purposes of enforcement of settlement agreements, a compromise agreement contemplating payment by defendant and dismissal of the action by plaintiff is the legal equivalent of a judgment in plaintiff's favor.’ [Citation.]” (DeSaulles, p. 115.)  

 

Neither Madrigal nor DeSaulles stand for the proposition that acceptance of a 998 Offer triggers the 15 day deadline to file a memorandum of costs. “Cases are not authority for propositions not considered.” (KCSFV I, LLC v. Florin County Water Dist. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 1015, 1032.)

 

Second, Rule 3.1700 applies only to allowable costs under CCP § 1033.5(a) and not statutory costs permitted under Civ. Code § 1794(d). (See Anthony v. City of Los Angeles (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 1011, 1015.)  Third, time provisions related to the filing of a memorandum are mandatory, but not jurisdictional. (Sanabria v. Embrey (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 422, 426.) Accordingly, the court has the discretion to consider an untimely memorandum of costs. (Anthony, supra, 166 Cal.App.4th at p. 1015, fn. 2.)

 

The court does not find Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs untimely and Defendant has cited no authority that precludes the court from awarding costs even if the memorandum was untimely filed.

 

B.        Discrepancy In Costs Sought

 

Defendant GM objects to the $1,615.71 sought in the memorandum of costs on the grounds that Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees sought costs in the sum of $1,296.86 but now the memorandum of costs seeks $318.85 more. Defendant GM fails to cite case law holding that the fact the memorandum of costs seeks additional costs is a sufficient basis to strike those costs or that the $318.85 sought by Plaintiff is not a recoverable cost under Civ. Code § 1794(d). “If the items on a verified cost bill appear proper charges, they are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred.” (Seever v. Copley Press, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1550, 1557.)

 

The fact that the memorandum of costs seeks additional costs from Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs does not mean that the costs were not reasonably incurred.  Moreover, Defendant cites no basis that would permit the court to strike those costs.

 

C.        Item 2: Jury Fees

 

Defendant GM objects $150.00 in costs for jury fees on the basis the case was settled and never went to trial. Plaintiff asserts that if he did not pay jury fees, he would have waived the right to a jury trial, and until the action was settled, Plaintiff had the obligation to continue to prepare for trial.

 

The court finds that Defendant GM has failed to show that Plaintiff’s jury fees costs were not reasonably incurred nor necessary to the conduct of the litigation. Therefore, the court will not strike jury fees from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.


 

D.        Item 16- Other Costs

 

Defendant GM objects to the $37.70 sought by Plaintiff’s counsel for filing a Notice of Change of Address or Other Contact Information. Defendant GM asserts that the change of address was unnecessary and is not properly compensable. Plaintiff asserts that the Change of Address motion is an allowable cost and Defendant GM has not shown that the cost was unreasonable. (CCP, 1033.5(a)(1); see Jensen v. BMW of North America (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112, 137-138 [examining the legislative history of Song-Beverly to determine that the “costs and expenses” language was to be interpreted broadly to include such things as filing fees, expert witness fees, marshal’s fees, etc., to “‘open the litigation process to everyone.’”].)

 

The court agrees that Defendant GM has failed to show that the filing of Notice of Change of Address or Other Contact Information was merely convenient or beneficial and not necessary to the conduct of the litigation. Therefore, the $37.70 will not be taxed.

 

Defendant GM also objects to the $97.95 in costs incurred from courtesy copies related to “the 3/2/2023 stipulation and order and 4/9/2024 motion for attorney’s fees” because courtesy copies are not required by the court. (Motion, at p. 6:18-21.) While the court does not require courtesy copies, the court accepts courtesy copies of motion papers. Plaintiff also cites authority that delivery fees are compensable at the court’s discretion because they are not a disallowed cost under section 1033(b). (See Ladas v. California State Automobile Association (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 776.)

 

“[A] party's ‘mere statements in the points and authorities accompanying its notice of motion to strike cost bill and the declaration of its counsel are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing [that the costs were necessarily incurred].’ (Jones, 63 Cal.App.4th at p. 1266 citing Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Service, Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.) The court finds that the courtesy copies and fees associated with the delivery were reasonably incurred and will not be stricken from the memorandum of costs.

 

Defendant GM’s motion to tax costs is denied.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant GM’s motion to strike costs is denied. Plaintiff to give notice.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendants Stephen Hightower, Stephen Hightower II, and Hightowers Petroleum Company

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Holy Oil Partnership

TRIAL DATE:                        Not set.

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

OPPOSITION:                        27 August 2024

REPLY:                                  3 September 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Defendants’ demurrer is sustained with leave to amend. Defendant Stephen Hightower’s demurrer is sustained without leave to amend. The motion to strike is granted with leave to amend. Plaintiff is granted 10 days leave to amend. The court sets the OSC RE: Amended Complaint for October 10, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. Defendants to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On August 24, 2023, Holy Oil Partnerships (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Stephen Hightower (“Hightower”); Stephen Hightower II (“Hightower II”); Hightowers Petroleum Company (“HPC”) (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 100.

On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following six causes of action:

1)     Breach of Contract;

2)     Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing;

3)     Promissory Estoppel;

4)     Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage;

5)     Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; and

6)     Breach of Fiduciary Duty.

 

On June 3, 2024, the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer with leave to amend as to the first, second, third, and sixth causes of action and overruled as to the fourth and fifth causes of action.

 

On July 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging the same six causes of action as the FAC. Defendants filed a demurrer and move to strike the SAC. Plaintiff opposes the Motions. The matter is now before the court.

 

demurrer[1]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

A.        Demurrer 

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP, § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.”¿(C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)¿For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded.¿ (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.)¿A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.”¿(Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿ 

 

B.        Motion to Strike 

 

¿Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

 

C.        Leave to Amend 

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿¿¿ 

 III.     Discussion

 

A.        First Cause of Action - Breach of Contract

 

The elements of a claim for breach of contract are: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal. 4th 811, 821.) In addition, the complaint must demonstrate damages proximately caused by the breach. (St. Paul Ins. v. American Dynasty (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1060.) Furthermore, “the complaint must [also] indicate on its face whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct.” (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458-59 citing CCP, § 430.10(g).)

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff fails to allege the existence of an oral contract as the contract lacked express terms and mutual assent. “Under California law, a contract will be enforced if it is sufficiently definite (and this is a question of law) for the court to ascertain the parties' obligations and to determine whether those obligations have been performed or breached.” (Ersa Grae Corp. v. Fluor Corp. (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 613, 623.)

 

The SAC alleges that Plaintiff and Defendant formed a partnership and entered a contract “based on the roles and commitments of the three parties made during the Partnership Meeting and the Proposal[.]” (SAC, ¶¶ 52, 53.) “On March 18, 2022, Plaintiffs, City’s Mayor, and Hightower II conducted a video conference (“Partnership Meeting”). The parties discussed the “City Gas” branded station as well as other components of the P3 Project.” (Id., ¶ 39.) The SAC asserts that “[t]he parties engaged in a thorough discussion of the Proposal and confirmed the roles, ideas, and commitments of the parties outlined in the Proposal.” (Id., ¶ 39.)

 

Plaintiff fails to state what the Proposal outlined and what role each of the Defendants agreed to take. Plaintiff fails to state what contractual responsibilities Defendant Hightower II agreed to complete pursuant to the Proposal. (SAC, ¶ 28.) Instead, the SAC asserts contractual obligations were made orally by the Parties at the March 18, 2022 Partnership Meeting. (Id., ¶ 39.) The SAC asserts that Defendant Hightower II also agreed to secure the funding necessary for the P3 Project but fails to state when the funding had to be obtained and if the funding required Hightower II to find investors or secure personal loans for the P3 Project. Plaintiff states Hightower II on behalf of Defendant HPC agreed to “deploy smart grid technology” but fails to state whether a date was provided for this project, if Defendants were required to obtain permits or leases, and whether the approval by the Adelanto City Council was a condition precedent to Defendants’ performance. (SAC, ¶ 42.)

 

Plaintiff further states that Defendant Hightower II agreed to handle the daily operations of the City Gas station, but the SAC fails to state if the City Gas station had been built and Defendants’ performance is now due. (SAC, ¶ 44.) According to Plaintiff’s proposed asset development, Plaintiff had spoken with the owners of the land for “the proposed Fueling Station” and the owners were willing to move forward to the sale of the land. (Id., 29, Ex. 3.) The SAC asserts that the land acquisition would be handled by the City of Adelanto, but the SAC fails to state whether the acquisition was in fact completed. (Id., ¶ 51.)

 

“In contract law, a ‘condition precedent’ is ‘either an act of a party that must be performed or an

uncertain event that must happen before the contractual right accrues or the contractual duty

arises.’ [Citations.]” (Wm. R. Clarke Corp. v. Safeco Ins. Co. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 882, 885, fn. 1.)

According to the facts alleged in the SAC, the City of Adelanto must first purchase the land, and the City Gas station built, before Defendants are obligated to deploy smart grid technology to the first location as part of the P3 Project. (SAC, ¶¶ 42, 50.) Moreover, Defendants were not obligated to handle the daily operations or bring in Operational Partners to handle the daily operations of the City Gas station when the gas station had not yet been built and no land was purchased. (SAC, ¶ 44.)  Therefore, Defendants cannot be liable for breach of performance when the conditions precedent to their performance have not occurred. Furthermore, the SAC does not allege that the City’s performance was contingent on Defendants’ acquiring funding and continuing to be part of the P3 Project.

 

The remaining breach alleged by the SAC is that Defendants agreed to take the lead on the P3 Project and secure funding, but the SAC fails to state when Defendants’ performance was due and how much funding Defendant agreed to procure for the P3 Project. (SAC, ¶¶ 40, 44.) “To be enforceable, a promise must be definite enough that a court can determine the scope of the duty and the limits of performance must be sufficiently defined to provide a rational basis for the assessment of damages.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 770.) Without an estimation of how much funding Defendants agreed to procure for the P3 Project and when performance was due, the court cannot find that the terms of the oral contract were sufficiently definite that a contract existed between the parties.

 

Lastly, the SAC alleges that “[t]here was no definitive date in which the Contract would be performed by.”  (SAC, ¶ 55.) “Plaintiffs are informed and believe that, given the resources and relative capabilities of the parties, that the Contract could be performed within one year.” (SAC, ¶ 56.) Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim is barred by the statute of frauds. (Civ. Code, § 1624.) The statute of frauds applies “only to those contracts which, by their terms, cannot possibly be performed within one year.” (White Lighting Co. v. Wolfson (1968) 68 Cal.2d 336, 343.)

 

The facts in the SAC do not support the finding that the Contract/P3 Project could be performed within a year because there are no facts showing that the City could purchase the land within a year, the City Gas station could have been be built within a year, that Defendants could deploy the smart grid technology as part of the first phase of the P3 Project within one year, that Defendants could oversee daily operations of the City Gas station within one year, and that within one year Defendants could have obtained funding for the P3 Project.

 

Based on the facts above, the court finds that Plaintiff has failed to allege the existence of an oral contract that is valid and enforceable against Defendants. The demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

B.        Second Cause of Action – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

“Every contract contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing providing that no party to the contract will do anything that would deprive another party of the benefits of the contract.¿The implied covenant protects the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties based on their mutual promises.” (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 885.) The covenant thus cannot “ ‘be endowed with an existence independent of its contractual underpinnings.’ ” (Waller v. Truck Ins. Exchange, Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 1, 36 quoting Love v. Fire Ins. Exchange (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 1136, 1153.) “It cannot  impose substantive duties or limits on the contracting parties beyond those incorporated in the specific terms of their agreement.” (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349–350.)

 

As Plaintiff failed to plead the existence of a valid oral contract, the demurrer to the second cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

C.        Third Cause of Action – Promissory Estoppel

 

“‘The elements of a promissory estoppel claim are ‘(1) a promise clear and unambiguous in its terms; (2) reliance by the party to whom the promise is made; (3)[the] reliance must be both reasonable and foreseeable; and (4) the party asserting the estoppel must be injured by his reliance.’ [Citation.]” (Advanced Choices, Inc. v. State Dept. of Health Services (2010) 182 Cal.App.4th 1661, 1672, citing US Ecology, Inc. v. State of California (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 887, 901.) “Conceptually, promissory estoppel is distinct from contract in that the promisee's justifiable and detrimental reliance on the promise is regarded as a substitute for the consideration required as an element of an enforceable contract.” (Signal Hill Aviation Co. v. Stroppe (1979) 96 Cal.App.3d 627, 640.)

 

The court finds that the SAC fails to plead a clear and unambiguous promise as the SAC fails to state what Defendants’ obligations were in taking the lead on the P3 Project and how much and by what means Defendants were to obtain the funding for the P3 Project. (SAC, ¶ 40.) Plaintiff further asserts that they justifiably relied on Defendants’ promise by securing additional partners and reached agreements with Kowrii Bank and Lincoln Avenue Brewery to support the P3 Project and incurred substantial expenses by conducting extensive research, engaging with potential partners, and covering costs to advance the P3 Project, all in reliance on Defendants’ assurances. (SAC, ¶¶ 58-60.)

 

The SAC fails to show that Plaintiff’s agreement with Lincoln Avenue Brewery was reasonable given the fact that the plot for the City Gas station had not been sold, the City had not approved the purchase, and the gas station had not yet been built. Moreover, the agreement with Kowrii Bank was finalized on or about February 12, 2021, as evidenced by the cashier’s check. (SAC, ¶ 59.) However, the SAC asserts that the agreement was reached on March 18, 2022, therefore Plaintiff could not have relied on Defendants’ promises related to the Kowrii Bank transaction when the Promise had not yet been made. (SAC, ¶ 37.)

 

The demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

D.        Sixth Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

“‘[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.’” (Hassov. Hapke (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 107, 140 (quoting Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp.(1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 221).) “A fiduciary duty under common law may arise ‘when one person enters into a confidential relationship with another.’” (Id.) A¿partnership relationship is a fiduciary relationship. (See Cleveland v. Johnson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1338.)

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action on the basis that the SAC fails to plead the existence of a fiduciary relationship because there was no Partnership Agreement.

 

While the SAC alleges the existence of a contract, the terms of the contract are not definite and do not support the finding that Defendants entered a fiduciary relationship with Plaintiff. Therefore, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            E.        Defendant Stephen Hightower’s Individual Demurrer

 

Defendant Stephen Hightower demurs to the entire SAC because the SAC failed to allege any specific allegations against him. The court previously sustained the demurrer as to Defendant Stephen Hightower because the FAC was devoid of factual allegations as to his wrongful conduct. (6/03/2024 Order.) Plaintiff’s opposition also failed to address Defendant Hightower’s contentions that the SAC was insufficiently pled as to him. All that the SAC states are that Defendant Hightower is the chief executive officer and president of Defendant HPC but there are no allegations as to how Defendant Hightower ratified Defendants HPC or Hightower II’s conduct.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer as to him is sustained without leave to amend.

Conclusion

 

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained with leave to amend. Defendant Stephen Hightower’s demurrer is sustained without leave to amend. The motion to strike is granted with leave to amend.
