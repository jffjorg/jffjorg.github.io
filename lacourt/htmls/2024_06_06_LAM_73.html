DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCP36316    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: 73

06/06/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

Martin Warren, et al. v. Scott Kenneth Warren, et al. (22STCV26316) 

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainant: Scott Warren (Wolfe & Wyman LLP)  

Counsel for Cross-Defendant: Lauri Shahar, James Felton (G&B Law, LLP)

 

(1)   CROSS-COMPLAINANT’S MOTION FOR ASSIGNMENT ORDER; FOR ORDER RESTRAINING JUDGMENT DEBTOR; AND ORDER REQUIRING DISCLOSURE (filed on 01/23/24)

(2)   CROSS-DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO CORRECT JUDGMENT NUNC PRO TUNC TO REMOVE IMPROPER COSTS AND EXPERT WITNESS FEES AWARDED AGAINST CROSS-DEFENDANT (filed on 02/23/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendant Laurie Shahar’s Motion to Correct Judgment Nunc Pro Tunc to Remove Improper Costs and Expert Witness Fees Awarded Against Cross-Defendant is DENIED.

 

Cross-Complainant Scott Warren’s Motion for Assignment Order; For Order Restraining Judgment Debtor; and Order Requiring Disclosure is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

 On August 15, 2022, plaintiffs Martin Warren (“Martin”) and Bessie Ellen Warren (“Bessie”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against defendants Scott Kenneth Warren (“Scott”), Bruce Michael Warren (“Bruce”), and Does 1 through 10 (collectively, “Defendants”). The First Amended Complaint alleges causes of action for:

1.     Financial Elder Abuse 

2.     Breach of Contract

3.     Fraud/Concealment

4.     Negligence

5.     Rescission

6.     Cancellation of Written Instrument under Civil Code section 3412 

7.     Restitution Based on Unjust Enrichment

8.     Promissory Estoppel

 

On November 17, 2022, Scott filed a Cross-Complaint against Martin, Bessie, Laurie Warren Shahar (“Laurie”) and Toren Shahar (“Torren”) alleging causes of action for:

1.     Declaratory Judgment

2.     Declaratory Judgment

3.     Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

4.     Anticipatory Breach of Contract

5.     Equitable Indemnity

6.     Equitable Indemnity

7.     Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

On January 26, 2023, Bruce filed a Cross-Complaint against Laurie and Toren alleging causes of action for:

1.     Equitable Indemnity against Laurie

2.     Equitable Indemnity against Toren

 

On September 27, 2023, Scott Kenneth Warren, individually, and as Trustee of the Hard Habit Trust, obtained a jury verdict of $600,000 against Lauri Shahar on his cross-complaint in the above entitled action. The Court entered judgment on September 27, 2023 and entered a corrected judgment on October 2, 2023.

 

a.     Allegations in the First-Amended Complaint

 

Plaintiffs Martin and Bessie are 90 and 92 years old, respectively, and have three adult children: daughter Lauri and sons Scott and Bruce. (FAC, ¶¶ 1-3.)  

 

“In the 1970’s [sic], Plaintiffs began purchasing various residential properties and apartment buildings – mostly in Los Angeles – and continued doing so over the next several decades.” (FAC, ¶ 11.)  

 

“Through their savvy investments, diligent management, and hard work, Plaintiffs amassed properties worth well over $20,000,000.” (FAC, ¶ 12.)  

 

“As Plaintiffs aged, they began turning over the management duties and responsibilities [of their properties] to their three adult children.” (FAC, ¶ 13.) 

 

“Scott, a tax attorney with a LLM from New York State University and licensed to practice law in both New York and California, represented and assisted Plaintiffs over the years with respect to their estate planning, tax planning, tax return preparation, and the gifting of many of the properties to the three children to mitigate tax estate consequences.” (FAC, ¶ 14.)

 

“Bruce, an attorney licensed to practice in California, also began to help manage some of Plaintiffs’ properties. ANALYSIS

 

B.     Cross-Defendant Laurie’s Motion to Correct Judgment Nunc Pro Tunc to Remove Improper Costs and Expert Witness Fees Awarded Against Cross-Defendant

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

“A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment. . .” (CRC, 3.1700(a)(1).) The memorandum of costs must be verified by a statement of the party, attorney, or agent that to the best of his or her knowledge, the items of costs are correct and were necessarily incurred in the case. (CRC, Rule 3.1700(a)(1).) A properly verified memorandum of costs is sufficient to establish the reasonable necessity of the costs claimed therein. (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.) In other words, a verified cost bill constitutes prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed on the cost bill were necessarily incurred. (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 624.) The items in the cost bill are only put in issue when they are properly objected to by a motion to strike or tax costs. (Id.)

 

“Any notice of motion to strike or tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum.” (CRC, 3.1700(b)(1).) Trial courts may extend the times for serving and filing a motion to strike or tax costs for a period not to exceed 30 days. (CRC, 3.1700(b)(3).) “After the time has passed for a motion to strike or tax costs or for determination of that motion, the clerk must immediately enter the costs on the judgment.” (CRC. 3.1700(b)(4).) The time provisions related to filing and opposing a memorandum of costs are mandatory. (Sanabria v. Embrey (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 422, 426.)

 

b.     Discussion

 

Lauri moves the Court to correct the judgment entered in this action to remove improper costs awarded against Lauri on the grounds that: (1) the clerk may have inadvertently entered costs in favor of Scott and against Lauri based on Scott’s memorandum of costs, and/or (2) Lauri and her counsel through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, justifiably but mistakenly believed that Scott’s memorandum of costs was directed solely against Plaintiffs and on that basis did not challenge it by way of a motion to strike/tax costs.

 

                                                    i.     Clerical mistake

 

CCP § 473 (d) provides: “The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” Unless the challenged portion of the judgment was entered inadvertently, it cannot be changed post-judgment under the guise of correction of a “clerical error.” (Tokio Marine & Fire Ins. Corp. v. Western Pacific Roofing Corp. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 110, 117.)

 

On September 27, 2023, Scott Kenneth Warren, individually, and as Trustee of the Hard Habit Trust, obtained a jury verdict of $600,000 against Lauri Shahar on his cross-complaint in the above entitled action. The Court entered judgment on September 27, 2023 and entered a corrected judgment on October 2, 2023.

 

In the Court’s October 2, 2023 corrected judgment, it states that Scott recovers against Lauri the sum of $600,000 with interest thereon at the rate of ten percent (10%) per annum from the date of the entry of this judgment and their costs and disbursements against Lauri. The corrected judgment included a blank space to enter Scott’s costs and disbursements against Lauri.

 

Lauri asserts she was not on notice of Scott’s costs because it was not input into the corrected judgment. However, pursuant to CRC, 3.1700(b)(4), the clerk is not required to input costs until after the time has passed for Lauri to move to strike or tax Scott’s cost bill. Thus, including a blank space to enter Scott’s costs and disbursements against Lauri was not an error inadvertently made by the clerk. Further, since Lauri did not timely file a motion to motion to strike or tax, the clerk did not inadvertently enter judgment in accordance with Scott’s uncontested Memorandum of costs. A clerical error only exists when the challenged judgment was entered inadvertently. (Bowden v. Green (1982) 128 Cal.App.3d 65, 70.) Here, Lauri has not established that the clerk entered the judgment inadvertently. The Court does not find this as grounds to correct the judgment.

 

Accordingly, the Court does not grant Lauri’s motion on this ground.

 

                                                   ii.     Mistake, Inadvertence, Surprise, Or Excusable Neglect

 

There are two types of relief set forth in section 473, subdivision (b): discretionary and mandatory. Under the discretionary provision, ”[t]he court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.“ (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b), italics added.) Application for relief “shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.“ (Ibid.)

 

Under the mandatory provision, ”[n]otwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.“ (§ 473, subd. (b), italics added.)

 

First, the Court notes that Lauri is not entitled to mandatory relief under section 473 because it only provides relief for default judgments or dismissals. (Douglas v. Willis (1994) 27 Cal. App. 4th 287, 291.) Here, the costs order does not constitute a default judgment or dismissal. Thus, Lauri is only able to seek discretionary relief under section 473.

 

Next, the Court also does not find that Lauri has met her burden of demonstrating that she is entitled to discretionary relief. A party seeking relief under section 473 on the grounds of excusable neglect bears the burden of demonstrating that the neglect was excusable in order to secure relief. (Cochran v. Linn (1984) 159 Cal.App.3d 245, 252.) The test of whether neglect was excusable is whether "`a reasonably prudent person under the same or similar circumstances might have made the same error. [Citations.]" (Bettencourt v. Los Rios Community College Dist. (1986) 42 Cal.3d 270, 276.)

 

Lauri argues that her and her counsel mistakenly believed that Scott’s cost memorandum was only intended to apply to Scott’s successful defense of his parents’ Complaint and not intended to address costs from his Cross-Complaint against Lauri. Lauri asserts that Scott’s memo was only directed to Plaintiff’s because the caption names Plaintiffs Martin Warren and Bessie Ellen Warren, but do not mention Lauri’s name. (Motion, Ex. 2, p. 1.) However, the Court finds that Scott’s memo includes the appropriate caption. The Plaintiffs in the underlying lawsuit are only Martin Warren and Bessie Ellen Warren. Lauri’s name would not be included on the line designated for Plaintiffs. The underlying lawsuit encompasses Scott’s Cross-Complaint against Lauri. Thus, the Court does not agree that a reasonably prudent person would have thought the Scott memo was solely directed at Plaintiffs based on the caption.

 

Lauri also argues some costs in Scott’s memo should not be recovered against Lauri because they were not related to Scott’s Cross-Complaint against her, Scott’s Cross-Complaint was not inextricable intertwined with the allegations in Plaintiffs’ Complaint and Lauri was not jointly and severally liable for the costs Scott seeks to recover in defense of Plaintiffs’ Complaint. However, Lauri should have disputed these costs in a Motion to Strike or Tax Costs. Further, Scott asserts there are costs in the Scott memo that could only be attributed to costs associates with the prosecution of his cross-complaint against Lauri. Scott also contends Lauri was on notice that she would be required to pay costs and disbursements as indicated on the Court’s judgment. Scott argues that a reasonably prudent person also could have contacted Scott’s counsel and inquired as to whether the memo was recovering costs against Lauri, however, Lauri and her counsel did not inquire and chose not to file a motion to tax. Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that Lauri has met her burden of demonstrating that she is entitled to discretionary relief.

 

Thus, the Court denies Lauri’s Motion to Correct Judgment Nunc Pro Tunc to Remove Improper Costs and Expert Witness Fees. Lauri is ordered to pay the total amount due of $175,195.47 within 10 days of the Court’s order.

 

C.     Cross-Complainant’s Motion for Assignment Order; For Order Restraining Judgment Debtor; and Order Requiring Disclosure

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

CCP section 708.510, subdivision (a) states, in relevant part:¿Except as otherwise provided by law, upon application of the judgment creditor on noticed motion, the court may order the judgment debtor to assign to the judgment creditor or to a receiver appointed pursuant to Article 7 (commencing with Section 708.610) all or part of a right to payment due or to become due, whether or not the right is conditioned on future developments, including but not limited to the following types of payments:¿ 

 

1.     Wages due from the federal government that are not subject to withholding under an earnings withholding order.¿ 

2.     Rents.¿ 

3.     Commissions.¿ 

4.     Royalties.¿ 

5.     Payments due from a patent or copyright.¿ 

6.     Insurance policy loan value.¿ 

 

(CCP 708.510, subd. (a).)¿ 

 

CCP section 708.510, subdivision (c) provides:¿In determining whether to order an assignment or the amount of an assignment pursuant to subdivision (a), the court may take into consideration all relevant factors, including the following:¿ 

 

1.     The reasonable requirements of a judgment debtor who is a natural person and of persons supported in whole or in part by the judgment debtor. 

2.     Payments the judgment debtor is required to make or that are deducted in satisfaction of other judgments and wage assignments, including earnings assignment orders for support. 

3.     The amount remaining due on the money judgment.¿ 

4.      The amount being or to be received in satisfaction of the right to payment that may be assigned.¿ 

 

(CCP § 708.510, subd. (c).)¿ 

 

Under CCP section 708.510, subdivision (d), the court may order the assignment of property only to the extent necessary to satisfy the money judgment.¿ (CCP § 708.510, subd. (d).)¿ The motion must include sufficient facts to permit the court to make a determination that the payment is assignable to the judgment creditor.¿ (Kracht v. Perrin (1990) 219 Cal.App.3d 1019, 1023.) 

 

b.     Discussion

 

At the time that Scott filed his motion, he contended that he had a judgment against Lauri in the amount of $775,195.47, plus 10% legal interest from entry of judgment, that remained unsatisfied. Thus, Scott sought an order directing Lauri to assign him the right to collect rents from tenants of apartment buildings owned by DD Partners, LLC until such time as the judgment is fully satisfied.

 

However, since the filing of the motion, the parties do not dispute that Lauri has paid the entire principal amount of Scott’s judgment totaling $600,000. (Oppos., p. 2; Reply, p. 1.) Thus, Scott’s motion is moot as to the principal amount of Scott’s judgment. The only amount remaining to be paid is Scott’s disputed cost bill of $175,195.47 which was the subject of Lauri’s Motion to Correct Judgment Nunc Pro Tunc to Remove Improper Costs and Expert Witness Fees. As discussed above, the Court denies Lauri’s motion and has ordered her to pay the total amount due of $175,195.47 within 10 days of the Court’s order.

 

Thus, Scott’s motion is denied.

 

III. CONCLUSION

 

Cross-Complainant’s Motion for Assignment Order; For Order Restraining Judgment Debtor; and Order Requiring Disclosure is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Cross-Defendant’s Motion to Correct Judgment Nunc Pro Tunc to Remove Improper Costs and Expert Witness Fees Awarded Against Cross-Defendant is DENIED. Cross-Defendant is ordered to pay the total amount due of $175,195.47 within 10 days of the Court’s order.

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 5/08/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 26, 2023, Plaintiff Wesco Insurance Company filed the instant action against Defendant Jerry Selvin Sanchez individually and dba Swish Cleaning Services.

 

The Complaint alleges three causes of action for breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges it provided Defendant with Workers Compensation and Employers Liability Insurance coverage in 2020, 2021, and 2022. Plaintiff performed an audit on the three policies and found the premium basis estimate provided by the Defendant was inaccurate. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant is obligated to pay the actual audited total premium and fees, but Defendant has refused to make further payments.

 

On April 18, 2024, default was entered against Defendant.  

 

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an OSC re: Default Judgment. Plaintiff seeks judgment against Defendant in the total amount of $59,586.84, consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $52,367.00, $6,659.84 in interest and $560.00 in costs. 

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.)
