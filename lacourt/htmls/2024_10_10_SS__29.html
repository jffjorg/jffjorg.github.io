DEPARTMENT 29 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 29 - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS IMPORTANT  (PLEASE SEND YOUR E-MAIL TO DEPT. 29 NOT DEPT. 2)

Communicating with the Court Staff re the Tentative Ruling 1. Please notify the courtroom staff by email not later than 9:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion. The email address is SSCDEPT29@lacourt.org. Please do not use any other email address. 2. You must include the other parties on the email by "cc." 3. Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the Subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. THE COURT WILL HEAR ARGUMENT UNLESS BOTH SIDES SUBMIT ON THE TENTATIVE.  4. Include the words "SUBMISSION BUT WILL APPEAR" if you submit, but one or both parties will nevertheless appear. 5. For other communications with Court Staff a. OFF-CALENDAR should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed to have a matter placed off-calendar. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) date of proceeding. b. CASE SETTLED should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed that the case has settled for all purposes. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) whether notice of settlement/dismissal documents have been filed; (c) if (b) has not been done, a date one year from the date of your email which will be a date set by the court for an OSC for dismissal of the case. c. STIPULATION should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have stipulated that a matter before the court can be postponed. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) what proceeding is agreed to be postponed e.g. Trial, FSC; (c) the agreed-upon future date; (d) whether all parties waive notice if the Court informs all counsel/parties that the agreed-upon date is satisfactory. This communication should be used only for matters that are agreed to be postponed and not for orders shortening time. 6. PLEASE MAKE SURE THAT ALL COMMUNICATIONS WITH COURT STAFF DEAL ONLY WITH SCHEDULING AND ADMINISTRATIVE MATTERS AND DO NOT DISCUSS THE MERITS OF ANY CASE. (UPDATED 6/17/2020) 
IMPORTANT:  In light of the COVID-19 emergency, the Court encourages all parties to appear remotely.  The capacity in the courtroom is extremely limited.  The Court appreciates the cooperation of counsel and the litigants. 
ALSO NOTE:  If the moving party does not contact the court to submit on the tentative and does not appear (either remotely or in person), the motion will be taken off calendar.  THE TENTATIVE RULING WILL NOT BE THE ORDER OF THE COURT.



Case Number: 21STCV35476    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 29

Davis v. Fink
21STCV35476
Defendants’ Motion to Continue Trial and to Compel Medical Examination

Tentative

The request to continue trial is denied as moot.

The request to compel a medical examination of Plaintiff is denied without prejudice on procedural grounds.

Background

On September 27, 2021, Dru Davis (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Alex Stuart Fink, 5 Star Hollywood Tours, LLC (collectively “Defendants”), and Does 1 through 50 for negligence arising out of an automobile accident occurring on September 30, 2019.

Defendants filed their answer on May 8, 2023.

On September 11, 2024, Defendants filed this motion to continue trial and for an order compelling a medical examination of Plaintiff. Defendants also seek sanctions.

No opposition has been filed.

A supplemental declaration in support of the motion was filed by Defendants on September 25, 2024.

The parties submitted a stipulation to continue the trial date on October 4, 2024, which the Court approved on October 7.  Accordingly, the request in the motion to continue the trial date is now moot. 

The Court will proceed to consider the merits of the request in the motion to compel a medical examination of Plaintiff.

Legal Standard

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by means of a physical or mental examination of (1) a party to the action, (2) an agent of any party, or (3) a natural person in the custody or under the legal control of a party, in any action in which the mental or physical condition (including the blood group) of that party or other person is in controversy in the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.020, subd. (a).)

Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.220 provides that in a personal injury action, the defendant may demand one physical examination of plaintiff as of right, without advance leave of the court:

“(a) In any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff, if both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1)  The examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive.

(2)  The examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.

(b)  A defendant may make a demand under this article without leave of court ….

(c)   A demand under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the physician who will perform the examination.

(d)  A physical examination demanded under subdivision (a) shall be scheduled for a date that is at least 30 days after service of the demand ….”

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220.) 

Within 20 days of service of a demand for a physical examination, the plaintiff to whom the demand is directed must respond in writing and state that the plaintiff “will comply with the demand as stated, will comply with the demand as specifically modified by the plaintiff, or will refuse, for reasons specified in the response, to submit to the demanded physical examination.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.230, subd. (a).)  Failure to serve a timely response waives any objection to the demand.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.240, subd. (a).)

If a plaintiff fails to respond to the demand for a physical examination, Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.240 provides that the defendant “may move for an order compelling response and compliance with [the] demand.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.240, subd. (b).)  No meet and confer is required.

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel response and compliance with a demand for a physical examination, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.240, subd. (c).)

In Chapter 7 of the Civil Discovery Act, section 2023.010, subdivision (d), defines “[m]isuses of the discovery process” to include “[f]ailing to respond to or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  Where a party or attorney has engaged in misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose a monetary sanction in the amount of “the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).)

Discussion

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Defendants seek two discrete items of relief in what is presented as a single motion. At best, this is poor practice; at worst, it is impermissible. As a leading treatise states, “Each motion or demurrer should normally be set forth in a separate document.” (2 Weil & Brown, California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial [2024] ¶ 9:24.3.)

The Court exercises its discretion, on this one occasion, to disregard this procedural defect. In the future, however, the Court expects the parties to file separate motions when multiple disparate items of relief are requested.

As noted above, Defendants’ request in the motion to continue trial has become moot based on subsequent events.  Accordingly, the Court now turns to the portion of the motion seeking an order compelling the medical examination of Plaintiff.

In particular, Defendants seek an order compelling Plaintiff to submit to a medical examination by Dr. Steven Nagelberg.  (Kwong Decl., ¶ 6 & Exh. B.)  Plaintiff alleges a variety of injuries from the accident, including back, neck, and shoulder pain, and Dr. Nagelberg is an orthopedic surgeon.  (Id., ¶ 5 & Exh. B.)

Defendants initially served a demand for the examination on July 29, 2024, with the examination scheduled for September 3.  (Id., ¶ 6 & Exh. B.)  Subsequently, however, Defendants changed the date of the examination (as a result of the doctor’s schedule): on August 21, 2024, served an amended demand with the examination scheduled for September 5.  (Id., ¶ 7 & Exh. C.)

Plaintiff did not serve a response or objection to the initial demand or the amended demand.  (Id., ¶ 8.)  Plaintiff did not appear for the examination by Dr. Nagelberg on September 5.  (Id., ¶ 9.)

Defendants now seeks a court order requiring Plaintiff to appear for and be examined by Dr. Nagelberg.  Defendants are entitled to the examination as of right (assuming that this is the first physical examination of Plaintiff requested by Defendants).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220, subd. (a).)  But to obtain a court order for the examination, Defendants must comply with all applicable statutory requirements, and they have not done so.

Specifically, Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.220, subdivision (d), requires that that an examination must be “scheduled for a date that is at least 30 days after service of the demand.”  Defendants initially complied with this requirement; on July 29 they served a demand for an examination on September 3.  But then Defendants changed the date of the examination to September 5, and they did not give 30 days advance notice of the new date: to the contrary, they served the amended demand on August 21.  (Kwong Decl., ¶ 7 & Exh. C.)  This was not timely.

Accordingly, the Court must deny the motion to compel, without prejudice, on procedural grounds.

Because the motion to compel is denied, the request for sanctions is also denied.  Sanctions are authorized only against a “party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel response and compliance with a demand for a physical examination.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.240, subd. (c).)

Conclusion

The Court DENIES as moot Defendants’ request in the motion to continue trial.

The Court DENIES without prejudice Defendants’ requests in the motion for an order compelling the physical examination of Plaintiff and for sanctions.

Moving party is to give notice. 

Case Number: 21STCV36480    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 29

Sahimi v. Scheiner
21STCV36480
Motion to Compel Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi to Respond to Form Interrogatories (Set One)
Motion to Compel Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi to Respond to Special Interrogatories (Set One)
Motion to Compel Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi to Respond to Requests for Production (Set One)

Tentative

The motions are granted.

The requests for sanctions are denied.

Background

On October 4, 2021, Soheil and Katherine Sahimi (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Leonard Scheiner and Leonard Joseph Scheiner, III (collectively “Defendants”) asserting causes of action for motor vehicle negligence, general negligence, and loss of consortium arising out of an automobile accident on October 3, 2019.

Defendants filed their answer on October 30, 2023.

On September 3, 2024, Defendants filed these three motions to compel Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi (“Plaintiff”) to respond to written discovery.  In each motion, Defendants also seek monetary sanctions.

No opposition has been filed.

Legal Standard

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd.(a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories. (Id., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel initial responses, and no meet and confer efforts are required. (See id., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.) Nor must a separate statement be filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(b)(1).)  In addition, a party who fails to provide a timely response generally waives all objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

When a party moves to compel initial responses to interrogatories, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes [the motion], unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).)

A party must respond to requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd.(a).) If a party to whom requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the demand. (Id., § 2031.300, subd. (b).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel initial responses, and no meet and confer efforts are required. (See id., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.) Nor must a separate statement be filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(b)(1).)  In addition, a party who fails to provide a timely response generally waives all objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

When a party moves to compel initial responses to requests for production, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes [the motion], unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).)

In Chapter 7 of the Civil Discovery Act, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivision (d), defines “[m]isuses of the discovery process” to include “[f]ailing to respond to or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  Where a party or attorney has engaged in misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose a monetary sanction in the amount of “the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.020, subd. (a).)

Discussion

In these three motions, Defendants seek orders compelling Plaintiff to respond to Form Interrogatories (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), and Requests for Production (Set One).

On October 27, 2023, Defendants propounded the written discovery in question on Plaintiff. (Laudencia Decls., ¶ 3 & Exhs. A.) Plaintiff failed to respond. (Id., ¶¶ 5, 8-9.)

Defendants need not show anything more.

Defendants’ motions to compel are granted.

Defendants’ requests for sanctions are denied.  In the chapters of the Civil Discovery Act governing interrogatories and requests for production, the Legislature has authorized sanctions in the context of a motion to compel initial responses “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes” the motion to compel.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c) & 2031.300, subd. (c).)  Here, however, Plaintiff has not opposed the motion.  Accordingly, the requests for sanctions are denied.

Conclusion

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ motions to compel.

 

The Court ORDERS Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi to provide written, code-compliant, verified responses, without objections, to Defendants’ Form Interrogatories (Set One), within 15 days of notice.

 

The Court ORDERS Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi to provide written, code-compliant, verified responses, without objections, to Defendants’ Special Interrogatories (Set One), within 15 days of notice.

 

The Court ORDERS Plaintiff Katherine Sahimi to provide written, code-compliant, verified responses, without objections, to Defendants’ Requests for Production (Set One), within 15 days of notice.

 

The Court DENIES the requests for sanctions.

 

Moving Party is to provide notice.

Case Number: 21STCV46993    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 29

Tripolskiy v. Clubbizz, LLC
21STCV46993
Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating, Issue, Evidence, and Monetary Sanctions

Tentative

The motion is denied as untimely.

Background

This is a personal injury case that arises out of an alleged physical attack against Plaintiff Allen Leon Tripolskiy (“Plaintiff”) that occurred at a New Year’s Eve event on December 31, 2019, at a club on Santa Monica Boulevard in Los Angeles.

Plaintiff filed his Complaint on December 27, 2021, asserting causes of action for negligence, premises liability, and negligent hiring, training, and supervision against Defendants Clubbizz.LLC (“Defendant”), Douglas C. MacLise, Alyce R. MacLise, and Does 1 through 50.

On December 2, 2022, Defendant filed an Answer.  On the same day, Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint against Roes 1 through 100.

On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff amended his Complaint to name Salient Consulting Group Inc. (“Salient”) as Doe 1.

On August 1, 2023, Salient filed a Demurrer.  On the same day, Salient filed a Cross-Complaint against Defendant and Roes 1 through 10.

On August 30, the Court overruled Salient’s Demurrer to the Complaint.

On September 5, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer to Salient’s Cross-Complaint.

On September 29, 2023, Salient filed an Answer to the Complaint.

On June 5, 2024, Defendant amended its Cross-Complaint to name Salient as Roe 1.

On May 22, 2024, the Court, at the request of Plaintiff, dismissed (with prejudice) the causes of action in the Complaint against Salient.

On June 7 and 12, 2024, the Court, at the request of Salient and Defendant, dismissed (without prejudice) the causes of action in Salient’s Cross-Complaint against Defendant and the causes of action in Defendant’s Cross-Complaint against Salient.

Before the Court at this time and set for hearing on October 10, 2024, is Plaintiff’s motion, filed September 13, 2024, for terminating, issue, evidentiary, and monetary sanctions against Defendant based on the alleged spoliation of evidence.  Defendant filed an opposition on September 30, and Plaintiff filed a reply on October 3, 2024.

(A prior motion filed by Plaintiff seeking similar relief was denied without prejudice, on September 12, 2024, on procedural grounds.)

Legal Standard

“The intentional destruction of evidence is a grave affront to the cause of justice and deserves our unqualified condemnation.” (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Super. Ct. (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, 4.)

“Destroying evidence can destroy fairness and justice, for it increases the risk of an erroneous decision on the merits of the underlying cause of action. Destroying evidence can also increase the costs of litigation as parties attempt to reconstruct the destroyed evidence or to develop other evidence, which may be less accessible, less persuasive, or both.”

(Id. at p. 8.)

Under the case law, spoliation is defined to include (among other things) destroying evidence, altering evidence, or “the failure to preserve evidence for another’s use in pending or future litigation.” (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1223.) “The duty to preserve relevant evidence is triggered when the party is objectively on notice that litigation is reasonably foreseeable, meaning litigation is probable and likely to arise from an incident or dispute and not a mere possibility.” (Victor Valley Union High School Dist. v. Super. Ct. (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1133.) The mere existence of a dispute or awareness of an underlying incident that subsequently gives rise to litigation is not, in and of itself, sufficient. (Id. at pp. 1153-1154.)

Spoliation, when it occurs, is a misuse of the discovery process. (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center, supra, 18 Cal.4th at p. 12; Williams, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th at p. 1223.) The remedies under Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 for a misuse of the discovery process include (a) a monetary sanction; (b) “an issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action”; (c) “an evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence”; and, in the most serious cases, (d) “a terminating sanction.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subds. (a)- (d).) The discovery remedies set forth in section 2023.030 “provide a substantial deterrent to acts of spoliation, and substantial protection to the spoliation victim.” (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center, supra, 18 Cal.4th at p. 17.)

As with all sanctions under the Civil Discovery Act, the purpose of sanctions for spoliation is not to punish. (Newland v. Super. Ct. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 613; Ghanooni v. Super Shuttle of Los Angeles (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 256, 262.) A discovery sanction should not create a “windfall” for a party or place a party in a better position than it would have been if the opposing party had simply complied with its obligations under the law. (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1194; see also 2 Weil & Brown, California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2024), ¶¶ 8:2214-2220.)

Terminating sanctions should be used sparingly. (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992; R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal. App. 4th 486, 496.) Nonetheless, a terminating sanction may be appropriate in the first instance, without a violation of a prior court order, “in egregious cases of intentional spoliation of evidence.” (Williams, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th at p. 1223.)

“[A] party moving for discovery sanctions based on the spoliation of evidence must make an initial prima facie showing that the responding party in fact destroyed evidence that had a substantial probability of damaging the moving party’s ability to establish an essential element of his claim or defense.” (Williams, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th at p. 1227.) Under some circumstances, this may be sufficient to establish a “prima facie showing of prejudice” from the loss of the evidence and to shift the burden to the party who has destroyed the evidence to disprove the prejudice. (Ibid.) 

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of a minute order from in Case No. 21STCV03729, Spikes v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority.  A minute order in another judicial proceeding is within the scope of Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d), and so the Court grants this request.

The Court notes, however, that a superior court ruling is not binding authority or precedent.  The Court gives the ruling no weight.

Discussion

Plaintiff requests terminating, issue, evidence, and monetary sanctions against Defendant for alleged spoliation of evidence.  Before considering the merits, the Court turns to a threshold issue raised by Defendant.

When this case was filed, the Court assigned a trial date of June 26, 2023.  In April 2023, on the stipulation of the parties, the Court continued the trial date to October 25, 2023.  On Defendant’s unopposed motion, in August 2023 the Court continued the trial date to July 17, 2024.  On Defendant’s opposed motion, in June 2024 the Court continued the trial date to October 18, 2024, but the Court did not extend any discovery deadlines (with one exception relating to Plaintiff’s medical records); by Court order, the discovery deadlines remained, and remain, in place with reference to the former trial date of July 17, 2024.

Defendant argues that this motion is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020, subdivision (a).  (Opp. at pp. 5-6.)  That statute requires that all “motions concerning discovery” must be “heard on or before the 15th day” before trial.  As applied here, the last day for the Court to hear “motions concerning discovery” (with the one exception noted above, which does not apply here) was months ago, on July 2, 2024. 

Is this a motion “concerning discovery”?  Under the plain language of section 2024.020, the answer is yes.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant committed spoliation of evidence, failing to preserve and produce in discovery relevant evidence regarding the events at issue.  The California Supreme Court has described spoliation as a misuse of the discovery process. (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center, supra, 18 Cal.4th at p. 12; accord Williams, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th at p. 1223 [California Court of Appeal].) The California Supreme Court has stated that the remedies under the Civil Discovery Act, and specifically Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, for a misuse of the discovery process (include monetary, evidence, issue, and terminating sanctions) apply to spoliation of evidence.  (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center, supra, 18 Cal.4th at p. 12.)  And the California Supreme Court has stated that the availability of remedies under the Civil Discovery Act “provide a substantial deterrent to acts of spoliation, and substantial protection to the spoliation victim.” (Id. at p. 17.) 

Moreover, Plaintiff himself states in his motion that he seeks relief under “California Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.010 et seq. [i.e., the Civil Discovery Act] and relevant case law.”  (Notice of Motion, at p. 2.)

Thus, this motion alleging that spoliation occurred is a motion “concerning discovery.” 

The policies underlying the existence of discovery deadlines supports this conclusion.  As the Court of Appeal has explained, the purpose of the discovery deadlines “is to expedite and facilitate trial preparation and to prevent delay.”  (Beverly Hosp. v. Super. Ct. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 1289, 1295.)  “Without a cutoff date, the parties could tie up each other and the trial court in discovery and discovery disputes right up to the eve of trial or beyond.”  (Ibid.)  One could reasonably conclude that this is precisely what is occurring here.  Although trial is only eight days away, Plaintiff has brought this motion and forced the parties to focus their attention on a discovery dispute that could have a huge (and, indeed, potentially dispositive) effect on the case, rather than spending their time and energy preparing for the trial.  (See Pelton-Shepherd Industries v. Delta Packaging Products (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1586-1588 [holding that trial court abused its discretion in granting motion to compel, and imposing sanctions, after the statutory deadline without first requiring moving party to file motion to reopen discovery].)

In his reply, Plaintiff argues that that the discovery cutoff does not apply to this motion, citing three cases in support of this argument.  (Reply, at pp. 5-6.)  None of these cases, however, ultimately supports Plaintiff’s position.

First, Plaintiff cites Valbona v. Superior Court (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525 (a case that Defendant also cites in its opposition, although for a different point).  In that case, the Court of Appeal affirmed a ruling by the trial court precluding a defendant from presenting evidence at trial when he had previously failed to produce the documents in discovery, even stating under oath that the documents had been stolen from him.  (Id. at p. 1545.)  In the opinion, the appellate court addressed (and rejected) the defendant’s argument that an evidence sanction was improper absent a violation of a prior court order compelling production, but the court did not address the issue of timeliness.  (Id. at pp. 1544-1546.)  Appellate decisions are not authority for propositions that were neither raised or addressed on appeal.  And, of course, there is quite a difference between imposing spoliation sanctions and the rather common situation in which a trial court orders that evidence requested, but not produced, in discovery cannot be admitted at trial.

Next, Plaintiff cites Do It Urself Moving & Storage v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 27.  There, the Court of Appeal held that there are circumstances in which non-monetary sanctions may be imposed for the misuse of the discovery process even absent a violation of a prior discovery order.  (Id. at pp. 34-36.)  This decision also does not address the timeliness issue.

Finally, Plaintiff cites Osborne v. Todd Farm Service (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 43, a case in which the Court of Appeal affirmed a trial court order dismissing plaintiffs’ case after counsel engaged in “pervasive misconduct” during trial, including repeatedly violating the court’s rulings on motions in limine precluding counsel from referencing certain evidence. (Id. at pp. 51-54.)  As the misconduct occurred during trial, there was no opportunity to address the misconduct 15 or more days before trial.  And, again, this decision says nothing about whether a spoliation motion, based on evidence occurring before trial (and in certain respects before the complaint was filed) may be heard after the statutory deadline for discovery motions.

In sum, the Legislature has determined that motions “concerning discovery” must be heard at least 15 days before trial.  This rule serves an important purposes of allowing for the orderly proceeding of a matter to trial.  Even if this Court were to question the wisdom of this rule (which the Court decidedly does not), this is a decision for the Legislature to make, and the Legislature has made it.  As Plaintiff’s motion concerns discovery, it is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020.  On that basis, the motion is denied. 

Conclusion

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion as untimely.

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.


Case Number: 22STCV13176    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 29

Torosyan v. Pour
22STCV13176
Defendants’ motion for leave to conduct medical examination of Plaintiff

Tentative

The motion is denied without prejudice.

Background

On April 20, 2022, Katerin Torosyan and Vigen Tsaturyan (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Shervin Dirin Pour, Hertz Corporation, and Does 1 through 25, asserting causes of action for general negligence and motor vehicle negligence in connection with an accident in which, Plaintiffs allege, they suffered property damages and personal injuries.

On December 6, 2022, Defendant Shervin Dirinpour (erroneously sued as Shervin Dirin Pour) (“Defendant”) filed an Answer to the complaint.

On September 10, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for leave to conduct a physical (otolaryngological) examination of Plaintiff Vigen Tsaturyan (“Plaintiff”). Plaintiffs filed an opposition on September 30, and Defendant filed a reply on October 2.

Legal Standard

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by means of a physical or mental examination of (1) a party to the action, (2) an agent of any party, or (3) a natural person in the custody or under the legal control of a party, in any action in which the mental or physical condition (including the blood group) of that party or other person is in controversy in the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.020, subd. (a).)

In a personal injury action, the defendant may demand one physical examination of plaintiff as of right, without advance leave of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220.) 

If a defendant seeks a further physical examination of plaintiff, or a mental examination, the defendant must first file a motion and “obtain leave of court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).) Such a motion must “specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination.” (Id., subd. (b).)

The court may grant such a motion “only for good cause shown.” (Id., § 2032.320, subd. (a).) A showing of good cause generally requires “that the party produce specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Vinson v. Super. Ct. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.) The moving party¿must support the motion with a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b).) A meet and confer declaration must state facts “showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)¿¿¿¿ 

“An order granting a physical or mental examination shall specify the person or persons who may perform the examination, as well as the time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (d).) “The court is to describe¿in detail¿who will conduct the examination, where and when it will be conducted, the conditions, scope and nature of the examination, and the diagnostic tests and procedures to be employed. The way to describe these ‘diagnostic tests and procedures’—fully¿and¿in detail—is to list them by name.” (Carpenter v. Super. Ct.¿(2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 260.)¿¿The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.020, subd. (a).) 

Discussion

Defendant noticed two physical examinations of Plaintiff: one by Dr. Taylor Kuhn on August 29 and a second one by Dr. Nguyen on September 4. (Nelson Decl., ¶¶ 5-6 & Exhs. B-C; Pogosyan Decl., ¶ 2.) As a threshold matter, this was improper: Defendant had no right to notice a second physical examination of Plaintiff, without first obtaining leave of the Court, and Plaintiff had no obligation to appear for that second physical examination. Any no-show fee incurred by Defendant based on Plaintiff’s failure to appear for the second, improperly noticed, examination is the responsibility of Defendant, not Plaintiff. (See Nelson Decl., ¶ 7.)

Regardless of that error, however, Defendant now moves for a court order granting leave for a second physical examination under Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.310. This motion (as opposed to a unilateral notice sent by Defendant) is the proper procedural vehicle for Defendant to seek a second physical examination of Plaintiff.

To obtain an order granting leave for a second physical examination, a moving party must comply with all of the procedural requirements of section 2032.310, including that the motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b).) The meet and confer declaration must show “a reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.) 

Defendant has not satisfied this requirement. (See Nelson Decl., ¶ 8; Pogosyan Decl., ¶¶ 3-7.) Accordingly, the motion is denied without prejudice.

Conclusion

The Court DENIES Defendant’s motion without prejudice.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.


Case Number: 22STCV28329    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 29

Arroyo v. Gonzalez
22STCV28329
Motion to Compel Defendant to Provide Further Responses to Requests for Production (Set One)

Tentative

The Court GRANTS the motions to compel Defendant to provide further responses to requests for production. 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions.

Background

On August 31, 2022, Plaintiff Angelica Arroyo (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Leonardo Moreno Gonzalez (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 50, asserting causes of action for motor vehicle and general negligence arising out of a car accident on December 10, 2021, at or near the intersection of Lankershim Boulevard and Tuxford Street in Los Angeles. 

 

On October 28, 2022, Defendant filed an answer. 

 

Previously before the Court and set for hearing on October 4, 2024, were four motions to compel further responses filed on August 14, 2024.  The Court ruled on three of those motions on October 4 and continued this one motion, regarding requests for production, to give the parties additional time to attempt to resolve the matter.

 

No opposition has been filed. 

 

No party has made any further filing since the October 4, 2024, hearing.

 

The parties participated in an Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) on September 17, 2024, but the disputes were not resolved.

 

Legal Standard

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.  (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.  (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).)

Notice of a motion to compel further responses must be given “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Id., subd. (c).)

A motion to compel further responses must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery and must be accompanied by a meet-and-confer declaration and a separate statement or, in the discretion of the Court, a “concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Id., subd. (b)(1)-(3); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345.)

“[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

In Chapter 7 of the Civil Discovery Act, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that any person “engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” A “misuse of the discovery process” includes (among other things) failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery; making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery; making an evasive response to a discovery request; disobeying a court order to provide discovery; and making or opposing, unsuccessfully, a motion to compel without substantial justification. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d)-(h).)

Discussion

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production (Set One).

There is a long procedural history with regard to these discovery requests and the pending motions, as set forth in the Minute Order dated October 4, 2024. 

Timeliness

For the reasons stated in the Minute Order dated October 4, 2024, the Court finds that the motion is timely.

Merits

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production Nos. 1 through 41.  The Court has reviewed the requests and the responses and rules that the responses are not code compliant.  (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.210-.230.)  The motion to compel further responses to the requests for production is granted.

Sanctions

The requests for sanctions are denied.  In connection with this motion to compel further responses, the Legislature has authorized sanctions against “any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)  Here, Defendant has not opposed the motion.

Conclusion

The Court GRANTS the motion to compel Defendant to provide further responses to requests for production. 

The Court ORDERS Defendant to provide further verified responses, without objection, that are code complaint to Requests for Production Nos. 1-41 within 10 days of notice.

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.
