DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00863    Hearing Date: January 4, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

CHARLES BRONSON, ET AL.

 

Plaintiffs.

 

   v.

 

AMERICAN EXPRESS COMPANY, ET AL.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

        22VECV00863         107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION AND STAY ACTION

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

[Tentative] Rulings

 

The Court CONTINUES the Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Action to February 22, 2024, 2024, at 8:30 AM, Dept. 107, Van Nuys Courthouse West.

 

Defendant American Express National Bank (erroneously sued as American Express Company) is ordered to file and serve a supplemental declaration at least ten (10) court days before the next hearing.

 

Plaintiffs Charles Bronson and Boulevard Motorcar Company are ordered to file and serve any opposition to the supplemental declaration at least five (5) court days before the next hearing.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a breach of contract case. Plaintiffs Charles Bronson (“Bronson”) and Boulevard Motorcar Company (“Boulevard”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants American Express National Bank (erroneously sued as American Express Company) (“American Express”) and Does 1 to 20, inclusive, asserting causes of action for (1) breach of oral contract, (2) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) intentional misrepresentation, (4) negligent misrepresentation, (5) promissory estoppel, and (6) unfair business practices.

 

The Complaint alleges the following, among other things. Boulevard is in the business of globally acquiring and selling antique racing automobiles. (Compl., ¶ 8.) Bronson works for Boulevard and travels extensively because of his work. (Compl., ¶ 8.) This case concerns Plaintiffs’ Business Platinum American Express credit card ending in 5006 (the “Business Credit Card). (Compl., ¶ 8.) Before COVID-19, Plaintiffs always made timely payments on their Business Credit Card. (Compl., ¶ 9.) Further, as of late 2020, they had accumulated more than one (1) million reward points from using their credit card. (Compl., ¶ 8.) Unfortunately, COVID-19 impacted their business, and they could no longer afford their credit card payments. (Compl., ¶ 11.) In or around September of 2020, Plaintiffs and the defendants entered into an oral agreement. (Compl., ¶ 41.) Pursuant to the terms of the oral agreement, the defendants agreed that (1) they would not cancel the Business Credit Card so long as Plaintiffs continued to make at least the minimum payments, (2) would reinstate Plaintiffs’ charging capabilities once Plaintiffs had paid the credit card balance down to below $9,100, and (3) would reinstate Plaintiffs’ ability to use their reward points once the balance was paid down below $9,100. (Compl., ¶ 41.) Plaintiffs did all, or substantially all, of what they were required to do under the agreement. (Compl., ¶ 42.) However, the defendants breached the contract by (1) failing to reinstate Plaintiffs’ charging capabilities, (2) canceling the Business Credit Card, and (3) causing Plaintiffs to lose over one (1) million reward points. (Compl., ¶ 44.) Due to the breach, Plaintiffs have suffered damages, including being unable to use the credit card for business purchases and losing their reward points, which carry substantial value. (Compl., ¶¶ 45, 46.)

 

On October 10, 2023, American Express filed the instant motion to compel arbitration and stay action.

 

On December 20, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their opposition, along with evidentiary objections.

 

On December 27, 2023, American Express filed its reply.

 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

In its moving papers, American Express argues that Utah law (not California law) governs the arbitration agreement at issue since the parties’ alleged contract contained a choice of law provision.

 

Even if that is true, “[i]t is well established [under California law] that while the courts generally enforce the substantive rights created by the laws of other jurisdictions, the procedural matters are governed by the law of the forum. [Citations.] [Footnote omitted.] As defined in the case law, the terms’ practice’ and ‘procedure’ include the mode of procedure by which a legal right is enforced as distinguished from the substantive law which gives or declares the right.” (World Wide Imports, Inc. v. Bartel (1983) 145 Cal.App.3d 1006, 1012.) The same is true under Utah law. (See Federated Capital Corp. v. Libby (Utah 2016) 384 P.3d 221, 226 [stating that under Utah law, “‘[m]atters of procedure in a contract action are ... governed by the law of the forum.’ A choice of law provision, in contrast, selects the substantive law that will govern a contract dispute” (italics removed)].)

 

Accordingly, the Court will apply California law to rule on evidentiary objections.

 

The Court rules on Plaintiff’s objections to the declaration of Keith Herr (“Herr”), which American Express filed on October 10, 2023, as follows.

 

  • Objections Nos. 1, 2, 3, and 6: Overruled.
  • Objections Nos. 4 and 5: Sustained on grounds of hearsay and lack of personal knowledge. Herr does not show he has personal knowledge of American Express mailing the documents attached to his declaration. In addition, as discussed below, he fails to show any American Express records he relies on to make that conclusion fall under the business record exception to the hearsay rule.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

  1. General Principles that Govern Motions to Compel Arbitration Under the California Arbitration Act and Federal Arbitration Act

 

Both the California Arbitration Act (“CAA”) and the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) “are driven by a strong public policy of enforcing arbitration agreements.” (Weiler v. Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Investment Services, Inc. (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 970, 979.) “Thus, a court generally must compel arbitration in accordance with the agreement when requested by one of the parties. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; 9 U.S.C. § 2.)” (Ibid.)

 

“The FAA applies to arbitration clauses in contracts involving interstate commerce and ‘was designed “to overrule the judiciary’s long-standing refusal to enforce agreements to arbitrate,” [citation], and to place such agreements “‘upon the same footing as other contracts.’”’ [Citation.]” (Rodriguez v. American Technologies, Inc. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1117 (“Rodriguez”).)

 

Section 2 of the FAA states that “[a] written provision in ... a contract ... to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract ... shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.” (9 U.S.C. § 2.)

 

“The court’s role under the [FAA] is … limited to determining (1) whether a valid agreement to arbitrate exists and, if it does, (2) whether the agreement encompasses the dispute at issue.” (Chiron Corp. v. Ortho Diagnostic Systems, Inc. (9th Cir. 2000) 207 F.3d 1126, 1130.) “If the response is affirmative on both counts, then the Act requires the court to enforce the arbitration agreement in accordance with its terms.” (Ibid.)

 

“Section 3 of the FAA mandates that, if suit is brought in a court ‘of the United States’ upon an issue ‘referable to arbitration,’ the court must, upon a party’s application, stay the judicial proceeding until the arbitration is completed.” (Rodriguez, supra, 136 Cal.App.4th at p. 1117.)

 

The CAA “echoing the FAA’s language and its policy favoring arbitration, likewise provides that a written arbitration agreement ‘is valid, enforceable and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.’” (Rodriguez, supra, 136 Cal.App.4th at p. 1117 [discussing Code of Civil Procedure section 1281].)

 

With regard to staying proceedings pending arbitration, the CAA states that “[i]f a court of competent jurisdiction, …, has ordered arbitration of a controversy …, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.) “If the issue which is the controversy subject to arbitration is severable, the stay may be with respect to that issue only.” (Ibid.)

 

“In determining the rights of parties to enforce an arbitration agreement within the FAA’s scope, courts apply state contract law while giving due regard to the federal policy favoring arbitration.” (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236.)

 

Under California law, “[t]he petitioner [seeking to compel arbitration] bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842.) “The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Ibid.)

 

  1. How California Courts Enforce Choice of Law Provisions in Arbitration Agreements

 

“California strongly favors enforcement of choice-of-law provisions [citation], and its courts have upheld application of other states’ internal statutes, rules and laws to arbitration contracts.” (Harris v. Bingham McCutchen LLP (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 1399, 1404.)

 

“In determining the enforceability of arm’s-length contractual choice-of-law provisions, California courts shall apply the principles set forth in Restatement section 187, which reflect a strong policy favoring enforcement of such provisions.” (Nedlloyd Lines B.V. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 459, 464–465 (“Nedlloyd”).)

 

“Briefly restated, the proper approach under Restatement section 187, subdivision (2) is for the court first to determine either: (1) whether the chosen state has a substantial relationship to the parties or their transaction, or (2) whether there is any other reasonable basis for the parties’ choice of law.” (Nedlloyd, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 466.) “If neither of these tests is met, that is the end of the inquiry, and the court need not enforce the parties’ choice of law.” (Ibid.)

 

For example, in Nedlloyd, the California Supreme Court found that the chosen forum – Hong Kong – had a substantial relationship with the parties because the plaintiff (Seawinds Limited) and another party (Red Coconut Trading Co.) were incorporated under the laws of Hong Kong and had a registered office there. (Nedlloyd, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 467.) The Supreme Court also found that since the two parties were incorporated in Hong Kong, there was a reasonable basis for the chosen forum. (Ibid. [explaining that “‘[i]f one of the parties resides in the chosen state, the parties have a reasonable basis for their choice.’ [Citation]”].)

 

If the chosen state has a substantial relationship to the parties or there is a reasonable basis for that chosen state, “the court must next determine whether the chosen state’s law is contrary to a fundamental policy of California. [Footnote omitted.] If there is no such conflict, the court shall enforce the parties’ choice of law.” (Nedlloyd, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 466 [italics in original].) For example, in Nedlloyd, the California Supreme Court “perceive[d] no fundamental policy of California requiring the application of California law to [the plaintiff’s] claims [that were] based on the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing,” since “[t]he covenant [was] not a government regulatory policy designed to restrict freedom of contract, but an implied promise inserted in an agreement to carry out the presumed intentions of contracting parties.” (Id. at p. 468.) Therefore, it was proper to apply Hong Kong law to those claims.

 

“If … there is a fundamental conflict with California law, the court must then determine whether California has a ‘materially greater interest than the chosen state in the determination of the particular issue....’ (Rest., § 187, subd. (2).)” (Nedlloyd, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 466.)

 

“If California has a materially greater interest than the chosen state, the choice of law shall not be enforced, for the obvious reason that in such circumstance we will decline to enforce a law contrary to this state’s fundamental policy.” (Nedlloyd, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 466.)

 

  1. Utah’s Arbitration Law

 

According to the Utah Supreme Court, “[w]hen parties agree to arbitration, the district court [i.e., a Utah trial court] is bound to refer the matter to arbitration.” (HITORQ, LLC v. TCC Veterinary Services, Inc. (Utah 2021) 502 P.3d 281, 285 (“HITORQ”).)

 

“But if a party challenges the appropriateness of arbitration, the Utah Uniform Arbitration Act provides that ‘the court shall proceed summarily to decide the issue and order the parties to arbitrate unless it finds that there is no enforceable agreement to arbitrate.’” (HITORQ, supra, 502 P.3d at p. 285 [footnote omitted].)

 

“For a dispute to be subject to arbitration, an agreement to arbitrate must exist that binds the party whose submission to arbitration is sought, [citation], and the dispute to be arbitrated must fall within the scope of the agreement, [citation].” (Bybee v. Abdulla (Utah 2008) 189 P.3d 40, 47.)

 

Like under California law, “[w]hether a claim falls under an arbitration clause [under Utah law] is a matter of contractual interpretation ….” (HITORQ, supra, 502 P.3d at p. 285 [footnote omitted].) “As with any contract, [Utah courts] determine what the parties have agreed upon by looking first to the plain language within the four corners of the document.” (Peterson & Simpson v. IHC Health Services, Inc. (Utah 2009) 217 P.3d 716, 720.) “When interpreting the plain language, ‘[Utah courts] look for a reading that harmonizes the provisions and avoids rendering any provision meaningless.’ [Citation.] If [the courts] find the language unambiguous, [they] interpret the contract as a matter of law. [Citation.] [They] find ambiguity only where the language of the contract ‘is reasonably capable of being understood in more than one sense.’ [Citation.]” (Ibid.)

 

“‘[I]t is a basic principle of [Utah’s] contract law that there can be no contract without the mutual assent of the parties.’ [Citations.] (‘Generally, a promise is legally enforceable where it is part of a bargain in which there is a manifestation of mutual assent to the exchange and a consideration.’ [citation]). A contract results when ‘there is a manifestation of mutual assent, by words or actions or both, which reasonably are interpretable as indicating an intention to make a bargain with certain terms or terms which reasonably may be made certain.’ [Citation.]” (Livingston v. Finco Holdings Corp. (Utah Ct. App. 2022) 513 P.3d 94, 100, cert. denied sub nom. Livingston v. Finco Holdings (Utah 2022) 525 P.3d 1263.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

American Express moves to compel Plaintiffs’ claims to arbitration and to stay action, based on (1) a cardmember agreement the defendant allegedly mailed Plaintiffs on or about July 6, 2011 (the “Governing Cardmember Agreement”), that included an arbitration provision (the “Original Arbitration Provision”) and (2) a monthly billing statement the defendant allegedly mailed to Plaintiffs on or about October 2021, that included an update to the arbitration provision (the “Updated Arbitration Provision”). (Motion, pp. 9:1-10:4; Declaration of Keith Herr, filed October 10, 2023 (“Herr Decl.”), ¶¶ 5-6; Exhibit A – a copy of the Governing Cardmember Agreement; Exhibit B – a copy of the Updated Arbitration Provision.)

 

According to the Governing Cardmember Agreement: “Utah law and federal law govern [the] Agreement and the Account. They govern without regard to internal principles of conflicts of law. [American Express is] located in Utah. [The defendant] hold[s] the Account in Utah. [The defendant] entered into [the] Agreement with [Plaintiffs] in Utah.” (Herr Decl., Exhibit A, Cardmember Agreement: Part 2 of 2, p. 5, the bottom of the page.)

 

The Original Arbitration Provision states:

           

“Any claim shall be resolved, upon the election by you or us, by arbitration pursuant to this Arbitration provision and the code of procedures of the arbitration organization to which the claim is referred in effect at the time the claim is filed (code), except to the extent the code conflicts with this Agreement. Claims shall be referred to either JAMS or the American Arbitration Association (AAA), as selected by the party electing to use arbitration.” (Herr Decl., Exhibit A, Cardmember Agreement: Part 2 of 2, p. 6, Initiation of Arbitration section.)

 

“The term claim means any claim, dispute or controversy between [Plaintiffs] and [American Express] arising from or relating to [Plaintiffs’ Account], [the Governing Cardmember Agreement], [an] Electronic Funds Transfer Services Agreement, and any other related or prior agreement that [Plaintiffs] may have had with [American Express], or the relationships resulting from any of the above agreements, except for the validity, enforceability or scope of [the] Arbitration provision.” (Herr Decl., Exhibit A, Cardmember Agreement: Part 2 of 2, p. 6, Definitions section [italics removed].)

 

The Original Arbitration Provision was “made pursuant to transactions involving interstate commerce and shall be governed by the FAA.” (Herr Decl., Exhibit A, Cardmember Agreement: Part 2 of 2, p. 6, Arbitration Procedures section.)

 

In addition, it was meant to “survive termination of [Plaintiffs’] Account; voluntary payment of the Account balance in full by [Plaintiffs]; any legal proceeding by [Plaintiffs] or [American Express] to collect a debt owed by the other; any bankruptcy by [Plaintiffs] or [American Express]; and any sale by [American Express] of [Plaintiffs’] Account ….” (Herr Decl., Exhibit A, Cardmember Agreement: Part 2 of 2, p. 6, Continuation section.)

 

The Updated Arbitration Agreement did not change the above definition of “claim,” scope of arbitration, or governing law of the arbitration agreement. (See Herr Decl., Exhibit B, p. 10, the top of the page, Important Changes to Your Account Terms section [“Terms not changed by [that] notice remain[ed] in full force and effect”].)

 

The updated arbitration provision stated that Plaintiffs could reject the arbitration provision by sending a written rejection notice to American Express by February 15, 2013. (Herr Decl., Exhibit B, p. 9, Summary of New Claims Resolution Provision (including Arbitration) Effective January 1, 2013 section, the bottom of the page.)

 

To prove that Plaintiffs received the above documents, American Express submits Herr’s declaration as evidence. Herr testifies as follows.

 

Herr is the Assistant Custodian of Records for American Express Company and its affiliates and subsidiaries, including Defendant American Express, a national bank with its main office in Utah. (Herr Decl., ¶ 1.) He has held that position for approximately ten (10) years. (Herr Decl., ¶ 1.)

 

“In connection with [his] duties as Assistant Custodian of Records, [he has] access to and [has] become generally familiar with the cardmember account records maintained by American Express, including account statements provided to cardmembers and the governing account agreements. The account records and exhibits referred to [in her declaration] were created and kept in the ordinary course of American Express’s business and were created at or near the time of the occurrence of the matters set forth in those records and/or were created based upon information transmitted by a person with knowledge of the matters set forth in those records.” (Herr Decl., ¶ 2.) “All American Express card accounts are governed by a written agreement (the ‘Cardmember Agreement’) setting forth the basic terms and conditions of the cardmember’s account. Among other things, the Cardmember Agreement provides that if the cardmember uses the account, he/she agree [sic] to the terms of the Cardmember Agreement.” (Herr Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

According to Herr, “[p]ursuant to American Express’s standard business practices, the current Cardmember Agreement is mailed to each American Express account holder, together with the cardmember’s physical American Express card, upon the opening of the account or a change to a new account.” (Herr Decl., ¶ 3.) In addition, “[m]onthly account statements are sent to account holders who carry a balance on their American Express account or are otherwise required to receive a monthly statement. Periodic updates to the Cardmember Agreement are included in the monthly statements. Cardmembers are also advised that they may access the most up-to-date version of their Cardmember Agreement online at www.americanexpress.com.” (Herr Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Herr reviewed American Express’s records concerning Plaintiffs’ “account originally ending in 1005, [but] currently ending in 5006 …, which … [the] records reflect was opened or about January 23, 2006, by American Express (the ‘Account’).” (Herr Decl., ¶ 4.) “After opening the Account, Plaintiffs proceeded to make purchases on the Account. (Herr Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

According to American Express records, Herr further testifies, Plaintiffs did not send a rejection notice of the Updated Arbitration Provision to American Express. (Herr Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

American Express argues the Court should compel Plaintiffs to arbitrate their claims because the documents attached to Herr’s declaration show that (1) Plaintiffs agreed to arbitrate their claims, (2) the FAA and Utah law govern the arbitration agreement, (3) the arbitration agreement is valid and enforceable under those laws, and (4) Plaintiffs’ claims fall within the scope of the arbitration agreement. (Motion, pp. 12:1-18:2.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs do not deny that they received the documents Herr has attached or that the FAA and Utah law apply to those documents.

 

Instead, Plaintiffs’ main argument is that American Express has not proved that it mailed Plaintiffs the documents attached to Herr’s declaration or that Plaintiffs received those documents. 

 

Plaintiffs mainly rely on a California Court of Appeal case, Chambers v. Crown Asset Management, LLC (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 583 (“Chambers”), for that argument.

 

In reply, American Express argues (among other things) that Chambers is distinguishable because, there, “the declarant provided no information regarding the regular business practices or procedures of the bank in regards to the mailing of credit card agreements, including whether they were prepared near or at the time of the alleged mailing, and thus the affidavit did not qualify under the business records exception to hearsay.” (Reply, p. 6:18-21.) “Additionally, … the declarant did not describe the specific records she relied upon to state that the bank had mailed the account agreements.” (Reply, p. 6:25-26.) Here, on the other hand, “Mr. Herr provided details regarding the American Express’s business practices with respect to creating and keeping the records in the ordinary course of its business, and testified the records of mailing were prepared near or at the time of the occurrence of the matters set forth in those records.” (Reply, p. 6:21-24.) “Mr. Herr [also] cited and attached the Cardmember Agreement and updates sent to Plaintiffs, including citing the date they were mailed and that they were sent with their physical American Express credit card and monthly billing statement.” (Reply, pp. 6:25-7:1.)

 

Contrary to American Express’s arguments, the Court does not find Herr’s declaration that much different from the affidavit at issue in Chambers with regard to the mailing issue.  

 

In Chambers, plaintiff Pamela Sheree Chambers (“Chambers”) filed a putative class action lawsuit against Crown Asset Management, LLC (“Crown”), based on alleged violations of the California Fair Debt Buying Practices Act (“CFDBPA”). (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 587.)

 

Chambers allegedly incurred debt because of a consumer credit account issued by Synchrony Bank (“Synchrony”), and Synchrony allegedly sold the debt to Crown for collection purposes. (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 587.)

 

“In her complaint, Chambers alleged that she received a written communication from a debt collector contracted by Crown that failed to comply with the CFDBPA’s notice formatting requirement. [Citation.] …. [In her lawsuit] Chambers sought statutory damages [citation] and other relief.” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 587.)

 

Crown moved to compel arbitration, arguing that Synchrony had sent Chambers a credit card account agreement containing an arbitration clause when Chambers opened her account and subsequently received a replacement card a few years later. (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at pp. 587-588.) According to Crown, “[t]he agreement allowed Chambers to reject the arbitration clause by written notice within 60 days, but Synchrony did not receive any such notice. Based on these and other facts, Crown argued that Chambers agreed to the arbitration clause, the clause covered her CFDBPA claim, and the dispute should be ordered to arbitration.” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 588.)

 

To support its contentions, including that Synchrony sent Chambers a credit account agreement with an arbitration clause, Crown submitted an affidavit from Synchrony’s employee, Jody Anderson (“Anderson”). (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 588.)

 

In her affidavit, Anderson testified, among other things, that “‘Synchrony’s records show[ed] that on or around December 13, 2012, [a credit] card and a copy of the [account agreement] were mailed to [Chambers] at the address of record on [the plaintiff’s account] …. Synchrony ha[d] no record that the card or the [agreement] were returned as underivable.’ In essentially the same language, Anderson described Synchrony’s mailing of a replacement card and another account agreement a few years later. Anderson attached copies of both account agreements to her affidavit. She did not attach any documentation regarding mailing.” (Ibid. [emphasis added].)

 

Chambers objected to portions of the affidavit on several grounds, including hearsay. (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 589.)

 

After the trial court sustained the objections and denied Crown’s motion to compel arbitration, Crown appealed, “contend[ing] [that] the [trial] court erred by excluding the Anderson affidavit because it was admissible under the secondary evidence rule (Evid. Code, §§ 1521, 1523) and the underlying … documents qualified as business records (§ 1271).” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 593 [footnote omitted].)

 

The Court of Appeal noted that while Evidence Code “‘[s]ection 1521 permits the introduction of “otherwise admissible secondary evidence” to prove the contents of a writing,” “[i]t does not excuse the proponent from complying with other rules of evidence, most notably, the hearsay rule.” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 594.)

 

Therefore, since Anderson relied on Synchrony’s records to testify that a credit card account agreement had been mailed to Chambers, those underlying records had to be admissible. (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 595.)

 

“The business records exception to the hearsay rule provides, ‘Evidence of a writing made as a record of an act, condition, or event is not made inadmissible by the hearsay rule when offered to prove the act, condition, or event if: [¶] (a) The writing was made in the regular course of a business; [¶] (b) The writing was made at or near the time of the act, condition, or event; [¶] (c) The custodian or other qualified witness testifies to its identity and the mode of its preparation; and [¶] (d) The sources of information and method and time of preparation were such as to indicate its trustworthiness.’ [Citation.]” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 594.)

 

The Court of Appeal disagreed with Crown that the underlying Synchrony’s records were admissible as business records under Evidence Code section 1271.

 

“While Anderson state[d] she regularly review[ed] and analyze[d] the records, she [did] not say anything about their preparation. Nor [did] she describe the specific ‘Synchrony records’ she relied upon to state that Chambers had been mailed the account agreement. [Therefore] [t]he trial court could reasonably [have found] that Anderson had not shown those specific records—whatever they were—were made in the regular course of Synchrony’s business.” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 595.)

 

In addition, “the trial court could reasonably [have found] that Anderson’s affidavit was insufficient to establish that the unspecified records were ‘made at or near the time of the act, condition, or event’ and ‘[t]he sources of information and method and time of preparation were such as to indicate its trustworthiness.’ (§ 1271, subds. (b), (d).) Information about the sources of information and the method and time of preparation of the records, including whether they were prepared at or near the time of alleged mailing, [was] completely absent from Anderson’s affidavit.” (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 595.)

 

Therefore, Chambers stands for the proposition that to the extent an affiant or declarant relies on business records to testify that their company mailed plaintiffs an arbitration agreement, the affiant must (1) describe with specificity the business records they are relying on to make that statement, and (2) provide information about method and time of preparation of those records, including whether they were prepared at or near the time of alleged mailing.

 

Notably, Utah law is consistent with the California Court of Appeal’s decision in Chambers. McCoy v. Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Utah (Utah 2001) 20 P.3d 901 (“McCoy”) is a Supreme Court of Utah case involving a dispute between a health insurance policyholder (Gerald McCoy (“McCoy”)) and the insurance companies (Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Utah, collectively, “Blue Cross”).

 

In McCoy, Blue Cross petitioned the state’s high court to reverse the state’s Court of Appeals’ decision, affirming the trial court’s denial of the defendants’ motion to compel arbitration. (McCoy, supra, 20 P.3d at p. 902.)

 

Like the plaintiff in Chambers, McCoy argued that Blue Cross (in moving to compel arbitration) failed to present sufficient evidence that they had mailed him an insurance policy amendment containing the alleged arbitration provision. Apparently, “[w]hen McCoy purchased the policy from Blue Cross it did not contain an arbitration provision. Blue Cross amended its policies to add an arbitration clause effective January 1, 1986.” (McCoy, supra, 20 P.3d at p. 903.) “Blue Cross [then] prepared a magnetic tape containing the names and addresses of over 30,000 affected policyholders who were to receive a copy of the policy amendment notification that contained the arbitration provision. The magnetic tape was sent to a printing company, which printed a cover letter to be mailed along with the arbitration notification. These were then forwarded to a mailing service with instructions to insert the materials into envelopes and deliver the mailings to the post office. Blue Cross did not retain a copy of the magnetic tape, nor did it obtain a verification list of affected policyholders from either the printer or the mailing service. McCoy denie[d] ever having received an actual notice of the binding arbitration amendment.” (Ibid. [emphasis added].)

 

The Supreme Court of Utah affirmed the state’s appellate court because (among other things) that “Blue Cross’s evidence failed to meet the minimum threshold of specificity [of showing that an agreement to arbitrate existed between the parties].” (McCoy, supra, 20 P.3d at p. 905.)

 

Specifically, “Blue Cross presented testimony that described the process by which a policyholder list was prepared and by which mailings were purportedly sent to persons on that list but was unable to produce a copy of the list itself. [In addition, although] [i]ts evidence described general procedures [it] did not establish any actual mailing, or even an attempt to mail, that was directed to McCoy personally. [T]herefore [the state’s high court held] that the court of appeals correctly affirmed the trial court on that ground.” (McCoy, supra, 20 P.3d at p. 905 [emphasis added].)

 

Here, Herr only testifies that he reviewed American Express records and that American Express mailed Plaintiffs the Governing Cardmember Agreement and Updated Arbitration Provision.

 

However, like the defendant in Chambers, Herr does not describe with specificity the business records (if any) he is relying on to claim that American Express mailed Plaintiffs those documents, or even provide information about the method and time of preparation of those records, including whether they were prepared at or near the time of alleged mailing. Indeed, the documents attached to his declaration do not contain Plaintiffs’ mailing address to allow an inference that they were “mailed letters.” In addition, although Herr claims that American Express sent Plaintiffs their credit card along with a copy of the original cardmember agreement when they opened their account, that original cardmember agreement is not before the Court and is not the one American Express is relying on to move for an order compelling arbitration. Finally, while Herr also testifies that cardmembers could access their most current cardmember agreement online, he does not testify that the Governing Cardmember Agreement and Updated Arbitration Provision were accessible to Plaintiffs online.

 

Notwithstanding the findings above, the defects in Herr’s declaration are not fatal to American Express’s motion to compel arbitration.

 

In several cases, the California Court of Appeal has suggested the moving party could correct the abovementioned issues. (Chambers, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at pp. 596-598 [discussing on People v. Dorsey (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 953 (“Dorsey”)]; see Dorsey, supra, 43 Cal.App.3d at p. 960 [discussing a declaration and finding the only apparent defect in the foundation required by Evidence Code section 1271 was in the failure of the declarant to testify as to the mode and time of preparation of the bank statements, but concluding that such “oversight obviously could have been remedied if appellant’s counsel had objected on that specific ground…” (emphasis added)]; see also People v. Tolmachoff (1943) 58 Cal.App.2d 815, 826 [“Where, as here, the proffered evidence is allegedly imperfect because of the lack of preliminary proof, which might or might not have been supplied by the party offering such evidence, the objection must be specific and it must point out the alleged defect. If this is not done, the objection cannot be urged on appeal” (emphasis added)]; People v. Owens (1899) 123 Cal. 482, 490 [same].)

 

Accordingly, the Court will continue the motion and ask American Express to submit a supplemental declaration correcting the deficiencies in Herr’s declaration. The Court will also allow Plaintiffs to oppose that declaration.

BACKGROUND

 

On September 1, 2022, Plaintiff Ararat Minasmaaihi (“Plaintiff”) commenced this employment action against multiple defendants, including The Better Baking Company (“Defendant”). The operative complaint is the First Amended Complaint filed on October 11, 2022. Plaintiff alleged the following six causes of action against Defendant: 1) failure to provide meal & rest breaks; 2) failure to pay overtime wages; 3) failure to furnish accurate itemized wage statements; 4) waiting time penalties in violation of Labor Code sections 201, 202, and 203; 5) failure to timely produce personnel & payroll records; and 6) unfair competition in violation of Business Profession code §17200.

 

Defendants filed its answer on March 28, 2023.       

 

On September 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed a notice of settlement.

 

On November 20, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to enforce settlement pursuant to CCP § 664.6.

 

Defendant filed its opposition on December 20, 2023.

           

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Plaintiff seeks for the Court to enforce the parties Confidential Settlement Agreement and Release (“Settlement Agreement”) pursuant to CCP § 664.6. Plaintiff argues that because Defendant failed to tender the second payment pursuant to the Settlement Agreement, Plaintiff is entitled to seek an enforcement under § 664.6. Plaintiff seeks a judgment awarding Plaintiff the outstanding balance of $45,000.00.  

 

OPPOSITION

 

            Defendant does not dispute the facts alleged in Plaintiff’s motion. Defendant asks the Court to deny the motion due to the hardships it has faced since signing the Settlement Agreement, including a plant fire, multiple inspections by the Health Department, and the resignation of its CEO.

 

REPLY

 

            No reply has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.               Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 (“CCP section 664.6”) states: “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).) For purposes of the statute, “a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following [among other individuals]: (1) ¶ The party. (2) ¶ An attorney who represents the party.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (b).)

 

“On a motion to enforce, the court must determine whether the settlement agreement is valid and binding. [Citation.] The court assesses whether the material terms of the settlement were reasonably well-defined and certain, and whether the parties expressly acknowledged that they understood and agreed to be bound by those terms. [In re Marriage of Assemi (1994) 7 Cal. 4th 896, 911.]” (Estate of Jones (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 948, 952.)

 

The court may interpret the terms and conditions of a settlement (Fiore v. Alvord (1985) 182 Cal.App.3d 561, 566), but the court may not create material terms of a settlement, as opposed to deciding what terms the parties themselves have previously agreed upon (Weddington Productions, Inc. v. Flick (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 793, 810).

 

II.                    Discussion

 

The Court finds the agreement to be valid and enforceable under CCP § 664.6. Plaintiff provides evidence that both parties consented to be bound by the Settlement Agreement, which is signed by Plaintiff and by Defendant through its CEO. (Id.) The Settlement Agreement became fully executed on August 23, 2023. (Mirzaie Decl. ¶3; Exh. 1, “Settlement Agreement”.) In it, Defendant agreed to pay Plaintiff $60,000.00 in four (4) monthly installments of $15,000.00 starting thirty days from the date of execution of the Settlement Agreement. (Settlement Agreement ¶2.)  The agreement was reached while in pending litigation.

 

Plaintiff requests that a judgment of the sum of $45,000.00, corresponding to Defendant’s remaining payment balance under the Settlement Agreement, be entered against Defendant. The relevant facts here are that Defendant requested, and was granted by Plaintiff, two extensions to pay the second installment, and offered to bundle payment of the second and third installments. Nonetheless, Plaintiff did not receive any payment after the initial installment, despite following up multiple times with defense counsel. (Id., ¶6-7; Exh. 2.) The Settlement Agreement provides remedies in the event of breach only if Plaintiff breaches the agreement. (Id., ¶12.) No provisions are made for Defendant’s breach.

 

Because the Agreement is valid and enforceable under CCP § 664.6, and Plaintiff has pled enough facts to allege breach, which is not contested by Defendant, the Court enters judgment for Plaintiff in the amount of $45,000.00, to be paid by Defendant.

                       

III.       Conclusion

 

            Motion is GRANTED and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $45,000.00.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

[Tentative] Ruling

 

            Motion is GRANTED and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $11,720.81, plus costs according to a detailed memorandum to be produced by Plaintiff.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 25, 2023, Plaintiff Benyamin Cabir (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action against multiple defendants, including Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (“Defendant”), alleging negligent repair and causes of action under the Song-Beverly Act.

 

On June 15, 2023, Defendant extended a repurchase offer to Plaintiff through Plaintiff’s counsel. Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond to this offer. (Hooper Decl. ¶5, Exh. C.)

 

On September 19, 2023, Plaintiff accepted a Section 998 settlement offer made by Defendant in the amount of $50,500.00. In addition to this amount, Defendants agreed in the offer that Plaintiff will surrender the subject vehicle and thereafter, should Plaintiff’s counsel and Defendants not agree on an attorney’s fees amount, Plaintiff will have the right to petition this Court for an award of the reasonable fees, costs, and expenses reasonably earned and incurred during this litigation, thereafter. (Saeedian Decl. ¶¶ 16-18, Hooper Decl. ¶9, Exh. D.).

 

Plaintiff filed the instant motion on October 17, 2023, seeking $17,065.50 in attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $1,131.53. Defendant opposes this motion and submits evidentiary objections to Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration. Plaintiff replied on December 27, 2023.

           

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Plaintiff seeks attorney’s fees for 36.4 hours of work by attorneys and legal staff with hourly billing rates of $695, $525, and $250, amounting to a total of $17,065.50 in attorney’s fees (Saeedian Decl. ¶ 20, see Exh. A). Plaintiff also seeks an award for costs totaling $1,131.53 (Saeedian Decl. Exh. B.) Plaintiff argues that he is entitled to an award of attorney’s fees and costs as the prevailing party in the suit, and that the requested fees were reasonably incurred.

 

OPPOSITION

 

            Defendant does not dispute that Plaintiff is the prevailing party, but requests the following adjustments:

 

  • No recovery for clerical tasks, for work not actually performed or not reasonable;
  • Reduction of Mr. Saeedian’s rate from $695 to $400/hour or less;
  • Reduction of Plaintiff’s counsel’s billing entries pursuant to Exhibit A to the Hooper Declaration;
  • Cost should not be awarded without affording Defendant the opportunity to review a complete memorandum of costs.

 

Based on the above, Defendant requests that the Court reduce Plaintiff’s requested award

to no more than $7,690.00 and costs to be determined upon a completed memorandum of costs. (Opposition, 10.)

 

REPLY

 

            In reply, Plaintiff’s counsel opposes Defendant’s characterization of some billing entries as clerical or duplicative, and therefore unrecoverable. Plaintiff maintains that time related to client intake is recoverable. Lastly, Plaintiff modifies the recovery it seeks for fees incurred as a result of this motion from an anticipated 3 hours to 3.7 actual hours spent, plus future time to attend the hearing, the exact length to  be provided by Plaintiff’s counsel. (Reply, 6:8-21.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Legal Standard

            Parties to litigation must generally bear their own attorney’s fees, unless they otherwise agree. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1021.) However, the Song-Beverly Act provides for the award of attorneys’ fees to prevailing plaintiffs as follows:

 

If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.

 

(Civ. Code § 1794, subd. (d).)

 

            “It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion.”  (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623.)  In exercising its discretion, the court should consider a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in handling the matter, the attention given, the success or failure, and the resulting judgment.  (See id.) 

 

            In determining the proper amount of fees to award, courts use the lodestar method.  The lodestar figure is calculated by multiplying the total number of reasonable hours expended by the reasonable hourly rate.  “Fundamental to its determination . . . [is] a careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney . . . in the presentation of the case.”  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48 (Serrano III).)  A reasonable hourly rate must reflect the skill and experience of the attorney.  (Id. at p. 49.)  “Prevailing parties are compensated for hours reasonably spent on fee-related issues.  A fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether.”  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635 (Serrano IV).)  The Court in Serrano IV also stated that fees associated with preparing the motion to recover attorneys’ fees are recoverable.  (See id. at p. 624.)

           

            If a court determines that some hours billed are not reasonable, it may exclude them using one of two methods: the court may either conduct an “hour-by-hour analysis” of the fee request and exclude those hours for which it would be unreasonable to compensate the prevailing party, or make an “across the board percentage cut” (Gonzalez v. City of Maywood (9th Cir. 2013) 729 F.3d 1196, 1203.)

 

II.                    Discussion

 

Hourly Rates

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s attorneys and their staff overcharged for fees in various ways, first of all by excessive hourly rates, which range from $250 to $695 per hour. (Opposition, 9:11-10:7.) There is no cause to reduce the hourly rates sought in this case. The range of rates charged in this matter by Plaintiff’s counsel is reasonable for attorneys of similar experience, in the same area, dealing with the same subject matter. (See Goglin v. BMW of North America, LLC (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 462, 473–74 [approving $625 per hour fee on lemon law action].) This conclusion is corroborated by evidence that the rates charged by the attorneys in this case are below the contingency rates reflected in the Laffey Matrix. The Laffey Matrix is a summary of rates charged by contingency attorneys, which has been cited as a reasonable source of attorney lodestar fees in multiple cases. (Saeedian Decl., ¶12.)

           

            Thus, Plaintiff has provided support for the hourly rates charged here.

 

Billable Hours

 

Defendant argues that the number of hours is excessive. The Court agrees: 37 hours is excessive when considering that this litigation commenced on April 25, 2023 and the Section 998 offer was accepted on September 19, 2023. Further, this case was not unusually complex, and no motions were filed during the pendency of the case. 

“When a ‘voluminous fee application’ is made, the court may, as it did here, ‘make across-the-board percentage cuts either in the number of hours claimed or in the final lodestar figure.’ These percentage cuts to large fee requests are, however, ‘subject to heightened scrutiny and the use of percentages, in any case, neither discharges the district court from its responsibility to set forth a ‘concise but clear’ explanation of its reasons for choosing a given percentage reduction nor from its duty to independently review the applicant's fee request.’” (Kerkeles v. City of San Jose (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 88, 102, quoting Gates v. Deukmejian (9th Cir. 1992) 987 F.2d 1392, 1399.)¿¿

 

The Court adopts that approach here and applies a one-third across-the-board cut to the number of hours claimed. The reason for this is three-fold: (1) the number of hours spent before the settlement appears to be excessive considering that no motions were filed in this matter, this case settled within six months of being filed, and this case did not involve any particularly complex issues; (2) the vast majority of Plaintiff’s counsel’s claimed hours spent after the settlement were excessive, particularly when considering that the Motion and Reply are mostly routine filings; and (3) Defendant provided specific objections to each billing entry. (See Hooper Decl., Exh. A.).

Consequently, the Court awards monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant for 25 billable hours at the requested hourly rates, which constitutes $11,720.81 in attorney’s fees.

 

Costs

 

Defendant finally objects to Plaintiff’s request for an award of costs on the grounds that Plaintiff only provided a summary of their memorandum of costs. (Opposition, 10:8-12.) The summary indicates $672.89 in “fees for electronic filing or service” and $266.99 in “other” fees. (Seedian Decl., Exh. B.) Due to the broad nature of these categories, the Court orders Plaintiff to produce a detailed memorandum of costs.

III.       Conclusion

           

            Motion is GRANTED and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $11,720.81, plus costs according to a detailed memorandum to be produced by Plaintiff.
