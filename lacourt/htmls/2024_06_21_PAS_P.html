DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00064    Hearing Date: June 21, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

1. Background Facts

This case follows the filing of an unlawful detainer 22PDU03151 arising from a landlord-tenant dispute at 770 Adobe Pl., Monterey Park, CA. On January 10, 2023, Plaintiff George Beltran (“Plaintiff”), in pro per, filed this action against Defendant Jaye Uribe (“Defendant”) for a preliminary injunction for discriminatory practices, breach of fiduciary duties, and declaratory relief. On September 15, 2023, default was entered against Defendant.  In December 2023, the Court held a Default-Prove Up Hearing and took the matter under submission. On February 26, 2024, the Court issued its ruling on submitted matter in which it denied entry of default judgment without prejudice and granted Plaintiff 30 days’ leave to file a First Amended Complaint clarifying his specific causes of action.  Specifically, the Court held that Plaintiff’s complaint did not fairly place Defendant on notice of the nature of the claims against him and the Court cannot allow a plaintiff to prove different claims or damages at the hearing than those pleaded in the complaint. (2/26/24 Minute Order.)  The Court found it was unable to grant Plaintiff the relief he sought based on the complaint as worded given it lacked the factual allegations for a breach of contract claim against Defendant and lacked causes of action relating to housing discrimination, retaliatory termination, due process violations, and claims for injunctive relief sought by Plaintiff in default judgment.

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant using the Judicial Council Form Pleading Complaint form for Personal Injury actions, PLD-PI-001.  In the FAC, Plaintiff asserts a cause of action for breach of contract and asserts that a cause of action for Intentional Tort is attached; however, the Intentional Tort Form Pleading cause of action is not attached. (FAC, caption, Item No. 10.) In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges he has suffered wage loss, loss of use of property, general damage, property damage, and has been subjected to harassment, retaliation, and a breach of a housing rental contract. (FAC, Item No. 11.)  In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges that the attached motion sets forth the three causes of action and explains the grounds for seeking damages from Defendant. (FAC, Item No. 15.) This 52-page filing constituting the FAC includes: (1) the Judicial Council Personal Injury Form Complaint; (2) the February 26, 2024 Minute Order [Exh. A1]; (3) the General Relief Housing Subsidy and Case Management Program Agreement between Plaintiff (as Tenant) and Defendant (as landlord) [Exh. A2]; (4) Plaintiff’s Utilities Application [Exh. C]; (5) Civil Code §1953 [Exh. D]; (5) Summons (undated); (6) a copy of the initial Form Pleading; (7) Civil Case Cover Sheet; and (8) a copy of Plaintiff’s Motion to File FAC which itself includes Exhibits A1, A2, C, and D as attachments  asserting actions for breach of contract and intentional tort arising from the improper termination of Plaintiff’s Los Angeles County’s General Relief Opportunities for Work (“GROW”) rental assistance benefits and $1,600 paid for water service. (FAC, Exh. A1.) At the time Plaintiff filed his FAC, he also filed a motion for leave to file the FAC in which he asserts he seeks to set forth additional causes of action for abridgment of the housing rental contract, retaliation, and harassment and discrimination, following Plaintiff’s answer to the unlawful detainer. Defendant is in default status and has filed no opposition to this motion.

2. Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.)  Ordinarily, the court will not consider the validity of the proposed amended pleading in ruling on a motion for leave since grounds for a demurrer or motion to strike are premature.  The court, however, does have discretion to deny leave to amend where a proposed amendment fails to state a valid cause of action as a matter of law and the defect cannot be cured by further amendment.  (See California Casualty General Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 274, 281, overruled on other grounds by Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390.)

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted, and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are: (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488.)

In this case, Plaintiff’s motion to amend the complaint includes a copy of the amended pleading, properly labeled as Amendment Number 1. However, Plaintiff does not state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located. Nor does Plaintiff state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located, as such, CRC Rule 3.1324(a) is not fulfilled by the filed FAC.

 Per CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration has been filed with the motion.  A separate declaration has been filed with the motion, but it does not contain any statements regarding (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend is denied. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV01895    Hearing Date: June 21, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING THE DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT WITH LEAVE TO AMEND, DENYING THE REQUEST FOR DISMISSAL WITH PREJUDICE, AND DENYING THE MOTION TO STRIKE AS MOOT

                          I.          INTRODUCTION

On August 21, 2023, Plaintiff Valarie B. Phillips (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Rusnak / Pasadena DBA (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 50 for a dispute arising from repairs to Plaintiff’s car.

On January 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant alleging: (1) breach of fiduciary duty, (2) breach of contract, (3) general negligence; (4) and three counts of fraud.

On February 9, 2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s FAC in its entirety pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 430.10 (e), (f) and concurrently filed a motion to strike Plaintiff’s demand for punitive damages and attorneys’ fees.

          On March 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the demurrer and motion to strike as a single filing.

          On June 16, 2024, Defendant filed replies to Plaintiff’s opposition to the motion to strike and demurrer, requesting dismissal with prejudice.

 

                       II.          DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

A.   Procedural Requirement

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (CCP § 430.41(a).)   

Defendant attempted to meet and confer on January 29, 2024. (Garcia Decl., ¶¶ 3-5; Exh. A.) The Court finds Defendant’s attempts to meet and confer are sufficient for purposes of the instant motion.

B.    Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action.

A demurrer admits, provisionally for purposes of testing the pleading, all material facts properly pleaded. Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1247.

A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.

The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.  A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb). Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc. §452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.

Judicial notice may be taken of a plaintiff’s unequivocal discovery responses based on his or her knowledge, even if they contradict the complaint. Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 83 (Bockrath).

“The court will take judicial notice of records such as admissions, answers to interrogatories, affidavits, and the like, when considering a demurrer, only where they contain statements of the plaintiff or his agent which are inconsistent with the allegations of the pleading before the court.” Del E. Webb at 605.

A demurrer challenges defects appearing on the face of the complaint or in judicially noticeable material but cannot be based on evidence. “Speaking demurrers” are not permitted. Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023), §7.8.

Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain.

Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.

  C.   ANALYSIS

1.     As to the Entire Complaint: Uncertainty

Defendant demurs to the entirety of Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that the pleading is uncertain. (Notice of Demurrer, pp. 3-4; C.C.P. §430.10 (f).) More particularly, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s allegation of breach of contract is uncertain as the complaint lacks facts to support that a contract existed between the parties. (Notice of Demurrer, pp. 3-4.) In opposition, Plaintiff responds to the uncertainty argument by citing that as an automotive business in California, Defendant must perform pursuant to the California Business and Professional Code. (Opp’n, p. 2.)

Demurrers for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification and apply where defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; Weil & Brown, Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2021) ¶¶7:85-7:86.) 

As alleged, the causes of actions are uncertain because the series of factual events giving rise to this complaint are ambiguously stated. Particularly, Plaintiff has not alleged facts leading to a reasonable determination of the relationship between herself and Defendant, nor how the car repairs went awry, what legal issues are implicated with these facts and what counts or claims are raised against Defendant. The deficiency in the facts leave the court with doubts as to what Plaintiff intends to allege against Defendant.

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s entire FAC is sustained with leave to amend. 

2.     First Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

A fiduciary duty is founded upon a special relationship imposed by law or when “confidence is reposed by persons in the integrity of others,” who voluntarily accept the confidence.  (Tri-Growth Centre City, Ltd. v. Silldorf, Burdman, Duignan & Eisenberg (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1139, 1150; see CACI 4100, et seq.) “‘[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.’” (City of Hope Nat’l Med. Ctr. v. Genentech (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386.)

“[B]ecause of ‘[t]he vagueness of the common law definition of the confidential relation that gives rise to a fiduciary duty, and the range of the relationships that can potentially be characterized as fiduciary,’ the ‘essential elements’ have been distilled as follows: ‘1) The vulnerability of one party to the other which 2) results in the empowerment of the stronger party by the weaker which 3) empowerment has been solicited or accepted by the stronger party and 4) prevents the weaker party from effectively protecting itself.’ (Richelle L. v. Roman Catholic Archbishop (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 257, 272 (“Richelle”).)  In short, vulnerability ‘is the necessary predicate of a confidential relation,’ and ‘the law treats [it] as “absolutely essential”’…” (Persson v. Smart Inventions (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1161.)

The breach of fiduciary duty cause of action is based on the following allegations: (1) Defendant owed a fiduciary duty of due care and loyalty to California Consumers including Plaintiff; (2) Defendant breached this duty by: (a) failing to ensure that standards for service were met (b) violating the Truth in Advertising section 43(a) of the Lanham Act; (c) submitting false reports  to the California Bureau of Automotive Repair; (d) failing to comply with regulatory requirements set forth by the California Bureau of Automotive Repair; and (d) failing to comply with Volvo diagnostic performance tests; and (3) as a result of the breaches, Plaintiff has suffered an unspecified amount of damages and requests economic damages and the removal of current officers and licensure penalties to Defendant. (FAC ¶¶ 1-4, 8-10.)  The Court notes Plaintiff also asserts the breach of fiduciary duty cause of action against DOE Defendants 1-5, the alleged officers, owners, and operators of Defendant based on the duty of due care and loyalty owed to California Consumers and “Volvo corporate shareholders.”  (FAC ¶9.)

Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to state a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty against Defendant.  Plaintiff has not alleged facts supporting the existence of a fiduciary duty between herself and Defendant.  The alleged fiduciary duty is based on a general duty of care owed by Defendant to California Consumers, but Plaintiff does not allege how this qualifies as a fiduciary relationship for purposes of the instant cause of action.  Plaintiff has not alleged facts supporting her conclusory assertion that Defendant breached any such duty.  Notably, there are no allegations as to what acts or omissions by Defendant with respect to Plaintiff amounted to a breach, when those acts occurred.  Conclusory allegations are insufficient. Finally, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged the resulting damages. Plaintiff failed to articulate a particular act or omission constituting a breach of fiduciary duty.

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty is sustained, with leave to amend.

 3.     Second Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

Plaintiff’s breach of contract is based on the following allegations: (1) the parties entered an agreement based on their behavior, under the California Bureau of Automotive Repair guidelines, when a vehicle is unaccompanied by its owner and towed for repair, the automotive repair shop must inspect and provide an estimated work order; (2) Plaintiff provided her unaccompanied vehicle to be towed; and (3) Defendant breached its duty by failing to: (a) keep Plaintiff informed of estimates and invoices, (b) only repair as authorized by Plaintiff, (c) maintain records of services performed and parts supplied, and (4) as a result, Plaintiff argues she suffered damages exceeding $30,000.00. (FAC ¶¶ 12-14.)

Defendant demurs to the FAC on the grounds that Plaintiff has not alleged whether an oral contract was created, nor has the Plaintiff included a copy of the alleged contract, or a verbatim statement of its terms. (Demurrer, pp. 6-7.)

Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to state a breach of contract cause of action against Defendant. Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged the existence of the contract, nor has Plaintiff sufficiently stated the relevant terms of the implied contract between Plaintiff and Defendant. 

Plaintiff has also not sufficiently alleged her performance or an excuse for non-performance under said contract. Plaintiff’s FAC alleges she performed her obligations under the contract by providing her unaccompanied vehicle for towing. The basis of Plaintiff’s breach claim is only that Defendant breached the contract by failing to timely inform her of repairs needed to the car, but the facts are insufficiently pled as they do not provide any notice of the expected time reserved for the repairs. 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action for breach of contract is sustained, with leave to amend.

 4.     Third Cause of Action: General Negligence

The elements of a negligence cause of action are the existence of a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) 

Defendant demurs on the third cause of action for negligence on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to allege the acts or omissions that constitute negligence. (Demurrer, p. 7; C.C.P. § 430.10(e).) The facts contained in the FAC are unclear in determining how Defendant proximately caused Plaintiff’s injuries. Therefore, Plaintiff has not sufficiently pled a cause of action for negligence. The complaint alleges in relevant part that an ordinary duty of care arose when Defendant “voluntary undertook” Plaintiff’s car for inspection of repairs. (FAC, ¶ 16.) However, Plaintiff fails to plead any facts as to how this undertaking began or how the need for repairs arose. A licensed automobile repair shop “shall not commit negligence in the maintenance of and/or repairs to a motor vehicle”; where “negligence” means deviating from an ordinary standard of care that could foreseeably result in injury or harm to a consumer, personal property, or the public or as a result of repairing the motor vehicle. (C.C.R. §335.1.)

Plaintiff argues that the ordinary duty of care was greatly deviated from when Defendant held Plaintiff’s car for over a year without attempting to obtain consent for inspection and diagnosis or providing an estimate of work. (FAC, ¶ 16.) However, Plaintiff does not allege any facts as to whether the car remained in the Defendant’s possession during that time, or what Plaintiff means by the car being “held” by Defendant. Next, Plaintiff alleges that the failure to provide a timely diagnosis “resulted in Plaintiff harm” leading to compensatory tangible and intangible and punitive damages exceeding $30,000.00. (Id.) At the crux of Plaintiff’s allegations are that Defendant never provided an estimate for the inspection and cost of repairs. However, Plaintiff also does not provide any facts to support the damages requested.

Defendant argues that as pled, the FAC leaves them unable to determine the counts or claims directed against them because Plaintiff only states that the inspection was not conducted according to “accepted practices”, without expounding on said practices and the harm this omission caused. (Reply, p. 5; Khoury v. Maly's of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; A.J. Fistes Corp. v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695.) In the FAC, Plaintiff makes general allegations about Defendant not responding in a timely manner, or conducting the inspection and repairs according to standard practices.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action for negligence is sustained, with leave to amend.

5. Fourth Cause of Action: Fraud

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

Plaintiff’s fraud cause of action is based on three counts of the following allegations: (1) Plaintiff relied on the advice of Defendant, who held themselves out as an authorized and licensed Volvo repair dealership, to have her unaccompanied vehicle towed to Defendant’s site of repair and inspection; (2) Plaintiff was notified that her car was ready for pick up on February 20, 2023 and Defendant refused to provide an itemized invoice for the repairs, later reporting to the California State Bureau of Automotive that there no repairs were done to the car on August 15, 2023, (b) Defendant concealed facts from Plaintiff by not requesting authorization to make repairs, as legally required; (c) Defendant promised to inspect the vehicle and make necessary repairs without the intent to perform; (2) Defendant knew the repairs were not complete because the inspections were complete on or about August 11, 2023, following Plaintiff’s intent to mitigate due to lack of response from Defendants; (3) Plaintiff was induced to schedule an appointment for a home inspection of the car, to which the service manager did not appear, and required her to contact Defendant to tow the car to their service location for inspection and repair; and (4) Plaintiff’s damage resulted in loss of wages, tow expenses incurred and the loss of use for the vehicle.  (FAC, ¶¶ 20-32.)

Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to state a cause of action for fraud. Plaintiff raises several grounds for misrepresentation such as intentional or negligent misrepresentation, concealment and promise without intent to perform, but does not provide sufficiently specific facts in support of each. Moreover, each element as to fraud pled by Plaintiff is not factually and specifically stated. The complaint is unclear as to the sequence of factual events to show Defendant’s possession of the car during the required repairs, the need to tow the vehicle and disclosures made to the automotive bureau.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action for fraud is sustained, with leave to amend.

 

                    III.          MOTION TO STRIKE

In light of the Court’s ruling sustaining the demurrer with leave to amend, the motion to strike is moot.

 

                    IV.          ORDER

Defendant’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to all causes of action. Plaintiff has 20 days to file an amended complaint.  Defendant’s Request for Dismissal with Prejudice is Denied. Defendant to give notice.
