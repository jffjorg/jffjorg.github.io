DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV02758    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: B

KARI ACOSTA V. HOSSEIN NOURI, ET AL.

 

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT

 

Date of Hearing:          July 3, 2024                             Trial Date:       N/A

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         21STCV02758

 

Moving Party:             Defendant The Hertz Corporation (“Moving Defendant”)

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Kari Acosta

 

RELEVANT BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from an automobile accident which occurred on January 23, 2019. On January 22, 2021, Plaintiff Kari Acosta (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint for Negligence against Defendants Hossein Nouri, Hertz Corporation, Hertz Vehicle LLC (collectively, the “Defendants”), and Does 1-50, inclusive.   

 

On July 8, 2022, the Court held the Final Status Conference (“FSC”), but none of the parties appeared at the hearing. Therefore, the Court placed the FSC off-calendar and kept the non-jury trial date of July 22, 2022.

 

On July 22, 2022, the Court held the non-jury trial, but Plaintiff did not make an appearance. Only defense counsel made a special appearance. Therefore, the Court ordered the Complaint dismissed without prejudice. The Court also vacated the Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal set for January 19, 2024.

 

On September 15, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to set aside the July 22, 2022 dismissal and the Court reinstated the action.

 

Defendants Hertz Corporation and Hertz Vehicle LLC were served with the summons, complaint, and statement of damages on December 1, 2022 via personal service. (See 12/12/22 Proofs of Service; see also 02/09/24 Proofs of Service.)

 

On February 9, 2024, default was entered against Defendants Hertz Vehicle LLC and Hertz Corporation.  

 

On February 15, 2024, Defendant The Hertz Corporation (erroneously named as both Hertz Corporation and Hertz Vehicle LLC) (“Moving Defendant”) filed a Demurrer to the Complaint.

 

On March 18, 2024, after hearing oral argument and taking the matter under submission, the Court overruled the demurrer without prejudice on the grounds that the entry of default against Moving Defendant cut off its right to appear in the action until the default is set aside. (03/18/24 Minute Order.)

 

On April 18, 2024, Moving Defendant filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Default (the “Motion”). The Motion is made on the following grounds: (1) the default should be set aside on the grounds of mistake and excusable neglect; and (2) the default should be set aside because it fails to contain a required statement of damages. The Motion was served on Plaintiff on April 19, 2024.

 

On June 5, 2024, on the Court’s own motion, the hearing on the Motion was continued to July 3, 2024 in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East. The Clerk mailed notice of the continued hearing date to Plaintiff’s counsel. (See 06/05/24 Certificate of Mailing.)

 

On June 6, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Michelle C. Kim to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz sitting in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East effective June 24, 2024.

 

On June 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served her opposition to the Motion. The opposition should have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (CCP § 1005(b).) Although filed and served late, the Court exercises its discretion and will consider the untimely filed opposition. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1300(d).)

 

As of June 28, 2024, no reply brief has been filed. The Court assumes that the lack of a reply brief is due to the untimely filing and service of Plaintiff’s opposition brief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Motion to Set Aside Default (the “Motion”) is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) Relief under CCP § 473(b) is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Ibid.) “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding was taken.” (Ibid.) “It is well settled that appellate courts have always been and are favorably disposed toward such action upon the part of the trial courts as will permit, rather than prevent, the adjudication of legal controversies upon their merits.” (Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 525.)

“The court may set aside a void default judgment at any time under section 473, subdivision (d).” (Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 749.) “In addition, the court has inherent equitable power to set aside a default judgment at any time for extrinsic fraud or mistake.” (Ibid.) “The court may grant relief under its inherent equity power if, because of the fraud of his opponent, the aggrieved party was prevented from presenting his claim or defense to the court.” (Id. at p. 750, citations omitted.) “Fraud is intrinsic and not a valid ground for setting aside a judgment when the party has been given notice of the action and has had an opportunity to present his case and to protect himself from any mistake or fraud of his adversary, but has unreasonably neglected to do so.” (Ibid.)

A party seeking equitable relief on the grounds of extrinsic fraud or mistake must show the following three elements: (1) a meritorious defense; (2) a satisfactory excuse for not presenting the defense in the first place; and (3) diligence in seeking to set aside the default judgment once discovered. (Ibid.) Extrinsic mistake “is not so much the fraud or other misconduct of one of the parties as it is the excusable neglect of the defaulting party to appear and present his claim or defense. If that neglect results in an unjust judgment, without a fair adversary hearing, the basis for equitable relief on the ground of extrinsic mistake is present.” (Mechling v. Asbestos Defendants (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1241, 1246.) “When a default judgment has been obtained, equitable relief may be given only in exceptional circumstances.” (Ibid., emphasis in original.)

“When evaluating a motion to set aside a default judgment on equitable grounds, the court must weigh the reasonableness of the conduct of the moving party in light of the extent of the prejudice to the responding party.” (Lasalle v. Vogel (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 127, 139, internal quotations omitted.) Where there is lack of prejudice to the opposing party, such factor weighs in favor of setting aside default and default judgment. (Gray v. Lawlor (1907) 151 Cal. 352, 357.)

Evidence in Support of the Motion

In support of the Motion, counsel for Moving Defendant, Christopher J. Feasel (“Feasel”), provides a declaration. Counsel declares that on January 17 and January 29, 2024, he sent emails to Plaintiff’s counsel informing him that Moving Defendant intended to demur to the Complaint and providing the basis for the demurrer. (Feasel Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. A.) Counsel also left a voicemail for Plaintiff’s counsel. (Id.) Based on the available minute entries, Mr. Feasel reasonably believed that, as of February 15, 2024, no default judgment had been entered against Moving Defendant. (Id., ¶ 3.) Additionally, he received no notice from Plaintiff’s counsel that he was filing or continuing to seek a default judgment against Moving Defenant prior to his filing of February 8, 2024, despite contacting his office three times prior to that date. (Id.) Mr. Feasel therefore filed Moving Defendant’s demurrer unaware that the default was actually granted on February 9, 2024. (Id.; Ex. B.) Mr. Feasel states that no statement of damages has ever been delivered to Defendant Hertz or contained in any pleadings. (Id., ¶ 5.)

Evidence in Opposition to the Motion

Plaintiff’s counsel, D. Hess Panah (“Panah”), provides a declaration in opposition to the Motion. The declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel states that the statement of damages was served on Moving Defendant on December 1, 2022. (Panah Decl., p. 6:21-22.) The adjuster for Moving Defendant was alerted that a default was pending against them. (Id., p. 6:25-27.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that Moving Defendant’s counsel has not stated a basis of fault, inadvertence, or mistake on their part in not providing an answer. (Id., at p. 6:23-24.)

Appropriateness of Setting Aside the Default  

Moving Defendant moves to set aside the default under CCP § 473(b) and on equitable grounds.

Initially, the Court finds that Moving Defendant has not shown a basis to set aside the default on equitable grounds given that it has not shown a meritorious defense, reasonable diligence, or a satisfactory excuse for its failure to present a defense in the first place.

The Court also rejects Moving Defendant’s contention that Plaintiff failed to serve a statement of damages. Plaintiff filed two proofs of service showing that Moving Defendant was served with the statement of damages. “The filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper.” (Floveyor Internat., Ltd. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 795.) Moving Defendant has not sufficiently rebutted the presumption that it was served with a statement of damages.

Thus, the sole basis for Moving Defendant to set aside the default is under CCP § 473(b). The Court finds that the Motion is timely under CCP § 473(b). The Court finds that counsel does not attest to why Moving Defendant did not file an answer or other responsive pleading even though Moving Defendant was served with the summons and complaint on December 1, 2022.

However, given that Mr. Feasel did reach out to Plaintiff’s counsel about the intent to demur prior to the entry of default, Mr. Feasel states that he was unaware that Plaintiff was seeing a default, and that default was entered. The Court therefore finds that Moving Defendant has made a showing of mistake and excusable neglect by failing to timely file its demurrer. The previously filed demurrer that Moving Defendant seeks to file in connection with the instant motion is attached to the declaration of Mr. Feasel.

There is a preference for the adjudication of cases on their merits pursuant to Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co., supra, 31 Cal.2d 523, 525. Plaintiff does not state any prejudice that she will incur if the default is set aside.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Motion.

CONCLUSION

 

The Motion to Set Aside Default filed by Defendant The Hertz Corporation is GRANTED.  Moving Defendant is ordered to file and serve its proposed demurrer within 10 days of this Order.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.


Case Number: 21STCV47619    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: B

BAHMAN KHODAYARI V. NOBERTO MARTINEZ CRUZ, ET AL.

 

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:          July 3, 2024                             Trial Date:       September 13, 2024

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         21STCV47619

 

Moving Party:             Defendants Noberto Martinez Cruz and Javier Martinez Mendez

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Bahman Khodayari   

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from an automobile accident which occurred on August 22, 2019. On March 4, 2021, Plaintiff Bahman Khodayari (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Noberto Martinez Cruz (“Cruz”) and Javier Martinez Mendez (collectively, the “Defendants”), and Does 1 to 10, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Motor Vehicle; and (2) General Negligence.

 

On March 11, 2022, Defendants filed and served a joint Answer to the Complaint.

 

On March 21, 2022, Defendants filed and served a joint Answer to the Complaint, which appears to be a duplicate of the joint Answer filed 3/11/2.

 

On August 7, 2023, Defendant Cruz filed and served a motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to set one of Defendant Cruz’s form interrogatories and request for monetary sanctions; a motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to set one of Defendant Cruz’s special interrogatories and request for monetary sanctions; and a motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to set one of Defendant Cruz’s demand for production of documents and request for monetary sanctions (collectively, the “Motions”).

 

On March 13, 2024, the Court granted the Motions and noted that the Motions were all unopposed. (03/13/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Motions were granted without a hearing as there was no appearance by Plaintiff and counsel for Defendant Cruz submitted on the Court’s tentative ruling. (03/13/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) The Court ordered Plaintiff to serve verified responses to Defendant Cruz’s (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for inspection of documents, set one, without objections, within 20 days. (03/13/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Court also awarded Defendant Cruz sanctions in the sum of $184.95 and ordered such sanctions to be paid by Plaintiff to Defendant Cruz within 20 days. (03/13/24 Minute Order at p. 3.) Defendant Cruz was ordered to give notice of the Court’s order. (03/13/24 Minute Order at p. 3.)

 

On April 4, 2024, Defendant Cruz filed and served notice of the Court’s March 13, 2024 order. The proof of service attached thereto shows that Plaintiff was served with the Court’s March 13, 2024 order via mail on April 4, 2024.

 

On April 29, 2024, Defendants filed and served the instant Motion for Terminating Sanctions Against Plaintiff (the “Motion”). The Motion is made on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to comply with the Court’s March 13, 2024 order. Defendants move for an order entering a judgment of dismissal in favor of Defendants for Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the Court’s March 13, 2024 order.

 

On April 30, 2024, after hearing, the Court granted Defendants’ ex parte application to continue trial and the Court continued non-jury trial from May 13, 2024 to September 13, 2024. (04/30/24 Minute Order.)

 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served an opposition to the Motion.

 

On May 31, 2024, Defendants filed and served their reply brief.

 

On June 5, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Michelle C. Kim to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East for all purposes effective June 24, 2024.

 

On June 5, 2024, the Court, on its own motion, continued the hearing on the Motion from June 5, 2024 to July 3, 2024 in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East. (06/05/24 Minute Order.)

 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a response to Defendants’ reply brief. The Court did not authorize Plaintiff to file a response to the reply brief. Plaintiff filed a sur-reply, which the Court did not permit and is generally not permitted as it deprives Defendants of the opportunity to respond. (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537-38.) The Court therefore strikes Plaintiff’s response to the reply and will not consider such improper filing pursuant to CCP § 436.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Court DENIES WITHOUT PREJUDICE Defendants’ Motion for Terminating Sanctions Against Plaintiff (the “Motion”).

 

DISCUSSION

A misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).) A misuse of the discovery process also includes disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).) A court may impose terminating sanctions, issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, or monetary sanctions against a party engaging in misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.) Where an issue sanction is imposed, “designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).) An issue sanction may also involve “any party engaging in misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).) An evidence sanction involves “an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (c).)  

A terminating sanction may be imposed by the court by one of the following orders: (1) an order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process; (2) an order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) an order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; or (4) an order rending judgment by default against that party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(1)-(4).)  

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)  “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Ibid.) “[C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  (Ibid.) Where discovery violations are “willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid.)  A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions. (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) When discovery sanctions are requested, there must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful. (Ibid.)   

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.” (Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702.) A trial court’s order to impose terminating sanctions will be reversed only if it “was arbitrary, capricious, or whimsical.” (Ibid.) Trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders. (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.) Terminating sanctions are warranted when a party’s lack of compliance with the discovery process has caused the opposing party prejudice. (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc., supra, 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 989.) 

 

 

Evidence in Support of the Motion

In support of the Motion, Defendants’ counsel, Sharon Pak, Esq. (“Pak”), provides a declaration. Ms. Pak declares the following: on May 18, 2022, Defendants served Plaintiff with Set One of Demand for Inspection of Documents, Form Interrogatories, and Special Interrogatories. (Pak Decl., ¶ 4.) Numerous extensions were granted but no responses were provided. (Id., ¶ 4.) On August 7, 2023, Defendants filed a Notice of Motion to Compel Responses to Discovery. (Id., ¶ 5.) The Court granted Defendants’ motions on March 13, 2024, and Plaintiff was ordered to provide verified responses, without objections, to the discovery requests within 20 days. (Id., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff was served with notice of the Court’s March 13, 2024 ruling. (Id., ¶ 7; Ex. A.) As of the date of the Motion, Defendants have yet to receive responses to the discovery as ordered by the Court. (Id., ¶ 9.)

In connection with the reply brief, Ms. Pak declares that Plaintiff provided unverified and wholly deficient responses on May 23, 2024. (Pak Reply Decl., ¶ 7; Ex. C.) Trial is currently scheduled for September 13, 2024, and Plaintiff’s continuous failure to provide verified discovery responses is prejudicing Defendants’ ability to properly evaluate and defend the case. (Id., ¶ 8.)

Evidence in Opposition to the Motion

Plaintiff declares that he did not receive notice of the motions to compel discovery or the Court’s March 13, 2024 order. (Khodayari Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.) Plaintiff states that upon becoming aware of the Court’ s order, he took immediate steps to address the outstanding discovery obligations and has hired a paralegal to expedite the preparation and submission of the required documents and responses. (Id., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff states that he has submitted the responses to Defendants on May 23, 2024, and that his failure to comply was not due to willful disobedience or disregard for the Court’s authority. (Id., ¶¶ 9-11; Exs. 1-2.)

Appropriateness of Imposing Terminating Sanctions

Initially, the Court rejects Plaintiff’s contention in the opposition that Plaintiff was not notified of the Court’s March 13, 2024 hearing date and minute order. (Khodayari Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.) Defendants filed a proof of service showing that: (1) Plaintiff was served with the motions to compel; and (2) Plaintiff was served with the Court’s March 13, 2024 minute order.

 The Court has reviewed Plaintiff’s purported discovery responses and finds that they are all unverified. (Pak Reply Decl., Ex. C.) The Court reminds Plaintiff that “[u]nsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.” (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.) While Plaintiff has chosen to represent himself in pro per, Plaintiff is still held to the same standard as an attorney. (Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543.) As such, the Court deems Plaintiff’s unverified responses to be no response at all, and the Court finds that Plaintiff has failed to comply with the Court’s March 13, 2024 order.

Plaintiff has disobeyed one order of this Court pertaining to discovery. The Court, however, must take an incremental approach to sanctions and therefore the Court does not find that terminating sanctions are appropriate at this time. The Court cannot impose sanctions beyond that which are required to protect Defendants’ interests. (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) While issue or evidentiary sanctions may have been appropriate, Defendants did not request such sanctions.

The Court therefore DENIES Defendants’ request for terminating sanctions.

The Court, however, ORDERS Plaintiff to comply with the Court’s March 13, 2024 order forthwith and in any event no later than 20 days from the date of notice of this order.

CONCLUSION

 

The Motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

Plaintiff is ORDERED to comply with the Court’s March 13, 2024 order forthwith and in any event no later than 20 days from the date of notice of this order.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
