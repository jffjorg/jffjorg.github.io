DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV35489    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 32

PLEASE NOTE:   Parties are encouraged to meet and confer concerning this tentative ruling to determine if a resolution may be reached.  If the parties are unable to reach a resolution and a party intends to submit on this tentative ruling, the party must send an email to the Court at sscdept32@lacourt.org indicating that party’s intention to submit.  The email shall include the case number, date and time of the hearing, counsel’s contact information (if applicable), and the identity of the party submitting on this tentative ruling.  If the Court does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on this tentative ruling and there are no appearances at the hearing, the Court may place the motion off calendar or adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court.  If all parties do not submit on this tentative ruling, they should arrange to appear in-person or remotely.  Further, after the Court has posted/issued a tentative ruling, the Court has the inherent authority to prohibit the withdrawal of the subject motion and adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court. 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

32

HEARING DATE:

September 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV35489

MOTIONS: 

Motion to Amend, Nunc Pro Tunc, the Court’s June 25, 2024 Order

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiffs Karen Flores and Michael Perry

OPPOSING PARTY:

Unopposed

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 25, 2024, the Court granted a petition to approve a minors’ compromise filed by Karen Flores on behalf of Claimant M.P.

 

Plaintiffs Karen Flores and Michael Perry (“Plaintiffs”) now move to amend, nunc pro tunc, the Court’s June 25, 2024 order granting the Petition to Approve Minor M.P.’s compromise. No opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(d), “[t]he court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.”

 

DISCUSSION

 

Here, Plaintiffs seek to correct item 8b2 on the Order Approving Compromise (MC-351), granted on June 25, 2024, which states the balance of the settlement will be “paid to Pacific Life Insurance Company and assigned to Pacific Life and Annuity Services, Inc. to fund annuity contracts and disbursed as follows: $2,500.00  Lump Sum Payment on 9/22/2035; $5,000.00  Lump Sum Payment on 9/22/2038;  and $13,281.08 Lump Sum Payment on 9/22/2042, for a total disbursement of $20,281.08.”

 

Instead, Plaintiffs contend that $13,281.08 should be $13,190.28, which means the total disbursement should be $20,690.28. This information was provided in the supporting petition, in the terms of the settlement agreement. (Petition, Hayden Decl., Exh. A.)

 

Therefore, the motion to correct the amounts within 8b2 is granted. Item 8b2 is amended to state: “paid to Pacific Life Insurance Company and assigned to Pacific Life and Annuity Services, Inc. to fund annuity contracts and disbursed as follows: $2,500.00 Lump Sum Payment on 9/22/2035; $5,000.00 Lump Sum Payment on 9/22/2038; and $13,190.28 Lump Sum Payment on 9/22/2042, for a total disbursement of $20,690.28.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, the Motion to Amend, Nunc Pro Tunc, the Court’s June 25, 2024 Order is GRANTED. The Court signs the proposed order attached as Exhibit 1.

 

Plaintiffs shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service of such.

BACKGROUND

 

Defendant The Vons Companies, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Trenna Jones (“Plaintiff”) for failure to comply with the Court’s May 28, 2024 discovery order compelling her deposition.

 

The hearing for this motion was originally scheduled for August 16, 2024 and originally unopposed. At the hearing, and pursuant to the request of Plaintiff, the motion was continued to September 20, 2024. Opposition papers were due September 9, 2024 and reply papers were due September 16, 2024. (Min. Order, 8/16/24.)

 

The Court also ordered Plaintiff to file a Notice of Change of Address immediately. (Min. Order, 8/16/24.) The Court notes that no such Notice appears in the docket. Additionally, while it appears Plaintiff is now represented by counsel Elina Shakhbazyan, no Substitution of Attorney has been filed.  

 

Plaintiff opposes and Defendant replies.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“To the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method or any other provision of this title, the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose…sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process.” (Code Civ. Proc. section 2023.030.) The court may impose a terminating sanction for misuse of the discovery process by any of the following: “(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process; (2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; (4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(d).)  Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery, or disobeying a court order to provide discovery, constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d), (g).)

 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390, quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal. App. 4th 1225, 1246.)  

 

“Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal. App. 4th at p. 390 [citation omitted].)   

 

“Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 citing Lang, supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1244-1246 [discussing cases]; see, e.g., Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1617-1622 [terminating sanctions imposed (by striking the defendant’s Answer and subsequently granting default judgment) after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery]; Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 481, 491, disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCutchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, n. 4 [terminating sanctions imposed against the plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes].) 

 

“If that party or party-affiliated deponent then fails to obey an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against that party deponent or against the party with whom the deponent is affiliated. In lieu of, or in addition to, this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against that deponent or against the party with whom that party deponent is affiliated, and in favor of any party who, in person or by attorney, attended in the expectation that the deponent's testimony would be taken pursuant to that order.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(h).)

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

The Court grants Defendant’s objections, except for objections to Exhibits A through C and to paragraphs 1 and 17. Defendant has not established that strict compliance with the hearsay rules is required here.

 

DISCUSSION

 

On May 22, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s former counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

 

On May 28, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. The deposition was first noticed in September 2023. (Min. Order, 5/28/24.) The Court found that Plaintiff had failed to appear at two noticed depositions and ordered Plaintiff to appear for a deposition within 20 days. (Id.) Plaintiff was also ordered to pay $1,372.60 in monetary sanctions.

 

On June 6, 2024, Defendant mailed notice of the order to Plaintiff’s address listed on the Order to Relieved Counsel. On that same day, Defendant also mailed a Fourth Amended Notice of Taking Plaintiff’s deposition, set for June 19, 2024. (Macksoud Decl. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff did not appear for the deposition. (Id. ¶ 8.)

 

An opposition has been filed on Plaintiff’s behalf, despite there being no Substitution of Attorney filed in this case. Despite the assertions that Plaintiff did not appear at previous depositions because former counsel did not inform her of the correct information and failed to send notices to her proper address, none of this is supported by a declaration from Plaintiff. In light of the evidentiary rulings above, Plaintiff provides no admissible evidence demonstrating a reason for the failure to abide by the Court’s discovery order.

 

In reply, Defendant argues that the Court should ignore the opposition since it was filed by counsel who has not filed a Substitution of Attorney. However, “the act of making a court appearance on behalf of a party creates a presumption that the attorney is authorized to do so, and hence is strongly presumptive of an attorney-client relationship.” (Streit v. Covington & Crowe (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 441, 446.) Defendant provides no authority that the Court has no discretion to consider papers submitted by a purported counsel, especially given the presumption of the attorney-client relationship.

 

Still, Defendant contends in reply that Plaintiff admitted over the telephone prior to the August 16, 2024 hearing that she received documents that Defendant sent via express mail, but had not opened them because they were not sent to the correct address. (Lewis Decl. ¶ 6.) Defendant also asserts it emailed Plaintiff the documents on August 1, 2024 to the same address that Plaintiff provided at the August 16, 2024 hearing. (Id. ¶ 7.)

 

Nevertheless, given the question of whether Plaintiff accessed the notices that Defendant sent (since she claimed at the prior hearing that she did not and they were not supposed to be sent to that address), and the fact new counsel has appeared on Plaintiff’s behalf purporting to fully prosecute this case (Shakhbazyan Decl. ¶ 17), the Court cannot conclude that Plaintiff’s actions were willful to warrant terminating sanctions at this time. The facts indicated by Defendant suggest that lesser sanctions may be able to induce compliance.[1]

 

To Court also denies Defendant’s request for a continuance since it was asserted for the first time in reply. The parties are ordered to meet and confer and attempt to reach a stipulation.

 

The Court further notes that it instructed Plaintiff to file any motion to seal as necessary to protect her address. (Min. Order, 8/16/24.) No motion has been filed. In light of Exhibit A, Plaintiff must file a motion and proposed order promptly.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Therefore, Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED. Purported counsel for Plaintiff, Elina Shakhbazyan, shall complete and file a Substitution of Attorney immediately.

Motion to Submit Tardy Supplemental Expert Designation

MOVING PARTY:

Defendants Long Beach Unified School District, Shayon Stamper, Virginia Divers, Jason Heesch, and Saul Hernandez 

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff Kysean Ferreira¿¿ 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

The parties in this case exchanged expert witness designations on March 20, 2024. At the time, trial was scheduled for May 9, 2024. (Min. Order, 7/12/23.) 

 

On April 24, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff Kysean Ferreira’s (“Plaintiff”) ex parte application to continue trial and reopen discovery. The Court observed that no motion to reopen discovery was filed. However, in light of the then-pending motion for judgment on the pleadings, the Court continued trial to May 23, 2024. All discovery remained closed and the pre-trial motion cut off remained in effect. (Min. Order, 4/24/24.)  

 

On May 3, 2024, the Court granted in part, Plaintiff’s ex parte application to continue trial and reopen discovery, by continuing trial from May 23, 2024 to August 23, 2024. The order specified that “all discovery and pre-trial motion cut off dates shall be associated with the previous trial date unless otherwise stipulated by the parties or otherwise ordered by the Court.” (Min. Order, 5/3/24.)  

 

On July 30, 2024, the Court granted in part Plaintiff’s motion to reopen discovery for the limited purpose of conducting the depositions of Jason Heesch and Renny Chu. (Min. Order, 7/30/24.) On September 18, 2024, the Court denied Defendant’s motion for reconsideration.

 

At the final status conference, given the motions on calendar for September, the Court, on its own, continued trial to October 14, 2024. All discovery remained closed and the pre-trial motion cut off remained in effect unless stipulated by all counsels or by further orders of the Court. (Min. Order, 8/8/24.)

 

Defendants Long Beach Unified School District, Shayon Stamper, Virginia Divers, Jason Heesch, and Saul Hernandez (“Defendants”) now move for leave to submit a tardy supplemental expert witness designation of a special education expert, Mary Schillinger, to respond to opinions by Plaintiff’s special education expert, Marian Stephens. The motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2034.250, 2034.710, and 2034.720. Plaintiff opposes and Defendants reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

At the appropriate date pursuant to the expert witness demand, the parties must simultaneously exchange information which contains a list of the name and address of any person whose expert opinion is expected to be offered at trial, and, among other things, a “brief narrative statement of the general substance of the testimony that the expert is expected to give.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.260, subds. (b), (c).) “[T]he very purpose of the expert witness discovery statute is to give fair notice of what an expert will say at trial. This allows the parties to assess whether to take the expert's deposition, to fully explore the relevant subject area at any such deposition, and to select an expert who can respond with a competing opinion on that subject area.” (Bonds v. Roy (1999) 20 Cal.4th 140, 146-47.)

 

Upon reviewing an opposing party’s expert witness list, a party may supplement their expert list within 20 days after the exchange. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.280.) Supplementing expert witnesses under section 2034.280 is done as a matter of right, unlike augmenting an expert witness list or making a tardy submission, which require leave of court. (Du-All Safety, LLC v. Superior Court (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 485, 497-98.)

 

On motion of any party who has engaged in a timely exchange of expert witness information, the court may grant leave to (1) augment that party’s expert witness list and declaration by adding the name and address of any expert witness whom that party has subsequently retained; and/or (2) amend that party’s expert witness declaration with respect to the general substance of the testimony that an expert previously designated is expected to give.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.610, subd. (a).)¿ This motion shall be made a sufficient time in advance to permit the deposition of any expert to whom the motion relates to be taken before the discovery cut-off, unless exceptional circumstances exist.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.610, subd. (b).)¿ The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.610, subd. (c).) ¿  

 

The court may grant leave to augment or amend an expert witness list only if all of the following conditions are satisfied:¿¿  

 

 (a) The court has taken into account the extent to which the opposing party has relied on the list of expert witnesses.¿  

 

(b) The court has determined that any party opposing the motion will not be prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.¿  

 

(c) The court has determined either of the following:¿  

 

(1) The moving party would not in the exercise of reasonable diligence have determined to call that expert witness or have decided to offer the different or additional testimony of that expert witness.¿  

¿ 

(2) The moving party failed to determine to call that expert witness, or to offer the different or additional testimony of that expert witness as a result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, and the moving party has done both of the following:¿  

¿ 

(A) Sought leave to augment or amend promptly after deciding to call the expert witness or to offer the different or additional testimony.¿  

¿ 

(B) Promptly thereafter served a copy of the proposed expert witness information concerning the expert or the testimony described in Section 2034.260 on all other parties who have appeared in the action.¿  

¿  

(d) Leave to augment or amend is conditioned on the moving party making the expert available immediately for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410), and on any other terms as may be just, including, but not limited to, leave to any party opposing the motion to designate additional expert witnesses or to elicit additional opinions from those previously designated, a continuance of the trial for a reasonable period of time, and the awarding of costs and litigation expenses to any party opposing the motion.¿  

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.620.)¿ 

 

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

The Declaration of Amy A. Evenstad, Defendants’ counsel, states the following: “Defendant met and conferred over the issues in this motion but Plaintiff refused to agree to deem the designation timely. Defendant has offered to make Mary Schillinger available for deposition.” (Evenstad Decl. ¶ 8.)  

 

DISCUSSION

 

This case stems from an alleged physical altercation with Plaintiff and a school security guard. Plaintiff had an Individualized Education Plan (“IEP”). Defendants contend that Plaintiff stipulated that his mental condition would not be at issue, only his physical injuries. Despite this, Defendants contend that on July 29, 2024, Plaintiff provided a report from its special education expert Marian Stephens, who opined that Plaintiff’s IEP was violated in the 2019-2020 school year during the incident, and in the months after the incident. (Evenstad Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. D.) Defendants argue this claim was not alleged previously. Defendants have filed a motion in limine to exclude this opinion, in case this motion is denied.

 

As an initial matter, the Notice of Motion seeks leave to submit a tardy supplemental expert under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2034.250, 2034.710, and 2034.720. First, section 2034.250 pertains to protective orders sought after a party is served with a demand to exchange expert witnesses. While Defendants argue this allows the Court to make an order that the date of exchange of supplemental experts may be at a later date, they provide no authority in support. (Motion, 4.) Additionally, Defendants point to sections 2034.710 and 2034.720 which apply when a party has failed to submit expert witnesses on the date specified in the demand. These sections do not appear to apply here because Defendants admit that the parties exchanged expert witnesses on March 20, 2024. (Motion, 4; Evenstad Decl. ¶ 5.) However, Defendants also contend that Plaintiff did not produce any expert reports. (Evenstad Decl. ¶ 5.)

 

The Court notes that sections 2034.610 and 2034.620 are appropriate here, since Defendants seek to augment their expert witness list after the parties already designated witnesses. However, even if brought under those sections, the motion would be denied for the reasons below.

 

Defendants do not attach the materials that Plaintiff produced on the date of the exchange and fail to show Plaintiff’s brief narrative statement of the general substance of the testimony that Marian Stephens was expected to give. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.260, subd. (c)(2).) In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that its expert declaration for Marian Stephens contained the following statement: “Dr. Stephens will provide opinions as to . . . the failure of defendants to comply with their duties under California and Federal law, the failure to comply with plaintiff’s Individualized Education Plan, the failure to comply with special education standards and education standards . . . .” (Scott Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1.)

 

As a result, given that Plaintiff designated a special education expert witness and produced the declaration above, Defendants fail to show that they would not, in the exercise of reasonable diligence, have decided to also retain a special education witness. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.620, subd. (c)(1).) Similarly, given the declaration statement of Stephen’s anticipated testimony, there are insufficient facts that the failure to designate a special education witness was the result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.620, subd. (c)(2).)[1] Therefore, Plaintiff’s expert designation list and supporting declaration undermines Defendants’ argument that the July 29, 2024 opinion report was a “surprise”. In light of the above, Defendants also fail to show whether failure to supplement an expert was excusable neglect.

 

In reply, Defendants do not set forth facts that it did not have possession of Plaintiff’s expert witness designation and the statement of Stephen’s testimony on the date of the exchange.

 

Therefore, the motion to submit the tardy supplemental expert designation is denied.[2]

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Submit Tardy Supplemental Expert Designation is DENIED.

MOTIONS: 

(1) Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Request for Admissions, Set One Admitted

(2) Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

(3) Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Andrew MacCaskey

OPPOSING PARTY:

Unopposed

 

BACKGROUND

 

 Plaintiff Andrew MacCaskey (“Plaintiff”) now moves for an order to deem admitted Requests for Admission, Set One and to compel verified responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production, Set One propounded on Defendant Irma Rodriguez (“Defendant”). Plaintiff seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Requests for Admission

 

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)¿ The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants that party relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (a)(1)-(2).)¿ The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)¿ 

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) 

 

Interrogatories

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)  

 

If a motion to compel responses is filed, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a). 

 

Requests for Production 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.300, if a party fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand. (Code Civ. Pro § 2031.300 (b).) The party who fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection waives any objection to the demand unless the court finds that the party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance or party’s failure was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 (a)(1)- (2).)  

 

Courts shall impose a monetary sanction against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection unless the party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).) 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Here, Plaintiff asserts that he served Requests for Admission, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production, Set One on Defendant on May 15, 2024. (Baker Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Plaintiff granted an extension, and the responses were due August 12, 2024. (Id. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff then demanded responses by August 15, 2024. Since then, Defendant has not responded and has not filed an opposition to this motion. (Id. ¶ 5.) Therefore, the motion to deem admitted and motions to compel are granted.   

 

Plaintiff requests the following monetary sanctions against Defendant and her counsel of record, representing an hourly rate of $750 and the $60.00 filing fee: $3,060 (Request for Admissions), $4,560 (Compel Interrogatories), and $4,560 (Compel Request for Production). The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Defendant has failed to respond. However, the amount requested is excessive due to the type of motions at issue, the lack of opposition, and the fact counsel can appear at the hearing remotely. Additionally, Plaintiff’s counsel sets forth no other evidence justifying the $750 hourly rate. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040 [a request for a sanction shall be “accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought”].) Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the amount of $1,380 (1 hour of attorney time at $400/hour to file and appear at the hearing, plus the $60 filing fee, for each of the three motions).

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to deem admitted Request for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production, Set One are GRANTED. Defendant Irma Rodriguez shall serve verified responses without objections within 15 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant and her attorney of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $1,380.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order.

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Ernesto Figueroa filed this action arising out of a dog bite on May 19, 2018 against Defendants Robert Harang, Robin Harang, Susana Bernard, and Does 1-20.  

 

On June 14, 2021, Cross Complainant Susannah Bernard aka Susana Bernard (“Cross Complainant”) filed a First Amended Verified Cross Complaint (FACC) against Cross Defendants Ronald Harang, Robin Harang, Shawdae Harang, Assad Ali, Yvette Geater, and Roes 1-20.

 

On February 6, 2024, at an OSC re: dismissal for failure to enter default judgments on the complaint and cross-complaint, the Court ordered the amended cross complaint dismissed without prejudice. (Min. Order, 2/6/24.)

 

On June 5, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) setting aside and vacating the dismissal on February 6, 2024. The hearing for that motion was originally scheduled for July 29, 2024. Cross-Complainant was present at that hearing, and the Court continued the motion to August 14, 2024. (Min. Order, 7/29/24.)

 

At the August 14, 2024 hearing, there were no appearances and the motion was placed off calendar. (Min. Order, 8/14/24.)

 

On August 19, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed the instant motion to set aside the February 6, 2024 dismissal under Civil Procedure section 473(b). No opposition has been filed.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b), the Court may relieve a party from a dismissal taken against him through his mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  This application must be filed no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought, and must contain an affidavit of fault demonstrating the moving party’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.

 

A mistake is a basis for relief under section 473 when by reason of the mistake a party failed to make a timely response.  Surprise occurs when a party is unexpectedly placed in a position to his injury without any negligence of his own. Excusable neglect is a basis for relief when the party has shown some reasonable excuse for the default.  (Credit Managers Association of California v. National Independent Business Alliance (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 1166, 1173; Davis v. Thayer (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 892, 905.)  Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, the moving party bears the burden of demonstrating an excusable ground, such as fraud or mistake, justifying a court’s vacating a judgment.  (Basinger v. Roger & Wells (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 16, 23–24.)   

 

Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Id.) However mandatory relief is only available when a party fails to oppose a dismissal motion (“which are procedurally equivalent to a default”). (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 620.) The mandatory relief provision does not apply to dismissals for “failure to prosecute [citations omitted], dismissals for failure to serve a complaint within three years [citations omitted], dismissals based on running of the statute of limitations [citations omitted], and voluntary dismissals entered pursuant to settlement [citations omitted]”, as well as “discretionary dismissals based on the failure to file an amended complaint after a demurrer has been sustained with leave to amend, at least where, as here, the dismissal was entered after a hearing on noticed motions which required the court to evaluate the reasons for delay in determining how to exercise its discretion.” (Id.)   

 

DISCUSSION

 

            This motion is not timely because it was not filed within six months after the cross complaint was dismissed. Cross-Complainant cites to Save Our Forest & Ranchlands v. County of San Diego (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1757, 1770, for the assertion the six-month period under section 473(b) “does not commence until the opposing party takes some action based on the missed time limit (e.g., motion to dismiss) or a court order is made as a result thereof.” (Motion, 6.) However, that rule applies to relief from failure to meet procedural time limits. (See Save Our Forest & Ranchlands, supra, 50 Cal.App.4th at 1769-70.) Here, Cross-Complainant is seeking relief from a dismissal, which occurred on a specific date.

 

            To the extent Cross-Complainant’s counsel argues that failure to attend the August 14, 2024 hearing was based on mistake, surprise, or neglect, that cannot be a basis for seeking relief from the six-month deadline. (See Arambula v. Union Carbide Corp. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 333, 345 [“There is no indication in the language of section 473, subdivision (b), that the Legislature intended to authorize a court to relieve a party from the failure to timely apply for relief. . . . [The language of the discretionary and mandatory provisions of section 473(b)] does not suggest that an exception should exist based on mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect or an attorney's affidavit of fault. Moreover, it appears unlikely that the Legislature would have intended to allow the use of section 473, subdivision (b), to excuse a party's noncompliance with that very statute without expressly stating so.”].)

             

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Cross-Complainant’s motion to set aside the dismissal.
