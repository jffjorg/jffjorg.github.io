If you desire to submit on the tentative ruling, you may do so by e-mailing Dept. 26 at the Spring Street Courthouse until the morning of the motion hearing.

The e-mail address is SSCdept26@lacourt.org

The heading on your e-mail should contain the case name, number, hearing date, and that you submit. The message should indicate your name, contact information, and the party you represent. Please note, the above e-mail address is to inform the court of your submission on the tentative ruling. All other inquiries will not receive a response.

If there are no appearances by either side and no submission on the Court's tentative ruling, the matter will be placed OFF CALENDAR. 

Due to overcrowding concerns of COVID-19, all parties shall make every effort to schedule a remote appearance via LACourtConnect (https://my.lacourt.org/laccwelcome) for their next hearing. The parties shall register with LACourtConnect at least 2 hours prior to their scheduled hearing time.

Case Number: 24STCP01197    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: 26

Diya Finance, LLC v. Malbros Ready Mix Concrete, LLC 

TENTATIVE RULING:

Petitioner Diya Finance, LLC’s Petition to Release Property from Mechanic’s Lien is GRANTED.  

ANALYSIS: 

Petitioner Diya Finance, LLC (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition for Release of Mechanic’s Lien against Respondent Malbros Ready Mix Concrete, LLC (“Respondent”) on April 15, 2024. The Petition initially came for hearing on September 12, 2024 and was continued to provide for proper notice to Respondent. (Minute Order, 09/12/24.) Petitioner filed a proof of service by mail on December 2, 2024. No response to the Petition has been filed to date. 

Discussion

Petitioner moves to have the mechanic’s lien recorded against their property by Respondent released. Under Civil Code section 8484 the petition for release order must be verified by the petitioner and allege the following:

(a) The date of recordation of the claim of lien. A certified copy of the claim of lien shall be attached to the petition.

(b) The county in which the claim of lien is recorded.

(c) The book and page or series number of the place in the official records where the claim of lien is recorded.

(d) The legal description of the property subject to the claim of lien.

(e) Whether an extension of credit has been granted under Section 8460, if so to what date, and that the time for commencement of an action to enforce the lien has expired.

(f) That the owner has given the claimant notice under Section 8482 demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien and that the claimant is unable or unwilling to do so or cannot with reasonable diligence be found.

(g) Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.

(h) Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien.

(Civil Code, § 8484.) The Petition contains all the required information. The Court previously noted that service of the Petition did not comply with Civil Code section 8486, subdivision (b) regarding the manner of service, nor with the requirements for service on a corporation under Code of Civil Procedure section 416.10. (Minute Order, 09/12/24.) The Court ordered Petitioner to file a code-compliant proof of service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing. (Minute Order, 11/25/24.) Petitioner filed a new proof of service of the Petition and notice of the new hearing date on December 2, 2024, showing service on that date by certified mail with return receipt requested and in compliance with Corporations Code section 418.10. (Proof of Service, filed 12/02/24.)

Conclusion

Therefore, Petitioner Diya Finance, LLC’s Petition to Release Property from Mechanic’s Lien is GRANTED. 

Moving party to give notice.

Gonzalez v. LA Auto Center, LLC et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Plaintiff Giovanni Gonzalez’s Motion to Compel Arbitration, for Court to Pick Arbitration Forum, Request for Stay, and Requests for an Order of Costs and Fees Related to the Arbitration is DENIED.                                                                                                                                

ANALYSIS: 

On April 4, 2024, Plaintiff Giovanni Gonzalez (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for automobile subrogation against Defendant LA Auto Center, LLC (“Defendant LA Auto”) and Jet Insurance Company (“Defendant Jet”). Following Defendant LA Auto’s failure to file a responsive pleading, the Court entered its default on June 26, 2024. Defendant LA Auto filed a Motion to Vacate Default, which the Court granted on September 12, 2024. (Minute Order, 09/12/24.) Defendant LA Auto filed its answer on September 20, 2024. 

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration, for Court to Pick Arbitration Forum, Request for Stay, and Requests for an Order of Costs and Fees Related to the Arbitration on October 11, 2024. Defendant LA Auto filed an opposition on December 5, 2024 and Plaintiff replied on December 12, 2024. 

Discussion 

Plaintiff moves to compel arbitration against Defendant LA Auto on the grounds that it materially breached the arbitration agreement by failing to pay fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.97. (Notice, p. 2:1-6.) This statute provides: 

(a)(1) In an employment or consumer arbitration that requires, either expressly or through application of state or federal law or the rules of the arbitration provider, the drafting party to pay certain fees and costs before the arbitration can proceed, if the fees or costs to initiate an arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days after the due date the drafting party is in material breach of the arbitration agreement, is in default of the arbitration, and waives its right to compel arbitration under Section 1281.2.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.97, subd. (a)(1).) Following such breach, an employee or consumer may “[c]ompel arbitration in which the drafting party shall pay reasonable attorney's fees and costs related to the arbitration.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.97, subd. (b)(2).) Plaintiff presents evidence, that Defendant LA Auto materially breached the arbitration agreement by failing to pay the invoice sent by AAA. Specifically, Plaintiff sent Defendant LA Auto a demand for arbitration on May 26, 2023. (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶5 and Exh. 3.) Plaintiff then filed for arbitration with the AAA on September 11, 2023. (Id. at ¶7.) AAA accepted arbitration and sent an invoice to the parties with payment due by December 6, 2023. (Id. at ¶8 and Exh. 4, p. 2.) When Defendant LA Auto did not make payment, AAA closed the arbitration file on December 13, 2023. (Id. at ¶9 and Exh. 5.)

Defendant LA Auto first argues that the Motion should be denied because Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.97 is preempted by the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”). In support of its preemption argument, Defendant LA Auto points to Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc. (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 222, in which the Court of Appeals ruled that where an arbitration agreement plainly indicates it is governed by the FAA, the trial court’s order based on section 1281.97, a state law statute, must be reversed. (Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc. (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 222, 241-244 [“unless the parties have expressly selected California’s arbitration provisions to apply to their agreement, the FAA preempts the portion of section 1281.97 that dictates findings of material breach and waiver as a matter of law.”].) Although Plaintiff’s reply attempts to distinguish Hernandez from this action by citing Court of Appeals cases that have come to a different result on the preemption issue, the Court finds it is the cases on which Plaintiff relies that are distinguishable from this action.

For example, in Keeton v. Tesla, Inc., while the parties’ arbitration agreement did not expressly incorporate the California Arbitration Act (“CAA”), the Court of Appeals held that those state law procedural provisions apply in California by default. (Keeton v. Tesla, Inc. (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 26, 38.) Plaintiff does not point to any case where the parties expressly selected the FAA in their arbitration agreement but section 1281.97 was held to be applicable for a finding of breach by the drafting party. The other case cited by Plaintiff also involved an arbitration agreement that expressly stated the arbitrator was to look “to the “California Arbitration Act ... to conduct the arbitration and any pre-arbitration activities.” (See Gallo v. Wood Ranch USA, Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 621, 630.)

The arbitration agreement in this action expressly states that “[a]ny arbitration under this Arbitration Provision shall be governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. §§ 1 et seq.).” (Motion, Sadr Decl., Exh. 1, p. 5.) While the procedural provisions of CAA apply in California courts by default, the first question that must be decided is what statutory scheme applies to the parties’ arbitration agreement. (See Hernandez, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at 238.) In arguing that section 1281.97 applies, Plaintiff does not answer this initial question nor address it in reply despite the issue being raised in the opposition. (Opp., p. 9:26-10:10.)

Furthermore, even if the Court were to find that section 1281.97 is applicable to the facts of this case with respect to just the request for attorney’s fees and costs, which it does not for the reasons explained above, Plaintiff has also not shown that its authority regarding the existence of a valid arbitration agreement under section 1281.2 of the CAA is applicable.

Conclusion 

Plaintiff Giovanni Gonzalez’s Motion to Compel Arbitration, for Court to Pick Arbitration Forum, Request for Stay, and Requests for an Order of Costs and Fees Related to the Arbitration is DENIED.

Case Number: 24STLC02579    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: 26

Orewyler v. Wang et. al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendants Yichuan Wang and Liang Guan’s Motion to Vacate Default is GRANTED. THE DEFAULT ENTERED ON AUGUST 20, 2024 IS HEREBY VACATED. DEFENDANTS ARE TO FILE AND SERVE THEIR PROPOSED ANSWER TO THE COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. ALSO, WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER, DEFENSE COUNSEL OF RECORD IS TO PAY ATTORNEY’S FEES OF $4,945.00 TO PLAINTIFFS’ COUNSEL.

ANALYSIS:

On April 9, 2024, Plaintiffs Brandon Orewyler and Chante Eliaszadeh (“Plaintiffs”) filed the instant action against Defendants Yichuan Wang and Liang Guan (“Defendants”). Following Defendants’ failure to file a responsive pleading, the Court entered their default on August 20, 2024.

Defendants filed their first Motion to Vacate Default on September 4, 2024. Following full briefing and oral argument, the Court denied the first Motion without prejudice on November 18, 2024. (Minute Order, 11/18/24.) Defendants filed the instant Motion to Vacate Default on the same date. Plaintiffs filed an opposition on December 6, 2024.

Discussion

Defendants move to vacate the entry of default pursuant to the mandatory relief provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). Under this statute an application for relief must be made no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of attorney fault attesting to their mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) The motion must also be accompanied by a copy of the moving defendant’s proposed pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) This can be corrected if the defendant submits a proposed responsive pleading by the hearing date. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403.)

The instant Motion was timely filed three months after entry of default and is supported by an attorney declaration of fault in which defense counsel states that following months of unsuccessful settlement negotiations, they failed to file a responsive pleading. (Motion, Cole Decl., ¶¶6-13.) Defense counsel contends that the failure to file a responsive pleading was entirely their own and not the fault of Defendants. (Id. at ¶¶13-14.) In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that this declaration by defense counsel contradicts their declaration in the first Motion to Vacate. Plaintiffs argue that where a defense attorney’s contradictory declarations demonstrate a lack of credibility, the request for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) must be denied.

The Court considers the cases on which Plaintiffs rely. In Cisneros v. Vueve (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 906, the Court of Appeals held that “the ‘unless’ clause of the statute is not a credibility testing device, it is a causation testing device. Specifically, the statute mandates relief “‘unless the court finds that the default ... was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence’ etc. (Italics added.)” (Cisneros v. Vueve (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 906, 912.) In Todd v. Thrifty Corp. (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 986, the Court of Appeals ruled the trial court abused its discretion by granting a motion for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) when the affidavits revealed that the attorney was trying to cover for their clients’ inexcusable conduct. (Todd v. Thrifty Corp. (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 986, 992.) Finally, in Behm v. Clear View Techs. (2015) 241 Cal. App. 4th 1, the Court of Appeals held that it defers to the trial court’s determination of credibility when evaluating whether the default was actually caused by the attorney. (Behm v. Clear View Techs. (2015) 241 Cal. App. 4th 1, 15-16.) Based on this authority, the Court must determine whether the entry of default was actually caused by defense counsel and not Defendants, and in doing so, may evaluate defense counsel’s credibility.

The Court finds defense counsel’s declarations credible and not contradictory. Both declarations explain that after defense counsel was hired, the attorneys met and conferred regarding the date to file

Defendants’ proposed Motion to Strike but could not reach a resolution. (Motion, Cole Decl., ¶¶6-9; Second Motion, Cole Decl., ¶¶6-10.) The only difference is that in the declaration in support of the Second Motion, defense counsel expressly states that the failure to file a responsive pleading was their fault and not that of their clients. (Id. at ¶¶11-13.) This comports with the shift in legal basis for relief from the discretionary provision to the mandatory provision under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), and is not grounds to deny the Second Motion. There is no indication that the lack of responsive pleading was caused by Defendants themselves.

Plaintiffs also argue that relief is not permitted under the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) where, as here, the failure to respond was part of Defendants’ intentional strategic decision. In Jerry’s Shell v. Equilon Enterprises, LLC (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1058, the Court of Appeals upheld denial of the motion for relief from default because “appellants’ counsel’s obvious plan was to claim attorney fault and revive the claims through a section 473(b) motion for relief.” (Jerry’s Shell v. Equilon Enterprises, LLC (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1073.) Another intentional strategy that warranted denial of a motion for relief was a defendant’s “strategy of delaying and throwing darts at others, rather than taking responsibility for her own actions.” (Del Junco v. Hufnagel (2007) 150 Cal. App. 4th 789, 800.) The evidence here does not bear out that Defendants engaged in a strategy to delay like that in Del Junco. In Del Junco, the conduct of both the defendant and their counsel was clearly to delay. (Id. at 794-797.) Similarly, the Court of Appeals in Jerry’s Shell described the extensive conduct that warranted denial of the request for relief:

Appellants’ attorneys regularly failed to respond to discovery when it was due, without informing the other side or seeking an extension. Any and all attempts by opposing counsel to discuss or “meet and confer” were similarly ignored. This gave opposing counsel no choice but to go to the trouble and expense of preparing and filing a motion to compel. But even a formal motion did not engender a response. And while appellants' counsel did not oppose such motions, neither did they concede. Thus, the court was drawn into the conflict, wasting judicial resources and—in the case of an appointed referee—leading to further expense. Finally, when an order issued compelling responses, appellants' counsel defied that too, leading to a second go round of each of these wearying steps.

(Jerry’s Shell v. Equilon Enterprises, LLC (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1073.) The proceedings in this action which amounted to a failure to file a responsive pleading began with defense counsel’s request for an extension of the time to respond at the end of May 2024. (Second Motion, Cole Decl., ¶6.) When that extension expired, defense counsel asked for another extension in order to meet and confer about a possible motion to strike. (Id. at ¶7.) Plaintiffs provided the second extension but no agreement was reached regarding the motion to strike; once the second extension expired, default was entered two months later. (Id. at ¶¶7-12.) The entry of default in this case, therefore, followed two short extensions of the time to respond and is nothing compared to the significant obstructionist tactics used by the attorneys in Jerry’s Shell. As the defense’s failure to file a responsive pleading in this action does not rise to the level of an intentional tactical decision in the above-cited cases, this is also not grounds to deny the Second Motion to Vacate.

Finally, Plaintiffs request that if relief from the entry of default is granted, they be awarded attorney’s fees under the moving statute. “The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) Plaintiffs’ counsel requests attorney’s fees of $4,945.00 based on 8.1 hours of attorney time billed at $575.00. (Opp., Orewyler Decl., ¶¶7-16.) The Court finds the requested fees based on a reasonable hourly rate and amount of time spent on this matter with respect to the entry of default following preparation of the request for default judgment and two opposed motions to vacate entry of default.

Conclusion

Defendants Yichuan Wang and Liang Guan’s Motion to Vacate Default is GRANTED. THE DEFAULT ENTERED ON AUGUST 20, 2024 IS HEREBY VACATED. DEFENDANTS ARE TO FILE AND SERVE THEIR PROPOSED ANSWER TO THE COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. ALSO WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER, DEFENSE COUNSEL OF RECORD IS TO PAY ATTORNEY’S FEES OF $4,945.00 TO PLAINTIFFS’ COUNSEL.

Moving party to give notice.
