DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV13351    Hearing Date: May 30, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 5-30-24 a/f 10-7-24

Case # 21STCV13351

Trial Date: 7-29-24 c/f 6-24-24.

 

 

STAY

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, San Marino Unified School District

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, John Doe

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Stay Action

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff John Doe was a student Huntington Middle School and San Marino High School with defendant San Marino Unified School District (SMUSD). Defendant Howard Cheung was a teacher in SMUSD. Cheung was also an employee for defendant River Way Ranch Camp, an overnight camping facility in Sanger, California.

 

Beginning in sixth grade in 2000-2001, a friendly relationship between Plaintiff and Cheung began, which placed the two alone together in various location. The continued contact provided Cheung opportunities to sexually molest Plaintiff, including at River Way Ranch Camp. Plaintiff alleges SUMSD faculty and staff saw the inappropriate time together and failed to investigate or report.

 

On April 7, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint for Sexual Abuse of a Minor, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Sexual Harassment, Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention of Unfit Employee, Breach of Mandatory Duty: Failure to Report Suspected Child Abuse, Negligent Supervision of a Minor, Negligent Failure to Warn, Train or Educate, and Negligence. On June 18, 2021, the court overruled the demurrer of SMUSD. SMUSD answered and filed a cross-complaint.

 

On June 28, 2022, the action was transferred from Department 32 (Personal Injury hub) to Department 68. On June 28, 2022, Plaintiff dismissed River Way Ranch Camp with Prejudice. On October 26, 2022, Plaintiff dismissed Howard Cheung without Prejudice. On May 17, 2023, the court granted the motion of Western Camps, Inc. dba River Way Ranch Camp for determination of a good faith settlement with Plaintiff.

 

On March 27, 2024, the court granted the motion for summary adjudication on the fourth and sixth causes of action for Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention of Unfit Employee Negligent Supervision of a Minor. The court denied the motion for summary judgment on the basis of its denial of the motion for summary adjudication on the fifth and seventh causes of action for Breach of Mandatory Duty, Failure to Report Suspected Child Abuse, and Negligent Failure to Warn, Train or Educate.

 

On April 16, 2024, the court entered the parties’ stipulation to continue the trial date.

 

RULING: Granted.

Requests for Judicial Notice: Granted.

·         The court takes judicial notice of the existence of the outside trial court orders, but not any content including factual findings. (Kilroy v. State of California (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 140, 147-148; Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1565.) The orders also constitute unpublished material, and therefore not citable for any reference. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(a); Rittiman v. Public Utilities Com. (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 1018, 1043 (footnote 18).)

·         The court also takes judicial notice of the petitions for writ of mandate, but again, only for notice of the existence of the petition, when considered in context of the First and Second Appellate District Dockets.

·         The court takes judicial notice of the correspondence from the Second Appellate District for purposes of establishing notice of the request for further briefing.

 

San Marino School District moves for a stay of the entire action pending resolution of two writs taken up to the First and Second Appellate District on the enforceability of the revival of the statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1, with an exemption to the government claim filing requirement for childhood sexual assault claims brought under the revived statute of limitations. (Gov. Code, § 905, subd. (m).) The exemption was codified following the passage of California Assembly Bill 218 (AB 218). The issues presented to the court challenges said exemption as a violation of the “gift prohibition section” of the California Constitution Article XVI, section 6 of the California Constitution. The argument challenging the constitutionality of AB 218 relies on the express link between a claim for money or damage against a public entity, and a required government claim. Allowing the recovery of monetary damages without a preceding claim constitutes a prohibited “gift” to any recovering victim, and therefore an unconstitutional provision.

 

San Marino School District represents that any potential recovery against it would require approval of both the West San Gabriel Liability and Property Joint Powers Authority and District Board of Education. San Marino School District maintains that no payment can and will be approved pending resolution of the appeals, thereby supporting the request for the stay.

 

Plaintiff in opposition contends the motion for an “indefinite” stay due to an unrelated case proceeding in outside jurisdiction unfairly prejudices Plaintiff. Plaintiff cites to over 60 trial court orders as a showing of the number of courts rejecting the argument submitted by Defendant.

 

San Marino School District in reply reiterates the pending appeals and benefits towards judicial efficiency. San Marino School District disputes the contention of an overly lengthy stay. San Marino School District challenges any argument regarding expected outcomes of the appeal(s).

 

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.” (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489; see OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 141 [“[A] court ordinarily has inherent power, in its discretion, to stay proceedings when such a stay will accommodate the ends of justice”]; Cooper v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 55 Cal.2d 291, 301; Bailey v. Fosca Oil Co. (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 813, 817.)

 

The court acknowledges the pending appeals on West Contra Costa USD v. Superior Court (First Appellate District Case No. A169314) and filed appeal on Roe #2 v. Superior Court (Second Appellate District Case No. B334707). The First Appellate District case is briefed, with oral argument to be scheduled. The Second Appellate District requested supplemental briefing on certain issues regarding the nature of the public purpose in waiving the claim exemption—the award of a gift to a private individual (a transfer of a personal right or property without any basis of consideration) caused by the relinquishment of an otherwise invalid claim. Plaintiff takes specific issue with the Second Appellate District appeal in that the court “has not even accepted review of the Writ of Petition,” and only requested “informal briefing.”

 

It remains undisputed that the writ of mandamus filed before the Second Appellate District from the Santa Barbara Superior Court remains to be determined for acceptance. The supplemental reply brief was due on May 17, 2024.

 

The court disagrees with the characterization of the requested stay as an “indefinite” period to the extent such a categorization implies an open ended period of time. The court itself has considered the exact issue from a different school district defendant, and appreciates the broader impacts of the multitude of cases currently under challenge caused by the vast revival of claims.

While a time frame on the Second Appellate District case regarding the setting of oral argument or rejection remains unknown, in considering the stay, the court cannot disregard the volume of challenges brought by school districts throughout the state. A number of courts rejected the gift clause argument, but a smaller proportion considered the constitutional implications differently. The implications of the revival are presumably at least part of the consideration of the First Appellate District acceptance of the appeal, and Second Appellate District consideration.

 

The court considers the issue of sufficient public importance that a published opinion appears more likely. While Plaintiff seeks to minimize the consideration of any decision from the First Appellate District, the court considers the potential impacts of a published opinion.

 

“Decisions of every division of the District Courts of Appeal are binding upon all the justice and municipal courts and upon all the superior courts of this state, and this is so whether or not the superior court is acting as a trial or appellate court. Courts exercising inferior jurisdiction must accept the law declared by courts of superior jurisdiction. It is not their function to attempt to overrule decisions of a higher court.” (Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court of Santa Clara County (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 455.) “Where California intermediate appellate court cases conflict, any trial court may choose the decision it finds most persuasive.” (Sears v. Morrison (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 577, 587.) “As a practical matter, a superior court ordinarily will follow an appellate opinion emanating from its own district even though it is not bound to do so. Superior courts in other appellate districts may pick and choose between conflicting lines of authority.” (McCallum v. McCallum (1987) 190 Cal.App.3d 308, 315 (footnote 4).)

 

Dual published opinions also remains a valid consideration. Said opinions may present a potential a range of granular interpretations. Furthermore, as anticipated by Plaintiff, in the case of potentially conflicting opinions, the court expects a writ of certiorari to the California Supreme Court.


“Grant of review by the Supreme Court of a decision by the Court of Appeal does not affect the appellate court's certification of the opinion for full or partial publication under rule 8.1105(b) or rule 8.1110, but any such Court of Appeal opinion, whether officially published in hard copy or electronically, must be accompanied by a prominent notation advising that review by the Supreme Court has been granted. [¶] (2) The Supreme Court may order that an opinion certified for publication is not to be published or that an opinion not certified is to be published. The Supreme Court may also order depublication of part of an opinion at any time after granting review.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1105(e)(1)(B), (e)(2).) “Pending review and filing of the Supreme Court's opinion, unless otherwise ordered by the Supreme Court under (3), a published opinion of a Court of Appeal in the matter has no binding or precedential effect, and may be cited for potentially persuasive value only. Any citation to the Court of Appeal opinion must also note the grant of review and any subsequent action by the Supreme Court.” (Cal. Rules of Court, 8.1115(e)(1).)

 

The court in no way discounts the impacts of a potential stay on the action to Plaintiff. Nevertheless, the subject action only appears before the court because of the revival of claims. A determination of the constitutionality of the revival potentially insulates an entire group of child sexual abuse victims or potentially significantly compromises any number of cases. Such potentially broad impacts renders a stay of the action warranted pending appeal. Allowing the individual case to proceed constitutes an inefficient and improper use of judicial resources. 

 

The court in its discretion therefore stays the action.

 

The court in no way intends an indefinite, open ended stay. The court will seek updates on the pending appeal(s), and therefore set an OSC re: Appeal(s) for 90-120 days from the hearing date. Depending on the state of the appeals and potential California Supreme Court review, the court will either further continue the stay or set new hearing dates for further consideration of the judgment on the pleadings.

 

The currently set trial for July 29, 2024, is vacated. The court takes the motion for judgment on the pleadings off-calendar.

Dept. 68

Case #23STC13988

Trial Date: Not Set

 

DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, ASB Watermarke Owner, LLC, A Delaware Corporation

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Carlos Valdez, pro per

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint

·         1st Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation

·         2nd Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Mental Distress

·         3rd Cause of Action: Source of Income Discrimination

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On September 10, 2022, plaintiff Carlos Valdez moved into an apartment building owned and/or managed by defendants ASB Watermarke Owner, LLC, A Delaware Corporation, with the assistance of a Section 8 voucher. Plaintiff provided a security deposit and a September 10, 2022, was scheduled. Plaintiff maintains Defendant improperly represented Plaintiff’s qualification for the rental via acceptance of the Section 8 certificate, and/or a promise to submit the required paperwork with the relevant compliance agency(ies). According to Plaintiff, both parties maintain the other was responsible for establishing compliance and participation into the Section 8 program. Apparently, no payments were made, and Plaintiff was subsequently residing in an unaffordable unit, thereby leading to an unlawful detainer action (23STUD02401).

 

On June 16, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed complaint for Negligent Misrepresentation. On July 21, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed a first amended complaint for Negligent Misrepresentation, Intentional Infliction of Mental Distress, and Source of Income Discrimination. On January 22, 2024, the court granted the unopposed motion to quash service brought by ASB Watermarke Owner, LLC, A Delaware Corporation.

 

RULING: Sustained with Leave to Amend.

Defendant ASB Watermarke Owner, LLC, A Delaware Corporation (ASB), submits a demurrer to the first amended complaint. Defendant moves on grounds of failure to state facts. Plaintiff in opposition contends the operative complaint articulates all causes of action. ASB in reply reiterates the lack of supporting facts in both the operative complaint, and sufficient legal argument in opposition. ASB also challenges the introduction of extrinsic information in the opposition.

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law . . . .” ’ ” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 [“[U]nder our liberal pleading rules, where the complaint contains substantive factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled or plaintiff given leave to amend.]

 

Plaintiff appears in pro per. The court is not required to extend special treatment to such parties. (Gamet v. Blanchard (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284–1285.) The court reviews the case under the same standards as a represented party, including equal review under the law with an understanding regarding certain ignorance of legal procedure expected of a licensed attorney. (Petrosyan v. Prince Corp. (2013) 223 Cal.App.4th 587, 594.)

 

Plaintiff lists three causes of action in the caption for Negligent Misrepresentation, Intentional Infliction of Mental Distress, and Source of Income Discrimination. The court quotes the entirety of the body of the complaint following the introductory paragraph identifying the parties.

 

“Plaintiff moved into a unit at ASB Watermarke Tower on September 10, 2022, under a Section 8 voucher thanks to The. Los Angeles Department of Mental Health (LADMH) after being homeless for over a year. The application was submitted on August 4th 2022 over one month prior to the move in date which was September 10th, 2022.

 

“Fast forward to September 27th, 2022, Plaintiff contacted HACLA (Housing Authority) to follow up on the status on the security deposit of his reimbursement. HACLA confirmed that they had no record of his move-in, deposit or the required documents that are required to approve payments to a building.

 

“Immediately frantic and stressed-out Plaintiff confronted Defendant, they just made him feel that this was his responsibility and through intimidation given his lack of knowledge Plaintiff believed them. Plaintiff started reaching out to HACLA, MCC, APLA and even Mayor Bass's representative after - reaching out for help.

 

“Defendant negligent misrepresentation made Plaintiff rely that everything was in order. 'Defendant confirmed to Plaintiff that all documents had been submitted to housing via email on Aug 21, 2022 (20 days prior to move in). Defendant brought Plaintiff in on August 28th to sign the lease by both Plaintiff and Defendants. They request Plaintiff move-in funds and accepted them.

 

“But the most reckless misrepresentation was giving the Plaintiff keys to an apartment that they knew he could not afford without the voucher. With little to no regards to the damages Plaintiff would face due to their reckless business practices. Note that in two occasion Defendant confirm that all documents would be process in two different email and on different dates.

 

“Defendant has resulted to “Intentional Infliction of Mental Distress” as of July 19th 20123 by requesting Defendants own Unlawful Detainer trial against Plaintiff (23STUD02401) to be moved to September from original date set back on June 8 2023. On Trial Day July 19, 2023.

“Despite Defendant emailing Plaintiff all morning aski @lamnff to take a settlement or Defendant would “marke ready” (sic) once they arrived to court. [Defendant] knows that the new date will result in added debt which will affect [Plaintiff]. Mainly because of last week all outstanding rent charges of over $29,249 were fully paid. –[Defendant] is retaliating and is not acting in good faith.

 

“Defendant is using Plaintiffs mental health history to pressure him to move out since Defendant now has all of Plaintiff evidence provided for the Unlawful Detainer filed against Plaintiff (Case# 23STUD02401)”

 

The court first notes the failure to separately identify each and every cause of action identified in the caption within the body of the complaint. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.112.) The court sustains the demurrer on this basis.

 

Even considering the substance of the claims, the court finds the allegations lack support for any and all of the identified subject causes of action in the caption. (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001; Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. (1992) 3 Cal.4th 370, 407; Shamsian v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 967, 983; Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 173–174; Visions Sports, Inc. v. Soren/McAdam/Bartells (2000) 86 Cal.App.4th 303, 309–310; Agricultural Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 385, 390; (Yau v. Santa Margarita Ford, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 144, 160–161.) The court finds no cited basis of authority for the third cause of action listed as “source of income discrimination,” and declines to speculate as to a potential basis of the claim on behalf of Plaintiff.

 

The demurrer is sustained with 30 days leave to amend. Plaintiff may not add any new causes of action without leave of court, but may articulate additional facts in order to support the challenged claims. (Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023.) Any new causes of action added without leave of court may be subject to a motion to strike. The court reminds Plaintiff to separately identify each and every relied upon cause of action within the complaint.

 

The court will concurrently hold the Case Management Conference.
