1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Soledad Gutierrez filed this action against Defendants, County of Los Angeles (erroneously sued as County of Los Angeles Harbor) and UCLA Medical Center for various FEHA violations, the details of which will be discussed below.    

 

Plaintiff’s complaint includes causes of action for:

·         Race and National Origin Discrimination in Violation of FEHA;

·         Retaliation in Violation of FEHA;

·         Failure to Take Reasonable Steps to Prevent Discrimination in Violation of FEHA;

·         Declaratory and Injunctive Relief;

·         Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code §1197.5.

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

  1. Evidentiary Objections

Defendant filed evidentiary objections with its reply papers.  Objections 1, 2, 7, and 8 are sustained.  The remaining objections are overruled.

 

  1. Issues Presented

Defendant moves for summary judgment on the complaint on three grounds.  First, it contends Plaintiff failed to exhaust administrative remedies prior to filing her complaint.  Second, it contends Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the statute of limitations.  Finally, it contends it had a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for Plaintiff’s termination, such that it is not liable for wrongful termination or related claims. 

 

  1. Timeline of Allegations

Plaintiff commenced employment with Defendant in 2002.  Plaintiff was hired as a laboratory assistant, which remains her position to this day.  In November of 2018, she complaint to the Director of Compliance about illegal training or registered nurses and doctors of non-Hispanic descent.  In April of 2022, she requested documents regarding her complaint, but was denied assistance and documents. 

 

In January of 2019, Plaintiff was asked to take a competency assessment while she was on approved bereavement leave; she ultimately was able to take all of her leave, but believes the request was insensitive and indicative of her hostile work environment. 

 

In June of 2021, she complained to the Director of Employee Relations regarding high-ranking management positions and promotions provided to non-Hispanic individuals who knowingly and willingly lied during official investigations, while qualified Hispanic individuals were overlooked and held to a higher standard.  In August of 2021, Plaintiff was removed from her work assignment area pending what Plaintiff believes was a fabricated safety related violation and she has not been able to return to work by her supervisor. 

 

At unspecified times, Plaintiff received written reprimands and a counseling memo re: attendance; the reprimands were rescinded when she filed her grievance, but she believes they were intended to be retaliatory measures. 

 

In February of 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint with the DFEH and EEOC.  She contends this complaint has led to ongoing discrimination in the form of an ongoing refusal to permit her to return to work and failing to prevent discrimination. 

 

  1. Exhaustion of Administrative Remedies

The County’s first argument is that Plaintiff failed to exhaust her administrative remedies prior to filing this action.  The County concedes Plaintiff filed an DFEH complaint, but contends it was not sufficient to put the County on notice of the claims against it.  Specifically, the County contends the DFEH complaint alleges whistleblower retaliation, but does not include any facts showing racial or ethnic discrimination, which is the basis for her civil complaint.    

 

Plaintiff’s DFEH complaint, attached as Exhibit B-3 to Defendant’s appendix of evidence, indicates that Plaintiff complained to Defendant about illegal training of doctors and nurses.  She requested documents, but was denied assistance and documents.  She later complained about violation of civil service rules regarding pre-selection for high-ranking management positions and promotions of persons who lied during official investigations.  Thereafter, she suffered various adverse work consequences. 

Defendant argues the foregoing is sufficient to warn it that Plaintiff is pursuing a whistleblower claim of some sort, but not a race- or ethnicity-based FEHA claim, which is what she ultimately asserted in this lawsuit.  Plaintiff argues the allegations in her DFEH claim need only be reasonably related to the allegations in her civil complaint, and because the same factual allegations underpin both the administrative claim and the civil complaint, she concludes her administrative claim was sufficient.

 

Plaintiff relies on Soldinger v. Northwest Airlines, Inc. (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 345, 381, wherein the Court held:

The issue before us revolves around the fact that Soldinger's charges submitted to DFEH do not duplicate the allegations in her amended civil complaint. Some authorities state the more specific the original charge filed with the administrative agency, the less likely a civil lawsuit may be expanded into other areas. (Okoli v. Lockheed Technical Operations Co., supra, 36 Cal.App.4th at p. 1617.) However, even if this is an accurate statement of the law, all recognize that a DFEH charge “is not intended as a limiting device.” (Watson v. Department of Rehabilitation (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1271, 1288 [ 261 Cal.Rptr. 204].) Incidents not described in a DFEH charge can be included in the subsequently filed lawsuit if they would necessarily have been discovered by investigation of the charged incidents, i.e., if the allegations in the civil complaint were “like or related” to those specified in the DFEH charge. (Okoli v. Lockheed Technical Operations Co., supra, at p. 1615;  Sanchez v. Standard Brands, Inc., supra, 431 F.2d at p. 466.) “Essentially, if an investigation of what was charged in the [administrative agency filing] would necessarily uncover other incidents that were not charged, the latter incidents could be included in a subsequent [civil] action.” (Okoli v. Lockheed Technical Operations Co., supra, 36 Cal.App.4th at p. 1615; original italics.)

 

Defendant fails to cite anything on point or to the contrary.  The Court finds Plaintiff’s allegations, while not mentioning race, were sufficient to meet the test set forth above.  The motion for summary judgment on the ground that Plaintiff’s DFEH complaint did not adequately reflect her claims asserted in the civil suit is therefore denied. 

 

  1. Burden Shifting Analysis in FEHA Claims

Special rules govern allocation of the burden of proof on motions for summary judgment in wrongful termination and employment discrimination cases, under both federal and state law. State courts follow the approach taken by federal courts in these cases.  Moore v. Regents of Univ. of Calif. (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 216, 233.  These special rules are intended to sharpen the inquiry into the elusive factual question of intentional discrimination. The summary judgment procedure “provides a particularly suitable means to test the sufficiency of … the defendant's nondiscriminatory motives for the employment decision.”  Caldwell v. Paramount Unified School Dist. (1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 189, 203.

 

But “many employment cases present issues of intent, and motive, and hostile working environment, issues not determinable on paper”; such cases are “rarely appropriate for disposition on summary judgment.”  Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 286. 

 

Because direct evidence of discrimination is seldom available, courts use a system of shifting burdens as an aid to the presentation and resolution of such cases both at trial and on a motion for summary judgment.   Guz v. Bechtel Nat'l, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 354-355.  At trial, the initial burden is on the employee to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. (Not much is required, however; circumstantial evidence creating an inference of discrimination is sufficient.)  Id. at 354-355. 

 

When the employer seeks summary judgment, however, the initial burden rests with the employer to show that no unlawful discrimination occurred.  CCP §437c(p)(2); see Id. at 354-355; see also Cornell v. Berkeley Tennis Club (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 908, 926.  To satisfy its initial burden, the moving party employer must either undermine an element of the plaintiff's prima facie case by affirmatively negating it or showing the plaintiff cannot prove it, or must provide a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse employment action; McGrory v. Applied Signal Tech., Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1523. 

 

First, the employer (moving party) must carry the burden of showing the employee's action has no merit (§ 437c(p)(2)). It may do so by evidence either:

•       negating an essential element of the employee's claim (difficult to do, because prima facie case of discrimination is so easy to establish); or

•       showing some legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the action taken against the employee.   Caldwell, supra, at 202-203. 

 

If the employer meets this initial burden, to avoid summary judgment the employee must produce “substantial responsive evidence that the employer's showing was untrue or pretextual,” thereby raising at least an inference of discrimination.  Hersant v. California Dept. of Social Services (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 997, 1004-1005.  A plaintiff's “suspicions of improper motives … primarily based on conjecture and speculation” are clearly not sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact to withstand summary judgment.  Kerr v. Rose (1990) 216 Cal.App.3d 1551, 1564.   Evidence showing facts inconsistent with the employer's claimed reasons tends to prove the employer's discriminatory intent.  Martin v. Lockheed Missiles & Space Co. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1718, 1735.   “Pretext” does not require proof that discrimination was the only reason for the employer's action. When there are mixed motives for the employer's action, it is enough that discriminatory intent was a substantial motivating factor in the employer's decision to take the adverse action.  Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 232. 

An employee may also avoid summary judgment by attacking the credibility of the employer's declarations; i.e., by demonstrating “such weaknesses, implausibilities, inconsistencies, incoherencies, or contradictions in the employer's proffered legitimate reasons for its action that a reasonable factfinder could rationally find them unworthy of credence” and hence infer discriminatory intent.”   Hersant, supra, at 1005.  However, though “disbelief of an Employer's stated reason for a termination gives rise to a compelling inference that the Employer had a different, unstated motivation, … it does not, without more, reasonably give rise to an inference that the motivation was a prohibited one.”  McGrory v. Applied Signal Technology, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1531-1532. 

 

The defendant employer may proceed directly to provide evidence that its action was taken for a legitimate, nondiscriminatory purpose. The burden is then on the plaintiff employee to rebut with evidence raising an inference that intentional discrimination occurred. Summary judgment for the employer should be granted where, “given the strength of the employer's showing of innocent reasons, any countervailing circumstantial evidence of discriminatory motive, even if it may technically constitute a prima facie case, is too weak to raise a rational inference that discrimination occurred.”   Guz, supra at 362. 

 

  1. Prima Facie Case of Discrimination Under FEHA

Defendant contends Plaintiff cannot make a prima facie case establishing discrimination under FEHA.  To establish a prima facie case of discrimination, she must show that (1) she was a member of a protected class, (2) she was performing competently in the position she held, (3) the County subjected her to an adverse employment action, and (4) some other circumstance suggests discriminatory motive.  See Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc., 24 Cal.4th 317, 355 (2000).

 

Defendant contends Plaintiff cannot establish she suffered any adverse employment action, which is an element of a prima facie case.  The parties discuss three alleged adverse employment actions:

·         Paid administrative leave;

·         An assessment during bereavement leave;

·         Retracted reprimands and a counseling memo.

 

Defendant cites McRae v. Dep’t of Corrs. And Rehab. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 377, 386 to support its contention that paid administrative leave does not constitute an adverse employment action.  McRae did not consider paid administrative leave, but generally discussed whether and when adverse employment action occurs.  The Court held (citations omitted):

In California, an employee seeking recovery on a theory of unlawful discrimination or retaliation must demonstrate that he or she has been subjected to an adverse employment action that materially affects the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, rather than simply that the employee has been subjected to an adverse action or treatment that reasonably would deter an employee from engaging in the protected activity (citation).  A change that is merely contrary to the employee's interests or not to the employee's liking is insufficient.” (citation) “ ‘[W]orkplaces are rarely idyllic retreats, and the mere fact that an employee is displeased by an employer's act or omission does not elevate that act or omission to the level of a materially adverse employment action.’ [Citation.] If every minor change in working conditions or trivial action were a materially adverse action then any ‘action that an irritable, chip-on-the-shoulder employee did not like would form the basis of a discrimination suit.’ [Citation.]” (citation) The plaintiff must show the employer's retaliatory actions had a detrimental and substantial effect on the plaintiff's employment. (citations).

 

“Requiring an employee to prove a substantial adverse job effect ‘guards against both “judicial micromanagement of business practices” [citation] and frivolous suits over insignificant slights.’ [Citation.] Absent this threshold showing, courts will be thrust into the role of personnel officers, becoming entangled in every conceivable form of employee job dissatisfaction. While the Legislature was understandably concerned with the chilling effect of employer retaliatory actions and mandated that FEHA provisions be interpreted broadly to prevent unlawful discrimination, it could not have intended to provide employees a remedy for any possible slight resulting from the filing of a discrimination complaint.” (citation). In addition, while the employee and the public have a significant interest in preventing discrimination or retaliation for engaging in protected activity, those are not the only interests at stake. The employer and the public have a legitimate interest in efficient, cost-effective, and high-quality work. Employers need to be able to manage employees without fear that routine employment decisions, or attempts at improving employee performance, will lead to litigation. Employees themselves have an interest in learning if their work habits are substandard, particularly if they wish to advance in their professions. “On the one hand, we worry that employers will be paralyzed into inaction once an employee has lodged a complaint ..., making such a complaint tantamount to a ‘get out of jail free’ card for employees engaged in job misconduct. On the other hand, we are concerned about the chilling effect on employee complaints resulting from an employer's retaliatory actions.” (citation). 

 

Plaintiff argues paid administrative leave can constitute adverse employment action.  She relies on Whitehall v. County of San Bernardino (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 352, 356 and Fonseca v. LAUSD, 2023 Cal. Super. LEXIS 95337 to support her position.  In Whitehall, the plaintiff, a social worker with DCFS, sought legal advice concerning her supervisors’ instructions that she falsify information in juvenile court.  Her attorney prepared a filing for the juvenile court apprising it of the falsified information, and the County placed the plaintiff on administrative leave for violating confidentiality rules.  During her two-month leave, the County decided to terminate her employment; she ultimately resigned to avoid being terminated.  The Court of Appeals held that, under the circumstances and in light of the ultimate termination of employment (which termination decision was made during the leave), the administrative leave was an adverse employment action.  The Court did not hold that administrative leave is ALWAYS an adverse employment action; indeed, the Court noted that it “may” constitute an adverse employment action depending on the specific circumstances of the case. 

 

Fonseca is, as Defendant correctly notes in reply, an unpublished case.  Additionally, it is Federal District Court case that was not decided at the appellate level.  The Court has not considered the citation. 

 

In this case, the facts do not support a conclusion that Plaintiff’s leave constituted an adverse employment action.  Plaintiff does not dispute fact 44, which establishes that Plaintiff’s doctor restricted her from working the night shift, indicating that, if this could not be accommodated, a leave of absence would be necessary; in light of the note, Plaintiff was on leave from August 27, 2019 to February 2, 2020.  Plaintiff’s second leave is more troublesome, but ultimately cannot be said to have been an adverse employment action under the circumstances.  Facts 50 and 51 establish that other employees lodged multiple complaints against Plaintiff, which complaints caused Defendant to place Plaintiff on a paid leave of absence to investigate; the leave commenced on 8/03/21 and was to conclude on 1/16/23, but was extended until 2/17/23 in light of Plaintiff’s doctor’s note indicating she needed additional time off.  She remains employed by Defendant in her position to this day. 

 

The Court finds the paid administrative leave does not, under the circumstances, meet the test set forth in McRae, and therefore was not an adverse employment action. 

 

With respect to Plaintiff’s other claims, the parties discuss the holding of Kelley v. The Conco Companies (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 191, 192.  The Kelley Court held (citations omitted): Mere ostracism in the workplace is insufficient to establish an adverse employment decision (citations).  However, “‘[W]orkplace harassment, if sufficiently severe or pervasive, may in and of itself constitute an adverse employment action sufficient to satisfy the second prong of the prima facie case for ... retaliation cases.’ 

 

In this case, Plaintiff has not even stated a claim for harassment.  The complaint is entirely devoid of any cause of action for harassment under FEHA.  The bereavement leave incident was certainly not severe or pervasive; Plaintiff alleges her supervisor accidentally contacted her while she was on leave but ultimately did not interfere with her leave and did not require her to engage in any work-related activity while on leave.  The work criticisms complained of cannot constitute an adverse employment action as a matter of law.  See Light v. Dept of Parks & Rec. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 75, 92, cited by Defendant in the moving papers and not addressed by Plaintiff in opposition, holding that verbal and written criticism from supervisors does not, as a matter of law, constitute an adverse employment action. 

 

  1. Conclusion

Plaintiff failed to make a prima facie showing of adverse employment action, which is a prerequisite to each of her claims.  Defendant is therefore entitled to judgment as a matter of law on the complaint, and the motion for summary judgment is granted.  The Court declines to rule on the parties’ additional arguments, as doing so is not necessary to dispose of the entire action.

2. Motion to Compel Deposition

Plaintiffs propounded a notice of deposition on Defendant on 6/19/24, setting the deposition for 8/07/24. On 8/02/24, Defendant served an objection, indicating she was not available that day. She did not provide alternative dates for deposition. On the same day, Plaintiffs sent correspondence to Defendant indicating they would move to compel the deposition. Plaintiffs took the deposition off calendar and filed the motion on 8/06/24, a day before the deposition was scheduled to go forward. There was, therefore, no failure to appear. The Court understands Plaintiffs’ frustration with Defendant’s decision to wait 44 days after the notice was propounded to indicate she was not available. CCP §2025.270(a) permits a party to schedule a deposition with ten days’ notice. Plaintiffs chose to give approximately fifty days’ notice. The Court will not attempt to determine Defendant’s motivation for waiting to indicate her unavailability. The Court finds only that Plaintiffs failed to meet their burden to show Defendant failed to appear at a scheduled deposition and also failed to meet and confer properly prior to filing the motion.

The motion is denied. Counsel must meet and confer forthwith to schedule all depositions without the need for further law and motion practice.

The Court notes that the motion also seeks an order precluding Defendant from taking Plaintiffs’ depositions until after Plaintiffs take Defendant’s deposition, as well as an order continuing the trial date. MOTION TO QUASH

Moving Party: Non-Parties, Jeff Macomber and Amarik K. Singh

Opposing Party: Plaintiff, Kurry Moultry

Notice: OK

1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Kurry Moultry filed this action against Defendants, Marvin Speed and the California Department of Corrections for damages arising out of illegal surveillance.

Plaintiff filed his complaint on 1/12/24. Plaintiff filed his operative First Amended Complaint on 2/16/24. The FAC adds additional defendants – Dexter L. Barnes, Carol Fowler, John Winston, David

Mitchell, Russell Aster, and Joe Martinez, and appears to omit the original two defendants. The FAC includes only one page, but appears to continue to state a claim for illegal surveillance.

2. Demurrer

a. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading. It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within]. Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond. CCP § 430.10(f).

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the

incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

b. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he left a voicemail for Plaintiff detailing the grounds for her anticipated demurrer, but Plaintiff did not return the phone call. Defense Counsel adequately established sufficient attempts to meet and confer.

c. Analysis

Defendants demur to the FAC on the ground that Plaintiff failed to allege compliance with the Government Tort Claims Act. Government Code §911.2 requires a plaintiff to present a government tort claim to the public entity he intends to sue prior to commencing suit. Pursuant to City of San Jose v.

Superior Court (1974) 12 Cal.3d 447, 454, failure to allege compliance with the tort claims requirement is fatal to a complaint.

The Court was previously scheduled to hear this demurrer on 9/12/24. Plaintiff failed to oppose the demurrer prior to the hearing. Plaintiff appeared at the hearing and sought a continuance, which the Court granted. On 9/11/24, Plaintiff filed opposition to the demurrer, wherein he attaches a Government Tort Claim that he contends he served on Defendant.

Any reply to the opposition was due on or before 9/25/24. Defendant did not file timely reply papers, and the Court will not consider late-filed reply papers.

Pursuant to City of San Jose, supra, compliance with the Act must be pled in the complaint. Plaintiff failed to do so, and therefore the demurrer is sustained. Leave to amend is granted. Plaintiff must file a Second Amended Complaint, pleading compliance with the Act and attaching a copy of his Claim, within twenty days. The Court is not, at this time, passing judgment on the sufficiency of the Claim attached to the opposition papers. Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time after receipt of the SAC.

3. Motion to Quash

Plaintiff propounded a subpoena on Jeff Macomber, Secretary of the California Department of Rehabilitation and Corrections, as well as Amarik K. Singh, Inspector General of California, purporting to require their personal appearance at the hearing on the above demurrer and the CMC. They move to quash the purported subpoena.

There is no authority permitting a party to subpoena a witness for hearing on a demurrer or motion or for appearance at a routinely scheduled Case Management Conference. These matters are decided purely based on the parties’ papers and oral argument. Notably, ruling on a demurrer does not require the Court to make any evidentiary rulings, and instead requires the Court to determine whether the pleadings, on their face, are sufficient. No witnesses are necessary to do so. The motion to quash is granted.

ELISA CARRILLO, Plaintiff(s), vs. GARDAWORLD SECURITY SERVICES OF CALIFORNIA, INC., ET AL., Defendant(s). ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 24LBCV00302 [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION; CASE MANAGEMENT CONFERENCE Dept. S27 8:30 a.m. October 3, 2024

Moving Party: Defendants, Gardaworld Security Services of California, Inc and Ecamsecure

Opposing Party: Plaintiff, Elisa Carrillo

Notice: OK

1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Elisa Carrillo filed this action against Defendants, Gardaworld Security Services of California, Inc and Ecamsecure for violations of FEHA and violation of wage and hour laws.

2. Motion to Compel Arbitration

a. Parties’ Positions

Defendants move to compel Plaintiff to submit this dispute to binding arbitration. Defendants contend Plaintiff agreed, upon accepting employment with Defendants, to submit all claims to binding arbitration. They contend the FAA governs the parties’ agreement, and all requirements to submit the

claims to arbitration are satisfied. They seek an order staying this action pending the outcome of binding arbitration.

Plaintiff opposes the motion. The opposition contends the motion should be denied because (a) Defendants failed to show any agreement to arbitrate exists, and (b) the agreement is unconscionable.

Defendants, in reply, contend the evidence is sufficient to establish the existence of an arbitration agreement, procedural unconscionability has not been shown, and even if procedural unconscionability were shown, substantive unconscionability is also required.

b. General Law Governing Motions to Compel Arbitration

Pursuant to CCP §1281, “A written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” Pursuant to §1281.2, ”[O]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that:

(a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or

(b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.

(c) A party to the arbitration is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party . . .. This subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.”

c. Is There Sufficient Evidence that Plaintiff Signed the Agreement?

There is a three-step process used to determine whether a defendant has adequately established the plaintiff’s electronic signature on an employment agreement. Fabian v. Renovate America, Inc. (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 1062; Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047; Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836

STEP 1 – Defendant meets its initial burden to show an agreement to arbitrate by attaching a copy of the contract to its petition, which purportedly bears the plaintiff's electronic initials and signature. Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1057-1058 ["as a preliminary matter the [trial] court is only required to make a finding of the agreement's existence, not an evidentiary determination of its validity.”]). To meet its burden, the moving party need only attach a copy of the agreement to the petition and incorporate it by reference. (Id. at 1058; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference."]).

STEP 2 – Plaintiff shifts the burden by declaring that she/he did not sign the contract

STEP 3 – Defendant then has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that the electronic signature was authentic. Defendant’s burden of authenticating an electronic signature is not great. (Ruiz at 844) The party seeking authentication may carry its burden in any manner, including by presenting evidence of the contents of the contract in question and the circumstances surrounding the contract's execution. (ibid).

Defendant can attribute the signature to Plaintiff by showing the "efficacy of any security procedure applied to determine the person to which the electronic record or electronic signature was attributable.” (Civ. Code, § 1633.9, (a)). The effect of an electronic signature attributed to a person "is

determined from the context and surrounding circumstances at the time of its creation, execution, or adoption, including the parties' agreement, if any, and otherwise as provided by law." (Civ. Code, § 1633.9, (b)).

A party can establish that the electronic signature was the act of Plaintiff “by presenting evidence that a unique login and password known only to that person was required to affix the electronic signature, along with evidence detailing the procedures the person had to follow to electronically sign the document and the accompanying security precautions.” (Bannister v. Marinidence Opco, LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 541, 545).

In Espejo v. Southern Cal. Permanente Med. Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1053-54, the employer’s systems consultant submitted a declaration detailing how the username and password were generated, how the system worked, and how the employer was able to conclude that the employee had signed the subject arbitration agreement. The Court of Appeals found the evidence sufficient to establish the employee had signed the agreement.

In this case, Defendant provided evidence with its moving papers sufficient to meet the requirements of the reply papers discussed above in the three-part test. Specifically, Defendant supported its motion with the Declaration of Evelyn Bendik, who works in Defendant’s HR department and who details, at ¶¶4-7, how electronic signatures are obtained and why they were reliable.

Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, contends only that she “doesn’t remember” signing the agreement, and that Bendik’s declaration is deficient. To support her position that the declaration is deficient, she relies on Adams v. Superior Court, 2010 WL 602515. The case is not published and cannot be relied upon. Additionally, it was decided prior to the authorities discussed above, and this is a rapidly

evolving area of the law. Thus, even if Adams were to support her position (which the Court declines to consider), Adams is neither published nor controlling.

Defendant met its burden to show Plaintiff signed the agreement, and Plaintiff’s declaration that she does not recall signing the agreement is insufficient to defeat Defendant’s showing that she did so during the hiring process.

d. Is the Agreement Unconscionable?

I. General Law Governing Unconscionability

A contract will be found to be unconscionable where: (1) it is adhesive, in that all or part of the contract falls outside the reasonable expectations of the weaker party; and (2) equitably, the terms unreasonably favor the other party. (Stirlen v. Supercuts (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1524.) Unconscionability has both a “procedural” and a “substantive” element.

The procedural element focuses on two factors: oppression and surprise. “Oppression” arises from an inequality of bargaining power which results in no real negotiation and an absence of meaningful choice. “Surprise” involves the extent to which the supposedly agreed upon terms of the bargain are hidden in the prolix printed form drafted by the party seeking to enforce the disputed terms. A contractual term is substantively suspect if it reallocates the risks of the bargain in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner. Procedural unconscionability has to do with matters relating to freedom of assent.

Substantive unconscionability involves the imposition of harsh or oppressive terms on one who has assented freely to them. The prevailing view is that these two elements must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability. (Id., at 1532-1533.) In Stirlen, the court found that an arbitration provision was

unconscionable in that included a forced waiver of punitive damages by the plaintiff employee, in violation of Civil Code §1668, and accorded the defendant employer greater rights in pursuing a court action for injunctive or equitable relief.

II. Procedural Unconscionability

Plaintiff argues the parties’ agreement is procedurally unconscionable. She fails, however, to raise any argument regarding substantive unconscionability. As noted above, both must be present in order to defeat the agreement. This argument therefore also fails.

e. Conclusion

Defendants met their moving burden to show there is a binding arbitration agreement, signed by Plaintiff, that requires Plaintiff to submit his claims to arbitration. Plaintiff failed to show that the agreement was not actually signed, and failed to show any defense to enforcement of the agreement.

Per CCP §1281.2, the motion to compel arbitration is granted.

The litigation against Defendants is stayed pending the outcome of arbitration. CCP §1281.4.

INOLA VALENZUELA, Plaintiff(s), vs. METECH RECYCLING, INC., ET AL., Defendant(s). ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 24LBCV00754 [TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE Dept. S27 8:30 a.m. October 3, 2024

Moving Party: Defendant, MeTech Recycling, Inc.

Opposing Party: Plaintiff, Inola Valenzuela

Notice: OK (continued by Department S26)

1. Background Facts

Plaintiff filed suit against MeTech, Sedgwick, and York Risk Services (York) (collectively “Defendants”), on April 15, 2024, alleging Breach of Contract.

Plaintiff alleges that on or about April 20, 2022, she and Defendants entered into an oral agreement, whereby the parties agreed to resolve “personal injuries for an amount not to exceed soft tissue damages.” (Complaint, section BC-1.) Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached the oral agreement on May 1, 2022, by not paying the personal injury damages. (Id., BC-2.)

2. Special Motion to Strike

a. Parties’ Positions

MeTech moves to strike the complaint under the anti-SLAPP statute, arguing that the alleged breach of the oral contract constitutes protected activity, and that the breach of contract cause of action is barred by the litigation privilege, such that Plaintiff cannot prevail on the merits of the action.

Specifially, MeTech contends Plaintiff previously sued MeTech in Sacramento County for personal injuries suffered from a motor vehicle accident in 2019. MeTech claims that action was dismissed after the Court sustained MeTech’s demurrer without leave to amend based upon the statute of limitations. MeTech claims that to circumvent the statute of limitations issue, Plaintiff argued that MeTech breached an oral agreement to settle the matter, but the Sacramento County Court disagreed and entered judgment in MeTech’s favor.

Plaintiff then sued MeTech’s firm, Matheny and Sears, in this court, for breach of contract, alleging Matheny and Sears breached an agreement to settle the underlying personal injury matter. (RJN, Ex. 9.) Matheny and Sears then filed an anti-slapp motion, similar to the instant motion here. Plaintiff dismissed the action while the motion was pending. (Id. Ex. 11.)

Plaintiff opposes the motion, arguing only that the matter does not involve protected activity. Plaintiff’s opposition does not address her probability of prevailing on the merits at all.

b. Request for Judicial Notice

MeTech asks the Court to take judicial notice of the following Court documents:

1. Plaintiff’s complaint filed in the instant matter

2. Plaintiff’s complaint filed against MeTech in Sacramento Case no. 34-2022-00322626

3. Court’s TR sustaining MeTech’s demurrer with leave in the Sacramento Case

4. Plaintiff’s FAC filed in the Sacramento Case

5. The Court’s TR sustaining MeTech’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC in the Sacramento case

6. Plaintiff’s SAC filed in the Sacramento Case

7. Notice of Entry of Order sustaining Demurrer to SAC in Sacramento Case

8. Notice of Entry of Order granting motion to strike portions of the SAC in Sacramento Case

9. Notice of Entry of Amended Judgment of Dismissal in Sacramento Case

10. Defendant Matheny, Sears, Linkert & Jaime LLP’s anti-slapp motion filed in case no. 24LBCV00384

11. Plaintiff’s Request for Dismissal in case no. 24LBCV00384

The RJN is unopposed and granted.

c. Timeliness

The motion is timely filed and is being timely heard. An anti-SLAPP motion may be filed within 60 days of the service of the complaint or, in the court's discretion, at any later time upon terms it deems proper. The motion shall be scheduled by the clerk of the court for a hearing not more than 30 days after the service of the motion unless the docket conditions of the court require a later hearing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (f).)

Plaintiff served MeTech on June 10, 2024, and the instant motion was filed July 10, 2024. While the motion was originally scheduled to be heard 32 days after service of the motion in Department S26, the scheduling of the hearing more than 30 days after filing is not fatal. The anti-slapp statute does not require the moving party to ensure that the hearing is so scheduled and does not justify the denial of a special motion to strike solely because the motion was not scheduled for a hearing within 30 days after the motion was served. (Karnazes v. Ares (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 344). Moreover, S26 was dark the week the motion was filed, suggesting that docket conditions warranted a later hearing. The motion was then continued for hearing in S27, and Defendant was unable to control the date scheduled for the hearing on the motion.

d. Law Governing Anti-SLAPP motions

Anti-SLAPP motions involve a two-prong test:

· The moving party must show the cause of action against the that party arises from the their acts in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. section 425.16, subd. (b)(1).)

o An act in furtherance of the defendant’s right of petition or free speech includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest. (Id. at subd. (e).)

· Upon such a showing, the burden shifts to the opposing party to demonstrate a probability that they will prevail on the claim. (Id. at subd. (b)(1).)

e. Moving Burden

Generally, while the court must consider the pleadings, the court must look past how Plaintiff characterizes MeTech’s conduct and determine, based upon the evidence presented in the form of supporting and opposing affidavits from both parties, whether Plaintiff’s claims are based upon protected speech or conduct and whether Plaintiff can establish a probability of prevailing on the merits. (Id., subd. (b)(2); Stewart v. Rolling Stone LLC, (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 664, 679.) Looking beyond the pleadings is of utmost importance here because of the scant factual allegations in the complaint.

MeTech’s alleged conduct is clearly protected activity. MeTech is alleged to have, on May 1, 2022, breached an oral agreement, entered into on April 20, 2022, to pay for personal injury damages. Pursuant to MeTech’s RJN, Plaintiff filed a Negligence action against MeTech in Sacramento County on or about June 28, 2022. (RJN, Ex. 2.) This establishes that the alleged oral agreement was made during settlement negotiations preceding Plaintiff filing the Sacramento action.

Settlement negotiations that precede actual litigation filed constitute protected activity. “Just as communications preparatory to or in anticipation of the bringing of an action or other official proceeding are within the protection of the litigation privilege of Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b) [citation], ... such statements are equally entitled to the benefits of section 425.16.”. (Briggs v. Eden Council for Hope & Opportunity (1999) 19 Cal.4th 1106, 1115.) Prelitigation communications may qualify for anti-SLAPP protection so long as they concern the subject of the dispute and are made in anticipation of litigation contemplated in good faith and under serious consideration. (Flickinger v. Finwall (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 822.)

Here, clearly MeTech’s settlement negotiations were contemplated in good faith and related to litigation under serious discussion. As of April 2022, no action was filed, and the parties clearly were engaging in settlement negotiations. Any attorney knows that if a settlement is not reached, litigation likely follows. The alleged conduct is protected activity pursuant to CCP 425.16(e)(2).)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that MeTech failed to prove that any official proceeding took place, and finding as so would be inconsistent with the pleadings. This assertion is incorrect on numerous levels.

Firstly, Plaintiff clearly disregards the procedural history established by the requests for judicial notice. Plaintiff filed the Sacramento Case two months after the alleged breach. Moreover, per the authority discussed above, Plaintiff is incorrect that the Court’s inquiry here is limited to the complaint’s allegations. The Court is to consider information beyond the four corners of the complaint.

f. Shifted Burden

Because MeTech met its moving burden to show the complaint implicates protected conduct, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to show a probability of success on the merits. Plaintiff’s opposition does not address this prong. This is an implied concession that she cannot prevail on the merits.

g. Conclusion

MeTech met its moving burden to show the complaint is based on protected conduct. Plaintiff failed to meet her shifted burden to show a probability of success on the merits. The anti-SLAPP motion is therefore granted.
