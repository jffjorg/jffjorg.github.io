DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 20STCV20703    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: B

Hearing:             10/03/2024

Re:                         20STCV20703 Crystal Coleman, et al. vs John Doe

 

 

Plaintiffs Crystal Coleman, et al.’s Request for Default Judgment

 

Defendant: Joshua Randles

 

TENTATIVE RULING

            Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment is DENIED and the Complaint is dismissed without prejudice. 

           

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiffs Crystal Coleman and Renee Larkins allege that, on June 14, 2018, Defendant Joshua Randles recklessly opened the door of his vehicle on the side of moving traffic, causing Plaintiffs’ vehicle, which was lawfully travelling southbound on Van Ness Ave., to suddenly and violently collide with the door of Defendant’s vehicle. Plaintiffs allege this constitutes negligence per se under Cal. Veh. Code § 22517 and seek damages of $9,594.19.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Code Civ. Proc., § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs seek damages in the amount of $9,594.19. Specifically, Plaintiffs seek $7,385.00 in damages, $1,809.19 in costs, and prejudgment interest at the annual rate of 10%. Plaintiffs did not provide a calculation of interest.

 

Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment is denied for the following deficiencies:

 

1.      Plaintiffs failed to file a Statement of Damages for Renee Larkins. (CRC 3.250(a)(20).)

2.      Plaintiffs failed to complete a declaration of nonmilitary status on either CIV-100s. (CRC 3.1800(a)(5).)

3.      Plaintiffs failed to provide a calculation of interest. (CRC 3.1800(a)(3).)

 

Because the Court finds Plaintiffs have not submitted filings required by CRC 3.250(a) and CRC 3.1800(a), the Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment.

 

Plaintiffs have attempted, and failed, to enter or obtain default judgment multiple times due to repeated filing deficiencies under CRC 3.250 and CRC 3.1800. These instances are as follows:

 

·         May 5, 2023, request for default rejected (outdated CIV-100; no declaration under penalty of perjury in support of damages sought; no copy of Statement of Damages; failure to dismiss unnamed Does; no evidence of damages)

 

·         February 6, 2024, request for default rejected (failure to file CIV-100; only provided a Statement of Damages on behalf of Coleman; failure to dismiss unnamed Does; no evidence of damages)

 

·         May 20, 2024, request for default rejected (excessive General Damages sought; failure to complete declaration of nonmilitary status; requested costs not permitted under CCP § 1033.5)

 

Plaintiffs’ most recent request for default judgment is denied for a fourth time on

account of filing deficiencies, some of which the Court has previously identified and addressed.

 

Failure to Timely Obtain Default Judgment

 

            Pursuant to CRC 3.110(h), the party who requested an entry of default must obtain default judgment within 45 days after the default was entered. The court may issue an order to show cause why sanctions should not be imposed if that party fails to obtain default judgment within that time, including terminating sanctions and dismissal of the action.

 

Pursuant to CCP § 583.410(a), the court may dismiss an action on its own motion if doing so “appears appropriate under the circumstances of the case.” Dismissal shall be appropriate pursuant to the procedure and criteria prescribed by rules adopted by the Judicial Council. (CCP § 583.410(b).) The court may dismiss pursuant to CCP § 583.140 if the action is not brought to trial within three years after the action is commenced against the defendant. (CCP § 583.420(a)(2)(A).)

           

            Here, Plaintiffs’ Complaint naming Defendant was filed on June 2, 2020. The action was commenced well over three years ago and it has not yet been brought to trial or judgment. Therefore, it is within the court’s discretion to dismiss the case pursuant to CCP § 538.410 and CCP 583.420(a)(2)(A).

 

            The Court exercises its discretion and dismisses the Complaint against Defendant Joshua Randles without prejudice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

I.       BACKGROUND  

            On October 14, 2020, Plaintiff Emerson Langlois-Ulrich (“Plaintiff") filed a complaint in the instant action against Defendant Michael Evich Kirsanov (“Defendant”) alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. The complaint arises from a March 1, 2008, incident, at which time Defendant allegedly operated his motor vehicle in a negligent manner and hit Plaintiff, who was a pedestrian. Plaintiff was a seven-year-old minor at the time of the incident. Plaintiff alleges personal and economic injuries as a result.

            On June 5, 2024, the matter was assigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East.

            On July 16, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

II.       RELIEF REQUESTED

            Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint for motor vehicle negligence. Defendant alleges that, in August 2008, Plaintiff entered a binding settlement of his claims with Defendant’s insurance carrier Mercury Insurance Company (“Mercury”) for $15,000 (Shaver Decl. ¶¶ 3-5.) Defendant alleges that Mercury tendered the $15,000 settlement amount to Plaintiff on August 28, 2008. (Ibid. at ¶ 5.) As a result, Defendant alleges that Plaintiff’s claim is barred by the compromise settlement. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 13-15.)

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

            In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)  “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

            A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)

            Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence.

            On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)    

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Requests for Judicial Notice

            Defendant’s July 16, 2024, requests for the Court to take notice of Plaintiff’s complaint and Defendant’s first amended answer in the instant action are denied. Judicial notice of the Court’s own records and case file is unnecessary and redundant as the Court can always review the case at hand.

 

Defendant’s Burden

            Alleged August 2008 Settlement Agreement

            Defendant fails to meet his initial burden.

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s complaint for motor vehicle negligence must fail as a matter of law because Plaintiff entered a binding settlement agreement with Defendant’s insurance carrier, Mercury, in August 2008. However, Defendant fails to proffer evidence of this agreement.

            Settlements Involving Minors

            “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” (CCP § 664.6.) A compromise and release agreement “has many of the attributes of a judgment” and is determinative of the rights of the parties in a dispute. (Shriver v. Kuchel (1952) 113 Cal.App.2d 421, 425.)

            An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; CCP § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.)¿ 

            Defendant Offers No Evidence of a Minor’s Compromise

            Defendant asserts that, following the March 1, 2008, incident, Plaintiff retained counsel from the Law Offices of Gary A. Dordick (“Dordick”). (Undisputed Separate Statement of Fact “USSF” No. 2. [Decl. of Bustamante ¶¶5-7, Exhs. E, F, and G.) Defendant claims that Dordick, on behalf of Plaintiff, sent Mercury a written demand for payment of Defendant’s policy limits as a full and final settlement of Plaintiff’s claims. (Ibid. at No. 5.; Ex. F.) In response, Mercury timely accepted and complied with the letter by tendering $15,000 to Plaintiff, in addition to (1) a certified copy of the declarations page for Mr. Kirsanov’s insurance policy (2) written confirmation of acceptance of Plaintiff’s policy limit demand, (3) written confirmation of payment, and (4) Defendant’s signed declaration that he had no excess liability insurance and that he was not working for an employer at the time of the underlying incident. (Ibid. at No. 7; Ex. G.)

            Based on these facts, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff entered into a settlement of his claims against Defendant arising out of the March 1, 2008, incident, “pending court approval of the settlement as required under Probate Code section 3500 and Code of Civil Procedure section 372.” (Ibid. at No. 8)(emphasis added). However, Defendant offers no evidence of approval by the Court. Instead, Defendant acknowledges that the alleged settlement offer was pending court approval as required by Prob. Code § 3500 and CCP § 372.

            Plaintiff was a seven-year-old minor at the time of the incident and proposed settlement. Accordingly, court approval was required for the settlement to take effect. “As with any other contract where one party lacks capacity, or a necessary contractual formality has been ignored, the [settlement] is voidable until the [court ratifies the settlement.]” (Scruton, supra, 39 Cal.App.4th at 1605.) Defendant fails to offer evidence of a court-ratified agreement and therefore fails to meet their initial burden.

            Unless and until a defendant has met their burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. supra, 91 Cal.App.4th at 468.) Here, Plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence.

            Even assuming, arguendo, Defendant had met his burden, Plaintiff submitted evidence suggesting the existence of a triable issue of fact as to whether a valid minor’s compromise exists. (Lisa Langlois Decl. ¶ 2.) Furthermore, Lisa Langlois, appointed guardian ad litem of Plaintiff at the time of the alleged settlement, denies authorizing Dordick’s demand letter. (Ibid.) An attorney cannot enter settlement on behalf of their client without their client’s authorization. (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct Rule 1.4.1.)  Therefore, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether a valid settlement offer existed at all.

V.       CONCLUSION

            A triable issue of material fact exists as to the existence of a valid and binding settlement agreement. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 20STCV46821    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: B

Re:                         ZOILA MONROY, et al. vs CITY OF GARDENA, et al. (20STCV46821)¿

 

 

Petitioner Zoila Monroy’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Zoila Monroy’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a motor vehicle collision in which Defendants City of Gardena, a municipal corporation (“Gardena”), Brandon Pastre (“Pastre”), an individual, and DOES 1 to 50 (collectively, “Defendants”) negligently owned, maintained, entrusted, and operated a vehicle in a distracted manner, causing it to collide with Plaintiffs Zoila Monroy (“Petitioner”), Genesis Dubon (“Genesis”), and Kelly Dubon (“Kelly”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) who were in a vehicle stopped at a red traffic light. As a result, Plaintiffs suffered injuries. (Complaint ¶¶11-14.)  

 

On December 7, 2020, Plaintiffs filed an action against Defendants for motor vehicle negligence.

 

On December 7, 2020, Petitioner Zoila Monroy (“Petitioner”) filed an application and order for appointment of guardian ad litem for Kelly Dubon, which was granted. On December 9, 2020, Petitioner filed an application and order for appointment of guardian ad litem for Genesis Dubon, which was granted.

 

On October 27, 2023, and November 7, 2023, Petitioner filed two Petitions to Confirm Minor’s Compromise with Special Needs Trust for Kelly and Genesis. The Court denied both petitions for medical record and filing deficiencies, including the failure to file an Order to Deposit Funds Into Blocked Account (MC-355) for both Claimants.

 

On November 27, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a notice of settlement.

 

On December 18, 2023, Petitioner again filed two Petitions to Confirm Minor’s Compromise with Special Needs Trust for Kelly and Genesis. The Court denied both petitions for medical record deficiencies.

 

On September 9, 2024, Petitioner filed the two instant Petitions for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability for Genesis and Kelly. Petitioner also filed two corresponding forms MC-355.

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Genesis and Kelly would receive $7,500 and $6,000, respectively.

 

Legal Standard

 

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Id., at p. 1606.) 

 

Probate Code section 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows: 

 

The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code, § 3601, subd. (a).) 

 

In cases pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955, subd. (a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955, subd. (a)(2).)

 

Discussion

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Genesis and Kelly would receive $7,500 and $6,000, respectively, out of a total settlement of $87,500. No costs or attorney’s fees will be deducted from either Claimant’s settlement amounts.

 

Having reviewed the petitions, the Court finds that the settlement is fair considering the risk of going to trial and the alleged injuries sustained by Plaintiffs. Petitioner has satisfied the requirements under CRC 7.950 by disclosing information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise. (Kelly MC-350 Attachment 8; Genesis MC-350 Attachment 8.) There is no fee agreement between Plaintiffs and Counsel as Counsel represented Plaintiffs pro bono. (Kelly MC-350 line 13a; Genesis MC-350 line 13a.) Petitioner’s settlement amount is reasonable given her injuries were more severe than the minors involved. (Kelly MC-350 Attachment 11(b)(3); Genesis MC-350 Attachment 11(b)(3).)

 

However, Petitioner failed to file forms MC-351 as required by Cal. Rules of Court 7.950.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES without prejudice the petition to confirm minor’s compromise.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On October 7, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Martin Junior Becerra (“Becerra”), Rafael Rico Estrada (“Estrada”), Sergio Mosqueda (“Mosqueda”), Lyft, and DOES 1-50 (“DOES”)(collectively “Defendants”), for damages from an October 13, 2020, vehicle collision, alleging that Defendants negligently entrusted, maintained, and operated their vehicle, resulting in collision with Alvarez’s vehicle. Alvarez alleges injuries and damages as a result. (Alvarez Complaint ¶¶ 9-10.) 

On September 16, 2022, prior to Plaintiff’s initial complaint, Mosqueda filed a separate complaint for negligence arising from the same vehicle collision, naming Becerra, Estrada, and DOES 1-50 as defendants.  

On June 5, 2024, Berrera filed a motion to consolidate the actions commenced by Mosqueda’s September 16, 2022, complaint and Plaintiff’s October 7, 2022 complaint.  

On June 6, 2024, the Court granted Berrera’s motion to consolidate. 

On July 17, 2024, Lyft filed the instant motion to compel arbitration of all claims asserted by Plaintiff. 

On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to Lyft’s motion to compel. 

On September 25, 2024, Lyft filed a reply to Plaintiff’s opposition. 

Plaintiff opposes the motion to compel arbitration on the following grounds: (1) the case involves third parties not bound to Lyft’s arbitration agreement, and therefore arbitration risks inconsistent verdicts arising from the same facts; and (2) no valid arbitration agreement exists between Plaintiff and Lyft. 

 

II. LEGAL STANDARDS

Evidentiary Objections 

Lyft’s September 25, 2024 evidentiary objections to Plaintiff’s August 27, 2024 declaration are overruled as to Nos. 1, 2, and 3. Lyft’s objections substantially misquote Plaintiff’s declaration and are therefore inapt. 

            Motion to Compel Arbitration 

In deciding a motion to compel arbitration, trial courts must first decide whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then determine the second gateway issue of whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. (See Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)(See also Chiron Corp. v. Ortho Diagnostic Systems, Inc. (9th Cir. 2000) 207 F. 3d 1126, 1130 (“The court’s role under the [Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”)] is therefore limited to determining (1) whether a valid agreement to arbitrate exists and, if it does, (2) whether the agreement encompasses the dispute at issue.”).  “The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 64 Cal.Rptr.2d 903, 915-16.) “In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Ibid. at 916.) No jury trial is available for a petition to compel arbitration. (Ibid.)  

Accordingly, under both the FAA and California Law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract.  (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)   

 

III. DISCUSSION

            Existence of a Valid Arbitration Agreement 

            Lyft proved the existence of a valid arbitration agreement with Plaintiff. Lyft submitted evidence that on January 12, 2019, Plaintiff affirmatively accepted Lyft’s February 6, 2018, Terms of Service (“Terms”)(McCachern Decl. ¶ 12; Compendium of Evidence (“COE”) Ex. 4.) Plaintiff could not have ordered the ride at issue on October 13, 2020, without affirmatively accepting these Terms. (McCachern Decl. ¶ 8.) To order a ride on the Lyft platform, riders must first create a Lyft user account on Lyft’s mobile app. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) After creating an account, the Lyft user must accept Lyft’s most current terms of service before ordering a ride. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.) Plaintiff does not deny ordering the October 13, 2020, ride with his Lyft account. 

            The Terms provide that Lyft and Plaintiff mutually “AGREE TO WAIVE OUR RESPECTIVE RIGHTS TO RESOLUTION OF DISPUTES IN A COURT OF LAW BY A JUDGE OR JURY AND AGREE TO RESOLVE ANY DISPUTE BY ARBITRATION…”

(COE Ex. 4.) 

            The Terms also provide the following: 

“This agreement to arbitrate (“Arbitration Agreement”) is governed by the Federal Arbitration Act…  

 

 …These Claims include, but are not limited to, any dispute, claim, or controversy, whether based on past, present, or future events, arising out of or relating to: [the Terms] and prior versions thereof (including the breach, termination, enforcement, interpretation, or validity thereof), the Lyft Platform, the Services, any goods or services made available through the Lyft Platform, [and] your relationship with Lyft…  

(COE Ex. 4.) 

            Plaintiff’s claim against Lyft, which alleges negligent entrustment of a motor vehicle, directly relates to the rideshare service made available through Lyft’s platform. Therefore, pursuant to the Terms, the arbitration agreement covers Plaintiff’s claim. 

            Plaintiff argues that Lyft’s Terms contain “no indicia to call [the arbitration agreement]’s attention to the reader.” (See Opposition p. 4, lines 19-20.) However, there is an all-caps advisory of the binding arbitration agreement at the very beginning of the Terms which Plaintiff accepted. (COE Ex. 4.) This advisory also points users to Section 17 where they may read the agreement in detail. (Ibid.) Section 17 provides the agreement in large, conspicuous text and plain language. Additionally, Lyft’s terms of service are always available to its users for review within the app. (McCachern Decl. ¶ 9.) Therefore, the Court does not find merit in Plaintiff’s argument. 

            Lyft has proved the existence of a valid arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and Lyft and therefore meets its initial burden. 

            Plaintiff’s Burden 

            The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate a valid defense against enforceability of the Agreement.  

             Plaintiff argues that arbitration would risk inconsistent verdicts in disputes arising from the same accident since it involves third parties who are not bound to Lyft’s Terms. (Opposition pp. 2-4.) Specifically, Plaintiff argues that the FAA only applies to litigation where the legal rights of all litigants are “referable to arbitration.” (Opposition p. 3, lines 6-8.) Therefore, Plaintiff contends, the FAA does not apply to the instant litigation since the legal rights of all parties involved are not referable. (Ibid.)  

            Plaintiff cites California Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2(c) which allows courts to refuse the enforce an arbitration agreement if it determines that “[a] party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.” (CCP § 1281.2(c).) A “third party” is one who is neither bound by nor entitled to enforce the arbitration agreement. (Daniels v. Sunrise Living, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 674, 679.) Additionally, arbitration agreements governed by the FAA do not preclude application of CCP § 1281.2, since the state’s statutory scheme is designed to enforce parties’ arbitration agreements as the FAA procedurally requires. (Williams v. Atria Las Posas (2018) 235 Cal.Rptr.3d 341, 346). For the FAA to preclude CCP § 1281.2, the arbitration agreement must explicitly state that FAA procedural rules are to apply. (Ibid.) 

            Here, Plaintiff, a party to the Arbitration Agreement with Lyft, is also a party to the instant action which involves third parties Becerra and Estrada. Becerra is the operator of the other vehicle involved in the collision and Estrada is the owner of said vehicle. Neither party is affiliated with Lyft, nor were they using Lyft’s goods or services at the time of the collision. Therefore, they are neither bound by nor entitled to enforce the arbitration agreement. Additionally, there is a risk of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. Plaintiff named both Becerra and Estrada as Defendants in his action for negligence. There remains a possibility that, if Lyft and Plaintiff proceed to arbitration, an arbitrator could find Mosqueda wholly responsible for the collision while this Court simultaneously finds Becerra and/or Estrada wholly responsible. 

            Lyft’s Reply 

            Lyft denies § 1281.2’s applicability on the grounds that “[t]he U.S. Supreme Court has expressly rejected arguments that courts may deny a motion to compel arbitration based on the presence of non-signatory third parties to the agreement.” (Reply, pp. 2-3, lines 26-1.) Specifically, Lyft states that “[u]nder the FAA, an arbitration agreement must be enforced notwithstanding the presence of other persons who are parties to the underlying dispute but not to the arbitration agreement” even if doing so produces “piecemeal resolution[s].” (Reply, p. 3, lines 4-6 [quoting Moses H. Cone Mem. Hosp. v. Mercury Constr. Corp. (1983) 460 U.S. 1, 20, emphasis added.])  

            Lyft also relies on Laswell v. AG Seal Beach, LLC (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1399 to dispute § 1281.2’s applicability. However, both Moses and Laswell differ from the case at hand. In these cases, the Court enforced arbitration agreements despite the presence of non-signatory third parties because the third parties had the power to enforce the agreement. (Moses, at p. 25 [arbitration agreement between hospital and contractor when contractor subcontracted with architect]) (Laswell, at p. 1407 [third party was parent company of party to arbitration agreement and thus had the power to enforce the agreement]). 

            Here, unlike the third parties in Moses and Laswell, Becerra and Estrada are distinct third parties who neither signed the arbitration agreement nor have the capacity to enforce. 

            Therefore, CCP § 1281.2(c) applies to the instant action. Arbitration may not proceed because there is a risk of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. 

 

IV. CONCLUSION

              CCP § 1281.2(c) applies to the instant action. Therefore, Lyft’s motion to compel arbitration is denied.
