Case Number: 22STCV30508    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 10/10/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV30508 DARRYL ADONIS MURPHY vs LUKAS HUYNH, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

On September 19, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present complaint. On February 1, 2024, Defendants, including Defendant 12 Trucking LLC, filed their answers. On August 8, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motions to compel initial responses from Defendant 123 Trucking. Defendant was ordered to pay sanctions and provide responses by September 9, 2024. However, no responses nor sanctions were provided by the deadline. Plaintiff now moves the Court for terminating sanctions, evidentiary sanctions, issue sanctions, and monetary sanctions against Defendant. No opposition was filed.

The motion is untimely. On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff served the motion electronically on Defendant. The motion is to be heard 18 court days from the date of service (16 court days under CCP 1005(b) plus 2 court days for electronic service under CCP 1010.6(a)(3)(B)). 18 court days from September 16, 2024, is October 11, 2024, but the motion is set for October 10, 2024. The court denies the present motion as untimely. 

Furthermore, it does not appear that Defendant 123 Trucking has abandoned its case. Mr. Bauer, Defendant 123 Trucking’s attorney, recently filed a declaration stating that on September 30, 2024, his mother passed away, and he will be on bereavement leave, noting that he will prepare for trial upon his return. The lack of an opposition and failure to provide discovery responses may be related to counsel’s mother’s passing. Additionally, the Court observes that Plaintiff has withdrawn numerous motions on calendar within this time frame, raising the question of whether there is an agreement between the parties to suspend court activities to accommodate Mr. Bauer’s bereavement leave. In any event, as noted above, the motion is denied because it did not provide sufficient notice.

If a party intends to submit on this tentative ruling, the party must send an email to the court at sscdept27@lacourt.org with the Subject line “SUBMIT” followed by the case number.  The body of the email must include the hearing date and time, counsel’s contact information, and the identity of the party submitting.

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

MOTION TO PERMIT DISCOVERY REGARDING DEFENDANT CAESAR VALIENTE’S FINANCIAL CONDITION
Hearing Date: 10/10/24 
CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV30916 HORACIO GALVAN vs CAESAR VALIENTE, et al.
Moving Party: Plaintiff 
Responding Party: Defendant Cesar Valiente
Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO PERMIT DISCOVERY REGARDING DEFENDANT CAESAR VALIENTE’S FINANCIAL CONDITION IS DENIED

Background 

On September 21, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present case alleging that he was attacked by off-duty police officer Caesar Valiente. Plaintiff claims that Valiente's dog jumped on Plaintiff's dog, and as he tried to prod the dog away, Defendant Valiente tackled Plaintiff and arrested him. Plaintiff alleges various causes of action against Defendant Valiente including assault and battery, false imprisonment, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and violation of the Bane Act, along with claims for punitive damages. Plaintiff now moves the Court to conduct pretrial discovery of Defendant Valiente's financial condition, arguing that there is substantial probability that he can prevail on these various causes of action and punitive damages. 

Legal Standard

Under CC § 3295(c), pretrial discovery of a defendant's financial condition is generally not permitted. However, the statute also provides: "Upon motion by the plaintiff . . . the court may at any time enter an order permitting the discovery otherwise prohibited by this subdivision if the court finds, on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented, that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim [for punitive damages]. (Jabro v Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 754, 756-757.) Yet, before a trial court may enter an order allowing discovery of financial condition information under CC § 3295(c), it must (1) weigh the evidence presented by both sides and (2) make a finding that it is very likely the plaintiffs will prevail on their claim for punitive damages. (Id., at 755.) The words "substantial probability" meant "very likely" or "a strong likelihood," just as their plain meaning suggested. (Id.) The Legislature did not use the term "reasonable probability" or simply "probability," which would have implied a lower threshold of "more likely than not.” (Id.) 

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)¿  Clear and convincing evidence requires proof making the existence of a fact highly probable— falls between the “more likely than not” standard commonly referred to as a preponderance of the evidence and the more rigorous standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt.” (Conservatorship of O.B. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 989, 995.) 

“Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civil Code section 3294 (c)(1).) Under the statute, malice does not require actual intent to harm. Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.) ‘Despicable’ is a powerful term that refers to circumstances that are ‘base,’ ‘vile,’ or ‘contemptible.’ (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)     

“‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civil Code section § 3294(c)(2).)¿ 

“‘Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civil Code section¿§ 3294(c)(3).)¿

Discussion

Assault and Battery

An element of assault and battery is that Defendant acted either with the intent to harm or to cause offensive contact. Plaintiff cites Defendant’s deposition (Exhibit 1 at 20:18-21:2, 101:8-12, and 102:22-103:2) for the claim that Defendant "chose to punch GALVAN as hard as he could in the face multiple times." (Motion at pg. 11.) However, the deposition excerpts cited refer only to Valiente’s military and police academy training and his understanding that punching someone would cause injury. None of these excerpts support the conclusion that Defendant actually punched Plaintiff in the face multiple times.  The video presented (but not cited for the proposition) appears to show that Defendant punches Plaintiff, but the following is unclear to the Court: where Defendant hits Plaintiff, how many times he hits Plaintiff, and the force used.

Plaintiff further contends that Defendant's statement calling Plaintiff “an idiot” and asserting his authority by saying he wielded the law Plaintiff hoped would protect him (Valiente Depo at 241:19-21) constitutes malicious and oppressive conduct. (Motion at pg. 12.) However, this is an overstatement of Valiente’s testimony. What Valiente actually said was, "I am a f***ing cop, you idiot," after his neighbor identified him as being a cop. The Court does not find this statement very likely to convince a jury that Valiente intended to cause injury or subjected Plaintiff to cruel and unjust hardship. A jury could reasonably conclude that Valiente made the statement in response to being identified, rather than with malicious intent.

Moreover, based on the video, the Court is not persuaded that a finder of fact is likely to award punitive damages against Defendant Valiente.  Defendant Valiente was initially acting in defense of his own dog after Plaintiff started beating the dog with a stick of some sort.  The level of beating appears to the Court to  exceed what was necessary to ensure that Plaintiff’s dog was safe – in fact, from the video, it is unclear that Defendant Valiente’s dog was, as Plaintiff alleges, “attacking” Plaintiff’s dog; it is just as likely that Defendant Valiente’s dog was hoping to engage in dog play when Plaintiff started beating Valiente’s dog.  At that point, Defendant Valiente acted to restrain Plaintiff.  Although Defendant appears to have allowed his emotions to get the better of him upon doing so, the Court is not persuaded that it is a substantial probability that a trier of fact would find that under the circumstances Defendant, in restraining and apparently punching Plaintiff at least one time, acted maliciously or despicably. 

        False Arrest and Imprisonment

An officer is not liable for false imprisonment when making an arrest without a warrant if he has reasonable grounds to believe the person is guilty of a crime (Allen v. McCoy (1933) 135 Cal. App. 500, 507-508.) Plaintiff acknowledges this principle but argues that because Valiente’s dog attacked Plaintiff’s dog, Plaintiff acted in self-defense by attacking the dog, and therefore, there was no probable cause for his arrest. (Motion at pg. 14.)

To establish self-defense, Plaintiff must demonstrate that he reasonably believed Defendant’s dog was going to harm his dog, and that he used only the amount of force reasonably necessary to protect his dog (CACI 1304). If force is applied in excess of that which is justified, the actor remains subject to liability for the damages resulting from the excessive use of force. (Calvillo-Silva v. Home Grocery (1998) 19 Cal.4th 714, 730-731.) The Court finds it unlikely that there is a substantial probability a jury will conclude Plaintiff acted within the scope of reasonable self-defense. Video of the incident showed that Plaintiff struck Defendant’s dog numerous times and continued to do so even as the dog was retreating. A jury could find Plaintiff’s use of force to be excessive and went beyond what is considered reasonably necessary for self-defense.

 

Plaintiff further contends that Defendant committed fraud by misrepresenting the events to the responding officer. (Motion at p. 15.) Plaintiff cites Defendant's deposition (Exhibit 4 at 61:25-62:13), where Defendant does not recall that Plaintiff was charged with assault with a deadly weapon or possession of a billy club. However, this lack of recollection is not evidence of fraud. Plaintiff has not cited to any concrete evidence to show that Defendant made a fraudulent statement to the responding officer. Accordingly, Plaintiff has failed to meet his evidentiary burden as the moving party.

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

An element of IIED is that Defendant intended to cause Plaintiff emotional distress or acted with reckless disregard for the probability that Plaintiff would suffer emotional distress.

Plaintiff argues that Defendant acted with willful and conscious disregard for the effects of his conduct when he allegedly “countered Galvan’s pleas for help by maliciously calling him an idiot and reminding him that Valiente wielded the law Galvan hoped would protect him. (See Valiente Depo, Exh. 1 at 241:19-21.).” (Motion at pg.17.) However, the deposition excerpt cited by Plaintiff does not support this assertion. Valiente’s statement, “I am a cop, you idiot,” was made after he was identified by his neighbor, not in response to Plaintiff’s pleas for help. There is no evidence in the cited deposition that Valiente made this statement in direct response to Plaintiff’s pleas, as Plaintiff claims.

Furthermore, the Court does not find it highly likely a jury would find this statement to be clear and convincing evidence of malice. Defendant's comment appears to have been made in response to being identified, rather than with an intent to cause harm or subject Plaintiff to cruel or unjust hardship. Therefore, the Court does not find a substantial probability that a jury would conclude Valiente acted with malice or oppression.

Bane Act

Plaintiff argues that he was exercising his right to protect himself and his dog, and that Defendant used violence to prevent Plaintiff from exercising those rights. (Motion at pp. 18-19.) However, Plaintiff cites no evidence to support the claim that the alleged arrest was motivated by retaliation for exercising his rights. (Id.) Furthermore, to justify opening pretrial discovery into Defendant’s financial condition, the standard is "substantial probability," which means "very likely" or "a strong likelihood." While it is possible for a jury to find that Defendant acted in retaliation when he allegedly restrained Plaintiff, it is equally likely that a jury could find that Defendant acted because Plaintiff exceeded the bounds of reasonable self-defense when attacking Defendant’s dog.

Conclusion

        Plaintiff needs to establish that there is a substantial probability that he will prevail on the claim for punitive damages. Plaintiff failed to meet this burden and therefore the motion is denied.

I.                   INTRODUCTION

This personal injury action arises out of a vehicular collision.

On May 16, 2023, Plaintiff Jason Melendez (Plaintiff) filed the instant action against Defendants Alan Saul Greenstone (Defendant) and Does 1 through 30, alleging two (2) causes of action: (1) General Negligence; and (2) Motor Vehicle damages.

The complaint alleges the following: On July 8, 2021, Plaintiff was operating his vehicle on La Cienega Boulevard in West Hollywood when Defendant, who was operating his own vehicle traveling in the opposite lane beside Plaintiff’s vehicle, attempted to do a U-turn across double yellow lines without using his turn signal. To avoid colliding with Defendant, Plaintiff entered in the center left lane. However, Plaintiff was unable to avoid Defendant’s vehicle which clipped Plaintiff’s vehicle. Plaintiff’s vehicle spun out of control and collided with two parked cars resulting in injuries and damages to Plaintiff.

 

 

On October 6, 2023, Defendant filed an answer denying all allegations and asserting fourteen (14) affirmative defenses.

On August 29, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motions seeking an order compelling Plaintiff to submit to a neurological independent medical examination (IME) with neurologist Dr. Barry I. Ludwig, M.D. at 9:00 am on October 23, 2024, and a neuropsychological IME with Dr. Taylor P. Kuhn, Ph.D., ABPP/CN at 9:00 am on October 24, 2024.  

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions asserting Defendant’s motions seek duplicative IMEs because Plaintiff underwent a medical examination by Dr. Luke Mcyszyn, M.D., a neurosurgeon specializing in complex spinal disorders, on September 4, 2024. Also on this same day, Plaintiff filed pleadings agreeing to Defendant’s requests for examinations on certain conditions including limiting the exams to two (2) hours and prohibiting “EMG’s, nerve conduction studies, MRI’s, EEG’s, any type of injection and any type of electrical testing.” (Plantiff’s Agreement, ¶¶ 7, 11.)

II.                LEGAL STANDARD

When the physical condition of the plaintiff is in controversy in a personal injury case, the defendant may obtain a physical examination of the plaintiff, if: (1) “the examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive”; and (2) “the examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220, subd. (a)(1)-(2).) Such demand must specify the “time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination” and the “identity and the specialty, if any, of the physician who will perform the examination.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220, subd. (c).) Additionally, the examination must be scheduled at least “30 days after service of the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220, subd. (d).)

If a party seeks additional examinations “other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.310, subd. (a).) “A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.310, subd. (b).)

The examination requested must directly relate to the subject of the litigation: the examinee’s specific injury or condition which is “in controversy.” (Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337.) The court cannot order the examination of a party who has not asserted his or her physical or mental condition to support a defense or claim. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 389.) However, once a “party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties,” puts his mental state in controversy. (Ibid.)

The moving party has the burden to show by declarations or other evidence that the examinee’s condition is “in controversy.” (Reuter v. Superior Court (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 332, 340.) Pleadings and discovery tools such as interrogatories or depositions may also put a party’s mental or physical condition in controversy when the pleading alleges a party suffers psychiatric conditions as a result of sustained injuries. (Ibid.)

 

 

 

III.             DISCUSSION

1.      Meet and Confer

A party seeking a court order to compel IMEs must fulfill the “meet and confer” requirement. “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

Defendant submits he attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff so that Plaintiff would stipulate to two physical examinations prior to filing this motion. Defendant states that on April 10, 2024, his counsel electronically propounded on Plaintiff’s counsel a Demand for Physical Medical Examination by Dr. Luke Macyszyn per Civil Procedure Code section 2032.020, to be performed on May 21, 2024. (Declaration of Erwin A. Nepomuceno, Esq. [Nepomuceno Decl.], ¶ 4, Exh. 1.) This was Defendant’s demand to the one physical examination permitted under section 2032.220(a). On April 26, 2024, Defendant’s counsel electronically served a letter to Plaintiff’s counsel enclosing IME notices with Dr. Luke Macyszyn, Dr. Barry I. Ludwig, and Dr. Taylor Kuhn, Ph.D. detailing that:

1) Dr. Macyszyn would conduct Defendant’s one-allotted physical IME of Plaintiff on May 21, 2024, pursuant to CCP §2032.210, 2) the qualifications of Dr. Ludwig and Dr. Kuhn to conduct a physical IME and mental health IME, respectively, 3) a detailed explanation of Plaintiff’s complaints and why the IMEs with Dr. Ludwig and Dr. Kuhn were necessary, citing Plaintiff’s relevant responses to written discovery, Plaintiff’s relevant deposition testimony, and relevant findings from Plaintiff’s treating neurologist Dr. Eskenazi and treating neuropsychologist Dr. Grewal, and 4) a request that Plaintiff stipulate to Dr. Ludwig and Dr. Kuhn’s IMEs, so as to avoid unnecessary law and motion.

 

(Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 7, Exhs. 2-3.) On May 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel responded to Defendant’s request stating, “I am not going to subject my client to 3 IMEs, it is unnecessary” without explaining why the three (3) IMEs were unnecessary. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶8, Exh. 4, at p. 1.)

On May 8, 2024, Defendant asked Plaintiff’s counsel to stipulate to the neurological IME with Dr. Ludwig or the neuropsychologist/mental health exam with Dr. Kuhn. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. 5, p. 1.) Plaintiff’s counsel stipulated to Dr. Ludwig’s IME. (Ibid.) Based upon this exchange, and based on Defendant’s prior demand for the physical IME with Dr. Macyszyn, Defendant assumed that Dr. Macyszyn would conduct a physical IME of Plaintiff pursuant to section 2032.220 and that Dr. Ludwig would conduct a physical IME of Plaintiff per Plaintiff’s stipulation. (Ibid.)

On May 14, 2024, Defendant emailed Plaintiff’s counsel to confirm Plaintiff’s attendance at Dr. Macyszyn’s IME scheduled for May 21, 2024. (Nepomuceno, Decl. ¶ 10, Exh. 6, at p. 2.) Two days later, Plaintiff’s counsel responded stating “I only agreed to one IME, is Dr. Macyszyn going to be the one?” (Nepomuceno, Decl. ¶ 10, Exh. 6, at p. 1.)

On May 16, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff’s counsel, by email, notice withdrawing Dr. Ludwig and Dr. Kuhn’s IME notice and repeated Defendant’s election for Dr. Macyszyn to conduct Plaintiff’s physical IME per section 2032.220. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 11, Exh. 7.) On May 17, 2024, Defendant sent another email to confirm Plaintiff would attend Dr. Macyszyn’s IME on May 21, 2024. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 12, Exh. 8, at p. 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel responded, “only agreed to one IME” and attempted to clarify if Dr. Macyszyn was “going to be the one.” (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 12, Exh. 8, at p. 2.) Defendant’s counsel responded explaining that Dr. Macyszyn’s IME was the one provided for by statute and that Defendant requested that Plaintiff stipulate to the other two exams, to which Plaintiff’s counsel responded they would confirm with their client first. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 12, Exh. 8, at p. 1.)

Plaintiff did not appear for the Dr. Macyszyn IME on May 21, 2024. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 13.) Defendant incurred a $1,000.00 no-show fee. (Ibid.)

Because Defendant sent Plaintiff’s counsel multiple messages attempting to set up IMEs and to confirm with Plaintiff’s counsel which IMEs to do, the Court finds that the “meet and confer” requirement is met.

2.      PHYSICAL EXAMINATION - Neurological IME

When the physical condition of the plaintiff is in controversy in a personal injury case, the defendant may obtain a physical examination of the plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2032.020, 2032.220.)  A defendant is permitted to one physical examination of the plaintiff in a personal injury action on demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220, subd. (a).) If the defendant seeks to obtain an additional physical examination of the plaintiff, the defendant must obtain leave of court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).) A motion to compel an additional physical examination must “specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b).) 

The defendant must make a showing of “good cause” to obtain the second physical examination.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).) Good cause requires a showing of both (1) “relevan[ce] to the subject matter”; and (2) specific facts which justify discovery (such as allegations showing the need for information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere.) (Vinson v. Superior Court, supra, 43 Cal.3d at p. 840.)

Under the Discovery Act, there is no limit on the number of mental or physical examinations, provided that a showing of good cause is made to justify more than one mental or physical examination of a party. (See Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255.) The Shapira court also noted that “multiple examinations should not be ordered routinely; the good cause requirement will check the potential harassment of plaintiffs by repetitive examinations,” and “multiple examinations by medical specialists in different fields” may be necessary based upon a plaintiff's claimed injuries. (Ibid.)

Here, Plaintiff contends that because of the motor vehicle collision, Plaintiff sustained a traumatic brain injury. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges “head, spine, and brain injuries” as well as “mental health” suffering which did not exist prior to the accident. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 16, Exh. 10, at pp. 9, 10, 16-17.)

Defendant submits there is good cause for a neurological examination of Plaintiff because Plaintiff raised mental, emotional, and brain injury claims in his discovery responses, thereby putting these issues into “controversy.” Defendant also contends that Plaintiff has already seen a neurologist for his alleged injuries, thereby demonstrating a need for an IME to enable Defendant to assess Plaintiff’s claims through its own expert examination. In support of his motion, Defendant presents Plaintiff’s responses.

At deposition, Plaintiff testified that he was experiencing “mental health and emotional”;  he couldn’t sleep; he would wake up every night at 3:00 am with “headaches, dizziness . . . Like sometimes my neurons will send a signal, I don’t know”; and that he suffers from “depression, PTSD, light sensitivity, sound sensitivity, and smell sensitivity.” (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 18, Exh. 12, at pp. 93-94.) Plaintiff also testified that neurologist Dr. Jonathan Eskenazi treated him and told him the impact and brain trauma are what’s causing all the symptoms. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatory Responses identify treatment by TBI specialists who conducted “electromyography, [a] nerve conduction velocity test, trigger point injections, and an occipital nerve block” on Plaintiff. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 16, Exh. 10, at pp. 9-10.) Plaintiff’s responses also identified Dr. Bal Grewal, a neuropsychologist, who diagnosed Plaintiff with mild traumatic brain injury and Post Concussive Syndrome. (Nepomuceno Decl. ¶ 19, Exh. 13.) Dr. Grewal stated Plaintiff suffered several “cognitive, behavioral, and emotional conditions” resulting from the accident including short term memory loss, poor attention span, anxiety, severe depression, poor mental processing, and altered behavior. (Ibid.)

Finally, Defendant alleges that by not having access to its own IME, Defendant will be prejudiced and unable to use its expert to obtain necessary information to defend itself from Plaintiff’s claims. Defendant notes that a principal purpose of discovery is “to enable a party to obtain evidence in the control of his or her adversary to further the efficient and economical disposition of cases on the merits.” (Caryl Richards, Inc. v. Superior Court (1961) 188 Cal.App.2d 300, 303.)

Plaintiff opposes, stating Dr. Ludwig’s neurological IME is duplicative because Plaintiff has already undergone a medical examination by neurosurgeon Dr. Macyszyn, the doctor Defendant initially requested Plaintiff be examined by on September 4, 2024.[1] (Plaintiff’s Response at 1:23-25.) Plaintiff also opposes on the grounds that neurosurgeons and neurologists are distinct specialists but that their specialties overlap. (Id. at 2:18-20.) Plaintiff cites Riverside County case Ramos v. Kim to support these contentions. (See Ramos v. Kim (2023) 2023 Cal. Super. LEXIS 79361.)

In Ramos, the court in fact granted the Defendant’s motion to compel a second physical IME reasoning that a neurologist IME was needed to address Plaintiff’s additional ongoing neurological injuries and complaints. The court noted that “a neurosurgeon is a physician who operates on the brain, and spinal cord and spinal column, and peripheral nerves within all parts of the body,” while “neurologists specialize in treating the diagnosing disorders that affect the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nervous system, including headaches, sleep disorders, multiple sclerosis, epilepsy, neuropathic pain, dementia, Parkinson’s disease and stroke, but do not perform surgery.” (Id. at p. *6.)

The Court finds that Defendant has set forth good cause to compel Plaintiff to submit to the physical examination by Dr. Ludwig. Further, Defendant sets forth who will conduct the examination, where and when it will be conducted, the conditions, scope and nature of the examination, and the diagnostic tests and procedures to be employed.  (Memorandum and Points of Authority, pgs. 7:9-27, 8:1-6.) Additionally, Defendant has met his burden of showing “good cause.” Defendant provides specific facts showing why the IMEs are necessary and provides evidence of Plaintiff’s statements bringing Plaintiff’s neurological and physical injuries into issue. Finally, the Court finds that Defendant’s request to compel is appropriate as it appears Plaintiff never complied with Defendant’s initial demand to an IME with Dr. Macyszyn.

Although Plaintiff initially agreed to Defendant’s initial IME demand with Dr. Macyszyn on May 21, 2024, Plaintiff did not appear on that date. Confusion exists regarding Defendant’s attempts to set up examinations back in April 2024 and May 2024 because Plaintiff’s counsel stopped responding to Defendant’s requests after May 18, 2024. The record also does not reflect whether Defendant was aware that Plaintiff had chosen to submit to an IME with Dr. Macyszyn on September 4, 2024.

Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to submit to physical IME with Dr. Ludwig.

3.      Neuropsychological Examination

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.310, a party that seeks to require another party to submit to a mental examination must obtain leave of court. The motion to compel a mental examination must specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and specialty of the person who will perform the examination. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310.) The court must grant the motion if the moving party shows good cause for the mental examination. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).) 

A showing of good cause generally requires “that the party produce specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Vinson v. Superior Court, supra, 43 Cal.3d at p. 840.) Where a party claims “great mental pain and suffering” as a result of the physical injury, the court may order a psychiatric exam. (Reuter v. Superior Court, supra, 93 Cal.App.3d at p. 342.) And “[a] party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.” (Vinson v. Superior Court, supra, at 43 Cal.3d at p. 839.)

Here, Defendant submits there is good cause for a neuropsychological examination of Plaintiff because Plaintiff raised mental and emotional claims in his discovery responses, thereby putting these issues in controversy. Defendant also contends that because Plaintiff has already seen a neuropsychologist for his alleged psychological and neuropsychological injuries, this demonstrates a need for an IME to appropriately assess Plaintiff’s claims. In support of his motion, Defendant presents Plaintiff’s responses to discovery requests.

At deposition, Plaintiff testified about his psychological impairments. Plaintiff testified that he experienced “mental health and emotional”; that he couldn’t sleep; he would wake up every night at 3:00 am with “headaches, dizziness . . . Like sometimes my neurons will send a signal, I don’t know”; that he suffers from headaches, dizziness, nauseas, depression, PTSD, light sensitivity, sound sensitivity, and smell sensitivity.” (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 18, Exh. 13, at pp. 93-94, 112-113.) Plaintiff also alleged that these impairments limit his future earning capacity. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 18, Exh. 13, at pp. 201:20-25.)

In his Form Interrogatory Responses, Plaintiff alleged “pain and suffering” resulting from the car accident and that his “personal relationships and mental health suffered.” (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 16, Exh. 10, at p. 16.) Plaintiff also denied Defendant’s Request for Admission to “Admit that you suffered no psychological or emotional injury as a result of this accident.” (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 17, Exh. 11, at p. 3.) In his Special Interrogatory responses, Plaintiff alleged that he now “suffers from poor concentration and episodes of mental confusion.” (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 18, Exh. 12, at p. 11.) Plaintiff further testified that neurologist Dr. Jonathan Eskenazi informed him the impact and trauma to Plaintiff’s brain caused these symptoms. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 18, Exh. 13, at p. 115:9-15.)

Plaintiff’s discovery responses also identified Dr. Bal Grewal, a neuropsychologist, who diagnosed Plaintiff with mild traumatic brain injury and psychiatric injuries. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 19, Exh. 14.) Dr. Grewal stated that Plaintiff suffered several “cognitive, behavioral, and emotional conditions” resulting from the accident including short term memory loss, poo attention span, anxiety, severe depression, poor mental processing, and altered behavior. (Ibid.) Dr. Grewal diagnosed Plaintiff with a psychiatric injury of Acute Stress Reaction and Post Concussive Syndrome as a direct result to the traumatic injury to Plaintiff’s brain. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 19, Exh. 14.)

 

Defendant attempts to distinguish neuropsychology from neurology:

Neuropsychology is the study of the relationship between the brain and behavior. A neuropsychologist may assess an individual’s emotional well-being as well as their ability to concentrate, learn information, and attend to their environment. Neuropsychologists administer a battery of psychological tests, including those listed above, to evaluate an individual’s cognitive functioning. In particular, neuropsychologists can assess and monitor the recovery of individuals who experience neurological injuries.

 

(Memorandum and Points of Authority, pp. 9:13-18.) Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s mental injuries (cognitive issues resulting from the vehicle collision) are distinct from his physical injuries (traumatic brain injury).

Finally, Defendant argues that fairness requires that Defendant’s expert do a neuropsychological IME because the record reflects that Plaintiff consulted a neuropsychologist, Dr. Grewal, to diagnose Plaintiff’s neuropsychological issues. Plaintiff alleges this issues against Defendant. Defendant asserts that if Dr. Kuhn cannot do a neuropsychological IME, Defendant will be prejudiced because it will not have access to the key information that is necessary to defend itself.

Plaintiff opposes Defendants arguments with the same arguments as those used for the neurological exam above.

The Court finds that Defendant set forth good cause to compel Plaintiff to submit to a neuropsychological examination with Dr. Kuhn. Defendant sets forth who will conduct the examination, where and when it will be conducted, the conditions, scope and nature of the examination, and the diagnostic tests and procedures to be employed. (See Memorandum and Points of Authority, pgs. 8:5-28, 9:1-13.) Additionally, Defendant has met his burden of showing “good cause.” Defendant provides specific facts showing why the IMEs are necessary as well as his attempts to work with Plaintiff to do IMEs without court intervention. Specifically, Defendant explains the role of a neuropsychologist and how that individual’s area of expertise addresses mental examinations rather than physical examination. Finally, Defendant has shown that the neuropsychological IME is relevant to this case because Plaintiff put his mental condition “in controversy” through statements made during his deposition and his other responses to discovery.

Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to submit to a neuropsychological IME with Dr. Kuhn.

 Sanctions

Defendant seeks sanctions against Plaintiff for misuse of the discovery process. “The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel response and compliance with a demand for a physical examination, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.240, subd. (c).)

Here, Defendant seeks $2,543.30 in sanctions against Plaintiff: $1,271.65 for the Neurological IME motion and $1,271.65 for the Neuropsychological IME motion. (Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 20; Nepomuceno Decl., ¶ 21.) This amount includes $2,420.15 in attorney’s fees and $123.30 in filing fees ($61.65 each for two filing fees).

However, the Court is not persuaded that sanctions for the neuropsychological examination are appropriate.  Leave of court is required for such an examination, and there is no basis for the Court to conclude Plaintiff was in any way abusing the discovery process in requiring Defendant to obtain court approval for that examination. 

Therefore, the Court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions only as to the required medical examination.  The Court grants sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, in the reasonable amount of $1,250.00, to be paid within 20 days.

 CONCLUSION

The Court therefore GRANTS Defendant’s motion for leave to conduct one neurological and one neuropsychological IME of Plaintiff.

The Court also GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $1,250.00.
