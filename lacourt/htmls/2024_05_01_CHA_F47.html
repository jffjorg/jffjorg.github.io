DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 21STCV21293    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 5/1/24                                                            

Case #21STCV21293

 

SUMMARY JUDGMENT/SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

Motion filed on 2/8/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Right Choice In-Home Care, Inc.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Phyllis Ginolfi

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order granting summary judgment in favor of Defendant Right Choice In-Home Care, Inc. (Right Choice) and against Plaintiff Phyllis Ginolfi, by and through her Conservator, Christin C. Solis (Plaintiff). 

 

Alternatively, Right Choice requests summary adjudication of the following issues in its favor and against Plaintiff:

 

Issue No. 1: Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Negligence against Right Choice has no merit because there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s claim, that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

Issue No. 2: Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Dependent Care/Elder Abuse has no merit because there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s Claim that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

Issue No. 3: Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Dependent Care/Elder Abuse has no merit because Plaintiff cannot establish physical abuse or neglect and recklessness, malice, oppression, or fraud as to Right Choice to prevail under the Elder Abuse Statute.

 

RULING: The motion is denied in its entirety. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of an alleged fall that occurred while Plaintiff Phyllis Ginolfi (Plaintiff)

attended Defendant HDL, Inc. dba Pacoima Adult Day Care’s (HDL) adult day care facility on 8/20/19. 

 

Plaintiff is a developmentally disabled adult.  (Separate Statement (SS 1)).  Plaintiff resides with her niece and conservator Christin C. Solis (Solis) and her husband.  (SS 2).  HDL is an adult day care facility located in Pacoima, California.  (SS 4).

 

On 8/20/19, Plaintiff attended HDL, an adult day care program that she had been attending since earlier in the year 2019.  (SS 3).  On that day, Plaintiff had a one-to-one caregiver at HDL named Miriam Muwummuza.  (SS 5).  Muwummuza was an employee of Right Choice In-Home Care, Inc. (Right Choice).  (SS 6).  Right Choice provides non-medical support services to individuals for both short- and long-term assistance, including senior care, temporary respite care, day care, and supported or independent living.  (SS 7). 

 

On 8/20/19, Plaintiff attended the program at HDL from approximately 10:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m. (SS 8).  On that day, Plaintiff was dropped off and picked up by a paratransit service to and from HDL.  (SS 9). 

 

After arriving at HDL on 8/20/19, at approximately 10:10 a.m., Plaintiff was wheeled in her wheelchair into the bathroom by Muwummuza as seen in surveillance video.  (SS 10).  There was no video surveillance in the bathroom at HDL.  (SS 11).  Right Choice contends that after Plaintiff used the toilet, Muwummuza noticed Plaintiff was wobbly and gently assisted Plaintiff to the floor of the facility’s restroom and obtained help from HDL staff, Mireya Delgadillo to place Plaintiff back into her wheelchair.  (SS 12).  Right Choice claims that prior to transferring Plaintiff back to her wheelchair, Muwummuza and Delgadillo performed a body examination of Plaintiff to check for any signs of injury before placing her back in her wheelchair.  (SS 13). Right Choice claims that no broken skin, deformity, redness, resistance, distress, crying or any indication of any kind of injury was observed by Muwummuza and Delgadillo on the body exam of Plaintiff.  (SS 14). 

 

Thereafter, there is video surveillance of Plaintiff at HDL.  (SS 15-16).  At approximately 3:00 p.m., Plaintiff is wheeled by Muwummuza outside to the paratransit van.  (SS 21).  Plaintiff was dropped off by Defendant Keolis Transit America, Inc.’s (Keolis) paratransit van back to her home at approximately 4:05 p.m.  (SS 22).  Plaintiff’s private at-home caregiver, met Plaintiff when she returned home.  (SS 23).  Solis referred to Plaintiff’s private at-home caretaker on 8/20/19 as “Justin” at her deposition, in Plaintiff’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Plaintiff has since clarified that “Justin” is actually named “Dustin” [Stothoff].  (SS 24). 

 

While in the company of her at-home caregiver, Dustin called Solis and told her that Plaintiff  could not stand and that she was yelling and upset.  (SS 25).  Solis was not home and could hear Plaintiff yelling in the background when Dustin called her.  (SS 26).  Solis returned home, found Plaintiff agitated and upset, and called for the paramedics.  (SS 27).  Los Angeles City Fire Department emergency medical services was dispatched at approximately 6:08 p.m. (18:08:14 military time) to Plaintiff’s/Solis’ home.  (SS 28).

 

Plaintiff was transported from her home at approximately 6:28 p.m. (18:28:06 military time) to Providence Cedars Sinai Tarzana Medical Center (Tarzana Hospital).  (SS 29).  At Tarzana Hospital, Plaintiff was diagnosed with a serious femur fracture where she was diagnosed as having broken her left leg in three places.  (SS 30).

 

On 6/7/21, Plaintiff filed this action against HDL and Right Choice for: (1) Dependent Adult/Elder Abuse and (2) Negligence.  Keolis was later named in place of Doe 1.  Right Choice answered on 8/3/21. 

 

Right Choice claims that it is medically impossible that Plaintiff sustained the left femur fracture while at HDL on 8/20/19 and that Right Choice did not cause Plaintiff to fracture her left femur. (SS 31-32). 

 

On 2/8/24, Right Choice filed and served the instant motion seeking an order granting summary judgment in favor of Right Choice and against Plaintiff.    

 

Alternatively, Right Choice requests summary adjudication of the following issues in its favor and against Plaintiff:

 

Issue No. 1: Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Negligence against Right Choice has no merit because there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s claim, that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

Issue No. 2: Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Dependent Care/Elder Abuse has no merit because there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s Claim that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

Issue No. 3: Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Dependent Care/Elder Abuse has no merit because Plaintiff cannot establish physical abuse or neglect and recklessness, malice, oppression, or fraud as to Right Choice to prevail under the Elder Abuse Statute.

 

Plaintiff has opposed the motion and Right Choice has filed a reply to the opposition. 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Right Choice’s Request for Judicial Notice (RJN) is denied.  Right Choice has failed to establish that the Los Angeles City Fire Department Emergency Medical Services Report is/are “[f]acts and propositions that are of such common knowledge within the territorial jurisdiction of the court that they cannot reasonably be the subject of dispute” or “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”  Evidence Code 452(g), (h).  Even if the Court did take judicial notice of the contents of the report, it does not establish that Plaintiff’s injury could not have occurred while she was at HDL on 8/20/19.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Right Choice’s objection to the entire “declaration” of Dustin Stothoff is sustained.  The document is not a declaration signed under penalty of perjury.  See CCP 2015.5.  The handwritten statement is referred to in Plaintiff’s evidence as a “witness statement.”  Based on the sustaining of the objection to the entire declaration, the Court declines to rule on the remaining objections to this declaration.   

 

Right Choice’s objections to the declaration of Jabob Tauber are overruled.

 

Right Choice’s objections to the declaration of Christin C. Solis are overruled. 

ANALYSIS

 

On this motion, Right Choice bears the initial burden of showing that Plaintiff’s causes of action have no merit.  CCP 437c(a)(1), (p)(2).  A defendant has met its burden of showing a cause of action has no merit if the defendant has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.  CCP 437c(o), (p)(2).  Once the defendant has met this initial burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.  CCP 437c(p)(2).  The plaintiff may not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, must set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.  Id. 

 

In ruling on a motion for summary judgment/adjudication, the moving party’s evidence must be strictly construed while the opposing party’s evidence must be liberally construed.  Schachter (2009) 47 C4th 610, 618.  Doubts about the propriety of granting summary judgment or summary adjudication must be resolved in favor of the party opposing the motion.  Johnson (2006) 143 CA4th 297, 304.

 

CCP 437c(f)(1) provides:

 

“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”

 

Right Choice’s first two alternative issues for summary adjudication are oddly worded, at best.  As noted above, in the alternative to an order granting summary judgment in its favor, Right Choice seeks summary adjudication of the following issues:   

 

Issue No. 1: “Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Negligence against Right Choice has no merit because there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s claim, that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.”

 

Issue No. 2: “Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Dependent Care/Elder Abuse has no merit because there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s Claim that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.”

 

Issue No. 3: “Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Dependent Care/Elder Abuse has no merit because Plaintiff cannot establish physical abuse or neglect and recklessness, malice, oppression, or fraud as to Right Choice to prevail under the Elder Abuse Statute.”

 

Right Choice’s first two alternative issues for summary adjudication are oddly worded.  It is not clear what a finding that “there is no triable issue of material fact supporting Plaintiff’s claim that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries” would establish.  It seems that Right Choice intended its first two alternative issues for summary adjudication to be that there is no triable issue of material fact that Right Choice did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries or, in other words, that Plaintiff’s 1st cause of action for dependent adult/elder abuse and 2nd cause of action for negligence fail because Plaintiff cannot establish that Right Choice was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.    

 

However, even if these alternative issues for summary adjudication had been properly/clearly set forth, the motion would still be denied in its entirety. 

 

NEGLIGENCE

 

The elements of a negligence cause of action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care, (2) breach, (3) proximate cause and (4) damages/injury.  Ladd (1996) 12 C4th 913, 917  Berkley (2007) 152 CA4th 518, 526.  In this motion, Right Choice addresses only the element of causation.  (See Motion, p.6:4-p.8:23). 

 

With regard to causation, a plaintiff must show the defendant’s act or omission was a substantial factor in bringing about the plaintiff’s harm.  Leyva (2018) 20 CA5th 1095, 1104; Saelzler (2001) 25 C4th 763, 778; Coyle (2018) 24 CA5th 627, 645.  A plaintiff must set forth evidence which affords a reasonable basis for the conclusion that it is more likely than not that the conduct of the defendant was a cause in fact of the result.  See Ortega (2001) 26 C4th 1200, 1205.  A mere possibility that the defendant caused the injury is insufficient to defeat summary judgment.  Id.

 

Ordinarily proximate causation is a question of fact; however, where the facts are such that the only reasonable conclusion is an absence of causation, the question is one of law.  State Department of State Hospitals (2015) 61 C4th 339, 353; Constance B. (1986) 178 CA3d 200, 201.

 

Here, Right Choice’s contends that “[a]fter Plaintiff used the toilet, Ms.  Muwummuza noticed Plaintiff was wobbly and gently assisted Plaintiff to the floor of the facility’s restroom and obtained help from Pacoima ADC [HDL] staff, Mireya Delgadillo to place Plaintiff back into her wheelchair. (UMF 12.).”  (See Motion, p.3:16-19).  Right Choice also claims that “[p]rior to transferring Ms. Ginolfi back to her wheelchair, Ms. Muwummuza and Ms. Delgadillo performed a body examination of Ms. Ginolfi to check for any signs of injury before placing her back in her wheelchair. (UMF 13.) No broken skin, deformity, redness, resistance, distress, crying or any indication of any kind of injury was observed by Ms. Muwummuza and Ms. Delgadillo on the body exam of Ms. Ginolfi. (UMF 14.)”  (See Motion, p.3:19-24).  The fact that there was an out of the ordinary incident in the restroom at HDL which resulted in Plaintiff being on the floor, body exam for injuries being conduct and multiple people needed to assist Plaintiff back in her wheelchair establishes that it is more than a mere possibility that Plaintiff was injured at HDL on 8/20/19 while in being cared for by Muwummuza.  (See Right Choice’s Ex.H, I).

 

Contrary to Right Choice’s assertion, the facts do not “show that it was not until after Ms. Ginolfi left Pacoima ADC [HDL], while in the care of her private caretaker at home, did Ms. Ginolfi exhibit any identifiable signs of injury or pain.”  The surveillance videos do not have any sound; therefore, it cannot be determined from the videos whether Plaintiff cried out or made some other audible sound in pain while in the bathroom (which may have been heard in the hallway where there were cameras) or thereafter.  Additionally, the surveillance video of Plaintiff while in the “quiet room” shows Plaintiff shifting in her wheelchair which Plaintiff’s niece and conservator, Solis, who has assisted in caring for Plaintiff since 2001, opines shows signs of distress.  (See Solis Decl. ¶5).  Solis also opines that Plaintiff was calling attention to her knee/injury while in the “kitchen area” video.  Id.

 

Right Choice’s reliance on Leyva, supra, and the declaration of Theodore K. Gregorius, M.D. (Dr. Gregorious) for the proposition that the evidence establishes as a matter of law that it is medically impossible that Plaintiff suffered a left distal femur fracture while at HDL on 8/20/19 is unavailing. 

 

Leyva, supra, is distinguishable from the instant case.  There, the issue was the cause of a fire and the defendant moving for summary judgment presented the testimony of two fire investigators with expertise in determining the cause of fires.  See Leyva, supra at 1105.  Both of the experts opined that the possible causes of the fire were: (1) combustible materials (e.g., the sofa or a blanket) were placed against or too close to the wall heater by plaintiffs, or (2) the wall heater itself malfunctioned.  Id.  The plaintiffs in Leyva did not provide any evidence in opposition to the motion or a responsive separate statement, which alone is a ground for granting a motion for summary judgment.  Id. at 1100, 1105; CCP 437c(b)(3).  

 

Here, Right Choice has submitted the declaration of Dr. Gregorious, a medical doctor specializing in Orthopedic Surgery and Sports Medicine.  (Dr. Gregorious Decl. ¶1).  First, this is not a medical malpractice action where a physician’s opinion is necessary to establish the standard of care and whether another physician has met such standard and/or whether a defendant physician’s conduct did or did not cause the Plaintiff’s injury to a reasonable medical certainty.  Second, Dr. Gregorious has not established that he is a behavioral expert that is qualified to testify that Plaintiff’s behavior (viewed solely from surveillance videos without sound) after the restroom incident at HDL establishes that she was not in pain and did not suffer injury to her leg.  (See Dr. Gregorious Decl. ¶¶21-22, 24, 26). 

 

As such, even without considering the declaration of Jacob Tauber offered by Plaintiff, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Plaintiff was injured while at HDL and while being tended to by Muwummuza, Right Choice’s employee, and whether a breach of duty owed by Right Choice to Plaintiff caused such injury.  As noted above, in this motion, Right Choice does not dispute that it owed Plaintiff and duty of care or that such duty was breached.  Rather, Right Choice addresses only the element of causation.  

 

The declaration of Lina Ann Latimer, offered in support of the motion, which claims that Solis told Latimer Dustin dropped Phyllis weighs against Plaintiff’s case against Right Choice.  (See Right Choice’s Ex.M).  The declaration is insufficient to establish as a matter of law that Plaintiff was injured as a result of being dropped by Dustin and not something that occurred at HDL.  Even if Solis did make the statement to Latimer, there seems to be no dispute that Solis was not present when the injury occurred, whether it was at HDL or at home with Dustin.  As such, she may not have had all of the information and/or may have been confused when she made the statement a few days after 8/20/19.  (See Latimer Decl.).  Ms. Latimer’s statement and the credibility of Plaintiff’s expert, if his opinion is offered at trial, will all be issues for the trier of fact to consider in weighing the evidence.      

 

DEPENDENT ADULT/ELDER ABUSE

 

To recover heightened remedies under the Dependent Adult/Elder Abuse Act (the Act), a plaintiff must prove, by “clear and convincing evidence,” that a defendant is liable for either physical abuse or neglect of an elder or dependent adult and that the defendant committed the abuse with “recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.”  Welfare & Institutions Code 15610.63, 15610.57, 15657; Delaney (1999) 20 C4th 23, 31.   

 

Under the Act, neglect means “[t]he negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder or a dependent adult to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.”  Welfare & Institutions Code 15610.57(a)(1).

 

To establish recklessness, a plaintiff must show that the defendant acted with “deliberate disregard of the high degree of probability that an injury will occur.”  See Delaney, supra at 31.   “Recklessness, unlike negligence, involves more than inadvertence, incompetence, unskillfulness, or a failure to take precautions, but rather rises to the level of a conscious choice of a course of action with knowledge of the serious danger to others involved.”  Id. at 31-32.  “Acts of simple or gross negligence” are not sufficient to plead elder abuse.  Berkley (2007) 152 CA4th 518, 529.  “[A] plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.”  Covenant Care, Inc. (2004) 32 C4th 771, 789.

 

Right Choice’s argument that the dependent adult/elder abuse claim fails for lack of causation fails for the same reasons set forth above with regard to the negligence cause of action.

 

Alternatively, Right Choice argues that the cause of action fails because Plaintiff cannot establish physical abuse or neglect and recklessness, oppression, fraud or malice.  Under the Act, “neglect refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults...to carry out their custodial obligations.”  Carter (2011) 198 CA4th 396, 404; Welfare & Institutions Code 15610.57(a). 

 

One of the surveillance video clips shows: (1) Plaintiff being wheeled into the restroom, where there is no video surveillance, by one individual, presumably Muwummuza and Plaintiff seemingly being left alone as the motion admits that Muwummuza had to summon others to assist her to put Plaintiff back in her wheelchair after she used the restroom; (2) several individuals who seem to work at or for HDL are in the hallway outside the restroom; (3) the individual who wheeled Plaintiff into the restroom, presumably Muwummuza, re-entering the restroom; and (4) thereafter three more individuals being summoned and entering the restroom before Plaintiff is wheeled back out in her wheelchair.  Considering Plaintiff’s known disabilities and the need for assistance while using the restroom, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Muwummuza, Right Choice’s employee, acted recklessly in leaving Plaintiff alone in the restroom.   

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied in its entirety.

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On 12/26/23, Shew and Van filed and served a demurrer, with a 3/8/24 hearing date, to the 1st – 5th and 7th and 8th causes of action in the complaint.  On 2/22/24, Voskanian, Keryan and Five Star filed their First Amended Complaint for: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Accounting, (6) Declaratory Relief, (7) Intentional Interference, (8) Negligent Interference, (9) Assault and (10) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

The Court notes that Shew and Van have not filed a response to the First Amended Complaint (i.e., neither an answer nor demurrer to the First Amended Complaint).

 

ANALYSIS

 

The notice of demurrer indicates that it is based on CCP 430.10(e), (f) and (g).  (See Notice of Demurrer, p.2:2-9).  The demurrer document indicates that Shew and Van demurrer to the 1st, 5th, 7th and 9th causes of action solely on the ground that the 1st, 5th, 7th and 9th causes of action fail to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action, CCP 430.10(e).  (Demurrer, p.3:5-23).  However, the demurrer document incorrectly identifies the 1st cause of action as Negligence (Legal Malpractice), the 5th cause of action as Negligence (Legal Malpractice) and incorrectly refers to it as the 2nd cause of action, the 7th cause of action as Breach of Contract and incorrectly refers to it as the 3rd cause of action, and the 9th cause of action as Breach of Contract and incorrectly refers to it as the 3rd cause of action.  Id.

 

1st cause of action – Fraud – Promise Without Intent to Perform

 

The elements of a fraud cause of action are: (1) false representation by defendant, (2) intent to deceive or induce reliance, (3) justifiable reliance by plaintiff, (4) resulting damages.  Service by Medallion, Inc. (1996) 44 CA4th 1807, 1816.  Fraud claims must be pled with specific facts showing how, when, where, to whom and by what means the misrepresentations were made.  Lazar (1996) 12 C4th 631, 645. 

 

A promise made without intending to perform it, or promissory fraud, is actionable as fraud.  Engalla (1997) 15 C4th 951, 973.  The elements of a claim for promissory fraud are: (1) a promise made regarding a material fact without any intention of performing it; (2) the existence of the intent not to perform at the time the promise was made; (3) intent to deceive or induce the promisee to enter into a transaction; (4) reasonable reliance by the promisee; (5) nonperformance by the party making the promise; and (6) resulting damage to the promisee.  Gruber (2020) 48 CA5th 529, 540 citing Behnke (2011) 196 CA4th 1443, 1453.

 

Contrary to the assertions in the demurrer, Shew and Van have pled each of the necessary elements with the required factual specificity.  (See Cross-Complaint ¶¶28-32).

 

 

 

5th cause of action – Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Every contract includes an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing that neither party will do anything to interfere with the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement.  Howard (2010) 187 CA4th 498, 528 citing Restatement 2d Contracts, §205.  The exact nature and extent of the duty depends on the nature and purpose of the underlying contract and the parties’ legitimate expectations arising from the contract.  Howard, supra.  Generally, breach of the implied covenant gives rise only to contract damages; however, in the insurance context, breach of the implied covenant is also actionable in tort allowing for the recovery of extracontractual damages.  See Foley (1988) 47 C3d 654, 683-684; State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. (1970) 9 CA3d 508, 528; California Capital Insurance Co. (C.D. California 2020) 472 F.Supp.3d 754, 758-761; Chu (2021) 60 CA5th 346, 356-357.

 

Here, the breach of implied covenant claim seeks the same damages and the breach of contract claim.  As such, it is duplicative and unnecessary.  Since this case does not involve an insurance contract between the parties, the defect in the breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cause of action cannot be cured by amendment.    

 

7th cause of action – Intentional Interference

 

The elements of an intentional interference with contract cause of action are: (1) a valid contract between plaintiff and a third party; (2) defendant’s knowledge of this contract; (3) defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.  Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (1990) 50 C3d 1118, 1126.

 

Contrary to Voskanian and Keryan’s contention, the cross-complaint sufficiently alleges their interference with contractual relationships between Shew and Van and third parties.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶14(a)-(b), 60-61).  The cross-complaint also sufficiently alleges the remaining elements of the claim.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶62-67).

 

9th cause of action – Declaratory Relief

 

When an actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the parties to a contract arises, a person interested under the contract may seek a declaration from the court of his or her rights or duties under the contract.  See CCP 1060.  Such remedies are cumulative and do not preclude additional relief based on the same facts.  CCP 1062.

 

Here, the cross-complaint sufficiently sets forth the basis for a declaratory relief cause of action.  Since Voskanian and Keryan also make a claim for declaratory relief in their First Amended Complaint, they cannot now deny the existence of an actual controversy.  See Ludgate Insurance Co. (2000) 82 CA4th 592, 604. 

 

CONCLUSION

 

The demurrer is overruled as to the 1st, 7th and 9th causes of action and sustained without leave to amend as to the 5th cause of action.  Answer is due within 30 days.
