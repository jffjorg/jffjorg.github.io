DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01458    Hearing Date: April 8, 2024    Dept: W

KIMBERLY DAVIS, et al. v. PAIGE HEMMIS, et al.

 

COMPASS CALIFORNIA, INC.’S MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFFS’ VERIFIED THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT

Date of Hearing:        April 8, 2024                          Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV01458

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Compass California, Inc. dba Compass

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Cortes Decl. ¶4)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis filed a third amended complaint against Defendants Paige Hemmis, Jason Short, Ameka Benton, Scott Fields, Hallmark Cards, Inc., Crown Media Holdings, Inc., Paige Hemmis, Inc., Aria Music Group, The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners Association, Melissa Improta, Jason Improta, and Compass California, Inc. for 1) Intentional Interference with Contract; 2) Negligence; 3) Trespass; 4) Violations of Civil Code § 1798.93; 5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6) Professional Negligence; 7) Unfair Business Practices; 8) Fraudulent Concealment; 9) Negligent Misrepresentation; 10) Fraud; 11) Slander of Title; 12) Constructive Fraud; 13) Wrongful Eviction; 14) Quiet Title; and 15) Conversion.

 

Plaintiffs allege about September 15, 2019, when Plaintiffs were absent from the subject property, Defendants Hemmis and Short unlawfully entered upon the subject property and remained in possession of the property until early July 2022. While Defendants Hemmis and Short were in possession of the property, Plaintiffs were engaged in a dispute with their mortgage lender, US Bank, related to a settlement agreement that was entered into to address the pay off of a mortgage loan. Plaintiffs further allege at some point prior to September 12, 2019, Defendants Hemmis, Short, Improta and Compass engaged in a conspiracy to attempt to steal title to the Plaintiffs’ home. On this point, Plaintiffs claim Hemmis and Short fraudulently forged a lease that purported to have Kimberly Davis' signature on it. As a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged. Plaintiff dismissed Defendants Ameka Benton and Scott Fields.

 

On March 24, 2023, this action was related to Kimberly Davis v. Nationstar, et al. (23VECV01125).

 

On August 18, 2023, Paige Hemmis and Jason Short filed a cross-complaint against Kimberly Ann Davis, Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta, and Jason Improta for breach of contract, money had and received, implied indemnification, express indemnification, and comparative contribution. Cross-Complainants dismissed their cause of action for express indemnification.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Compass California, Inc. dba Compass’ Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs request this court take judicial notice of the May 5, 2023 Ruling on the Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Complaint and the Third Amended Complaint filed February 6, 2024.

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §452(d).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Compass California, Inc. dba Compass moves the court for an order striking several portions of the complaint regarding punitive damages against Compass.

 

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the¿defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.) The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Id.) A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged, if proven, would not support a finding that the defendant acted with malice, fraud or oppression. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California (2010) 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63.)¿¿ 

 

“An employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages ..., based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct.... With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, [or] ratification ... must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civil Code § 3294.)

 

Defendant Compass argues the allegations against them assert conclusory statements that Compass ratified the Improtas alleged malicious, fraudulent, and oppressive misconduct. Specifically, there are no facts alleged to support one of Compass’ officers, directors, or managing agents ratified alleged fraudulent conduct of the Improtas or had any knowledge that the person with whom the Improtas were communicating with was not the actual owner of the subject property. Nor are there facts alleged to support Compass in some way ratified or authorized the alleged failure to verify the identity of the owner of the property. According to Compass, the allegations “Compass' conduct was unquestionably intended to harm the Plaintiff by permitting Ms. Hemmis to unlawfully occupy the property, as part of her scheme with Compass to attempt to take title to the property and/or potentially extract case from Plaintiffs' lender[]” merely alleges a type of harm without any facts to suggest the intent to cause said harm and is therefore a conclusory statement. As a result, Defendant Compass contends there can be no allegation that Compass ratified any alleged fraudulent conduct because there was no fraudulent conduct to ratify.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the TAC properly alleges that an officer, director, and/or managing agent (Matt Ogden) was made fully aware of the Improta's fraud before the parties entered into the 2019 lease. Specifically, the complaint plainly alleges that Mr. Ogden was aware that the Improtas failed to verify the identity of the owner of the property at the time that the lease was signed, but decided to proceed with the transaction anyway. Plaintiffs further note the complaint alleges that another director at Compass, Kathy Mehringer, was made aware of the Improta’s and Mr. Ogden’s misconduct and did nothing to discipline either the Improta’s or Mr. Ogden. Accordingly, Plaintiffs contend the jury could certainly infer that the fact that a very sophisticated real estate brokerage would most certainly not have simply forgot to check the identity of someone they were involved in a complicated lease, with a right of first refusal to buy of a multi-million dollar home, but that they did so intentionally, thereby disregarding the Plaintiff’s rights, with their motive to profit off of the commission for finalizing the lease and the substantial additional profit that they could hope to earn in the future.

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages for the purposes of a motion to strike. The allegations are much more than conclusory as Plaintiffs have alleged an officer, director and/or managing agent spoke with the Improtas about never having met or spoken to Plaintiffs but continued to facilitate the transaction in order to receive a commission. The complaint further alleges another director at Compass was made aware of the Improta’s and Mr. Ogden’s misconduct yet took no action and therefore, ratified the fraudulent conduct. These allegations are just sufficient enough to support a claim against Compass for punitive damages.  

 

Accordingly, Defendant Compass’ Motion to Strike is DENIED.
