DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies). 


Case Number: 22CHCV0108    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: F49

through 60

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT F49

AUGUST 19, 2024

MOTION TO STAY PROCEEDINGS

Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 22CHCV01086

Motion filed: 6/26/24

MOVING PARTY: Defendant/Cross-Defendant William S. Hart Union High School District

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Jane Doe M.K.

NOTICE: OK.

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order granting William S. Hart Union High School District’s Motion to Stay Proceedings in this matter pending the decisions of two matters currently before the Court of Appeals regarding the constitutionality of California Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218”).

RECOMMENDED RULING: The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This is a childhood sexual abuse case.

On November 7, 2022, Plaintiff Jane Doe M.K. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Doe 1, a public entity; Doe 2, an individual employed by Doe 1, and Does 3 through 60. Subsequently, Plaintiff filed amendments to the Complaint on April 7 and June 20, 20, 2023, substituting Defendant/Cross-Defendant William S. Hart Union High School District (“District” or “Defendant/Cross-Defendant”) for Doe 1 and Defendant Martin John Spalding (“Spalding” or “Defendant/Cross-Complainant”) for Doe 2, respectively. District and Spalding filed their respective Answers to the Complaint on May 17, and July 26, 2023, respectively.

On July 26, 2023, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Spalding filed a Cross-Complaint against Defendant/Cross-Defendant District, and Roes 1 to 100, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Implied Equitable Indemnity, (2) Total Equitable Indemnity, (3) Apportionment; Contribution and Partial Indemnity, (4) Declaratory Relief, and (5) Apportionment of Fault. District filed its Answer to the Cross-Complaint on August 25, 2023.

On June 26, 2024, District filed the instant Motion to Stay Proceedings. Subsequently, Plaintiff filed her Opposition on August 6, 2024, and District replied on August 12, 2024.

ANALYSIS

The court has the inherent authority to “amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a)(8).)

Courts “have an inherent obligation to decide cases properly brought before them[.]” (Top Ins. Co. v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Companies (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1331, 1345.) “Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.” (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.) They may also exercise their inherent power to “prevent wrongful use of process….” (Western Steel & Ship Repair, Inc. v. RMI, Inc. (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 1108, 1116.)

A. Request for Judicial Notice

Case Number: 23CHCV02664    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: F49

Motion filed: 4/3/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant General Motors LLC (“GM”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Robert Henderson (“Plaintiff” or “Henderson”)

NOTICE: OK.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order to grant GM’s demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint, and to strike the prayer for punitive damages.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The demurrer is OVERRULED. The motion to strike is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a Song-Beverly Act case. Plaintiff filed this action over alleged defects in their 2018 Chevrolet Silverado (the “Subject Vehicle”), which was manufactured by Defendant GM. Plaintiff purchased the Subject Vehicle on August 29, 2018, from Chevrolet of Montebello, an authorized dealership of GM. (FAC. ¶ 6.)

 

On September 5, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this action. Subsequently, on April 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed their operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) in response to a demurrer filed by GM on November 3, 2023, resulting in GM’s withdrawal of its demurrer. The FAC alleges the following causes of action: (1) Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, (2) Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, (3) Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, (4) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civ. Code, §§ 1791.1, 1794, and 1795.5), and (5) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment.

 

On May 17, 2024, GM filed the instant Demurrer (the “Demurrer”) with Motion to Strike (the “Motion”). Subsequently, on August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed their Opposition. On August 12, 2024, GM replied.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal 3d 311, 318.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered.

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.) 

 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.) 

 

A.    Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A party filing a demurrer “shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a).) A failure to meet and confer does not constitute grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., sections 430.41 (a)(4).)

 

            Here, GM’s counsel has not provided a declaration stating that any meet and confer efforts were made to resolve the issues raised in the Demurrer regarding the FAC filed on April 18, 2024. GM’s counsel’s declaration only states that he spoke with Plaintiff’s counsel to meet and confer regarding the initial Complaint; however, based on his extensive experience with Plaintiff’s counsel’s firm, he believed that the parties would reach an impasse again. (Kay Decl. ¶ 2.)

 

            Consequently, the Court concludes that the meet and confer requirement has not been satisfied.

 

B.     Statute of Limitations

 

The statute of limitations for an action for relief on the ground of fraud or mistake is three years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) “The cause of action in that case is not deemed to have accrued until the discovery, by the aggrieved party, of the facts constituting the fraud or mistake.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).)

 

“A close cousin of the discovery rule is the ‘well accepted principle ... of fraudulent concealment.’” (Bernson v. Browing-Ferris Industries (1994) 7 Cal.4th 926, citing Sanchez v. South Hoover Hospital (1976) 18 Cal.3d 93, 99.) “It has long been established that the defendant's fraud in concealing a cause of action against him tolls the applicable statute of limitations, but only for that period during which the claim is undiscovered by plaintiff or until such time as plaintiff, by the exercise of reasonable diligence, should have discovered it.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges that the GM concealed material facts related to the Subject Vehicle, specifically that “its 8-speed transmission were defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure.” (FAC ¶ 61.) The FAC further alleges that “GM continued to conceal the defect and its inability to repair it.... For example, Defendant GM did not disclose its wrongdoing nor offer to rectify it during successive repair visits.” (Id. ¶ 71.) 

 

Moreover, the FAC details these successive repair visits, beginning with the earliest on “January 4, 2022, with approximately 37,913 miles on the odometer, Plaintiff presented the Subject Vehicle to Defendant’s authorized repair facility with various concerns, including transmission concerns.” (FAC ¶ 21.)

 

            GM argues that the FAC conceded the alleged defects were discovered during the warranty period, thereby Plaintiff cannot sustain his burden of demonstrating that he did not discover alleged fraudulent concealment within the applicable limitations period.

 

However, this argument is unpersuasive. Even if it is assumed that Plaintiff should have discovered the defects by the exercise of reasonable diligence, the earliest alleged repair occurred on January 4, 2022 (FAC ¶ 21), which would mark the commencement of the three-year statutory of limitations. Given that this action was initiated on September 5, 2023, it is clear that it falls within the limitations period.

 

            Consequently, the Court concludes that the statute of limitations does not provide a basis for sustaining the demurrer.

 

C.    Fifth Cause of Action – Fraudulant Inducement - Concealment

 

“As with all fraud claims, the necessary elements of a concealment/suppression claim consist of ‘“(1) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); (3) intent to defraud (i.e., to induce reliance); (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage.”’” (Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1185–1186.) Suppression of a material fact is actionable when there is a duty of disclosure, which may arise from a relationship between the parties, such as a buyer-seller relationship. (Id. at pp. 1186–1187.) Fraud, including concealment, must be pleaded with specificity. (Linear Technology Corp. v. Applied Materials, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 115, 132.)

           

In Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828 (review granted [“Superior courts may, in the exercise of their discretion, choose to follow a published review-granted Court of Appeal opinion.” Comment, par. 2, concerning Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(e)(3)]), the Court of Appeal concluded that the plaintiff’s claim is adequately pleaded by alleging: the CVT transmissions installed in numerous Nissan vehicles (including the one plaintiffs purchased) were defective; Nissan knew of the defects and the hazards they posed; Nissan had exclusive knowledge of the defects but intentionally concealed and failed to disclose that information; Nissan intended to deceive plaintiffs by concealing known transmission problems; plaintiffs would not have purchased the car if they had known of the defects; and plaintiffs suffered damages in the form of money paid to purchase the car. (Id. at p. 844.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges that Defendant GM committed fraud by allowing the Subject Vehicle to be sold to Plaintiff without disclosing that the Subject Vehicle and its 8-speed transmission were defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure. (FAC ¶ 61.) GM was well aware and knew that “the 8-speed transmission had one or more defects that can result in various problems, including, but not limited to hard or harsh shifts, jerking, lurching, hesitation on acceleration, surging and/or inability to control the vehicle’s speed, acceleration, or deceleration (“Transmission Defect”). These conditions present a safety hazard and are unreasonably dangerous to consumers because they can suddenly and unexpectedly cause the driver to be unable to control the speed and acceleration/deceleration of the vehicle. Such unexpected inability to control the vehicle’s speed and acceleration/deceleration thereby exposes Plaintiff and his passengers (along with other drivers who share the road or garage with Plaintiff) to a serious risk of accident and injury.” (Id. ¶ 63.) GM was in a superior position from various internal sources to know (or should have known) the true state of facts about the material defects contained in vehicles equipped with the 8-speed transmission. (Id. ¶ 72.) Defendant concealed the Transmission Defect with the intent to induce Plaintiff to purchase Subject Vehicle. (Id. ¶ 77.) Had Plaintiff known that the Vehicle suffered from the Transmission Defect, Plaintiff would not have purchased the Vehicle. (Id. ¶ 69.)

 

 Based on the above records, the Court concludes that at the pleading stage, Plaintiff allegations are sufficient.

 

Conversely, GM contends that it had no duty to disclose to Plaintiff because there existed no fiduciary relationship, nor has Plaintiff offered support for an exception to this requirement. (Reply at p. 3.) As shown below, the Court finds this contention lacks merit.

 

1)      Duty to Disclose Material Facts

 

“[T]o establish fraud through nondisclosure or concealment of facts, it is necessary to show the defendant ‘was under a legal duty to disclose them.’”  (OCM Principal Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 835, 845.)  “‘There are four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.’”  (Hoffman v. 162 N. Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 1178, 1186.)  “A relationship between the parties is present if there is ‘some sort of transaction between the parties.’”  (Ibid.) (Underlines added.)

 

A duty to disclose that is based upon a “transaction must necessarily arise from direct dealings between the plaintiff and the defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 312.)  Even where the defendant has volunteered information, it does not have a further obligation to correct half-truths where no sufficient relationship or transaction exists.  (Id. at p.312.)  Simply speaking does not give rise to a duty to disclose.  (Ibid.)  In Bigler-Engler, the court noted that the manufacturer in that case was not involved “in any way” with the patient, and that there was no evidence that the manufacturer “directly advertised its products to consumers such as” the patient.  (Id. at p.314.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that he purchased the Subject Vehicle from a GM dealership, that GM backed the car with an express warranty (FAC ¶ 6). In light of these allegations, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s claim is not barred on the ground there was no relationship requiring GM to disclose known defects.

 

Furthermore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has adequately alleged a duty to disclose based on Defendant’s superior knowledge. To plead that a defendant had a duty to disclose a material fact, a plaintiff must allege that “the defendant has exclusive knowledge of material facts not known or reasonably accessible to the plaintiff.” (See Collins v. eMachines, Inc. (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 249, 255.) Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant GM was in a superior position from various internal sources to know (or should have known) the true state of facts about the material defects contained in vehicles equipped with the 8-speed transmission; and Plaintiff could not reasonably have been expected to learn of or discover the Vehicle’s Transmission Defect and its potential consequences until well after Plaintiff purchased the Vehicle.” (FAC ¶ 72.) “Defendant GM acquired its knowledge of the Transmission Defect and its potential consequences prior

to Plaintiff acquiring the subject vehicle, though sources not available to consumers such as Plaintiff, including but not limited to pre- production testing data, early consumer complaints about the Transmission Defect made directly to Defendant GM and its network of dealers, aggregate warranty data compiled from GM’s network of dealers, testing conducted by Defendant GM in response to these complaints, as well as warranty repair and part replacements data received by Defendant GM from Defendant GM’s network of dealers, amongst

other sources of internal information.”

 

            Consequently, Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient to establish that GM had a legal duty to disclose the alleged defects due to its exclusive knowledge of material facts that were not known to the Plaintiff. While a fiduciary relationship between GM and Plaintiff is one sufficient condition to establish such a duty, it is not required in this case.

           

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Fifth Cause of Action in the FAC.

 

D.    Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

In its Motion to Strike, GM seeks to strike the following portions from the Prayer for Relief in the FAC:

 

            Paragraph g: “For punitive damages.”

 

            GM moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages from the FAC on the grounds that (1) the FAC has not alleged a viable fraud claim, (2) the punitive damages are not available under the Song-Beverly Act, and (3) the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to support punitive damages. (Mot. at p. 3.)

 

Firstly, given that the Court has concluded that the allegations for the fraud claim in the FAC are sufficient at this stage of pleading, it consequently finds that GM’s contention on the sufficiency does not form a basis to grant a motion to strike.

 

The Court proceeds to examine the remaining basis that GM replied upon.

 

1)      Punitive Damages and Song-Beverly Act Civil Penalties

 

GM argues that the statute limits recovery to a refund of the purchase price paid and payable plus a Civil Penalty under certain circumstances, citing that “the penalty under section 1794(c), like other civil penalties, is imposed as punishment or deterrence of the defendant, rather than to compensate the plaintiffs. In this, it is akin to punitive damages.” (Kwan v. Mercedes-Benz of N. America (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 174, 184.) GM further contends that “by seeking a ‘civil penalty’ and also attorney’s fees and all reasonable expenses as allowed by Civil Code section 1794, plaintiffs had in effect elected to waive punitive damages under section 3294,” citing Troensegaard v. Silvercrest Industries, Inc. (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 218, 228.)

 

            “The appropriate inquiry should be focused on the underlying conduct.” (Anderson v. Ford Motor Co. (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 946, 965 (Anderson).) The Anderson Court stated, “Our reading of Troensegaard is that the court’s holding was based on conduct that was the same, i.e., identical, not just ‘substantially’ the same. And that conduct tool place at the same time, post-sale during the warranty repair process.” (Ibid.) The Anderson court concluded that punitive damages and the Song-Beverly Act civil penalty were both properly awarded to the plaintiffs, reasoning that the punitive damages and statutory penalties in that case were based on different conducts that took place at different times – the punitive damage were based on conduct underlying the fraud cause of action and took place before the sale whereas the civil penalty was based on defendant’s post-sale failure to comply with its Song-Beverly Act obligations to replace the vehicle or make restitution when reasonable attempts to repair has failed. (Id. at p. 966.)

 

            Here, in the operative FAC, under the Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that GM induced him to purchase the Subject Vehicle equipped with GM’s 8-speed transmission by concealing that the Subject Vehicle and its 8-speed transmission were defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure. (FAC ¶ 61.) GM was well aware and knew that the 8-speed transmission installed on the Subject Vehicle was defective but failed to disclose this fact to Plaintiff prior to the sale. (Id. ¶ 62.) Had Plaintiff known that the Subject Vehicle suffered from the Transmission Defect, Plaintiff would not have purchased the Subject Vehicle. (Id. ¶ 69.)

 

            Consequently, in line with the precedent set forth in Anderson, the Court concludes that the recovery of punitive damage is not prohibited because the underlying conduct for the remedy is based on the Fraudulent Inducement -Concealment cause of action, distinct from the conduct alleged for violations of Song-Beverly Act.

 

            As a result, GM’s argument that the pursuit of civil penalty under the Sang-Beverly Act necessarily precludes recovery of punitive damage is not well-founded.

 

2)      Pleading Standards for Punitive Damages

 

GM argues that the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to support punitive damages.

 

Under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff may recover punitive damages “where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)

 

Case law further establishes that punitive damages may be awarded where a defendant fraudulently induces the plaintiff to enter into a contract. (Kuchta v. Allied Builders Corp. (1971) 21 Cal.App.3d 541, 549.)

 

Additionally, “[t]he words ‘oppression, fraud, or malice’ in Civil Code section 3294 being in the disjunctive, fraud alone is an adequate basis for awarding punitive damages.” (Glendale Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Marina View Heights Dev. Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 101, 135.)

           

Given that the Court has previously found that the allegations in the FAC are sufficient at this stage of pleading to state a fraud claim, it concludes that the allegations also provide an adequate basis for punitive damages.

 

Therefore, GM’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant General Motors LLC’s Demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendant’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

Defendant General Motors LLC is ordered to file and serve its Answer to the First Amended Complaint within 20 days.

NOTICE: OK.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order from this Court deeming each matter specified in the Moving Defendant’s Request for Admissions, Set One, as admitted, and imposing monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her attorney of record, in the amount of $2,610.00. 

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is GRANTED. The request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED IN PART.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 13, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this action. Subsequently, on January 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed her operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Emily J. Simanian D.D.S. (“Simanian”) and Emily J. Simanian D.D.S, Inc. d/b/a Golden Oak Dental Group Practice of Emil J Simanian DDS Inc (“GODGP” or the “Moving Defendant”) (collectively “Defendants”), and Does 1 to 25, alleging (1) Medical Malpractice, (2) General Negligence, and (3) Negligence Hiring, Training, and Supervision. Subsequently, Defendants filed their Answer to the Complaint on February 29, 2024. 

 

On May 23, 2024, GODGP filed the instant Motion to Deem Admitted Request for Admissions, Set One (the “Motion”). Additionally, it requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel.

No Opposition or Reply papers have been received by the Court. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)¿¿ 

 

If a party to whom request for admissions is served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)¿¿ 

 

A.    Motion to Deem Admitted Request for Admissions, Set One

 

Moving Defendant’s counsel (“Counsel”) attests that on February 29, 2024, it served Plaintiff with the first set of Request for Admissions, to which responses were due on April 1, 2024. (Conley Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. “A.”) Having not received any responses by the due date, Counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a written meet and confer letter on April 30, 2024, demanding Plaintiff to serve responses, without objection, by May 6, 2024. (Id. ¶ 4, Ex. “B.”) Subsequently, on May 2, 2024, in response to Plaintiff’s request for an extension of two weeks, Counsel agreed to grant a one-week extension. (Id. ¶ 6.) However, no responses had been received as of May 21, 2024. (Id. ¶ 7.)   

 

Given the absence of any Opposition from Plaintiff and the records above, the Court finds that Plaintiff has failed to serve a timely response, thereby waiving any objection to the interrogatories, including those based on privilege or the protection for work product, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (a).

 

Consequently, the Court shall make this order, deeming the genuineness of any documents and truth of any matters specified in the requests to be admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b) and (c).)

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Motion.

 

B.     Monetary Sanctions

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c), “It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”

 

Here, Plaintiff did not provide responses to the Request for Admissions, Set One, nor file an Opposition to the Motion. Accordingly, based on the above records and authorities, the Court finds that the mandatory monetary sanctions are applicable.

 

Utilizing a lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work in preparing the Motion is $637.50, calculated at a reasonable hourly rate of $425.00 for one and half hours reasonably spent.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS IN PART the Moving Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Emil J. Simanian D.D.S., Inc. d/b/a Golden Oak Dental Group Practice of Emil J Simanian DDS Inc’s Motion to Deem Admitted Request for Admissions, Set One, is GRANTED. 

 

Defendant Emil J. Simanian D.D.S., Inc. d/b/a Golden Oak Dental Group Practice of Emil J Simanian DDS Inc’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED IN PART. 

 

Plaintiff Liliana Castillo Onofre and her attorney of record are ordered to jointly and severally pay $637.50 to Defendant Emil J. Simanian D.D.S., Inc. d/b/a Golden Oak Dental Group Practice of Emil J Simanian DDS Inc’s counsel within 20 days.
