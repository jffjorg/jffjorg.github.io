Case Number: 20STCV21873    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL 

Hearing Date: 5/15/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV21873 NEOME VELASQUEZ vs WHOLE FOODS MARKET GROUP, INC.

Moving Party: Defendant Schwartz Investment Company

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard¿and Analysis

 

Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).)¿ The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).) The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial.¿ (See generally, CRC Rule 3.1332(d)(1)-(11).)¿ Additional factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1332(c), (d).)¿¿A trial court cannot refuse to hear a summary judgment motion filed within the time limits of California Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) § 437c. (Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 918, 919.)

On June 10, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present premises liability case. The current trial date is set for October 21, 2024. Defendant Schwartz Investment Company has a Motion for Summary Judgment (MSJ) scheduled for a hearing on December 17, 2024, after the trial date. No opposition to the request to continue has been filed.

Continuing the trial date so Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment can be heard constitutes good cause. Defendant requests a continuance to sometime in March 2025. However, given the age of the case and the four previous continuances, the Court is disinclined to grant any further continuances beyond the statutory requirement, allowing Defendant’s MSJ to be heard 30 days before trial.

 

Since Defendant’s sole basis for continuing the trial is so its MSJ can be heard and Defendant did not discuss the various factors to continue/reopen fact discovery deadline; therefore, fact discovery does not follow the new trial date.

 

The new trial date will be January 20, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. and all pretrial dates, except fact discovery deadline, are continued in accordance with the new trial date.¿ The Final Status Conference is continued to _______, at 10:00 a.m.

 

PLEASE TAKE NOTICE:¿ 

 

If a party intends to submit on this tentative ruling,¿the party must send an email to the court at¿sscdept27@lacourt.org¿with the Subject line “SUBMIT” followed by the case number.¿ The body of the email must include the hearing date and time, counsel’s contact information, and the identity of the party submitting.

 

Unless¿all¿parties submit by email to this tentative ruling, the parties should arrange to appear remotely (encouraged) or in person for oral argument.¿ You should assume that others may appear at the hearing to argue.

 

If the parties neither submit nor appear at hearing, the Court may take the motion off calendar or adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court.¿ After the Court has issued a tentative ruling, the Court may prohibit the withdrawal of the subject motion.

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED

 

Background

On June 9, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present slip and fall action, alleging that on the morning of June 1, 2019, Defendant’s property manager, Obioma Iwuoma, mopped the stairs, causing them to become slippery. As Plaintiff descended the stairs, he slipped and sustained injuries. He brought this action alleging premises liability and negligence.  Defendant Normandie Portfolio II now moves the court for summary judgment on the basis that Plaintiff cannot prove actual or constructive notice nor that it was Obioma Iwuoma who created the puddle of water that caused the fall.

Evidentiary Ruling

 

Defendant’s objection to lines 17 and 18 of the declaration of Dashida Gordon is sustained on the basis that it lacks foundation and is speculative. The Parties did not make any other objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice.

 

Legal Standard 

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). "When the motion for summary judgment is supported by affidavits sufficient to sustain the motion,…the burden of showing that triable issues exist shifts to the party opposing the motion. [Citation.] A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture…but instead must produce admissible evidence raising a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)

 

Premises Liability and Negligence

The elements of causes of action for premises liability and negligence are the same: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal. App. 4th 994, 998 (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal. 4th 1200, 1205; Civil Code § 1714(a)). “As a general rule, each person has a duty to use ordinary care and is liable for injuries caused by his failure to exercise reasonable care in the circumstances.” (Romero v. Superior Court (2001) 89 Cal. App. 4th 1068.)

 Because a landowner is not the insurer of a visitor’s safety, the owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is key to establishing liability. (Hall v. Aurora Loan Servs., LLC (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1134, 1139, Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206.)

In the ordinary slip and fall case, as in Ortega, the cause of the dangerous condition is not necessarily linked to an employee. Consequently, there is no issue of respondeat superior. Where, however, “the evidence is such that a reasonable inference can be drawn that the condition was created by employees of the [defendant], then [the defendant] is charged with notice of the dangerous condition.” (Getchell v. Jewelry, 203 (2012) Cal.App.4th 381, 385 citing (Oldham, supra, 85 Cal.App.2d at pp. 218–219; Hatfield v. Levy Bros. (1941) 18 Cal.2d 798, 806.))

Plaintiff alleges that an employee of the Defendants created the slippery condition on the stairs. In such a case, Plaintiff need not prove actual or constructive notice because if the condition was created by employees of the [defendant], then [the defendant] is charged with notice of the dangerous condition (Getchell v. Jewelry, 203 (2012) Cal.App.4th 381, 385.) Therefore, the question of notice is essentially the same as the question of causation: whether the alleged water on the stairs was created by Defendants’ employee, Obioma Iwuoma.

Defendant's Initial Burden

Defendant has met its initial burden in showing that the water that allegedly caused Plaintiff’s fall was not created by Iwuoma. Iwuoma declared that he inspected and cleaned the common area where Plaintiff fell one hour before the incident. During the inspection, he did not observe any puddles, spillage, or liquid substances on the staircase. (Iwuoma Decl. ¶ 3.) Iwuoma further declares that in the approximately one hour between his inspection and cleaning of the stairwell and the incident, he did not receive any reports of liquid spillage, puddles, or any issues with the staircase from anyone. (Iwuoma Decl. ¶ 6.)

Defendant also introduced Plaintiff’s testimony where Plaintiff testified that seven minutes prior to his fall, when he walked up the same set of stairs, he did not observe the stairs to be wet. (Wiltz Depo. 43:9-14.) Plaintiff further testified that although he observed water on the stairs about an inch deep when he fell, did not know where the water came from, how long the water had been there, and confirmed that he did not see the water seven minutes before when going up the same set of stairs. (Wiltz Depo. 59:4-17.)

Plaintiff’s Burden

Plaintiff attempts to create a triable issue of fact by arguing that Iwuoma did not dry mop the stairs after he wet mopped the floors in the morning, resulting in the water puddle that caused Plaintiff to fall. Plaintiff mainly relies on two pieces of evidence in support of his contention regarding the issue of causation: Plaintiff’s declaration and Dashida Gordan’s declaration. Plaintiff’s declaration states only that he slipped on water; he does not state where the water came from or that Iwuoma created the puddle/water. The Declaration of Dashida Gordan states, in pertinent parts, that “Earlier that morning, I saw Obi mopping the lobby on the ground floor of the building.” (Gordan Dec. par. 7).  She then states that “[b[efore Mr. Wiltz fell, I slipped and almost fell because 'Obi' kept leaving things wet when he mopped and didn't dry-mop afterward. I complained to him, and he promised he would, but I never saw him dry-mop, and I don't believe that he ever did. I saw water other times on the steps, even after I complained and before Mr. Wiltz fell." (Gordan Decl. ¶ 8.)

The Gordan declaration does not state what she observed Iwuoma do in relation to the stairs on the day of the incident. Gordan states only that she believes Iwuoma did not dry mop and left water behind based on prior incidents. In a motion for summary judgment, the testimony or evidence must be based on specific facts and observations. It is axiomatic that an inference may not be based on suspicion alone, or on imagination, speculation, surmise, conjecture, or guesswork (Estate of Gutierrez (1961) 189 Cal. App. 2d 165, 173.)

Gordan’s inference that the puddle of water that caused Plaintiff to fall was created by Iwuoma is based solely on the fact that Iwuoma had previously failed to dry mop. This assertion is not based on Gordan’s observation on the day of the incident and fails to account for other possible sources of the water. Without any additional other evidence to support this inference, the Court would consider Gordan’s declaration speculative and insufficient to overcome Plaintiff’s own testimony that he did not observe the water 7 minutes before the incident when going up the same set of stairs and that he does not know where the water came from or Iwuoma’s declaration stating that he inspected the stairs after mopping and did not see any puddles to create a triable issue of fact. Thus, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Unopposed

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUBSTITUTION IS CONDITIONALLY GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

“A pending action or proceeding does not abate by the death of a party if the cause of action survives.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.21.) “On motion after the death of a person who commenced an action or proceeding, the court shall allow a pending action or proceeding that does not abate to be continued by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.31.)

 

The person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating: (1) The decedent’s name, (2) The date and place of the decedent’s death, (3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate,” (4) If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor in interest, (5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof, (6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding,” and (7) the statements are true, under penalty of perjury. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32.)

 

A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (c).)

 

Discussion

On July 21, 2021, Plaintiffs filed the present car accident case arising out of a two-vehicle collision that occurred on December 18, 2019, in Hollywood. Plaintiff Mr. Man Hwa Yim, age 90, now deceased, and his wife, 83-year-old Chang Suk Kim, a front-seat passenger, were struck by a vehicle driven by Defendant Taylor Aschenbrener. Plaintiff Man Hwa Yim passed away on November 22, 2021, and now Plaintiff Chang Suk Kim, as his successor in interest, moves the court to substitute herself in place of her husband in the present case.

The court examined the filing and found that the successor in interest has filed a declaration fulfilling all the requirements under CCP § 377.32. A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate is also attached; however, the death certificate is illegible. The court will CONDITIONALLY GRANT the present motion upon the filing of a legible certificate of death.

MOTIONS TO COMPEL INTIAL RESPONSES AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/15/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV01100 ALFREDO BECERRA-AGUILAR vs UBER TECHNOLOGIES, INC

Moving Party: Defendant ALVIN BRUCE FERGUSON 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTIONS TO COMPEL INTIAL RESPONSES AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

1.          Requests for Production and Interrogatories 

 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)

 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

 

2.          Request for Admissions

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ CCP § 2033.010.¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”¿ CCP § 2033.250(a).

 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c). Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to deem RFA admitted does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Discussion

On November 13, 2023, Defendant Uber Technologies served its Requests for Admission (Set One), Requests for Production (Set One), Form Interrogatories (Set One), and Special Interrogatories (Set One) on Plaintiff Alfredo Becerra-Aguilar. Defendant gave Plaintiff numerous extensions with a final extension set for February 1, 2024. However, Plaintiff failed to serve any objections or responses. On March 19, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s counsel’s request to be relieved as counsel. On March 22, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff a copy of the discovery requests and requests discovery responses to be provided by March 27, 2024. However, Defendant received no responses from Plaintiff. Defendant now moves the Court to compel Plaintiff to provide discovery responses and deem the contents of its RFAs (Set One) admitted.

Plaintiff failed to serve discovery responses before the statutory deadline. Plaintiff did not file an opposition or any other documents showing that responses had been made prior to the hearing. Thus, Defendant’s motions are GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide discovery responses to Uber’s Requests for Production (Set One), Form Interrogatories (Set One), and Special Interrogatories (Set One) within 20 days of today without objections. Further, Uber’s Requests for Admissions (Set One) are deemed admitted.

Because of Plaintiff’s failure to provide discovery responses, Defendant was forced to file the present motion and incur attorney’s fees. Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $1,323.30 for each motion. Due to the simplicity of the motions and the lack of an opposition, the Court exercises its discretion and reduces the sanction to $2,000 for all four motions. Plaintiff is hereby ordered to pay Defendant the amount of $2,000 at a rate of $500 per month beginning June 1, 2024, until paid in full.

MOTION FOR RELIEF 

Hearing Date: 5/15/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV12263 EMILIO ARIAS RAMOS, et al. vs TAMARA NICOLE JORDAN. 

Moving Party: Defendant

Responding Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR RELIEF IS CONTINUED

 

Background

 

On April 12, 2022, Plaintiffs Emilio Ramos and Sylvia Arias filed the present motor accident case against Defendant Tamara Jordan. On May 20, 2022, Defendant, represented by counsel Thomas Kielty, Esq., filed a cross-complaint against Defendants for negligence. On September 30, 2022, Ibrahim N. Muhtaseb was associated in this case by Defendant’s insurance carrier. On February 14, 2023, defense counsel Ibrahim N. Muhtaseb amended the complaint to add additional causes of action for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief. On December 5, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s Request for Dismissal of the entire Cross Complaint filed by Thomas Kielty, Esq. The Request for Dismissal allegedly should have been limited to only the first cause of action of the cross complaint. Defendant now moves the Court for relief from the dismissal of the entire Cross Complaint.

Legal Standard

“It is … well established that it is the policy of the law to bring about a trial on the merits whenever possible, so that any doubts which may exist should be resolved in favor of the application, to the end of securing to a litigant his day in court and a trial upon the merits.”  (Frank E. Beckett Co. v. Bobbitt (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d Supp. 921, 928.)

 

Section 473, subdivision (b) provides for two distinct types of relief—commonly differentiated as “discretionary” and “mandatory”—from certain prior actions or proceedings in the trial court. (Luri v. Greenwald (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1119, 1124.) “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of ‘mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,’ the court has discretion to allow relief from a ‘judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against’ a party or his or her attorney.  Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of ‘mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,’ the court shall vacate any ‘resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.’” (Ibid., internal citations and quotation marks omitted, quoting CCP § 473, subd. (b).)

 

An application for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) must be made within a reasonable time and in no case, exceeding six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought. (CCP § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Discussion

The moving party submitted a declaration by defense counsel Ibrahim N. Muhtaseb stating that defense counsel Thomas Kielty mistakenly dismissed the entire cross-complaint against the Plaintiffs, beyond the scope of Defendant’s authorization to dismiss only the first cause of action of the cross-complaint. The basis for an order to vacate is proper; however, the moving party needs to submit either a declaration from Kielty acknowledging he inadvertently and mistakenly dismissed the entire case, or a declaration from the client confirming Kielty acted beyond the client's authorization.

Furthermore, the moving party states that the cross-complaint was dismissed with prejudice, but the Court examined the filing and notes that it was dismissed without prejudice. The Court will continue the hearing to May ___, 2024, and the moving party is ordered to file the requisite declaration(s) 5 days before the hearing date.

Moving Party: Defendant Diamante Electric & Construction (“Diamante”)

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: MOTION TO STRIKE IS GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Background

 

On August 5, 2022, Plaintiff filed a premises liability case against Defendants Sigal Family Trust, Allison Binder, and Diamante Electric & Construction, alleging that Defendants were performing construction at the front of the property which was left open with large trenches without any protective coverings. Plaintiff fell into the construction area while delivering food to the Defendants. On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the second amended complaint seeking punitive damages. Defendant Diamante now move the court to strike the allegations relating to punitive damages in the SAC, specifically the following allegations:

 

1. Complaint ¶ 8, 3:16-20 (“and willfully, maliciously, or with conscious disregard, failed to guard or warn against a dangerous condition, use, structure or activity on the Premises. In a premises liability case like this one, the standard for punitive damages includes ‘with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,’ which is a subcategory of malice.”)

 

2. Complaint ¶ 9, 4:1-5 (“Defendants, and each of them, knew these trenches were dangerous and could cause injury in multiple ways, including by individuals being able to fall into the open trenches, blunt force from hitting the concrete, cuts and lacerations from the corners of the concrete blocks and glass inside the trenches, as well as electrification from the wiring and light fixtures inside the trenches.”)

 

3. Complaint ¶ 11, 4:21-22 (“including Defendant Diamante Electric & Construction, Inc. demonstrated malice, conscious disregard, and extreme recklessness for the safety of individuals on the premises”)

 

4. Complaint ¶ 11, 4:23-25 (“By failing to take adequate safety measures to prevent persons on the property from falling into the trenches, demonstrated malice, conscious disregard, and extreme recklessness.”)

 

5. Complaint ¶ 14, 5:17-21 (“Defendants acted with extreme recklessness, and with “evil motive” sufficient for malice, found here in “an intention to perform an act that the actor knows, or should know, will very probably cause harm.” See Nolin v. National Convenience Stores, Inc. (1979) 95 Cal.App.3d 279, 286 [internal citations Donnelly v. Southern Pacific Co. (1941) 18 Cal.2d 863, 869, 118 P.2d 465, 468].”)

 

6. Complaint ¶ 14 5:24-6:2 (“This extreme recklessness and conscious disregard for Plaintiff and his safety, and where they knew, or should have known would probably cause him harm, demonstrates malice. This also constitutes as fraud for punitive damages purposes as Defendants acted with intentional misrepresentation, deceit and or concealment of a material fact known to the Defendants with the intention on the part of the Defendant of thereby depriving Plaintiff of his rights or otherwise causing injury.”)

 

7. Complaint ¶ 15, 6:3-9 “(Defendants, by verbally warning their friends/acquaintances of the dangerous condition and instructing them to avoid the front door/walkway where the trenches were and to enter the home through the garage or other areas, while not doing the same for service workers and/or other similarly situated such as Plaintiff, aka the “help”, shows wanton and reckless misconduct. Defendants instructing Plaintiff to walk on the known dangerous walkway with the open trenches displays malice and despicable conduct which was carried on by the Defendants with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights and safety of Plaintiff.”)

 

8. Complaint ¶ 16, 6:12-14 “(and willfully or maliciously failed to guard or warn against a dangerous condition, use, structure or activity on the Premises, demonstrating a conscious disregard for the safety of individuals on the premises.”)

 

9. Complaint ¶ 17, 6:16-18 (“As a result of the wrongful conduct of Defendants and DOES 1-100’s failure to obey multiple safety rules and regulations, and to provide signage and cover to fill in areas of the pavers or take protective measures to keep the area safe for persons entering the property.”)

 

10. Complaint ¶ 18, 6:20 (“willfully or maliciously”)  

 

11. Complaint ¶ 19, 6:24-25 (“unlawfully maintained the premises”)

 

12. Complaint ¶ 20, 7:2-3 (“recklessness, and unlawfulness”)

 

13. Complaint ¶ 21, 7:7-8 (“willfully or maliciously failed to guard or warn against a dangerous condition, use, structure or activity on the Premises.”)

 

14. Complaint ¶ 22, 7:9-10 (“recklessness, wantonness, and unlawfulness”)

 

15. Complaint ¶ 24, 7:15-16 (“Furthermore, Defendants were aware of the dangerous condition and acted with a conscious disregard for the safety of others, including Plaintiff and the residents of the premises.

 

16. Complaint ¶ 24, 7:29 (“As a result, Plaintiff is entitled to punitive damages.”)

 

17. Complaint ¶ 34, 9:10-11 (“res ipsa loquitor negligence, carelessness, recklessness, wantonness and unlawfulness”)

 

18. Prayer for Relief, 6 (“For punitive and exemplary damages as provided for by Cal. Civil Code § 3294 against Defendants.”)

 

Legal Standard 

 

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)¿ “Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civil Code section 3294 (c)(1).) Under the statute, malice does not require actual intent to harm. Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.) ‘Despicable’ is a powerful term that refers to circumstances that are ‘base,’ ‘vile,’ or ‘contemptible.’ (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)

 

A plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.¿ To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.¿(Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.)

 

Analysis

 

In this premises liability/negligence case, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant failed to ensure the safety of a construction area, resulting in Plaintiff sustaining injuries while delivering food to Defendants. Regarding punitive damages, the complaint primarily contains legal conclusions rather than specific facts necessary to substantiate such claims. (Opposition at pgs. 9-14.) Plaintiff references paragraphs 9-14 of the SAC, asserting that these sections contain specific factual allegations supporting the request for punitive damages. The Court finds that, while some allegations are specific, they do not demonstrate the willful and despicable conduct required for punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff claims that Defendants left the front of the property open with large trenches and unfilled concrete, stones, and pavers without protective coverings. The trenches contained large pebbles, electrical wires, and lightbulbs, measuring between six and eight inches wide and over one-foot deep. (SAC ¶ 9.) Defendants were aware that these trenches posed multiple dangers, including the risk of falls, blunt force trauma from hitting the concrete, cuts from the concrete blocks and glass, and electric shocks from the wiring and light fixtures. (SAC ¶ 9.) Despite this knowledge, Defendants intentionally failed to implement safety measures such as warning signs, fences, gates, or protective coverings. (SAC ¶ 10.) Defendants were aware that multiple people lived at and visited the property. (SAC ¶ 11.) Defendants advised their friends and close contacts to avoid the walkway where the trenches were located and to use alternative entrances. They warned these individuals of the dangerous conditions. (SAC ¶ 12.) Despite being aware of the risk, Defendants directed Plaintiff to deliver food to the front door via the walkway with the open trenches, without warning Plaintiff of the hazard. Defendant Binder specifically instructed Plaintiff to navigate the dangerous trenches to deliver the food, resulting in Plaintiff's injury. (SAC ¶ 14.)

 

In Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89, the court differentiates between risks that are foreseeable and those that are necessarily probable, holding that the risk must be necessarily probable for the conduct to be deemed willful. Here, the allegations must demonstrate that the risk was not merely foreseeable but necessarily probable to support claims for punitive damages. The specific facts alleging that the open trench is 6 to 8 inches wide and more than 1-foot deep and filled with construction material indicate risks that are foreseeable but not necessarily probable.

Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendant knew the trench was dangerous and intentionally failed to provide safety measures are legal conclusions and not facts pled with the requisite specificity. Furthermore, Plaintiff only pled with specificity Defendant Binder’s conduct in instructing Plaintiff to navigate the trenches to deliver food, and not specific facts relating to Defendant Diamante.

 

Moreover, the alleged facts do not rise to the level of "despicable conduct." Plaintiff's assertion that Defendant left  trenches with construction equipment and materials without installing warning signs, fences, or protective coverings suggests potential negligence but does not show conduct that is "base," "vile," or "contemptible."

 

Therefore, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED. Leave to amend is denied because this is Plaintiff’s second amended complaint. Based on the allegations in the SAC, it appears unlikely that Plaintiff can allege facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages with reasonable probability. However, if Plaintiff acquires sufficient information during discovery to substantiate a claim for punitive damages, the Court will consider Plaintiff’s motion to amend the complaint.

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO CONDUCT A SECOND IME 

Hearing Date: 5/8/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV14713 REGINALD WILSON vs JIYEON CHOI

Moving Party: Defendant Ji Yeon Choi 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO CONDUCT A SECOND IME IS GRANTED IN PART

 

Background

On June 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present auto accident action, claiming injuries to his left knee and back. The knee injuries included tears in both the inner and outer knee cartilage, inflammation of the tendon below the kneecap, and a painful condition affecting the front of the knee, all of which required injections and surgery. The back injuries included a loss of the natural curve in the lower back, muscle spasms or stiffness. There was also a 6mm bulging disc at one of the lower back levels, which required multiple steroid injections and nerve blocks to manage the pain and inflammation in the lower back.

On November 21, 2023, Dr. Kvitne conducted a first IME on Plaintiff’s knee, back, and spine. Defendant now moves the court for leave to conduct a second physical examination by Dr. Samudrala on Plaintiff’s spine. Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing that Plaintiff’s spine/back has already been examined by Dr. Kvitne and that this examination by Dr. Samudrala is duplicative. In its reply, Defendant argues that this examination is neurological in nature and different from that previously conducted by Dr. Kvitne.

Legal Standard

Except for defense physicals in personal injury cases (in which one examination is permitted as a matter of course) and exams arranged by stipulation, a court order is required for a physical or mental examination. Such order may be made only after notice and hearing, and for “good cause shown.” (CCP §2032.320(a).)

The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action. (CCP §2032.020(a).) This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation. Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337. Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.” See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837, wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer. Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged.

Where the plaintiff's injuries are complex, several exams may be necessary by specialists in different fields. There is no limit on the number of physical or mental exams that may be ordered on a showing of good cause. The good cause requirement checks any potential harassment of the plaintiff. See Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255.

The burden is on the moving party to show (by declarations or other evidence) that the examinee's condition is “in controversy” in the action. The moving party must also establish good cause for the examination(s) sought. A court order for physical or mental examination must be based on a showing of “good cause” (CCP § 2032.320(a)): (1) relevancy to the subject matter; and (2) specific facts justifying discovery: i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.) The purpose is to protect an examinee's privacy by preventing annoying “fishing expeditions” - i.e., one party may not compel another to undergo psychiatric testing “solely on the basis of speculation that something of interest may surface.” (Id.)

The notice of motion must state the time, place, identity and specialty of the examiner, and the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination.” (CCP § 2032.310(b).) The requirement for specification of the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination” requires disclosure of whatever diagnostic tests and procedures will be utilized (x-rays, blood and urine samples, etc.). (See CCP § 2032.220(c).) The notice of motion must be accompanied by a separate document setting forth the discovery request, the Declarations must state facts showing that “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to arrange the examination by stipulation was unsuccessful. (CCP § 2032.310(b).)

Preliminary matters

Defendant filed a declaration stating that the meet and confer requirement has been met, and Plaintiff in the opposition does not contest this. Defendant has provided the time, place, identity, and specialty of the examiner, as well as the manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination in the motion.

Analysis

The central dispute between the parties is whether the requested examination is duplicative. The Court compared the tests done by Dr. Kvitne and the tests proposed by Dr. Samudrala. The proposed IME by Dr. Samudrala includes numerous tests under five categories: general examination, examination of the neck, neurological examination of the upper limbs, neurological examination of the lower limbs, and general examination of the spine.

As for the examination of the neck and limbs, Defendant has not shown how these body parts are at issue. Plaintiff's response to form interrogatory no. 6.2, which Defendant relies on to show which body parts are at issue, indicates that Plaintiff only alleges injuries to the back and knee. Therefore, an IME for the neck and limbs is denied.

As for the examination of the spine, it includes evaluations that are general, orthopedic, or neurological in nature. Generic examination includes evaluating and measuring chest expansion. Neurological examination includes tests such as the femoral stretch test. Orthopedic examination includes tests such as the rotation of the spine. As a general rule, examinations of the same body part by different experts in different fields are allowed. Considering the car crash led to several injections and complaints of chronic pain, a neurological examination of the body parts at issue is reasonable.

Thus, the court will limit the second IME by Dr. Samudrala to the general or neurological examinations of the spine only. As Dr. Samudrala proposes more than 30 tests to be conducted on the spine, the parties are ordered to meet and confer in good faith on the exact neurological tests to be conducted within 7 days of today. To the extent the parties cannot agree on the tests to be conducted, they may come in on an ex parte application to obtain the Court’s assistance.  But, only after good faith efforts to agree.  Plaintiff is ordered to attend the second IME within 30 days of today pursuant to the agreement reached by the parties.
