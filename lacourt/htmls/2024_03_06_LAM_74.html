Case Number: 20STCV39869    Hearing Date: March 6, 2024    Dept: 74

Dwayne J. Chomyk v. Christina Development Corporation

Plaintiff Dwayne J. Chomyk’s Motion for Monetary Sanctions

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from an employment dispute.

            On October 16, 2020, Plaintiff Edwayne Chomyk (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendant Christina Development Corporation (Defendant) alleging wrongful termination under Labor Code section 1102.5, wrongful termination under Labor Code section 6310, wrongful termination in violation of public policy, and fraud.

            On June 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion for sanctions.

DISCUSSION 

            Plaintiff seeks sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.98, subdivision (c) and 1281.99, subdivision (a).

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, subdivision (a), if the party who drafted an arbitration agreement fails to pay fees and costs while an employment arbitration proceeding is pending, the drafting party is in material breach of the arbitration agreement and is in default of the arbitration. If the drafting party breaches the agreement or is in default, the employee may withdraw his or her claim from arbitration and proceed in a court of appropriate jurisdiction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98, subd. (b)(1).) And if the employee withdraws his or her claim from arbitration, the employee may bring a motion to recover attorney’s fees and costs associated with the abandoned arbitration proceeding. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98, subd. (c)(1).) Moreover, the court shall impose sanctions on the drafting party in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.99. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98, subd. (c)(2).)

            On September 21, 2021, the court granted Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration and stay proceedings in this action. But on May 9, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s motion to vacate the stay in this action on the grounds that Defendant did not comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, subdivision (a). As a result, the court found that Plaintiff could exercise his right to withdraw from arbitration. Defendant thereafter appealed the court’s May 9 ruling, and the appeal is still pending. Because Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions depends on Plaintiff’s proper withdrawal of his claims from arbitration, a ruling on this matter before the Court of Appeal issues its decision would be premature.

CONCLUSION 

            The court continues this hearing to a later date.

            Plaintiff shall give notice.


Case Number: 22STCV21788    Hearing Date: March 6, 2024    Dept: 74

Harmik Abrahamian v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Brigitte Brown to Appear for Deposition and Produce Documents and Request for Sanctions.

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from an employment dispute.

            On July 6, 2022, Plaintiff Harmik Abrahamian (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendant Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (Defendant). The complaint alleges retaliation, disability discrimination, failure to accommodate, failure to engage in the interactive process, wrongful discharge in violation of public policy, and failure to allow inspection of personnel files.

            On November 29, 2023, Plaintiff served a notice for Brigitte Brown to appear at deposition and produce documents. The deposition was set for January 4, 2024.

            On December 27, 2023, Defendant served objections to the deposition notice and document requests.

            On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel the deposition and production of documents.

            On January 8, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for a protective order that would prevent Plaintiff from taking the deposition.

            On February 6, 2024, the court denied Defendant’s motion for a protective order.

LEGAL STANDARD

            “The service of a deposition notice . . . is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff wishes to compel Brigitte Brown’s appearance at deposition and production of documents. The court finds Plaintiff has shown good cause for the testimony and documents sought.

            However, Defendant argues the court should deny Plaintiff’s motion as moot because Defendant has agreed to produce the deponent. (Papania Decl., ¶¶ 13; Ex. H.) But Defendant has not provided available deposition dates or expressly agreed to produce the documents. Thus, the motion is not moot. Defendant also claims Plaintiff cannot bring this motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a), because Defendant served timely, valid objections to the deposition notice on December 27, 2023. (Soleymani Decl., ¶ 28; Ex. 23.) The court finds Defendant’s boilerplate objections to the deposition notice and document requests improper, though. (Korea Data Systems v. Superior Court (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1513, 1516.) Defendant also objected that Plaintiff “unilaterally selected” the deposition date without sufficiently meeting and conferring. In addition, Defendant objected on the grounds of unavailability. But Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to schedule the deposition with Defendant’s counsel, who never provided available dates. (Soleymani Decl., ¶¶ 11-14, 20-21, 27; Ex. 9; Ex. 12, pp. 1-7.) Thus, Plaintiff’s motion to compel is proper.

            Given the foregoing, the court grants Plaintiff’s motion. As the prevailing party, Plaintiff seeks sanctions as well. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) The court finds that Defendant did not act with substantial justification because its objections to the deposition were unavailing. Plaintiff’s counsel’s proposed hourly rate was reasonable preparing what amounted to a template for the multiple motions filed on January 3 (and for which the court awarded $4,810.00 in sanctions on March 4, 2024) but excessive for what amounted to copying that template for the motions filed in addition to the first motion. The court orders $500 in attorney’s fees as discovery sanctions.

CONCLUSION

            The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel. Defendant shall produce Brigitte Brown for deposition and produce documents responsive to Category Nos. 1-15 by April 5, 2024. The court grants Plaintiff’s request for sanctions. Defendant shall pay Plaintiff’s counsel $500 in attorney’s fees as discovery sanctions by April 5, 2024.

            Plaintiff shall give notice.

Case Number: 22STCV28596    Hearing Date: March 6, 2024    Dept: 74

Jesus A. Carmaco, et al. v. American Honda Motor Company, Inc.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents (Set One) and Request for Sanctions

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND

            This action arises from a defective 2019 Honda Accord.

            On September 1, 2022, Plaintiffs Jesus Carmaco and Maria Luisa Carmaco (Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc. (Defendant). The complaint alleges various claims under the Song-Beverly Act.

            On May 25, 2023, Plaintiffs propounded on Defendant requests for production of documents (RFPs).

            Defendant responded on June 27, 2023, and provided documents on August 15, 2023. Plaintiffs were dissatisfied.

            The parties met and conferred to no avail.

            On August 15, 2023, Plaintiffs filed this motion to compel further response to RFP Nos. 16, 19, 20, and 21.

LEGAL STANDARD 

            A propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response to a demand for inspection if the propounding party deems that an answer or statement of compliance is evasive or incomplete, or that an objection is “without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) Such a motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery sought and be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subd. (b), 2031.210, subd. (b).)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiffs seek further response to RFP Nos. 16, 19, 20, and 21. As a preliminary matter, the court finds Plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause for their motion and the parties have sufficiently met and conferred. The court also finds Defendant’s initial boilerplate objections to the RFPs improper. (Korea Data Systems v. Superior Court (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1513, 1516.) Accordingly, the court will address each RFP at issue and the objections Defendant expanded upon in its separate statement.

RFP No. 16

            This request seeks all documents, including electronically stored information and emails, related to any internal analysis or investigation Defendant conducted regarding the electrical defect in vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle. In response, Defendant agrees to comply in part. But Defendant also raises several objections. First, Plaintiffs’ definition of “electrical defect” includes defects that are not a part of the subject vehicle’s repair history. Second, Defendant claims information about electrical defects in vehicles other than the subject vehicle is irrelevant. Third, Defendant asserts that Plaintiffs are not entitled to emails and electronically stored information. And fourth, Defendant objects that Plaintiffs are not entitled to information about the electrical defects’ cause. The court only agrees with Defendant in part. Defendant shall produce any internal analysis or investigation regarding defects that were part of the subject vehicle’s repair history. To the extent other vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle experienced those defects, Defendant shall also produce the applicable internal analysis and investigation. This includes recall notices and technical service bulletins. Defendant need not do a search of emails.

RFP No. 19

            This request seeks all documents, including electronically stored information and emails, related to customer complaints, reported failures, and warranty claims related to the electrical defect. In response, Defendant raises the same objections, with which the court only agrees in part. Defendant shall produce any customer complaints relating to the same defects that arose in the subject vehicle’s repair history in vehicles purchased in California for the same year, make, and model of the subject vehicle. Defendant shall also produce warranty claims submitted to and/or approved by Defendant concerning the subject vehicle.

RFP No. 20

            This request seeks all documents, including electronically stored information and emails, related to failure rates of vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle as a result of the electrical defect. In response, Defendant raises the same objections, with which the court only agrees in part. Defendant shall produce documents related to the failure rates of vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle as a result of the same defects that arose in the subject vehicle’s repair history.

RFP No. 21

            This request seeks all documents, including electronically stored information and emails, related to any fixes for the electrical defect in vehicle of the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle. Defendant raises the same objections, with which the court agrees in part. Accordingly, Defendant need only produce repair orders concerning the subject vehicle, in addition to communications with the dealer or factory representative concerning the subject vehicle.

CONCLUSION

                The court grants Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further response to RFP Nos. 16, 19, 20, and 21 subject to the limitations above. Defendant shall provide further Code-compliant responses and produce responsive documents within 30 days of this order.

                Plaintiffs shall give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV22347    Hearing Date: March 6, 2024    Dept: 74

You Hyuck Lim v. Aviators, Inc., et al.

Defendants’ Demurrer to Complaint

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from an employment dispute.

            On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff You Hyuck Lim (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendants Aviators, Inc. and Blake Purdy (Defendants).

            On December 8, 2023, Defendants filed this demurrer, which targets the following causes of action: (5) failure to pay overtime wages; (6) failure to reimburse business expenses; (8) retaliation in violation of Labor Code section 1102.5; (10) failure to pay wages due upon separation.

LEGAL STANDARD

            A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (¿¿Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318¿¿.) “¿To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.¿” (¿¿C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872¿¿.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (¿Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967¿.) A demurrer “¿does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.¿” (¿¿Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713¿¿.) DISCUSSION 

Fifth Cause of Action – Failure to Pay Overtime Wages

            Defendants argue this cause of action fails for several reasons. First, Defendants contend Labor Code sections 204 and 510 require the employer to pay overtime wages. According to Defendants, Plaintiff does not allege Defendant Purdy was Plaintiff’s employer. But the complaint alleges Defendant Aviators employed Plaintiff, Defendant Purdy owned Defendant Aviators, and the Defendants were alter-egos. (Complaint, ¶¶ 1, 3, 6.) This is sufficient for pleading purposes. Second, Defendants claim Labor Code section 558.1 does not impose liability for overtime violations. As Plaintiff notes, though, Labor Code section 558.1 covers violations of provisions regulating hours and days of work in Industrial Welfare Commission orders. Industrial Welfare Order No. 9 applies to Defendants’ business. Section 3 of the order entitles employees to receive overtime wages. Third, Defendants assert Plaintiff has not demonstrated how Defendants deprived him of overtime pay. The court disagrees. (Complaint, ¶¶ 30, 66.) Thus, the court overrules Defendants’ demurrer for this cause of action.

Sixth Cause of Action – Failure to Reimburse Business Expenses

            Defendants further argue Plaintiff has not alleged facts sufficient to support this cause of action. Labor Code section 2802 provides as follows: “An employer shall indemnify his or her employee for all necessary expenditures or losses incurred by the employee in direct consequence of the discharge of his or her duties […].” Plaintiff alleges he had to use his personal phone to perform his job duties, but Defendants never reimbursed him for this expense. (Complaint, ¶ 27.) This is sufficient. Thus, the court overrules Defendants’ demurrer for this cause of action.

Eighth Cause of Action – Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code Section 1102.5.

            Defendants contend this cause of action fails as well because Plaintiff’s allegations are too conclusory. The court disagrees. Under Labor Code section 1102.5, subdivision (b), “An employer, or any person acting on behalf of the employer, shall not retaliate against an employee for disclosing information […] to a person with authority over the employee […] if the employee has reasonable cause to believe the information discloses a violation [of some law].” Here, Plaintiff alleges he disclosed to Defendant Purdy, who had authority over Plaintiff, that Defendants had unlawfully deducted items from Plaintiff’s paystubs. (Complaint, ¶ 38.) Moreover, Defendants allegedly terminated Plaintiff because of his disclosure. (Complaint, ¶ 39.) These facts suffice for pleading purposes. The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer for this cause of action.

Tenth Cause of Action – Failure to Pay Wages Due Upon Separation

            Defendants also argue that the complaint lacks facts sufficient to support this cause of action. If an employer discharges an employee, the wages earned and unpaid at the time of the discharge are due and payable immediately. (Lab. Code, § 201.) Here, Plaintiff alleges Defendants failed to pay him all his wages due at the time of his termination. (Complaint, ¶ 40.) This is enough. The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer for this cause of action.

CONCLUSION 

The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer.

Defendants shall file and serve their answer within 20 days.

Defendants shall give notice.
