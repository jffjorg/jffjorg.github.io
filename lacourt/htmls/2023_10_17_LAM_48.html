[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART AND DENYING IN PART DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER; GRANTING IN PART AND DENYING IN PART PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 17, 2023

 

On February 19, 2020, Plaintiff Clarence Harrell filed this action against Defendant City of Pasadena.  The parties have been heavily litigating their discovery disputes.

DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

On August 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application to continue the September 5, 2023 trial date and all related dates, including discovery.

On August 3, 2023, the Court granted in part Plaintiff’s ex parte application.  The Court advanced and continued to September 5, 2023 jury trial to May 20, 2024.  The Court also ordered:  “Discovery deadlines are reset based on new Trial date only for discovery noticed on or before August 4, 2023.  All other discovery deadlines remain unchanged.  [¶]  All motion deadlines for discovery noticed on or before August 4, 2023 and for Summary Judgment are reset based on the new Trial date.  All other motion deadlines remain unchanged.”

On August 4, 2023, Plaintiff served twenty-six Special Interrogatories, thirty-four Requests for Admissions, Employment Form Interrogatory No. 217.1, fourteen Requests for Production, a Supplemental Interrogatory and Request for Production, and sixteen deposition notices.  (Motion, Price Decl. ¶¶ 6-7.)

On September 6, 2023, Defendant filed an ex parte application for a protective order.  On September 7, 2023, the Court set a hearing and ordered briefing.

Defendant filed its full motion for protective order on September 20, 2023.

As the Court observed at the August 3 hearing, the parties were then just a few days away from the thirty-day deadline for completing discovery based on the September 5, 2023 trial date, so Plaintiff should have already propounded everything he needed.  (Motion, Ex. E at pp. 8-9.)  In his ex parte application, Plaintiff argued that the interests of justice were best served by a continuance because “presently Plaintiff is unable to finish taking all necessary depositions due to Defendant’s counsel not being available due to a family emergency.  Additional depositions are still to be taken and review of documents produced.”  (08/02/2023 Ex Parte Application at pp. 11-12.)  There was no showing of necessity for a continuance for any written discovery.

Moreover, the focus of the hearing was on Plaintiff’s need for additional time for depositions only.  Defendant’s counsel stated that Defendant did not oppose a continuance “for the limited purpose of conducting depositions that could not go forward in the last week and a half or so due to my—my personal family emergency which necessitated taking them off calendar.  However, Defendant would oppose what’s essentially a discovery do-over.”  (Motion, Ex. E at p. 4.)  When the Court proposed setting new discovery deadlines for discovery noticed on or before August 4, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel stated, “Again, Your Honor, I will say that there are multiple depositions that we need to take.”  (Motion, Ex. E at p. 9.)  Near the end of the hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel stated, “Your Honor, I think that your—the solution that you crafted is workable by Plaintiff.  We can get the depositions noticed by August 4th, and then that will be it.  I think that we could live with that.”  (Motion, Ex. E at p. 19.)

Plaintiff’s August 2, 2023 ex parte application sought to continue trial and related dates so Plaintiff could take the depositions of certain witnesses: Greg Helstley (Vol. II); Jaime Arellano (Vol. II); Joe Armstrong; Varrojan Avedian; Susanne Stone; Susana Castro; Paul Bahn; Eric Klinkner; Jeff Barber; Mark Jomsky; Jose Trujillo; Sharon Moody; James Lohse; Gurchuran Bawa; and Richard Nadelman.  (08/02/2023 Ex Parte Application at p. 6.)

Defendant states that only seven depositions remain in dispute: Suzanne Stone; Susana Castro; Salvador Delgadillo (Vol. II); Jose Trujillo; Jeff Barber; Paul Bahn; and the PMK regarding compensation.  (Price Suppl. Decl. ¶ 4.)  Of these, Salvador Delgadillo (Vol. II) and the PMK regarding compensation were not part of Plaintiff’s ex parte application.  For Suzanne Stone, Susana Castro, Jose Trujillo, Jeff Barber, and Paul Bahn, Defendant does not show good cause to prevent these depositions.

The motion for protective order is DENIED as to the depositions of Suzanne Stone, Susana Castro, Jose Trujillo, Jeff Barber, and Paul Bahn.  These depositions may go forward.

The motion for protective order is GRANTED in all other respects, including for all written discovery served on August 4, 2023.

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

On May 5, 2023, Plaintiff electronically served on Defendant an Amended Notice of Deposition and Request for Production of Documents for non-party Andy Ly, noticing a deposition on May 12, 2023.  (Anglin Decl., Ex. 2.)  The Amended Notice was untimely served.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.270, subd. (a) [“An oral deposition shall be scheduled for a date at least 10 days after service of the deposition notice.”].)

Defendant served objections on May 10, 2023, and it served responses on May 12, 2023.  (Anglin Decl. ¶¶ 5-6; Price Decl. ¶¶ 3-4.)  Although these objections and responses were not served three calendar days before the May 12 deposition date (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a)), they were reasonably served in light of the untimely service of the Amended Notice of Deposition.

After attempting to meet and confer (Anglin Decl. ¶ 7), Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses on July 10, 2023.

If a party or an officer, director, managing agent, employee of a party, or person designated as the person most qualified to testify fails to appear for examination or produce documents, the demanding party may move to compel attendance, testimony, and production.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

For RFP Nos. 1-4, 8, 13, 16, 19, 20, 22, 23, after objections, the responses state, “There are no additional responsive documents.”  This is not a code-compliant response.  (See Code Civ Proc., § 2031.230.)  The motion is GRANTED for RFP Nos. 1-4, 8, 13, 16, 19, 20, 22, 23.  Mr. Ly is ordered to provide further code-compliant responses within 30 days.

For RFP Nos. 5-7, 9-12, 15, 17, 18, 21, 24-36, only objections were provided.

Plaintiff noticed Mr. Ly’s deposition in his individual capacity.  (Anglin Decl., Ex. 2.)  However, the Amended Notice of Deposition requested that “the Defendant City of Pasadena shall produce the following documents and/or things.”  (Anglin Decl., Ex. 2 at p. 5.)  The requests for production of documents define “YOU” or “YOUR” as including and referring to “City of Pasadena, as well as . . . employees (including the deponent, Andy Ly) . . . .”  (Anglin Decl., Ex. 2 at p. 4.)  In the Joint Statement’s arguments, Plaintiff refers only to Defendant, not Mr. Ly.  The Court therefore agrees with Defendant that “[m]any Requests for Production are totally vague, ambiguous, and unintelligible; specifically, but not only, Plaintiff’s incoherent use of the terms ‘YOU’ and ‘YOUR.’  Indeed, Plaintiff’s requests were drafted as if Mr. Ly were the City of Pasadena or held a management position within the City giving him access to certain records.  This simply is not true, and Plaintiff knows this.  Plaintiff’s crafting of its requests was careless and failed to consider the deponent, Mr. Ly.  Many of the Requests for Production were difficult to interpret as it was difficult to determine what was being requested and to who the request was being made.”  (Price Decl. ¶ 9.)  Furthermore, Plaintiff has not shown good cause for the discovery sought.  The objections are sustained.

Additionally, some of Plaintiff’s requests directed at Mr. Ly are the same as what he directed at Defendant.  For example, RFP No. 5 here seeks the same documents as RFP No. 115 directed at Defendant.  RFP No. 6 here seeks the same documents as RFP No. 116.  RFP No. 7 here seeks the same documents as RFP No. 117.  RFP No. 9 here seeks the same documents as RFP No. 140.  Defendant provided responses on September 1, 2020 and October 25, 2021, and Plaintiff did not move to compel Defendant’s further responses to those RFPs.  (See Price Decl. ¶¶ 10-12 & Exs. 1-2.)  The Court agrees with Defendant that the duplicate requests directed at Mr. Ly is Plaintiff’s “attempt[] to do an end-run around the 45 day motion to compel deadline to raise issues that Plaintiff should have raised two years ago.”  (Price Decl. ¶ 12.)

The motion is DENIED for RFP Nos. 5-7, 9-12, 15, 17, 18, 21, 24-36.

For RFP No. 14, the response states, “After a reasonable search and diligent inquiry, Defendant is not in possession of any documents responsive to this request because such documents either never existed or have been lost, misplaced, or destroyed.”  This is a code-compliant response.  The motion is DENIED for RFP No. 14.

The motion is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART as set forth above.

The request for sanctions is denied.

On May 26, 2023, Plaintiff Bahram Rasizadeh filed this action against Defendant Schuster Land Corporation.

On August 30, 2022, Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production, Set One on Defendant.  Defendant never served responses, so Plaintiff filed two motions to compel on December 5, 2022.

At the May 8, 2023 hearing, Defendant did not appear, but Plaintiff’s counsel represented that some late discovery responses were received.  The Court ordered Defendant to provide complete responses, without objections, to Form Interrogatories 4.1(e) and 12.1 within 20 days. All other discovery disputes were rendered moot by the Defendant’s late responses.

The first amended complaint, filed on May 9, 2023, alleges that Plaintiff was a tenant in one of Defendant’s property’s uninhabitable rental units.  On May 10, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel met and conferred with Defendant’s counsel about Form Interrogatory 12.1 and Plaintiff’s request for information about all tenants since May 26, 2018.  (Naudi Decl. ¶ 3.)

On May 17, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel reminded Defendant’s counsel about the Court’s order to provide responses to Form Interrogatory 12.1.  (Naudi Decl. ¶ 4 & Ex. B.)  Defendant did not provide timely responses.  (Naudi Decl. ¶ 5.)

On June 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion for an order to show cause re: contempt.  Thereafter, on July 25, 2023, Defendant provided incomplete supplemental responses.  (Naudi Decl. ¶ 6 & C.)  The Court later denied the motion for contempt, repeating its June 2, 2023 advisement, “If the disputed discovery is the subject of a Court order, and the Court order has been violated, there should be a motion for evidentiary exclusion or issue preclusion sanctions first, or for terminating sanctions if the first two approaches will not work, and only after those three approaches fail, for contempt. . . . The Plaintiff should seek appropriate sanctions for violation of that [May 8, 2023] order as discussed above at or before the Final Status Conference.”

On September 25, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion for evidentiary sanctions.  Plaintiff seeks an order prohibiting Defendant from introducing witnesses regarding the condition of the property, the cause of the conditions of the property, the citations, or any attempts to abate the conditions at the property.

A court may impose an issue sanction prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).)

Form Interrogatory 12.1 asks for the name, address, and telephone number of each individual who witnessed the incident or related events, made any statement at the scene of the incident, heard any statements made about the incident by someone at the scene, and who the responding party claims has knowledge of the incident.  Defendant’s July 25, 2023 response identified thirteen tenants other than Plaintiff, with only their unit numbers.  (Naudi Decl., Ex. C.)  Defendant further responded that it “has made a diligent and reasonable effort to obtain current addresses for tenants who no longer reside at the property but such information has never been within Responding Party’s possession, custody or control.  Discovery and investigation are continuing.”

Defendant has had many opportunities to comply with the May 8, 2023 discovery order, and it failed to fully respond.  Trial is scheduled for December 11, 2023, with a discovery deadline of November 11, 2023.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020.)  The parties should be preparing for this trial date, and Plaintiff cannot do so without Defendant’s complete discovery responses and potential witnesses to the conditions of the property.

Accordingly, the unopposed motion for sanctions is GRANTED.  Defendant is prohibited from introducing witnesses regarding the condition of the property, the cause of the conditions of the property, the citations, or any attempts to abate the conditions at the property.

Defendant is also ordered to pay sanctions of $3,000.00 to Plaintiff within 30 days.

MOTIONS TO COMPEL RESPONSES

When a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300; Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)   A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a), 2031.300, subd. (a).)  Sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300, subd. (c).)

Plaintiff served discovery on Defendants on July 31, 2023.  Defendants did not serve responses to Plaintiff’s discovery and filed no oppositions to these motions.  Accordingly, the motions to compel are GRANTED.

Lucia Lopez is ordered to serve verified responses, without objection, to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories and Demands For Production of Documents within 30 days.

Yoel Lopez is ordered to serve verified responses, without objection, to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories and Demands For Production of Documents within 30 days.

The requests for monetary sanctions are discussed separately below.

MOTIONS TO DEEM ADMITTED

When a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants them relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (a)(1)-(2).)  The court shall grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

Plaintiff served discovery on July 31, 2023.  Neither Lucia Lopez nor Yoel Lopez served responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission.  They did not file any oppositions to the motions.  Accordingly, the motions to deem Requests for Admission admitted are GRANTED.

The requests for monetary sanctions are discussed separately below.

Lucia Lopez is ordered to pay sanctions of $1,710.00 to Plaintiff within 30 days.

Yoel Lopez is ordered to pay sanctions of $1,710.00 to Plaintiff within 30 days.
