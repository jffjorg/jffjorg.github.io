DEPARTMENT 57 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Inform the clerk if you submit on the tentative ruling. If moving and opposing parties submit, no appearance is necessary.

Case Number: 23STCV19817    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 57

1200 Management LLC (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendant Alan Bunao (“Defendant”) for recovery of COVID-19 rental debt. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1179.02.)  Pending before the Court are four discovery motions that Plaintiff filed.  The Court’s disposition of the motions is set forth below.  The bottom line is that the Court is granting all of the motions.

 

A.  Motion to Compel Compliance Regarding Production of Documents

 

On February 12, 2024, Plaintiff served its First Set of Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”) consisting of 23 document requests on Defendant. (RFP McConnell Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.)  On March 28, 2024, Defendant served his verified responses to the RFPs.  (Id., ¶ 3, Ex. B.)  In all 23 of his responses, Defendant stated as follows: “After reasonable inquiry and diligent search, the Responding Party will produce all responsive, non-privilged documents in their possession, custody, or control.” (Ibid.)  Also in his responses, Defendant referred to Exhibits 101 to 113 but produced only Exhibit 103. (Ibid.)  On April 18, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter. (Id., ¶ 4, Ex. C.)  At the time of filing the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel represents that Defendant’s counsel had neither responded to his letter nor produced additional documents. (Ibid.)

 

In Opposition, Defendant argues that his responses are sufficient and that he has produced responsive documents.  As to Defendant’s first point, the Court notes that his responses are agreements to comply with the RFPs, not a representation of an inability to comply as Defendant asserts in his Opposition. As to Defendant’s second point, the Court agrees with Plaintiff’s counsel that Defendant served verified supplemental responses on June 20, 2024 after having filed his Opposition. (RFP Supp. McConnell Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.)  The Court further agrees that Defendant’s supplemental responses to RFP Nos. 7, 10-18 and 21 not only fail to comply with his own agreement to produce but also do not constitute valid responses on their own. \Plaintiff’s counsel also represents in Reply that Defendant’s supplemental production only included Exhibits 101 to 104. (Id., ¶ 3.)

 

On a motion to compel compliance, there is no time limit and all that needs to be shown is the responding party’s failure to comply as agreed. (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group, 2023) ¶ 8:1508.1.)

 

Finding that Defendant has failed to produce documents including Exhibits 105-113, as agreed, the Court grants Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel and orders Defendant to serve Plaintiff the documents responsive to RFP Nos. 7, 10-18 and 21 by July 15, 2024.

 

 

B.  Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admissions

 

On February 12, 2024, Plaintiff served Requests for Admission, Set One, on Defendant (“RFAs”). (RFA McConnell Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant served verified responses to the RFAs on the agreed-upon deadline of March 29, 2024. (Id., ¶ 3, Ex. B.)  At issue is Defendant’s response to RFA No. 2, for which Plaintiff sought a supplemental response via Counsel’s meet and confer letter on April 18, 2024. (Id., ¶ 4, Ex. C.) Counsel represents that “Defendant refused to meet and confer in good faith,” prompting this [Motion].  (Ibid.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.220 sets forth the requirements for responding to RFAs:

 

(a)  Each answer in a response to requests for admission shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.

(b)  Each answer shall:

(1) Admit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true, either as expressed in the request itself or as reasonably and clearly qualified by the responding party.

(2) Deny so much of the matter involved in the request as is untrue.

(3) Specify so much of the matter involved in the request as to the truth of which the responding party lacks sufficient information or knowledge.

(c)   If a responding party gives lack of information or knowledge as a reason for a failure to admit all or part of a request for admission, that party shall state in the answer that a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter in the particular request has been made, and that the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable that party to admit the matter.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220(a)-(c).)

 

Here, RFA No. 2 asks Defendant to “Admit that the SB TOWER Twelve Month Rental Agreement attached hereto as Exhibit ‘A’ is a genuine copy of the LEASE.” (RFA McConnell Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.) In response, Defendant did not object but stated in relevant part, “Defendant received a lease that appears to be similar to the lease attached to Plaintiff’s complain.” (Id., ¶ 3, Ex. B.) Defendant’s “response that the lease he received when he rented the subject property ‘appears to be similar’ to the lease attached to the RFAs is evasive and does not clearly admit or deny the RFA.” (RFA Separate Statement, p. 2.)  The Court agrees.

 

In Opposition, Defendant claims for the first time that he “has conducted a reasonable inquiry and the information is not known or readily obtainable.” (RFA Opposition, p. 3.)  Defendant’s response did not, however, indicate “lack of information or knowledge as a reason for a failure to admit all or part of a request for admission” to trigger such claim. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220(c).) The Court thus rejects Defendant’s argument made in Opposition that his “response is substantive in nature.” (RFA Opposition, p. 3.)

 

The Court further notes that as with the RFPs, Defendant served verified supplemental response to the subject RFA after having filed his Opposition. (RFA Supp. McConnell Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.) Defendant’s supplemental response, too, is deficient. It states as follows:

 

There is a reason to believe another lease does not include [Defendant’s former wife] Jackie Bunao. The lease in question dates back to 2013, with several rent increases until 2021. Following the divorce in 2017, a verbal request was made for Jackie Bunao’s removal from the lease. Jackie relinquished all interest in the company as part of the divorce agreement. This information was provided to the management team via email. Please see Exhibit 106[.]

 

(Ibid.) This supplemental response still fails to admit or deny the genuineness of the lease and does not state an inability to admit or deny due to lack of information or knowledge.

 

Finding that Defendant’s original and supplemental responses do not comply with section 2033.220, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion as to RFA No. 2 and orders Defendant to serve a Code-compliant further response by July 15, 2024.

 

C.  Motions to Compel Further Responses to Special and Form Interrogatories

 

If a timely motion to compel a further response to an interrogatory has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer the interrogatories. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)¿¿  Here, Plaintiff timely filed its motions to compel further responses to special and form interrogatories.  The issue is whether Defendant satisfied his burden to justify his responses. In his Oppositions, Defendant argues that his original responses are substantive.   The Court notes that Defendant has since served supplemental interrogatory responses, leaving Special Interrogatories 5, 6, 9, 11, 12, 20, and 22 and Form Interrogatories 2.6, 2.7, 2.12, 12.1, 12.3, 15.1, 17.1, 50.1, and 50.2 at issue. (SROG Supp. McConnell Decl., ¶ 2; FROG Supp. McConnell Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Defendant’s supplemental responses remain deficient because they fail to answer subparts of an interrogatory, improperly refer to other documents, and are evasive or incomplete. Defendant has also failed to supplement his response to Form Interrogatory 15.1. Finding that Defendant failed to justify his failure to fully answer the interrogatories that remain at issue, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories 5, 6, 9, 11, 12, 20, and 22, and Form Interrogatories 2.6, 2.7, 2.12, 12.1, 12.3, 15.1, 17.1, 50.1, and 50.2. Defendant is ordered to serve Code-compliant further responses to these interrogatories by July 15, 2024.

 

 

D.  Sanctions

 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Defendant and his counsel in the form of attorney’s fees and costs incurred in connection with the motions.   The Court finds that the hourly billing rate of Plaintiff’s counsel of $355 per hour is reasonable and that the hours counsel spent on the motions are reasonable.  The filing fee for each of the four motions was $60.  Based on a review of the material that Plaintiff’s counsel submitted in connection with the motions, the Court is imposing sanctions against Defendant and his counsel as follows:

 

RFP Motion -- $ 1,900.

 

RFA Motion --  $ 1,700

 

SROG Motion  --  $2,100

 

FROG Motion  --   $2,100

 

The total amount of the sanctions is $6,800.  That amount must be paid to the Plaintiff by August 19, 2024.
