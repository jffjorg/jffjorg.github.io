DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV04644    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Mekbeb v. Interstate Battery System of America Inc., et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCV04644

MOTION:                  Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

HEARING DATE:   11/18/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (CCP § 284(2).) The attorney seeking to withdraw must take “reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the rights of the client, including giving due notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel.” (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, 3-700(A)(2). See, e.g., Vann v. Shilleh (1975) [holding withdrawal prejudicial where attorney withdraw from the representation of defendant on the Friday before trial began the following Monday].) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.) 

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (CRC 3.1362(a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (CRC, 3.1362(d).) When a client is served by mail, the attorney’s declaration must indicate that the client’s address was confirmed within the last 30 days and how it was confirmed. (Id.) If the attorney is unable to confirm the client’s current address, the declaration must state the reasonable efforts made within the last 30 days to obtain the client’s current address. (Id.)

 

Additionally, the declaration “must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship why” a motion is brought instead of filing a substitution of attorney. (CRC, 3.1362(c).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Counsel Jason Lonas Esq. moves to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff Menbere Mekbeb. Counsel submits all the mandatory forms. Counsel states that an irreparable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship has occurred. Counsel served his client by mail at his last known address, which was confirmed within the past 30 days by email correspondence. The declaration notes the hearings that are on calendar, including the trial date. Given that trial is not until May 12, 2025, the Court does not find that the client will be prejudiced by the withdrawal.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED. The order is effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the order on the client. Until then, counsel remains counsel of record.


Case Number: 24SMCP00411    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Creative Artists Agency LLC v. Range Media Partners LLC

CASE NO.:                24SMCP00411

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Depositions of Respondents Micelli and PMK

HEARING DATE:   11/18/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

Service of a proper deposition notice obligates a party or “party-affiliated” witness (officer, director, managing agent or employee of party) to attend and testify, as well as produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying. (CCP § 2025.280(a).) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party deponent fails to appear, testify, or produce documents or tangible things for inspection without having served a valid objection under CCP § 2025.410, the deposing party may move for an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production. (CCP § 2025.450(a).) The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, or, when a party deponent fails to attend the deposition, the motion must also be accompanied by a declaration stating that the moving party has contacted the party deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(2).) If the deposition notice included a request for production of documents, the motion to compel attendance must also show good cause to justify the production. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(1).) 

  

A motion to compel production of documents described in a deposition notice must be accompanied by a showing of good cause. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(1).) In other words, the moving party must provide declarations containing specific facts justifying inspection of the documents described in the notice. Courts liberally construe good cause in favor of discovery where facts show the documents are necessary for trial preparation. 

 

The motion to compel must be “made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition.” (CCP § 2025.480(b).) This time limit also applies to motions based on a deposition subpoena for production of documents or a business records subpoena. The 60-day time limit runs from the date objections are served because the deposition record is then complete. (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1192.) 

 

Analysis

 

Petitioners Creative Artists Agency, LLC; Creative Artists Agency Holdings, LLC; CAA Holdings, LLC; and CAA Equity Incentive, Series 2 LLC (collectively “CAA”) move to compel Respondents Range Media Partners, LLC, and Peter Micelli (“Respondents”) to comply with CAA’s discovery requests and appear at their depositions with the requested documents, within two weeks.

 

Separate Statement

 

A separate statement is generally required to compel the production of documents or tangible things at a deposition. (CRC Rule 3.1345(a)(5).) Here, Respondents responded to the deposition notices and document requests. The motion involves the content of a discovery request and the responses to such a request, and thus required a separate statement. While that would be a basis to deny this motion, the Court will consider the merits.

 

Noticed Topics and Requests

 

On August 2, 2024, CAA filed the Petition in this action to compel Respondents to comply with certain third-party subpoenas issued in an underlying Arbitration Proceeding. (Pet. ¶¶ 8, 20-36.) Respondents failed to obey the Arbitration Subpoenas. The Petition requests that the court order Respondents to obey the Arbitration Subpoenas and appear for a hearing in January 2025, at which they shall produce the requested documents. (Pet. ¶ 38.)  The deposition notices and request for documents were served by CAA on September 24, 2024. (Fee Decl., ¶ 10, Exs. E & F.) CAA seeks to depose on the Respondents on the following ten topics:

 

TOPIC NO. 1: RANGE’s organizational structure, each CLAIMANT’S position within its structure (including whether they are members of the LLC), the date each CLAIMANT began performing services for RANGE, and each CLAIMANT’S possession, custody, and control over RANGE DOCUMENTS, including those sought by CAA in the ARBITRATION since January 1, 2020.

 

TOPIC NO. 2: Nonprivileged COMMUNICATIONS within RANGE regarding the MICELLI SUBPOENA, including service of the subpoena, compliance, and potential enforcement action.

 

TOPIC NO. 3: COMMUNICATIONS between RANGE and CLAIMANTS—including through their respective counsel—regarding the MICELLI SUBPOENA, including service of the subpoena, compliance, and potential enforcement action.

 

TOPIC NO. 4: Nonprivileged COMMUNICATIONS within RANGE regarding the RANGE SUBPOENA, including compliance and potential enforcement action.

 

TOPIC NO. 5: COMMUNICATIONS between RANGE and CLAIMANTS—including through their respective counsel—regarding the RANGE SUBPOENA, including compliance and potential enforcement action.

 

TOPIC NO. 6: COMMUNICATIONS between RANGE and CLAIMANTS—including through their respective counsel—regarding the ARBITRATION.

 

TOPIC NO. 7: RANGE’s document retention policy, including whether RANGE contends its DOCUMENTS include materials CLAIMANTS created while still members of Creative Artists Agency Holdings, LLC.

 

TOPIC NO. 8: All efforts by any CLAIMANT to secure and produce DOCUMENTS in the ARBITRATION and RANGE’s response on each occasion, including the identity of the decision maker, the decision maker’s title, and the rationale for any refusal or limitation on any CLAIMANT’S collection and production of RANGE DOCUMENTS in the ARBITRATION.

 

TOPIC NO. 9: Whether RANGE decided that the DOCUMENTS being sought by CAA from CLAIMANTS in ARBITRATION were outside the possession, custody, and control of CLAIMANTS and the basis for that decision if made.

 

TOPIC NO. 10: COMMUNICATIONS between RANGE and CLAIMANTS—including through their respective counsel—regarding producing, or restricting the production of, DOCUMENTS in the ARBITRATION.

 

CAA makes fourteen requests for production of documents. The first ten request documents pertaining to their respective topics (noted above), with the addition of the following:

 

RPD 11. All DOCUMENTS evidencing, constituting, discussing, or otherwise pertaining to RANGE’s organizational structure, including, but not limited to, RANGE’s investors, ownership, partners, managing partners, and members.

 

RPD 12. All DOCUMENTS evidencing, constituting, discussing, or otherwise pertaining to RESPONDENTS’ knowledge that CLAIMANTS were ordered on January 22, 2024, to undertake a search and produce documents in their possession, custody, and control, including RANGE DOCUMENTS, in the ARBITRATION.

 

RPD 13. All DOCUMENTS evidencing, constituting, discussing, or otherwise pertaining to correspondence or other written or oral COMMUNICATIONS between Robert Whittel and CLAIMANTS regarding any CAA discovery request to CLAIMANTS in the ARBITRATION.

 

RPD 14. All DOCUMENTS evidencing, constituting, discussing, or otherwise pertaining to whether CLAIMANTS have possession, custody, or control of RANGE DOCUMENTS to

produce them in the ARBITRATION.

 

CAA fails to show good cause for discovery on the above topics and requests. The requested information is not reasonably calculated to lead to evidence relevant to the outcome on this Petition. Rather than seeking information on the enforceability of the Arbitration Subpoenas, the instant discovery is designed to secure the substantive relief sought on the Petition itself prior to the hearing on the Petition. For instance, Range’s organizational structure and document retention policy have nothing to do with the enforceability of the arbitration subpoenas. Rather, this relates to Range’s ability to respond substantively to the subpoenas. Likewise, Respondents’ communications concerning the arbitration and/or arbitration subpoenas have no bearing on the arbitration subpoenas’ enforceability. Respondents’ acts taken in compliance with the arbitration subpoenas or any contemplated responses to the subpoenas themselves would not affect the enforceability of the arbitration subpoenas, and thus have no bearing on the outcome of the Petition. Indeed, Respondents’ possession of responsive items to the Arbitration Subpoenas is not relevant to the enforceability of the subpoenas themselves.

 

CAA argue that the requests reflect “routine discovery” which is highly relevant to the petition, specifically: “information concerning Respondents’ possession, custody, and control of the items sought by the JAMS Subpoenas.” (Memo. at 9.) CAA concludes that this evidence will ensure that the Court is able to resolve the petition based on evidence, rather than speculation. However, CAA does not explain what any such discovered evidence might show. CAA suggests that this information may be used to file an anticipated amended petition, but again CAA fails to explain what these amendments might entail and how the requested discovery would lead to those amendments.

 

CAA’s position appears to be that it needs discovery from Respondents to determine whether it is entitled (in the arbitration) to discovery from Respondents. That is rather circular and nonsensical.  The issues in this Petition are straightforward – were CAA’s arbitration subpoenas valid or are they improper overly burdensome discovery subpoenas.

 

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.
