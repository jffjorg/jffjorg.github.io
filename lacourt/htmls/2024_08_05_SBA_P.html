DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV03021    Hearing Date: August 5, 2024    Dept: P

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. P

 

GIOVANNIE PIERRE-LOUIS,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03021

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] ORDER OVERRULING DEMURRERS

 

 

JEFFREY C. JAMES, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:              August 5, 2024           

 

Moving Parties:          Defendants Jeffrey C. James and Del Amo Construction Inc.

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Giovannie Pierre-Louis       

Demurrers

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendants’ demurrers are OVERRULED.

BACKGROUND

            On September 12, 2023, Plaintiff Giovannie Pierre-Louis filed a complaint against Defendants Jeffrey C. James, Del Amo Construction, Inc. Sterling V. McCarthy, and Lyft, Inc., alleging a cause of action for negligence.

The complaint alleges that on September 13, 2023, Plaintiff was a passenger in a Lyft Vehicle driven by defendant Sterling V. McCarthy. Further, it alleges that suddenly, defendant Jefferey C. James rear-ended the vehicle in which Plaintiff was a passenger, causing it to rear end the vehicle in front of them. Plaintiff alleges that defendants James and McCarthy failed to exercise reasonable care. Plaintiff claims that at the time, defendant James was employed by Del Amo Construction, Inc. and was acting within the scope of employment. Thus, Plaintiff contends, as an actual and proximate result of Defendants’ breach of their duty of care, Plaintiff sustained injuries.

 

            On April 15, 2024, Defendants Del Amo and James each filed a demurrer. On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On July 29, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

REQUEST FOR JUDIAL NOTICE

Defendants seek judicial notice of Plaintiff’s Complaint.

The request is granted pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d).

 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law ….” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525).) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters; therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

Demurrers for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification, and apply where defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; Weil & Brown, Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2011) ¶¶7:85-7:86.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will not lie where the ambiguous facts alleged are presumptively within the knowledge of the demurring party.” (Bacon v. Wahrhaftig (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 599, 605.)

 

DISCUSSION

i.                    Meet and Confer Requirements

            Defendant submits the Declaration of Christopher Grossi, which adequately shows Counsel met and conferred prior to bringing these demurrers. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41.) 

ii.                  Demurrer

Defendants demur to the cause of action for negligence arguing it fails to plead sufficient facts and it is uncertain.

 

The elements for negligence are: (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to the plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)  In California, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 407-408.) 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to plead sufficient facts as to duty, breach of duty, and causation.

The complaint alleges that on September 13, 2023, Plaintiff was a passenger in a Lyft Vehicle driven by defendant Sterling V. McCarthy. Further, it alleges that suddenly, defendant Jefferey C. James rear-ended the vehicle in which Plaintiff was a passenger, causing it to rear end the vehicle in front of them. Plaintiff alleges that defendants James and McCarthy failed to exercise reasonable care. Plaintiff claims that at the time, defendant James was employed by Del Amo Construction, Inc. and was acting within the scope of employment. Thus, Plaintiff contends, as an actual and proximate result of Defendants’ breach of their duty of care, Plaintiff sustained injuries.

As noted above, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. Plaintiff’s complaint is pleaded in general terms. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant rear-ended the vehicle he was in. This allegation is more than sufficient to allege the element of breach of the general duty of care. Plaintiff alleges this caused him injuries. The facts in this case are very straightforward, and no further elaboration is required. The general rule is that the plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts, not evidentiary facts.  (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, 42 Cal. 4th 531, 550 (2007).) These allegations are ultimate facts and are thus sufficient to plead general negligence against Defendants.  At a later stage of the litigation, Plaintiff will need to prove his allegations with substantial evidence, but for the purposes of the subject demurrer, these allegations are enough.  (See Doe, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.)   

 

Further, Plaintiff alleges that James was employed by Defendant Del Amo. “Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, an employer may be held vicariously liable for torts committed by an employee within the scope of employment.” (Mary M. v. City of Los Angeles (1991) 54 Cal.3d 202, 208.)  As Defendant James’ alleged employer, Del Amo’s liability is based on vicarious liability.

 

Moreover, the complaint is not uncertain as ultimate facts have been pled. Any further clarifications as to the allegations and claims can be resolved during discovery. Lastly, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty will not lie where the ambiguous facts alleged are presumptively within the knowledge of the demurring party.” (Bacon v. Wahrhaftig (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 599, 605.)

 

As a result, the demurrers are OVERRULED.

Having considered defendant’s motion and plaintiff’s opposition, the court rules as follows: the motion to dismiss for lack of personal jurisdiction is denied.    

 

I.                   PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff Kyle Crowe (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Progress House, Inc. (“Progress”) and Anthony Arce (“Arce”) alleging causes of action for: (1) negligence, (2) negligent hiring, training, supervision, and retention, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress, (5) fraud, (6) negligent misrepresentation, (7) breach of contract, (8) unfair and fraudulent business practices, and (9) breach of the duty of good faith and fair dealing.

 

On January 24, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable David K. Reinert sitting in Department P at Torrance Courthouse effective January 29, 2024.

 

On June 9, 2024, Defendant Arce was served with the summons and complaint by personal service. (See 06/11/24 Proof of Service.)  

 

On July 11, 2024, Defendant Arce, specially appearing, filed and served the instant Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction. The motion was noticed for an August 5, 2024 hearing date.

On July 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served an opposition to the motion.

 

As of August 1, 2024, no reply brief has filed. Any reply brief was required to have been filed and served at least five court days prior to the hearing. (CCP § 1005(b).)

 

II.                FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff alleges that while staying at Progress House located at 1409 Marcelina Ave., Torrance, CA 90501 (the “Property”), he was exposed to bed bugs and suffered injuries therefrom. (Complaint, ¶¶ 3, 21.)   

 

 

III.             LEGAL STANDARD

A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead, may file and serve a motion to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)

IV.             DISCUSSION

 

Moving Party’s Arguments

 

Defendant Arce contends that he was not involved with Progress House at the time of the incident and therefore cannot be held liable for the claims alleged in the complaint.

 

Responding Party’s Opposition

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant Arce’s motion is untimely and must be denied. Plaintiff additionally argues that the motion is procedurally flawed.

 

Moving Party’s Reply

 

As of August 1, 2024, no reply brief has been filed.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

Procedural Defects in the Motion

 

“Unless otherwise ordered or specifically provided by law, all moving and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least 16 court days before the hearing. The moving and supporting papers served shall be a copy of the papers filed with the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) “[I]f the notice is served by facsimile transmission, express mail, or another method of delivery proving for overnight delivery, the required 16-day period of notice before the hearing shall be increased by two calendar days.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) “Any period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document . . . shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(A).) “When a document may be served by mail, express mail, overnight delivery, or fax transmission, the document may be served electronically . . . .” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.251(a).)

 

Plaintiff argues that the motion was untimely filed as it was filed and served on July 11, 2024 with an August 5, 2024 hearing date. The Court agrees. The hearing was noticed for August 5, 2024 so Plaintiff was entitled to 16 court days’ notice plus an additional two calendar days’ notice. Thus, the instant motion was untimely served and filed.

 

Additionally, the Court notes that Defendant Arce did not present any evidence in support of the motion to dismiss. No declaration accompanied the motion to dismiss. "In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

 

The Court also notes that Defendant Arce failed to cite any legal authority in his motion to dismiss. While the Court recognizes that Defendant Arce is self-represented, a self-represented litigant is “held to the same standards as attorneys.” (Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543.) 

 

 

     V. CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Anthony Arce’s Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction is DENIED in its entirety. Defendant Arce is ordered to file a responsive pleading.
