I. BACKGROUND  

Plaintiffs Marina Ruiz and Jazmin Guzman (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against defendants Fernando Cazares (“Cazares”), Fidel Calderon, Cazares Services LLC (Doe 1), Thomas Franklin Henry (Doe 2), and Orchids de Oro, Inc. (Doe 3) for damages arising from a motor vehicle incident.  

On April 2, 2024, Cazares filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions to dismiss plaintiff Marina Ruiz’s (“Plaintiff Ruiz”) complaint against them for failure to abide by the Court’s January 26, 2024 Order that she serve verified responses to form interrogatories, set one, and to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $560. Cazares also brings the motion in anticipation that Plaintiff Ruiz will likewise fail to provide verified responses to set one of special interrogatories and request for production of documents per the Court’s March 14, 2024 Order. Cazares further requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,185. 

Any opposition was due on or before April 22, 2024. Plaintiff Ruiz filed an untimely opposition on April 30, 2024. Considering the drastic consequences of terminating sanctions, although exceedingly late, the Court will consider the opposition. Plaintiff Ruiz argues verified and objection-free responses to all the outstanding discovery was served on April 30, 2024, and that the previously imposed discovery monetary sanctions have been paid in full at the time of this opposition. 

In reply, Cazares argues that no payments have been received to date by defense counsel. However, notably Cazares does not dispute that verified responses, without objections, had indeed been served. 

 

II. TERMINATING SANCTIONS  

  1. Legal Standard 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030 gives the court the discretion to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in a misuse of the discovery process.  A court may impose terminating sanctions by striking pleadings of the party engaged in misuse of discovery or entering default judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(d).)  A violation of a discovery order is sufficient for the imposition of terminating sanctions.  (Collison & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1620.)   

A terminating sanction is a "drastic measure which should be employed with caution."  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793.)  "A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction."  (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.)  While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery."  (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at 793.)  "[A] court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations."  (Ibid.)  Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information.  (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971].) 

 

  1. Discussion & Conclusion 

The Court had granted Cazares’ motion to compel discovery responses to form interrogatories set one, and ordered Plaintiff Ruiz to serve verified responses, without objections, within fifteen days of that Order. (Min. Order, Jan. 26, 2024.) The Court also ordered Plaintiff Ruiz and/or her counsel to pay sanctions in the amount of $560. (Ibid.) On March 14, 2024, the Court granted Cazares’ motions to compel responses to special interrogatories, set one, and request for production of documents, set one. Plaintiff Ruiz was ordered to serve verified responses to these set of discovery within twenty days, and to pay sanctions in the amount of $620. (Min. Order, March 14, 2024.)  

The Court notes that neither Plaintiff Ruiz nor her counsel of record has opposed any of Cazares’ prior motions against her. Plaintiff Ruiz failed to comply with the Court’s orders until on the eve of terminating sanctions by filing a late opposition. Nonetheless, the purpose of discovery sanctions is not for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information. (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971].) Cazares, in his reply, does not dispute that the discovery has now been served. To impose terminating sanctions now does not serve a remedial purpose, and would be disproportionate to Plaintiff’s misconduct. (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 1215, 1223.) Further, in terms of Cazares’ contention that Plaintiff has not actually paid the past owed monetary sanctions, terminating sanctions on the grounds of failure to pay is not the appropriate remedy. (See Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615 [holding, “a terminating sanction issued solely because of a failure to pay a monetary discovery sanction is never justified.”].) 

  

III. CONCLUSION 

Accordingly, Cazares’ motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED. However, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for additional monetary sanctions, but not for the unjustified requested amount of $1,185.   

Sanctions are imposed against Plaintiff Ruiz and her counsel, jointly and severally. Plaintiff Ruiz and/or her counsel are ordered to pay sanctions to Cazares, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $500, within twenty (20) days.

II. MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION 

The Court is unable to proceed on the merits of this motion due to a procedural deficiency. A motion to compel deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (CCP § 2025.450(b)(2).) The Court finds that Plaintiff has failed to submit a meet and confer declaration to inquire about the nonappearances within the express meaning of CCP § 2025.450(b)(2).

The motion is therefore denied. The denial is without prejudice as to Plaintiff Carrasco re-filing the motion with the required meet and confer declaration.

Dept. 31 

1:30 p.m.  

May 3, 2024 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Defendants Robin Zargar and Joseph Mesachi (collectively, “Defendants”) propounded (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for identification and inspection of documents, set one, on plaintiff Pedro Melgar-Cruz (“Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz”) on October 25, 2023. After sending a meet and confer letter regarding the lack of responses, Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz has failed to serve responses to date. Defendant therefore seeks an order compelling him to respond, without objections, to the outstanding discovery and to pay sanctions.  

Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz filed a “combined opposition” the motions to compel, arguing that he objected that service of the propounded discovery was not proper. Further, Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz argues that the key attorney responsible for the case departed from the firm, and that Defendants are required to engage in good faith communication to resolve the discovery dispute. He further contends that the amount in sanctions is not reasonable, and that the motions are near identical to the motions to compel brought against plaintiff Martha Melgar.  

 

II. MOTIONS TO COMPEL 

For a motion to compel initial discovery responses, all a propounding party must show is that it properly served its discovery requests, that the time to respond has expired, and that the party to whom the requests were directed failed to provide a timely response. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 906.) Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290; 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); § 2031.300, subd. (a).)    

Here, the proof of service evinces that the discovery at issue were served by electronic transmission to Law Offices of Sharona Eslamboly Hakim, counsel for plaintiffs Pedro Melgar-Cruz and Martha Melgar, on October 25, 2023 via the e-mail address geoffrey@sehlawfirm.com. (Kothary Decl. Exh. A.) Plaintiff Melgar’s opposition refers to an objection to the service of the discovery responses. (Opp. Exh. A.) The email objection, dated October 31, 2023 from Geoffrey Kasler (“Mr. Kasler”) at geoffrey@sehlawfirm.com provides: “Our current service list includes those I have CC:d to this email, and myself, including: Sharona Abazari, and myself. We will start the clock when service has been perfected.”  

First, Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz’s counsel does not deny that service upon Mr. Kasler at the electronic address was proper. Instead, counsel contests propriety on the grounds that three other members of the firm were not carbon copied on the email address. (Opp. Exh. A.) The purpose of proper service is for the other side to receive actual notice of the document being served. Here, Mr. Kasler does not deny that he is a proper recipient or that he had actual notice. Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz submits no authority that a defendant must also serve a number of other persons who work in the same firm for service to be properly effectuated, especially when the evidence demonstrates that service had indeed been accomplished. Second, Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz’s legal team being on vacation during the holiday season around December 15, 2023 is not an excuse to not provide timely responses; by that time, responses were already long overdue. Third, there is no statutory requirement that Defendants meet and confer prior to bringing motions to compel initial discovery. Although it would appear to be good practice, such a requirement relates to other discovery motions. Lastly, counsel’s inability to contact Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz in this matter is not an excuse for him to evade his discovery obligations. Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz initiated this action against Defendants, and Defendants are entitled to receive his responses. Counsel cites to no legal authority in support of any of their contentions.  

Therefore, because the evidence shows Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz was properly served with discovery and failed to timely respond, any objections have been waived. Defendants’ motions are GRANTED.  

Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz is ordered to serve verified responses to Defendants’ (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for identification and inspection of documents, set one, without objections, within twenty (20) days. (CCP §§ 2030.290(a),(b), 2031.300(a),(b).) 

 

III. SANCTIONS 

Sanctions are mandatory against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response, unless a court makes certain findings.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).)¿ Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz’s opposition provides no basis to warrant a denial of sanctions. Defendants seek sanctions in the amount of $572 for each motion. 

A court has discretion to award sanctions that are “suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks” but they should not be punitive in nature or levied for the purposes of punishing an offending party. (Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545.)         

Defendants are awarded one hour for each motion to compel, and one hour to appear at the hearing (awarded only once), at the requested rate of $133 per hour for a total of $532 in attorney’s fees. Further, Defendants are awarded three motion filing fees of $40, for a total of $120 as costs.       

Sanctions are sought against Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz and his counsel. However, given that Plaintiffs’ counsel is unable to contact Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz, the Court will not impose any sanctions against counsel absent direct evidence they in fact had the ability to contact Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz. As such, the Court will impose sanctions on Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz only at this time. 

Therefore, monetary sanctions are imposed against Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz. Plaintiff Melgar-Cruz is ordered to pay sanctions to Defendants, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $652, within twenty (20) days.

TENTATIVE 

 

Plaintiff Breana Jenkins (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Posh Management, Inc. dba Paradise LA Uncut (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 20 for damages arising from a slip and fall on liquid while Plaintiff was a customer on Defendant’s premises. Plaintiff alleges Defendant knew a dangerous condition existed on the premises and failed to correct the condition or warn of its existence. Plaintiff has properly dismissed all unnamed Doe defendants. 

 

Plaintiff seeks $10,558 in special damages, $20,000 in general damages, and $863.52 in costs against Defendant in default judgment.  

 

  1. SPECIAL DAMAGES 

 

Plaintiff declares that she incurred medical expenses of $10,558 for treatment arising from the incident. Here, the Statement of Damages served upon Defendant provides that Plaintiff seeks $5,988 in medical expenses, and acts as the cap for that particular damage sought. Section 425.11 requires special and general damages to be specified, including stating the amount sought to be recovered. (Plotitsa v. Superior Ct. (1983) 140 Cal. App. 3d 755, 761.)  The amount sought cannot exceed the amount pled in the operative complaint/cross-complaint or set forth in the statement of damages. (Code Civ. Proc., § 580(a); Airs Aromatics, LLC v. CBL Data Recovery Technologies, Inc. (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 1013, 1018.) Consequently, even though Plaintiff has submitted medical bills evincing $10,558 in medical specials, Plaintiff is necessarily limited by the amount pled in the Statement of Damages for that particular expense on default. The Court therefore grants Plaintiff her medical specials in the amount of $5,988 only.  

 

 

  1. GENERAL DAMAGES 

 

 

Plaintiff declares after slipping and falling, she was diagnosed with left shoulder muscle strain, left thigh contusion, left knee sprain, and lumbar muscle strain. Plaintiff received physical therapy and chiropractic treatment. Currently, Plaintiff is following a home exercise plan, and feels her pain slowly becoming less. Although Plaintiff has demonstrated she is entitled to general damages for pain and suffering, the evidence submitted is not sufficient to merit a $20,000 award. The amount has therefore reduced to $15,000. 

 

  1. COSTS 

 

Plaintiff seeks $863.32 in costs for clerk’s filing fees, process server’s fees, and fees to retrieve her medical records. The amount requested is granted.  

 

 

  1. CONCLUSION 

 

Therefore, the application for default judgment filed on April 30, 2024 is GRANTED in the principal amount of $20,988, and $863.32 in costs, against Posh Management, Inc. dba Paradise LA Uncut. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
