DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV05479    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

October 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV05479

MOTION: 

Motion to Deem Admitted

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Fidelity National Title Insurance Company

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

MOTIONS

            Plaintiff Fidelity National Title Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) moves to deem admitted matters specified in Requests for Admissions, set one (“RFA”), which Plaintiff served on Defendant Peter Mullins (“Defendant”).

ANALYSIS

When a party fails to respond to requests for admissions, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  The Court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted the matters specified in the requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) 

Plaintiff served the RFA on Defendant by mail and electronically on April 1, 2024.  As of the filing date of this motion, Defendant has not responded to the RFA.  Accordingly, the motion is granted.  Defendant is deemed to have admitted the matters specified in the RFA. 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions in connection with the motion.  The Court finds Defendant’s failure to respond to the RFA and provide an explanation for such failure is an abuse of the discovery process and awards sanctions.  The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $900, which represents three hours of counsel’s time to prepare the motion and attend the hearing at $300 per hour.  Defendant is to pay sanctions in the amount of $900 to Plaintiff, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV10042A    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: 39

  TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

October 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV10042A

MOTION

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Brendan Schultz

OPPOSING PARTIES

Defendant Pitzer College

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Brendan Schultz (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Defendant Pitzer College (“Defendant”) to Special Interrogatories, set one (“SROG”).  Defendant opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300.)

Defendant’s responses to the SROG consist only of objections.  As Plaintiff timely filed this motion to compel further responses, Defendant has the burden to justify its objections.  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  Defendant has not done so.  Defendant’s objections are general and boilerplate.  As such, the Court compels Defendant to further respond to the discovery at issue.  (Best Products, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1189.) 

In opposition, Defendant argues the SROG assume facts not in evidence.  That is irrelevant.  (See West Pico Furniture Company of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 421 (West Pico) [objections to form of the question may be appropriate at depositions but not for other manner of discovery].)  Likewise, Defendant’s objections that the SROG are vague and ambiguous are inapplicable because such objections cannot “justify a complete failure to comply.”  (Standon Co., Inc. v. Superior Court (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 898, 901.)  Even if Defendant had difficulty understanding Plaintiff’s SROG, Defendant, nonetheless, must respond to the extent possible.

Defendant cites Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216 (Calcor Space) in support of its argument Plaintiff must advance evidence to show the SROG are relevant to this case.  Calcor Space stands for no such proposition.  In that case, the Court of Appeal held a party seeking to compel a nonlitigant business to produce all documents in its possession with any connection to a product category must provide a specific justification “for imposing this great burden” on a nonlitigant.  (Id., at p. 224.)  Defendant is a party in this action.  As a party, if Defendant contends the discovery is unduly burdensome on Defendant, Defendant is required to produce evidence of “the quantum of work required” and show “the ultimate effect of the burden is incommensurate with the result sought.”  (West Pico, supra, 56 Cal.2d at p. 417.)  This would include providing evidence of “the total man hours required to accomplish  the task.” (Ibid.)  Defendant fails to advance such evidence.

Therefore, the motion is granted, and Defendant is to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s SROG, without objections, within 30 days of this order.

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

October 24, 2024

23STCV10042B

MOTION

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint and

Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Pitzer College

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Brendan Schultz

 

MOTION

Defendant Pitzer College (“Defendant”) demurs to and moves to strike portions of the  complaint of Plaintiff Brendan Schultz (“Plaintiff”).  Plaintiff alleges he is a former student of Defendant who reported Defendant for discriminating against Plaintiff based on Plaintiff’s religious faith.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 4, 7, 15-16.)  Plaintiff further alleges that, in retaliation, Defendant failed to endorse Plaintiff’s application for a grant as an alumnus of Defendant.  (Id., ¶¶ 17-18, 20.)  Plaintiff opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

First Cause of Action: Violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act

To state a cause of action under the Unruh Civil Rights Act (Unruh), Plaintiff must allege Defendant is a business establishment that intentionally denied Plaintiff full and equal accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, or services based on Plaintiff’s sex, race, color, religion, ancestry, national origin, disability, medical condition, marital status, or sexual orientation. (Civ. Code, § 51, subd. (b).)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant is a non-profit college.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 5, 7.)  A non-profit educational establishment is not a business establishment for purposes of Unruh unless it “has some significant resemblance to an ordinary for-profit business.”  (Doe v. California Lutheran High School Assn. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 828, 839.)  Plaintiff does not allege Defendant operates in a manner akin to a for-profit business.  Rather, Plaintiff alleges Defendant offers services to its students for compensation, employs staff and faculty for compensation, invests in its campus, and utilizes marketing efforts.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 29.)  These attributes are typical of non-profit educational establishments and do not convert Defendant into an ordinary for-profit business.  Likewise, Plaintiff alleges Defendant engages in fundraising.  3Iid.)  Again, this is typical of non-profit educational establishments.  Fundraising is not typical conducted by for-profit businesses, which tend to rely exclusively on revenue.  As such, Plaintiff fails to allege Defendant is liable under Unruh. 

Further, as the basis for his cause of action under Unruh, Plaintiff cites the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).  To state a cause of action under the ADA, Plaintiff must allege he is a disabled person who suffered adverse actions because of his disabilities.  (Bagatti v. Dept. of Rehabilitation (2007) 97 Cal.App.4th 344, 360.)  A violation of the ADA is also a violation of Unruh.  (Civ. Code, § 51, subd. (f); Baughman v. Walt Disney World Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 1438, 1446.)  Plaintiff does not allege any facts demonstrating Defendant discriminated against him because of a disability.  In fact, Plaintiff contends Defendant accommodated his disability.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 30.) 

Second Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.  The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.”  (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  In this regard, Plaintiff alleges Defendant “refus[ed] to nominate Plaintiff for the Davis Projects for Peace Alumni Grant, a professional opportunity that Plaintiff was qualified for and which would have significantly advanced his career . . . .”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 45.)  That does not amount to the extreme and outrageous conduct that would lead to liability under an IIED claim.  Thus, Plaintiff fails to allege facts to support the cause of action for IIED.

Third Cause of Action: Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (NIED) 

“NIED is a tort in negligence, and the plaintiff must establish the elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages.  [A] right to recover for emotional distress as a direct victim arises from the breach of a duty that is assumed by the defendant or imposed on the defendant as a matter of law, or that arises out of the defendant's preexisting relationship with the plaintiff.” (Moon v. Guardian Postacute Services, Inc. (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 1005, 1009, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant was negligent by “retaliating against Plaintiff due to Plaintiff initiating and participating in internal and external civil rights investigations.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 58.)  Plaintiff does not allege Defendant breached a duty of care, but rather that it breached a statutory obligation.  Plaintiff fails to allege a claim for negligent infliction of emotional distress.  For the same reason, Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for negligence fails.

Fourth Cause of Action: Breach of Contract 

Plaintiff is entitled to plead breach of contract in general terms.  (Ochs v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 795.)  Plaintiff, nonetheless, must allege Defendant offered to enter into an agreement with Plaintiff, and Plaintiff accepted that offer.  (See Donovan v. RRL Corporation (2001) 26 Cal.4th 261, 270-271.)  In this regard, Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached its own policies and procedures.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 66-69.)  Plaintiff fails to state facts to suggest Defendant intended these policies to be offers to enter into a contract with Plaintiff.  Rather, Plaintiff’s allegations suggest Defendant’s policies are open offers to contract with anyone who reviews them, which is irrational.  As such, Plaintiff fails to allege Defendant breached a contract with Plaintiff.  For the same reason, Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing fails because a prerequisite for that claim is the existence of a contractual relationship between the parties (Smith v. San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49), which, as explained above, does not exist.    

Sixth and Seventh Causes of Action: Fraud

Plaintiff must allege fraud with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.)  To state a claim for promissory fraud, Plaintiff must allege specific factual circumstances beyond contract breach, which reflect Defendant’s contemporaneous intent not to perform.  (Hills Transportation Co. v. Southwest Forest Ind., Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 702, 707.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant represented it would not retaliate against complaints of discrimination but discriminated against Plaintiff after Plaintiff reported having been discriminated against.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 86-89.)  Plaintiff fails to allege specific facts showing Defendant did not intend to abide by its representation at the time it made the representation. 

Additionally, Plaintiff’s claims are premised on Defendant’s failure to recommend Plaintiff for a grant.  As such, Plaintiff’s complaint seeks to “compel[] [Defendant] to speak a particular message,” which implicates Defendant’s First Amendment rights.  (National Institute of Family and Life Advocates v. Becerra (2018) 585 U.S. 755, 766.)  The Court cannot grant Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant to recommend Plaintiff for a grant unless that request is narrowly tailored to a compelling state interest.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff fails to identify any compelling state interest that would support his claims. 

Therefore, the Court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint. 

Motion to Strike

Sustaining the demurrer renders the motion to strike moot.  However, the Court notes Plaintiff fails to allege facts to support his prayer for punitive damages.  In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  A plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 (Grieves), internal citations and footnotes omitted.)

Plaintiff does not allege any specific facts to show Defendant acted with malice, fraud, or oppression.  As such, Plaintiff has not alleged a claim for punitive damages against Defendant.  Likewise, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts demonstrating he is entitled to attorney fees or restitution. 

Defendant also moves to strike various allegations it contends are false.  A motion to strike cannot be used as a “‘line item veto’ for the civil defendant . . . .”  (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1683.)  Defendant is not entitled to remove any allegation it contends is incorrect. 

The Court has already provided Plaintiffs with an opportunity to state claims against Defendant, and Plaintiff failed to do so.  As such, leave to amend is likely futile.  However, in an abundance of caution, the Court will provide Plaintiff with one more opportunity to amend to state viable claims against Defendant.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 10 days.

Defendant is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
