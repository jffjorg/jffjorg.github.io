Case Number: 23AHCV02049    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: X

MOTION TO VACATE DISMISSAL (8791)

RELIEF REQUESTED

Plaintiff moves to vacate the order of dismissal.

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff failed to appear at a hearing for an Order to Show Cause re: Failure to File Proof of Service on November 6, 2023.  The FAC was dismissed.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff’s motion to vacate the dismissal is denied.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) provides for two types of relief— commonly differentiated as “discretionary” and “mandatory”— from a default.  Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, the court has discretion to allow relief from default.  Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, the court shall vacate any resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615-616.)  The application for relief must be made no more than six months after entry of judgment and must be “accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein.”  (Id.)  The law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits.  Therefore, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief.  When the moving party promptly seeks relief and there is no prejudice to the opposing party, very slight evidence is required to justify relief.  (Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1343.)

 

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Jerome Vaughn, in pro per, has filed a motion to vacate the dismissal of his case against Defendant Monrovia Police Department, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473(d).

 

On November 6, 2023, the Court heard an Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to File Proof of Service.  There was no appearance by/for the plaintiff and no proof of service of the proceedings on the named defendant. (11/6/23 Minute Order).  For plaintiff's failure to appear and for failing to serve the complaint the Court ordered the first amended complaint dismissed without prejudice.  (11/6/23 Minute Order.)

 

On November 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended motion to vacate order of dismissal, which was denied on February 7, 2024. (2/7/24 Minute Order.)  On August 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed another amended motion to vacate order of dismissal, which was denied on October 7, 2024. (10/7/24 Minute Order.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has filed the same motion as before – to vacate the dismissal order so that the case can be reinstated.  Dismissal of the first amended complaint was entered on November 6, 2023, due to Plaintiff’s failure to file the Proof of Service of the complaint on Defendant.

 

Plaintiff’s motion relies on the Declaration of Jerome Vaughn, but no declaration is attached to the motion attesting to the facts asserted in the motion.  Without a declaration supporting Plaintiff’s motion, the Court cannot consider the assertions in the motion as evidence.  As such, Plaintiff’s motion is denied.

 

CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s motion is denied.

DEFENSE COUNSEL’S MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

The case involves breach of lease and guaranty.  Defendant Jason Arrow’s counsel moves to be relieved as counsel.  Attorney Aaron B. Fairchild moves on the grounds that “a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship has occurred, making further representation untenable.”

Tentative Ruling

The Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel for Defendant Jason Arrow filed by Attorney Aaron Fairchild is GRANTED. 

Legal Analysis 

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel (Code Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. (2)) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’  [Citation.]”  (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)  The Court should also consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.”  (Ramirez v. Sturdivant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-052)); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-053)).  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (a), (c), (d), (e).)

Counsel for Defendant Arrow, Attorney Aaron Fairchild, filed a motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) and California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362.  A declaration on Judicial Council Form MC-052 accompanies the motion, as required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, stating: 

This motion is made on the grounds that a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship has occurred, making further representation untenable.  Due to attorney-client privilege, specific details regarding the specific nature of the breakdown cannot be disclosed in this motion.  In the event that the court desires further information to ascertain the good faith basis for this motion and for withdrawal, it is respectfully requested that the court have an in camera hearing outside of the presence of all other parties so that the specific facts demonstrating good cause for this withdrawal may be demonstrated to the court.  (Manfredi & Levine v. Superio[r] Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th, 1136-1137.)

Proof of service of the motion was served by mail on Defendant Arrow and by electronic service on counsel for Plaintiff and Defendants on November 4, 2024. 

No oppositions have been filed.

The Court finds good cause to grant the request to be relieved as counsel.  Defendant Arrow will not be prejudiced if counsel’s motion is granted.  There is sufficient time for Defendant Arrow to engage new counsel and/or request a continuance before the next hearing, a Trial Setting Conference on January 29, 2025.  Counsel properly noted the remaining hearings in his MC-052 form.

Conclusion

The Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel for Defendant Jason Arrow by Attorney Aaron Fairchild is granted.  Counsel is ordered to file proof of service of the signed MC-053 on Fairchild and all other parties within five court days after the date of this order.

Case Number: 24AHCV00205    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: X


PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO QUASH DEPOSITION SUBPOENAS

Plaintiff moves to quash Defendant’s deposition subpoena for the production of medical and billing records of Plaintiff.  The complaint alleges Plaintiff was visiting Toby Peters’ residence when she tripped and fell.  She sustained bodily injuries and property damage.  She sued the Estate of Toby Peters and the trustee for general negligence and premises liability.

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff’s motion is granted in part and denied in part.

For medical and billing records, production of the records is limited to 10 years prior to the subject incident.  The scope of records sought are also limited to the body parts at issue, not the injuries at issue.

As to insurance records, the Court agrees with the Reply papers that “Defendant is entitled to the records accrued as a result of the subject incident, from the date of loss to the present. . . . any records that do not pertain to the subject injuries are not directly relevant, and thus private.” (Reply, p. 5.)

Legal Standard and Analysis

Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1 states, in pertinent part: 

If a subpoena requires … the production of books, documents, or other things … at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion … may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely…. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that Defendant’s negligent, careless, and reckless maintenance of his place of residence and/or common areas caused the accident.  As a result, Plaintiff seeks compensation for wage loss, loss of use of property, property damage, hospital and medical expenses, general damages, loss of earning capacity, and other damages.  The other damages include—past and future—medical expenses, wage loss and loss of earning capacity, household services, incidental expenses, and pain and suffering.  (Compl., ¶ 11.)

On September 3, 2024, Defendant served Deposition Subpoenas on Plaintiff’s medical providers and the California Department of Health Services Recovery Section/Medi-Cal.  (Motion, Exh. A)  Plaintiff immediately objected to the scope of the subpoenas, asserting that they were overly broad, invasive, and improperly sought information irrelevant to the injuries and claims at issue in the litigation.  (Motion, Exh. B.)  Plaintiff’s counsel raised concerns that the requests sought prior billing and confidential medical records unrelated to the specific body parts and injuries at issue, which constituted an invasion of privacy under California law.

Plaintiff moves to quash Defendant’s deposition subpoena for medical and billing records of Plaintiff from the following facilities:

1. CA Department of Health Services Recovery Section/Medi-Cal 
2. San Gabriel Valley Medical Center
3. San Gabriel Valley Medical Center – Billing 
4. San Gabriel Valley Medical Center-ER Physician 
5. San Gabriel Valley Medical Center – Radiology 
6. San Gabriel Valley Medical Center/KC Tan MD Medical Corporation 
7. Keyvan Bamshad DDS 8. Baldwin Park Injury Center 
8. Diagnostic Imaging Network 
9. Ronald E Bishop MD Inc 
10. Los Angeles Center for Orthopedics 
11. Plaza Surgical Center 
12. Plaza Surgical Center – Billing 
13. Full Range Spine & Ortho 
14. Full Range Spine & Ortho – Billing

The motion is based on the grounds that Defendant’s subpoenas as written are overly broad as to time and subject matter, irrelevant, invade Plaintiff’s constitutional right to privacy, and represent an unnecessarily invasive fishing expedition.

The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonably expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.  (Hill v. Nat’l Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 35-37.)  The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure services, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interest or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy.  (Id., at pp. 37-40.)  The court must then balance those competing considerations. (Id., at p. 40.) 

Medical records fall within the zone of privacy protected by the Constitution.  (Cal. Const., art. I, § 1; Jones v. Superior Court (1981) 119 Cal.App.3d 534, 548-549, disapproved on other grounds by Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531.)  Individuals have an expectation that medical history is kept private to preclude humiliation of the patient that might follow disclosures of his ailments.  (Bd. of Medical Quality Assurance v. Gherardini (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 669, 678-679, disapproved on other grounds by Williams, supra.)

Plaintiff contends that the subpoenas are overly broad in time and scope, seeking records beyond the injuries and claims at issue in this litigation.  Plaintiff emphasizes the constitutional right to privacy and argues that the subpoenas represent an invasive fishing expedition.  Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not demonstrated a compelling need for the requested records, nor have they shown that the subpoenas are narrowly tailored to the claims in this case.  Further, Plaintiff highlights the speculative nature of the Defendant’s requests, which do not justify an invasion of privacy rights. 

In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s broad claims for damages necessitate similarly broad discovery.  They assert that the subpoenas are limited to the body parts at issue and the last 10 years.  Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s medical records are necessary for a meaningful defense and right to a fair trial.  Defendants posit that by making claims for damages, Plaintiff has waived the right to claim that the relevant medical records are privileged.  They argue that the Plaintiff cannot make serious allegations without affording the Defendant an opportunity to test their truth.

The reply asserts that a subpoena’s inquiry “should be limited to only those medical records pertaining to Plaintiff’s injuries sustained in this litigation and treatment rendered to those body parts.  Plaintiff sustained injuries to her [] spine, lower back with radiation/numbness in left thigh and left leg, SI joint, left elbow pain with multiple bruises, right knee pain with bruises, right shoulder pain and tooth/mouth injury because of the subject incident.  Therefore, the inquiry should be limited to the injuries sustained.”  (Reply, p. 4.)

Where a party seeks to discover documents subject to the constitutional right to privacy or protected by a statutory privilege, it is improper to “order discovery simply upon a showing that the Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.010 test for relevance has been met.”  (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 556.)  Rather, the court must balance the privacy interest against the countervailing interest served by disclosure.  (Id., at p. 552 [citing Hill, supra, 7 Cal.4th 35, 37-40).)  Any intrusion on the right to privacy “should be the minimum intrusion necessary to achieve its objective.”  (Lantz v. Superior Court (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1839, 1855, overruled in part on other grounds by William, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 557, fn. 8.)

Here, records demonstrating Plaintiff’s physical health are relevant to determine the existence, cause, and extent of Plaintiff’s injuries, and whether some other cause may be attributable to the alleged past and future pain and suffering.  (See Compl., ¶ 11.)  It would be difficult—if not impossible—for Defendant to defend against the allegations in the complaint, particularly claims for damages, if Defendant was blocked from obtaining Plaintiff’s physical medical history.  Plaintiff’s concerns regarding the overbreadth of the subpoenas and the potential for invasion of privacy are valid.  The Court must, therefore, ensure that any disclosure is narrowly circumscribed and proceeds in the least intrusive manner possible.  The Court determines that records limited to 10 years prior to the incident and to the body parts at issue achieve this balance.  

Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion is granted in part and denied in part as set forth above.

DEFENDANTS’ APPLICATION FOR A TRO/PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for a temporary restraining order and/or preliminary injunction to prevent the foreclosure sale of his home.

This action arises out of a lien agreement between Plaintiff Yerkanyan and Countywide Bank, N.A. in the amount of $73,400 for the property located at 10914 Hillhaven Avenue, Los Angeles, California.  (Compl., ¶ 6.)  The lien agreement is attached to the complaint as Exhibit A, and the deed of trust is attached as Exhibit B.  (Compl., ¶ 7.)  The parties to the lien agreement effectuated the deed of trust for the property in the amount of $73,400.  (Ibid.)

The complaint alleges Defendant Real Time Resolutions, Inc. purchased the lien held by COUNTRYWIDE BANK, N.A.  (Compl., ¶ 8.)  For the period of approximately 18 years, Real Time Resolutions, Inc. allegedly failed to send any notice of deficiencies and/or notice of transfers to Plaintiff notifying him of the transfer of the lien or that no payments were made on the lien and that Plaintiff was in default.  (Compl., ¶ 9.)  Then in 2023, Real Time Resolutions sent Plaintiff a correspondence demanding repayment for the principal balance of $73,400 along with interest.  (Compl., ¶ 10.)  Defendants Real Time Resolutions and MTC Financial Inc. dba Trustee Corps issued a Notice of Sale of the subject property claiming Plaintiff was in default under the Lien Agreement and Deed of Trust dated September 7, 2005.  (Compl., ¶ 11, Exh. C.)  The Notice of Sale regarding the property was scheduled for sale on November 5, 2024 at 10:00AM.

The complaint alleges Defendants cannot sell the property as their rights under the lien are extinguished and the lien is unenforceable.  (Compl, ¶ 13.)  The complaint explains further:

The LIEN AGREEMENT executed on September 8, 2005 provided that the final maturity date on the Lien Agreement is 60 months for said agreement.  (Exhibit A).  Specifically, the Lien Agreement provides in paragraph 1 that “…you agree to lend money to me from time to time until the last day of the sixtieth (60th) consecutive calendar month following the date set forth above (“Initial Draw Period”).”  (Exhibit A.)  Here, 60 consecutive months following 09/08/2005 would be September 8, 2010.  Thereby, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §882.020 the lien expires 10 years after the September 8, 2010 date which would be September 8, 2020.

(Compl., ¶ 12.)

The complaint filed on October 30, 2024 alleges four causes of action: (1) violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 882.020, (2) violation of Homeowner Bill of Rights, (3) wrongful foreclosure, and (4) declaratory relief. 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for a temporary restraining order restricting the Defendants’ November 5, 2024 sale of the subject property.  Since then, the opposition maintains that no sale took place.

On December 2, 2024, the Court entered Plaintiff’s request to dismiss the cause of action for wrongful foreclosure only, leaving the three remaining causes of action pending against Defendant Real Time.

Tentative Ruling

The Court denies Plaintiff’s application for a temporary restraining order and/or preliminary injunction to prevent the foreclosure sale of the property pending further litigation on the merits of the case.

Legal Analysis

The court may issue a temporary restraining order to preserve the status quo or prevent irreparable harm pending the hearing of an application for preliminary injunction on notice.  (Gray v. Bybee (1943) 60 Cal.App.2d 564, 571.)  Where a temporary restraining order (“TRO”) is sought, the court may issue the TRO and at the same time order the defendant to “show cause” why a preliminary injunction should not be issued.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1150(c).)

“[T]rial courts should evaluate two interrelated factors when deciding whether or not to issue [a restraining order].  The first is the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial.¿ The second is the interim harm that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the [restraining order] were denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the [order] were issued.”  (Church of Christ in Hollywood v. Superior Court (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1244, 1251.)  “The trial court’s determination must be guided by a ‘mix’ of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support [a restraining order]. . . .¿ Of course, ‘[t]he scope of available preliminary relief is necessarily limited by the scope of the relief likely to be obtained at trial on the merits.’¿ . . .¿ A trial court may not grant a [restraining order], regardless of the balance of interim harm, unless there is some possibility that the plaintiff would ultimately prevail on the merits of the claim.”¿(Id., at pp. 1251-1252.)

The Court first considers Plaintiff’s likelihood of succeeding on the merits.  Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ foreclosure on the Subject Property was illegal, as Defendants did not comply with the statutory requirements of Civil Code section 2923.5.  Section 2923.5 requires a mortgage servicer, mortgagee, trustee, beneficiary, or authorized agent to “contact the borrower in person or by telephone in order to assess the borrower’s financial situation and explore options for the borrower to avoid foreclosure” before recording a notice of default.  (Civ. Code, § 2923.5.)  Plaintiff alleges that no such contact occurred as the Defendants “recorded a notice of default on the property without first contacting the Plaintiff[.]”  (Compl., ¶ 28.)  Additionally, Plaintiff attaches to the complaint the Notice of Sale.  (Compl., Exh. C.)

In opposition, Defendant argues Plaintiff has not demonstrated a reasonable likelihood of success on the merits of his claims.  Defendant argues that injunctive relief is an “extraordinary and drastic remedy” that requires the moving party to show a reasonable probability of prevailing on the merits of the action and the existence of irreparable injury.  Defendant contends that Plaintiff has not demonstrated a reasonable likelihood of success on the merits of his claims, and therefore the injunctive relief should be denied.  Defendant argues that the enforcement of the Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) Deed of Trust is not time-barred under Code of Civil Procedure section 882.020(a)(2), contrary to Plaintiff’s assertion that enforcement is time-barred under section 882.020(a)(1). 

When a recorded deed of trust does not reflect the maturity date of the underlying promissory note, the 60-year period of Civil Code section 882.020(a)(2), applies to it, rather than the 10-year period of section 882.020(a)(1).  (Robin v. Crowell (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 727, 750.)  Defendant argues the maturity date or last date fixed for payment is not apparent on the face of the recorded Deed of Trust (Compl., Exh. B.), so section 882.020(a)(2) applies, and the lien expires 60 years after the Deed of Trust was recorded.  Therefore, Defendant concludes the time limit in section 882.020(a)(1) is not applicable and enforcement of the HELOC Deed of Trust is not untimely.

Also, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s HBOR claims are based on the unenforceability of the HELOC Deed of Trust due to the statute of limitations, which Defendant argues is not applicable for the reasons above.  Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claims for violations of sections 2923.55, 2924.9, and 2924.11 of the HBOR fail because those sections only apply to first lien mortgages or deeds of trust, and Plaintiff’s HELOC loan is a junior lien.  Defendant cites to Stephens v. World Sav. Bank (N.D. Cal. Feb. 22, 2013) 2013 WL 664615, *7, where the court found that the HBOR sections cited by the plaintiff did not apply to a second deed of trust.  Finally, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s section 2924.11 claim also appears to be time-barred, as it seems to rely on conduct in 2008, and Real Time Resolutions was not involved in any modification in 2008.  Defendant further asserts section 2924.11 was not enacted and not operative in 2008.

Plaintiff notes that irreparable harm exists here.  Plaintiff argues his family (his wife and two children) are at risk of homelessness, psychological and emotional damages, and disruption of family life.  There is a chance Plaintiff’s family would be without a home while the instant litigation is pending.  Meanwhile, no major hardship will befall Defendants by temporarily preventing further encumbrance on the subject property.

Regarding the likelihood of success on the merits, the Court must consider the conflicting interpretations of the statute of limitations under section 882.020.  Defendant has pointed out that the Deed of Trust (Compl., Exh. B) is the pertinent document to consider under section 882.020, not the Home Equity Credit Line Agreement (Compl., Exh. A) which Plaintiff relies on to calculate the date the lien expires.  The Court agrees.  Here, the recorded HELOC Deed of Trust attached to the Complaint as Exhibit B is the security instrument for Plaintiff’s line of credit, and it does not identify a maturity date.  Thus, per Civil Code section 882.020, subdivision (a)(2), the 60-year statute of limitations applies.  Defendant’s right to conduct nonjudicial foreclosure proceedings will not expire until 60 years after the date the instrument that created the security interest was recorded (September 7, 2005), i.e., September 7, 2065. 

As Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently demonstrate some probability of success to obtain a temporary restraining order or preliminary injunction, the Court denies Plaintiff’s application.
