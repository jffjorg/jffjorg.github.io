Case Number: 19STLC02088    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Amica Mutual Ins. Co. v. Laborman, et al

VACATE DISMISSAL AND ENTER JUDGMENT PURSUANT TO STIPULATION

(CCP § 664.6)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Amica Mutual Insurance Company’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement is GRANTED. JUDGMENT TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT BRADLEY J. LABORMAN IN THE AMOUNT OF $7,138.21 PRINCIPAL and $720.95 COSTS.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On February 27, 2019, Plaintiff Amica Mutual Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this subrogation action against Defendant Bradley J. Laborman (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer on February 18, 2020. On August 24, 2021, Plaintiff filed a copy of the parties’ settlement agreement with a request for dismissal and retention of jurisdiction under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. The Court dismissed the action pursuant to the stipulation on the same date. (Order for Dismissal, 08/24/21.)

 

On July 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard

 

The instant motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6, which states in relevant part:

 

If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).) Prior to January 1, 2021, “parties” under section 664.6 meant the litigants themselves, not their attorneys.  (Levy v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 578, 586.) The current statute provides that “parties” includes “an attorney who represents the party” and an insurer’s agent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (b).) The settlement must include the signatures of the parties seeking to enforce the agreement, and against whom enforcement is sought. (J.B.B. Investment Partners, Ltd. v. Fair (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 974, 985.) The settlement agreement complies with the statutory requirements set forth above because it was signed by both parties and their attorneys. (Motion, Benson Decl., Exh. 1, p. 4.)

 

Furthermore, the request for retention of jurisdiction must be made in writing, by the parties, before the action is dismissed for the Court’s retention of jurisdiction to conform to the statutory language. (Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 433 [“If, after a suit has been dismissed, a party brings a section 664.6 motion for a judgment on a settlement agreement but cannot present to the court a request for retention of jurisdiction that meets all of these requirements, then enforcement of the agreement must be left to a separate lawsuit.”].) The parties’ request for retention of jurisdiction complies with these requirements because it was made in writing to the Court before the action was dismissed. (Motion, Benson Decl., Exh. 1, ¶11.)

 

The settlement provides that Defendant would pay Plaintiff $7,766.00 through an initial payment from Defendant’s insurer of $4,066.00, followed by Defendant’s monthly payments starting on October 1, 2021. (Id. at Exh. 1, ¶2.) The settlement agreement also provides that in the event of Defendant’s default, Plaintiff may seek judgment in the demand of the Complaint ($12,583.98), less any monies paid. (Id. at ¶¶1, 5.) Payments of $5,445.77 were made towards the settlement, after which Defendant defaulted. (Id. at ¶¶5-9 and Exh. 2.) Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to entry of judgment against Defendant in the amount of $7,138.21 principal ($12,583.98- $5,445.77) and $720.95 in costs. (Id. at ¶9.)

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Amica Mutual Insurance Company’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement is GRANTED. JUDGMENT TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT BRADLEY J. LABORMAN IN THE AMOUNT OF $7,138.21 PRINCIPAL and $720.95 COSTS.

Case Number: 20STLC00111    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Mold Masters, Inc. v. Lahiji, et al.

MOTION FOR TO REOPEN DISCOVERY 

(CCP §§ 2030.300; 2033.290) 

TENTATIVE RULING: 

 

Defendant Nahid Lahiji’s Motion to Reopen Discovery is DENIED.

 

 

ANALYSIS: 

 

Plaintiff Mold Masters, Inc (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendant Nahid Lahiji, individually, and as Trustee of the Nahid Lahiji Trust dated September 22, 2017, (“Defendant”) on January 7, 2020. On December 13, 2021, Defendant filed an answer to the Complaint in propria persona.

 

Trial was set for September 8, 2022. (Minute Order, 04/12/22.) Following Defendant’s ex parte application, trial was continued to March 15, 2023. (Minute Order, 07/29/22.) Pursuant to the parties’ stipulations, trial was then continued to August 21, 2023, November 20, 2023, and January 22, 2024. On January 12, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to continue the trial date to April 15, 2024. (Minute Order, 01/12/24.) When the parties appeared for trial, the Court ruled on Defendant’s ADA request and continued the trial date to June 26, 2024. (Minute Order, 04/15/24.) Defendant filed an ex parte application to continue the trial date and discovery cutoffs on June 21, 2024. On June 24, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s request for emergency relief, including a request to reopen discovery for failing to show diligence. (Minute Order, 06/24/24.)

 

On June 26, 2024, the trial date was continued to November 6, 2024. (Minute Order, 11/06/24.) Defendant filed a substitution of attorney on July 17, 2024. The Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend the Complaint on September 11, 2024. (Minute Order, 09/11/24.) The First Amended Complaint was filed on the same date.

 

Defendant filed the instant Motion to Reopen Discovery on September 5, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on September 12, 2024, which was only eight court days prior to the hearing. This is less than the required statutory notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005.) However, Defendant filed a substantive reply on September 18, 2024.

 

Discussion

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 allows the Court to extend or reopen discovery at its discretion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (b)) In determining whether to grant such relief, the Court should consider (1) the necessity and the reasons for the discovery; (2) the diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier; (3) any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party; and (4) the length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (b).)

 

Defendant moves to reopen discovery in order to complete what they characterize as “limited discovery.” Defendant argues that the relates directly to Plaintiff’s performance under the contract for which Plaintiff seeks compensation, and to the timeliness of the mechanic’s lien for which Plaintiff seeks foreclosure. Also, the Motion is supported by Defendant’s declaration, in which they contend they have struggled with health and family difficulties, and with obtaining counsel, leading to a delay in seeking this discovery. (Motion, Lahiji Decl., ¶¶2-7.) The Court is not persuaded that Defendant has been significantly hindered in their defense of this action by their health difficulties. The Court ruled that Defendant’s request for an ADA accommodation did not provide evidentiary support for any accommodation other than remote appearances. (Minute Order, 04/15/24.) Yet Defendant again attempts to rely on the ADA request to support the instant Motion. (Motion, Lahiji Decl., ¶7.)

 

Defendant contention that she has been unable to obtain counsel until very recently also lacks credibility. In their initial declaration, Defendant stated that they “encountered significant difficulties in securing legal representation within my financial means.” (Motion, Lahiji Decl., ¶4.) In the reply, however, Defendant states that they were unable to find an attorney because of the attorneys’ workloads and the modest sum in dispute in this action. (Reply, Lahiji Decl., ¶2.)

 

Defendant also changes position with respect to the trial date. Defendant initially acknowledged that a trial continuance will be required. (Id. at ¶6.) Trial in this action has been so frequently continued that the Court strongly disfavors any additional continuances. In the reply, however, Defendant contends that they wish to keep the current trial date of November 6, 2024. (Reply, Lahiji Decl., ¶10.) Yet it is not explained how it will be possible to keep the current trial date if discovery is reopened. Plaintiff will need 30 days to respond to Defendant’s requests, and if a dispute over the responses arises no time will be left for discovery motions.

 

Conclusion 

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Nahid Lahiji’s Motion to Reopen Discovery is DENIED.

Case Number: 22STCV10343    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Cazares, et al. v. Central Storage, Inc., et al.


MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

(CCP § 437c)

TENTATIVE RULING: 

 

Defendant Central Storage, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. DEFENDANT IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 10 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On March 25, 2022, Plaintiffs Marcus Cazares and Victoria Murdock (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendant Central Storage, Inc. (“Defendant”). Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint on June 27, 2023. At the case management conference on June 29, 2023, the Court set motions to strike or grant judgment on the pleadings on its own motion “because the case fails to state a cause of action as a matter of law with the exception of a possible one-month overpay for a month without adequate notice meaning about $100 due.” (Minute Order, 06/29/23.) The Court also set a Walker hearing for the same date to transfer the action to the limited jurisdiction court. (Ibid.) On July 20, 2023, the action was transferred to a limited jurisdiction court. (Minute Order, 07/20/23.)

 

Following transfer, the limited jurisdiction court held a trial setting conference and noted that no response had been filed to the First Amended Complaint. (Minute Order, 08/31/23.) The Court set an order to show cause regarding filing Defendant’s answer for November 2, 2023. (Ibid.) On October 31, 2023, Defendant filed a Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint, which the Court sustained without leave to amend as to the second and fourth causes of action for negligence, and overruled as to the first and third causes of action for breach of contract. (Minute Order, 12/13/23.) Defendant filed its Answer to the First Amended Complaint on January 25, 2024. 

 

Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment on March 22, 2024. Plaintiffs filed an opposition on August 22, 2024 and Defendant replied on August 29, 2024. 

 

Discussion

 

Allegations in the First Amended Complaint

 

The First Amended Complaint alleges that since about 2010, Plaintiffs contracted with Defendant to store their personal belongings and at all times have been current on their rent. (FAC, ¶10-11.) Defendant allegedly changed the terms of the contract by increasing the rent without providing 30 days notice. (FAC, ¶¶BC-1.) Defendant also illegally included an unconscionable term in violation of Civil Code section 1770, subdivision (a)(19). (Id. at Attachment to Cause_1, BC-2.) Defendant was also not registered as a business with the City of Glendale. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs have been damaged in the amount of overpayment of rent from March 1, 2022 to July 1, 2023. (Id. at Attachment to Cause_1, BC-2.) Defendant had a duty to be registered to do business in the City of Glendale per Ordinance Number 5.04.030. (FAC, ¶GN-1.) Defendant unlawfully collected rent because it did not have a right to raise rent on the storage units. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs have been damaged in the amount of rent charged illegally. (Ibid.)

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant’s request for judicial notice that January 30, 2022 is 30 days before March 1, 2022 is granted pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h).

 

Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice that January 30, 2022 was a Sunday is also granted pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h).

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

A defendant seeking summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established; or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) Plaintiff is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until Defendant meets its initial moving burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839-840.)

 

An action for breach of contract must demonstrate the following elements: (1) the existence of the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff. (D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.) Defendant moves for summary judgment on the grounds that there are no disputed facts as to its lack of liability for breach of storage agreement. The breach of contract causes of action are based on the allegation that Defendant changed the terms of the contract by increasing the rent without providing 30 days’ notice.

 

Defendant’s Initial Burden of Proof

 

In support of its Motion for Summary Judgment for the breach of contract causes of actions, Defendant presents the following facts as undisputed. The parties contracted for Plaintiffs to store their personal belongings. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 1; FAC, ¶¶10-11; Rosas Decl., ¶3.) The contract requires Defendant to provide 30 days’ notice before increasing the rental rate. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 2; FAC, Attachment, Term (4); Rosas Decl., ¶4.) The rent increase notices were sent to Plaintiffs on January 30, 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 3; Rosas Decl., ¶5 and Exh. 5.) The effective date of the rent increases was March 1, 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 4; Rosas Decl., ¶6 and Exh. B.) On January 30, 2022, Defendant received confirmation that Plaintiffs received and opened the messages with the rate change notice. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 5; Rosas Decl., ¶7 and Exh. B.) January 30, 2022 was 30 days before March 1, 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 6; RJN.)

 

This evidence carries Defendant’s initial burden of proof that there are no disputed facts regarding its lack of liability for breaching the parties’ contract. Specifically, Defendant provided notice of the rental charge increase as required by the parties’ contract such that Defendant did not breach that term of the contract. The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to demonstrate the existence of triable issues of material fact regarding Defendant’s breach of the notice provision.

 

 

 

Plaintiffs’ Responsive Burden of Proof

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that the parties’ agreement does not allow notice by electronic mail. (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact No. 3.) The opposition, however, does not cite to any language in the parties’ rental agreement prohibiting electronic notice. (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact No. 3.) The parties’ rental agreement merely states “Operator may adjust Monthly Rental Rate effective on the next monthly due date after giving Occupant(s) thirty (30) days written notice.” (FAC, Attachment, Term (4).)

 

Plaintiffs also argue that Defendant’s Motion does not address the causes of action for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing or unconscionable contract term. No such causes of action are alleged in the First Amended Complaint, however. Plaintiffs may have included conclusory allegations in the breach of contract causes of action that Defendant “illegally included an unconscionable term in the contract” and “breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing” but this is not sufficient to state those causes of action without supporting facts. (See FAC, ¶¶BC-2.)

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs have not demonstrated the existence of triable issues of material fact regarding Defendant’s breach of the parties’ agreement.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Central Storage, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. DEFENDANT IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 10 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

Midway Rent a Car, Inc. v. Salazar, et al.

MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED;

REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(CCP § 2033.280)

TENTATIVE RULING:  

           

Plaintiff Midway Rent a Car, Inc. dba Midway Car Rental’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. DEFENDANT CARLOS A. SALAZAR IS ORDERED TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $360.00 TO PLAINTIFF’S COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Midway Rent a Car, Inc. dba Midway Car Rental (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Carlos A. Salazar (“Defendant”) on October 19, 2023. Defendant answered in propria persona on December 1, 2023. On December 7, 2023, Plaintiff served Requests for Admissions on Defendant. (Motion, Moss Decl., Exh. 1.) To date, despite a meet and confer effort, Defendant has not served verified responses to the Requests for Admission. (Id. at ¶¶6-7 and Exh. 2.) Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted (“the Motion”) on July 11, 2024. The Motion initially came for hearing on September 10, 2024, at which time Defendant appeared through an attorney, Evan Selik (“Selik”). (Minute Order, 09/10/24.) The Court, on its own motion, continued the hearing to September 24, 2024. (Ibid.)

 

No substitution of attorney, nor additional papers regarding the Motion, have been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

There is no requirement for a prior meet and confer effort before a motion to deem requests for admission can be filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.) Further, the motion can be brought at any time after the responding party fails to provide the responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.) Due to Defendant’s failure to timely serve verified responses and resultant waiver of objections, Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming the Requests for Admission admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.)

 

The Court is required to award sanctions under the moving statute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) While properly noticed, the sanctions sought are excessive. Under a lodestar calculation, sanctions are granted against Defendant in the amount of $360.00 based on one hour of attorney time billed at $300.00 per hour, plus the $60.00 filing fee. (Motion, Moss Decl., ¶8.)

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Midway Rent a Car, Inc. dba Midway Car Rental’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. DEFENDANT CARLOS A. SALAZAR IS ORDERED TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $360.00 TO PLAINTIFF’S COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER.
