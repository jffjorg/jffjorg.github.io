DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

Case Number: 21CMCV00214    Hearing Date: July 9, 2024    Dept: A

21CMCV00214 Tabitha Patton v. Ramona King

Tuesday, July 9, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

 [TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

 

I.        BACKGROUND

      The verified, first amended complaint (“FAC”) alleges that Defendant, Ramona King (“Ramona”), forged Plaintiff’s signature on a quitclaim deed purporting to transfer Plaintiff’s 50 percent interest in real property to Ramona, leaving Ramona as the sole owner of the property. Plaintiff alleges claims for (1) forgery, (2) cancellation of instrument, (3) quiet title, (4) slander of title, (5) fraudulent concealment, (6) theft (Penal Code § 496), (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (8) declaratory and injunctive relief and (9) negligence.     

      At a non-contested, non-jury trial held on April 17, 2024, the Court found in Plaintiff’s favor specifically against Ramona on the claims for (1) forgery, (2) cancellation of instrument, (3) quiet title, (4) slander of title, (6) theft (8) declaratory and injunctive relief. The Court cancelled the quit claim deed at issue and quieted each of Defendants’ respective title to the real property. The Court declared that Plaintiff and Ramona have a 50 percent interest in the property as tenants in common. Otherwise, the Court found that Plaintiff did not prove compensatory or punitive damages.

 

II.      PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

      Plaintiff argues that the Court found in favor of Plaintiff and found that Ramona committed forgery and filed a false quit claim deed. Ramona forged Plaintiff’s signature and forged Plaintiff’s thumb print. Plaintiff argues she is entitled to attorney’s fees under Penal Code § 496 and § 484 governing theft by any false or fraudulent misrepresentation.

      Plaintiff timely served the motion on Ramona, who did not file an opposition.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

      A prevailing party in any action or proceeding is entitled to costs, including attorney’s fees, if such fees are authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a) (4) and (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10(B).) If a statute refers to the award of costs and attorney’s fees, attorney’s fees are an “item and component of the costs to be awarded and are allowable as costs pursuant to subparagraph (B) of paragraph (10) of subdivision. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5)(A).) If any party recovers other than monetary relief, the “prevailing party” shall be determined by the court, and in its discretion, the court may award costs or not.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032 subd. (a)(4).)

      In pertinent part, Penal Code, section 496 imposes criminal liability on a person who buys or receives any property obtained "in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing that property is so stolen or obtained.” (Pen. Code, § 496, subd. (a).) Moreover, any person injured by a violation of section 496 subd. (a) or (b) "may bring an action for three times the amount of actual damages, if any, sustained by the plaintiff, costs of suit, and reasonable attorney's fees." (Pen. Code, § 496, subd. (c).) (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 119 ["Although section 496 defines a criminal offense, it also provides an enhanced civil remedy in the event there has been a violation of the statute—that is, where a person has knowingly received, withheld or sold property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft."].)

      For purposes of the statute “property” means real or personal property. (Pen. Code, § 450, subd. (c).) A person is guilty of “theft” if the person "shall knowingly and designedly, by any false or fraudulent representation or pretense, defraud any other person of money, labor or real or personal property … .” (Pen. Code, § 484, subd. (a).) A criminal conviction of Section 496 is not a prerequisite to recovering civil remedies. (Lacagnina v. Comprehend Systems, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 955, 970 [acknowledging entitlement to civil treble damages under section 496, subd. (c)].)  

      Accordingly, Plaintiff has established entitlement to an award of attorney’s fees as the Court found in Plaintiff’s favor on the sixth cause of action for theft pursuant to Penal Code, section 496. Plaintiff requests an award of $12,084 dollars in fees which counsel charged Plaintiff as a “flat fee”. However, an award of fees as an element of costs under Civil Procedure, section 1033.5 “shall be reasonable in amount and necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5 subd. (c).)  Fees may be fixed by noticed motion or by other enumerated means. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd (b)(5)(A).)

      To determine whether fees are reasonable, the court begins with the lodestar, which is the number of hours reasonably spent multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) The court considers several factors including "the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.’” (PLCM Group at 1096.) The lodestar figure can be adjusted based on the factors specific to the case. (Id.)

      To determine a reasonable market rate, "the courts will look to equally difficult or complex types of litigation.” (Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 700.) The “market rate” is generally based on the rates prevalent in the community where the court is located. (Id.) The trial court is in the best position to value the services rendered by the attorneys in his or her courtroom for the type of litigation at issue. The prevailing party is entitled to “’compensation for all the hours reasonably spent‘ in litigating the action to a successful conclusion. (Ibid., italics in original.) ‘Reasonably spent’ means that time spent ‘in the form of inefficient or duplicative efforts is not subject to compensation. (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 394.)

      The Court has considered the Declaration of Mark Bruce filed on June 27, 2024, and all supporting documentation. The Courts finds that counsel’s hourly rate of $400 per hour is reasonable and $185 for legal assistance is reasonable. Having reviewed the itemized billing record the Court finds that 37.8 hours in litigating the action were reasonable and necessary.

IV.    CONCLUSION

      Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees is GRANTED. The Court awards fees of $10,734.00
