DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

      CASE NO: 23CMCV00831

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S DEPOSITION

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 17, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 06/06/2023

TRIAL: None set

 

 

This is an assault case. Plaintiff Norma Wilson (“Plaintiff”) alleges in the complaint that an unknown employee (“Doe”) of defendant Nails on Fleek (“Nails”) aggressively touched and tugged on Plaintiff’s arm despite being asked to stop, resulting in injuries to Plaintiff. The complaint alleges causes of action for assault, battery, and intentional infliction of emotional distress against Doe. The complaint alleges a cause of action for negligence against all defendants and a cause of action for negligent hiring, supervision, and retention against Nails.

Nails moves for an order compelling deposition of Plaintiff, on the grounds that Plaintiff did not appear for a deposition set for November 6, 2024. Nails subsequently attempted to confer with Plaintiff’s counsel to arrange a new date for Plaintiff’s deposition, but did not receive a response. Nails also requests sanctions and costs in the amount of $665.  Plaintiff filed a late opposition to the motion on December 16, 2024, which the court declines to consider.

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, where a party or party-affiliated deponent fails to appear for a noticed deposition without having served a valid objection, the noticing party may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony and the production of any document or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

If such a motion is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the noticing party and against the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) The burden is on the party opposing sanctions to show substantial justification. (Doe v. United States Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1436.)

On October 2, 2024, Nails served a notice of deposition on Plaintiff for a deposition scheduled for October 23, 2024. (Powers Decl. ¶ 3; Ex. A.) Plaintiff requested to reschedule the deposition due to a scheduling conflict, and later due to complications from surgery, but did not provide alternative dates either before or after October 23, 2024; nonetheless, Nails canceled the October 23 deposition, and on October 25, 2024 rescheduled it for November 6, 2024. (Powers Decl. ¶¶ 4-8.) On November 5, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email advising that she just had surgery and was having severe complications, but did not serve objections. (Powers Decl. ¶ 9; Ex. D.) Nails did not reschedule the deposition, and on November 6, 2024, neither Plaintiff nor her counsel appeared for deposition. (Powers Decl. ¶ 10; Ex. E.) On November 8, 2024, Nails again attempted to confer with Plaintiff concerning available dates for the deposition, but did not receive a response by the time of the filing of the instant motion on November 15, 2024. (Powers Decl. ¶ 11.) 

On November 27, 2024, twelve days after the instant motion was filed, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Change of Handling Attorney, advising that attorney Yasmeh had left Downtown LA Law Group. 

Nails requests an order compelling Plaintiff to appear for deposition, and an order awarding $665 in monetary sanctions jointly and severally against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, Downtown LA Law Group and former counsel Delina Yasmeh.  This sum is comprised of reimbursement for the court reporter’s fee of $255, a filing fee of $60, and Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rate of $175 for two hours. Despite having been served with the motion seeking monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Ms. Yasmeh, neither Ms. Yasmeh nor Mr. Hankins or any other attorney from Downtown L.A. Law Group filed a timely opposition to the motion, or a declaration setting forth any reasons why monetary sanctions should not be awarded as requested in the motion.  Moreover, the belated opposition asserts facts that are not supported by a sworn declaration with respect to the reasons for Plaintiff’s failure to appear for deposition.

Notwithstanding the foregoing, the record as presented in the moving papers and declaration of counsel submitted therewith sufficiently establishes that Plaintiff and her counsel acted with substantial justification in failing to appear for the November 6 deposition, thereby making the imposition of monetary sanctions unjust.  Plaintiff’s counsel notified counsel for defendant the day before the November 6 deposition that she was having severe complications from surgery.  This was sufficient notice of the basis for an inability to appear at the deposition the following day, so as to trigger counsel for defendant’s duty under the Los Angeles Superior Court Guidelines for Civility in Litigation.

Appendix 3.A(a)(2) of the LASC Local Rules provides that “[a]fter a first extension, any additional requests for time should be dealt with by balancing the need for expedition against the deference one should ordinarily give to an opponent’s schedule of professional and personal engagements, the reasonableness of the length of extension requested…”  Section 3.A(e)(2) provides, “[i]n scheduling depositions, reasonable consideration should be given to accommodating schedules of opposing counsel and of the deponent, where it is possible to do so without prejudicing the client’s rights.” 

Defendant seeks an order compelling Plaintiff to submit to deposition on January 3, 2025, or alternatively before January 31, 2025 (nearly three months after the November 6 deposition).  Thus, it is apparent that there was and is no pressing need for expedition, either on November 6 or today.  Based upon counsel’s November 5, 2024 representation as an officer of the court that she was having “serious complications” from surgery, a reasonable delay to accommodate Plaintiff’s counsel’s recovery from surgery was warranted under 3.A(a)(2) and (e)(2), and would not have prejudiced Defendant.

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Nails’ motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition, but denies the request for monetary sanctions.  The parties are ordered to meet and confer on or before December 20, 2024 to decide on a mutually agreeable date on or before January 31, 2025 on which Plaintiff’s deposition shall proceed.

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

 [TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING  IN PART PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT’S PERSON MOST KNOWLEDGEABLE AND PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 17, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 09/25/2023

TRIAL: 10/29/2025

 

 

1.     Background

This is a lemon law case. Plaintiff Al Coronado (“Plaintiff”) alleges in the complaint that he purchased a 2023 Honda Passport with the VIN number 5FNYF8H69PB019828 (the “Vehicle”), for which defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) issued a written warranty establishing warranty and repair obligations. Plaintiff alleges that the Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other series defects and nonconformities.

Plaintiff alleges causes of action for:

(1)  Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of express warranty;

(2)  Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of implied warranty; and,

(3)  Violation of the Song-Beverly Act section 1793.2(b).

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff served a Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable on Defendant, containing Requests for Production of Documents. The deposition was noticed for February 12, 2024. On February 6, 2024, Defendant served its objections to the Notice of Deposition and Request for Production.

On September 17, 2024, Plaintiff served an Amended Notice of Deposition with Requests for Production of Documents on Defendant, noticing the deposition for October 9, 2024. On October 4, 2024, Defendant served its objections to the Amended Notice of Deposition.

On October 8, 2024, Plaintiff served a Second Amended Notice of Deposition with Requests for Production of Documents on Defendant, noticing the deposition for October 22, 2024. On October 18, 2024, Defendant served its objections to the Second Amended Notice of Deposition.

2.     Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable and Production of Documents

Plaintiff filed the instant motion on November 18, 2024, on the grounds that Defendant has failed to provide a witness as noticed and has not proffered all of the responsive documents to the Request for Production. Plaintiff requests sanctions and an order waiving Defendant’s objections and compelling Defendant to product a qualified Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) witness and all responsive documents. Plaintiff contends that the testimony and documents that he seeks are relevant to whether Defendant complied with the Song-Beverly Act. Plaintiff also contends that Defendant’s objections are boilerplate.

In opposition, Defendant contends that it has, in meet and confers, offered dates on which its PMK will be available for deposition, which Plaintiff has rejected. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s counsel is abusing the discovery process because Plaintiff’s counsel is aware that Defendant’s PMK witnesses are heavily impacted with numerous depositions and trials, and Plaintiff’s counsel therefore regularly makes unilateral demands for deposition on imminently upcoming dates then moves to compel. Defendant further argues that Plaintiff’s Requests for Production seek information that is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and therefore not discoverable.

In reply, Plaintiff argues that the proffered deposition date in June 2025 would require Plaintiff to wait eighteen months from the date of Plaintiff’s initial notice of deposition to depose Defendant’s PMK, which demonstrates a lack of good faith and would unduly prolong the resolution of the matter. Plaintiff also contends that the documents he requests are discoverable because they are probative of nonconformities within the Vehicle, Defendant’s knowledge of such nonconformities, and technical information required to obtain reliable expert opinion testimony.

3.     Motion to Compel Discovery Standards

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, where a party or party-affiliated deponent fails to appear for a noticed deposition without having served a valid objection, the noticing party may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony and the production of any document or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. If an objection is made and served at least three calendar days before the deposition date, any subsequent deposition shall not be used against the objecting party if the party did not attend the deposition and the court determines that the objection was valid. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (b).)

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . . [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . . .” Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 provides that misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery.

4.     Discussion

          a. Motion to Compel Deposition

In Defendant’s objections to Plaintiff’s notices of deposition, Defendant objected to Plaintiff’s unilateral selection of the deposition date and time, stating that neither the deponent nor Defendant’s counsel is available on the selected date and time. Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s three notices of deposition were identical. During meet and confer efforts, Defendant offered June 17, 2025 and June 18, 2025 as potential deposition dates for Defendant’s PMK, which Plaintiff rejected as too late. Defendant and Defendant’s PMK did not appear at any of Plaintiff’s noticed depositions.

June 17, 2025 and June 18, 2025 are not reasonable deposition dates, considering that Plaintiff first served a notice of deposition for Defendant’s PMK on January 26, 2024, and trial is set for October 29, 2025. These deposition dates would delay deposition of Defendant’s PMK for almost eighteen months from Plaintiff’s first notice of deposition. In its objections, Defendant expressed an intention to meet and confer to agree upon a mutually convenient deposition date; however, in all meet and confer efforts, Defendant offered and reiterated only the June 2025 dates. (Maugeri II Decl. ¶¶ 11-13.) Defendant did not adequately cooperate with rescheduling the deposition to a reasonable and mutually convenient date, and Defendant’s objection to the date of the deposition is therefore improper and overruled.

Defendant also objected that the proposed deposition would be unduly burdensome and oppressive. Defendant has stated in a declaration to this motion that the demand for depositions in California warranty litigation exceeds deponent availability. (Pacheco Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.) This is not a sufficient basis to establish that a particular deposition request would be unduly burdensome and oppressive, as it would permit Defendant to object to any deposition request as unduly burdensome and oppressive.

Defendant additionally objected to the matters on which Defendant’s PMK is to be examined. After consideration of Defendant’s objections, the court denies the motion to compel deposition matters for examination 30 and 32 (improper contention questions), and 34 (duplicative). The court grants the motion as to the remaining categories, with the exception that categories 2, 16, 18-29 are limited to the period starting with the date of purchase of the subject vehicle to the present.

          b. Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production

All of Defendant’s objections to each request in Plaintiff’s Requests for Production are identical, constituting the following:

AHM objects to this request as vague, ambiguous, and overly broad. AHM further objects to this request to the extent it may be interpreted to be asking for communications between AHM and its employees and AHM's legal counsel that is protected by the attorney-client privilege. In addition, AHM objects to this request as calling for information that is confidential, commercially sensitive, and proprietary or trade secret. The request is vague and overbroad because the term “YOU”/“YOUR” as defined includes third parties such as dealerships, and those who “represent themselves to be affiliated with, or work for, AHM.” AHM further objects to this request as it calls for a legal conclusion. AHM further objections to the request because is misleading and argumentative.

(Maugeri Decl. Ex. B.)

After consideration of Defendant’s objections, the court grants the motion as to requests for production 1, 3-9 and 15. The court grants the motion as to requests 2, and 11-14, but will limit the scope of definition of “DOCUMENTS,” contained with RFP No. 2, to exclude a search of internal emails in order to avoid undue burden on Defendant.  The court grants the motion as to request 10 to the extent it calls for production of non-privileged responsive documents, and Defendant is ordered to produce a privilege log for any documents withheld that are responsive to this request or any other requests for which the motion is granted.  The court denies the motion as to requests 16 and 17 as overbroad, unduly burdensome and oppressive, and as to 16 the request calls for attorney-work product.

c. Sanctions

Plaintiff has requested sanctions against Defendant in connection with the instant motion. However, Plaintiff has not informed the court of an amount of money that he desires be granted in sanctions, and has therefore not met his burden. The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

5.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition and GRANTS in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel request for production, as set forth herein. The court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

Parties are ordered to meet and confer no later than December 31, 2024 in order to determine a mutually agreeable date for deposition of Defendant’s PMK, subject to the court’s rulings on deposition categories and requests for production. The deposition shall take place no later than February 18, 2025.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PETITION FOR APPROVAL FOR TRANSFER OF STRUCTURED SETTLEMENT PAYMENT RIGHTS

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 17, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

PETITION FILED: 10/16/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

 

1.     Background

This is a petition for transfer of structured settlement payment rights (the “Petition”). The real party in interest, Kaiwann King, wishes to transfer some portion of their settlement rights to Asset Security Consulting, LLC (“Petitioner”).

2.     Petition for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights Standards

Insurance Code section 10139.5 requires the direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights to be approved in advance in a final court order. (Ins. Code, §10139.6, subd. (a).) In its order, the Court must make the following express, written findings:

“(1) The transfer is in the best interest of the payee, taking into account the welfare and support of the payee's dependents.

(2) The payee has been advised in writing by the transferee to seek independent professional advice regarding the transfer and has either received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing, the opportunity to receive the advice.

(3) The transferee has complied with the notification requirements pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (f), the transferee has provided the payee with a disclosure form that complies with Section 10136, and the transfer agreement complies with Sections 10136 and 10138.

(4) The transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority.

(5) The payee understands the terms of the transfer agreement, including the terms set forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136.

(6) The payee understands and does not wish to exercise the payee's right to cancel the transfer agreement.”

(Ibid.)

When determining whether the proposed transfer is in the payee’s best interest, taking into account the welfare and support of the payee’s dependents, the Court must consider the totality of the circumstances, including, but not limited to, all of the following factors:

“… (4) The terms of the transaction, including whether the payee is transferring monthly or lump sum payments or all or a portion of his or her future payments.

… (8) Whether the payee has other means of income or support, aside from the structured settlement payments that are the subject of the proposed transfer, sufficient to meet the payee's future financial obligations for maintenance and support of the payee's dependents, specifically including, but not limited to, the payee's child support obligations, if any. The payee shall disclose to the transferee and the court his or her court-ordered child support or maintenance obligations for the court's consideration.

(9) Whether the financial terms of the transaction, including the discount rate applied to determine the amount to be paid to the payee, the expenses and costs of the transaction for both the payee and the transferee, the size of the transaction, the available financial alternatives to the payee to achieve the payee's stated objectives, are fair and reasonable.

(10) Whether the payee completed previous transactions involving the payee's structured settlement payments and the timing and size of the previous transactions and whether the payee was satisfied with any previous transaction.

…”

(Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (b).)

3.     Discussion

Here, the Petition is partially censored, obscuring all information concerning the amount of the transfer or the amount of settlement rights currently possessed by Kaiwann King or to be possessed after the transfer. The court cannot reasonably make a determination of whether the transfer is in Kaiwann King’s best interest without this information.  Nor can the court ascertain whether all supporting documents were executed by Kaiwann King in light of the redactions of the signatures on all documents.  Moreover, a First Amended Verified Petition for Approval for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights was filed on November 6, 2024, after the filing of the October 23, 2024 Notice of Motion for Order Approving Petition for Approval For Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights, thereby rendering the instant motion moot.  Finally, no proof of service of the October 23, 2024 Notice of Motion for Order Approving Petition for Approval For Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights on Real Party in Interest/payee Kaiwann King has been filed.

4.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court DENIES WITHOUT PREJUDICE the Petition.

Petitioner is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 17, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 05/06/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

 

1.     Background

The court cannot identify what events underlie plaintiff DeAnn Loughridge’s (“Plaintiff") complaint. Plaintiff states in the complaint that she is alleging causes of action for obstruction of justice and civil rights against defendant State of California (“Defendant”).

2.     Demurrer

Defendant filed a demurrer on July 3, 2024, demurring to the entire complaint. Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to comply with the claim filing requirement of the Government Claims Act, that Plaintiff fails to plead a statutory cause of action, that Defendant is not a person subject to suit under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, and that the complaint’s allegations are uncertain.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that she has complied with all necessary procedural requirements and has provided in her complaint detailed accounts of her false arrest, excessive force, and subsequent injuries derived therefrom. Plaintiff also argues that the state can be held liable for the actions of its employees when those actions are part of official policy or custom and that the complaint clearly outlined Plaintiff’s allegations in a logical and comprehensible manner.  

3.     Demurrer Standards

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿(SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿A plaintiff must set forth “the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source and extent of his cause of action.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irr. Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.)

The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿(Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)

Leave to amend a complaint following a sustained demurrer must be granted if there is a reasonable probability that the defects in the complaint may be resolved by amendment. (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 69.)

4.     Discussion

The court cannot identify the facts underlying the complaint, nor can the court identify the causes of action being brought except that they are stated in the cover sheet of the complaint. Plaintiff has therefore failed to set forth the essential facts of her case with sufficient particularity to put Defendant on notice of the nature, source, and extent of her cause of action.  Plaintiff failed to allege compliance with the claim filing requirements of the Government Claims Act (Gov. Code §810, et seq.)  The complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §430.10 by failing to comply with the Government Claims Act, by failing to plead a statutory cause of action (Gov. Code § 815), and because defendant is not a person subject to suit under 42 U.S.C. § 1983.  In addition, the complaint is uncertain pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(f).

5.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer with leave to amend.

Defendant is ordered to give notice.
