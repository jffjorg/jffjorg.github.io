(2) Defendant Darlene Agarwala’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admission from Plaintiff Paul Herrera

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) Plaintiff’s combined motion to compel original responses and to deem RFAs admitted as to an octet of discovery requests is MOOTED by the tardy but still pre-hearing verified responses.  No sanctions are awarded considering the totality of the circumstances as more fully discussed below.

 

                                                (2) Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to requests for admission is GRANTED, and sanctions are awarded against Plaintiff and his counsel in the amount of $3,082.

 

                                                (3) Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories is GRANTED as to Interrogatories No. 1, 2, 12, 15–17, 19, 22, 24, 25, 27, 28, 30, 31, 33, 34, 36–40, 43, 45, 46, 49, 51, 52, 55, 57, 58, 61–64, 67, and 69, and DENIED as to the others. Partial sanctions are awarded in the amount of $2,082 given the partial success and partial lack of success of the motion as detailed below.

 

                                                (4) Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED as to Interrogatories No. 9.1 and 17.1, and DENIED as to Interrogatories No. 15.1, 50.3, and 50.5, except as noted below. No monetary sanctions are awarded for the FROGS motion.

¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ ¿¿¿ 

1.       Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿ This is an action for partition. The Second Amended Complaint alleges that the current owners of real property located on 129th Street in Hawthorne are the Plaintiff trustee of the Jan Agarwala Separate Property Trust, and two other trusts. (SAC ¶ 27.) A dispute has arisen between Plaintiff and Defendants regarding the management and sale of the property, and Plaintiff seeks partition by sale. (SAC ¶¶ 32–34.)

 

Cross-Complainants Pronita Ann Agarwala, Sabina Agarwala Miro, Stephen Miro and Sabina Miro seek an order quieting title to the property, seeking a declaration that the Agarwala defendants have no individual interest in the property at issue. (Cross-Complaint ¶ 15.)

2.       ¿¿Procedural  

The Complaint was filed on January 5, 2024, and the cross-complaint was filed on April 20, 2023.  Plaintiff filed the present motion to compel on February 2024. Defendants filed an opposition on March 28, 2024. Plaintiff filed a reply on April 2, 2024.

 

Defendant Darlene Agarwala filed the present motions to compel further on January 26, 2024. Plaintiff filed oppositions on March 7, 2024. Defendant filed replies on March 13, 2024.

 

On April 11, 2024, this Court directed the parties to meet and confer regarding the special interrogatories and requests for production (at issue in a motion not set currently set for hearing) and directed the filing of a truncated separate statement of the special interrogatories remaining at issue by May 2, 2024. That separate statement was filed on May 3, 2024.

 

II. ANALYSIS¿¿ ¿¿ 

 

A.    MOTIONS TO COMPEL

1.               Legal Standard ¿¿ 

A propounding party may demand a responding party to produce documents that are in their possession, custody or control. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010.) A party may likewise conduct discovery by propounding interrogatories to another party to be answered under oath. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.010, subd. (a).) The responding party must respond to the production demand either by complying, by representing that the party lacks the ability to comply, or by objecting to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210.) The responding party must respond to the interrogatories by answering or objecting. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.210, subd. (a).) If the responding party fails to serve timely responses, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses to the production demand and interrogatories. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.)

A party who fails to serve a timely response to interrogatories or a demand for inspection waives any objection to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.)

Likewise, “[a]ny party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.) If a party fails to serve a timely response to requests for admissions, “[t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280 subd. (b).)

A party who fails to timely respond to requests for admission waives all objections to the requests. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (a).)

2.               Discussion   

¿ Plaintiff Paul Herrera (Plaintiff) seeks to compel responses to the following discovery from the following parties:

·       Form Interrogatories, Set Three, from Defendant Darlene Agarwala;

·       Special Interrogatories, Set Three, and Requests for Production, Set Two, from Defendant Pronita Agarwala;

·       Special Interrogatories, Set Three, and Requests for Production, Set Two, from Defendant Sabina Agarwala Miro;

·       Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Production, Set Two, from Defendant Stephen Miro;

·       Gregory Mollner.

(Motion at pp. 2–3.) Plaintiff also seeks an order deeming matters admitted against Defendant Darlene Agarwala. (Motion at p. 3.)

All of the above discovery was served upon Defendants on November 16, 2023, with responses due on February 23, 2024, pursuant to extensions agreed to by the parties.. (Amboss Decl. ¶¶ 2–7.) However, no responses were timely served. (Amboss Decl. ¶ 8.)

Defendants in opposition argue that the motion is procedurally improper, as it is filed by Paul Herrera, rather than Catherine Herrera, the party who served the discovery. (Motion at pp. 2–3.) Defendants also argue that the motion impermissibly combines ten separate sets of discovery responses against four defendants into one motion. (Motion at pp. 3–4.) Defendants finally argue that the motion is moot, as all defendants other than Gregory Mollner (who is deceased) have served responses. (Opposition at p. 4; Stein Decl. ¶ 6.)  The Court disagrees with Defendants’ procedural objections, but agrees that the motion to compel initial responses is moot given the tardy responses served before the hearing on this motion. 

There is no procedural defect with the present motion. Paul Herrera may bring the motion to compel, even though Catherine Herrera served the discovery, because, as stated in the Second Amended Complaint filed on January 5, 2024, Catherine Herrera has resigned as trustee of the plaintiff trust, and Paul Herrera has assumed the duties of a successor trustee. (SAC ¶¶ 2–3.) As this is an action prosecuted by the trustee of a particular trust, Paul Herrera may seek responses to discovery propounded by his predecessor.

There is also no material defect in the combining of discovery responses and defendants in this single motion. While a party could potentially use such a practice to evade restrictions on a court’s calendar and seek resolution of multiple complex issues in a single omnibus discovery motion, that is not what occurred here. As noted in the motion, all discovery at issue here was served on November 16, 2023, and the main issue is simply whether responses were served by the date agreed. To separate these motions into ten separate filings would increase the burden on the Court and parties, not reduce it.

However, Plaintiff does not contest that responses have been provided since the motion was filed. Although Plaintiff argues that the requests for admission should nonetheless be deemed admitted, this is specifically prohibited by statute, since an order deeming admissions shall be made unless responses are served “before the hearing on the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (b).) Accordingly, the motion to deem the RFAs admitted is MOOTED since belated responses have been served before the hearing.  As the Court advised the parties on May 7 during the ex parte hearing to advance a motion to set aside deemed admissions, there is no need to combine the hearings on the Motion to Deem RFAs admitted with the now-mooted motion for relief from deemed admissions.  The fact that counsel could not resolve that issue without Court involvement, even after the Court gave its view of how it would be resolving the Plaintiff’s motion, is troubling for the future of this case.

This leaves only the question of sanctions. The prevailing party on a motion to compel is generally entitled to monetary sanctions, unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.) Sanctions are also mandatory against a party whose failure to serve responses to requests for admission makes the motion necessary. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c).) The Court is aware of the general rule that “If a party fails to serve a timely response, and the propounding party moves for and obtains a court order compelling a response, the trial court must impose a monetary sanction against the delinquent party unless that party acted with ‘substantial justification’ or the sanction would otherwise be unjust.”  (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)  Here, the Court finds the highly unusual circumstances that would make the imposition of monetary sanctions unjust. 

 

Plaintiff seeks $1,500 in sanctions against Defendants and their counsel. (Motion at p. 15.) But in the declaration supporting the motion, Plaintiff seeks $4,075.00 in sanctions, representing ten hours of attorney work at $400 per hour, plus $75 in filing fees. (Fleck Decl. ¶ 16.)

No sanctions are warranted here. The likely cause of the delay in serving responses was as stated in Defendant’s opposition: the grave illness of Defendant Gregory Mollner, leading to his death in January 2024, the illness and death of Defendant Stephen Miro’s father in the same month, and Defense counsel’s contracting of COVID during that month, and the illness and hospitalization of counsel’s mother in February 2024. (Agarwala Decl. ¶¶ 2–4; Stein Decl. ¶¶ 5–6.) Circumstances would make the imposition of sanctions here unjust.  The sanctions aspect of the motion is therefore DENIED.

B.    DEFENDANTS’ MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

1.     Legal Standard

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.010(a).) If a propounding party is not satisfied with the response served by a responding party, the former may move the court to compel further interrogatory responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)  The propounding party must demonstrate that the responses were incomplete, inadequate or evasive, or that the responding party asserted objections that are either without merit or too general.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(a)(1)–(3); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 403.)

A motion to compel further responses to requests for admissions may be made on the grounds that an answer is incomplete or evasive, or an objection is without merit. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.290, subd. (a)(1)–(2).)

2.     Discussion

Defendant Darlene Agarwala filed motions on January 26, 2024, seeking further responses from Plaintiff Paul Herrera to Form Interrogatories No. 9.1, 15.1, 17.1, 50.3, and 50.5; Special Interrogatories No. 1–19, 21, 22, 24, 25, and 27–69; and Requests for Admission No. 19, 20, and 60–63.

On April 11, 2024, this Court, per a stipulation of the parties, continued the hearing on these motions to May 8, 2024, and directed that the parties meet and confer regarding the discovery at issue. The Court directed that a separate statement was to be filed containing the Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production that remain at issue. Such a statement addressed to the special interrogatories was filed on May 3, 2024, indicating that Special Interrogatories No. 1–8, 11–19, 21, 22, 24, 25, 27, 28, 30, 31, 33, 34, 36–40, 43, 45, 46, 49, 51, 52, 55, 57, 58, 61–64, 67, and 69 remain at issue.

a.     Requests for Admission

The requests for admission will be addressed first, as this is the simplest discovery dispute of those presented. Responses to requests for admission must “[a]dmit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true,” and “[d]eny so much of the matter involved in the request is untrue.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.220, subd. (b)(1), (2).)

A further response is required as to Requests No. 19 and 20. These requests sought admissions that the amount of capital contributions stated on an escrow closing statement reflected “the capital contribution” of Jan Agarwala to the acquisition of a given property, to which Plaintiff responded with admissions that it reflected “a capital contribution” by Jan Agarwala, and a denial “that this number reflects the entirety of Jan Agarwala’s contribution.” (Separate Statement at pp. 3–4.) These responses are evasive, however, as they seek an admission or denial concerning the amount of “the capital contribution of Jan Agarwala toward the acquisition of the . . . property,” and Plaintiff’s response is equivocal, admitting only that the amounts identified reflect “a capital contribution,” and implying that other “contribution[s]” were made, without stating whether these contributions were capital contributions toward the acquisition of the property or some other type of contribution besides a capital contribution.

Plaintiff in opposition contends that its response is proper because “Responding Party does not yet have all the answers.” (Opposition at p. 5.) But he also contends in his separate statement that it is his “understanding and belief that Jan Agarwala  . . . contributed more than is reflected  in the closing statement,” again without specifying whether this “contribution” was a capital contribution to the acquisition of the property. (Separate Statement at p. 5.) A further verified admission or denial is required.

Requests No. 60–63 seek admissions that, prior to Jan Agarwala’s death, Catherine Herrera obtained documents related to the purchase, maintenance, income and expenses of the 12th Street Property, and further obtained documents related to the Oak Property. (Separate Statement at pp. 4­–6.) Plaintiff initially responded with objections, then provided a supplemental response to each request stating, “Unable to admit or deny.” (Ibid.)

This was insufficient. There is a means for a party responding to admissions to indicate when they lack information: a responding party may “[s]pecify so much of the matter involved in the request as to the truth of which the responding party lacks sufficient information or knowledge.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.220, subd. (b)(3).) When the party does so, they must “state in the answer that a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter in the particular request has been made, and that the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable that party to admit the matter. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220, subd. (c).) If Plaintiff truly lacks sufficient knowledge to answer, he did not avail himself of this mode of response. Thus further verified responses are required on all requests for admission at issue.

b.     Form Interrogatories

The form interrogatories at issue seek facts, witnesses, and evidence supporting Plaintiff’s claims for damages (No. 9.1); Plaintiff’s denials of allegations and affirmatives defenses (No. 15.1); and Plaintiff’s responses to requests for admission that were not unqualified admissions (No. 17.1). Interrogatories No. 50.3 and 50.5 asked Plaintiff to identify and explain each agreement alleged in the agreement is unenforceable, or for which performance was excused.

Plaintiff responded to Interrogatory No. 9.1 by stating he could not provide an amount of damages because he does not possess a full accounting for the properties. (Separate Statement at p. 2.) Plaintiff responded to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1, with respect to denials of facts alleged in the cross-complaint, that it “lacks sufficient information to admit or deny what Cross-Complainants are informed and believe.” (Separate Statement at pp. 7–15.) Plaintiff responded to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1 with factual summaries justifying its denials, and identified no documents, instead stating “all relevant documents have been produced or will be produced pursuant to First Set of Inspection Demands.” (Separate Statement at pp. 15–24.) Plaintiff responded to Interrogatories No. 50.3 and 50.5 by objecting that they called for a legal conclusion, denying that performance under any agreement had been excused, and stating that the statute of limitations for any breach of the oral agreement had expired. (Separate Statement at pp. 25–26.)

Further responses are required as to Interrogatory No. 9.1. Although Plaintiff contends he lacks a full accounting for the properties, he must provide what information is currently in his possession: “If only partial answers can be supplied, the answers should reveal all information then available to the party. If a person cannot furnish details, he should set forth the efforts made to secure the information.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 782.)

No further response is required as to Interrogatory No. 15.1, however, because Plaintiff asserts, without rebuttal in reply, that the paragraphs at issue formed no part of the parties’ prior meet and confer efforts. (Amboss Decl. ¶ 10.)   The Discovery Act allow for supplemental interrogatories later in the case, so the Court’s denial of the motion as to the 15.1 series is without prejudice to a later supplemental interrogatory. 

Further responses are required as to Interrogatory No. 17.1, specifically relating to the generally framed and unhelpful document identifications offered by Plaintiff, which identify no documents, and which merely promise that responsive documents have been (or will be) produced at some point. This is insufficient.

No further responses are required as to Interrogatories No. 50.3 and 50.5, to which Plaintiff provided a substantive response that Defendant does not challenge. (Separate Statement at p. 26.) Plaintiff only objects to the “legal conclusion” objection that frames the response. (Ibid.)  The Court overrules that objection, so the substantive response shall stand.

The motion is therefore GRANTED as to Interrogatories No. 9.1 and 17.1, and DENIED as to Interrogatories No. 15.1, 50.3, and 50.5 except as noted above.

c.      Special Interrogatories

For the special interrogatories, the court may first address Interrogatories No. 19, 22, 25, 28, 31, 34, 37, 40, 43, 46, 49, 52, 55, 58, 61, 64, and 67. These interrogatories are similar. Although they relate to different allegations, they each called upon Plaintiff to identify witnesses with knowledge of responsive facts, to which Plaintiff responded by offering a list of names. Defendant here contends that the call of the interrogatory was to “identify” individuals, with the term “identify” defined as providing the name and all known contact information, such as addresses, phone numbers, and email addresses. However, Plaintiff contends that four of the people identified are Defendants themselves, and that the other persons identified are already known to Defendant, who was a party to all transactions identified. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement at pp. 26–27.)

No further response need be provided as to contact information for people who are parties to this action, as such information is already in Defendant’s possession. However, Plaintiff’s statements concerning Defendant’s preexisting knowledge of other individuals named in the responses are unsupported. Accordingly, Defendant must provide responsive contact information for non-party individuals identified in its response, as is available through a reasonable inquiry. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220, subd. (c).)

The motion is therefore GRANTED as to Special Interrogatories No. 19, 22, 25, 28, 31, 34, 37, 40, 43, 46, 49, 52, 55, 58, 61, 64, and 67.

Special Interrogatories No. 1 and 2 asked how much money Jan Agarwala contributed to the purchase of the property, to which Plaintiff responded by stating that she contributed $154,241.55 “toward the acquisition cost of the 129th Street Property. . . plus an additional $130,000.” (Separate Statement at pp. 4–5.) These responses, however, are not clear as to whether the “additional” $130,000 was part of the acquisition, or is attributable to some other sum or claimed contribution of value. Thus a further response is required, and the motion is GRANTED as to Special Interrogatory No. 1 and 2.

Interrogatories No. 3–8 asked Plaintiff to state monetary contributions by Jan Agarwala toward specified sums, to which he initially responded by reference to broad categories of production already provided, then amended the response to state only that, after a reasonable inquiry, “I do not have sufficient information to answer this question.” (Separate Statement at pp. 5–8.) This answer is sufficient under Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.220, and no further response is required.

Interrogatories No. 11–16 asked how much money Jan Agarwala contributed to various costs, to which Plaintiff responded either with a statement that he lacked the documentation that would contain responsive information, or else by pointing to his general document production. (Separate Statement at pp. 8–11.)

No further response is necessary as to Interrogatory No. 11, 13, and 14, as Plaintiff has responded that the answer depends upon documentation that is not within its possession, and identifying the documents needed to make the response. However, a further response is necessary as to Interrogatories No. 12, 15, and 16, as Plaintiff’s statement that answers may be found in his general document production is overly broad and unhelpful to the propounding party. As the Second District ruled decades ago, “it is not proper to answer by stating, “See my deposition”, “See my pleading”, or “See the financial statement”. Indeed, if a question does require the responding party to make reference to a pleading or document, the pleading or document should be identified and summarized so the answer is fully responsive to the question.”  (Deyo v. Kilbourne, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at pp. 783–784.) 

Thus the motion is GRANTED as to Interrogatories No. 12, 15, and 16.

Interrogatories No. 17 and 69 asked Plaintiff to state the items and basis by which he seeks costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 874.010, and to itemize all costs heretofore incurred. (Separate Statement at p. 12.) Defendant responded that costs would be determined at the close of the action and had not yet been fully incurred. (Ibid.) A further response is required, as the costs awardable under Code of Civil Procedure § 874.010 include more than litigation costs, but those incurred by any party determined by the court to have been “incurred or paid for the common benefit.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 874.010, subd. (e).) Although the court will presumably determine the amount and  apportionment of these costs at the appointed time, the same is true for virtually all factual matters submitted to the court, which are generally proper matters for discovery. Defendant may inquire into the costs incurred under this statute, and the motion is GRANTED as to Interrogatories No. 17 and 69.

Interrogatories No. 18, 21, 24, 27, 30, 33, 36, 39, 45, 48, 51, 57, and 63 are similar to one another, in that they asked Plaintiff to state “in complete detail” the facts supporting a given allegation or denial thereof. For two of these interrogatories — Interrogatories No. 18 and 21 —Plaintiff responded with a permissible reference to a document as provided in Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.230, stating that certain trust documents contained the facts relied upon. (Separate Statement at pp. 12–14.)

However, further responses are warranted for the other interrogatories. Interrogatory No. 24 sought facts justifying the contention that Jan Agarwala contributed to 48% of a given property’s acquisition costs, to which Plaintiff responded by reference to the dollar amount that Jan Agarwala contributed, rather than the percentage of the acquisition cost. (Separate Statement at p. 15.) Interrogatories No. 27, 30, and 33 sought information concerning Defendant’s challenge to apportionments contained in a 2019 grant deed, to which Plaintiff responded only by reference to the grant deed itself, without citation to facts justifying any statements in the grant deed. (Separate Statement at pp. 16–19.) When asked for facts and documents to support the contention that Jan Agarwala does not owe $25,000 in Interrogatories No. 36 and 38, Plaintiff responded only by stating that he was “unaware” of facts or documents to support the allegation. (Separate Statement at pp. 20–21.) And with respect to Interrogatories No. 39, 45, 51, 57, and 63, which asked Plaintiff to state the facts supporting the contention that a given Defendant owned a personal interest in the property, Plaintiff responded by stating that each party “has an interest” in the property “in [their] capacity as trustee.” (Separate Statement at pp. 21–29.) These responses neither deny the premise of the interrogatory nor offer responsive information.

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to Special Interrogatories No. 1, 2, 12, 15–17, 19,, 22, 24, 25, 27, 28, 30, 31, 33, 34, 36–40, 43, 45, 46, 49, 51, 52, 55, 57, 58, 61–64, 67, and 69. The motion is otherwise DENIED.

d.     Sanctions

The Discovery Act statute provides that the court shall impose sanctions upon a party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to interrogatories or requests for production of documents, absent substantial justification otherwise. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (d); 2031.310, subd. (h).)

Defendant seeks $7,785.36 in connection with the form interrogatories motion, representing 14 hours of attorney work at $550 per hour, plus $85.36 in filing expenses; $5,032.80 in connection with the special interrogatories motion, representing nine hours of attorney work at the same rate, plus an additional $82.80 in filing expenses; and $4,482.80 in connection with the requests for admissions motion, representing eight hours of attorney work at the same rate, plus an additional $82.80 filing fee. (Stein Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendant thus seeks a total of $17,300.96 in connection with these motions. The amount requested is unreasonable and excessive. 

Sanctions are appropriate here for the motion to compel further requests for admissions, as Plaintiff may obtain further responses to all requests at issue, and the defects could have been mitigated by modest changes to the responses. The Court awards $3,082 in monetary sanctions for the RFA motion.  However, as Defendant has only obtained partial relief with respect to the other motions, the circumstances require a downward adjustment consistent with the success and lack of success of the motions.  The Court awards $2,082 as to the special interrogatories motion but none as to the form interrogatories motion.

2.       ¿¿Procedural  

The original complaint was filed on October 19, 2022.

 

On June 6, 2023, this Court issued guidance concerning the parties’ meet and confer efforts on Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production, and continued the hearing.

 

On September 13, 2023, this Court granted in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel further.

 

Plaintiff filed the present motion to compel compliance on January 30, 2024. Defendant filed an opposition on February 26, 2024. Hearing on the motion was held on March 8, 2024, with the parties directed to supply additional briefing for the court’s consideration. Defendant filed its supplemental briefing with a Declaration of defense counsel Habib on March 20, 2024.  In her declaration, Ms. Habib listed Bates numbers as the Court required for the technical documents and TSBs GM produced under a verification of a GM employee in response to RFPs 24, 25, 76, 76, 78, and 79. 

 

Plaintiff filed a supplemental declaration of counsel Ebony Randolf on May 1, 2024, which essentially asserts GM did nothing and is in complete violation of prior orders.  She asserts that GM did not produce ESI or emails relating to its internal investigation of the battery defect issues But there is an ambiguity in Ms. Randolf’s presentation, which is the use of the word “verified” when describing what GM did or did not do.  It would be helpful to the Court in oral argument is Plaintiff could separate out the question of whether GM has provided further responses and further documents, or whether what is missing is the verification for such materials.  It appears that Ms. Randolph asserts what while GM may have turned over some technical literature, it still has not produced a single communication as required by the granted motion as to RFP 76 between GM and NHTSA regarding a remedy, repair or replacement for a Chevy Volt’s battery-related issues of a fire, melting, burning, or failure to maintain or reach a full charge.  Typically there is a transmittal letter and a response letter or a report, but per Plaintiff, GM has not provided such things.  Ms. Randolph also asserts that GM has not produced the Court-ordered single document as to RFP 24 and 25 that evidences the date and circumstances when a GM engineer and a GM employee who makes recommendations to consider a safety recall campaign first learned of the existence of a battery-related issue with 2017 Chevy Volts like the plaintiff’s here.  Ms. Habib filed a supplemental declaration on May 3, 2024.

 

            Part of the difficulty for the Court is that the lawyers are repeatedly arguing their position, without showing the Court what the Bates-numbered documents are so the Court can make an independent determination of compliance.  For example, GM identified Bates numbers for its response to RFPs 24 and 25, but without showing those documents to the Court, the judge cannot determine which of the two lawyers is telling the Court the truth about compliance or lack of compliance. 

 

Ms. Randolph does not explain in her declaration any RFPs as to which no documents were produced, or what is incomplete about the document production.  She does attach excerpts from a class action complaint in 2015 in the Eastern District of Michigan to evidence databases that GM was alleged a decade ago to have maintained where documents could be located, but did not attach a single Bates-numbered document from the production in this case so the Court could see that GM’s listing is non-compliant.  GM is guilty of the same problem.  The Habib declaration asserts that GM is fully compliant, but there is no verification provided as an exhibit to the declaration, none of the Bates-numbered documents are provided to show that GM in fact complied.  For example, did GM produce an early warning or TREAD report in response to RFPs 78 and 79?  Did GM produce a “communication” between GM and NHTSA?

 

II. ANALYSIS¿¿ ¿¿ 

This Court on September 13, 2023, granted in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production No. 24, 25, 42, 76, 78, 79, and 90,  The court’s order as to each response was as follows:

·       No. 24: Defendant “to produce the “single document that evidences the date and circumstances when a GM employee with the title or job description of engineer first learned of the existence of a battery charging, melting, or burning problem in a Chevrolet Volt vehicle”;

·       No. 25: Defendant “to produce the single document that evidences the date and circumstances when a GM employee -- who then sat on a team or committee that makes recommendations to the Legal Department or management to consider a safety recall campaign -- first learned of the claim of a battery-related fire, melting, or burning problem in a Chevrolet Volt vehicle.

·       No. 42: Defendant “to produce non-privileged documents . . . disseminated to the dealer body that evidences a remedy, repair, or component replacement to address a customer complaint -- applicable to a model year 2017 Chevy Volt – of a failure to maintain or reach a full charge, or for a battery-related fire, melting, or burning complaint.”

·       No. 76: Defendant to produce “communications” with NHTSA or EPA “regarding a report to either of those government agencies or a response to a request from either of those agencies that evidences a remedy, repair, or component replacement applicable to the battery in a model year 2017 Chevy Volt of a failure of an e-vehicle battery to maintain or reach a full charge, or for a battery-related fire, melting, or burning complaint.”

·       No. 78: Defendant to produce “to produce any early warning reports GM submitted to NHTSA that included in their scope model year 2017 Chevy Volts.”

·       No. 79: Defendant “to produce any TREAD reports GM submitted to NHTSA that included in their scope model year 2017 Chevy Volts.”

·       No. 90: Defendant to produce “any customer literature . . . or any technical literature dated in 2017  .. . that evidence the battery performance standards GM promulgates for model year 2017 Chevy Volts.”

 The Court is inclined to order GM to provide a duplicate copy of its actual verified written response, whether original, supplemental, amended or otherwise, PLUS the Bates-numbered documents produced.  Only then can the Court evaluation the merits of the disputing counsels’ positions.

HEARING DATE:                 May 8, 2024 


CASE NUMBER:                   24TRCV00144  


CASE NAME:                        Jorge Lara Rojas v. String King Lacrosse, LLC   


MOVING PARTY:                Defendant String King Lacrosse, LLC  

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Jorge Lara Rojas (no opposition)


TRIAL DATE:                        None set  


MOTION:                              (1) Motion to Stay Proceedings 


Tentative Rulings:                  (1) The motion to stay is GRANTED for 90 days, at which point the parties are to return for a further CMC


I. BACKGROUND

1.     Factual 

 This is a PAGA action. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff Jorge Lara Rojas (Plaintiff) worked for Defendant String King Lacrosse, LLC (Defendant), who failed to provide employees overtime, minimum wages, meal and rest breaks, accurate wage statements, access to personnel records, or wages due on termination. (Complaint ¶¶ 10–18.)

2.     Procedural  

  Plaintiff filed the Complaint on January 16, 2024, alleging one PAGA cause of action.

 

Defendant filed the present motion for stay of proceedings on April 12, 2024. Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition on April 25, 2024.  In a brief Reply filed May 1, 2024, Defendant sought a ruling without hearing or the granting of the motion at the scheduled hearing, which is the Court’s tentative ruling.

 

II. ANALYSIS 

1.                  Legal Standard 

 “Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.” (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.) The decision of a trial court to stay proceedings is thus generally a matter of discretion. (Bains v. Moores (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 445, 480.)

But where a court finds that “another action is pending upon the same cause of action . . . an interlocutory judgment shall be entered in favor of the defendant pleading the same to the effect that no trial of other issues shall be had until final determination of that other action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 597.) “A trial court has no discretion to allow the second action to proceed if it finds the first involves substantially the same controversy between the same parties. “ (Leadford v. Leadford (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 571, 574.)

Additionally, “[u]nder the rule of exclusive concurrent jurisdiction, when two California superior courts have concurrent jurisdiction over the subject matter and all parties involved in litigation, the first to assume jurisdiction has exclusive and continuing jurisdiction over the subject matter and all parties involved until such time as all necessarily related matters have been resolved. The rule is based upon the public policies of avoiding conflicts that might arise between courts if they were free to make contradictory decisions or awards relating to the same controversy, and preventing vexatious litigation and multiplicity of suits.” (People ex rel. Garamendi v. American Autoplan, Inc. (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 760, 769–70.)

2.                  Discussion   

           Defendant String King Lacrosse LLC (Defendant) identifies two earlier-filed and pending PAGA actions involving the same parties and causes of action: LASC Case No. 23STCV23225, Moreno v. Stringking, Inc., filed on September 25, 2023, and LASC Case No. 23TRCV03194, De La Luz v. Stringking, Inc. (RJN Exhs. 1, 2.) Because these are PAGA actions, the real party in interest on the plaintiff’s side in all three cases is the government. (Iskanian v. CLS Transportation Los Angeles, LLC (2014) 59 Cal.4th 348, 382.)

Plaintiff does not oppose the motion. In a notice of non-opposition, Plaintiff states that a stay is not objectionable to allow a global mediation scheduled for May 24, 2024 to proceed. (Non-Opposition at p. 2.) Plaintiff hopes to lift the stay after the mediation, on the grounds that the other actions are subject to arbitration. (Ibid.)

Defendant has shown good cause for the stay requested, as the prior claims involve the same Plaintiffs seeking the same penalties for the same violations. Plaintiff does not challenge the argument in opposition.  

III. CONCLUSION


 The motion for stay is GRANTED.  The Court will have the parties come back in 90 days to assess how the case or cases will be proceeding.
