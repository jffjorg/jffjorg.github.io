DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV48811    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement

Case Name: Douglas Da Silva McGlew v. Michelle Carriger

Moving Party: Defendant Michelle Carriger (“Carriger”)
Responding Party: Defendants Fatameh Shamie and Mohammed Shamie (“Defendants”)
Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

This is a car accident case where Carriger collided with a vehicle driven by Douglas Da Silva McGlew (“Plaintiff”). Plaintiff claims to have suffered severe pain in back, neck, knee: cervical/neck strain, cervical spasms, lumbago, lumbar radicular pain, myofascial pain, spasms, knee strain claiming past medical expenses damages of approximately $212, 266.54. Plaintiff was also involved in two separate other accidents — one prior to and the other after subject accident. Plaintiff filed lawsuits for these two matters as well. (See McGlew v. Shamie, Case No. 20STCV48002); (McGlew v. Coxwell, Case No, 21STCV16214.) Plaintiff has sought treatment from many of the same medical and chiropractic providers for all three accidents. (Motion For Determination of Good Faith Settlement, p. 3.) Carriger denies any liability for Plaintiff’s injuries but reached a settlement to avoid further litigation costs and the uncertainty of further litigation for $30,000.00. (Id. at 4.) 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Carriger argues the Court should grant the motion for determination of good faith settlement because it represents a rough approximation of Carriger’s proportionate liability and is within the ballpark of a reasonable settlement. (Motion, p. 7.)

OPPOSITION

Defendants argue the Court should deny the motion because Carriger failed to provide evidence that the settlement was in good faith, arguing it comprises too small a portion of the total damages sought and makes no mention of Carriger’s financial condition. Defendants also argue that there is no evidence of their own disproportionate liability, and therefore that they should not bear a greater proportion of medical damages related to the same injuries. (Opposition, p. 9-11.) 

REPLY

Carriger argues that case law supports the good faith of Carriger’s settlement with Plaintiff, that Carriger’s financial condition favors granting the motion, and that the proportion Carriger’s liability is merely theoretical because Plaintiff was involved in a prior and subsequent car accident that caused Plaintiff a greater amount of his injuries and damages. (Reply, p. 3-4.)

ANALYSIS

Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement 

Any party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors, upon giving notice in the manner provided in subdivision (b) of Section 1005. Upon a showing of good cause, the court may shorten the time for giving the required notice to permit the determination of the issue to be made before the commencement of the trial of the action, or before the verdict or judgment if settlement is made after the trial has commenced. 
(Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (a).) 

[T]he intent and policies underlying section 877.6 require that a number of factors be taken into account including a rough approximation of plaintiffs' total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial. Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of nonsettling defendants. [Citation.] Finally, practical considerations obviously require that the evaluation be made on the basis of information available at the time of settlement. “[A] defendant's settlement figure must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant's liability to be.” [Citation.] The party asserting the lack of good faith, who has the burden of proof on that issue [citation], should be permitted to demonstrate, if he can, that the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark” in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute. 
(Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Assocs. (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499-500 (Tech-Bilt).)  

The Court finds Carriger’s agreement with Plaintiff represents a good faith settlement. The issue is whether Carriger’s potential liability is within the “ballpark” of what a reasonable person would estimate their liability to be with the information available at the time of settlement. (Id.) Here, Plaintiff’s deposition testimony indicates Plaintiff was not seriously injured in his collision with Carriger, and that it only exacerbated a prior injury somewhat. Plaintiff openly states as much in his deposition, saying: “The first accident that happened caused all the injuries. The second one and which we are here for happened, and it made it a little worse. The third one happened and made everything a lot worse, a lot worse than I was already feeling.” (Motion p. 48, Exhibit C, Plaintiff’s August 26, 2024 Deposition.) Plaintiff further testified that the third accident — which occurred after Carriger’s collision — took his injuries “to a different level of exponentially worse.” (Id.) Plaintiff’s deposition strongly supports Carriger’s settlement in the amount of $30,000.00 is within the ballpark of liability, representing a minority proportion of liability of Plaintiff’s $212,266.54 economic damages claim. 

That the settlement amount of $30,000.00 represents the full policy limit of Carriger’s insurance further supports that the agreement is not “out of the ballpark” to warrant denying the parties’ settlement agreement. (See Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Assocs. (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499-500.)  While Carriger contends her financial condition also supports a finding of good faith, no admissible evidence concerning her financial condition was provided.

Conversely, Defendants provide no evidence suggesting Carriger’s percentage of liability is any greater than the settlement agreement or motion suggest. The settlor's percentage of liability is the touchstone question to be considered by the trial court in a contested good faith settlement hearing. (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1262.) Defendants’ argument that the motion lacks discussion concerning Plaintiff’s potential total recovery in relation to Carriger’s proportional liability is also incorrect. (See Motion, p. 6-7.)  The Court accordingly finds Defendants did not meet their burden of proof to demonstrate that the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark” in relation to the Tech-Bilt factors to warrant denying the parties’ settlement agreement. (See Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Assocs. (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499-500.) 

The Court thus finds Carriger’s agreement with Plaintiff represents a good faith settlement. 

RULING

The Court GRANTS Carriger’s motion for determination of good faith settlement.

Case Number: 21STCV26605    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Further Responses


Case Name:                             Lilia Montalvo v. Jaime Munguia-Vera

Moving Party:                         Defendant Jaime Munguia-Vera

Responding Party:                   N/A

Tentative Ruling:                    GRANT

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a car accident case arising from a July 25, 2019 incident. (Declaration of Leah J. Kim, ¶2.) Defendant served Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Plaintiff on May 31, 2024. (Kim Decl. ¶3, Exh. A) Responses were originally due on July 3, 2024. Plaintiff served unverified responses on August 30, 2024 (Kim Decl., ¶7, Exh. D) On September 3, 2024, counsel met and conferred regarding the lack of verification and incomplete, non-code compliant responses to Form Interrogatories Nos. 2.6, 4.1, 20.3-20.5, and 20.9-20.11. (Kim Decl., ¶8.) Defense counsel has since reached out several times to remind Plaintiff’s counsel to provide verified responses and address the incomplete, non-code compliant responses. (Kim Decl., ¶¶10-11) (Motion to Compel Further, p. 63.)

 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Defendant argues Plaintiff’s failure to timely provide amended verified responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories Nos. 2.6, 4.1, 20.3-20.5; 20.9-20.11 forced Defendant to file the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s verified amended responses. Defendant requests the Court grant an order compelling Plaintiff to provide further, verified responses to the at-issue interrogatories within twenty days.

 

Defendant also requests the Court award monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,3115.65 against Plaintiff for violating the Discovery Act and misusing the discovery process. 

  

OPPOSITION

No opposition has been filed to this motion.

ANALYSIS

I.                   Motion to Compel Further Responses

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290; 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following (1) an answer containing the information sought to be discovered (2) an exercise of the party’s option to produce writings (3) an objection to the particular interrogatory. (Cal. Code of Civ Proc. § 2031.210 (a).) “Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Emphasis added.) (Code of Civil Procedure §2030.220(a).) “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.220(c).

There is good cause to grant Defendant’s motion to compel further responses.  . As an initial matter, Defendant attempted in good faith to meet and confer with Plaintiff regarding Plaintiff’s failure to provide adequate responses. Additionally, Plaintiff’s responses to each of Defendant’s identified Form Interrogatories are incomplete. For instance, Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory 2.6 provides: “(a) Self-employment, Hairstyle, Part Time; (b) Uber Driver” — and fails to address several subparts of the interrogatory, including the name, address, and telephone number. (Plaintiff Lilia Montalvo Form Interrogatories, Set One, p. 2.) Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory 4.1 similarly omits most information, and merely acknowledges Plaintiff had health insurance but failing to provide information regarding the health insurance policy. (Id. at 3.) Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories 20.3, 20.4, 20.5, and 20.9-20.11 are identical, stating “Expert discovery has not yet commenced, and Responding Party reserves the right to amend and/or supplement this response as discovery and investigation are ongoing.” (Id. at 10-11.)  In each instance, Plaintiff’s responses — each referring to expert discovery — are not applicable to the nature of the information requested, and are therefore incomplete. The Court thus finds Plaintiff has failed to provide adequate responses to Defendant's Form Interrogatories, Set One. It is thus appropriate to grant Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories.

II.                Sanctions

The Court shall impose monetary sanctions against any party or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(d).)

Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030:

The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. The court may also impose this sanction on one unsuccessfully asserting that another has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process, or on any attorney who advised that assertion, or on both. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

Here, Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $1,3115.65 against Plaintiff based on a total of 5.0 hours of work at a $250.00 per hour rate, in addition to a $61.65 filing fee. Defendant reports spending 3.0 hours preparing the instant motion, and an anticipated 1.0 hour to review any opposition and draft a reply in addition to 1.0 hour to attend the hearing. (Motion to Compel Further, p. 13.) The Court finds Defendant is entitled to filing fees ($61.65). Additionally, the Court finds Defendant’s number of hours worked preparing the instant motion is reasonable, as well as Defendant’s anticipated 1.0 hour to attend the hearing. Because no opposition has been filed, the Court reduces Defendant’s awarded number of hours to 4.0. Defendant’s $250.00 hourly rate is reasonable. As a result, Defendant is entitled to $1,061.65 in sanctions.

RULING

Defendant’s motion to compel further is GRANTED. Defendant’s motion for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,061.65.
