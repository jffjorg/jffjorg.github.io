DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV42444    Hearing Date: March 8, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendant Ikenna Mmeje’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is GRANTED. His demurrer is rendered moot.

Defendant Ikenna Mmeje’s Motion for Sanctions is DENIED.

Defendant Lynette Scherer’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike are SUSTAINED, without leave to amend. The motion to strike is moot.

Defendants to give notice.

 

I.       MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS

“When the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he must state that fact in the complaint, or the affidavit if the action is commenced by affidavit, and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by any name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 474.)

 

The criteria for a motion to quash under Code of Civil Procedure § 474 was discussed in A.N. v. County of Los Angeles:

First, section 474 includes an implicit requirement that a plaintiff may not “unreasonably delay” his or her filing of a Doe amendment after learning a defendant's identity. Second, a defendant named in an action by a Doe amendment under section 474 may challenge the amendment by way of an evidence-based motion, which argues that the plaintiff “unreasonable delayed” his or her filing of the challenged amendment. Third, “unreasonable delay” . . . includes a prejudice element, which requires a showing by the defendant that he or she would suffer prejudice from plaintiff's delay in filing the Doe amendment.

(A.N. v. County of Los Angeles (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1066–67.)

This element of “prejudice” does not apply when the Doe defendant is added after the statute of limitations has already run. (See Woo v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 169, 177.)

Defendant Ikenna Mmeje (Mmeje) moves to quash the service of summons made against him based on his belated addition as Doe Defendant No. 2 on September 19, 2023. Mmeje contends that given Plaintiff Gergis Ghobrial’s (Plaintiff) March 4, 2020 termination (4AC ¶ 25), and the applicable three-year limitations period on statutory claims (Code Civ. Proc. § 338, subd. (a)), the two-year period applicable to claims for intentional infliction of emotional distress (Code Civ. Proc. § 335.1), and the one year time in which to bring a civil action after filing an administrative complaint with the Department of Fair Employment and Housing (DFEH). (Gov. Code § 12965, subd. (c)(1)(C).) Mmeje argues that Plaintiff knew of his role in the alleged discrimination because he is listed among those “hospital administrators or physician supervisors who aided, conspired or colluded in the unlawful harassment, discrimination, and retaliation of Plaintiff” in Plaintiff’s verified October 30, 2020 administrative charge. (4AC Exh. A.)

Plaintiff in opposition does not address his allegation against Mmeje in the administrative charge. Plaintiff instead argues that prior discovery responses indicated his belief that Mmeje, as the recipient of some of Plaintiff’s workplace complaints, was “neutral or helpful during the harassment.” (Opposition at p. 2.) Plaintiff instead argues that he only learned of Mmeje’s role in the alleged wrongdoing during the September 13, 2023 deposition of Glenn Koh, wherein Koh testified that Mmeje had informed him that Plaintiff wished to be taken off a schedule dated November 30, 2019, and Koh further stated that, while Mmeje never suggested that Plaintiff be terminated, he had expressed concerns about Plaintiff being a “team player.” (Opposition Exh. 1.)  

Plaintiff’s argument is unpersuasive. From the first iteration of the complaint, Mmeje (the COO of Defendant Long Beach Memorial Medical Center) was listed as one of the final persons to whom Plaintiff made his complaints before his termination. (Complaint ¶¶ 47–49.) Plaintiff accordingly suspected Mmeje’s involvement from the outset of this case, going so far as to list him among the individual wrongdoers in his DFEH complaint. Plaintiff offers no explanation for his inclusion in the DFEH charge, or the delay that followed, to the point of extending beyond all applicable limitations period. Mmeje’s addition to this case therefore comes too late.

The motion to quash is therefore GRANTED. Mmeje’s demurrer is thus rendered MOOT.

II.                MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

(b) By presenting to the court, whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating, a pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person's knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, all of the following conditions are met:

 

(1) It is not being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation.

 

(2) The claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law.

 

(3) The allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery.

 

(4) The denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (b).)

Mmeje seeks terminating sanctions and monetary sanctions in the amount of $37,335.00 against Plaintiff for the defective addition of Mmeje as a Defendant. This motion incorporates the arguments made in the motion to quash, as well as those in the demurrer concerning the inadequacy of the allegations of harassment and intentional infliction of emotional distress made against Mmeje, as well as the procedural defect in his addition as a defendant without leave of court. (Motion at pp. 11–12.)

No sanctions are appropriate. “After the responsive pleadings are filed an amendment to a complaint, including an amendment to add a defendant, requires leave of court.” (Woo v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 169, 175.) However, this procedural defect is susceptible to cure by obtaining leave to amend. Plaintiff’s failure to allege facts concerning Mmeje in the 4AC is likewise capable of cure, and the fact that the factual allegations may ultimately prove inadequate is no basis for the sanctions that Defendant seeks. Likewise, Plaintiff’s claimed reliance on recent deposition testimony as a means of adding Mmeje to the case, while unconvincing, furnished a nonfrivolous basis for amending the complaint, even if such relief is unavailable. Defendant’s contention that the amendment is intended to harass is unsupported.

The motion is DENIED.

III.             DEMURRER

A demurrer should be sustained only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Pro., §§ 430.30, et seq.) In particular, as is relevant here, a court should sustain a demurrer if a complaint does not allege facts that are legally sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (See id. § 430.10, subd. (e).) As the Supreme Court held in Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311: “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. . . . Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Id. at p. 318; see also Hahn. v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 [“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. [Citation.]”)

 

“In determining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer … if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Such demurrers “are disfavored, and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.” (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Insurance Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848.)

 

A demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if the complaint, liberally construed, can state a cause of action under any theory or if there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured by amendment. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1081.) The demurrer also may be sustained without leave to amend where the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead render it probable plaintiff cannot state a cause of action. (Krawitz v. Rusch (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 957, 967.)

 

Defendant Lynette Scherer (Defendant) demurrers to the third and fifth causes of action for FEHA harassment and intentional infliction of emotional distress alleged against her, as well as the second cause of action for retaliation under Labor Code § 1102.5, a claim which was added against her after this court’s order of October 9, 2023, sustaining Scherer’s demurrer to the third and fifth causes of action with leave to amend.

 

An individual supervisor may be personally liable for FEHA harassment that they engage in. (See Fiol v. Doellstedt (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1318, 1331.) And a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress requires extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant. (See Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.) To be actionable, the sexual harassment complained of must be “sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment.” (Guthrey v. State of California (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1122.) “Outrageous conduct” is an element of an IIED claim, and the conduct alleged must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of decency in a civilized community. ( Ibid.) The defendant's conduct must be directed to the plaintiff, but malicious or evil purpose is not essential to liability.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 671.)

Plaintiff’s previous complaint attempted to plead harassment and IIED against Defendant based on her November 6, 2019 transmitting of “fabricated and detrimental employment records concerning the professional performance and conduct of Dr. Ghobrial to several personnel of LBMMC and MHS, including Nurse Thomas.” (TAC ¶ 64.) This court determined that these allegations did not allege outrageous conduct or severe and pervasive harassment, as the alleged conduct constituted personnel management activity, and Plaintiff had specifically stated that he was unaware of the communications during his time working with Scherer.

 

The operative 4AC omits any specific mention of this November 6, 2019 transmission. Instead, Plaintiff alleges that Scherer “spread[] false accusations related to Dr. Ghobrial’s foreign origin,” the nature of the accusations being unspecified, and “falsely claimed that Dr. Ghobrial could not integrate well with the rest of the ACSMG team.” (4AC ¶ 33.) It is alleged these statements “were used to justify Dr. Ghobrial’s termination.” (4AC ¶ 33.) The 4AC finally includes an allegation that Scherer “refus[ed] [Plaintiff’s] access to necessary robotic technology for gallbladder surgery and coerc[ed] the use of subpar laparoscopic equipment.” (4AC ¶ 64.)

 

Plaintiff once more has failed to allege that Scherer engaged in harassment or outrageous conduct, and indeed has attempted to rectify the defects of the prior pleading by making them less specific. As with the prior allegations, Plaintiff does not allege that he was ever aware of Scherer’s “false accusations related to [his] foreign origin,” does not specify what these accusations consisted of, or when or how frequently they took place. The addition of an allegation of obstructed access to robotic surgery technology adds little of magnitude to either claim, and in any event would constitute personnel management activity for which such liability would not lie. (See Janken v. GM Hughes Electronics (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 55, 64–65.)  The demurrer is properly SUSTAINED to the third and fifth causes of action, without leave to amend.

 

Plaintiff’s retaliation claim is also infirm. Plaintiff previously attempted to hold Scherer liable for retaliation under the first cause of action for Health & Safety Code § 1278.5, to which Scherer’s first demurrer was sustained without leave to amend, as no individual liability is available under that statute. Plaintiff did not attempt to plead retaliation against Scherer in the TAC, but now attempts to plead retaliation under Labor Code § 1102.5 against Scherer individually.

 

To plead a prima facie case of retaliation under Labor Code § 1102.5, “a plaintiff must show (1) she engaged in a protected activity, (2) her employer subjected her to an adverse employment action, and (3) there is a causal link between the two.” (Patten v. Grant Joint Union High School Dist. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1378.)

 

Plaintiff does not plead a causal relation between any action taken by Scherer individually and his own complaints of workplace wrongdoing. Plaintiff does not allege that any of his workplace complaints were directed to Scherer or that Scherer even knew of the complaints. The closest any allegation comes on this point is paragraph 89, in which it is stated that defendants, including Scherer, “took adverse employment actions against Plaintiff , as alleged herein, in retaliation for . . . Plaintiff’s complaints.” (4AC ¶ 89.) This is an ill-pleaded legal conclusion. Plaintiff in opposition argues that he may plead Scherer’s knowledge on information and belief. (Opposition at p. 3.) But Plaintiff pleads no such knowledge, whether on information and belief or otherwise.[1]

 

This is Plaintiff’s third attempt to plead claims against Scherer in her personal capacity, the third demurrer to such attempts, and the second attempt to plead retaliation specifically. The demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Scherer’s motion to strike is directed to the second cause of action for retaliation, based on arguments that it was added to the complaint without leave of court following this court’s sustaining of the demurrer to the TAC on November 27, 2023. The motion is rendered moot by the ruling sustaining the demurrer.
