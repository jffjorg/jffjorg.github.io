DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00073    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 May 9, 2024 

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   21TRCV00073

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Iman Peykar v. BMW of North America, LLC

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff, Iman Peykar

¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Defendant, BMW of North America, LLC

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion to Tax Costs

¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) Motion to Tax Costs GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

 

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

Plaintiff Iman Peykar (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant BMW of North America, LLC (“Defendant”) on February 2, 2021. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act § 1793.2; and (4) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act § 1793.22 – Tanner Consumer Protection Act.

 

This case proceeded to trial, and on February 28, 2024, the jury returned a verdict in favor of BMW on all of Plaintiff’s causes of action. On March 14, 2024, BMW filed a Memorandum of Costs.

 

Now, Plaintiff has filed this Motion to Tax costs.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Tax Costs. On April 26, 2024, Defendant, BMW filed an opposition brief. To date, no reply brief has been filed.

 

¿II. ANALYSIS¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the costs memorandum.  If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(1).)  The failure to timely file a motion to tax or strike costs constitutes a waiver of the right to object to costs, unless the Court in its discretion grants relief under Code of Civil Procedure Section 473, such as based upon a finding of excusable neglect.  (Douglas v. Willis (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 287, 289; see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(3) [“The party claiming costs and the party contesting costs may agree to extend the time for serving and filing the cost memorandum and a motion to strike or tax costs.  This agreement must be confirmed in writing, specify the extended date for service, and be filed with the clerk.  In the absence of an agreement, the court may extend the times for serving and filing the cost memorandum or the notice of motion to strike or tax costs for a period not to exceed 30 days.”].) 

 

A prevailing party claiming costs must file and serve a memorandum of costs either (1) within 15 days after the date of service of a notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.5, (2) 15 days after the service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or (3) within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700, subd. (a).) Any motion to strike or tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the memorandum, plus an additional 5 days if served by mail or 2 days if served electronically. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700, subd. (b)(1).) “Unless objection is made to the entire cost memorandum, the motion to strike or tax costs must refer to each item objected to by the same number and appear in the same order as the corresponding cost item claimed on the memorandum of costs and must state why the item is objectionable.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700, subd. (b)(2).) 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a), sets forth items allowable as costs.  And section 1033.5, subdivision (b) lists the items that are not allowable as costs.  The court has discretion to allow costs that are not barred by subdivision (b), but are not listed under subdivision (a).  (Science Applications International Corporation v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1095, 1103.)  If an item of costs is expressly allowed by statute and if items appear on their face to be proper, the verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of their propriety, shifting the burden of proof to the objecting party to show that the items are not “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation” or “reasonable in amount.”  (Benach v. County of L. A. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 836, 855; (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131-32 [“trial court erred in requiring additional proof from” the party claiming costs, where the party attacking costs had the burden]; Santantonio v. Westinghouse Broad. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 102, 116, 121 [after a prima facie showing based on verified cost memorandum, objecting party has the burden to prove costs should be disallowed]; Ladas v. Cal. State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773.)  But if an item of cost does not appear proper on its face, the burden of showing that it is reasonable and necessary shifts to the party claiming the cost.  (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131; Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1267; but see Bach v. County of Butte (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 294, 308 ["Only after such costs are challenged by a motion to tax do the parties need to justify their claims by submitting documentation of the costs they have incurred."].)Accordingly, the court must determine whether section 1033.5 expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face.  (See Nelson, supra, 72 Cal.App.4th at 131.)  

 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff moves on this Motion to Tax Costs, seeking to tax Defendant’s claimed costs by $42,661.81, which reduces the total costs by 85%.  Plaintiff asserts that Defendant’s claimed fees and costs were unnecessary, excessive, or unrecoverable as follows:

 

1.     Item 4 (deposition costs): The Court should tax the amount of $11,8350.58 because Defendant has not satisfied its burden of demonstrating that these claimed costs are necessary and reasonable under section 1033.5.

2.     Item 8 (witness fees): The Court should tax the amount of $2,500.00 because fees of experts not ordered by the court are not allowable as costs.

3.     Item 11 (court reporter fees as established by statute): The Court should tax the amount of $24,200.00 because fees of court reporters are established in the Government Code, section 69948(a): “The fee for reporting testimony and proceedings in contested cases is fifty-five dollars ($55) a day, or any fractional part thereof.”

4.     Item 12 (models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits): The Court should tax the entire amount of $2,710.92 because Defendant has not satisfied its burden of demonstrating that these models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits were reasonably helpful to aid the trier of fact.

5.     Item 16 (other): The Court should tax the entire $1,420.31 because parking and mileage are not recoverable, cannot be recovered by Defendant under the Song-Beverly Act, and Defendant has not satisfied its burden of demonstrating that these claimed costs are necessary and reasonable under section 1033.5.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s submission of the above contains entries are not recoverable under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(a) and (c). Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not and cannot meet its burden of proof to establish these costs were reasonably necessary. BMW largely disagrees, so the Court will parse out the items on a line by line basis. 

 

Item No. 4

 

            Item No. 4 seeks to recover costs for deposition. Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s claimed deposition costs are inflated and are unnecessary and unreasonable.  The Court agrees in part and disagrees in part.

 

Michael McCaffrey’s Deposition Costs

           

            Plaintiff contends that he should not be charged with having to pay for three (3) of Michael McCaffrey’s Defendant’s PMK deposition transcripts. Plaintiff argues that McCaffrey’s deposition should have only taken place one time, however, he notes he was forced to take McCaffrey’s deposition a second time in the Court’s jury room because Defense counsel instructed McCaffery not the answer the most vital question pertinent to Plaintiff’s case in chief – How many electric mils can the subject vehicle obtain on battery-mode only? Further, at the second deposition, Plaintiff asserts that McCaffrey was supposed to produce, but failed to produce, any Service Information Bulletins, which the Court addressed and ruled McCaffrey was to produce at the time of his deposition (which necessitated the third deposition). Thus, Plaintiff argues he should only be responsible for McCaffrey’s first deposition on June 8, 2023 in the amount of $374.75. Further, Plaintiff asserts this Court should tax McCaffrey’s deposition in the amount of $2,369.28 and be charged no more than the amount of for McCaffrey’s first deposition on June 8, 2023. The Court tentatively GRANTS the motion to tax the costs claimed for the second and third sessions of McCaffrey’s deposition, which was required because of BMW’s failure to comply with its discovery obligations until the Court granted a motion to compel.  The Court DENIES the motion to tax the costs of the first McCaffrey deposition.

 

           

Plaintiff, Iman Peykar’s Deposition Costs

 

            Next, Plaintiff argues that Plaintiff’s deposition cost in the amount of $1,500.20 is excessively high and unreasonable. Plaintiff notes that Veritext legal Solutions charged $655.20 for transcript services; $400 for professional attendance; $150 for logistics; Processing & Electronics Files; and $295 for Virtual Services. Plaintiff asserts that the costs for professional attendance, logistics, processing & Electronic files, and virtual services are not recoverable under section 1033.5. Thus, Plaintiff has requested that his deposition cost taxes in the amount of $845 and be charged no more than the amount of $7 for the transcript services regularly charged by Steno for one deposition. The Court tentatively DENIES the motion to tax the costs claimed as the amount charged is within the range of other deposition transcripts in civil litigation since the COIV 19 pandemic began. 

 

James Langan’s Deposition Costs

 

            As to James Langan’s deposition costs, Plaintiff notes that Defendant did not submit any bills for James Langan’s deposition costs, and thus, Plaintiff contends there is no evidence that it did in fact pay for the deposition. Therefore, Plaintiff argues that this deposition cost be taxed in the amount of $575 as the Defendant has not met its burden of proof that it reasonably incurred James Langan’s deposition costs. The Court will consider continuing the hearing on this part of the motion pending BMW’s submission of the corroboration, i.e., invoice, for the Langen deposition which is claimed in the Cost Bill. 

 

Joe Grijalva’s Deposition Costs

 

            Next, Plaintiff asserts that he should not be charged with having to pay for two of Joe Grijalva’s expert deposition transcripts. With this deposition, like McCaffrey’s, Plaintiff asserts that Joe Grijalva’s deposition should have only taken place one time, but that Plaintiff was forced to take Grijalva’s deposition a second time because Defense counsel interfered with Plaintiff’s taking of Grijalva’s deposition and did not let the deposition finish. Plaintiff argues that this led to Defendant’s unnecessary ex parte application to limit Grijalva’s deposition. As such, Plaintiff argues that Grijalva’s second deposition could have been entirely avoided if Defense counsel allowed Plaintiff to finish Grijalva’s deposition for the first time. Further, Plaintiff argues that this Court should tax the second deposition as there was no reason for Defendant to order expedited transcripts of the deposition.

 

The Court tentatively GRANTS the motion to tax the costs claimed for the second deposition, which was required because of BMW’s failure to comply with its discovery obligations until the Court granted a motion to compel.  The Court DENIES the motion to tax the  costs of the first Grijalva deposition.

 

Thomas Lepper’s Deposition Costs

 

            Lastly, as to the Thomas Lepper deposition, Plaintiff argues that he should not be charged for Lepper’s second deposition because Defendant failed to timely produce documents regarding similar defects by the first deposition. Further, Plaintiff argues the second deposition was totally unnecessary because Mr. Lepper testified he did not have any new or changed opinions based on the document production that was eventually and finally produced by Defendant. Plaintiff argues that the first deposition being $4,249.45  and $1,237.85 for his second deposition is unreasonable and excessive. Part of the costs of the depositions include: (1) for the first deposition, Vertitext Legal Solutions charged $1,795.00 for transcript services; $1,624.50 for Priority Request, $350 for professional attendance; $34.45 in Exhibits; $150.00 for Logistics; Processing & Electronic Files; and $295.00 for Virtual Services; and (2) for the second deposition, Vertitext Legal Solutions charged $495.00 for transcript services; $122.85 for Priority Request, $175 for professional attendance; $34.45 in Exhibits; $150.00 for Logistics; Processing & Electronic Files; and $295.00 for Virtual Services.

 

            Plaintiff argues that these deposition costs should be taxed in the amount of $4,912.30 and that Plaintiff be charged no more than the amount of $575 for the transcript services regularly charged by Steno for one deposition.

 

            Opposition Brief

 

            In opposition to this argument as to Item No. 4, BMW argues that Plaintiff required all of the additional depositions, and that it is claiming deposition costs which are expressly provided for in Section 1033.5.

 

            Tentative Ruling:  The Court GRANTS the motion to tax $1,237.85 for the second deposition, which was required because of BMW’s failure to comply with its discovery obligations until the Court granted a motion to compel.  The Court DENIES the motion to tax the $4,249.45 in costs for the first deposition.

 

 

Item No. 8

 

            Next, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s claimed expert expenses are not allowable costs. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(b)(1), fees of experts not ordered by the court are not allowable as costs, except when necessary authorized by law. Here, Plaintiff notes that Defendant is claiming costs in the amount of $2,500 for Thomas Lepper’s expert fees for his two depositions are not recoverable because they were not ordered by the Court.

 

            In opposition, BMW asserts that Defendant deposed Plaintiff’s expert, Thomas Lepper, on two occasions. BMW contends that prior to the second deposition, Plaintiff demanded that Defendant pay for Mr. Lepper’s time in advance of the deposition, which Defendant consented to do. BMW contends that these were not expert fees associated with DEFENDANT’s own expert – these were fees necessary to take the deposition of Plaintiff’s expert.

 

            This Court finds that the $2500 advance for Mr. Lepper’s time was not a fee for the defendant’s expert but rather was a cost of the PLANTIFF’s expert witness.  Thus, these are not “fees of experts” as prohibited by Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(b)(1).   The Court will entertain oral argument as to why all costs BMW incurred for the second Lepper deposition should be chargeable against Plaintiff rather than borne by BMW, which had failed to comply with its discovery obligations until the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel. 

 

Item No. 11

 

            Plaintiff further asserts that Defendant is not entitled to recover court reporter fees except as established by statute. Plaintiff notes that Defendant claims $24,750 for court reporter fees. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(b)(5), transcripts of court proceedings not ordered by the Court are not allowable as costs, except when expressly authorized by law. However, as noted by Defendant, BMW, Court reporter fees as established by statute are allowable costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(a)(11).) California Government Code section 68086(c) states that “[t]he costs for the services of the official court reporter shall be recoverable as taxable costs by the prevailing party as otherwise provided by law.” Plaintiff asserts that California Government Code Section 69948(a): “The fee for reporting testimony and proceedings in contested cases is fifty-five dollars ($55) a day, or any fractional part thereof.” Plaintiff asserts that the trial was ten (10) days, and thus, the maximum recoverable amount should be $550.

 

            In opposition, Defendant BMW notes that reporter fees are authorized by statute, but does not state the statute which it is bringing this request under. BMW merely argues that the court reporter fees are reasonable and necessary.

 

            The Court’s tentative ruling is to DENY this aspect of the motion to tax, because the Court appointed Ms. Cabello as an official pro tem court reporter for the trial, her official record of the proceedings thus being available to both parties for post-trial motions or appeal.  With the Los Angeles Superior Court having discontinued the use of court-employed court reporters for civil matters, parties who desire an official record of the proceedings are obliged to provide their own reporters and thus provide millions of dollars a year in net savings to the court system.  Had Ms. Cabello been a court employee, there would be no doubt that her reasonable fees would be recoverable on a Cost Bill by the prevailing party. 

 

Item No. 12

 

            Plaintiff also asserts that Defendant cannot recover costs for models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits. As noted by Plaintiff, Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(a)(13) allows recoverable costs when models and blowups of exhibits and photocopies of exhibits may be allowed if they were reasonably helpful to aid the trier of fact. Plaintiff asserts that none of the items listed in this section fall under the category of recovery, nor were any of the items listed actually used by Defendant at trial as all exhibits were shown to the witnesses via Defendant Counsel’s iPad.

 

            In opposition, Defendant does not disagree that the items listed in this section were not used at trial. Instead, Defendant contends that it nonetheless prepared them as it had to prepare to defend against every argument Plaintiff might have raised. This Court does not find this to be a particularly persuasive argument. The models and/or enlargements were not needed at trial and thus do not fit the criteria of section 1033.5(a)(13) as they did not aid the trier of fact.

 

            Thus, this Court GRANTS this motion as to Item No. 12 and taxes the claimed exhibits, models and blow-up costs asserted int eh Cost Bill.

 

Item No. 16

 

Finally, Plaintiff argues that Defendant is not entitled to recover Defendant’s “other” claimed costs, including parking and mileage. Plaintiff asserts that Code of Civil Procedure, section 1033.5(a) does not contemplate the recovery of Defendant’s counsel’s parking and mileage costs. Defendant’s memorandum of costs accounts for $1,420.31 in court fees parking & mileage. Plaintiff also notes that Defendant does not attach an Attachment 16a to its memorandum of costs, and is asking for an additional $44.47 without any support.

 

In opposition, Defendant merely argues that these claimed “other” costs were all reasonable and necessary, but provides nothing more than some citations to declarations. Defendant offers no case precedent, statute, etc., that would warrant these “other” costs being awarded.

 

As such, this Court GRANTS this motion as to Item No. 12 and taxes the entire cost of this category.

 

III. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Tax Costs is Granted in part and Denied in part.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to calculate the amounts the Court taxed, provide a proposed order to BMW’s counsel within 7 calendar days of this ruling (for review, edits, and approval), and then submit the proposed order to the Court on or before May 20, 2024.  If Plaintiff fails to do so, then BMW shall submit a proposed order to the Court within 7 calendar days after the Plaintiff’s May 20 deadline reflecting its calculations and the resultant net costs award.

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

            On February 16, 2024, Plaintiff, FNNP Investments, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed an Unlawful Detainer Complaint against Defendants, Bryce Egbert, Taisiia Popova, and DOES 1 through 5.   The Complaint is based on the allegations that Plaintiff is the owner of a single family residential real property at 868 5th St., Manhattan Beach, CA (“Premises”) where Defendants, Bryce Egbert and Taisiia Popova were leasing the Premises. Plaintiff alleges that since September 1, 2023 until the date of filing the current motion, Defendants have failed to pay any rent to Plaintiff and continue to occupy the premises without payment of any rent in direct breach of the lease.

 

            The moving papers assert that Plaintiff has been in periodic settlement communications with the Defendants, no settlement was ever reached despite its good faith efforts. Thus, Plaintiff notes that after continued failure to pay rent, and failure to settle with the Defendants, Plaintiff was forced to initiate this unlawful detainer action and filed its unlawful detainer complaint on February 16, 2024.

 

            On March 28, 2024, Bryce Egbert and Taisiia Popova filed an Answer to the Unlawful Detainer Complaint, and included with the Answer, five attachments. The fifth attachment, Attachment No. 5 was exchanged between the parties during settlement discussions and for settlement purposes, and was never signed nor finalized between the parties but was drafted for settlement purposes.

 

            Now, Plaintiff has filed a Motion to Strike Portions of Attachment No. 5 from Defendants’ Answer to the Unlawful Detainer Complaint.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

 

On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Strike. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿                                                                                                                           

A. Motion to Strike

¿ Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).) The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)¿¿ 

 

B. Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff has brought this motion on the grounds that Attachment No. 5 to defendants’ verified answer to the unlawful detainer complaint, filed on March 28, 2024, titled “stipulation” and consisting of 35 pages in total, to be stricken in its entirety from Defendants’ answer as the document was communicated to Defendants during settlement discussions and was specifically drafted for settlement purposes and considered a suggested settlement agreement and is therefore inadmissible pursuant to California Evidence Code section 1152. Evidence Code section 1152(a) states “Evidence that a person has, in compromise or from humanitarian motives, furnished or offered or promised to furnish money or any other thing, act, or service to another who has sustained or will sustain or claims that he or she has sustained or will sustain loss or damage, as well as any conduct or statements made in negotiation thereof, is inadmissible to prove his or her liability for the loss or damage or any part of it.”¿¿ 

 

Specifically, Attachment No. 5 clearly states a section labeled “Stipulation” which includes headings such as: (1) Addendum; (2) Lease Termination Date; (3) Move Out Date; (4) Stipulation for Entry of Judgment; (5) Rent through December 31, 2023; (6) Rent as of January 1, 2024; (7) Incentive for early move-out; (8) Incentive for Tenancy Beyond February 29, 2024; (9) Holding Over Tenancy beyond April 30, 2024; (10) Security Deposit; and (11) Payments. The above attachment is clearly an offer to compromise.   It is generally impermissible to include settlement offers or counter-offers as evidence in this case. (See generally¿Simandle v. Vista de Santa Barbara Associates, LP¿(2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1317, 1323¿[“It is well established that statements made during settlement negotiations are not admissible to show liability”]; Armstrong v. Kline¿(1944) 64 Cal.App.2d 704, 715 [“It is a well–established rule of law that it is not permissible to show that a party to litigation has offered to compromise the cause.¿The rule not only excludes the offer to compromise, but also all negotiations with relation thereto”] (internal quotations and citations omitted).)  

 

As this Attachment No. 5 is an inadmissible exhibit, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Attachment No. 5 from Defendants’ Answer.  

III. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike all of Exhibit 5 to Defendants’ Answer is GRANTED.
