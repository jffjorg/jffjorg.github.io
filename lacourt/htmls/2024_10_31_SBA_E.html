Case Number: 19STCV13960    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

CYNTHIA SHELTON, an individual,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

19STCV13960

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

 

LOS ANGELES INTERNATIONAL AIRPORT a.k.a. LAX; CITY OF LOS ANGELES; and DOES 1 to 25,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             October 29, 2024

 

Moving Parties:          Defendants Los Angeles World Airports and the City of Los Angeles

 

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Cynthia Shelton

 

HEARING: Motion for Summary Judgement

 

            The Court considered the moving and opposition papers.

 

RULING

           

            The Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On April 19, 2019, Plaintiff Cynthia Shelton filed the Complaint against Defendants Los Angeles World Airports and the City of Los Angeles, alleging causes of action for negligence, willful failure to warn, and dangerous conditions of public property arising from a fall at Los Angeles International Airport.

 

            On November 7, 2023, Defendants filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

            On October 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On October 24, 2024, Defendants filed a Reply.

 

            Trial is set for April 16, 2024.

.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437 subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)  

 

As to each cause of action, as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action ... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c subd. (p)(2); see also Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            This case arises from a fall that occurred on a stairwell in the Tom Bradley International Terminal at Los Angeles International Airport. On July 30, 2018, Plaintiff flew from San Salvador, El Salvador, to Los Angeles on Avianca Airlines. After deplaning, she proceeded toward the baggage claim area. On her way, she encountered a broken-down escalator and was allegedly ushered toward an adjacent stairwell. Unfortunately, as she descended the stairs and was approximately three to four steps from the bottom, she fell. (UMF 1.)

 

Plaintiff subsequently filed a premises liability lawsuit against the Defendants, alleging they were responsible for her fall. Plaintiff asserts that the Defendants negligently owned, maintained, managed, and operated the stairwell and further alleges that they willfully or maliciously failed to guard or warn against the dangerous condition that led to her injury.

 

 

            Cause of Action 1 – Negligence

 

            “To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care, (2) the defendant breached that duty, and (3) the breach proximately caused the plaintiff's damages or injuries.” (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 62.) 

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s claim of common law negligence against the Defendants is barred. Plaintiff alleges that the Defendants negligently owned, maintained, managed, and operated the premises where she fell. However, the California Government Claims Act eliminates common law tort liability for public entities and employees. (Gov. Code § 815.) The Government Claims Act states that public entities can only be held liable for injuries if liability is explicitly provided by statute and not based on common law tort principles. (Gov. Code § 815 subd. (a); See, Quigley v. Garden Valley Fire Prot. Dist. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 798, 803; Guzman v. Cnty. of Monterey (2009) 46 Cal. 4th 887, 897.)

 

As both Defendants are public entities, any tort liability related to the stairwell must be based exclusively on § 835 of the Government Claims Act. (Gov. Code § 835.) Consequently, Plaintiff’s first count for common law negligence is legally barred and must be dismissed.

 

Plaintiff does not specify her arguments as to each cause of action but instead provides one full response. Plaintiff argues that Defendants, as owners and operators of public property, had a duty to maintain their premises in a reasonably safe condition for the public, particularly for individuals using the escalators and stairwells at the airport. Plaintiff references Phifer v. Iceland Air (S.D.N.Y. 2019), which Plaintiff argues holds that airlines and related entities, including airports, are responsible for injuries occurring during disembarkation. Plaintiff contends that Defendants failed this duty by leaving the escalator inoperative and not providing adequate crowd control or warnings. Additionally, Plaintiff cites Niemiec v. Polar Air Cargo, LLC (E.D.N.Y. 2009), which Plaintiff argues states that airport operators are responsible for ensuring safe passage for passengers. Here, Plaintiff argues that Defendants neglected its responsibility to warn passengers of potential hazards and control the crowd, thereby creating unsafe conditions that led to her injuries. However, the Court could locate neither of these cases. The Court was able to locate a case titled Phifer v. Icelandair, from the Ninth Circuit, which found that an airline was liable to a passenger for an injury sustained after hitting her head on a television while boarding the aircraft. (Phifer v. Icelandair (9th Cir. 2011) 652 F.3d 1222, 1224.)

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that her fall was an unexpected and unusual event caused by unsafe external conditions, which qualifies as an "accident" under the Montreal Convention. Plaintiff asserts that an "accident" under the Convention is defined as an unexpected or unusual event external to the passenger, and the push she experienced, which caused her fall, meets this definition, making Defendants liable. (Air France v. Saks (1985) 470 U.S. 392, 405.)  Plaintiff also references McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (5th Cir. 2004), in which she argues a passenger's injury caused by another during disembarkation was deemed an "accident." In Plaintiff's case, her injury resulted from being pushed by another passenger in a crowded, unmanaged stairwell, further supporting her claim of liability under the Montreal Convention. Again, the Court had issues locating Plaintiff’s cited cases. The Court could not find a case named McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. from the Fifth Circuit. The Court did locate McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (S.D. Tex. 2001) 133 F.Supp.2d 514, however, it does not hold what Plaintiff has asserted. (McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (S.D. Tex. 2001) 133 F.Supp.2d 514.)

 

Last, Plaintiff does not provide a response to the Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts but does argue that several material facts remain in dispute, including whether Defendants had knowledge of the broken escalator and failed to adequately warn passengers, whether they provided sufficient crowd control and assistance, and whether Plaintiff's fall was directly caused by Defendants' negligence in maintaining safe premises. Plaintiff contends that these unresolved issues are central to determining whether Defendants fulfilled their duty of care and whether their actions or inactions contributed to her injury.

 

Here, Defendants have met their burden of showing that Plaintiffs' claim for negligence is barred by the Government Claims Act. (Gov. Code § 815 subd. (a).) Plaintiff has provided cases that show airlines may be held liable for their negligence, but none showing that government-owned airports can. Furthermore, Defendant has provided no evidence which shows a triable issue of material fact.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgement as to cause of action one.

 

            Cause of Action 2 – Willful Failure to Warn

 

            The Second cause of action asserts a count for willful failure to warn under California Civil Code § 846.

           

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s claim of willful failure to warn, based on California Civil Code § 846, is inapplicable to public entities like the City Defendants. (Civ. Code, § 846.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants willfully or maliciously failed to warn of the dangerous condition of the stairwell where she fell. However, Defendants assert that liability for public entities regarding the condition of public property is governed exclusively by the Government Claims Act, specifically § 835, which covers dangerous conditions on public property. (Gov. Code § 835.) Defendants argue that § 846 applies only to private property owners who grant access for recreational purposes and does not apply to public entities or commercial travel, such as the incident involving Plaintiff. (Civ. Code § 846.) Furthermore, Defendants argue that the Court has explicitly rejected the use of § 846 to impose liability on public entities. (Delta Farms Reclamation Dist. v. Superior Court (1983) 33 Cal. 3d 699, 704-06.) Since the stairwell is public property maintained by Los Angeles World Airports, a proprietary department of the City of Los Angeles, Defendants argue that § 846 cannot be used to hold them liable. Therefore, they contend that Plaintiff’s second count for willful failure to warn is barred by law and must be dismissed.

 

Again, Plaintiff does not specify her arguments as to each cause of action but instead provides one full response. Plaintiff argues that Defendants, as owners and operators of public property, had a duty to maintain their premises in a reasonably safe condition for the public, particularly for individuals using the escalators and stairwells at the airport. Plaintiff references Phifer v. Iceland Air (S.D.N.Y. 2019), which Plaintiff argues holds that airlines and related entities, including airports, are responsible for injuries occurring during disembarkation. However, the Court could locate neither of these cases. The Court was able to locate a case titled Phifer v. Icelandair, from the Ninth Circuit, which found that an airline was liable to a passenger for an injury sustained after hitting her head on a television while boarding the aircraft. (Phifer v. Icelandair (9th Cir. 2011) 652 F.3d 1222, 1224.)

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that her fall was an unexpected and unusual event caused by unsafe external conditions, which qualifies as an "accident" under the Montreal Convention. Plaintiff asserts that an "accident" under the Convention is defined as an unexpected or unusual event external to the passenger, and the push she experienced, which caused her fall, meets this definition, making Defendants liable. (Air France v. Saks (1985) 470 U.S. 392, 405.)  Plaintiff also references McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (5th Cir. 2004), in which she argues a passenger's injury caused by another during disembarkation was deemed an "accident." In Plaintiff's case, her injury resulted from being pushed by another passenger in a crowded, unmanaged stairwell, further supporting her claim of liability under the Montreal Convention. Again, the Court had issues locating Plaintiff’s cited cases. The Court could not find a case named McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. from the Fifth Circuit. The Court did locate McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (S.D. Tex. 2001) 133 F.Supp.2d 514, however, it does not hold what Plaintiff has asserted. (McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (S.D. Tex. 2001) 133 F.Supp.2d 514.)

Here, Defendants have met their burden of showing that Plaintiff’s claim for willful failure to warn is barred by the Government Claims Act. (Civ. Code, § 846; Delta Farms Reclamation Dist., supra, 33 Cal. 3d 699, 704-06.) Plaintiff has provided no evidence to show that Defendants can be held liable for willful failure to warn under California Civil Code § 846.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgement as to cause of action two.

           

Cause of Action 3 – Dangerous Condition of Public Property

 

            A public entity may be liable for a dangerous condition of public property if the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, the dangerous condition caused the injury, the condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the type of injury that occurred, and either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee within the scope of their employment created the condition, or the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to take measures to protect against it. (Gov. Code § 835; Sun v. City of Oakland (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1183.)

 

            Defendants argue that a dangerous condition is defined as one that presents a substantial risk of injury when the property is used with due care. Allegations must identify specific physical deficiencies, such as damage or defects, rather than a general lack of safeguards. (Brenner v. City of El Cajon, (2003) 113 Cal. App. 4th 434, 439.) Defendants assert that Plaintiff's claims lack specificity regarding any physical defect or dangerous condition of the stairwell. In this case, Plaintiff did not provide evidence of any physical defect on the stairwell, as it was clean, dry, and well-lit at the time of the incident. (UMF Nos. 4-6.) Defendants contend that the lack of crowd control or signage alone does not constitute a dangerous condition under the statute, and without evidence of a physical defect, Plaintiff's claim cannot succeed. (See, Davis v. City of Pasadena (1996) 42 Cal. App. 4th 701, 702-03; Brenner, supra, 113 Cal. App. 4th 434, 439.)  Therefore, Defendants argue that Plaintiff's case should be dismissed.

 

            Furthermore, Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claim that a crowd of passengers in the stairwell constituted a dangerous condition due to Defendants’ failure to control the crowd is legally insufficient. They assert that third-party conduct alone, unrelated to the physical condition of the property, does not constitute a dangerous condition for which a public entity may be held liable. (Zelig v. Cnty. of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal. 4th 1112, 1135-36; Moncur v. City of Los Angeles (1977) 68 Cal. App. 3d 118, 121.) Since Plaintiff does not allege or provide evidence of any physical defect or deterioration in the stairwell, Defendants argue that the third-party conduct is insufficient to establish liability.

 

Defendants also argue that the lack of crowd control or supervision does not amount to a dangerous condition, as courts have consistently ruled that without a physical defect in the property, the failure to impose additional security measures, such as crowd control, does not create a dangerous condition. (See, Hacala v. Bird Rides, Inc. (2023) 90 Cal. App. 5th 292, 300-301; Gray v. America West Airlines, Inc. (1989) 209 Cal. App. 3d 76, 79-80.) Since the stairwell had no physical defect and Plaintiff’s claim relies solely on third-party actions, Defendants conclude that the claim for dangerous condition of public property should be dismissed as a matter of law.

 

Again, Plaintiff does not specify her arguments as to each cause of action but instead provides one full response. Plaintiff argues that Defendants, as owners and operators of public property, had a duty to maintain their premises in a reasonably safe condition for the public, particularly for individuals using the escalators and stairwells at the airport. Plaintiff references Phifer v. Iceland Air (S.D.N.Y. 2019), which Plaintiff argues holds that airlines and related entities, including airports, are responsible for injuries occurring during disembarkation. Plaintiff contends that Defendants failed this duty by leaving the escalator inoperative and not providing adequate crowd control or warnings. Additionally, Plaintiff cites Niemiec v. Polar Air Cargo, LLC (E.D.N.Y. 2009), which Plaintiff argues states that airport operators are responsible for ensuring safe passage for passengers. Here, Plaintiff argues that Defendants neglected its responsibility to warn passengers of potential hazards and control the crowd, thereby creating unsafe conditions that led to her injuries. However, the Court could locate neither of these cases. The Court was able to locate a case titled Phifer v. Icelandair, from the Ninth Circuit, which found that an airline was liable to a passenger for an injury sustained after hitting her head on a television while boarding the aircraft. (Phifer v. Icelandair (9th Cir. 2011) 652 F.3d 1222, 1224.)

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that her fall was an unexpected and unusual event caused by unsafe external conditions, which qualifies as an "accident" under the Montreal Convention. Plaintiff asserts that an "accident" under the Convention is defined as an unexpected or unusual event external to the passenger, and the push she experienced, which caused her fall, meets this definition, making Defendants liable. (Air France v. Saks (1985) 470 U.S. 392, 405.)  Plaintiff also references McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (5th Cir. 2004), in which she argues a passenger's injury caused by another during disembarkation was deemed an "accident." In Plaintiff's case, her injury resulted from being pushed by another passenger in a crowded, unmanaged stairwell, further supporting her claim of liability under the Montreal Convention. Again, the Court had issues locating Plaintiff’s cited cases. The Court could not find a case named McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. from the Fifth Circuit. The Court did locate McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (S.D. Tex. 2001) 133 F.Supp.2d 514, however, it does not hold what Plaintiff has asserted. (McCaskey v. Continental Airlines, Inc. (S.D. Tex. 2001) 133 F.Supp.2d 514.)

 

Last, Plaintiff does not provide a response to the Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts but does argue that several material facts remain in dispute, including whether Defendants had knowledge of the broken escalator and failed to adequately warn passengers, whether they provided sufficient crowd control and assistance, and whether Plaintiff's fall was directly caused by Defendants' negligence in maintaining safe premises. Plaintiff contends that these unresolved issues are central to determining whether Defendants fulfilled their duty of care and whether their actions or inactions contributed to her injury.

 

            While Plaintiff argues that her injury was a result of being pushed in a crowded stairwell and that Defendant did not adequately provide crowd control, third-party conduct and failure to impose security measures do not create a dangerous condition. (See, Hacala, 90 Cal. App. 5th 292, 300-301; Moncur, 68 Cal. App. 3d 118, 121.) Therefore, Defendants have met their burden of showing that Plaintiff’s claim for dangerous condition of public property is deficient as Plaintiff has not shown that property was in dangerous condition at the time of the incident.

 

            Plaintiff does state that there are triable issues of fact regarding whether Defendants had knowledge of the broken escalator and adequately warned passengers, whether Defendants provided sufficient crowd control and assistance to passengers, and whether Plaintff’s fall was caused by Defendants' negligence in maintaining safe premises. (Opposition p. 3.) However, Plaintiff provides not evidence to show that these matters are disputed. Furthermore, even if these matters were disputed, none of these matters show that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury.

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgement as to the third cause of action.

RULING

           

            The Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On April 19, 2019, Plaintiff Cynthia Shelton filed the Complaint against Defendants Los Angeles World Airports and the City of Los Angeles, alleging causes of action for negligence, willful failure to warn, and dangerous conditions of public property arising from a fall at Los Angeles International Airport.

 

            On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff amended the Complaint, adding Defendant Aerovías del Continente Americano S.A. (erroneously named Avianca, S.A.)

 

            On November 7, 2023, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

            On October 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On October 24, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

 

            Trial is set for April 16, 2024.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437 subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)  

 

As to each cause of action, as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action ... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c subd. (p)(2); see also Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            This case arises from a fall that occurred on a stairwell in the Tom Bradley International Terminal at Los Angeles International Airport. On July 30, 2018, Plaintiff flew from San Salvador, El Salvador, to Los Angeles on Avianca Airlines. After deplaning, she proceeded toward the baggage claim area. On her way, she encountered a broken-down escalator and was allegedly ushered toward an adjacent stairwell. As she descended the stairs and was approximately three to four steps from the bottom, she fell. (UMF 1.)

 

Plaintiff subsequently filed a premises liability lawsuit against Defendants, Los Angeles International Airport, and the City of Los Angeles, alleging they were responsible for her fall. Plaintiff asserts that the Defendants negligently owned, maintained, managed, and operated the stairwell and further alleges that they willfully or maliciously failed to guard or warn against the dangerous condition that led to her injury.

 

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the Complaint (Fictitious/Incorrect Name), substituting the fictitiously named Doe 1 with Avianca. However, the Plaintiff did not modify or alter any of the allegations made in the original complaint against the City of Los Angeles and Los Angeles World Airports. As a result, the allegations against Avianca remain the same as those initially asserted in the original complaint.

 

 

            Cause of Action 1 – Negligence

 

            “To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care, (2) the defendant breached that duty, and (3) the breach proximately caused the plaintiff's damages or injuries.” (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 62.) 

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s premises liability claim must be dismissed because Defendant was substituted for a fictitious "Doe" defendant after the statute of limitations had expired. The incident occurred on July 30, 2018, and the statute of limitations for personal injury claims in California, extended by tolling due to COVID-19, expired on January 24, 2021. (Complaint p. 4, Exhibit 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 335.1.) However, Plaintiff did not file the amendment to add Defendant until May 2, 2022, over 15 months after the expiration. (Doe

Amendment, Exhibit 3.)

 

Defendant asserts that while the "relation-back" doctrine can allow a plaintiff to add a defendant after the statute of limitations if they were genuinely ignorant of the defendant's identity. (Woo v. Superior Court, 75 Cal. App. 4th 169, 176; Code Civ. Proc., § 474.) However, Plaintiff cannot prove such ignorance. Plaintiff knew Defendant's identity at the time of the incident, as shown by her testimony and documentation. Plaintiff testified that she knew the airline she flew with was Defendant Avianca. (Plaintiff’s Dep. Tr., Vol. II, 93:17-20, Exhibit 5.) Plaintiff produced a boarding pass that showed she flew with Defendant Avianca on the date of the incident. (Avianca Airlines Boarding Pass, Exhibit 7; Plaintiff’s Dep. Tr., Vol. II, 93:21-94:2, Exhibit 5.) Defendant also testified that on the date of the incident, she told the airport police that Defendant Avianca’s staff were not helpful. (Plaintiff’s Dep. Tr., Vol. II, 115:24 -119:10, Exhibit 5.) Last, in a deposition taken before Defendant Avianca was added, Plaintiff stated that Avianca employees arrived at the scene of the incident and provided a wheelchair. (Plaintiff’s Dep. Tr., Vol. I, 52:10-53:19, Exhibit 4.) Therefore, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot claim to have been unaware of Defendant’s involvement in the facts giving rise to her claim. Consequently, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Premises Liability claim is barred by the Statute of Limitations and must be dismissed.

 

            In opposition, Defendant provided no argument regarding the Statute of Limitations, nor did she provide a response to the separate statement of undisputed facts. However, Defendant does state that there are triable issues of fact regarding whether Avianca Airlines provided adequate assistance to passengers during disembarkation and whether the non-operational escalator constituted a hazardous condition that Defendant failed to address.  However, Plaintiff does not address whether Plaintiff knew of Defendant Avianca's involvement in the incident.

 

            Defendant has provided sufficient evidence to show that Plaintiff knew of Defendant Avianca’s involvement in the incident long before the filing of the Doe Amendment. (See, Plaintiff’s Dep. Tr., Vol. II, 115:24 -119:10, Exhibit 5.) Furthermore, in the Opposition, Plaintiff does not dispute that she knew of Defendant Avianca’s involvement before the filing of the Doe Amendment. Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiff was not genuinely ignorant of Defendant's involvement at the time of the filing of the Complaint or before the filing of the Doe Amendment. (see Marasco v. Wadsworth (1978) 21 Cal. 3d 82, 88.)

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for summary judgment as to the first cause of action.

 

            Cause of Action 2 – Willful Failure to Warn

 

            The second cause of action asserts a count for willful failure to warn under Civil Code § 846.

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s willful failure to warn claim under Civil Code § 846 is inapplicable because § 846 provides immunity, and exceptions to immunities, to property owners for premises liability claims related to recreational use of their land. (Civ. Code, § 846.) This statute was designed to protect property owners who allow recreational activities on their land by limiting their liability, not to impose liability on parties with no property interest, such as Defendant, an airline with no ownership or control over the site of Plaintiff’s alleged injury. (See, Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. Superior Court, 10 Cal. App. 5th 563, 568.)

 

Section 846 immunity and its exceptions only apply to owners of real property interests used for recreational purposes, which does not include commercial travel, like Plaintiff’s flight. (Civ. Code, § 846; UMF No. 5.) Additionally, since Defendant had no ownership, control, or interest in the premises, the statute cannot be used to support liability against Defendant. (UMF No. 5.) Thus, Defendant contends that Plaintiff's willful failure to warn claim should be dismissed as a matter of law.

 

Plaintiff does not directly confront Defendants argument but does assert that “carriers owe a heightened duty of care to passengers during the disembarkation process. By abandoning Plaintiff and other passengers at the top of a non-operational escalator, Avianca failed in its duty to ensure that Plaintiff could safely disembark.” (Opposition p. 3.) Defendant did not provide a response to the separate statement of undisputed facts. However, Defendant does state that there are triable issues of fact regarding whether Avianca Airlines provided adequate assistance to passengers during disembarkation and whether the non-operational escalator constituted a hazardous condition that Defendant failed to address.

 

A threshold issue over whether one can bring a suit under Civil Code § 846 is whether Defendant has an ownership interest in the property. (Civ. Code, § 846; Pacific Gas & Electric Co., supra, 10 Cal. App. 5th 563, 568.) Defendant has provided evidence showing that they are not the owner of the subject stairwell at Los Angeles International Airport, which Plaintiff has not disputed. (UMF No. 5.) Thus, the claim for willful failure to warn fails as a matter of law.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgement as to second cause of action.

 

 

            Cause of Action 3 – Dangerous Condition of Public Property

 

            A public entity may be liable for a dangerous condition of public property if the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, the dangerous condition caused the injury, the condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the type of injury that occurred, and either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee within the scope of their employment created the condition, or the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to take measures to protect against it. (Gov. Code § 835; Sun v. City of Oakland (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1183.)

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s dangerous condition of public property claim should be dismissed because Defendant is not a public entity. Defendant is a private airline based in Colombia, not a public entity, and it owns no public property in California. (UMF No. 4.) Additionally, Defendant neither owns nor manages the stairwell at Los Angeles International Airport where the incident occurred. (UMF No. 5.) Given these undisputed facts, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s third count should be dismissed entirely as a matter of law.

 

            Plaintiff does not directly dispute this argument. Instead, she argues that Defendant breached its duty of care to passengers during the disembarkation process and that airlines are responsible for accidents during the disembarkation process under the Montreal Convention. (Opposition p. 3.) Defendant did not provide a response to the separate statement of undisputed facts. However, Defendant does state that there are triable issues of fact regarding whether Defendant provided adequate assistance to passengers during disembarkation and whether the non-operational escalator constituted a hazardous condition that Defendant failed to address. Defendant does not state there is a triable issue of material fact regarding whether Defendant is a public entity.

 

            A claim for dangerous conditions of public property can be brought against a public entity. (Gov. Code § 835; Sun v. City of Oakland, supra, 166 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1183.) Defendant has provided sufficient evidence to show that it is not a public entity. (UMF No. 4.) Plaintiff does not dispute that Defendant is not a public entity. Thus, Plaintiffs cause of action for dangerous conditions of public property fails as a matter of law.

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for Summary Judgement as to the third cause of action.

            On October 24, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“In any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff, if both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive.  (2) The examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220, subd. (a).)  “If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).)  “The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under Section 2032.310 only for good cause shown.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).)  “If a defendant who has demanded a physical examination under this article, on receipt of the plaintiff’s response to that demand, deems that any modification of the demand, or any refusal to submit to the physical examination is unwarranted, that defendant may move for an order compelling compliance with the demand.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.250, subd. (a).)   

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant requests that the Court issue an order granting leave to conduct additional physical examinations of Plaintiff’s right hip and an order directing Plaintiff to appear for said examination, which has been reserved as follows:

 

Examining Physician: Alexandra Starvrakis, M.D.

Examinations: Visual inspection and palpation, range of motion tests, which include movement and positioning; strength tests, which include resistance, etc.; gait; range of motion; lower extremity neurovascular examination; motor examination, medical history including pain history and other non-evasive tests regarding the right hip if applicable/indicated. No X-rays or other radiological studies will be taken without the consent of Plaintiff.

Proposed Date of Examination: November 19, 2024, at 11:45 a.m.

Location of Examination: 1225 15th Street

         Suite 2100

         Santa Monica, California 90404

 

Procedure

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s motion for a second physical examination should be denied due to a failure to specify the particular tests and procedures to be conducted, as required by California Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.310. This statute mandates that a motion for examination detail the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and specific diagnostic tests and procedures, which must be explicitly named in the motion notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310 subd. (b).) A motion under Section 2032.310 may not be granted where the moving party fails to specifically identify the particular diagnostic tests and procedures to be performed during the examination. (Carpenter v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 270.)  Defendant’s motion lacks these specifics and instead only mentions general types of tests and body areas in the memorandum, which does not meet the statutory requirements.

 

            In reply, Defendant argues that its Motion is not procedurally defective, as the Notice of Examination complies with Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.310(b) by detailing the examination’s nature and scope. The Notice specifies that the exam will include visual inspection, range of motion, strength, flexibility tests, and other non-invasive procedures, all focused on Plaintiff’s hip. Defendant contends that this level of detail is adequate and that Dr. Stavrakis’s qualifications as an orthopedic surgeon specializing in hip surgery further establish good cause for the examination.

 

            In Carpenter, the Court found that tests described as “standardized written psychological tests to test emotional and cognitive functioning” were not specific enough to satisfy Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.310(b) because they did not list the specific test to be administered. (Carpenter, supra, 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 269.) Here, the test descriptions are much more specific as they state what is being measured and how that attribute is being ascertained. For example, “strength tests, which include resistance, etc.” (See, Id; Motion for leave p. 5-6.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant’s motion is not procedurally defective.

           

            Good Cause

 

            Good cause generally requires a showing of both (1) “Relevancy to the subject matter” and (2) specific facts justifying discovery, i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere. (Vinson v. Superior Court, (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)

 

            Defendant argues that good cause exists for an additional physical examination because Plaintiff’s physical condition, specifically the right hip injury, is central to the case. Plaintiff's Complaint details multiple injuries, including severe pain in the neck, back, and hip, stemming from a fall. (See Exhibit 2.) A prior examination by Dr. Kaloostian was limited to the neck and back, with no evaluation of the hip, as Dr. Kaloostian’s expertise is in neurosurgery. (See Exhibit 10, First Amended Notice of Examination by Dr. Kaloostian.) Therefore, Defendant contends that a separate exam by Dr. Stavrakis, a certified orthopedic surgeon specializing in joint replacement, is warranted to properly assess the hip injury. Without this examination, Defendant argues, they would be disadvantaged in adequately preparing a defense regarding Plaintiff's full scope of injuries and claims for damages.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant is not entitled to a second physical examination under California law, which limits defendants in personal injury cases to a single examination, regardless of separate injuries. Plaintiff asserts that Defendant had the opportunity to cover all claimed injuries, including orthopedic and neurological issues, during the initial examination by Dr. Kaloostian. Despite this, Defendant now seeks an additional examination by another specialist without demonstrating good cause or showing why further information could not be obtained from Dr. Kaloostian's examination or existing medical records. Plaintiff argues that Plaintiff’s original counsel never actually agreed to two exams. (Silva Decl., Decl. Exh. 5 at p. 2.) Furthermore, Plaintiff knew of Defendant’s objections to the second examination a month before the examination with Dr. Kaloostian took place. (Silva Decl., Decl. Exh. 7.) Thus, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not shown good cause for a second examination.

 

            While a defendant is entitled to one physical examination in a personal injury trial, additional examination may be ordered by the Court for good cause. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.320, subd. (a).) Good cause generally requires a showing of both (1) “Relevancy to the subject matter” and (2) specific facts justifying discovery, i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere. (Vinson, supra, 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)

           

            The Court finds that Defendant has shown good cause for an additional examination. Plaintiff has placed their right hip in controversy as Plaintiff’s response to Defendant's discovery states that he has suffered pain in his right hip, including severe right hip pain after bending. (Motion for Leave Exhibit 2; Exhibit 8 p. 30:10-17) Furthermore, Defendant will be prejudiced if it cannot examine Plaintiff’s hip if the trial reaches the damages stage and Defendant lacks knowledge of Plaintiff’s hip injury. While Plaintiff points out that it is good practice to examine multiple injuries in one examination, Plaintiff does not show that Defendant lacks good cause.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion for Leave to Conduct Additional Physical Examination of Plaintiff.

 

            Conditions

 

            Plaintiff requests the Court put ten conditions on the examination.

 

            1: No later than fourteen calendar days before the examination, Defendant shall provide to Plaintiff’s counsel by electronic mail a complete list of specific tests and procedures to be performed during the second examination.

 

            The Court denies this request as Defendant has already given a list of examinations to be performed.

 

            2: The examiner shall perform no other tests or procedures other than those specified in the communication set forth in paragraph (a) above.

 

            The Court grants Plaintiff’s request as to the examinations listed in Defendant’s motion.  Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.310 subdivision (b) requires that a specific list of examinations be specified. It makes little sense for the Court to allow the Defendant to perform examinations not detailed in the instant motion.

 

            3: Should Plaintiff elect to bring a motion for protective order or similar motion after receiving the list of tests and procedures from Defendant, the examination shall be stayed until resolution of any such motion

 

            The Court denies this request.

 

            4: The examination shall last no longer than one hour (consistent with the demand served by Defendant)

 

            The Court denies this request.

 

            5: Plaintiff/Examinee shall be entitled to have her attorney or a representative designated by her attorney present for all aspects of the examination if Plaintiff should so elect (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.510)

 

            The Court grants this request as it is required under Code Civ. Proc., section 2032.51.

 

            6: Plaintiff is entitled to record all parts of the examination with audio technology and/or to have the examination recorded stenographically, and, if Plaintiff so elects, a court reporter shall be allowed to also attend the examination.

 

            The Court grants either the use of a stenographer or audio recording.

 

            7: No X-rays or other radiological tests shall be taken during the examination.

 

            See request No. 2.

 

            8:  No blood shall be drawn and no procedure may be employed that will break or puncture the Examinee’s skin or be likely to cause abrasion

 

            See request No. 2.

 

            9:  After the examination, Defendant shall provide to Plaintiff’s counsel a copy of a detailed written report setting out the history, examinations, findings, including the results of all tests made, diagnoses, prognoses, and conclusions of the examiner, and a copy of reports of all earlier examinations of the same condition of the examinee made by that or any other examiner, all in accordance with Section 2032.610 of the Code of Civil Procedure

 

            The Court requires that Defendant provide all documents required under Section 2032.610 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

 

            10.  At no point in any written examiner’s report shall the examiner refer to the examination as independent, as an “independent medical examination,” and/or as an “IME”

 

            The Court denies this request.

 

            Overall, the Court grants requests 2, 5, and 6 and denies all others.
