DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00019    Hearing Date: October 10, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Ariel Knight filed this action against Defendants, Robert Louis Wood, Jacqueline Amy Wood, The Wood Family Trust, Robert Louis Wood and Jacqueline Amy Wood as Trustees, and Henry Perry for damages arising out of the parties’ landlord-tenant relationship. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Psychological Examination

a.     Relief Sought

Defendants seek an order compelling Plaintiff to attend a psychological examination.  In the alternative, they seek an order shortening the time to respond to a demand for psychological examination. 

 

b.     Opposition

Any opposition to the motion was due on or before 9/27/23.  Plaintiff has not filed timely opposition to the motion.  The Court will not consider late-filed opposition papers. 

 

c.     Authority for Psychological Examination

Except for defense physicals in personal injury cases (in which one examination is permitted as a matter of course) and exams arranged by stipulation, a court order is required for a physical or mental examination. Such order may be made only after notice and hearing, and for “good cause shown.”  (CCP §2032.320(a).) 

 

The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action.  (CCP §2032.020(a).)  This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation.  Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337.  Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.”  See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837, wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer.  Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged. 

 

Additionally, CCP §2032.310(b) requires a party moving to conduct a mental examination to specify “the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination.”  §2032.320(d) requires the Court, in any order granting a motion to compel mental examination, to specify the above information.  Pursuant to Carpenter v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 260, a court order granting a motion to compel mental examination must specify and list by name the diagnostic tests and procedures to be used in the examination.

 

d.     Good Cause

Defendant has clearly shown good cause for the examination sought.  Defendant shows that Plaintiff, in response to discovery, has indicated her sole damages are for emotional distress, including PTSD and anxiety. 

 

e.     Diagnostic Tests and Procedures

Defendant did not, in the notice of motion or points and authorities, list the diagnostic tests or procedures to be conduct.  Defendant did, however, propound a demand for psychological examination on Plaintiff, and attached the demand as Exhibit A to the motion.  The demand includes a list of diagnostic tests and procedures that could potentially be conducted.  The Court therefore finds the requirement is satisfied.  The Court incorporates the list of diagnostic tests and procedures from Exhibit A into this order by reference, and finds the scope of the exam cannot exceed the scope identified in Exhibit A.

 

f.      Examination

Defendant seeks an order requiring Plaintiff to sit for examination on 10/09/23 at the office of Judy Ho at 1600 Rosecrans Avenue in Manhattan Beach.  Defendant set the motion for hearing, however, on 10/10/23.  The Court cannot order an exam to occur in the past.  Counsel must meet and confer by the end of the week to select a mutually agreeable date, time, and location for the examination.  If Plaintiff does not meaningfully participate in the meet and confer communications, Defendant may unilaterally schedule the examination with three days’ notice (by email). 

g.     Alternative Relief

Defendant seeks an alternative order shortening time for Plaintiff to respond to the demand.  A mental examination cannot be conducted by demand.  It can only be conducted by stipulation or court order.  §2032.320(a).  There is, therefore, nothing for Plaintiff to respond to.  There is only this order granting Defendant’s request to have Plaintiff sit for a psychological examination. 

 

Defendants are ordered to give notice.

This Court has, in the past six to nine months, seen a dramatic increase in motions to compel further responses in lemon law cases.  Similar issues are presented on all of the motions, and the attorneys for both parties fail and refuse to meet and confer in good faith in light of this Court’s prior rulings in order to resolve issues relating to each case.  The Court’s docket has been inundated with these motions, with discovery motions in lemon law cases often taking up more than five motion hearing slots in a week. 

 

The Court finds Counsel, with the assistance of this Court’s prior rulings, can easily resolve the issues on these discovery motions.  The Court has ruled on discovery motions concerning the instant defendant, American Honda Motor Co., Inc., in the following cases (among others):

22LBCV00548

22LBCV00586

23LBCV00165

19LBCV00395

BC661850

 

The Court has read and considered Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Court (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 331-334, wherein the Court of Appeals affirmed the trial court’s award of sanctions against BOTH attorneys on the case, which sanctions were ordered payable to the other party directly.  The Court finds the attorneys in these lemon law cases are acting in a way that does not benefit their clients, constantly incurring attorneys’ fees in connection with discovery disputes when they are already aware of how the Court will rule on the motions. 

 

The hearing on this motion is continued to 11/21/23.  The Court expects Counsel to meet and confer, with this Court’s prior rulings in mind (rulings by other trial courts are not relevant and should not be used in the meet and confer discussion).  If Counsel are unable to resolve all of their issues prior to the continued hearing on the motion, they must file a joint statement of items that remain in dispute.  The joint statement must list each discovery item in dispute, the most current response to that item, and each party’s statement of why a further response should or should not be ordered.  The joint statement must make arguments relevant only to the item in dispute, not to the other items, so the Court can easily and quickly rule on each item. 

 

If the parties resolve their issues, they must take the motion off calendar using the online reservation system.  If they are unable to resolve their issues, they must also file short (no more than five pages) briefs and declarations detailing their efforts to meet and confer and making any argument against imposition of sanctions that they wish to articulate.

Plaintiff, E.L. filed this action against Defendant, Doe #1, a California corporation, Doe #2, an individual, and Does 3-60 on 12/07/22.  The complaint includes causes of action for childhood sexual abuse (Doe 2), IIED (Doe 2), negligent hiring, supervision, and retention of unfit employee (Doe 1), and negligent supervision of a minor (Doe 1). 

 

On 12/12/22, Plaintiff filed an attorney certificate of merit re: Doe 2 and also one re: Doe 1; the certificates are filed under seal and are marked “received 12/09/22.”

On 2/06/23, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application to serve Does 1 and 2.  On 2/06/23 and 2/07/23, Plaintiff filed additional certificates of merit, under seal, for Does 1 and 2.  On 2/15/23, the Court granted the ex parte application to serve the doe defendants.

2.     Demurrer

a.     Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant seeks judicial notice of various documents in the court file.  The Court grants the RJN and has considered its entire file in ruling on the demurrer. 

 

b.     Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff’s attorney filed a declaration with the opposition to the demurrer.  Defendant objects to consideration of the declaration, contending it is outside the four corners of the complaint.  Defendant cites Allred v. Bekins Wide World Van Services, (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 984, 993 to support its position that the Court cannot consider the declaration in ruling on the demurrer.  The portion of the holding cited, however, confirms that the Court can consider the declaration in determining whether to grant leave to amend or not.  Ultimately, the Court finds the attorney declaration is not necessary to rule on the motion, and therefore declines to rule on the objections. 

 

c.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

d.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel filed a declaration adequately showing that the parties met and conferred prior to the filing of the demurrer. 

 

e.     Certificate of Merit

The parties agree that the statute of limitations ran on 12/31/22, such that Plaintiff was required to file certificates of merit prior to that time.  Defendant contends judicially noticeable documents show Plaintiff failed to do so.  Plaintiff contends she did so. 

 

The Court is unsure why Defendant is unable to see the documents in eCourt detailed above.  The Court’s docket clearly shows the required certificates of merit were “received” on 12/09/22 and “filed” on 12/12/22, both of which were before 12/31/22.  This comports with Plaintiff’s attorney’s declaration concerning what happened, but again, the Court need not consider the extrinsic evidence in the declaration because the Court’s own docket shows the COMs were timely received and filed.  The demurrer is therefore overruled.

Case Number: 23LBCV00249    Hearing Date: October 10, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Cataline Maese filed this action against Defendants, Marine View Apartments, LLC and Greystar Management for breach of the warranty of habitability, breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, nuisance, negligence, and breach of contract.  The crux of the complaint is that Plaintiff’s unit has inadequate heating, unapproved and illegal construction, rubbish, electrical wiring not up to code, filth, intentional interference with estate, lack of hot water, leaking plumbing, and general non-maintenance. 

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Counsel for the demurring defendant filed a meet and confer declaration, stating that the parties attempted to meet and confer but were unable to resolve the issues presented by way of the demurrer (and motion to strike). 

 

c.     Second Cause of Action, Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

i.              Actual or Constructive Eviction

Defendant argues the problems with the property must rise to the level of actual or constructive eviction to constitute a breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, citing Clark v. Spiegel (1971) 22 Cal.Apap.3d 74, 80.  Plaintiff argues this is a mis-statement of the law.  The Court has reviewed the Landlord-Tenant Rutter Guide, and finds it explains the following in this regard:

A breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment “can take many forms, including actual or constructive eviction.” [See  Nativi v. Deutsche Bank Nat'l Trust Co. (2014) 223 CA4th 261, 293, 167 CR3d 173, 200 (collecting cases);  Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apts. (2009) 171 CA4th 1004, 1036, 90 CR3d 453, 479 (collecting cases)—landlords' removing furniture from apartment and changing locks “could support judgment” on breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment claim;  Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 CA4th 873, 897, 141 CR3d 62, 81]

• [4:11] Interference with a tenant's water supply is an actionable breach of the covenant. [Marchese v. Standard Realty & Develop. Co. (1977) 74 CA3d 142, 146, 141 CR 370, 372]

• [4:11.1] Termination or reduction in services due under the lease breaches the covenant of quiet enjoyment. [Public Employees' Retirement System v. Winston (1989) 209 CA3d 205, 208, 258 CR 612, 613]

• [4:12] Likewise, there may be a breach where the landlord acts to terminate the lease in “bad faith.”

…

• [4:14] Similarly, a landlord breached the covenant of quiet enjoyment when construction work on the story above tenant's unit exposed the unit to adverse weather conditions for 1 1/2 years, causing flooding from rainstorms and ultimately rendering the unit uninhabitable. [ Barkett v. Brucato (1953) 122 CA2d 264, 274-275, 264 P2d 978, 986-987]

• [4:15] Violating a restrictive covenant in the lease not to rent to a competing business in the same building breaches the warranty of quiet enjoyment … because it interferes with the tenant's beneficial use of the property as contemplated by the lease. [ Kulawitz v. Pacific Woodenware & Paper Co. (1944) 25 C2d 664, 669-670, 155 P2d 24, 27]

 

Notably, however, actually vacating the property is not required.  The guide goes on to explain the following:

Historically, there could be no actionable breach of the covenant (either through an affirmative damages action or as a defense or offset to an unlawful detainer for nonpayment of rent) unless the tenant vacated the unit: i.e., there either had to be an actual eviction, or conduct so serious on the part of the landlord as to cause the tenant to vacate (a “constructive eviction”). [ Kulawitz v. Pacific Woodenware & Paper Co. (1944) 25 C2d 664, 670, 155 P2d 24, 27;  Petroleum Collections Inc. v. Swords, supra, 48 CA3d at 847-848, 122 CR at 117;  Clark v. Spiegel (1971) 22 CA3d 74, 79-80, 99 CR 86, 89; and see  Green v. Sup.Ct. (Sumski) (1974) 10 C3d 616, 625, 111 CR 704, 710, fn. 10 (dicta)]

However, this restrictive view is now out of sync with both the common law development of tenant rights and remedies (¶ 4:21.1 ff.) and a tenant's statutory civil penalty cause of action for landlord intimidation interfering with the tenant's right to quiet enjoyment (¶ 4:24.5).

(1) [4:21.1] Case law development against conditioning actionable breach on tenant “ouster”: Case law development takes a more liberal approach to the issue, holding that a tenant can recover damages for a breach of the covenant although still in possession of the premises. These cases recognize that tenants may lose the benefits of their rental agreements by landlord conduct amounting to something less than complete ouster—i.e., “some act of molestation, affecting, to [tenant's] prejudice, the possession of the [tenant].” [ Guntert v. City of Stockton (1976) 55 CA3d 131, 140-141, 126 CR 690, 695 (internal quotes omitted), opn. on denial of rhng. 55 CA3d 131, 127 CR 602;  Lee v. Placer Title Co. (1994) 28 CA4th 503, 513-514, 33 CR2d 572, 578;  Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 CA4th 873, 898, 141 CR3d 62, 81-82; see also  Nativi v. Deutsche Bank Nat'l Trust Co. (2014) 223 CA4th 261, 292, 167 CR3d 173, 199 (collecting cases)—“some authorities recognize that a tenant may sue for breach of the covenant while remaining in possession”; and Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 CA4th 1281, 1300, 173 CR3d 159, 172 (same)]

 

Thus, the Court finds actual or constructive eviction need not be pled in order to state a claim for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment. 

ii.             More than Mere Trivialities

While actual or constructive eviction is no longer required, pursuant to Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 588, in order to rise to the level of breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, problems with the property must be more than minor inconveniences or annoyances and must substantially interfere with the tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy. 

 

Defendant correctly notes that the complaint fails to include allegations concerning the scope or duration of the problems about which Plaintiff complains, or of how those problems have affected the tenancy.  The cause of action alleges that Defendants breached the covenant by failing to correct the habitability conditions and by illegally and improperly installing fixtures in a manner that caused gas leaks.  The cause of action fails, however, to allege when Plaintiff reported the problems, how long the problems persisted after they were reported, or any details about how the problems affected the tenancy. 

 

The demurrer is sustained on the ground that the complaint fails to state a cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment.  Plaintiff seeks leave to amend in the event the demurrer is sustained.  If Plaintiff can plead facts rising to the level detailed above, Plaintiff is permitted leave to amend to do so. 

 

d.     Third Cause of Action, Nuisance

Pursuant to Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-263, cited in the demurrer, to support a cause of action for nuisance, the conduct at issue must be unreasonable, meaning of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the property.  For the reasons detailed above in connection with the covenant of quiet enjoyment cause of action, Plaintiff’s complaint fails to do so.  The demurrer is therefore sustained on the ground that the complaint fails to state a cause of action for nuisance.  Again, leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiff can plead facts rising to the level of nuisance in an amended complaint. 

 

3.     Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for injunctive relief, punitive damages, statutory damages and restitution, and attorneys’ fees, as well as the punitive damages allegations in the body of the complaint.  Plaintiff failed to timely file opposition to the motion.  The Court finds the motion is well-taken, as Plaintiff has not pled a basis for recovery of any of the above.  The motion is therefore granted without leave to amend.
