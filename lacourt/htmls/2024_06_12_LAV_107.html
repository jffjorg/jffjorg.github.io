Case Number: 23VECV03284    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Pacific Western Bank,

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

Gevork Shashikyan et al.

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    23VECV03284                  107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Default Judgment

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

[Tentative] Ruling

Plaintiff's default judgment application is valid in all respects except:

 

  1. The demand of the complaint, $63,539.29 is different from the principal amount set forth on both the CIV-100 and JUD-100, which both give a principal amount of $63,359.29. Specifically, the amount requested in default is less than the demand of the complaint given the 5 and 3 are switched, in what appears to be a typo. This value should be corrected.

 

  1. CIV-100 Item No. 2 includes “$14.01” as General Damages; however, this amount is unsupported and it’s not clear what it is from—the late fee of 14.01 for late charges associated with Loan 1 are already included in the principal damages amount. The $14.01 in General Damages should be struck or explained by Plaintiff as it appears to be duplicative of the amount in the Demand (once the typo is corrected).

 

  1. Attorneys’ Fees Calculation is incorrect. Counsel incorrectly applied 3% when it should have been 2%, inflating the attorney fee request by $100 (assuming Counsel’s fee request is based on the “principal amount” of $60,000 [the loans not including interest that accrued prior to filing the complaint and late fees, totaling $3,539].) Attorneys’ fees request must be reduced by $100.

 

  1. Interest Calculation is unclear and possibly unsupported. The Demand of Complaint includes interest and late fees for the time period between the payments becoming due and the complaint being filed (February to July 2023); however, Plaintiff also includes interest in the amount of “$7,290.57”.  It is not clear if this amount is based on a prejudgment interest rate of 10% or the 11% and 15% interest rates of the loans themselves. The interest calculation does not include the number “7,290.57” so it is not clear how Plaintiff arrived at this value. Plaintiff must submit more information supporting the prejudgment interest request specifically setting forth the calculation based on 10% interest on the principal amounts of the loans from the date the complaint was filed until now.

 

Plaintiff's default judgment application is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

Dated:             June 12, 2024                                      _______­­­­­­­­­­___________________________                                                                                                                  Hon. Eric Harmon

                                                                                    Judge of the Superior Court

I. BACKGROUND

 

  1. Factual

 

Plaintiff Riviera Champ, LP, a California limited partnership (“Plaintiff”) owns real property located at 28375 Collins St., Tarzana, CA 91356 (the “Property”), encumbered by another building owned by Plaintiff and a deed of trust in favor of Clear Valley, LLC (the “Trust Payee”). (Compl. ¶¶ 6-7.) On October 5, 2023, via US Mail, Plaintiff mailed its monthly payment for the deed of trust using check number 1716 from its account at City National Bank (the “Check”) to the Trust Payee. (Compl. ¶ 8.) On November 9, 2023, Plaintiff was notified that Clear Valley, LLC had not received the Check or any payment due for October 2023 on the deed of trust. (Compl. ¶ 9.) With this notice, Plaintiff inspected the Check online and discovered that the check was deposited by Defendant Plainjainebeauty, LLC, a California limited liability company (“Defendant”), a company that was previously unknown to Plaintiff. (Compl. ¶¶ 10-11.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant “washed or otherwise erased” the Check to replace Clear Valley LLC’s name as payee and include it for own name and address. (Compl. ¶ 11.) Plaintiff subsequently filed a Fraudulent Check Affidavit with City National Bank and filed an Investigative Report with the Los Angeles Police Department in connection with the alleged fraudulent deposit of the Check. (Compl. ¶¶ 12-13.) To date, Plaintiff has not received the amount deposited on the Check from any source. (Compl. ¶ 14.)

 

  1. Procedural

 

On January 9, 2024, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendant and DOES 1 through 100. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) conversion, (2) money had and received, (3) violation of Penal Code §496, (4) civil conspiracy and (5) constructive trust.

 

On February 28, 2024, an answer (the “Answer”) responding to the summons was filed by MaryJaine Morales (“Morales”), pro per, stating that she played no part in stealing, washing, or cashing any checks. (Answer, p. 2.) Morales further states that she is a victim of fraud herself because her business information was stolen. (Id.)

 

On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel, Joel Shackelford, appeared at a Case Management Conference (“CMC”) where the Court permitted him to file a motion to strike Defendant’s Answer for a failure to be represented by counsel, and noted Plaintiff’s indication that it would possibly amend the Complaint to add an additional defendant, Morales. (5/9/24 Minute Order.) The CMC was then continued until July 17, 2024. (5/10/24 Notice of Continued CMC.)

 

On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff moved to strike the Answer to the Complaint. As of the date of this hearing, an opposition has not been filed.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

 Per Code of Civil Procedure §430.40(b), a plaintiff has only 10 days after service of an answer to file a demurrer to the answer. Since a motion to strike an answer may be brought, “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” a motion to strike an answer must likewise be filed within 10 days after service of the answer. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435(b)(1), 435(b); CRC Rule 3.1322(b).)  Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading.  (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.)

 

Here, the Answer was filed on February 28, 2024; however, there is no proof of service indicating when or by what means the Answer was served on Plaintiff.  At the May 9, 2024 CMC, Plaintiff sought to strike the Answer; however, the Court instructed Plaintiff to file a motion to strike the Answer.  Plaintiff’s motion to strike was then filed on May 13, 2024. The Court finds the motion to strike was timely filed.

 

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)

 

A natural person may represent himself and present his own case to the court although he is not a licensed attorney. (Paradise v. Nowlin, (1948) 86 Cal. App. 2d 897, 898.) A corporation is not a natural person. It is an artificial entity created by law and as such it can neither practice law nor appear or act in person. Out of court it must act in its affairs through its agents and representatives and in matters in court it can act only through licensed attorneys. A corporation cannot appear in court by an officer who is not an attorney and it cannot appear in propria persona. (Paradise, at 867.)  

 

“It is a longstanding rule that ”[c]orporations and other unincorporated associations must appear in court through an attorney.” (Defendant-Beam Ltd. Partnership v. Roller Derby Skates, Inc. (2004) 366 F.3d 972, 973-974.) 

 

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that the Answer must be stricken because as filed in propria persona by a member of the limited liability company, rather than an attorney, the Answer is not in conformity with the laws of California.

 

Plaintiff moves to strike on the grounds that a natural person cannot represent a corporation. A corporation cannot appear in court by an officer who is not an attorney, and it cannot appear in propria persona. (Paradise, at p. 898.) However, Defendant is not a corporation, but a limited liability company. The Court finds the same reasoning applies equally to a limited liability company, which, like a corporation, is legal entity separate and apart from its members. (PacLink Communications Internat., Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 958, 963 [“A limited liability company is a hybrid business entity formed under the Corporations Code and consisting of at least two ‘members’ who own membership interests. The company has a legal existence separate from its members. Its form provides members with limited liability to the same extent enjoyed by corporate shareholders, but permits the members to actively participate in the management and control of the company.” (Citations omitted.)].)

 

Here, the Answer filed by Defendant was not filed in conformity with the law given it was filed by an entity without attorney representation; rather, Morales, the owner of Defendant, filed the Answer as a self-represented litigant.  As discussed above, an entity cannot be self-represented, and as such, the Answer was filed not in conformity with the law.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion to Strike Answer with leave to amend.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Demurrer to the Complaint by Defendants

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Tentative Ruling:

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Complaint is sustained in part and overruled in part.

 

Background: 

 

            On February 8, 2024, Plaintiff Asha Enterprises, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint for damages and equitable relief against defendants Wilmington Savings Fund Society, FSB (“WSFS”), California TD Specialists (“TD”), New Yok Mortgage Trust, Inc. (“NYMT”), and DOES 1-10 alleging six causes of action for (1) Set Aside Foreclosure Sale, (2) Quiet Title, (3) Cancel Trustee’s Deed, (4) Declaratory and Injunctive Relief, (5) Negligent Misrepresentation, and (6) Intentional Misrepresentation. The complaint alleges as follows:

 

            On March 15, 2022, Plaintiff purchased the real property located at 4252 Tosca Road, Woodland Hills, CA 91364 (the “Property”). (Compl., ¶ 8.) Thereafter, Plaintiff borrowed $846,000 against the Property from Conventus, LLC. (Id., ¶ 9.) The note was secured by a Deed of Trust. (Id., ¶ 9.) On March 17, 2023, two weeks before Plaintiff’s note came due, Conventus recorded an assignment of the Deed of Trust to defendant WSFS as Trustee of the Residential Mortgage Aggregation Trust. (Id., ¶¶ 10-11.) Defendant NYMT served as WSFS’s agent with respect to the loan and the loan servicer. (Id., ¶ 10.) On April 1, 2023, when the note came due, Plaintiff contacted Conventus to request an extension. (Id., ¶ 12.) Neither Conventus nor WSFS informed Plaintiff of the assignment. (Id., ¶ 13.)

 

            On May 29, 2023, WSFS substituted defendant TD as Substitute Trustee. (Id., ¶ 14.) On June 9, 2023, TD executed a Notice of Default and Election to Sell under Deed of Trust, and recorded the same on June 13, 2023. (Id., ¶ 15.) On August 15, 2023, NYMT told Plaintiff that the lender would reinstate the loan and extend the maturity date to October 1, 2023. (Id., ¶ 18.)

 

            On September 19, 2023, TD recorded a Notice of Trustee’s Sale, providing notice of a trustee’s sale of the Property on October 11, 2023. (Id., ¶ 19.) Plaintiff therefore contacted NYMT regarding the terms of the offer to reinstate or extend the loan, and informed NYMT that the Property was under contract to be sold for $1,350,000, an amount that would pay off the loan. (Ibid.) On October 4, 2023, NYMT informed Plaintiff that the offer was no longer available, and an expedited payoff demand had been ordered. (Id., ¶ 21.) However, after further communication, NYMT stated on behalf of WSFS that the sale would be postponed to October 24, 2023 to allow Plaintiff to close its sale to a third party and allow the loan to be paid off in full through the sale escrow. (Id., ¶22.)

 

            Despite NYMT’s statements to Plaintiff that it would cooperate with the payoff, NYMT and WSFS failed to provide a payoff demand to the escrow and title company handling the sale of the Property for Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 23.) Between October 11 and 24, 2023 Plaintiff advised WSFS, NYMT and TD that Plaintiff’s pending escrow for sale of the Property was scheduled to close on October 31, 2023 and requested both entities to postpone the trustee’s sale for another week so that the payoff the loan could be made through the sale escrow. (Id., ¶ 23.) Both TD and NYMT (on behalf of WSFS) agreed. (Id., ¶ 24.) However, the trustee’s sale went forward anyway on October 24, 2023. (Id., ¶ 24.) WSFS was the winning bidder with a credit bid of $910,057.51. (Id., ¶ 24.) The price at which Plaintiff was selling the Property in escrow was $1,350,000.00. (Id., ¶ 24.) As a result of the misrepresentations of the Defendants, Plaintiff lost the Property and the difference between the trustee’s sale price and escrow sale price in the total sum of $439,942.49. (Id., ¶ 24.) If Defendants had not represented that they would continue the trustee’s sale, Plaintiff would have filed a voluntary petition for reorganization under Chapter 11 of the Bankruptcy Code to preserve the Property and permit the sale escrow to close with Bankruptcy Court approval. (Id., ¶ 26.)

 

            After the sale was completed and within the statutory redemption period, Plaintiff requested Defendants for the right to redeem the Property. (Id., ¶ 25.) Defendants refused. (Id., ¶ 25.)

 

            On April 17, 2024, Defendants NYMT and WSFS (“Defendants”) filed a demurrer to the complaint. No opposition has been filed.

 

Analysis

 

Meet and Confer

 

            Before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the moving party must meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading. (CCP §§ 430.41, 435.5.)

 

            The requirement is met. (See Shakibi Decl.)

 

Legal Standard

 

            A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (CCP §§ 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [internal citations omitted].)

 

First Cause of Action to Set Aside Trustee’s Sale

 

            “The elements of the tort of wrongful foreclosure are: ‘ “(1) the trustee or mortgagee caused an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale of real property pursuant to a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust; (2) the party attacking the sale (usually but not always the trustor or mortgagor) was prejudiced or harmed; and (3) in cases where the trustor or mortgagor challenges the sale, the trustor or mortgagor tendered the amount of the secured indebtedness or was excused from tendering” ’; and (4) ‘ “no breach of condition or failure of performance existed on the mortgagor’s or trustor’s part which would have authorized the foreclosure or exercise of the power of sale.” ’ ”(Majd v. Bank of America, N.A. (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 1293, 1306-1307; CACI no. 4920.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff alleged that (1) the sale was “wrongful” and that Plaintiff was “misled” (Compl., ¶ 31); (2) Plaintiff was harmed (id., ¶ 25); and (3) Plaintiff was excused from tendering (id., ¶ 30). However, Plaintiff repeatedly acknowledges that it failed to repay the loan when the note became due. Plaintiff failed to meet the fourth element. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

Second Cause of Action for Quiet Title

 

            An action for quiet title seeks “to establish title against adverse claims to real or personal property or any interest therein.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 760.020, subd. (a).) In an action for quiet title, Plaintiff must plead (1) “[a] description of the property that is the subject of the action,” specifically the location of tangible personal property and the legal description and street address or common designation of real property, (2) “[t]he title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title,” (3) “[t]he adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought,” (4) “[t]he date as of which the determination is sought,” and (5) “[a] prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.)

 

            Plaintiff (1) described the property (Compl., ¶ 8); (2) alleged its ownership in fee simple of the Property (id., ¶ 35); (3) described Defendants’ adverse claims to Plaintiff’s title (id., ¶ 34); identified the date as of which the determination is sought as October 24, 2023 (id., ¶ 38); and (5) included a prayer for the determination of Plaintiff’s title. (Id., ¶ 38.) Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Third Cause of Action for Cancellation of Trustee’s Deed

 

            “To prevail on a claim to cancel an instrument, a plaintiff must prove (1) the instrument

is void or voidable due to, for example, fraud; and (2) there is a reasonable apprehension of serious injury including pecuniary loss or the prejudicial alteration of one’s position.” (Thompson v. Ioane (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1180, 1193–94 [citing U.S. Bank National Assn. v. Naifeh (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 767, 778].)

 

            Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a basis for the cancellation of Trustee’s deed based on Defendants’ waiver of Plaintiff’s performance before Plaintiff’s sale of the Property to a third party. As a result, Plaintiff suffered damages in excess of $400,000. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action for Declaratory and Injunctive Relief

 

            The Court agrees with Defendants that declaratory relief and injunctive relief are not stand-alone causes of action, but rather remedies. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action for Negligent and Intentional Misrepresentation

 

            Defendants demur to these causes of action on the ground that they’re barred by the economic loss rule. The economic loss rule requires a purchaser to recover in contract for purely economic loss due to disappointed expectations, unless she, he or they can demonstrate harm above and beyond a broken contractual promise. (Robinson Helicopter, Inc. v. Dana Corp (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 988.) In Robinson the court held that the economic loss rule did not bar the alleged fraud claim against the manufacturer because there were affirmative intentional misrepresentations of fact via the manufacturer providing false certificates of conformance. (Id. at 991.) To proceed on a fraudulent concealment claim, plaintiff still must show that the fraud by concealment exposed “plaintiff to liability for personal damages independent of the plaintiff’s economic loss.” (Id. at 993.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff has failed to meet the requirement of pleading damages independent of economic loss. The Complaint only alleges loss of the Property and monetary damages. (See Compl., ¶ 52.) Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth and sixth causes of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Application for Writ of Possession

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Plaintiff Mercedes-Benz Financial Services (“Plaintiff”) seeks a writ of possession against Defendant Bruce Halprin (“Defendant”) over the following property: 2019 Mercedes-Benz SL55OR motor vehicle with serial No. WDDJK7DA7KFO56 128

 

Statement of the Case

 

On March 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Bedford Avenue LLC and Does 1-20 alleging four causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Common Count; (3) Claim & Delivery; and (4) Conversion. The complaint alleges that Defendant defaulted on a written Retail Installment Sale Contract for the purchase of a 2019 Mercedes-Benz SL550R with serial No. WDDJK7DA7KFO56 128 (the “Vehicle”).

 

Summary of Applicable Law

 

“Upon the filing of the complaint or at any time thereafter, the plaintiff may apply pursuant to this chapter for a writ of possession by filing a written application for the writ with the court in which the action is brought.”  (CCP § 512.010(a).)

           

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 512.010(b), the application must be submitted under oath and include:

 

(1) A showing of the basis of the plaintiff's claim and that the plaintiff is entitled to possession of the property claimed. If the basis of the plaintiff's claim is a written instrument, a copy of the instrument shall be attached.

 

(2) A showing that the property is wrongfully detained by the defendant, of the manner in which the defendant came into possession of the property, and, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the reason for the detention.

 

(3) A particular description of the property and a statement of its value.

 

(4) A statement, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the location of the property and, if the property, or some part of it, is within a private place which may have to be entered to take possession, a showing that there is probable cause to believe that such property is located there.

 

(5) A statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to a statute; or seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff; or, if so seized, that it is by statute exempt from such seizure.

 

Before the hearing on the Writ of Possession, the Defendant must be served with (1) a copy of the summons and complaint; (2) a Notice of Application and Hearing; and (3) a copy of the application and any affidavit in support thereof.  (CCP § 512.030.)

 

“The writ will be issued if the court finds that the plaintiff's claim is probably valid and the other requirements for issuing the writ are established.”  (CCP § 512.040(b).)  “A claim has ‘probable validity’ where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.”  (CCP § 511.090.) 

 

Prior to the issuance of a writ of possession, the Plaintiff must file an undertaking “in an amount not less than twice the value of the defendant's interest in the property or in a greater amount.”  (CCP § 515.010(a).) 

 

Analysis

1.         Notice

 

Notice appears proper. Plaintiff filed proofs of service on defendants Bruce Halprin and Bedford Avenue LLC on April 29, 2024.

 

2.         Basis of Plaintiff’s Claim

 

            Plaintiff seeks a writ of possession based on its claim that Defendant defaulted on monthly payments required under a written Retail Installment Sale Contract that the parties entered into for the sale/purchase of the Vehicle. After applying all payments received, there is due, owing and unpaid from Defendants, the principal balance of $63,683.01, late charges of $333.62, insufficient funds charge of $30.000, plus interest in the amount of $1,756.50, for a total of $65,803.13, plus other costs and interest. (Declaration of Todd McClurg in Support of Application (“McClurg Decl.”), ¶ 17.)

 

3.         Wrongful Detention

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 512.010(b)(2), the application must include “a showing that the property is wrongfully detained by the defendant, of the manner in which the defendant came into possession of the property, and, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the reason for the detention.”

Plaintiff has demanded payment from Defendants and that Defendants return the Vehicle to Plaintiff. (McClurg Decl., ¶¶ 17, 19.) Defendant refused. (Id.)

 

4.         Description and Value of Property

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 512.010(b)(3), the application must include a particular description of the property and a statement of its value.

 

The approximate value of the Vehicle is $54,215.00, according to Kelley Blue Book. (McClurg Decl., ¶ 21.)

 

5.         Statutory Statements

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 512.010(b)(4)-(5), the application must include:

 

(4) A statement, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the location of the property and, if the property, or some part of it, is within a private place which may have to be entered to take possession, a showing that there is probable cause to believe that such property is located there.

 

(5) A statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to a statute; or seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff; or, if so seized, that it is by statute exempt from such seizure.

 

Plaintiff is informed and believes that the Vehicle is located at 26632 Alsace Dr., Calabasas, CA 91302. (McClurg Decl., ¶ 20.) Plaintiff requests that in the event the Vehicle is behind a locked door, garage, gate or other locked facility that the County Sheriff be authorized to demand entry, break locks and/or charge into private property upon Defendant’s refusal of entry. (Id.)

 

6. Undertaking. 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 515.010 requires an undertaking to be filed before the writ issues in the amount of “not less than twice the value of the defendant’s interest in the property.” However, “[i]f he court finds that the defendant has no interest in the property, the court shall waive the requirement of the plaintiff’s undertaking and shall include in the order for issuance of the writ the amount of the defendant’s undertaking sufficient to satisfy the requirements of subdivision (b) of Section 515.020.” (CCP § 515.010, subds. (a) and (b).)

 

Based on the total balance of $65,803.13 and estimated Vehicle value of $54,215.00, no equity exists for Defendants as they owe more than the value of the Vehicle. The undertaking requirement is waived pursuant to CCP § 515.010(b).

 

Defendants request that a redelivery bond be required by Defendants in the sum of $67,000.00, which is the account balance, plus an additional $1,000.00, for estimated attorneys’ fees and court costs pursuant to CCP § 515.020(b).

 

Conclusion

 

The application for writ of possession is GRANTED.
