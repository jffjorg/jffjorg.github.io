DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV37953    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Friday, June 7, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   22STCV37953

CASE NAME:                        Jacob Shaw v. Honda Performance Development, Inc., et al

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Honda Racing Corporation USA, erroneously named Honda Performance Development, Inc.

 

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Jacob Shaw

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer with Motion to Strike

OPPOSITION:                        20 May 2024

REPLY:                                  24 May 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Defendant Honda’s demurrer to the first, fourth, sixth, seventh, and ninth to fourteenth causes of action are overruled and sustained with 10 days leave to amend as to the second, third and fifth causes of action. Defendant Honda’s motion to strike is granted with leave to amend. The court sets the OSC RE: Amended Complaint for June 20, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. Defendant to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On December 5, 2022, Jacob Shaw (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Honda Performance Development, Inc (“Honda”).; Experis US, Inc.; Experis US, LLC (together “Experis”); Manpower Group US, Inc. (“Manpower”) (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 50.  

 

On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint.  

 

On July 7, 2023, the parties stipulated to allow Plaintiff to file the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging fourteen causes of action: (1) disability discrimination, (2) failure to engage in the interactive process, (3) failure to accommodate disability, (4) retaliation in violation of Gov. Code § 12940(m)(2), (5) violation of the California Family Rights Act, (6) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment, (7) wrongful termination in violation of public policy; (8) failure to pay overtime wages; (9) failure to provide meal periods, (10) failure to authorize and permit rest periods, (11) failure to pay all wages due upon separation, (12) failure to furnish accurate itemized wage statements, (13) failure to maintain required records, and (14) unfair and unlawful business practices.  

 

On October 4, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to file a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”). The TAC alleges fifteen causes of action: (1) disability discrimination, (2) failure to engage in the interactive process, (3) failure to accommodate disability, (4) retaliation in violation of Gov. Code § 12940(m)(2), (5) violation of the California Family Rights Act, (6) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment, (7) violation of Labor Code section 233 and 234, (8) wrongful termination in violation of public policy; (9) failure to pay overtime wages; (10) failure to provide meal periods, (11) failure to authorize and permit rest periods, (12) failure to pay all wages due upon separation, (13) failure to furnish accurate itemized wage statements, (14) failure to maintain required records, and (15) unfair and unlawful business practices.  

 

On November 15, 2023, the demurrer with motion to strike to the first to seventh and ninth through fifteenth cause of action was sustained with leave to amend. On December 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Fourth Amended Complaint (4AC) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the TAC.

 

On January 17, 2024, Defendant Honda filed a demurrer with a motion to strike the 4AC. Plaintiff has filed opposition papers. The matter is now before the court.

 

discussion

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

A.        Demurrer 

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP § 430.30(a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.”¿(C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)¿For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded.¿ (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.)¿A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.”¿(Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿ 

 

B.        Motion to Strike 

 

¿Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

 

C.        Leave to Amend 

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿¿¿ 

 

II.        Demurrer[1]

 

A.        Plaintiff Has Alleged a Disability Covered by FEHA

 

Defendant Honda alleges that Plaintiff has failed to plead a disability under FEHA. “Physical disability under FEHA includes ‘[h]aving any physiological disease, disorder, condition, cosmetic disfigurement, or anatomical loss that” both affects one or more of the body's major systems and “[l]imits a major life activity.’ (Gov. Code § 12926(m)(1).) Major life activity is ‘broadly construed’ and includes working. [Citation.]” (Soria v. Univision Radio Los Angeles, Inc. (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 570, 584.) In considering the limitations of a disability on a major life activity “ ‘Limits’ shall be determined without regard to mitigating measures, such as medications, assistive devices, or reasonable accommodations, unless the mitigating measure itself limits a major life activity.” (Gov. Code, § 12926(j)(1)(A).)

 

The 4AC alleges that in or about October 2021, Plaintiff “began to suffer from persistent and chronic heartburn, acid reflux symptoms and cyclical vomiting.” (4AC, ¶ 25.) Plaintiff stated he was “sick in his stomach in the mornings” and lost a significant amount of weight. (4AC, ¶ 25.) Plaintiff told Honda that he suspected he had a stomach ulcer. (4AC, ¶ 26.) It was not until January 2022, that “Plaintiff was diagnosed with and/or treated for persistent gastroesophageal reflux disease with esophagitis, cyclical vomiting syndrome and/or anxiety and depression.” (4AC, ¶ 27.) “These impairments limited Plaintiff’s major life activities including, but not limited to, eating, sleeping and working.” (4AC, ¶ 27.) The court finds that Plaintiff’s “persistent gastroesophageal reflux disease with esophagitis” and/or “anxiety and depression” are mental and physical disorders that impact a major life activity, including Plaintiff’s ability to travel for work. (4AC, ¶¶ 27, 28.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer based on Plaintiff’s lack of a FEHA disability is overruled.

 

B.        Demurrer Based on Failure to Exhaust Administrative Remedies

 

“An employee who wishes to file suit under the FEHA must exhaust the administrative remedy provided by the statute by filing a complaint with the DFEH, and must obtain from the [DFEH] a notice of right to sue. [Citation.] The timely filing of an administrative complaint before the DFEH is a prerequisite to the bringing of a civil action for damages.” (Pollock v. Tri-Modal Distribution Services, Inc. (2021) 11 Cal.5th 918, 931 [internal quotation marks omitted]; Gov. Code, § 12960.) “It is plaintiff's burden to plead and prove the timely filing of the DFEH complaint.”(Jumaane v. City of Los Angeles (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 1390, 1402.)

 

Defendants demur to Plaintiffs’ second and sixth causes of action on the basis that Plaintiff’s FEHA complaint “does not mirror each FEHA-based cause of action in Plaintiffs’ civil complaint.” (Demurrer, at p. 16:13-14 [bold original].) Specifically, the FEHA Complaint fails to allege claims for Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process and Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment. (Demurrer, at p. 16:15-18.) In opposition, Plaintiff asserts the FEHA complaint alleged claims for Disability Discrimination and Failure to Accommodate such that the claims for Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process and Failure to Prevent Discrimination are necessarily implicated.

 

“The administrative exhaustion requirement is satisfied if FEHA claims in a judicial complaint are ‘like and reasonably related to’ those in the DFEH complaint [citation] or ‘likely to be uncovered in the course of a DFEH investigation’ [citation].” (Clark v. Superior Court (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 289, 301 [internal quotation marks omitted].) The court finds that the second and sixth causes of action are reasonably related to the claims alleged in Plaintiff’s original DFEH Complaint and Plaintiff’s 4AC is not required to mirror exactly the claims alleged in the DFEH Complaint. The demurrer on this basis is overruled.

 

C.        Second Cause of Action - Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process

 

Under FEHA, an employer is liable if they “fail to engage in a timely, good faith, interactive process with the employee or applicant to determine effective reasonable accommodations, if any, in response to a request for reasonable accommodation by an employee or applicant with a known physical or mental disability or known medical condition.” (Gov. Code, § 12940(n).) “Typically, an applicant or employee triggers the employer's obligation to participate in the interactive process by requesting an accommodation. (§ 12940, subd. (n).) Although it is the employee's burden to initiate the process, no magic words are necessary, and the obligation arises once the employer becomes aware of the need to consider an accommodation.” (Gelfo v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 34, 62, fn. 22 (Gelfo).) “Nonetheless, an employer's duty to accommodate is inextricably linked to its obligation to engage in a timely, good faith discussion with an applicant or employee whom it knows is disabled, and who has requested an accommodation, to determine the extent of the individual's limitations, before an individual may be deemed unable to work.” (Id. at p. 61.)

Defendant Honda alleges that the second cause of action fails because Honda was not aware of Plaintiff’s digestive condition. The 4AC alleges that in December 2021, Plaintiff told Honda that he believed he had a stomach ulcer and that “he may need an accommodation of a flexible start time at work.” (4AC, ¶ 27.) Plaintiff does not allege that in 2021, he requested a flexible start time and Honda failed to engage in the interactive process to determine if a flexible start time was a reasonable accommodation.

It was not until January 2022 that Plaintiff was diagnosed with his disability symptoms. (4AC, ¶ 27.) Plaintiff alleges that “Plaintiff advised Defendant Honda that he was not able to travel for work” and that he “requested an accommodation of not traveling and the opportunity to support offsite work from Defendant Honda’s worksite or from home.” (4AC, ¶ 28.) However, the 4AC fails to allege that Defendant Honda failed to provide the requested accommodations of leave and travel restrictions.

 

“Section 12940(n), which requires proof of failure to engage in the interactive process, is the appropriate cause of action where the employee is unable to identify a specific, available reasonable accommodation while in the workplace and the employer fails to engage in a good faith interactive process to help identify one, but the employee is able to identify a specific, available reasonable accommodation through the litigation process.” (Nadaf-Rahrov v. Neiman Marcus Group, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 952, 983 (Nadaf-Rahrov).) Here, Plaintiff’s requested accommodations “in the form of travel limitations and time off from work to seek medical treatment.” (4AC, ¶ 80.) The 4AC alleges facts that show that the requested accommodations were granted, although begrudgingly. “Plaintiff’s supervisors and managing agents Pierre Descamps and Masatsugu Endo, were angry with Plaintiff because of his inability to travel and his need for accommodation.” (4AC, ¶ 30.) “[A]n employer must engage in an informal interactive process aimed at effecting a reasonable accommodation, and provide a necessary and reasonable accommodation to an applicant or an employee whom it regards as physically disabled.” (Gelfo, supra, 140 Cal.App.4th at p. 29.)

 

As the 4AC alleges facts that suggest that Plaintiff’s requested accommodation of leave and time off were granted, Defendant Honda is not required to hold an interactive process meeting when an accommodation has been identified and granted. “[T]he interactive process is an informal process designed to identify a reasonable accommodation that will enable the employee to perform his or her job effectively[.]” (Swanson v. Morongo Unified School Dist. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 954, 971.) The fact that Plaintiff believes he experienced retaliation by his contract not being renewed because he requested accommodations and/or because of his actual or perceived disability, are facts that give rise to separate causes of action not encompassed in a claim for failure to engage in the interactive process.

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the second cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

D.        Third Cause of Action – Failure to Accommodate

 

“The elements of a failure to accommodate claim are (1) the  plaintiff has a disability under the FEHA, (2) the plaintiff is qualified to perform the essential functions of the position, and (3) the employer failed to reasonably accommodate the plaintiff's disability.” (Scotch v. Art Institute of California (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 986, 1009–1010.) “Where a necessary accommodation is obvious, where the employee requests a specific and available reasonable accommodation that the employer fails to provide, or where an employer participates in a good faith interactive process and identifies a reasonable accommodation but fails to provide it, a plaintiff may sue under section 12940(m).” (Nadaf-Rahrov, supra, 166 Cal.App.4th at p. 983.)

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the third cause of action on the basis Plaintiff fails to allege that Defendant Honda failed to accommodate his request for time off. The 4AC alleges that “Plaintiff requested reasonable accommodations in the form of travel limitations and time off from work to seek medical treatment.” (4AC, ¶ 80.) The 4AC alleges that “Defendant Honda deferred issues regarding time off and leaves of absences to Defendants Experis and/or Manpower.” (4AC, ¶ 29.) “Defendants, including Plaintiff’s supervisors and managing agents Pierre Descamps and Masatsugu Endo, were angry with Plaintiff because of his inability to travel and his need for accommodation.” (4AC, ¶ 30.)

 

The 4AC fails to specifically allege that Defendant Honda denied Plaintiff’s request for time off and leaves of absence. In a conclusory manner, and without facts, Plaintiff alleges “Defendants, and each of them, engaged in unlawful employment practices in violation of Government Code section 12940(m) by failing to make reasonable accommodation for Plaintiff’s known disability or perceived disability” and “Defendants failed and refused to accommodate Plaintiff’s needs based on his disabilities or perceived disability.” (4AC, ¶¶ 79, 80.) “These conclusionary allegations, which allege no specific acts, are insufficient to survive demurrer.” (Bagatti v. Department of Rehabilitation (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 344, 366, fn. 8.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues he is not required to list every possible accommodation Defendants failed to provide. This proposition is without merit.

 

“An employee cannot necessarily be expected to identify and request all possible accommodations during the interactive process itself because ‘ “ ‘[e]mployees do not have at their disposal the extensive information concerning possible alternative positions or possible accommodations which employers have....’ ” ’ ” [Citation.] But the employee should be able to identify specific, available reasonable accommodations through the litigation process, and particularly by the time the parties have conducted discovery and reached the summary judgment stage. [Citation.]

 

(Nealy v. City of Santa Monica (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 359, 379.)

 

That Plaintiff need not identify every possible accommodation at the pleading stage does not negate the fact Plaintiff is still required to allege facts showing a reasonable accommodation did in fact exist and Defendants failed to provide it. “A complaint must set forth the facts with sufficient precision to put the defendant on notice about what the plaintiff is complaining and what remedies are being sought.”  (Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.) The 4AC is devoid of facts showing that Plaintiff requested other accommodations, other than leave and travel restrictions, that were not provided by Defendants.

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

E.        Fourth Cause of Action – Retaliation in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(m)(2)

 

To establish a cause of action for FEHA retaliation (third cause of action), a plaintiff must establish “(1) they engaged in activities protected by the FEHA (2) their employers subsequently took adverse employment action against them, and (3) there was a causal connection between the protected activity and the adverse employment action.” (Miller v. Department of Corrections (2005) 36 Cal.4th 446, 472.) An employee engages in protected activity when they have “opposed any practices forbidden under [the FEHA] or because the person has filed a complaint, testified, or assisted in any proceeding under [the FEHA].” (Gov. Code, § 12940(h).) “[I]n addition to the employee protections provided pursuant to subdivision (h), retaliate or otherwise discriminate against a person for requesting accommodation under this subdivision, regardless of whether the request was granted.” (Gov. Code, § 12940(m)(2).)

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the fourth cause of action on the bases that Plaintiff fails to plead facts to show a causal connection between his request for accommodation and the non-renewal of Plaintiff’s employment because almost 8-months passed between Plaintiff’s initial request and the non-renewal of Plaintiff’s employment. “The cases that accept mere temporal proximity between an employer's knowledge of protected activity and an adverse employment action as sufficient evidence of causality to establish a prima facie case uniformly hold that the temporal proximity must be ‘very close,’ [citation].” (Clark County School Dist. v. Breeden (2001) 532 U.S. 268, 273–274.)

 

The 4AC does not rely solely on temporal proximity to allege causation “Temporal proximity does not alone satisfy this burden.” (Nadaf-Rahrov, supra, 166 Cal.App.4th at p. 990.) “It is well established that a plaintiff in a retaliation case need only prove that a retaliatory animus was at least a substantial or motivating factor in the adverse employment decision.”(George v. California Unemployment Ins. Appeals Bd. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1492.)

 

The 4AC alleges facts to show a retaliatory animus. “Defendants, including Plaintiff’s supervisors and managing agents Pierre Descamps and Masatsugu Endo, were angry with Plaintiff because of his inability to travel and his need for accommodation.” (4AC, ¶ 30.) “Masatsugu Endo in front of other employees at Defendant Honda questioned whether Plaintiff was sick and falsely accused Plaintiff of making an excuse so that he did not need to travel.” (4AC, ¶ 31.) “Defendants continued to criticize Plaintiff’s absences related to his health conditions and need for accommodation and referred to him as ‘lazy.’ ” (4AC, ¶ 34.) In June of 2022, Plaintiff informed Defendant Honda that despite various projects and upcoming shop visits that would require trave, the was still suffering symptoms from his disability. (4AC, ¶ 39.) “In or about June 2022, Gray Warren of Defendants Experis and/or Manpower contacted Plaintiff and advised him that Defendant Honda was not renewing his contract.” (4AC, ¶ 38.) Defendants also “refused to consider Plaintiff for open positions.” (4AC, ¶ 38.)

 

Based on the above allegations, the court finds that  Defendant Honda was aware of Plaintiff’s health condition, that Plaintiff’s health condition had not improved, and said considerations were a substantial factor in Honda’s decision to not renew Plaintiff’s contract and not offer him other open positions.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled.

 

F.        Fifth Cause of Action – Violation of the California Family Rights Act

 

The California Family Rights Act (“CFRA”) makes it unlawful for an employer “to interfere with, restrain, or deny the exercise of, or the attempt to exercise, any right” provided by CFRA. (Gov. Code, § 12945.2(q).) “Any violation of CFRA or these implementing regulations constitutes interfering with, restraining, or denying the exercise of rights provided by CFRA.” (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 2, § 11094.) “CFRA interference claim ‘consists of the following elements: 1) the employee's entitlement to CFRA leave rights; and (2) the employer's interference with or denial of those rights.’ ” (Moore v. Regents of University of California (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 216, 250 (Moore).) “An interference claim under CFRA does not invoke the burden shifting analysis of the McDonnell Douglas test.” (Id. at p. 250.)

 

“The elements of a cause of action for retaliation in violation of CFRA are: ‘(1) the defendant was an employer covered by CFRA; (2) the plaintiff was an employee eligible to take CFRA leave; (3) the plaintiff exercised her right to take [leave] for a qualifying CFRA purpose; and (4) the plaintiff suffered an adverse employment action, such as termination, fine, or suspension, because of her exercise of her right to CFRA [leave].’ [Citation.] Similar to causes of action under FEHA, the McDonnell Douglas burden shifting analysis applies to retaliation claims under CFRA.” (Moore, supra, 248 Cal.App.4th at p. 248.) Defendants first demurrer to the fifth cause of action on the basis that Plaintiff has not pled that CFRA applied to Honda. However, the 4AC alleges that “Plaintiff was an employee eligible to take leave and Defendants were employers covered under the California Family Rights Act (“CFRA”).” (4AC, ¶ 99.)

 

Next, Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action on the basis that Plaintiff fails to allege that “Plaintiff’s mild acid reflux condition (never actually diagnosed by a medical professional) was so severe that it prevented him from working.” (Demurrer, at p. 19:3-4.) A claim for retaliation or interference with CFRA requires that the plaintiff prove they were entitled to take CFRA leave. “CFRA's regulations provide that, for an employee to be entitled to a medical leave for her own serious health condition, the condition must cause her to be unable to work at all or unable to perform one or more of the essential functions of her position.” (Neisendorf v. Levi Strauss & Co. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 509, 516–517; see Gov. Code, § 12945.2(b)(C) [“Leave because of an employee's own serious health condition that makes the employee unable to perform the functions of the position of that employee, except for leave taken for disability on account of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions.”].) “

 

While Plaintiff alleges he had a “serious health condition,” the 4AC fails to allege that Plaintiff was entitled to CFRA leave. (4AC, ¶ 104.) “Indeed, the statutory language of the CFRA reveals that it is not triggered by a mere notice that a [plaintiff] has a serious medical condition.” (Stevens v. California Dept. of Corrections (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 285, 291.) Plaintiff fails to cite case law to support the finding that “continuing treatment by a health care provider”  or having a “serious medical condition” are sufficient to establish that Plaintiff is entitled to leave under CFRA because he was unable to perform the functions of his position. (4AC, ¶ 104.)

 

Plaintiff further argues that Honda failed to provide notice of CFRA and inform Plaintiff of his rights under CFRA. “Defendants, and each of them, engaged in unlawful employment practices in violation of the CFRA by failing to properly notify Plaintiff of his rights in regards to the CFRA; and/or interfering with, restraining, discouraging and/or denying the exercise of Plaintiff’s right to take leave for his serious health condition.” (4AC, ¶ 105.) The 4AC further alleges that “Defendants knew of Plaintiff’s serious health conditions, but during this time, Defendants did not advise Plaintiff of his rights under the California Family Rights Act, including his right to take intermittent leave to seek treatment for his serious health conditions.” (4AC, ¶ 34.)

 

“Employers subject to the CFRA are required to provide notice to their employees of the right to request CFRA leave. (Moore, supra, 248 Cal.App.4h at p. 252.) “This is because ‘[a] failure to notify an employee of his rights under FMLA [and thus CFRA] can constitute interference if it affects [the employee's] rights under [the statute].’ ” (Id. a p. 253 citing Alcala v. Best Buy Stores, LP (C.D. Cal., Nov. 7, 2012, No. EDCV 11-00798-JVS) 2012 WL 6138332, at *13 [“A failure to notify an employee of his rights under FMLA can constitute interference if it affects his rights under FMLA, although there is no relief unless the employee was prejudiced by the violation”].) “Failure of the employer to give or post the required notice regarding an employee's right to request CFRA leave precludes an employer ‘from taking any adverse action against the employee, including denying CFRA leave, for failing to furnish the employer with advance notice of a need to take CFRA leave.’ (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 2, § 11091, subd. (a)(5).)” (Bareno v. San Diego Community College Dist. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 546, 559.)

 

Plaintiff’s CFRA claim based on interference cannot be premised solely on the fact that the employer failed to provide notice. Plaintiff’s must allege facts to show that Honda’s failure to provide CFRA notice interfered with his right to take leave or prejudiced him in some way. In other words, Plaintiff must allege that had he known about his CFRA rights, he would have taken CFRA leave. As the fifth cause of action fails to allege such facts, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

G.        Sixth Cause of Action - Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment

 

The FEHA makes it unlawful employment practice “[f]or an employer ... to fail to take all reasonable steps necessary to prevent discrimination and harassment from occurring.” (Gov. Code. § 12940(k).)

 

A cause of action for failure to prevent discrimination or harassment includes the following elements: (1) actionable discrimination or harassment by employees or non-employees; (2) defendant’s legal duty of care toward plaintiff (defendant is plaintiff’s employer); (3) breach of duty (failure to take all reasonable steps necessary to prevent discrimination and harassment from occurring); (4) legal causation; and (5) damages to plaintiff.  (See Trujillo v. North County Transit District(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 280, 287, 289.) Defendant Honda demurs to the sixth cause of action on the basis there was no discrimination because Plaintiff’s request for accommodations were deferred to Manpower and Experis. (4AC, ¶ 29.)

 

The 4AC alleges that “Plaintiff’s supervisors and managing agents Pierre Descamps and Masatsugu Endo, were angry with Plaintiff because of his inability to travel and his need for accommodation.” (4AC, ¶ 30.) “Masatsugu Endo in front of other employees at Defendant Honda questioned whether Plaintiff was sick and falsely accused Plaintiff of making an excuse so that he did not need to travel.” (4AC, ¶ 31.) “Defendants continued to criticize Plaintiff’s absences related to his health conditions and need for accommodation and referred to him as ‘lazy.’” (4AC, ¶ 34.) In May or June 2022, when Plaintiff informed Honda that he still was suffering from his “symptoms of his disability” and that if he were to travel it would be at the cost of his health, but he would do so. (4AC, ¶ 36.) In June 2022, Plaintiff learned that Honda was not renewing his contract. (4AC, ¶ 38.)

Based on the facts above, the court finds that Plaintiff has pled sufficient facts alleging that Honda’s decision to not renew Plaintiff’s contract was motivated by Plaintiff’s disability/or perceived disability relating to his health. Therefore, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is overruled.

 

H.        Seventh Cause of Action – Violation of Lab. Code §§ 233 and 234

 

Labor Code § 233 provides in relevant part as follows:

 

(a) Any employer who provides sick leave for employees shall permit an employee to use in any calendar year the employee’s accrued and available sick leave entitlement, in an amount not less than the sick leave that would be accrued during six months at the employee’s then current rate of entitlement for the reasons specified in subdivision (a) of Section 246.5. . . .

 

(c) An employer shall not deny an employee the right to use sick leave or discharge, threaten to discharge, demote, suspend, or in any manner discriminate against an employee for using, or attempting to exercise the right to use, sick leave [for diagnosis, care, or treatment of an existing health condition of, or preventive care for an employee....]

 

(d) Any employee aggrieved by a violation of this section shall be entitled to reinstatement and actual damages or one day’s pay, whichever is greater, and to appropriate equitable relief.

 

Labor Code § 234 states:

 

An employer absence control policy that counts sick leave taken pursuant to Section 233 as an absence that may lead to or result in discipline, discharge, demotion, or suspension is a per se violation of Section 233. An employee working under this policy is entitled to appropriate legal and equitable relief pursuant to Section 233.

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the seventh cause of action on the ground that because the Lab. Code § 233 does not mandate employers, to offer sick leave, the 4AC must specifically allege that Honda maintained a sick leave policy, what the policy states, and how Honda violated such policy. Honda fails to cite case law to show that such specificity is required.

 

“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) “[T]o withstand a demurrer, a complaint must allege ultimate facts, not evidentiary facts or conclusions of law.” (Logan v. Southern California Rapid Transit District (1982) 136 Cal.App.3d 116, 126.) The 4AC sufficiently alleges that Defendants provided sick leave to its employees, that Plaintiff worked sufficient hours to accrue paid sick leave, and that Defendants violated Lab. Code §§ 233 and 234 by discriminating against Plaintiff. (4AC, ¶¶ 127-129.) Specifically, Defendants ended Plaintiff’s employment by refusing to renew his contract and refusing to place Plaintiff in another position because Plaintiff attempted to or did exercise his right to use his sick leave. (4AC, ¶ 29.)

 

What Defendant Honda’s sick leave policy stated is a fact that within Honda’s knowledge and questions as to how much sick leave Plaintiff had and used, are discoverable facts that are not essential to Plaintiff’s Labor Code claims. Defendant Honda fails to show that Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action makes “[ ]t impossible to decipher which sections, other than Section 233, of the Labor Code defendant allegedly violated by this pleading and thus does not put defendant on notice of how to defend against those allegations.” (Smith v. Level 3 Communications Inc. (N.D. Cal., Dec. 30, 2014, No. C 14-05036 WHA) 2014 WL 7463803, at *4.) “Plaintiff pled that he was attempting to use accrued or accumulated sick leave that he was entitled to use. [ ]This is sufficient to state a claim, and Plaintiff does not have to plead the specific number of days he was entitled to.” (Scott v. Evans Adhesive Corporation (C.D. Cal., Nov. 30, 2023, No. 5:23-CV-00978-AB-PVC) 2023 WL 9421124, at *3.)

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the seventh cause of action is overruled.

 

I.         Ninth to Thirteenth Causes of Action - Failure to Pay Overtime Wages (Lab. Code §§ 510, 1194, 1198), Failure to Provide Meal Periods (Lab. Code §§ 227.7, 512), Failure to Authorize and Permit Rest Periods (Lab. Code § 226.7),  Failure to Pay All Wages Due Upon Separation (Lab. Code §§ 201, 202, 203), and Failure to Furnish Accurate Itemized Wage Statements (Lab. Code § 226.)

 

i.          Plaintiff has a Private Right of Action to Enforce Wage and Hour Violations under the Lab. Code

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the ninth to thirteenth causes of action on the basis Plaintiff has no standing to bring an action for violations of the Industrial Welfare Commission (“IWC”) Wage Orders. (See Thurman v. Bayshore Transit Management, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 1112, 1132.)

 

Plaintiff’s opposition cites case law supporting the finding that an employee has a right to bring a private right of action to recover wages. Although there is no private right of action to directly enforce a wage order, claims for wage order violations may be brought through various provisions of the California Labor Code. (See Kennedy v. El Centro Regional Medical Center (S.D. Cal., Mar. 29, 2023, No. 22-CV-1522 JLS (LR)) 2023 WL 6035593, at p. *3; Cole v. East Bay Municipal Utility District (N.D. Cal., Mar. 23, 2016, No. 16-CV-00694-TEH) 2016 WL 1139009, at p. *2; Kamar v. RadioShack Corp. (C.D. Cal., May 15, 2008, No. CV07-2252AHM(AJWX)) 2008 WL 2229166, at pp. *4-12.) In Cuadra v. Millan (1998)  the California Supreme Court explained that “[i]f an employer fails to pay wages in the amount, time or manner required by contract or by statute, the employee has two principal options. The employee may seek judicial relief by filing an ordinary civil action against the employer for breach of contract and/or for the wages prescribed by statute. (§§ 218, 1194.)” (17 Cal.4th 855, 858 [italics original] disapproved of on other grounds by Samuels v. Mix (1999) 22 Cal.4th 1 .)

 

Defendant Honda fails to cite case law to support showing that wage and hour violations under the Labor Code do not contain a private right of action. Accordingly, the demurrer to the ninth and thirteenth causes of action is denied on this basis.

 

ii.         Plaintiff Alleges Sufficient Facts to Show He was a Non-Exempt Employee

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the ninth and thirteenth causes of action on the basis that Plaintiff has no standing to plead wage and hour violations because Plaintiff was a “chassis engineer” (4AC, ¶¶ 14, 17.) and was exempt as a matter of law under the Licensed Professional Exemption. (Cal. Code Regs. Tit. 8, § 11040(1)(A)(3)(a); Wage Order 4-2001(A)(3)(a).)

 

Defendant Honda does not dispute that under the Licensed Professional Exemption, the employee must be “licensed or certified by the State of California,” and must be engaged in certain profession, including “engineering.” (Cal. Code Regs. Tit. 8, § 11040(1)(A)(3)(a); Wage Order 4-2001(A)(3)(a).) The 4AC, alleges that “Plaintiff was also nonexempt in that he was not licensed and not primarily engaged in exempt duties and/or did not satisfy the salary basis test.” (4AC, ¶ 19.) The court finds that the 4AC sufficiently alleges that Plaintiff was not a licensed engineer, and he was not an exempt worker under the License Professional Exemption. Moreover, whether the Plaintiff is a licensed or certified engineer is a disputed issue of fact not subject to demurrer. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113-114.) Accordingly, the demurrer is overruled on this basis.

 

                        iii.        Failure to Allege Specific Wage Amounts

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the ninth to thirteenth causes of action on the basis that Plaintiff fails to allege the specific amount of wages due to Plaintiff. (See Oppenheimer v. Robinson (1957) 150 Cal.App.2d 420, 423 (Robinson) [“The mere allegation that plaintiff was damaged in the sum of $6,000 by reason of the failure to pay the wages earned is meaningless in the absence of any allegation as to the amount of wages due plaintiff”]; Oppenheimer v. Moebius (1957) 151 Cal.App.2d 818, 819–820 (Moebius) [“a count for a statutory penalty for  failure to pay wages does not state a cause of action unless it alleges facts from which the amount of the claimed penalty can be ascertained”].)

In opposition, Plaintiff states that the 4AC alleges that Honda has no record of the actual hours worked because Defendants advised Plaintiff to not account for his overtime. (4AC, ¶¶ 20-21.) “[W]here the employer has failed to keep records required by statute, the consequences for such failure should fall on the employer, not the employee.” (Hernandez v. Mendoza (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 721, 727.) “[I]mprecise evidence by an employee can provide a sufficient basis for damages when the employer fails to keep accurate records of the employee's work hours.” (Furry v. East Bay Publishing, LLC (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 1072, 1075.)

 

The court agrees that Robinson and Moebius focus on questions regarding the sufficiency of the pleading for reasons of jurisdiction and do not stand for the proposition that Plaintiff must plead the exact amount of wages due, especially when the burden is on Defendants to prove they kept accurate wage statements. To hold otherwise, would be contrary to CACI 2703. Therefore, the demurrer to the ninth and thirteenth causes of action is overruled on this basis.

iv.        Ninth, Tenth, Eleventh, and Thirteenth Causes of Action are Not Barred

 

Defendants argue that a one-year statute of limitations applies for penalties under Lab. Code § 226. (CCP § 340; Falk v. Children's Hospital Los Angeles (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 1454, 1469.) Plaintiff asserts that the ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action do not seek penalties under Lab. Code § 226. Moreover, the thirteenth cause of action, which is premised on Lab. Code §  226, for Failure to Furnish Accurate Itemized Wage Statements, is timely because Plaintiff’s contract was not renewed in June 2022 and Plaintiff filed this action on December 22, 2022. The court overrules the demurrer on this basis.

 

                        v.         Demurrer to Ninth and Thirteenth Causes of Action

 

Lastly, Defendant Honda demurs to the ninth and thirteenth causes of action on the basis that they are inadequately pled. As stated above, the ninth cause of action does not require Plaintiff to plead the exact amount of wages due.

           

As to the thirteenth cause of action, the court finds that Plaintiff sufficiently pled that his wage statement was inaccurate because Defendants failed “timely provide Plaintiff with accurate itemized wage statements in writing showing each employee’s gross wages earned, total hours worked, all deductions made, net wages earned, and all applicable hourly rates in effect during each pay period and the corresponding number of hours worked at each hourly rate, in violation of California Labor Code § 226.” (4AC, ¶ 176.) The court finds that the thirteenth cause of action adequately pleads that Defendant Honda failed to provide an accurate itemized wage statement.

 

J.         Fourteenth Cause of Action – Unfair and Unlawful Business Practices

 

Business and Professions Code § 17200 (“UCL”) prohibits “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200; see Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 610.) To plead this statutory claim, the pleadings must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation. (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)

 

“An unlawful business practice or act is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969.) “A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1473.) Lastly, a fraudulent business practice claim under section 17200 “is not based upon proof of the common law tort of deceit or deception, but is instead premised on whether the public is likely to be deceived.” (Pastoria v. Nationwide Ins. (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1490, 1499.)

 

Defendant Honda demurs to the fourteenth cause of action on the basis the 4AC fails to plead with particularity that Honda’s actions were unlawful or unfair. Defendant Honda further asserts that the 4AC fails to allege facts supporting restitution. The court disagrees. “ ‘What is important is that the complaint as a whole contain sufficient facts to apprise the defendant of the basis upon which the plaintiff is seeking relief.’ ” (Thompson v. Spitzer (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 436, 452 [internal quotations omitted] [italics original.].) The thirteenth cause of action incorporates Paragraphs 1 through 22 and 148 to 169, which includes wage and hour violations under the Labor Code alleged in ninth through thirteenth causes of action. (4AC, ¶ 175.) The court has overruled the demurrer to these causes of action and finds that they are sufficiently pled. Moreover, the thirteenth cause of action states that the UCL claim is premised on violations of Lab. Code section 226.7, 510, and 1194. (4AC, ¶ 177.)

 

The 4AC further alleges Defendants’ practices were unfair because Defendants reaped unfair and illegal profits at the expense of Plaintiff and should be made to disgorge their ill-gotten gains. (4AC, ¶ 181.) Under the unfair prong of the UCL, the claim may be premised on the allegation that a business practice is unfair because the defendant's conduct is tethered to a statutory violation. (See Graham v. Bank of America, N.A. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 613.) Here, Honda’s unfair business conduct is tethered to violations of the Lab. Code. 

 

Lastly, the court finds that Plaintiff has alleged Defendant Honda unlawfully withheld wages from Plaintiff, such that Plaintiff is entitled to restitution. “Defendants’ unfair and unlawful business practices entitle Plaintiff to seek preliminary and permanent injunctive relief, including but not limited to orders that Defendants account for, disgorge, and restore to Plaintiff the wages and other compensation unlawfully withheld from them.” (4AC, ¶ 181.)

 

While Plaintiff may not seek restitution for statutory penalties, he may seek restitution for unpaid wages. (See Pineda v. Bank of America, N.A. (2010) 50 Cal.4th 1389, 1401.) “We conclude that orders for payment of wages unlawfully withheld from an employee are also a restitutionary remedy authorized by section 17203. The employer has acquired the money to be paid by means of an unlawful practice that constitutes unfair competition as defined by section 17200.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 177.) “[P]ayments under Section 226.7 are restitutionary because they are akin to payment of overtime wages to an employee: both are ‘earned wages’ and thus recoverable under the UCL.” (Tomlinson v. Indymac Bank, F.S.B. (C.D. Cal. 2005) 359 F.Supp.2d 891, 896; Valenzuela v. Giumarra Vineyards Corp. (E.D. Cal. 2009) 614 F.Supp.2d 1089, 1103 [accord].)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourteenth cause of action is overruled.

 

III.      Motion to Strike

 

Defendant Honda moves to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages as follows:

 

1)     Page 35, Prayer for Relief, Paragraph 5: “For punitive … damages where available by law;”

 

2)     Pages 10, 13, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, and 27 at Paragraphs ¶¶58, 71, 84, 95, 109, 118, 134, and 145

 

3)     Pages 11, 14, 16, 18, 21, 23, 26, and 28 at Paragraphs ¶¶59, 72, 85, 96, 110, 119, 135, and 146.

 

4)     Pages 28 - 29, Paragraph ¶147.

¿When the defendant is a¿corporation, “the oppression, fraud, or malice must be perpetrated, authorized, or knowingly ratified by an officer, director, or managing agent of the¿corporation.” (Wilson v. Southern California Edison Company (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 123, 164; see Civ. Code § 3294(b).)¿ 

 

Plaintiff fails to point to allegations in the 4AC that show that a managing agent of Defendant Honda engaged in the malice or oppression or how said individual authorized or knowingly ratified the malice or oppression. The 4AC fails to allege Pierre Descamps and Masatsugu Endo are alleged the managing agents of Defendant Honda who the individuals who perpetrated or ratified the malice or oppression. (4AC, ¶ 30.) Moreover, Sarah Huges and Anthony Del Preto, are alleged to have ratified “illegal conduct” but no facts are given as to how such conduct was knowingly ratified or that said individuals are managing agents for Defendant Honda.

 

Therefore, Defendant Honda’s Motion to Strike is granted with leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Honda’s demurrer to the first, fourth, sixth, seventh, and ninth to fourteenth causes of action are overruled and sustained with 10 days leave to amend as to the second, third and fifth causes of action. Defendant Honda’s motion to strike is granted with leave to amend. The court sets the OSC RE: Amended Complaint for June 20, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. Defendant to give notice.
