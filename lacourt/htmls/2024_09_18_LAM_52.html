DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 21STCV44242    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Shane Perel-Wertman’s Motion to Compel Second Further Response to Form Interrogatories

Plaintiff Shane Perel-Wertman moves to compel defendant Liza Perel to serve second further responses to form interrogatories Nos. 12.1, 15.1, and 17.1.  A party propounding interrogatories may move to compel further responses when an answer “is evasive or incomplete,” “[a]n exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate,” or “[a]n objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”  (CCP § 2030.300(a).) 

On June 26, 2024, the court granted plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories – general, including Nos. 12.1, 15.1, and 17.1.  “If a party then fails to obey an order compelling further response to interrogatories, the court may make those orders that are just, including” issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, terminating sanctions, or monetary sanctions.  (CCP § 2030.300(e).)

Payment of Sanctions

            Plaintiff argues defendant disobeyed the court’s order issuing sanctions because she refuses to pay them by cashier’s check as plaintiff requested.  Plaintiff’s counsel offers no authority nor any valid reason that permits him to insist on payment by his preferred method.  Defendant’s counsel did ask plaintiff’s counsel about his preferred method of payment, but that does not give plaintiff free reign to be unreasonable. 

Timeliness of Further Responses and Opposition

            Plaintiff argues defendant disobeyed the court’s order by serving further responses after close of business on July 26, 2024.  The court ordered defendant to serve further responses within 30 days of June 26, which was July 26.  The court did not order her to serve the responses by a particular time that day.  Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (a)(4) provides, “Any document that is served electronically between 12:00 a.m. and 11:59:59 p.m. on a court day shall be deemed served on that court day.”  Defendant served her responses on July 26, a court day.  Doing so complied with the court’s order.

            Plaintiff also argues defendant’s opposition was untimely.  The opposition was due September 5, nine court days before this hearing.  The clerk’s stamp on the opposition indicates it was “Electronically FILED” at “9/06/2024 12:00 AM.”  Defendant thus filed the opposition one minute late.  Plaintiff suffered no real prejudice.  This conduct, however, raises a concern that defendant’s counsel, David B. Felsenthal (State Bar No. 140203), is intentionally serving documents as late as possible.  Doing so would be discourteous, uncivil, and unprofessional in violation of local and state rules of court.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.7 [“As an officer of the court, I will strive to conduct myself at all times with dignity, courtesy and integrity”]; Local Rules, Appendix 3.A, Guidelines for Civility in Litigation, (b)(1) [“The timing and manner of service of papers should not be used to the disadvantage of the party receiving the papers”].)

Answers to Form Interrogatories

            Plaintiff contends defendant’s response to form interrogatory No. 12.1 is evasive because she redefined the term “incident.”  Plaintiff’s form interrogatories use the default definition: “INCIDENT includes the circumstances and events surrounding the alleged accident, injury, or other occurrence or breach of contract giving rise to this action or proceeding.” 

            Defendant gave a sufficient answer to form interrogatory No. 12.1.  It asks her to identify anyone who witnessed the incident, who made any statement at the scene, who heard any statements made about the incident, or whom she claims has knowledge of the incident.  She responded by identifying herself, the plaintiff, and Joel Wertman.

Plaintiff argues defendant must answer as to his allegations about “the first 21 years of his life.”  (SAC, ¶ 3.)  Events occurring over 21 years are not an “incident” and did not give rise to this action.  As defendant asserts, “None of my alleged failings as Shane’s mother in ‘the first 21 years of his life’ or otherwise have anything to do with whether I committed an assault and battery against Shane.”  (L. Perel Decl., ¶ 8.)  Moreover, defendant cannot feasibly identify every person who witnessed 21 years of events.  Plaintiff’s second amended complaint only alleges a handful of events that can reasonably be considered “incidents” or events giving rise to this action.  (SAC, ¶¶ 8, 11, 16, 22.) 

Defendant did not give a complete response to form interrogatory No. 15.1.  It asks her to identify each denial of a material allegation and, for each, to (a) state all supporting facts, (b) identify all supporting witnesses, and (c) identify all supporting documents or tangible things.  Defendant responded, “In his Second Amended Complaint, Plaintiff describes encounters between Plaintiff and Defendant which occurred at 6517 Whitworth Drive, Los Angeles, CA 90035.  Defendant generally recalls the encounters with Plaintiff (the adult son of Defendant), but the encounters were different than what Plaintiff describes in his Second Amended Complaint.  Defendant did not threaten her adult son or offensively touch her adult son.  Defendant did exchange some harsh words with her adult son, but that is all.” 

This response is sufficient as to No. 15.1(a).  The encounters alleged in the second amended complaint are the material allegations defendant denied.  Allegations about plaintiff’s first 21 years of life are not material.  Defendant sufficiently states facts on which she bases her denial: the things he alleges did not happen, and what did happen is that she “exchange[d] some harsh words with” him.  Defendant did not, however, answer parts (b) or (c).  Plaintiff is entitled to answers to those parts of No. 15.1.

Finally, defendant gave a valid response to form interrogatory No. 17.1.  The interrogatory asks various questions about “each response” to requests for admission “that is not an unqualified admission.”  Plaintiff argues defendant’s response is incomplete because it omitted requests for admission Nos. 9, 15, and 16.  Defendant, however, served amended responses to plaintiff’s requests for admission along with her further responses to the form interrogatories.  (Felsenthal Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 2.)  Her amended responses to Nos. 9, 15, and 16 are simply, “Admit.”  (Id., pp. 2-4.)  Those are unqualified admissions.  Form interrogatory No. 17.1 does not apply to them.

Plaintiff argues amending the responses to requests for admission violated Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.300.  Subdivision (a) of that section provides, “A party may withdraw or amend an admission made in response to a request for admission only on leave of court granted after notice to all parties.”  Subdivision (b) provides, “The court may permit withdrawal or amendment of an admission only if it determines that the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.”  This section applies to withdrawal or amendment of “an admission”—not a denial.  Defendant amended her responses to make unqualified admissions.  Doing that gave plaintiff the admissions he asked for.  There is no good reason to protest now.

Sanctions

            Plaintiff moves for various types of sanctions against defendant.  Plaintiff’s motion achieved only minimal success.  Defendant acted with substantial justification.  The court will not impose any sanctions against defendant.

Disposition

Plaintiff Shane Perel-Wertman’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories is granted in part only as to form interrogatory No. 15.1, parts (b) and (c).  Defendant Liza Perel is ordered to serve further verified responses without objections to form interrogatories – general, No. 15.1, part (b) and part (c) within 10 days.


Case Number: 23STCV14304    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD) demurs to the second through seventh causes of action and the ninth cause of action alleged in the second amended complaint by plaintiff Jane M.R. Doe.

Summary of Allegations

            Plaintiff alleges she was abused by David Lee Scott, a counselor at her high school, CATCH Prep Charter High School.  (SAC, ¶¶ 22-28.)  She alleges Scott previously worked for LAUSD at Crenshaw Senior High School, where he “had inappropriate relationships with multiple female students, including inappropriate touching and sexual misconduct with at least one student.”  (¶ 14.)  LAUSD allegedly “gave Scott multiple warnings related to him having inappropriate relationships with students” and gave him a “below standard final evaluation for failing to maintain appropriate relationships with students and staff.”  (¶ 15.)  The second amended complaint alleges LAUSD decided “to terminate him" (ibid.), but “allowed Scott to quietly resign from his position at Crenshaw Senior High School in exchange for LAUSD facilitating his move to another school and/or school district.”  (¶ 17.)

            Plaintiff further alleges Scott quickly applied to work at CATCH, which “required Scott to submit a background [check] to LAUSD, and … required Scott to work and/or train with LAUSD.”  (¶ 18.)  The second amended complaint alleges CATCH’s executive director contacted the principal and assistant principal of Crenshaw Senior High School, both of whom did not mention the problems with Scott’s performance.  (¶ 19.)  Instead, “both provided” the executive director “with false glowing recommendations for Scott” and “represented that Scott was in good standing with LAUSD and eligible for re-hire.”  (Ibid.)  LAUSD allegedly “failed to notify CATCH of the complaints LASD had received from students and staff regarding Scott” or “that Scott was unfit to work with children.”  (¶ 21.)  Plaintiff alleges, “[R]elying on LAUSD’s recommendation, CATCH employed Scott in August of 2005,” and LAUSD thereby “rid itself of Scott.”  (Ibid.)

The second amended complaint includes extensive new allegations about the relationship between LAUSD and CATCH.  (SAC, ¶¶ 4, 29-36.)  It alleges, “LAUSD retained the highest level of authority within CATCH and maintained the top position of the CATCH organizational chart … .  LAUSD was at the beginning of its charter movement, and LAUSD’s relationship with CATCH exceeded that of a modern-day chartering authority to charter school relationship, so much so, that LAUSD and CATCH essentially operated as the same organization.”  (¶ 4.)

Plaintiff further alleges: “LAUSD oversaw both the educational and operational components of CATCH, including but not limited too: 1) the development of the CATCH Charter Petition; 2) overseeing staff recruitment, background checks, selection, evaluation, and termination; 3) conducting on-site observations; 4) providing monthly trainings for CATCH staff to attend at LAUSD’s facilities; 5) student safety; and 6) and monitoring CATCH’s financial situation, including by requiring CATCH to maintain specific liability insurance coverage. According to testimony by CATCH’s Executive Director at the time, Patricia Smith, LAUSD also maintained a CATCH Oversight Folder which contained information and documentation that LAUSD required CATCH to provide to LAUSD for inspection, including but not limited to documents related to CATCH’s financial position, liability coverage, employee hiring documents, employee training documents, employee credentialing information, student safety, etc. LAUSD was required to inspect these documents to make sure CATCH was compliant, and to ensure that CATCH was in compliance with the requirements LAUSD had in place. LAUSD was also required to perform site visits and to observe students, teachers, and staff as part of their oversight duties.”  (¶ 30.) 

Sham Pleading Doctrine

            LAUSD argues the court should apply the sham pleading doctrine to the allegations concerning LAUSD’s oversight of CATCH.  (¶¶ 29-36.)  “Under the sham pleading doctrine, plaintiffs are precluded from amending complaints to omit harmful allegations, without explanation, from previous complaints to avoid attacks raised in demurrers or motions for summary judgment.”  (Deveny v. Entropin, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 408, 425.)  The doctrine is not “intended to prevent honest complainants from correcting erroneous allegations of generic terms which may have legal implications but which are also loosely used by laymen or to prevent the correction of ambiguous statements of fact.” (Contreras v. Blue Cross of California (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 945, 950.)

            LAUSD contends the allegations discussed above contradict portions of the first amended complaint about the relationship between LAUSD and CATCH.  For example, the first amended complaint alleged, “At all relevant times, Defendant CATCH was an LAUSD-authorized charter school, operating under a contract with LAUSD, and a partner of LAUSD.  Defendant LAUSD” was “responsible for overseeing existing charter schools within the district, including CATCH.  Specifically, LAUSD oversaw both the educational and operational components of CATCH during all relevant times.”  (FAC, ¶ 4.)  The amendments to the complaint are not so inconsistent as to warrant applying the sham pleading doctrine.  Rather than contradicting the prior allegations, the new allegations provide further detail about LAUSD’s oversight of CATCH.

Immunity under the Charter Schools Act

Charter schools, including CATCH, are created pursuant to the Charter Schools Act.  Under this statutory scheme, charter schools “operate independently” from local school districts (Ed. Code, § 47601), though they are subject to “supervisorial oversight” by the chartering authority, which can be a local school district like the LAUSD.  (Ed. Code, § 47613).  Chartering authorities have “continuing oversight and monitoring powers.”  (Mendoza v. State of California (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1034, 1060.)  Those powers do not, however, include the power or responsibility to hire, fire, and supervise employees, including teachers. 

Education Code section 47604, subdivision (d) provides, “A chartering authority that grants a charter to a charter school to be operated as or by a nonprofit public benefit corporation is not liable for the debts or obligations of the charter school or for claims arising from the performance of acts, errors, or omissions by the charter school if the chartering authority has complied with all oversight responsibilities required by law, including, but not limited to, those required by Section 47604.32 and subdivision (m) of Section 47605.” 

LAUSD argues Education Code section 47604, subdivision (d) immunizes it from liability to plaintiff for events that occurred at CATCH.  The second amended complaint, however, does not allege LAUSD is vicariously liable “for claims arising from the performance of acts, errors, or omissions by the charter school.”  Instead, plaintiff’s causes of action against LAUSD arise from its own alleged conduct with respect to Scott and overseeing CATCH.  Plaintiff alleges that, contrary to state law, LAUSD “oversaw” “staff recruitment, background checks, selection, evaluation, and termination” of employees.  (SAC, ¶ 30.)  In other words, Plaintiff alleges LAUSD itself was negligent, not that it is liable for CATCH’s negligence.

In contrast to federal court, this court must accept plaintiff’s allegations as true, however implausible.  Further, the court assumes plaintiff’s counsel complied with their ethical obligation of diligently confirming that the allegations have evidentiary support or are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation and discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (b)(3).)

Duty

            LAUSD contends it owed no duty to plaintiff.  For example, it argues, “A public school district has no duty to a nonstudent at a site that is not owned or controlled by that district.”  (Demurrer, p. 4.)  The second amended complaint, however, alleges detailed facts about the extent of LAUSD’s oversight of CATCH.  (¶¶ 29-36.)  It alleges that, in contrast with the modern relationship between public school districts and charters, “LAUSD was at the beginning of its charter movement” and its “relationship with CATCH exceeded that of a modern-day chartering authority to charter school relationship, so much so, that LAUSD and CATCH essentially operated as the same organization.”  (¶ 4.)  Plaintiff thus alleges LAUSD did control CATCH, the site where the abuse occurred. 

2nd Cause of Action: Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention of an Unfit Employee

Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action.  Negligent hiring, retention, and supervision applies “where the plaintiff’s injury occurred in the workplace, or the contact between the plaintiff and the employee was generated by the employment relationship.”  (Mendoza v. City of Los Angeles (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1339–1340.)  “[A]n employer does not owe a plaintiff a duty of care in a negligent hiring and retention action for an injury or other harm inflicted by a former employee on the plaintiff even” if the employee “initially met the plaintiff while employed by the employer.”  (Phillips v. TLC Plumbing, Inc. (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1133, 1141.) 

The second amended complaint alleges Scott sexually assaulted plaintiff after LAUSD “terminated” him.  (¶ 16.)  LAUSD no longer supervised or retained Scott in the usual sense.  But “[i]rrespective of the labels attached by the pleader to any alleged cause of action, [courts] examine the factual allegations of the complaint, ‘to determine whether they state a cause of action on any available legal theory.’ ”  (Adelman v. Associated Intern. Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 352, 359.)  Plaintiff alleges LAUSD exercised such control over CATCH (¶¶ 29-36) that, while LAUSD no longer directly employed or retained Scott, it continued to supervise him.  She further alleges LAUSD could and should have taken action to prohibit Scott from working at CATCH.  (¶¶ 15-21.)  Plaintiff thus alleges sufficient facts to establish (a) LAUSD owed a duty to her via its control over CATCH and its employees and (b) LAUSD breached that duty.

3rd Cause of Action: Breach of Mandatory Duty

            Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  Government Code section 815.6 provides, “Where a public entity is under a mandatory duty imposed by an enactment that is designed to protect against the risk of a particular kind of injury, the public entity is liable for an injury of that kind proximately caused by its failure to discharge the duty unless the public entity establishes that it exercised reasonable diligence to discharge the duty.”  The elements of this claim are: “(1) a mandatory duty is imposed by enactment, (2) the duty was designed to protect against the kind of injury allegedly suffered, and (3) breach of the duty proximately caused injury.”  (State Dept. of State Hospitals v. Superior Court (2015) 61 Cal.4th 339, 348.) 

            The second amended complaint does not allege sufficient facts for the third element: breach of any mandatory duty that proximately caused injury to plaintiff.  The second amended complaint refers to numerous statutes and regulatory duties—none of which meet this element.  (¶ 53.)  Education Code section 44870 and California Code of Regulations, Title 5, sections 5530, 5531, and 5552 impose duties to monitor pupils’ conduct.  For example, Education Code section 44807 provides, “Every teacher in the public schools shall hold pupils to a strict account for their conduct on the way to and from school, on the playgrounds, or during recess.”  Even assuming students at CATCH constitute LAUSD’s “pupils,” plaintiff does not allege she was harmed because a teacher failed to hold pupils to account for their conduct.  She alleges Scott, a counselor or dean, harmed her.

            The second amended complaint also cites Education Code sections 44662, 44664, and 44830.  These sections govern procedures for evaluating employees, such as the permitted criteria for evaluations (Ed. Code, § 44662, subds. (b)-(e)) and the frequency of evaluations (Ed. Code, § 44664(a)).  These statutes concern the technical process of evaluating employees.  Plaintiff does not allege LAUSD failed to do so or that any such failure proximately caused harm to her.  She alleges LAUSD did evaluate Scott.  (SAC, ¶¶ 15, 18.)  Section 44830, meanwhile, requires that “for positions requiring certification qualifications” (id., subd. (a)), school districts must only employ people who have certain qualifications such as passing a “state basic skills proficiency examination” (id., subd. (c)) or have a bachelor’s degree (id., subd. (m)).  Plaintiff does not allege Scott lacked a required credential. 

            Finally, the second amended complaint refers to Education Code section 44870 (¶ 53), which provides, “No one shall be employed to supervise the work of teachers for more than half time during any school week unless he is the holder of a valid teacher’s certificate authorizing him to teach in the schools and classes in which he is to supervise instruction and a valid supervision certificate.”  Plaintiff does not allege someone without a valid teacher’s certificate supervised the work of teachers more than half time or that any such failure harmed her.

4th Cause of Action: Failure to Report Suspected Child Abuse

Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action.  The Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act (CANRA) “requires a mandated reporter to make a report to a law enforcement agency or a county welfare department ‘whenever the mandated reporter, in his or her professional capacity or within the scope of his or her employment, has knowledge of or observes a child whom the mandated reporter knows or reasonably suspects has been the victim of child abuse or neglect.’ ”  (B.H. v. County of San Bernardino (2015) 62 Cal.4th 168, 186.)  The California Supreme Court has stated, “Reasonably construed, [CANRA] was intended to protect only those children in the custodial care of the person charged with reporting the abuse, and not all children who may at some future time be abused by the same offender.”  (Randi W. v. Muroc Joint Unified School Dist. (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1066, 1087.)

The second amended complaint alleges, “The employees and agents of Defendants knew or reasonably suspected that Defendant SCOTT and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, and each of them, were acting inappropriately with Plaintiff.”  (¶ 66.)  LAUSD argues this allegation is conclusory, so the court should not accept it.  But someone’s knowledge, suspicion, or other state of mind is generally an ultimate fact that courts accept on demurrer.  (See, e.g., Johnson v. Casetta (1961) 197 Cal.App.2d 272, 276 [“an allegation of knowledge of incompetency is an allegation of ultimate fact”]; Rosin v. Superior Court (1960) 181 Cal.App.2d 486, 490 [someone’s “intent” was “an allegation of fact, not a mere conclusion”].)  Plaintiff’s allegations suffice on demurrer.

5th Cause of Action: Negligent Supervision of a Minor

            Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action for the same reasons as the second cause of action.  Though she was not a student of an LAUSD school, plaintiff alleges LAUSD exercised such control over CATCH that it was effectively supervising the students there.     

6th Cause of Action: Negligent Failure to Warn, Train, or Educate

            Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action.  LAUSD’s demurrer relies on the premise that this cause of action seeks to hold LAUSD liable for not educating plaintiff about avoiding sexual abuse.  (Demurrer, pp. 8-9.)  This cause of action, however, also alleges LAUSD breached duties to train faculty: “Defendants had a duty to warn, train, and educate the students in their custody, care, and control, like Plaintiff, on known and knowable dangers posed by its employees and/or agents.  Defendants also had a duty to warn, train, and educate its employees and/or agents on its sexual misconduct policy and on inappropriate boundary crossing with minor students.”  (¶ 84.)  It further alleges LAUSD breached those duties both by failing to warn plaintiff and “failing to adequately and properly train its faculty, including” Scott “on their sexual misconduct policies.”  (¶ 85.)  Thus, assuming school districts have no duty to teach or warn students about avoiding sexual abuse (see Ed. Code, § 51950(a) [“A school district may provide abuse, including sexual abuse, and human trafficking prevention education”]), plaintiff alleges LAUSD breached a duty to teach or warn faculty and other employees.

7th Cause of Action: Negligence

            Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action for the same reasons as her other negligence claims. 

9th Cause of Action: Sexual Harassment

            Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action against LAUSD.  “[A] school district can be held liable for damages when a teacher sexually harasses a student.  [Citation.]  However, the district is not liable ‘unless an official of the school district who at a minimum has authority to institute corrective measures on the district’s behalf has actual notice of, and is deliberately indifferent to, the [employee’s] misconduct.’ ”  (Roe v. Hesperia Unified School District (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 13, 33; accord Donovan v. Poway Unified School Dist. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 567, 601.) 

            Plaintiff alleges LAUSD knew Scott had “had inappropriate relationships with multiple female students, including inappropriate touching and sexual misconduct with at least one student he was counseling” at Crenshaw Senior High School.  (SAC, ¶ 14.)  Plaintiff further alleges, “The employees and agents of Defendants knew or reasonably suspected that” Scott was “acting inappropriately with Plaintiff.”  (¶ 66.)  Considering the new allegations about LAUSD’s extensive control over CATCH, plaintiff adequately alleges LAUSD officials had actual notice of Scott’s misconduct with plaintiff and failed to institute corrective measures.    

Possibility of Amendment

            After a successful demurrer, where “there is a reasonable possibility that the defects can be cured by amendment, leave to amend must be granted.”  (Stevens v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 594, 601.)  The plaintiff bears the burden of “demonstrat[ing] how the complaint can be amended.”  (Smith v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 700, 711.)  Plaintiff does not meet her burden of demonstrating how she can cure the defects with her third cause of action for breach of mandatory duty.

Disposition

            Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s demurrer to plaintiff’s third cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  Defendant’s demurrer to plaintiff’s second, fourth through seventh, and ninth causes of action is overruled.  Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District shall answer the second amended complaint within 20 days.
