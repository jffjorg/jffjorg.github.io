Case Number: 21VECV00125    Hearing Date: January 4, 2024    Dept: W

LEWITT, HACKMAN, SHAPIRO, MARSHALL & HARLAN, A LAW CORP v. JILL DAILEY, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees and costs

 

Date of Hearing:        January 4, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        21VECV00125

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Lewitt, Hackman, Shapiro, Marshall, & Harlan, a Law Corp.

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 29, 2021, Plaintiff Lewitt, Hackman, Shapiro, Marshall, & Harlan filed a complaint against Defendants Jill Dailey and TDM Franchise Company, LLC for breach of contract, Open Book Account and Account Stated. Plaintiff and Defendants entered into a written agreement whereby Plaintiff agreed to represent Defendants and Defendants agreed to pay Plaintiff's fees and costs for such services. Plaintiff alleges Defendants, however, breached the agreement on March 29, 2019 when Defendants ceased payment to Plaintiff for legal services performed and costs incurred.

 

Default was entered against TDM Franchise Company, LLC on August 2, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff Lewitt, Hackman, Shapiro, Marshall, & Harlan, a Law Corp.’s Motion for Attorney Fees and Costs is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff Lewitt, Hackman, Shapiro, Marshall, & Harlan move the court for an order awarding attorney fees to Plaintiff as prevailing party. Plaintiff seeks $1,200.00 in attorney fees.

 

Parties to litigation must generally bear their own attorney’s fees, unless they otherwise agree. (CCP § 1021.) In contract actions based on a book account when fees are not otherwise specified, the prevailing party is entitled to reasonable fees. (Civil Code §1717.5.) This section is limited by subsection (b) which states: “The attorney’s fees allowed pursuant to this section shall be the lesser of either the maximum amount allowed by this section, the amount provided by any default attorney’s fee schedule adopted by the court applicable to the suit, or an amount as otherwise provided by the court.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 1717.5(b).)  

 

Plaintiff requests $1,200 for attorney’s fees and $2,875.86 in costs. On November 20, 2023, the court found in favor of Plaintiff. (See 11/20/23 Minute Order.) Plaintiff’s claims were for Defendants failure to make payments pursuant to a written agreement for Plaintiff’s legal services. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s claims fall within Civil Code section 1717.5.

 

Plaintiff’s unopposed Motion for Attorney fees is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s unopposed Memorandum of Costs is GRANTED less the attorney fees listed in section 10. 

 

The court will sign the proposed judgment.

BACKGROUND

 

On March 9, 2023, Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Paige Hemmis, Jason Short, Ameka Benton, Scott Fields, Hallmark Cards, Inc., Crown Media Holdings, Inc., Paige Hemmis, Inc., Aria Music Group, The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners Association, Melissa Improta, Jason Improta, and Compass California, Inc. for 1) Intentional Interference with Contract; 2) Negligence; 3) Trespass; 4) Violations of Civil Code § 1798.93; 5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6) Professional Negligence; 7) Unfair Business Practices; 8) Fraudulent Concealment; 9) Negligent Misrepresentation; 10) Fraud; 11) Slander of Title; 12) Constructive Fraud; 13) Wrongful Eviction; 14) Quiet Title; and 15) Conversion.

 

Plaintiffs allege about September 15, 2019, when Plaintiffs were absent from the subject property, Defendants Hemmis and Short unlawfully entered upon the subject property and remained in possession of the property until early July 2022. While Defendants Hemmis and Short were in possession of the property, Plaintiffs were engaged in a dispute with their mortgage lender, US Bank, related to a settlement agreement that was entered into to address the pay off of a mortgage loan. Plaintiffs further allege at some point prior to September 12, 2019, Defendants Hemmis, Short, Improta and Compass engaged in a conspiracy to attempt to steal title to the Plaintiffs’ home. On this point, Plaintiffs claim Hemmis and Short fraudulently forged a lease that purported to have Kimberly Davis' signature on it. As a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged. Plaintiff dismissed Defendants Ameka Benton and Scott Fields.

 

On March 24, 2023, this action was related to Kimberly Davis v. Nationstar, et al. (23VECV01125). That action involves a mortgage foreclosure.

 

On August 18, 2023, Paige Hemmis and Jason Short filed a cross-complaint against Kimberly Ann Davis, Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta, and Jason Improta for breach of contract, money had and received, implied indemnification, express indemnification, and comparative contribution.

 

Plaintiffs dismissed Aria Music Group on December 29, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Certain Special Interrogatories from Defendants Crown Media Holdings, Inc. and Hallmark Cards, Inc. is GRANTED, in part.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In support of Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to certain special interrogatories from Defendants Crown Media Holdings, Inc. and Hallmark Cards, Inc., Plaintiffs’ request this court take judicial notice of certain court documents and documents filed with the California Secretary of State (Exhs. 1-7). The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice. (See Evid. Code §452(c),(d).)

 

In opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion, Hallmark Defendants request this court take judicial notice of the following documents: (1) a screenshot of the At Home With Our Family video segment entitled “Paige Hemmis DIY Mason Jar Candles,” posted to a publicly available YouTube page on April 1, 2020 (Exh. 4); (2) California Governor Gavin Newsom’s COVID-19 stay-at-home order, Executive Order N-33-20, issued by the Executive Department of the State of California on March 19, 2020 (Exh. 5); and (3) A press release issued by PR Newswire on March 9, 2016 entitled “Hallmark announces intent to take Crown Media private” (Exh. 6).

 

Plaintiffs object to Hallmark Defendants’ request for judicial notice on the grounds it is not appropriate to take judicial notice of information on a website. (See Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872.) The court agrees generally courts do not take judicial notice of websites, even government websites, as “the official acts of a governmental entity does not in and of itself require acceptance of the truth of factual matters.” (See Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 194.) However, the court may take judicial notice of official acts and not the truth of the facts stated therein. The court GRANTS Hallmark Defendants’ request for judicial notice as to the existence of the websites. (See Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 193.)

 

Plaintiffs, in reply, further request this court take judicial notice of several more documents filed with the California Secretary of State and in the United States District Court, Central District of California. The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice. (See Evid. Code §452(c),(d).)

In reply to Defendant’s opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to certain special interrogatories from Hallmark Defendants, Plaintiffs’ subject evidentiary objections to the declaration of Christina Salvato.

Plaintiffs Jonathan Davis and Kimberly Davis move this court for an Order compelling Defendants Crown Media Holdings, Inc. and Hallmark Cards, Inc. to provide further responses to certain interrogatories contained in Plaintiffs’ First Set of Special Interrogatories (SROG Nos. 64-66). Plaintiffs also request that this court award Plaintiffs an award of $13,880.00 against Defendants Crown Media Holdings, Inc. and Hallmark Cards, Inc. as sanctions for having to file this motion.

 

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿(CCP § 2030.300(a).)¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿(CCP § 2030.300(b).) In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ (CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).)

¿ 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.”¿ (CCP § 2030.300(c).)¿“[T]he time limitations set forth in section 2030 and section 2034, subdivision (a) [are] mandatory and [when] not met, the trial court's order is in excess of its jurisdiction.”¿¿(Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)

 

Special Interrogatories Nos. 64-66 seek information about Defendants’ managing agents, officers, and/or directors. Plaintiffs contend Defendants’ objections were predicated on the lack of a stipulated protective order in the case at that time; however, the parties entered a protective order on October 18, 2023 and yet Plaintiffs claim Defendants continue to impose increasingly arbitrary and restrictive conditions on the Plaintiffs in exchange for the discovery which they previously promised to provide and to which the Plaintiffs are plainly entitled.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ interrogatories are overbroad and not reasonably related to a legitimate discovery need. Defendants contend Plaintiffs seek this information for the purpose of maintaining their baseless allegations against two parent companies that were not involved in the subject matter of this dispute and for the purpose of harassing Hallmark-entity executives who had no role in any of the conduct alleged in Plaintiffs’ complaint. Hallmark Defendants further contend they never agreed to provide responses pursuant to a protective order for SROG No. 66. Moreover, Plaintiffs are aware these short videos were commissioned by H&F Productions, LLC, a production company that has since been dissolved, and were distributed by Crown Media United States, LLC, the media company that operates the Hallmark Channel and is now known as Hallmark Media United States, LLC. Plaintiffs, nevertheless, refuse to substitute Hallmark Media for the incorrectly named Hallmark Defendants.

 

The court finds discovery into the officers, directors, and managing agents reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Hallmark Defendants cite several cases regarding whether there was shown ultimate control and ratification. However, at this stage, it is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. However, the court agrees SROGs Nos. 64-66 should be limited to the relevant time period of the alleged facts of the complaint as well as scope to the relevant facts alleged in the complaint. Specifically, the SROGs should be limited to the identity of “all … officers, directors, and managing agents involved in ‘At Home With Our Family’.”  As for Hallmark Defendants’ contention that Plaintiffs failed to name the proper defendant, the court finds at this stage, discovery is allowed into ‘managing agent’  of defendants, including H&F Productions.. If Hallmark Defendants’ response ends up being ‘no one’ then that is their response. Lastly, regarding Hallmark Defendants’ lack of good faith claim by Plaintiffs, both parties must act in good faith when working together.

 

Sanctions

 

Where the court grants a motion to compel further responses, sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification, or the sanction would otherwise be unjust.  (CCP § 2030.300(d).)   

 

The court, finding there being substantial justification on both sides, denies the request for sanctions.

 


Case Number: 23VECP00396    Hearing Date: January 4, 2024    Dept: W

JODEE MAREE GREEN v. SAFA INVESTMENTS LLC

 

defendant’s demurrer to the first amended petition/complaint  

 

Date of Hearing:        January 4, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECP00396

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Safa Investments LLC

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Jodee Maree Green  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 28, 2023, Plaintiff Jodee Maree Green filed a First Amended Petition/Complaint. Plaintiff seeks an order perfecting Plaintiff’s title to certain property located on Saticoy Street. Plaintiff also asserts claims for fraud, unjust enrichment, and intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Safa Investments LLC’s Demurrer to the First Amended Petition/Contract is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Safa Investments LLC demurs to the First Amended Complaint/Petition of Plaintiff on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to state any valid grounds for a writ of mandate and fails to state any valid cause of action against Defendant.

 

Writ of Mandate

 

Defendant Safa first demurs to the petition/complaint on the grounds there simply are no allegations that would support the issuance of a writ of mandate, or the basis of the Court’s jurisdiction to issue such an order. Plaintiff seeks a writ of mandate under which Defendant would be ordered to perfect Plaintiff’s title in the Unit.

 

The court agrees. “’”A traditional writ of mandate under [Code of Civil Procedure] section 1085 is a method of compelling the performance of a legal, usually ministerial duty[.]”’ [Citations.]” (Pomona Police Officers' Assn. v. City of Pomona (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 578, 583–584.) A respondent may test the legal sufficiency of a petition for writ of mandate by demurrer. (Santa Paula Animal Rescue Center, Inc. v. County of Los Angeles (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 630, 639.)

 

The ‘petition’ is void of any allegations that Defendant had a ministerial duty to perform. From what the court can gleam of the amended petition/complaint, Plaintiff alleges she paid rent for the current unit she is residing in located at 15045 Saticoy Street, Van Nuys, California and therefore, she is a bona fide purchaser of the unit.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Although unlabeled in the First Amened Petition/Complaint, the first cause of action looks to be one for breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges Defendant refuses to perfect title to Plaintiff’s portion of real property even though she is a bona fide purchaser and Defendant gave reassurances that the title would be perfected.

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) “A written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) “In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must allege the substance of its relevant terms.” (Id.) 

 

The petition/complaint fails to allege whether this understanding was written or oral and moreover, what the terms of the agreement were.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Plaintiff is given one more opportunity to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for breach of contract.

 

Fraud

 

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.)

 

Defendant argues the FAC fails to allege any specific false representations made by Defendant upon which she seeks relief, instead offering only conclusory allegations such as Defendant “perpetrated a fraud by making promises that it never intended to keep” which caused Plaintiff “to look forward with her hopes up”. (FAC p. 8.) Moreover, as to an entity Defendant, a plaintiff must allege the names of those who made the misrepresentations; the authority to speak for the entity; to whom they spoke; what they said or wrote; and when it was said or written.

 

The court agrees. In California, fraud must be pled with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)  There are zero allegations of when this alleged fraud occurred, who on behalf of corporate defendant made such allegations, and by what means was the misrepresentation communicated to Plaintiff.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Plaintiff is given one more opportunity to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for fraud.

 

Unjust Enrichment

 

“The elements of an unjust enrichment claim are the “receipt of a benefit and [the] unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.”  (Peterson v. Cellco Partnership (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1593.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s unjust enrichment claim appears to be based on her contention that she owns the dwelling underneath her Unit and Defendant has enriched itself by collecting rent from a third party who resides in the dwelling underneath Plaintiff’s Unit. This assertion, however, presupposes that Plaintiff is entitled to ownership of the Unit which she cannot establish based on the allegations in the Complaint.

 

The court agrees Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged Defendants retention of the claimed profits at Plaintiff’s expense. Plaintiff’s unjust enrichment claim relies on the alleged fact that she is the bona fide purchaser of Unit 242 “from the ground to the sky” which “will include 142 by definition.” (FAC p. 10.) Taking these allegations as true, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged she is the bona fide purchaser of Unit 242 or that the agreement included Unit 142.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Plaintiff is given one more opportunity to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for unjust enrichment.  

 

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) intention to cause or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) severe emotional suffering; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress. (See Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780; Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009.) To satisfy the element of extreme and outrageous conduct, defendant’s conduct “‘must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’” (Moncada, supra, 221 Cal.App.4th at 780 (quoting Trerice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883).)  

 

Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action revolve around Defendant’s alleged treatment of Plaintiff’s 87-year-old mother thereby causing Plaintiff’s mother to move out. Shortly thereafter, Plaintiff’s mother went missing. Plaintiff’s allegations include the property manager physically abusing Plaintiff’s mother, destroying Plaintiff’s personal property, and loss of income due to their direct manipulations.

 

These allegations do not support a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. The complaint does not allege what severe emotional distress Plaintiff has suffered or that the alleged conduct was intentional or done with reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Plaintiff is given one more opportunity to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.
