DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV28088    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

May 20

September 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV28088

MOTION: 

Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant LA-Fig Parking Lot Lessee, LLC

OPPOSING PARTIES:

Plaintiffs Mohill Holdings, LP and MO Foundation Holdings, LLC

 

MOTIONS

            Plaintiffs Mohill Holdings, LP and MO Foundation Holdings, LLC (“Plaintiffs”) dismissed their claims against Defendant LA-Fig Parking Lot Lessee, LLC (“Defendant”).  Now, Defendant seeks attorneys’ fees.  Plaintiff opposes the motion. 

ANALYSIS

The parties disagree as to whether this case is a contract action or a tort action.  In a contract action, “[w]here an action has been voluntarily dismissed . . . , there shall be no prevailing party . . . .”  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (b)(2).)  Plaintiffs’ complaint in this action is for unlawful detainer.  Plaintiffs voluntarily dismissed their complaint on February 21, 2024.  As such, Plaintiffs argue Defendant is not the prevailing party, and is not entitled to attorneys’ fees.

However, Plaintiffs’ claims do not constitute an action on the contract.  Pursuant to Drybread v. Chipain Chiropractic Corp. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 1063 (Drybread), “the unlawful detainer statute encompasses breach of lease (arguably contract-type matters) and holdover possession after expiration of the lease (arguably a noncontract issue).”  (Id., at p. 1074.)  In the instant case,  Plaintiffs claim Defendant failed to surrender the premises after Plaintiffs served Defendant with a notice to quit.  The form complaint specifies Plaintiff requested possession of the premises, reasonable attorney fees, forfeiture of the lease, and damages (Complaint, filed November 13, 2023, ¶ 19), and the 30-day notice to quit attached to the complaint does not identify any breach of the lease, for example, such as breach of the lease for failure to pay past-due rent (Id., Exhibit 2). 

Plaintiffs’ claims are based on Defendant’s holdover possession and are, therefore, noncontractual.  As such, Plaintiffs’ claims “did not sound in contract,” and the Court determines Defendant is a prevailing party under Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (a)(4).  (Drybread, supra, 151 Cal.App.4th at p.  1077.)

The prevailing party must seek an award of attorney fees by a noticed motion and bears the burden of proof to justify the amount sought.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5)(A).) 

Defendant seeks an attorney fee award of $50,000.  At first, it had not, however, presented any billing records to support this amount.  The Court determines a request for attorney fees based on a “careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney involved.”  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 626, fn. 6, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  Without such evidence, the Court cannot grant Defendant’s request for attorney fees. 

Defendant cited Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, which is not on point.  In that case, the Court of Appeal stated, “An attorney's testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records.”  (Id., at p. 293.)  In this case, Defendant has not provided a declaration that includes information concerning the number of hours Defendant’s attorneys worked or the nature of the work Defendant’s attorneys performed.  Instead, in connection with its moving papers, Defendant submitted a declaration of its attorney, Navi Signh Dhillon (“Dhillon”) in which Dhillon states, “I reviewed the billing records for this matter and I am confident that the number of hours billed are commensurate with the tasks performed.”  (Declaration of Navi Singh Dhillon, ¶ 7.)  Dillon did not state the number of hours he worked on this case or the number of hours other attorneys and/or individuals supporting the attorneys worked on this case.  Dillon also did not describe the nature and extent of the work Defendant’s attorneys performed. 

Defendant then filed a supplemental declaration of Dhillon in which Dhillon provided detailed billing records.  (Supplemental Declaration of Navi Singh Dhillon, Exhibit K.)  The Court continued the matter to this date to provide Plaintiff with the opportunity to respond to that submission.  In the supplemental opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant’s attorneys billed excessive hours at excessive hourly rates.  However, although Defendant has provided billing records that reflect $270,335 in fees incurred, Defendant requests an award of $50,000 in attorney fees.  The Court concludes this reduction adequately addresses any issues concerning an  excessive amount of attorney fees.  The Court finds Plaintiff’s suggested reductions in Defendant’s requested fees unduly restrictive and concludes $50,000 is a reasonable fee award in light of “the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, and the skill required and success of the attorney's efforts, his or her learning, age and experience in the particular type of work demanded, the intricacies and importance of the litigation, the labor and necessity for skilled legal training and ability in trying the cause, and the time consumed.”  (Contractors Labor Pool, Inc. v. Westway Contractors, Inc. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 152, 168.)  As such, the Court awards Defendant’s motion for attorneys’ fees in the amount sought of $60,000, which includes $10,000 incurred with respect to litigating this motion for attorney fees.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 24STCV08775    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV08775

MOTIONS

Special Motion to Strike

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Mark Skvarna, Capitol Advisors Group, LLC, Norma Copado, Alvarez-Glasman & Colvin, Jeff Marderosian, and Elizabeth Cabrera

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Personnel Commission of the Montebello Unified School District and Law Office of Willoughby & Associates, Inc.

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Personnel Commission of the Montebello Unified School District (“Commission”) and Law Office of Willoughby & Associates, Inc. (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Mark Skvarna (“Skvarna”), Capitol Advisors Group, LLC (“Capitol Advisors”), Norma Copado (“Copado”), Alvarez-Glasman & Colvin, Jeff Marderosian (“Marderosian”), and Elizabeth Cabrera (“Cabrera,” collectively, “Defendants”)   

ANALYSIS

In ruling on a defendant's special motion to strike, the trial court uses a two-step process. First, the defendant must show the act or acts of which the plaintiff complains were taken in furtherance of the defendant's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitutions in connection with a public issue. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(1).)  If the defendant carries that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(3).)

Plaintiffs allege general counsel for the Montebello Unified School District (“MUSD”) resigned in a Board of Education (“Board”) meeting on February 5, 2021 (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 42); the Board of Education appointed Copado as its legal counsel in a closed session at that meeting, without conducting interviews or opening the position for public applications, thereby preventing the Commission from reviewing the contract between MUSD and Copado (id., ¶¶ 43-44); on February 15, 2021, the Board appointed Alvarez-Glasman & Colvin as general counsel for MUSD without presenting the contract to the Commission for review (id., ¶ 45-46); on March 30, 2021, the Board appointed Skvarna as interim superintendent of MUSD, even though Skvarna did not have a valid teaching credential or valid administrative credential as required (id., ¶¶ 47-48);  Copado performed the background investigation of Skvarna, knew Skvarna lacked the appropriate qualifications, and misrepresented Skvarna’s qualifications to the Board (id., ¶¶ 51-54); the Board hired Capitol Advisors as an independent contractor without presenting the contract to the Commission for review (id., ¶¶ 55-56); the Commission sought to retain independent legal counsel, but Marderosian told the Commission MUSD would refuse to pay for independent legal counsel, and Cabrera also indicated MUSD would refuse to pay for independent legal counsel for the Commission (id., ¶¶ 57-61); and Copado and Marderosian sought to force the Commission to use their services as legal counsel (id., ¶ 65.)   

Defendants move to strike Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations.  In support of this claim, Plaintiffs allege the Commission declared a conflict of interest between the Commission and MUSD and hired outside counsel (Complaint, ¶¶ 67-70); Plaintiffs gave notice to MUSD of the agreement by which the Commission hired outside counsel and requested ratification of the contract be placed on the agenda for the Board’s public meeting on December 7, 2022 (id., ¶¶ 73-75); Skvarna, Copado, and Marderosian refused to place the contract on the agenda, thereby precluding the Board from ratifying the agreement (id., ¶¶ 76-77); the Commission has requested approval of the invoices on the contract for legal services be placed on the agenda for Board meetings, but Skvarna, Copado, Marderosian, and Cabrera have refused (id., ¶¶ 79-82); and because approval of the invoices has not been on the agenda for the Board meetings, MUSD has not paid those invoices (id., ¶¶ 83-85.)   

Defendants have the burden of making a prima facie showing that a cause of action arises from an act in furtherance of their constitutional rights of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue.  (Equilon Enterprises, LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.)  Defendants argue the allegations detailed above fall within Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (e)(1), which protects statements and writings made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, “or any other official proceeding authorized by law.”  “Under the plain terms of [section 425.16] it is the context or setting itself that makes the issue a public issue: all that matters is that the First Amendment activity take place in an official proceeding or be made in connection with an issue being reviewed by an official proceeding.”  (Briggs v. Eden Council for Hope & Opportunity (1999) 19 Cal. 4th 1106, 1116.) 

In the instant case, given Plaintiffs’ allegations, among other things, Defendants failed to bring approval of the invoices before the Board meetings, which are official proceedings authorized by law.  (See Olaes v. Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1501, 1506 [section 425.16 is to be construed broadly as it “codifies the Legislature’s desire to encourage continued participation in matters of public significance, a participation that should not be chilled through abuse of the judicial process”].)  As such, Plaintiffs’ claim falls within the scope of the special motion to strike. 

However, “where a defendant brings a motion to strike under section 425.16 based on a claim that the plaintiff's action arises from activity by the defendant in furtherance of the defendant's exercise of protected speech or petition rights, but either the defendant concedes, or the evidence conclusively establishes, that the assertedly protected speech or petition activity was illegal as a matter of law, the defendant is precluded from using the anti-SLAPP statute to strike the plaintiff's action.”  (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 320.)  In the instant case, Plaintiffs allege Defendants’ actions violated Education Code section 45313, subdivision (c), which provides the legal counsel for the governing board of a school district must represent the district’s personnel commission unless there is a conflict of interest between the commission and the governing board, in which case the commission may employ its own counsel.  If a conflict of interest existed between the Commission and the Board, then MUSD was required to pay for counsel for the Commission, and the refusal of Defendants to seek approval of these payments was unlawful.  

While Defendants argue, “there is no identifiable conflict of interest on any particular legal matter that has been identified by the [Commission]” (Special Motion to Strike, p. 20),  Defendants fail to advance any evidence or argument to show their interpretation of what constitutes a conflict of interest is correct.  Defendants submitted, for example, Marderosian’s declaration in which he states the Commission “had not identified an actual conflict of interest that would justify the employment of its own legal counsel.”  (Declaration of Jeff C. Marderosian, ¶ 7; see also Declaration of Mark Skvarna, ¶ 6.)  This bare conclusion lacks evidentiary value.  Pursuant to Education Code section 45313, a conflict of interest exists between the district’s personnel commission and the governing board if the Commission finds a conflict by a majority vote of its members.  (Educ. Code, § 45313, subd. (b)(2).)  Defendants do not dispute a majority of the Commissions’ members found a conflict.  (See, e.g., Exhibit List in Support of Special Motion to Strike, Exhibit 16.) 

While Defendants apparently contend the Commission was required to identify a specific legal matter as to which a conflict of interest exists between the Commission and the Board, that is contrary to the language of Education Code section 45313.  That statute states the personnel commission need only declare “that a conflict exists between the interests of the commission and the interests of the governing board . . . .”  (Educ. Code, § 45313, subd. (b)(2).)   “When the language is clear and unambiguous, there is no need for construction.”  (People v. Woodhead (1987) 43 Cal.3d 1002, 1007-1008.)  As such, the evidence before the Court shows Defendants were required to pay for outside counsel for the Commission, and their refusal to do so violated Education Code section 45313.  Defendants are, therefore, not entitled to invoke the protections of the special motion to strike, and their motion in that regard is denied.

Defendants are to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
