Case Number: 23STLC01199    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: 25

Case No.:                                23STLC01199

(2) Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One

(3) Request for Sanctions

Moving Party:                         Defendant Marton Price

Responding Party:                   Plaintiff Kim Youngblood

Notice:                                    OK

 

Tentative Ruling:                    (1) Defendant Marton Price’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Request for Identification and Production of Documents, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

                                                (2) Defendant Marton Price’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified responses within 30 days of notice of this Court’s order.

 

                                                (3) Defendant Marton Price’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED in the Reduced Total Amount of $750.

 

Sanctions are to be paid by Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel Howard J. Choi, Esq. of Law Offices of Howard J. Choi, to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from an multi-vehicle car accident that occurred on February 16, 2021. Plaintiff Kim Youngblood (“Plaintiff”) alleges that Defendant Marton Price (“Defendant”) was the negligent cause of the accident, and that Defendant’s negligence Plaintiff’s injuries that resulted from the crash.

 

On February 16, 2023, Plaintiff Kim Youngblood (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Marton Price (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 20, inclusive, alleging causes of action for (1) negligence, and (2) negligent entrustment.

 

On May 20, 2024, Defendant propounded discovery requests on Plaintiff, including Request for Identification and Production of Documents, Set One (“Request for Production”) and Form Interrogatories, Set One (“Form Interrogatories”). According to the written discovery requests, Plaintiff’s verified responses were due on or before July 26, 2024 based on a stipulated extension from the original June 21, 2024 deadline.

 

On August 21, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motions to compel Plaintiff’s verified responses to the Request for Production and Form Interrogatories.

 

On September 25, 2024, this Court heard argument on Defendant’s motions. Counsel for Plaintiff represented that he had produced responses to Defendant’s requested discovery (without verifications). This Court continued the hearing on Defendant’s motions to October 23, 2024, to allow Plaintiff to provide the appropriate verification to the discovery responses provided to Defendant. Plaintiff was ordered to electronically file with this Court a Declaration with the appropriate verified responses five (5) days prior to the hearing date, on October 18, 2024.

 

As of October 22, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel has not filed any supplemental declarations or the verified responses.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Defendant claims that this Court has the authority to compel Plaintiff’s verified responses to these discovery requests because they are basic requests directly relating to Plaintiff’s claims and contentions. Defendant claims that the at-issue requests were properly served, the time to respond has expired, and no responses were served. Defendant further claims that monetary sanctions are warranted, and mandatory in this case, given that Plaintiff does not have substantial justification for failing to provide verified responses.

 

OPPOSITION

 

            In Opposition, Plaintiff’s Counsel submitted a declaration stating that the verified responses were delayed because of a substitution of attorney that occurred seventeen (17) days before the discovery response were due. Plaintiff’ Counsel’s declaration stated that he expected to file responses before the original September 25, 2024, hearing date. Plaintiff’s Counsel also contends that monetary sanctions are not warranted because the motions were apparently harvested from a different case, indicating that they were prepared by a non-attorney, and because the two different motions requested monetary sanctions against different Plaintiffs.

 

REPLY

 

            In Reply, Defendant argues the Opposition admits that Plaintiff had not submitted verified responses and that no extensions were timely requested. Even still, Defendant granted Plaintiff a thirty (30) day extension to provide responses without objections. Finally, Defendant contends that, since trial is scheduled for December 13, 2024, Plaintiff’s responses to the initial discovery requests are necessary, and timeliness is essential.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Legal Standard

Similarly, a party must respond to interrogatories and requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260(a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260(a).) If a party to whom interrogatories or requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(c).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories or production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020(a), 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

II.        Motions to Compel Verified Responses

 

            Defendant seeks to compel Plaintiff’s verified responses to its Request for Production and Form Interrogatories, originally due June 21, 2024.

 

On September 25, 2024, this Court continued the hearing on Defendant’s motions to compel Plaintiff’s verified responses to Defendant’s Request for Production and Form Interrogatories until October 23, 2024. (Minute Order, 9/25/24.) Plaintiff represented that it had served unverified responses to Defendant’s requested discovery on September 24, 2024, and requested to continue the hearing to allow appropriate time to provide verifications. (Id.) This Court granted the Plaintiff’s request to continue the hearing and ordered Plaintiff to file a supplemental declaration, with the verified responses attached, by October 18, 2024.

 

Here, as of October 22, 2024, no supplemental declaration or verified responses have been filed. Plaintiff’s unverified responses are legally insufficient to preclude the trial court’s authority to compel responses to Defendant’s outstanding discovery requests. (See Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 481, 489 (responses to interrogatories were legally invalid because they were unverified).)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Request for Identification and Production of Documents, Set One, is GRANTED. And Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

III.       Request for Sanctions

 

            Defendant seeks the imposition of monetary sanctions against Plaintiff for failing to provide verified responses.

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, subdivision (c) provides that “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey and order compelling answers, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 …. In lieu of or in addition to that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 ….” (See also Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010(d)).

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s failure to provide verified responses to Defendant’s discovery requests constitutes a misuse of the discovery process.

 

Defendant requests sanctions of $2,370.00, based on 10 hours of attorney time billed at $225.00 per hour and two $60.00 filing fees. (Motions, Lawrence Decl., ¶¶ 8; Reply, p. 4:26-5:2.) Defendant cites four (4) hours of attorney time spent drafting the Motions, three (3) hours to review Plaintiff’s Opposition and draft the Reply, and three (3) hours anticipated for preparing, attending, and arguing the hearings on the Motions.

 

The Court finds that the anticipated hours spent for attending the hearings on the Motions is reasonable given the unopposed continuance. However, the Court finds that seven (7) hours of attorney time for drafting the Motions, reviewing Plaintiff’s Opposition, is excessive given the simplicity of these Motions and their largely duplicative language.

 

The Courts grants sanctions in the reduced total amount of $750.

 

IV.       Conclusion

           

            In all, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Request for Identification and Production of Documents, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Verified Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified responses within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $750.  Sanctions are to be paid by Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel Howard J. Choi, Esq. of Law Offices of Howard J. Choi, to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of notice of this order.

ANALYSIS

 

I.          PETITION TO VACATE ARBITRATION AWARD

A.    Legal Standard

            “Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award. The petition shall name as respondents all parties to the arbitration and may name as respondents any other persons bound by the arbitration award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.)

 

            “A response to [the] petition … may request the court to dismiss the petition or to confirm, correct or vacate the award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.2.)

 

            The “petition … shall: ¶ (a) Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement. ¶ (b) Set forth names of the arbitrators. ¶ (c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4.)

 

            “A petition … vacate an award, or a response requesting such relief, shall set forth the grounds on which the request for such relief is based.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.8.)

            “The scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow. Courts may not review the merits of the controversy, the sufficiency of the evidence supporting the award, or the validity of the arbitrator’s reasoning.” (Dept. of Personnel Admin. v. Cal. Correctional Peace Officers Ass'n (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1200 [emphasis added].) Indeed, “‘“[w]hen parties agree to leave their dispute to an arbitrator, they are presumed to know that his award will be final and conclusive....” [Citation.]’” (Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 10 (“Moncharsh”).)

 

Section 1286.2(a) provides the exclusive grounds upon which a court may review private arbitration awards. (Moncharsh, supra, 3 Cal.4th at pp. 26-28.) The statute authorizes a court to vacate an arbitration award if one of the following is shown:

 

(1)   The award was procured by corruption, fraud or other undue means.

 

(2)   There was corruption in any of the arbitrators.

 

(3)   The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced by misconduct of a neutral arbitrator.

 

(4)   The arbitrators exceeded their powers and the award cannot be corrected without affecting the merits of the decision upon the controversy submitted.

 

(5)   The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced by the refusal of the arbitrators to postpone the hearing upon sufficient cause being shown therefor or by the refusal of the arbitrators to hear evidence material to the controversy or by other conduct of the arbitrators contrary to the provisions of this title.

 

(6)   An arbitrator making the award either: (A) failed to disclose within the time required for disclosure a ground for disqualification of which the arbitrator was then aware; or (B) was subject to disqualification upon grounds specified in Section 1281.91 but failed upon receipt of timely demand to disqualify himself or herself as required by that provision.

 

“The court may not vacate an award unless: ¶ (a) A petition or response requesting that the award be vacated has been duly served and filed … ¶ … and; ¶ (1) All petitioners and respondents are before the court; or (2) All petitioners and respondents have been given reasonable notice that the court will be requested at the hearing to vacate the award or that the court on its own motion has determined to vacate the award and all petitioners and respondents have been given an opportunity to show why the award should not be vacated.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.4.)

 

B.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

On July 15, 2024, Petitioner filed a request for judicial notice of the CSLB Guide. The unopposed request is GRANTED. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (c).)

 

C.    Discussion

 

“The allegations of a petition are deemed to be admitted by a respondent duly served therewith unless a response is duly served and filed.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1290; 1290.6 [“A response shall be served and filed within 10 days after service of the petition except that if the petition is served in the manner provided in paragraph (2) of subdivision (b) of Section 1290.4 [inapplicable here], the response shall be served and filed within 30 days after service of the petition”].)

 

Here, the Court finds the allegations of the Petition are deemed admitted by (1) Dillon Construction because the respondent did not file any response to the Petition despite being served, and (2) CSLB because its Answer is not evidence (the pleading is not supported by any declaration, it is not verified, and does not provide any factual support for its general denial and affirmative defenses). 

 

“‘Every reasonable intendment is indulged to give effect to arbitration proceedings; the burden is on the party attacking the award to affirmatively establish the existence of error by a proper record.’ [Citation.]” (Rivera v. Shivers (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 82, 94.)

 

Here, the Court finds that Petitioner has met its burden of affirmatively establishing the existence of an error in the Award through the unopposed facts in the verified Petition. According to the Petition, Petitioner was never given any notice of Dillon Construction’s claims and never entered or signed any arbitration agreement agreeing to arbitrate those claims. Yet, the Arbitrator not only refused to continue the arbitration hearing to allow Petitioner time to respond to Dillon Construction’s claims but also issued an Award giving Dillon Construction approximately $25,000 for its claims against Petitioner. (See Petition, Exhibit 2 – a copy of the Award, p. 2 [showing that the Arbitrator awarded the following: (a) “Respondent’s claim for amount owed on Lymon project. Remedy considers a credit to Complainant for the deposit paid” - $233.40; (b) “Respondent’s claim for amount owed on Flint-Gogek project” – $2,545; (c) “Respondent’s claim for amount owed on McCardle project” – $7,200; and (d) “Respondent’s claim for amount owed on Henderson project” – $15,720].)

 

The Court finds the undisputed facts in the verified Petition warrant vacating the Award under Section 1286.2(a)(3) and (5).

 

For those reasons, the Petition is granted.

 

II.        Conclusion

           

            The Petition to Vacate Arbitration Award is GRANTED.

I.          Demurrer

A.                Meet and Confer Requirement

Prior to filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (CCP §§ 430.41 and 435.5.)

            On June 21, 2024, Defendants’ counsel sent Plaintiff a detailed letter outlining the issues with the initial Complaint and requesting that Plaintiff’s counsel contact him to discuss these issues in greater detail. (Harney Decl., ¶ 2.) This same day, Plaintiff’s counsel met and conferred with Defendants’ counsel and on July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a FAC. On August 21, 2024, Defendants’ counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel another letter outlining the issues with the FAC and requesting Plaintiff’s counsel contact him to discuss. (Harney Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. B.) However, Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond, the parties were unable to come to an agreement and Moving Defendants moved forward with the instant demurrer. (Harney Decl., ¶ 5-6.) The court finds Defendants’ good faith meet-and-confer efforts is sufficient to satisfy this procedural requirement.

B.        Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747. When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994; Weil & Brown, Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2011) ¶ 7:8. “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed (430.30, 430.70). The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” Hahn 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.  A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense.  Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50. The same standard applies to demurrer made toward an answer. A demurrer to an answer may be taken to the whole answer or to any one or more of the several defenses set up in the answer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§430.50(b).) The demurrer to the answer may object to failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., §§430.20.)

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Moving Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for failure to state specific facts to sustain a claim for breach of contract.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) A plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 401–02 (Miles) (quoting Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

 

In general, members of a limited liability company (LLC) are not liable for the debts, obligations, or other liabilities of the limited liability company. (Cal. Corp. Code § 17703.04(a); CB Richard Ellis, Inc. v. Terra Nostra Consultants (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 405, 411.) Under both California and Delaware law, the alter ego doctrine extends to limited liability companies (LLCs), and generally requires the proponent to demonstrate two elements: (1) a unity of interest and ownership such that the separate personalities of the corporation and the individual do not exist; and (2) an inequitable result if the corporate identity is not disregarded. (JPV I L.P. v. Koetting (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 172, 189.)

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, the court finds Plaintiff has insufficiently alleged a claim for breach of contract. First, similar to Miles, where the plaintiff sufficiently alleged an express contract to refinance his loan by describing the loan balance due from defendant, interest rate and monthly payment, here, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges express agreements and purchase orders for certain fabric goods, including the respective purchase order numbers, invoice numbers, goods descriptions, payment amounts and payment due dates.

 

However, Plaintiff does not allege which entity or entities constituted the counterparty-buyer for these contracts, omitting an essential term. The FAC alleges that Defendant Tony Ocampo used the corporate entities of Defendants E.J. Lauren, LLC and E.J. Lauren of Texas, LLC for his personal business, commingled personal and corporate funds, and directed payments and transferred assets of defendant-corporations, sufficient to show alter ego. (JPV  88 Cal.App.5th  at 189.) Without factual allegations to support that the contracts at issue involved the Moving Defendants, Plaintiff has failed to show the existence of a contract with them. (FAC ¶ 12.)

 

Moreover, Plaintiff alleges Defendants have “failed to many any payment” toward the sums due to Plaintiff and have received no credits for the specified goods, indicating breach; however, without pleading which entities entered into the sales contract Plaintiff has not pleaded specific facts as to which party is in breach. Overall, without alleging the existence of a contract with respect to Moving Defendants, Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently plead a breach of contract claim.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the First Cause of Action.

 

Second Cause of Action – Open Book Account

 

Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff insufficiently pleaded its Open Book Account claim.

 

To establish a cause of action for open book account, the following elements must be satisfied: (1) plaintiff and defendant had financial transactions, (2) plaintiff kept an account of the debits and credits involved in the transactions, (3) defendant owes plaintiff money on the account, and (4) the amount of money that the defendant owes the plaintiff.¿ (State Comp. Ins. Fund v. ReadyLink Healthcare, Inc. (2020) 50 Cal. App. 5th 422, 449.)¿ A book account is “open” if a balance remains due on this account.¿ (Interstate Group Administrators, Inc. v. Cravens, Dargan & Co. (1985) 174 Cal.App.3d 700, 708; Eloquence Corporation v. Home Consignment Center (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 655, 664–65 (Eloquence).) “An express contract, which defines the duties and liabilities of the parties, whether it be oral or written, is not, as a rule, an open account.” (Eloquence, supra, at p. 665 (quoting Durkin v. Durkin (1955) 133 Cal.App.2d 238, 290.) An exception “may lie where the parties had agreed to treat money due under an express contract as items under an open book account.” (Ibid.) An express agreement is not necessary. (Id. at p. 666.) But, “mere[ly] recording in a book of transactions or the incidental keeping of accounts under an express contract does not itself create a book account.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that the amounts Defendants owes it are pursuant to various written agreements, making these liabilities subject to an express contract that, as a rule, is not considered an open account (Eloquence, supra, at 665.) Further, Plaintiff makes no allegation that the parties agreed to treat the various written agreements as an open book account. Plaintiff alleges Defendants created an account with Plaintiff but provides no factual allegations as to when, how and with which entity or entities the account was opened. (FAC ¶ 10.) Accordingly, no exception to the rule applies here, and the express contracts govern the commercial transaction. Without any verbatim language or a copy of the contracts showing Moving Defendants entered into the financial transactions at issue with Plaintiff, Plaintiff has similarly failed to establish it had any financial transaction with Moving Defendants. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently plead its claim for an open book account.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Cause of Action.

 

Third Cause of Action – Account Stated

 

Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff insufficiently pleaded its Account Stated claim.

 

“The essential elements of an account stated are: (1) previous transactions between the parties establishing the relationship of debtor and creditor; (2) an agreement between the parties, express or implied, on the amount due from the debtor to the creditor; [and] (3) a promise by the debtor, express or implied, to pay the amount due.” (Zinn v. Fred R. Bright Co. (1969) 271 Cal.App.2d 597, 600.) “An express contract, which defines the duties and liabilities of the parties, whether it be oral or written, is not, as a rule, an open account.” (Eloquence, supra, 49 Cal.App.5th at 665 (quoting Durkin v. Durkin (1955) 133 Cal.App.2d 238, 290.) An exception “may lie where the parties had agreed to treat money due under an express contract as items under an open book account.” (Ibid.) An express agreement is not necessary. (Id. at p. 666.) But, “mere[ly] recording in a book of transactions or the incidental keeping of accounts under an express contract does not itself create a book account.” (Ibid.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently plead an account stated for the same reasons as the second cause of action.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the Third Cause of Action.

 

Fourth Cause of Action – Goods Sold and Delivered – Quantum Valebant

 

Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff insufficiently pleaded its action for Goods Sold and Delivered – Quantum Valebant

 

“Where goods are purchased on a credit, with the fraudulent intent never to pay for them, the seller, on discovering the fraud, may treat the credit, or whole special contract, as void, and maintain an action immediately, on an implied assumpsit, for the value of goods sold and delivered.” However, a creditor cannot bring an action for goods sold and delivered before the term of credit, expressly agreed to, has expired. (Patrick v. Montader (1859), 13 Cal. 434, 438.)

 

Here, Plaintiff insufficiently pleads its claim of Quantum Valebant. First, Plaintiff alleges that it had an express contract with Defendants, the express terms of which would thus govern the transaction. (Ibid.) Plaintiff makes no factual allegation as to the term of credit with Defendants, and so it cannot maintain an action for quantum valebant. (Ibid.) Further, because Plaintiff failed to allege details about when and where the goods in dispute were sold, and which Defendant took possession, it fails to establish a claim against Moving Defendants specifically. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently plead its claim for Goods Sold and Delivered.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action.

 

Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action – Voidable/Fraudulent Transfers

 

Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff insufficiently pleaded its actions To Set Aside Voidable/Fraudulent Transfers.

 

In California, a fraudulent conveyance under the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (“UVTA”) involves “a transfer by the debtor of property to a third person undertaken with the intent to prevent a creditor from reaching that interest to satisfy its claim.” (Filip v. Bucurenciu (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 825, 829.) A transfer under the UVTA is fraudulent as to a creditor if the debtor made the transfer with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor. (See Civ. Code, § 3439.04(a); Filip, supra, 129 Cal.App.4th at 829.)¿  

 

To find a transfer voidable as to a creditor, the transfer by the debtor must be made with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud the creditor or the debtor. (See Id., § 3439.04(a).) Some factors that may be considered in determining actual intent are: (1) whether the transfer or obligation was to an insider; (2) whether the debtor retained possession or control of the property transferred after the transfer; (3) whether the transfer or obligation was disclosed or concealed; (4) whether before the transfer was made or obligation was incurred, the debtor had been sued or threatened with suit; (5) whether the transfer was of substantially all the debtor’s assets; (6) whether the debtor absconded; (7) whether the debtor removed or concealed assets; (8) whether the value of the consideration received by the debtor was reasonably equivalent to the value of the asset transferred or the amount of the obligation incurred; (9) whether the debtor was insolvent or became insolvent shortly after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred; (10) whether the transfer occurred shortly before or shortly after a substantial debt was incurred; and (11) whether the debtor transferred the essential assets of the business to a lienor that transferred the assets to an insider of the debtor. (Id., § 3439.04(b).) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

Here, the same facts discussed under alter ego liability analysis apply. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Tony Ocampo made transfers of corporate assets for “non-business purposes for the use and/or benefit, either directly or indirectly, of Tony and/or his friends and family.” (FAC ¶ 8.) These allegations are conclusory and not factual, and so Plaintiff fails to satisfy the pleading standard set forth in Lazar. For example, Plaintiff makes no factual allegations as to which property Defendant Tony Ocampo transferred or any scienter or knowledge he had of wrongdoing. Thus, Plaintiff fails to plead sufficient facts showing fraudulent transfer.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action.

 

Seventh Cause of Action – Temporary Restraining Order

 

Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff insufficiently pleaded its action for injunctive relief because this serves as a remedy and not a cause of action.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for injunctive relief are (1) a tort or other wrongful act constituting a cause of action; and (2) irreparable injury, i.e., a factual showing that the wrongful act constitutes an actual or threatened injury to property or personal rights which cannot be compensated by an ordinary damage award.” (Brownfield v. Daniel Freeman Marina Hospital (1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 405, 410, citation omitted.) Notably, “injunctive relief is a remedy and not, in itself, a cause of action, and a cause of action must exist before injunctive relief can be granted.” (Camp v. Board of Supervisors (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 334, 356.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged a proper cause of action for which the remedy of injunction relief may be granted. (Ibid.) Further, Plaintiff seeks damages and makes no allegation that such a remedy would be insufficient warranting injunctive relief by the court. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently plead its request for injunctive relief.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the Seventh Cause of Action.

 

II.        Motion to Strike

 

            Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole or any part thereof – i.e., even single words or phrases. (Code. Civ. Proc. § 435(b)(1)); Warren v. Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe Ry. Co. (1971) 19 Cal App.3d 24, 40. A motion to strike lies either: (1) to strike any “irrelevant, false or improper matter inserted in any pleading”; and/or (2) to strike any pleading or part thereof “not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule or order of court.” (Code. Civ. Proc. § 436); Ferraro v. Camarlinghi (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 528; As with demurrers, the grounds for a motion to strike must appear on the face of the pleading, or from any matter which the court may judicially notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

 

Moving Defendants seek to strike Paragraph 8, 9, 11, 21, 33 and 38 of the FAC and Plaintiff’s Prayer for Relief as to attorneys’ fees in the First and Second Causes of Action and punitive damages as to the Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action. Defendant argues the statement in Paragraphs 8, 9, 11 and 21 are irrelevant to any of Plaintiff’s claims and that Paragraphs 33 and 38 fail to state facts sufficient to show the requisite oppression, fraud or malice for punitive damages. Plaintiff argues, in opposition, that its allegations in Paragraphs 8, 9, 11 and 21 are relevant to the alter-ego relationships between Defendants underlying its claims and that it sufficiently alleges conduct that amounts to oppression, fraud, and malice warranting punitive damages in Paragraphs 33 and 38.

 

In light of the ruling on the demurrer, the motion to strike is MOOT.

 

III.       Conclusion

           

            In all, Plaintiff’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED in its entirety with leave to amend. Plaintiff is ordered to file a Second Amended Complaint within 30 days.

 

            The Motion to Strike is DENIED as MOOT.

 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Any party may obtain discovery by inspecting, copying, testing, or sampling documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically stored information in the possession, custody, of control of any other party to the action.  (CCP § 2031.010, subd. (a).)  A party may demand that any other party produce and permit the party making the demand, or someone acting on the demanding party’s behalf, to inspect and to photograph, test, or sample any tangible things that are in the possession, custody, or control of the party on whom the demand is made.  (CCP § 2031.010, subd. (c).)  A party may demand that any other party allow the party making the demand, or someone acting on the demanding party’s behalf, to enter onto any land or other property that is in the possession, custody, or control of the party on whom the demand is made, and to inspect and to measure, survey, photograph, test, or sample the land or other property, or any designated object or operation on it.  (CCP § 2031.010, subd. (d).)  

 

Within 30 days of a demand for inspection, the party to whom the demand is directed shall serve the original of the response to it on the party making the demand and a copy on all other parties who have appeared in the action.¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).)¿The party to whom a demand for inspection has been directed shall respond separately to each item or category of item with: (1) a statement that the party will comply with the particular demand for inspection; (2) a representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand for inspection; or (3) an objection to the particular demand for inspection.¿ (Id., § 2031.210, subd. (a).)¿ 

 

 

 

III.              Discussion

Two days later, on September 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served the Motion to Compel responses to Demands for Inspection, Request for Sanctions.

 

IV.              Monetary Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d)).

 

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to inspection against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §§2030.300(d), 2031.320(b).)

 

Plaintiff requests $1,000.00 in sanctions against Defendant for bringing the motion to compel responses to Demands for Inspection. Because Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses to Demands for Inspection is moot and given that Defendant had timely served its responses, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in relation to the instant motion.

 

In opposition papers, Defendant also requests sanctions against Plaintiff for improperly bringing the motion to compel responses to Demands for Inspection.  Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.040 requires the sanction request to be in the “notice of motion.” The Court finds that proper notice of the sanction request is not provided to Plaintiff since the opposition papers do not contain all the information required in the noticed motion. 

 

 

 

Therefore, the Court DENIES Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Ally Bank’s Responses to Demands for Inspection is MOOT.

 

Moreover, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions.

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s Request for Monetary anctions against Plaintiff. 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
