DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV01803    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: W

Osvaldo Martignoni, et al., v. Arash Majlessi, et al.,

 

Motion for leave to file amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        May 1, 2024                           Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        19VECV01803

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni

Responding Party:     Defendant Bank of Hope 

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a contractual fraud action. On December 19, 2019 Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action. Plaintiff filed a Fourth Amended Complaint on February 9, 2022 against Defendants Arash Majlessi, Majlessi Corporation, Gloria Center LLC, Kamran Hekmat, United Escrow Co., John C. Lee, and Ian Bhak for (1) rescission, (2) fraud, (3) breach of contract, (4) rescission, (5) breach of duty and gross negligence, (6) breach of duty and gross negligence, (7) fraud, and (8) declaratory relief.  

 

On November 18, 2022, the court sustained, without leave to amend, Defendants Kamran Hekmat and Gloria Center LLC’s (“Hekmat Defendants”) demurrer to the fourth and seventh causes of action and dismissed the Hekmat Defendants with prejudice.

 

On December 7, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their operative Fifth Amended Complaint against Arash Majlessi, Majlessi Corporation, United Escrow Co. Harry Hwang, John C. Lee and Ian Bhak.

 

On March 9, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Arash Majlessi, Majlessi Corporation, Harry Hwang, and Ian Bhak. On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Gloria Center, LLC. Plaintiff named Bank of Hope as Doe 1.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni’s Motion for Leave to file Amended Complaint is CONTINUED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni move the court for an order allowing Plaintiffs to file an amended complaint to add a cause of action for negligence against Bank of Hope.

 

Motions for leave to amend the pleadings are directed to the sound discretion of the court. “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading . . . .” (CCP § 473(a)(1); see CCP § 576.) Policy favors liberally granting leave to amend so that all disputed matters between the parties may be resolved. (See Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) Absent prejudice to the adverse party, the court may permit amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial.” (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761 [internal quotes omitted].) Where leave is sought to add entirely new claims, the court may grant leave to amend if the new claims are based on the same general set of facts, and the amendment will not prejudice the opposing party. (Austin v. Massachusetts Bonding & Ins. Co. (1961) 56 Cal.2d 596, 600-602; Glaser v. Meyers (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 770, 777 [holding trial court did not abuse its discretion in permitting amendment of complaint, which originally alleged constructive eviction, to allege retaliatory eviction where the new claim was based on the same general set of facts].)

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See CRC, rule 3.1324(a), (b).)

 

Plaintiff seeks to add a new cause of action for negligence against Defendant Bank of Hope as the claim is based on the same general facts that they have alleged against other defendants. Plaintiff contends that “United Escrow Co., and Harry Hwang now contend that Bank of Hope was negligent in the manner in which it investigated the profitability of the Restaurant before loaning money to the Plaintiffs and so do the Plaintiffs contend.” (Mot. at p. 4:10-13.)

 

In opposition, Bank of Hope argues the motion is procedurally and substantively defective. The court agrees. As noted above, Plaintiff must comply with California Rules of Court, 3.1324. Not only has no declaration been submitted to this court, but there is also no copy of the proposed amendment. As noted by Bank of Hope, this failure to comply with Rule 3.1324 prejudices them as they cannot determine whether Plaintiff is alleging a special relationship beyond the scope of the borrower-lender relationship.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file an amended complaint is CONTINUED for  plaintiff to comply with the rules of court.    


Case Number: 24VECV00580    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: W

BVK COURTYARD COMMONS, LLC vs CALABASAS MEDICAL CENTER, INC.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR, ALTERNATIVELY, FOR PARTIAL ADJUDICATION OF THE ISSUE OF POSSESSION

 

Date of Hearing:        May 1, 2024                                                   Trial Date:       None set. 

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        24VECV00580

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff BVK Courtyard Commons, LLC

Responding Party:     Defendant Calabasas Medical Center, Inc.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an unlawful detainer action. On April 16, 2020, Plaintiff conveyed to JANA, LLC a leasehold interest in the Premises for a term of approximately ten years. On March 10, 2022, JANA assigned the Lease to Tenant. Plaintiff alleges Tenant breached the Lease by failing to pay Rent and on November 21, 2023, Plaintiff notified Tenant in writing that it had defaulted in its payment of Rent. Despite the written notice, Plaintiff alleges Tenant failed to bring its Rent current and on January 24, 2024, Plaintiff served Tenant with a statutory Five Day Notice to Pay Rent or Quit.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, Alternatively, for Partial Adjudication of the Issue of Possession is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of Plaintiff’s verified Complaint (Exh. A) and Defendant’s Answer (Exh. B). The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

Defendant also requests this court take judicial notice of the Resolution of the Board of Supervisors of the County of Los Angeles Further Amending and Restating the County of Los Angeles COVID-19 Tenant Protections Resolution from January 24, 2023 (Exh. 6) and the Revised Guidelines to Aid in the Implementation of the Los Angeles County Eviction Moratorium During the COVID-19 Pandemic-Date Revised June 10, 2022 (Exh. 7). The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff BVK Courtyard Commons, LLC requests this court to enter summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds there are no triable issues of fact with respect to the only issue before the court – the right to immediate possession of the premises; and Defendant actually denies it is the successor tenant under the subject lease.

 

Defendant Calabasas Medical Center, Inc. filed their own motion for summary judgment, on the grounds that Plaintiff’s Complaint as a whole has no merits as a matter of law. Specifically, Defendant does not owe anywhere near the amounts alleged.  Plaintiff points out Defendant is a suspended Corporation and therefore its papers should not be considered (Reply, Exh. A). However, Defendant filed a supplemental declaration saying this has been corrected. (Izad Supp. Decl.)

 

“A motion for summary judgment must be decided on admissible evidence in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.”  (Guthrey v. State of California (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1119-1120.) “It is generally understood ... that a party cannot rely on the allegations of his own pleadings, even if verified, to make or supplement the evidentiary showing required in the summary judgment context. [Citations.]”  (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 720, fn. 7.) In an unlawful detainer action, notice of a motion for summary judgment must be given in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure sections 1013 and 1170.7. (CRC 3.1351(a).) Code of Civil Procedure §¿1170.7 provides that a motion for summary judgment may be made at any time after the answer is filed upon giving five days’ notice.  Summary judgment shall be granted or denied on the same basis as a motion under Section 437c.  (CCP §¿1170.7.) 

 

Plaintiff presents the following evidence: Plaintiff is the owner of the Courtyard Commons retail development in Calabasas, California, including the subject premises (“Premises”). (UMF 1.) On April 16, 2020, Plaintiff conveyed to JANA, LLC a leasehold interest in the Premises for a term of approximately ten years. (UMF 2.) Plaintiff delivered possession of the Premises to JANA and otherwise performed under the Lease. (UMF 2.) On March 10, 2022, JANA assigned the Lease to defendant Tenant. (UMF 2.) Tenant assumed all of the obligations of the tenancy under an “Assignment and First Amendment of Lease.” (UMF 2.) Tenant agreed to pay rent and other charges, (all included as “Rent”) payable monthly. (UMF 2.) At all relevant times, Tenant’s rent was approximately $14,900 per month. (UMF 3.) In November of 2022, Plaintiff and Tenant entered the Second Amendment of Lease. (UMF 4.)  In that agreement, Tenant stipulated that it “owes to Landlord an amount equal to . . . $100,576.00.” (UMF 4.) Tenant promised to pay the November 2022 debt in four installments. (UMF 4.) Tenant defaulted. (UMF 4.)  By November 2023, Tenant’s arrears had grown to $249,899.55. (UMF 5.) That month, Plaintiff served Tenant with a Notice of Default and requested payment. (UMF 6.) Tenant admits it received the notice but did not pay default. (UMF 6.) On January 24, 2024, Plaintiff served Tenant with a statutory Five-Day Notice to Pay Rent or Quit (“Pay/Quit Notice”). (UMF 7.) Tenant admits it received the Pay/Quit Notice. (UMF 7.) Tenant did not pay the amount demanded or any part of it nor did Tenant quit the Premises. (UMF 8.) Tenant denies under penalty of perjury that it is the successor tenant under the Lease. (UMF 9.) The fair rental value of the Premises is at least $496 per day. (UMF 10.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff has met its burden to show entitlement to judgment pursuant to CCP §1161 section 2. A tenant of real property is guilty of unlawful detainer “(1) the tenant is in possession of the premises; (2) that possession is without permission; (3) the tenant is in default for nonpayment of rent; (4) the tenant has been properly served with a written three-day notice; and (5) the default continues after the three-day notice period has elapsed.”  (Kruger v. Reyes (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th Supp. 10, 16; CCP §1161(2).) In the instant matter, Plaintiff has demonstrated that Defendants are in possession of the premises after defaulting on payment of the lease.

 

In opposition, Defendant first argues Plaintiff’s Notice to Pay or Quit, although estimated, is far from being a reasonable estimate. (CCP §116.1.) Specifically, the Notice itself is ambiguous as to which period the rent is being demanded for, how the amount demanded is calculated and what charges are included in such calculations. Section 1161.1 provides, among other things, a mechanism by which a landlord can demand an estimated amount of past-due rent, and the tenant can pay that amount and avoid forfeiture pending the court’s determination of the actual amount due. Defendant contends it is apparent that the amount demanded includes, amongst other things, base rent, CAM charges, unauthorized TAX reconciliation charges, improper late fees, which under the terms of the Article 4 of the Lease should not even be included as a rent or additional rent. Defendant further contends the running Ledger includes CAM and TAX reconciliation (page 4 of the Ledger) for 2022 and late fees that were charged even for the months when Defendant’s rent was paid in full and in time. Moreover, Defendant is still owed $57,697 in TIA, which was never credited to the Defendant in violation of 2nd Amendment of Lease. (Izad Decl. ¶9.)

 

The court finds notice proper. The Notice complies with 1161.1 as the amount stated is clearly identified as an estimate and reasonably estimated. First, Plaintiff provides how they came to $128,024.35 as demanded in the Notice. (Separate Statement, ¶5.) Defendant points to how this includes unauthorized TAX reconciliation charges, CAM charges, and improper late fees. However, the lease agreement provides late charges, CAM, and taxes are additional rent. (Exhibit List, Exh. A, ¶¶2.5 and 4.1.) Plaintiff also points out that the $57,697 credit Defendant claims did not come due during the One-Year Period. Thus, the credit simply reduces the balance existing at the time the One-Year Period commenced. Further, the fact that more money may have been owed prior to the One-Year Period does not negate the estimate set forth in the notice, provided the notice does not seek payment for that period (which it does not) and that the amount sought is no more than the actual rent due.  Defendant has not created a triable issue of fact that the notice is deficient or overstated. 

As for defendant’s argument that the notice was defective or that it is entitled to possession because of the Covid-related eviction moratoria, plaintiff correctly notes that as of September 2021, state law preempts any local moratoria relating to evictions of commercial tenants.   The notice period here (January 2023 through January 2024) is well after the expiration of moratoria as they relate to commercial properties. 

 

Lastly, Defendant argues the judgment should be issued for the Defendant because Plaintiff accepted rent from Defendant for a period after expiration of the notice to pay or quit and, thus, invalidated the notice and reinstated Defendant’s tenancy.  But acceptance of partial payments does not preclude a landlord from bringing an action to recover possession.  

Moreover, based on the Ledger, the three payments were applied as Defendant requested. Defendant has not provided authority for how acceptance of partial payment voids the Notice. (See CCP 1161.1(b).)

 

Based on the foregoing, the court grants Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment.


Case Number: 24VECV01222    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: W

LUXURY LEASE PARTNERS, AS SERVICER FOR LUXURY LEASE COMPANY v. TAX LEADERS OF AMERICA, LLC, et al.

 

plaintiff’s application for writ of possession

 

Date of Hearing:        May 1, 2024                                       Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV01222

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Luxury Lease Partners, as Servicer for Luxury Lease Company, a Delaware Statutory Trust

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 19, 2023, Plaintiff Luxury Lease Partners, as Servicer for Luxury Lease Company, a Delaware Statutory Trust filed a complaint against Tax Leaders of America, LLC and Mario Sanchez for 1. Recovery and Possession of Personal Property (Claim and Delivery); 2. Breach of Contract; 3. Fraud; 4. Conversion; and 5. Declaratory Relief. Plaintiff entered into a lease agreement with Defendant Sanchez whereby Sanchez leased a used 2012 Ferrari 458 Coupe on behalf of Tax Leaders of America, LLC. Plaintiff alleges since the date of lease of the Ferrari, Luxury Lease has received the monthly payments due up through January 2024; however, Sanchez and Tax Leaders’ February 2024 and March 2024 payments bounced and Luxury Lease has not received the agreed upon monthly payments since.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Application for Writ of Possession is CONTINUED

 

DISCUSSION

 

Before the hearing on the Writ of Possession, the Defendant must be served with (1) a copy of the summons and complaint; (2) a Notice of Application and Hearing; and (3) a copy of the application and any affidavit in support thereof. (CCP §512.030.) 

 

There is no proof of service in the court’s record. Plaintiff must provide proof of service. In the event Plaintiff can show Defendant was properly served the copy of the summons and complaint; notice of the application; and copy of the application, the court rules as the following.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 512.010(b), the application must be submitted under oath and include: 1) Plaintiff’s basis for the claim and entitlement to possession; 2) Defendant’s wrongful detention; 3) description of the property; 4) location of the property and  probable cause to believe that such property is located there; and 5) the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment or fine or seized under an execution against the property. 

 

Plaintiff provides the declaration of Jim Moscatello, an authorized signatory of Plaintiff, to establish the facts relevant to the writ application. The declaration states that the agreement is attached, but it is not.  The court has already continued this hearing one time to allow plaintiff to submit the agreement.   Without the agreement or other sufficient evidence, plaintiff’s application lacks evidentiary support.  The application also lacks evidence of the vehicle’s approximate value.

 

The application for writ of possession is CONTINUED.
