SUBJECT:                 DEMURRER W/ MTS

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants Sotheby’s International Realty, Inc. and Lori Berris

RESP. PARTY:         Plaintiff Kerry E.H. Berger, as trustee of the Kerry Ellen Hersch Family Trust

 

TENTATIVE RULING

            Defendants Sotheby’s International Realty, Inc and Lori Berris’ Demurrer to the 2nd and 3rd causes of action are OVERRULED.  Plaintiff Kerry E.H Berger plead all the essential elements of the 2nd and 3rd causes of action, and met the heightened burden of alleging specific facts for the 3rd cause of action for fraudulent concealment.  

 

Defendants Sotheby’s International Realty, Inc and Lori Berris’ Motion to Strike is DENIED. Plaintiff Kerry E.H. Berger has successfully plead the essential elements of fraud and thus may recover punitive damages pursuant to Cal Civ. Code § 3294(a).

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Plaintiff is only required to allege ultimate facts, not evidentiary facts. (See Committee on Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212 [“the complaint should set forth the ultimate facts constituting the cause of action, not the evidence by which plaintiff proposes to prove those facts”); 1 Cal. Affirmative Def. § 10:2 (2d ed.) [allegations of agency, course and scope of employment, etc. qualify as ultimate facts].) Plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true on demurrer. (See Yvanova v. New Century Mortgage Corp. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 919, 924 [“For purposes of reviewing a demurrer, we accept the truth of material facts properly pleaded in the operative complaint”].)

 

I.                Demurrer to 2nd COA – Breach of Fiduciary Duty —OVERRULED

 

"The elements of a claim for breach of fiduciary duty are (1) the existence of a fiduciary relationship, (2) its breach, and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach." (Mendoza v. Continental Sales Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1405; CACI, 4101.)

 

As a fiduciary, a real estate broker must disclose to the broker’s client all material information that the broker knows or could reasonably obtain regarding the property or relating to the transaction.

 

The facts that a broker must learn, and the advice and counsel required of the broker, depend on the facts of the transaction, the knowledge and experience of the client, the questions asked by the client, the nature of the property, and the terms of sale. Brokers must place themselves in the position of their clients and consider the type of information required for the client to m8ke a well-informed decision.

 

            (CACI, 4107.)

 

            “A fiduciary must tell its principal of all information it possesses that is material to the principal's interests. A fiduciary's failure to share material information with the principal is constructive fraud, a term of art obviating actual fraudulent intent.” (Michel v. Moore & Associates, Inc. (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 756, 762, internal citations omitted.)

 

            Plaintiff Kerry E.H. Berger, as trustee of the Kerry Ellen Hersch Family Trust (“Berger”) successfully plead all the elements of the second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty:

 

(1) Berger pleads that Defendants Sotheby’s International Realty, Inc,  Lori Berris, and DOES 11–20 (“Sotheby’s Defendants”) were the brokers and brokerage firm for both the seller and Berger. (Compl., ¶ 8.)

 

(2) Berger pleads that the Sotheby’s Defendants owe Berger a fiduciary duty of care and loyalty. (Id., ¶ 18.)

 

(3) Berger pleads that the Sotheby’s Defendants breached their fiduciary duty to Berger through “intentionally concealing from Berger the fact that the condominium complex was being inspected for structural damage to its balcony support columns, and that if sufficient damage were found, then expensive repairs would be necessary, a portion of which would be assessed to Berger.” (Id., ¶ 19; see also Id., 9–12.)

(4) Berger pleads that due to the Sotheby’s Defendants breach of fiduciary duty Berger was damaged at an estimated amount of $120,000. (Id., ¶ 20.)

 

II.             Demurrer to 3rd Cause of Action for Fraudulent Concealment - OVERRULED

 

            “The required elements for fraudulent concealment are: (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.”  (Graham v. Bank of America, N.A. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 606.)

 

            Berger successfully plead all the elements of the 3rd cause of action for fraudulent concealment:

 

(1)   Berger pleads that at no point in time did any of the Sotheby’s Defendants inform Berger of the balcony support column issues prior to the close of escrow. (Compl., ¶ 12; see also id., ¶¶ 9–11.)

(2)   Berger pleads that the Sotheby’s Defendants owed Berger a fiduciary duty of care and loyalty and thus “were bound to disclose to Berger the existence of the ongoing inspections of the condominium complex’s balcony support columns.” (Id., ¶ 23.)

(3)   Berger pleads that the Sotheby’s Defendants were aware of the balcony support column structural damage issue and intentionally concealed the information from Berger. (Id., ¶¶ 9–12, 19, 24.)

(4)   Berger pleads that Berger was unaware of the possible structural damage issue, relied on the information provided by the Sotheby’s Defendants, and if Berger knew of the issue Berger would have canceled the escrow. (Id., ¶ 25.)

(5)   Berger pleads that due to alleged intentional concealment of the “existence of the ongoing inspections of the condominium complex’s balcony support columns, Berger has suffered damages in an amount according to proof, presently estimated to be in excess of $120,000.00.” (Id., ¶ 27.)

 

III.           Sotheby’s Defendants Motion to Strike Punitive Damages

 

            “[A] motion to strike is generally used to reach defects in a pleading which are not subject to demurrer. A motion to strike does not lie to attack a complaint for insufficiency of allegations to justify relief; that is a ground for general demurrer.” (Pierson v. Sharp Memorial Hospital, Inc. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 340, 342.) A motion to strike may be directed to all or a portion of a complaint, cross-complaint, answer, or demurrer. (See Code Civ Proc., § 435, subdiv., (a).) On a motion to strike, a judge must read the complaint as a whole, considering all parts in their context, and must assume the truth of all well-pleaded allegations. (See Atwell Island Water Dist. v. Atwell Island Water Dist. (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 624, 628, as modified (Feb. 27, 2020).)

           

            Whether Defendants’ conduct is “despicable conduct” is a question of fact for the jury that a court should not decide in ruling on a motion to strike. (Robinson v. Managed Accounts Receivable Corp., (2009) 654 F. Supp. 2d 1051, 1065.)

 

            A plaintiff has the right to recover punitive damages against a defendant “where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice....” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294(a).) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).)

 

            “In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 836–837.)

 

            “In determining whether a complaint states facts sufficient to sustain punitive damages, the challenged allegations must be read in context with the other facts alleged in the complaint. Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of punitive damages.  (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.) “Malice and oppression may be inferred from the circumstances of a defendant's conduct.” (Id., at p. 511.)

 

            Here, Berger pleads enough specific facts within the complaint to state, and infer, that the Sotheby’s Defendants allegedly committed a fraudulent act by intentionally concealed specific information to Berger’s financial detriment. (Compl., ¶¶ 7, 11, 20, 26.) Thus, the Sotheby’s Motion to Strike Punitive damages from the Complaint is DENIED.

SUBJECT:                 SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFFS’ SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT PERSUANT TO CCP § 425.16 (ANTI-SLAPP)

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants Gerald I. Gillock and Gerald I. Gillock Associates

RESP. PARTY:         Plaintiffs Tensor Law P.C. and Aaron G. Filler

 

TENTATIVE RULING

            Defendants Gerald I. Gillock and Gerald I. Gillock Associates SLAPP Motion is GRANTED in full as to the 10th Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty of Confidentiality, and the 11th Cause of Action for Attempted Extortion.

 

            Defendants Gerald I. Gillock and Gerald I. Gillock Associates Objections to Plaintiffs’ Evidence Opposing Special Motion to Strike are SUSTAINED.

 

            Defendants Kevin V. DeSantis, James A. McFaul, Doug Gillie, and Dunn DeSantis Walt & Kendrick LLPs’ RJN is GRANTED as to the existence of the documents requested. The Court does not take judicial notice of the truth of the facts stated in the requested documents.

 

I.            Anti-SLAPP Analysis

 

            “Litigation of an anti-SLAPP motion involves a two-step process. First, the moving defendant bears the burden of establishing that the challenged allegations or claims arise from protected activity in which the defendant has engaged.  Second, for each claim that does arise from protected activity, the plaintiff must show the claim has “at least ‘minimal merit.  If the plaintiff cannot make this showing, the court will strike the claim.” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1009.)

 

a.     10th Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty of Confidentiality  

 

                                                    i.     1st Step of Analysis – Defendants’ Burden

 

            “[C]ourts are to consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.  The defendant’s burden is to identify what acts each challenged claim rests on and to show how those acts are protected under a statutorily defined category of protected activity.”  (Bonni, supra, 11 Cal.5th at 1009.) 

 

            “The moving defendant must show that the challenged claim or claims arise from the defendant's constitutionally protected free speech or petition rights.  A defendant need only make a prima facie showing at this stage.”  (Ojjeh v. Brown (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 1027, 1035–1036.)  A defendant has the “burden of identifying all allegations of protected activity” on the first prong of SLAPP. (Bel Air Internet, LLC v. Morales (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 924, 936-937.)  Defendant only meets this burden by demonstrating that the act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action “fits one of the categories spelled out in section 425.16, subdivision (e).”  (Sprengel v. Zbylut (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 140, 150.)   

 

            Defendants Gerald I. Gillock and Gerald I. Gillock Associates (“Gillock Defendants”) show Plaintiffs’ Breach of Duty of Confidentiality claim is based on the Gillock Defendants attaching the Arbitration Award to a declaration filed in their successful Anti-SLAPP motion within the separate Iulianelli Superior Court Case No. 23SMCV00356 (the “Underlying Iulianelli Action”). (Motion, p. 10.)  The Gillock Defendants show the filing of the motion in the underlying Iulianelli Action is based on protected activity under CCP §425.16(e)(2) because the “declaration was a written statement made in connection with an issue under consideration by a judicial body.” (Id.).  CCP §425.16(e)(2) protects communications “made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subdiv., (e)(2).)

 

            Plaintiffs Tensor Law P.C. and Aaron G. Filler (“Filler”) argues their claim is based on the Gillock Defendants decision to file the arbitration award, instead of actually filing the award. Filler reliance on Jenni Rivera Enter., LLC v. World Entmt. Holdings, Inc. (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 766, 781 is misplaced.  Jenni Rivera did not involve claims against attorneys, nor does it support the Plaintiff’s alleged position that “a knowing and intentional breach of fiduciary duty by an attorney when it involves a breach of attorney-client privilege and legally required confidentiality for arbitration privilege is unlikely to sustain an Anti-SLAPP action.” (See SAC, ¶ 185.) The Court finds that Filler’s framing of the cause of action as a decision to breach, versus actually breaching the duty, does not plead around the protected litigation activity at issue in this case.

 

            The Gillock Defendants satisfy their burden on the first step for the Anti-SLAPP motion.  The Gillock Defendants establish that Filler’s 10th cause of action for breach of duty of confidentiality arises from protected conduct under CCP §425.16(e)(2).  The burden therefore shifts to Filler to establish the probable validity of Filler’s claim. 

 

                                                  ii.     2nd Step of Analysis ­–Plaintiff’s Burden

 

            Once defendant demonstrates that a cause of action arises from protected conduct, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.  (See Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88-89.)  “Precisely because the statute (1) permits early intervention in lawsuits alleging unmeritorious causes of action that implicate free speech concerns, and (2) limits opportunity to conduct discovery, the plaintiff's burden of establishing a probability of prevailing is not high: We do not weigh credibility, nor do we evaluate the weight of the evidence. Instead, we accept as true all evidence favorable to the plaintiff and assess the defendant's evidence only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff's submission as a matter of law.  Only a cause of action that lacks ‘even minimal merit' constitutes SLAPP.”  (Overstock.com, Inc. v. Gradient Analytics, Inc. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 688, 699.)

 

            “The second prong of the statute deals with whether the plaintiff has “demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim.  Under section 425.16, subdivision (b)(2), the superior court, in making these determinations, considers ‘the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.’ For purposes of an anti-SLAPP motion, the court considers the pleadings and evidence submitted by both sides, but does not weigh credibility or compare the weight of the evidence. Rather, the court's responsibility is to accept as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff.  A plaintiff need only establish that his or her claim has minimal merit to avoid being stricken as a SLAPP.  With these descriptions in mind, we will not strike a cause of action under the anti-SLAPP statute unless it lacks even minimal merit.”  (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 261.)

 

            The “probability of prevailing” is tested by the same standard governing a motion for summary judgment, nonsuit, or directed verdict.  Thus, in opposing a SLAPP motion, it is plaintiff's burden to make a prima facie showing of facts that would support a judgment in plaintiff's favor.  (Crossroads Investors, L.P. v. Federal National Mortgage Assn. (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 757, 785; Taus v. Loftus (2007) 40 Cal.4th 683, 714 [a “summary-judgment-like procedure”].)     

 

            Here, Filler makes no prima facie showing of facts that would support a judgment in Filler’s favor the breach of fiduciary duty.  Filler’s apparent argument that the attached arbitration award in the Underlying Iulianelli Action did not have binding effect and therefore could not be admissible, or used for any valid litigation purpose, does not defeat the broadly interpretated litigation privilege. (See Klem v. Access Ins. Co. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 595, 613 [The litigation privilege “is broadly applied[,] and doubts are resolved in its favor.”]; Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 322 [The litigation privilege is an “‘absolute privilege,’ and it bars all tort causes of action except a claim of malicious prosecution.”]; Feldman v. 1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1490 [“[I]t is now well established that the litigation privilege applies without regard to motives, morals, ethics or intent. The litigation privilege is simply a test of connectedness or logical relationship to litigation. [Citation.]”].)

 

            More important, Filler provides no authority to show the Gillock Defendants owe Filler a fiduciary duty other than citing to the LACBA Fee arbitration rules which do not create the legal standard that legal adversaries owe each other fiduciary duties. Filler cites LACBA Rule 44(d) which does not concern confidentiality issues and does not create a fiduciary duty to an adversarial party. (See SAC, ¶ 171, (LACBA Rule 44(d).) “The key factor in the existence of a fiduciary relationship lies in control by a person over the property of another. (Vai v. Bank of America National Trust & Savings Ass'n (1961) 56 Cal.2d 329, 338.) “[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.” (City of Hope Nat’l Med. Ctr. v. Genentech, Inc. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386.) Here, Filler does not make any prima facie showing that the Gillock Defendants had control over the Filler’s property, nor knowingly undertook to act on Fillers’ behalf, thus Filler cannot show a probability of success that the Gillock Defendants ever owed Filler any fiduciary duty.

 

            Because Filler fails to make a satisfactory prima facie showing of a probability of success on the merits he fails to meet the plaintiff’s burden for the second step of the Anti-SLAPP analysis. The Court GRANTS the Gillock Defendants Special Motion To Strike Filler’s 10th Cause of Action for Breach of the Fiduciary Duty of Confidentiality Pursuant To CCP § 425.16 (Anti-SLAPP).

 

 

b.     11th COA for Attempted Extortion

 

                                                    i.     1st Step of Analysis – Defendants’ Burden

 

            Here, Defendants identify their protected behavior as communications the Gillock Defendants allegedly made to Filler in relation to the Zuckerman v. Regents matter, the Zuckerman fee arbitration, and the Plaintiff’s possible challenge to the Arbitration award. (Motion, p. 14:24–26; Gillock Decl., ¶¶ 9-14.) The Gillock defendants show these text communications arise out of “written or oral statement[s] made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law,” and thus arises from protected activity. (C.C.P., § 425.16(e)(2), (b)(1).; see also Bergstein v. Stroock & Stroock & Lavan LLP (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 793, 811 [granting Anti-SLAPP on because all conduct considered “litigation activities”]; Optional Capital, Inc. v. Akin Gump Strauss, Hauer & Feld LLP (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 95, 113-115 [attorneys’ alleged assisting with misappropriation of settlement funds protected activity because it arose out of representation of client in the underlying litigation that led to settlement]

 

            Filler’s reliance on Flatley v. Mauro (the “Flatley Exception”), to argue the Gillock Defendants extortion threats were illegal and thus cannot be a subject of an Anti-SLAPP filing, is misplaced.  (See Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 316 [“[W]here either the defendant concedes the illegality of its conduct or the illegality is conclusively shown by the evidence, the [Anti-SLAPP] motion must be denied.”].) Here, the Gillock Defendants have not conceded to the illegality of the alleged extortion conduct, nor can the communications at issue conclusively constitute criminal extortion as a matter of law. Furthermore, the text communications at issue do not contain express threats to report or file a complaint against Filler to the State Bar of California, nor to have any action initiated against Filler whatsoever. (Gillock Decl., ¶ 15; see also Malin v. Singer (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 1283, 1298 [Flatley exception did not apply where defendant’s “demand letter did not expressly threaten to disclose [plaintiff’s] alleged wrongdoing to a prosecuting agency or the public at large.”].) The Court finds this communication more similar to the protected communications in Malin. (See Malin, supra, 217 Cal.App.4th at p. 1298.) Since the Gillock Defendants have not conceded to the illegal conduct, nor is the conduct illegal by conclusive evidence, nor did the text message expressly threaten to disclose Filler’s alleged wrongdoings to a specific agency or the general public, the conduct does not fall within the Flatley illegality exception.

 

            The Gillock Defendants satisfy their burden on the first step for the Anti-SLAPP motion.  The Gillock Defendants establish that Filler’s 11th cause of action for attempted extortion arises from protected conduct under CCP §425.16(e)(2).  The burden therefore shifts to Plaintiff to establish the probable validity of Plaintiff’s claim. 

 

                                                  ii.     2nd Step of Analysis ­–Plaintiff’s Burden

 

            The Gillock Defendants argue that Filler cannot meet their burden of establishing a probability of success on their Attempted Extortion claim because “1) the litigation privilege precludes this claim, 2) there is no civil cause of action for attempted extortion, and 3) the Gillock Defendants did not engage in any extortionate conduct.” (Reply, p. 8:13–15.)

 

            Regarding the litigation privilege, the privilege “applies to any communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that have some connection or logical relation to the action.” (Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205, 212.) Here, the Gillock Defendants correctl argue that “[f]irst any text messages that Gillock sent Filler concerning Zuckerman v. Regents or the Fee Arbitration were communications ‘made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings.’ Second, Gillock was a participant in both Zuckerman v. Regents and the Zuckerman Fee Arbitration. And the third and fourth elements are met because any text messages between Gillock and Filler relating to Zuckerman v. Regents, the Zuckerman Fee Arbitration, and/or the Arbitration Award concerned settlement negotiations and attempts to resolve the fee dispute. (Motion, p. 15:16–25; see Home Ins. Co. v. Zurich Ins. Co. (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 17, 24 [“[t]he statement meets the third and fourth criteria as it was made to induce settlement”]; see also Mancini & Associates v. Schwetz (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 656, 661 [“the [litigation] privilege also applies to statements made during settlement negotiations”].) The Court agrees with the Gillock Defendants and finds that all the elements of the privilege are met, mainly that the text communications at issue were in furtherance of a settlement.

 

            Thus, the Court finds that Filler cannot meet their burden of establishing a probability of success on their Attempted Extortion because that the litigation privilege under CCP § 425.16(e)(2) effectively bars Filler’s claim. The Court GRANTS the Gillock Defendants Special Motion To Strike Filler 11th Cause of Action for Attempted Extortion Pursuant To CCP § 425.16 (Anti-SLAPP).
