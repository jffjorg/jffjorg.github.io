DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 24STCP02232    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

SO CAL DEVELOPMENT GROUP LLC,

                        Petitioner,

            v.

CALIFORNIA REALTY & INVESTMENT GROUP INC.,

                        Respondent.

 

 Case No.:          24STCP02232

 Hearing Date:   October 11, 2024

 Trial Date:        n/a

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion for Entry of Judgment for Attorney Fees and Costs

 

I. Background

    Petitioner So Cal Development Group filed a Petition for Release of Mechanic’s Lien against Respondent California Realty & Investment Group Inc. on July 15, 2024. The Court granted the Petition on August 21, 2024. (Minute Order, 08/21/24.) Notice of Entry of Judgment or Order was filed on August 21, 2024.

     On August 21, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant motion for Entry of Judgment for Attorney Fees and Costs.

     No opposition has been filed.

 

II. Motion

A.    Legal Standard

The Court’s objective is to award attorney fees at the fair market value based on the particular action.  (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.) “‘[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the 'lodestar,' i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate . . . .’” (Ketchum v. Moses¿(2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1134.) “The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”¿¿(PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)

Regarding the number of hours expended, the lodestar method looks to factors relevant to the particular case: “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.”  (Ketchum, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 1132.) “The ‘‘experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in his court, and while his judgment is of course subject to review, it will not be disturbed unless the appellate court is convinced that it is clearly wrong.’’” (Ibid.)

  1. Analysis 

Petitioner moves for an award of attorney’s fees and costs in the total amount of $7,567.27 pursuant to Civil Code section 8488. As the prevailing party on the Petition to Release Mechanic’s Lien, Petitioner is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney’s fees. (Civ. Code, § 8488, subd. (c).)  

Petitioner’s counsel has been practicing law for 20 years and bills at a rate of $500.00 per hour. (Weisberg Decl., ¶¶ 4,7.) The declaration adequately supports the requested rate based on counsel’s experience and years of practice.  

Petitioner requests attorney’s fees and costs in the total amount of $7,567.27 based upon counsel’s rate of $500/hour for: (1) 12.9 hours of work on the Petition to Release Mechanic’s Lien and (2) 1.0 hour for hearing on the motion for attorney’s fees, and (3) $617.27 in costs. (Weisberg Decl., ¶¶ 4-6, Ex. 1; Memorandum of Costs).

Details regarding this work demonstrate that counsel did not duplicate any efforts or spend an excessive amount of time on any task. (Weisberg Decl., Ex. 1.)

Thus, Petitioner’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs in the total amount of $7,567.27 is GRANTED.

III. Conclusion

So Cal Development Group’s Motion for Entry of Judgment for Attorney Fees and Costs is GRANTED in the amount of $7,567.27. The Proposed Judgment lodged on August 21, 2024, will be signed.

C. Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact for trial or that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) To meet this burden, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

D. Analysis

The sole remaining claim in the Third Amended Complaint which is asserted against Pacquiao is the ninth claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. “A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress [IIED] exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051].) 

Pacquiao argues that the IIED claim fails because Pacquiao did not, directly or indirectly [the two “severable claims”], engage in outrageous conduct that caused Rueda severe emotional distress.

1.     Direct Liability for IIED

Here, it is undisputed that Pacquiao and Rueda have never met, spoken, or directly communicated. (UMF 1-2.) The parties also do not dispute that Pacquiao did not personally threaten, harass, assault or personally participate in the conduct that caused Rueda’s emotional distress. (UMF 3-8.; Opp. to UMF 3-8)

Thus, Pacquiao has met his initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists as to his direct liability for IIED. Rueda does not argue otherwise. (Opp., p. 19:17-19.)

2.     Vicarious Liability for IIED

a.      Agency

Agency is either actual or ostensible.  (Civ. Code §2298.)  An agency is actual when the agent is employed by the principal.  (Civ. Code §2299.)  An ostensible agency is established when a principal intentionally, or by want of care, causes a third person to believe another is an agent.  (Civ. Code §§ 2300, 2317.) 

 A person is liable for the torts committed by his agent when the acts are committed within the scope of the agency or where the principal directed, authorized, or otherwise ratified the acts of its agents. (Montague v. AMN Healthcare, Inc. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1515, 1520; Desai v. Farmers Insurance Exchange (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1118.)

The essential elements of ostensible agency liability are (1) representations by the principal, (2) justifiable reliance thereon by a third party and (3) change of position or injury resulting from such reliance.  (Hartong v. Partake Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 942, 960.) Before recovery can be had against the principal for the acts of an ostensible agent, the person dealing with an agent must do so with belief in the agent’s authority and this belief must be a reasonable one.  (Ibid.)  Such belief must be generated by some act or neglect by the principal sought to be charged and the¿person relying on the agent's apparent authority must not be guilty of neglect (Ibid.)¿ Ostensible agency cannot be established by the representations or conduct of the purported agent; the statements or acts of the principal must be such as to cause the third party to believe the agency exists. (Ibid.)  

Here, Pacquiao argues first that there is no actual agency relationship because there is no evidence that he authorized or directed anyone to engage in harassing or threatening behavior against Rueda. (UMF 15, 57.) He states that he did not hire Davidson to represent him with respect to Rueda or authorize Davidson to do anything on his behalf. (UMF 23-24, 26.) Pacquiao further asserts that while he has worked with Davidson and Khan in the past, his business relationship with them ended in January 2007. (UMF 20-14.)

Second, Pacquiao argues that there is no ostensible agency relationship because Pacquiao and Rueda have never met or communicated (UMF 1-2.). Accordingly, Pacquiao argues that he could not have caused Rueda to believe that Davidson, or any other party, were employed by Pacquiao. He argues that “[o]stensible authority of an agent cannot be established based solely by the agent’s conduct.” (Reply pp. 7-8, quoting Lindsay-Field v. Friendly (1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 1728, 1734.) But rather, “[l]iability of the principal for the acts of an ostensible agent rests on the doctrine of estoppel, the essential elements of which are representations made by the principal, justifiable reliance by a third party, and a change of position from such reliance resulting in injury.” (Reply p. 8, quoting Preis v. American Indemnity Co. (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 752, 761.)

Thus, Pacquiao has met his initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists as to his vicarious liability for IIED under an agency theory.

In opposition, Rueda argues that Roach, Davidson, and Khan were all actual agents of Pacquiao. He asserts that Roach was Pacquiao’s boxing trainer and manager (AMF 7, 16-20), and that Davidson and Khan had previously acted as Pacquiao’s co-managers and lawyers (AMF 30.) Further, Rueda argues that Pacquiao can be held liable under an ostensible agency relationship theory because Rueda reasonably believed that Roach and Davidson had authority to act as agents on Pacquiao’s behalf because Roach had previously purported to act on Pacquiao’s behalf and Davidson had told Rueda he was a close friend of Pacquiao and Roach. (Opp. p. 16; UMF 13-14; AMF 47.) Lastly, Rueda argues that even if Pacquiao did not authorize the harassing and threatening conduct, Pacquiao can still be held liable if he ratified the conduct. (Opp. p. 18.) Rueda argues that one can conclude that Pacquiao ratified Davidson’s actions because the “tortious acts occurred immediately after defendants authorized Khan and Davidson to act on their behalf.” (Opp. p. 19.)

However, Rueda’s arguments are unavailing. First, Rueda has not cited any evidence that Pacquiao actually employed, directed or authorized anyone to threaten or harass him. The fact that Pacquiao had hired Davidson and Khan in years past, does not establish an actual agency relationship as to the current allegations. Second, while it may not have been entirely unreasonable for Rueda to assume that Roach or Davidson were acting on Pacquiao’s behalf based on their communications to him, Rueda's belief alone is insufficient to establish ostensible authority. “Ostensible agency cannot be established by the representations or conduct of the purported agent; the statements or acts of the principal must be such as to cause the third party to believe the agency exists.” (Hartong, supra, 266 Cal.App.2d at 960.) Rueda does not allege any statements of or acts by Pacquiao that led to his belief that Roach or Davidson were Pacquiao’s agents. Finally, while a principal can be held liable for an agent’s tortious conduct if the principal ratifies the conduct, as discussed above, Rueda has not set forth specific facts that establish that Roach or Davidson were his agents.

Thus, Rueda has not met his burden to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to establishing a cause of action against Pacquiao for IIED based on an agency relationship.

b.     Conspiracy

“[T]he basis of a civil conspiracy is the formation of a group of two or more persons who have agreed to a common plan or design to commit a tortious act. The conspiring defendants must also have actual knowledge that a tort is planned and concur in the tortious scheme with knowledge of its unlawful purpose.” (Kidron v. Movie Acquisition Corp. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1571, 1582) (cleaned up). “Knowledge of the planned tort must be combined with intent to aid in its commission.” (Ibid.)

However, such knowledge and intent “may be inferred from the nature of the acts done, the relation of the parties, the interests of the alleged conspirators, and other circumstances.” (Wyatt v. Union Mortgage Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 773, 785).

Here, Pacquiao argues that Rueda’s conspiracy allegation is based on speculation, without supporting evidence, that Pacquiao knew about, agreed to, or aided in a scheme to terrorize Rueda. Pacquiao asserts that there is no evidence connecting him to Rueda’s meetings with Davidson in June 2015 and that he did not know that Rueda was even asserting a claim for a finder’s fee against him until 2016. (UMF 45; Pacquiao Decl. ¶ 21.)

Thus, Pacquiao has met his initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists as to his vicarious liability for IIED under a conspiracy theory.

In opposition, Rueda argues that proof of a conspiracy can be inferred from circumstantial evidence. Rueda asserts that Pacquiao’s motive to dissuade Rueda from pursuing a finder’s fee and the temporal proximity between Davidson’s threats and the third-party tortious conduct is enough circumstantial evidence to support an inference of conspiracy.

While it is true that proof of a conspiracy may reasonably be inferred from indirect and circumstantial evidence, Rueda has not shown sufficient circumstances to do so here. Pacquiao has an obvious motive to avoid paying Rueda a finder’s fee. Nonetheless, construing the papers in the light most favorable to the non-moving party, as the Court must, Pacquiao’s “role in the chain of causation” is far too attenuated to reasonably infer, as a matter of law, that he was involved in a conspiracy to harass and threaten Rueda. Rueda recites the chain of causation as follows: “After Rueda met with Moonves to discuss his finder’s fee, Moonves determined that Roach and Pacquiao needed to be involved, and roped in Nick Khan (Roach’s agent and lawyer and Pacquiao’s former manager and lawyer) and Davidson (Pacquiao’s prior comanager and lawyer) to act on everybody’s behalf. (Rueda’s UMF 29-32.) Davidson thereafter contacted Rueda, purporting to be a close friend of Pacquaio and Roach, and Pacquiao’s former business manager. (Rueda’s UMF 47.) Moreover, after Davidson threatened Rueda, Rueda was harassed by men he believed to be members of the Wild Card Gym (which Roach owned and where Pacquiao trained) and was attacked by a man who was sitting with Roach, Richie Palmer, and Liam Vaughan (Pacquiao’s sparring partner). (Rueda’s UMF 69, 73.) Indeed, all the tortious conduct that occurred after Davidson’s threats to Rueda can be traced back to defendants collectively, and certainly back to Pacquiao.” (Opp. p. 22.)

Pacquiao’s involvement in the above chain of events is notably lacking. While it is undisputable that Pacquiao knows many of the offending parties, Rueda does not offer even circumstantial facts that Pacquiao knew of the specific conduct or aided in its commission. Rueda compares the circumstances here to the facts of Balistreri v. Turner (1964) 227 Cal.App.2d 236. In Balistreri, the plaintiff alleged that defendants Turner and Burnside, and a third-party, Mercer, conspired to assault and injure him. (Ibid.) The Court of Appeal found that there was sufficient evidence of a relationship between the defendants and Mercer to support an inference that the assault was in furtherance of a conspiracy. (Id. at 242.) There, defendant Turner hired Mercer three months prior to the assault, Mercer admitted that defendant Burnside assaulted the plaintiff at the his suggestion, defendant Burnside visited defendant Turner’s office immediately after the assault, and two days after the assault, defendant Burnside was admitted to a school of which defendant Turner was president. (Ibid.) Thus, even there was no evidence of an actual agreement to conspire, the circumstantial evidence leading up to and directly following the assault, was sufficient to support an inference of a conspiracy. (Ibid.)

The circumstances in the present case are readily distinguishable, in that there is no evidence that Pacquiao had any communication or interaction with the other Defendants, alleged co-conspirators, or Rueda’s assailants in the time preceding or following the alleged tortious conduct. The mere fact that Pacquaio knows Davidson and worked with him in years past, without more, does not support an inference that Pacquaio conspired to assault or harass Rueda.

Thus, Rueda has not met his burden to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to establishing a cause of action against Pacquiao for IIED based on conspiracy.

Accordingly, there is a no genuine issue of material fact regarding Pacquiao’s liability for intentional infliction of emotional distress. 

III. Conclusion

Defendant Emmanuel D. Pacquiao’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Defendant Emmanuel D. Pacquiao’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is denied as MOOT.
