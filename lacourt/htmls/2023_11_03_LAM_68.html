DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV02797    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 68

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

Jesus Agama vs. Young Generation Video, Inc., et al., 21STCV02797

Moving Parties: Defendants Young Generation Video, Inc. and David Skura

Responding Party: Plaintiff Jesus Agama

I.                   Background

Plaintiff Jesus Agama (Plaintiff) was injured at a construction site owned by Defendants David Skura (Skura) and Young Generation Video, Inc (YGV) (collectively “Defendants”). (UMF Nos. 1, 9.) Plaintiff was injured when a piece of metal that he was holding came into contact with a power line. (UMF Nos. 9-10.)

Plaintiff was hired by Defendant Gustavo Espinoza as an independent contractor. (Plaintiff’s Comp., ¶ 3.) Espinoza was hired by Defendant Numa Brothers as a subcontractor to work on the premises. (Plaintiff’s Comp., ¶ 4.) Numa Brothers were hired by YGV for the construction. (Plaintiff’s Comp., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff was not directly hired by YGV. Neither YGV nor Skura retained control of the work performed by Plaintiff. (UMF No. 4.) Numa Brothers were aware of the proximity of the power lines to the roof of the property at the construction site. (UMF No. 5.) Defendants were not asked to remedy the issue of the power line’s proximity to the roof. (UMF No. 6.)

After sustaining his injuries, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants Skura and YGV and other parties involved in the construction. Plaintiff alleged two causes of action in his complaint: one for negligence and one for premises liability. Defendants filed a motion for summary judgment to both of Plaintiff’s causes of action based on the Privette Doctrine, or in the alternative, based on Plaintiff’s alleged contributory negligence. Plaintiff opposes Defendants’ motion.

II.                Evidentiary Objections

a.      Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections

                                                              i.      Sustained: 6, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 15

                                                            ii.      Overruled: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 13, 14

b.      Defendants’ Evidentiary Objections

                                                              i.      Sustained: 1, 2, 3

                                                            ii.      Overruled: None

III.             Analysis

a.      Standard for Summary Judgment and Adjudication

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿ 

Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿ (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ 

When interpreting § 437c, courts have held that a three-step analysis is required: (1) Identify the issues framed by the pleadings since it is these allegations to which the motion must respond by establishing a complete defense or otherwise showing there is no factual basis for relief on any theory reasonably contemplated by the opponent’s pleading; (2) Determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor; and (3) Determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (AARTS Production, Inc. v. Crocker National Bank (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 1061, 1064-1065.)¿¿ 

Summary Judgment may be granted only where all the supporting and opposition papers show there is no triable issue as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment “as a matter of law.” (CCP § 437c(c).)¿¿ 

As a result, the Plaintiff “must present evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not—otherwise, he would not be entitled to judgment as a matter of law but would have to present his evidence to a trier of fact.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 851, fns. omitted; Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 562-563.)¿¿ 

The Defendant need only show the existence of a triable issue of material fact. (Union Bank v. Super Ct. (Demetry) (1995) 31 Cal.4th 573, 590; see Lopez v. SuperCt. (Friedman Bros. Invest. Co.) (1996) 45 Cal.4th 705, 713; Leslie G. v. Perfy & Assocs. (1996) 43 Cal.4th 472, 482.) Summary judgment would not be proper where the facts support a triable issue of fact.¿ 

b.      Issues for Summary Judgment

                                                              i.      Issue No. 1

Defendants move for summary judgment against Plaintiff’s First and Second Causes of Action on the basis that Plaintiff’s claims against Defendants are barred by the Privette Doctrine.

The Privette Doctrine “deals with whether an entity that hires an independent contractor can be liable for on-the-job injuries sustained by the independent contractor’s workers. Under the Privette doctrine, the answer is no, unless an exception applies.” (Miller v. Roseville Lodge No. 1293 (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 825, 828.)

“The Privette doctrine takes its name from Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689 … which held that a person or entity that hires an independent contractor to do work generally is not liable for on-the-job injuries to the independent contractor’s workers.” (Miller, 83 Cal.App.5th at 831–32.)  (Se also, Acosta v. MAS Realty, LLC (Cal. Ct. App., Sept. 20, 2023, No. B316420) 2023 WL 6889820, reh’g denied (Oct. 19, 2023), as modified (Oct. 19, 2023).)

 

“There is a strong presumption under California law that a hirer of an independent contractor delegates to the contractor all responsibility for workplace safety.” (Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021) 12 Cal.5th 29, 37.) “This means that a hirer is typically not liable for injuries sustained by an independent contractor or its workers while on the job.” (Id. at 37-38.)

The Privette Doctrine applies to any injured worker, whether labeled an “employee” or “independent contractor” and “includes ‘the independent contractor personally, the independent contractor’s employees, the independent contractor’s subcontractors personally, the subcontractors’ employees, and so on.’” (Miller, 83 Cal.App.5th at 829, n.1 (quoting Sandoval v. Qualcomm Inc. (2021) 12 Cal.5th 256, 270 & n.2).)

There are two exceptions to the Privette Doctrine: (1) if the hirer retains control over the manner in which the contractor performs the work; and (2) if the hirer is also an owner or possessor of land and knew or should have known of a latent or concealed hazardous condition on the property and failed to warn about the condition. (Miller, 83 Cal.App.5th at 833-834.)

Plaintiff was hired by Defendant Gustavo Espinoza as an independent contractor. (Plaintiff’s Comp., ¶ 3.) Espinoza was hired by Defendant Numa Brothers as a subcontractor to work on the premises. (Plaintiff’s Comp., ¶ 4.) Numa Brothers were hired by YGV for the construction. (Plaintiff’s Comp., ¶ 4.) Despite these allegations being in Plaintiff’s own Complaint, Plaintiff’s opposition disputes this hiring chain as presented by Defendants in their motion. To oppose this, Plaintiff points to a deposition from a person from OSHA named Dimenstein who says that Espinoza told her that Espinoza did not hire Plaintiff. (Plaintiff’s DMF 2; Dimenstein Depo. at 172:5-8.) In Defendants’ reply, Defendants argue that this is a misrepresentation of what Dimenstein said, as she actually did make a determination that Espinoza was Plaintiff’s employer. (Landes Decl., Ex. 9, Dimenstein Depo. At 163:6-10.)

With the hiring chain established, it is clear that the Privette Doctrine would apply to this situation. The contractors would be responsible for safety at the construction site, not Defendants. The next question is whether either of the exceptions to the Privette Doctrine would apply.

For the first exception, the hirer, Defendants, did not retain control over the manner in which the contractor performed the work. (UMF 4.) While Skura testified at deposition that he made sure the project progressed (Plaintiff’s DMF 4), this does not amount to retaining control over the project based on the case law definition. This exception applies if the hirer “retain[s] control over safety conditions at a worksite and negligently exercises that control in a manner that affirmatively contributes to an employee’s injuries.” (Hooker v. Department of Transportation (2002) 27 Cal.4th 198, 213.) Plaintiff has presented no evidence that Defendants exercised control in a way that contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries.

For the second exception, the power line was not a latent or concealed hazardous condition. For this exception to apply, the hazard must be a “concealed hazard on the property that the contractor did not know of and could not have reasonably discovered.” (Kinsman v. Unocal Corp. (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659.) There is no evidence presented by Plaintiff that he did not know of the power line or could not have reasonably discovered it.

Neither exception to the Privette Doctrine would apply in this case. Because the Privette Doctrine is applicable to this action and there are no triable issues of material fact, Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligence and premises liability fail as a matter of law against Defendants YGV and David Skura.

            The Court grants Defendants’ motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s First and Second Causes of Action.

                                                            ii.      Issue No. 2

In the alternative, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s contributory negligence bars recovery even if Defendants were negligent.

Because the Court granted Defendants’ motion for summary judgment based on the Privette Doctrine issue, it is not necessary for the Court to address whether Plaintiff was contributorily negligent. However, in reviewing the case law regarding power lines and Defendants’ evidence supporting this argument, the Court would likely have found that Plaintiff was contributorily negligent if he knew about the power line.

IV.             Order

Defendants Skura and YGV’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED IN ITS ENTIRETY.


Case Number: 23STCV14488    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 68

Lauren Swaim vs. Greystar California, Inc.; 23STCV14488

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Greystar California, Inc.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Lauren Swaim

Demurrer to Complaint with Motion to Strike

I. BACKGROUND

            This case arises out of an alleged incident in which Plaintiff Lauren Swaim (Plaintiff) was attacked by an unknown security guard assailant. The attack happened at 548 Spring Street, and Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Greystar California Inc. (Greystar) is one of several entities that owns the property. (Comp., ¶ 19.) It appears from Plaintiff’s complaint that she lives at the property where she was attacked.

Plaintiff’s complaint attempts to allege 12 causes of action against all named and Doe defendants. The causes of action include assault; battery; intentional infliction of emotion distress; false imprisonment; negligence; tenant harassment; housing discrimination; breach of implied warranty of habitability; breach of implied covenant of quiet enjoyment; violation of LAMC, article 5.3; Violation of Los Angeles County Code, Section 8.52.130; and bad faith retention of security deposit.  Every claim is asserted against every defendant.  There is nothing in the Complaint that provides information related to the actions of any particular defendant.

After stating that Greystar owns the property, Plaintiff’s complaint never mentions Greystar by name again. Instead, Plaintiff just uses the collective “Defendants” throughout her complaint. There are no specific allegations alleged against Greystar.

Greystar demurs to all of Plaintiff’s causes of action on the basis that they are uncertain and fail to allege any specific allegations against Greystar. Plaintiff opposes.

            Greystar has also moved to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint.

II. ANALYSIS

A. The Demurrer

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

1.    All Causes of Action

Defendant Greystar demurs to each of Plaintiff’s causes of action on the basis that they are uncertain and do not contain specific allegations against Greystar, only conclusory allegations of wrongdoing against all defendants.

A complaint is subject to a special demurrer if the pleading is uncertain. (CCP § 430.10(f).) As used in this subdivision, “'uncertain' includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (CCP § 430.10(f).) If a complaint is so uncertain that a defendant “cannot reasonably respond,” the complaint is properly subject to a special demurrer. (Khoury v. Maly's of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) The inability to reasonably respond occurs when a defendant cannot determine what issues to admit or deny, or what claims are being made against him. (Id.; see also, Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139.) Furthermore, the failure to allege material facts essential to a cause of action may also subject a pleading to special demurrer for uncertainty, particularly if the plaintiff’s knowledge of those material facts is superior to that of the defendant. (Corum v. Hartford Accident and Indemnity Co. (1945) 67 Cal.App.2d 891.) In pleading, the essential facts upon which a determination of the controversy depends should be stated with clearness and precision so that nothing it left to surmise; recitals, references to, or allegations of material facts which are left to surmise are subject to special demurrer for uncertainty. (Ankey v. Lockheed Missiles and Space Co. (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 531.)

In this case, because Plaintiff has not pled any specific allegations against Greystar, Greystar is left to surmise which ones apply to it. This makes Plaintiff’s complaint uncertain. Indeed, it is difficult for the Court to understand the allegations in the Complaint as they are simply legal conclusions, with virtually nothing factual supporting the claimed cause of action.  Nothing of substance is communicated.

Plaintiff’s opposition argues that the complaint is sufficient, but then Plaintiff’s opposition points to facts that are not, in fact, evident from the complaint. Plaintiff must plead her complaint with specificity and certainty; otherwise, Greystar cannot determine what to admit or deny.

The lack of facts is glaring.  Facts, not conclusion should be pled to support each cause of action against each of the defendants that Plaintiff alleges is implicated in a particular cause of action.

Defendant Greystar’s demurrer to all of Plaintiff’s causes of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

B. Motion to Strike

Defendant Greystar has moved for the Court to strike the following from Plaintiff’s Complaint:

1. Page 7, Paragraph 33, Lines 3-8, in their entirety.

2. Page 8, Paragraph 38, Lines 1-6, in their entirety.

3. Pages 8-9, Paragraph 45, Lines 27-5, in their entirety.

4. Page 10, Paragraph 50, Lines 1-6, in their entirety.

5. Page 11, Paragraph 57, Lines 7-13, in their entirety.

6. Page 13, Paragraph 69, Lines 14-20, in their entirety.

7. Page 14, Paragraph 77, Lines 17-23, in their entirety.

8. Page 16, Paragraph 85, Lines 3-9, in their entirety.

9. Page 17, Paragraph 90, Lines 6-12, in their entirety.

10. Page 18, Paragraph 95, Lines 5-11, in their entirety.

11. Page 19, Prayer for Relief, Lines 2, 9, and 19, in their entirety.

Defendant Greystar’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

III. ORDER

1.    Defendant Greystar’s demurrer to all of Plaintiff's causes of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

2.    Defendant Greystar’s motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

3.    Plaintiff is given 20 days to amend the complaint.
