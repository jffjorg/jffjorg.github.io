DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01431    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: W

JANE DOE V. LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT, ET AL.

 

plaintiff’s consolidated motion to quash or modify subpoenas issued to various medical provideres by defendant los Angeles unified school district

 

Date of Hearing:        October 23, 2024                  Trial Date:       February 3, 2025

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV01431

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Jane Doe

Responding Party:     Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of allegations by Plaintiff Jane Doe that she was sexually abused by a former employee of Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”), Defendant William Paden, while he was employed by LAUSD. On September 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against LAUSD and Paden for (1) sexual abuse of a minor, (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (3) sexual harassment, (4) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention of an unfit employee, (5) breach of mandatory duty: failure to report suspected child abuse (Government Code section 815.6), (6) negligent failure to warn, train, or educate, (7) negligent supervision of a minor, and (8) negligence.

 

On March 22, 2024, Los Angeles Unified School District filed a cross-complaint against William Paden and Richard Coleman for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief. LAUSD dismissed Richard Coleman from the cross-complaint on June 6, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Consolidated Motion to Quash or Modify Subpoenas Issued to Various Medical Providers by Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Jane Doe moves this court for an order quashing or modifying subpoenas for production of business records served upon various medical providers by Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District. The providers subpoenaed are as follows: (1) Dr. Daniel Buckner - Plaintiffs children's pediatrician; (2) Dr. Steven Yoon - Plaintiffs ophthalmologist; (3) Dr. Jasmine Yun - Plaintiffs dermatologist; (4) Harbor UCLA - Dr. Roy Mansano – Plaintiff’s gynecologist; and (5) Providence Cedars-Sinai Tarzana - Dr. Shelley Susman – Plaintiff’s gynecologist.

Plaintiff moves to quash or modify the subpoenas on the grounds there is no good cause for the discovery sought, the subpoena is overly broad and not relevant to the subject matter of this litigation or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, the subpoena seek documents protected by Plaintiffs constitutional right of privacy, the subpoena is unreasonable and oppressive in its requests, the subpoena was issued for the purpose of harassing Plaintiff, and that any opposition to this motion will lack substantial justification. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $4,460.00 against LAUSD and/or its counsel of record.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1 states that “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.”  (CCP § 1987.1(a).)  “In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”  (CCP § 1987.1(a).)  Subdivision (b) provides that a party may make a motion pursuant to subdivision (a).  (CCP § 1987.1(b).) 

 

As a general rule, all unprivileged information that is relevant to the subject matter of the action is discoverable if it would itself be admissible evidence at trial or if it appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010; Schnabel v. Superior Court. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 711.) When the information sought to be discovered impacts a person’s constitutional right to privacy, limited protections come into play for that person. (Shaffer v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 993, 999.) The protections cover both a person’s personal and financial matters. (Id.) The court must balance competing rights — the right of a litigant to discover relevant facts and the right of an individual to maintain reasonable privacy — in determining whether the information is discoverable. (Id.) 

 

Plaintiff argues the subpoenas for all medical providers violate Plaintiff's right to privacy, as well as her children's right of privacy, are harassing, oppressive, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Plaintiff asserts she is entitled to retain confidentiality of all unrelated medical treatment they may have undergone and evidence relating to Plaintiff's health records for parts of her body other than those parts that Plaintiff has put into controversy in this lawsuit are protected by Plaintiff's "inalienable right of privacy" and the "inalienable right of privacy" of third parties." (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal. 3rd 844, 855-56.)

 

In opposition, LAUSD briefly argues Plaintiff failed to meet and confer prior to filing this motion. It notes while Section 1987.1 does not specifically impose a ‘meet and confer’ requirement, Department W mandates that all parties ‘meet and confer’ before every motion is filed. Plaintiff, in response, argues Plaintiff has always tried to reason with LAUSD regarding the subpoenas but LAUSD was unreasonable during the process. First, the court notes that Department W’s rules encourages parties to meet and confer prior to filing a motion to avoid delay in proceedings or filed solely for tactical reasons. It is not required. Regardless, based on the correspondence, it appears the parties have reached an impasse to this issue.

 

LAUSD argues it issued 7 subpoenas to medical providers identified in Blue Cross records to seek records solely related to Plaintiff as established by Item # 3 in each of the subpoenas. The Blue Cross information did not specify the type of medicine that three of the subpoenaed providers specialized in. The other four subpoenas were for hospital treatment and billing records (not limited to one medical provider). Moreover, none of the subpoenas requested any records regarding Plaintiff’s children or family. Regardless, Plaintiff’s complaint specifies broad physical injuries as a basis for her damages. Specifically, Plaintiff has alleged she has suffered “physical and emotional injuries” in ¿¿ 28, 39 and 47, that she suffered “physical injuries” in ¿ 53, and further alleged that she “has been severely damaged emotionally and physically” in ¿ 61. LAUSD also argues Plaintiff has not presented any evidence that Dr. Buckner is a pediatrician, Dr. Yoon is an opthamologist, Dr. Yun is a dermatologist, or that Dr. Susman and Dr. Mansano are gynecologists.

 

Given that counsel for Plaintiff has established the medical providers’ area of specialty under penalty of perjury and the court has previously established medical records sought should be limited to emotional distress and psychological symptoms, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to quash. LAUSD has not established how such information is relevant and reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

The court notes both parties seek monetary sanctions. Plaintiff seeks $4,460 for having to bring this motion and LAUSD seeks $2,791.25 for Plaintiff’s failure to cooperate and having to oppose this motion.

 

The court DENIES both requests for sanctions.


Case Number: 22VECV02514    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: W

BRYAN GUINO v. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

 

PLAINTIFF’S motion to compel deposition attendance of a person most qualified and custodian of records of defendant american honda motor co, inc.

 

Date of Hearing:        October 23, 2024                              Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02514

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Bryan Guino

Responding Party:     Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Nandivada Decl. ¶11.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action which arises from Plaintiff’s lease of a 2020 Honda Accord. On December 21, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant American Honda Motor Co. asserting causes of action for Violation of Song-Beverly Act and Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment. Plaintiff alleges their vehicle had a sensing defect, which Defendant knew about and concealed.

 

Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint on April 17, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of A Person Most Qualified and Custodian of Records of Defendant American Honda Motor Co, Inc. is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Bryan Guino moves this court, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(a), for an order compelling Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. to produce a Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian of Records to be deposed. Plaintiff also moves this court for an order awarding monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,685.00 against Defendant and its counsel of record, Bowman and Brooke LLP for Defendant’s willful and intentional failure to comply with the relevant code sections of the Discovery Act.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2025.450, if after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action, without having served a valid objection, fails to appear for examination, or proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document. (CCP § 2025.450(a).) A motion to compel the deposition of a party to the action must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, or, when the deponent failed to attend the deposition, a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(2).) 

 

Plaintiff Guino argues in preparation for trial, on June 18, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with a Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s PMQ for August 2, 2024. (Nandivada Decl. ¶4, Exh. A.) Plaintiff also requested Defendant to provide alternative dates by July 9, 2024 in the event Defendant or its counsel were unavailable. (Nandivada Decl. ¶6, Exh. B.) On July 22, 2024, Defendant served a Response to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition. (Nandivada Decl. ¶7, Exh. C.) Defendant stated that it would not produce a witness for the deposition noticed for August 2, 2024, but provided no alternative dates. (Nandivada Decl. ¶7, Exh. C.) The day before deposition, Plaintiff sent Defendant a meet and confer letter addressing the deficiencies in Defendant’s objections to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition. (Nandivada Decl. ¶8, Exh. D.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel attended the deposition and stayed on for 30 minutes. (Nandivada Decl. ¶9.) Defendant did not appear. (Nandivada Decl. ¶9.) Plaintiff’s counsel then sent Defendant an email asking for potential alternate dates for this deposition to go forward. (Nandivada Decl. ¶9.) Defendant did not respond with any alternative dates for the deposition to proceed. (Nandivada Decl. ¶9.) Plaintiff sent subsequent follow up emails on September 4, 2024, and September 16, 2024, seeking dates for the deposition To date, Defendant has not provided a date for the deposition. (Nandivada Decl. ¶10.)

 

In opposition, Defendant argues it is not refusing to provide a PMQ witness in this matter. However, Defendant has an impacted schedule and Plaintiff was aware of this. Defendant asserts Plaintiff’s counsel fails to substantively meet in good faith regarding the deposition of AHM’s PMQ in any of the multiple cases pending between the two firms. Accordingly, Defendant argues the court should not grant the motion or sanctions as requested by Plaintiff. If the court does see fit to order a witness by a certain deadline, Defendant requests the deadline to comply be at least 90 days from the date of any order to allow Defendant to move witness schedules.

 

Since the parties cannot come together on a date for the deposition, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition of Defendant’s PMQ. The parties are ordered to meet and confer regarding availability. The deposition date is to be no earlier than 70 days from the date of this motion.

 

At this time, the request for sanctions is DENIED. The court will revisit the issue of sanctions if the parties do not depose Defendant’s person most qualified by the deadline.  (See CCP § 2025.450.) 


Case Number: 24VECV02166    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: W

KY SHAMLOU vs MERCEDES-BENZ USA, LLC

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to plaintiff’s requests for production of documents, set one

 

Date of Hearing:        October 23, 2024                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV02166

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Sky Shamlou

Responding Party:     Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Serrano Decl. ¶¶17-19.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff Sky Shamlou filed a complaint against Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC for Violation of the Song Beverly Act. Plaintiff leased a 2023 Mercedes-Benz EQE-Class SUV. Plaintiff alleges the vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to, electrical and suspension system defects. 

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request For Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED in Part.

 

request for judicial notice

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of Local Rule 3.4, of the Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles, and Question No. 59 of this Court’s “Civil eFiling Frequently Asked Questions,” pursuant to Sections 452(e) and 453 of the California Evidence Code.

 

The court GRANTS the request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Sky Shamlou moves for an order overruling Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC’s meritless objections and compelling further objection-free responses and production to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 1-4, 7-9, 11, 13, 14, 15-22, 23-29, 30, and 31. Plaintiff makes this motion on the grounds Defendant failed to provide timely and objection-free responses and production to Plaintiff’s RFPs, which seek documents relevant to their claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(a) provides that if, on receiving responses to an inspection demand, a propounding party deems that responses are incomplete, evasive, or inadequate, or that an objection is without merit, that party may move for an order compelling a further response. A motion for further responses to requests for production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought. (CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.  (Digital Music News LLC v. Sup. Ct. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224, overruled on other grounds by Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557, fn. 8.)  However, this burden is not an onerous one: the burden to show good cause for production “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.”  (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)   

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant responded to each of the disputed RFPs using the exact same inappropriate boilerplate objections. For example, Defendant has objected on the grounds of undue burden. However, Defendant failed to identify the types or categories of ESI sources that are not reasonably accessible. Moreover, MBUSA served untimely responses replete with general and specific boilerplate objections, despite having waived all objections.

 

In opposition, Defendant first argues they did not waive their objections because the RFPs were improperly served. Defendant also argues the responses served were code compliant. For example, Defendant included code-compliant language to RFPs Nos. Nos. 1, 2, 4, 9, 13 and 14 as it agreed to produce documents and to RFPs Nos. 3, 7, 15-22 because it has clearly stated after a diligent search, Defendants do not have any relevant and non-privileged documents currently in its possession, custody or control. As for the others, Defendant stands by their objections. Defendant contends RFPs nos.  11 and 23-29 to one extent or another seek to illicit an improper legal conclusion which seeks to impose obligations upon MBUS beyond those required the Discovery Act. Or RFP Nos. 15-22 improperly seek information regarding policies, procedures, training materials and the like regarding many topics of a general nature and not specific to the vehicle at issue in this case. Defendant notes, however, Nos. 23-29 are no longer in dispute because they will provide supplemental responses.

 

First, the court notes Defendant did not waive their objections.  Plaintiff has not presented any evidence or authority establishing Defendant fell within the language of Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, California Rules of Court, rule 2.251, or Local Rules 3.4.

 

However, the court agrees some of the responses are not code compliant. Defendant does state it will produce all relevant and non-privileged documents to RFPs Nos. 1, 2, 4, 9, 13 and 14. However, these responses were served on August 6, 2024. Defendant has not provided why it has yet to produce documents. Moreover, Defendant has not substantiated their objections that the information sought in RFP Nos. 1-4, 7-9, 11, 13, 14 (regarding Plaintiff’s own vehicle) is vague, ambiguous, over broad or not reasonably calculated to lead to discovery of admissible evidence.

 

As for RFPs Nos. 3, 7, 15-22, the court finds the responses not code compliant. Although Defendant states “any relevant and non-privileged” documents are not within Defendant’s possession, custody or control, Defendant fails to include a privilege log. Plaintiff cannot determine if there are privileged documents in Defendant’s possession. Moreover, it does not state if there is any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.

 

As for all of these requests, the court finds the request overbroad to the extent that it goes back to 2020.  The relevant time period is 2023 and 2024.

 

Moreover, although Defendant would have us believe their policies, procedures, training materials and the like differ for each vehicle, they argue it is not limited to the specific vehicle (RFP Nos. 15-22). As such, the court finds the discovery requests should be limited to the vehicle at issue.

 

As for RFP No. 8, the court finds further responses necessary. The objections are meritless and the request seeks relevant information. Regarding RFP Nos. 30 and 31, however, the court agrees the requests are burdensome and/or ambiguous. The phrase “Field Service Action” is not defined. In addition, it is unclear what Plaintiff means by “in response to complaints experienced by Plaintiff”.

 

As noted above, Defendant states it will produce supplemental responses to RFP Nos. 23-29 as follows: “Without waiving any prior made objections, MBUS provides the following supplemental response to [RFP No. 23, 24, 25, 26, 27, 28, 29]: After a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, Responding Party does not have any documents responsive to this request in its possession, custody or control because none ever existed.” This response is sufficient. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request For Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED in Part.

 

Defendant is ordered to serve further, code-compliant responses to Nos. 1-4, 7-9, 11, 13, 14, 15-22. The request to compel further responses to other requests is DENIED.
