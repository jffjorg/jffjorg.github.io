Case Number: 23TRCV02571    Hearing Date: January 15, 2025    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

ANA MARILU LANDAVERDE

RIVERA, an individual,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

23TRCV02571

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted in Part/denied in part

 

AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC., a California Corporation, and SONIC-HARBOR CITY H, INC, a California Corporation d/b/a CARSON HONDA, and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             January 15, 2025

 

Moving Parties:          Plaintiff Ana Marilu Landaverde Rivera

 

Responding Party:      Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

 

HEARING: Motion To Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

RULING

 

            The Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant’s Most Knowledgeable. The Court denies Plaintiff's request to strike all objections to the deposition. The Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant’s Most Knowledgeable as to RFPs 1, 3, 5-9, 11, and 16. The Court denies the Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant’s Most Knowledgeable as to RFPs 2, 10, and 12-15.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff Ana Marilu Landaverde Rivera filed the Complaint against Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc., alleging causes of action for violation of the Song-Beverly Act and negligent repair.

 

            On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff served a Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s

Person Most Knowledgeable.

 

            On March 21, 2024, Defendant served its objections to Plaintiff’s Notice of PMK

Deposition.

 

            On May 2, 2024, Plaintiff served an Amended Deposition Notice of Defendant’s PMK.

 

            On May 14, 2024, Defendant served objections to Plaintiff’s Amended Notice of PMK

Deposition.

 

            On July 11, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel emailed Defendant’s Counsel to inquire about PMK deposition dates.

           

On July 24, 2024, Defendant’s Counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that it would hold the entire week of October 28, 2024, for PMK depositions and proposed depositions for four matters. Defense counsel followed up 6 times between July 30, 2024, and September 15, 2024, asking whether Plaintiff’s counsel intended to take the deposition of AHM’s PMK during the week of October 28, 2024.

 

On July 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable.

 

On January 2, 2025, Defendant filed the Opposition.

 

On January 7, 2025, Plaintiff filed the Reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd. (a).) 

 

“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450 subd. (b)(2).)  “Although the statute appears to apply only if the deponent fails to both appear¿and¿produce documents, it has been held to apply simply on a deponent's failure to appear.” (Leko v. Cornerstone Building Inspection Service (2001) 86 Cal.App. 4th 1109, 1124.)   

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff requests the Court compel the deposition of Defendant PMK and compel Defendant to produce all responsive documents requested in Plaintiff’s Notice of PMK Deposition within the next 10 days.

 

Meet and Confer

           

A motion to compel deposition must be “accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450 subd. (b)(2).)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to make a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the scheduling of the deposition prior to filing the motion, as required under the Code of Civil Procedure § 2016.040. Defendant asserts that the issue at hand was merely finding a mutually agreeable date, but Plaintiff's counsel unilaterally demanded dates that aligned with their own timeline and refused to accommodate any alternative. Having only received a single email from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant claims there was no meaningful attempt at communication or compromise.

 

Defendant highlights that Plaintiff’s counsel relied on boilerplate correspondence and demanded a response within an unreasonable timeframe, such as requiring a date the next day. (Rein Decl. at ¶¶ 4-5, 7-8.) Defendant contends that this lack of cooperation wasted resources and unnecessarily involved the Court. Defendant requests that the Court sanction Plaintiff and/or her counsel $1,617.00 for these repeated, avoidable actions. (Rein Decl. at ¶ 15.)

 

Additionally, Defendant urges the Court to encourage Plaintiff and her counsel to collaborate with opposing counsel to schedule depositions amicably and avoid requiring Court intervention in every case. Defendant argues that cooperation, rather than unilateral demands, is a more practical and efficient solution.

 

Plaintiff argues that they attempted to meet and confer on February 28, May 2, and July 11, 2024. Furthermore, Plaintiff states that Defendant’s offer for a PMK deposition in October did not include this matter.

 

While Defendant states they have offered dates for the deposition of their PMK, none of these offers were related to the instant case. Instead, they were merely offers for Plaintiffs Counsel to depose a PMK in any of the matters Plaintiff’s counsel has against Defendant. The Court finds that these offers do not constitute an offer of dates of availability in the instant case. Furthermore, Plaintiff has made multiple attempts to schedule the deposition of Defendant’s PMK. Therefore, Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

Deposition

 

If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd. (a).) 

 

While Defendant has failed to schedule a deposition date, Plaintiff has provided no argument as to Defendant’s objections and has provided no separate statement regarding the Matters for Examination.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition but denies Plaintiff’s request to strike objections.

 

RFP’s

 

Defendants have objected to all of Plaintiff’s 17 RFPs. Plaintiff requests the Court strike Defendants objections and compel responses to the RFPs.

           

RFPs 1, 3, 5-8: Documents relating to the subject vehicle.

RFPs 2, 10, 12-15: Documents relating to Defendant’s policies regarding customer complaints that a vehicle is defective.

RFPs 9, 11: Documents relating to the Deponent’s knowledge.

RFP 16: All Technical Service Bulletins for vehicles for the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle.

 

RFPs 1, 3, 5-8

 

These RFPs seek documents regarding the subject vehicle itself. Since these categories only request information regarding the Subject Vehicle, the Court finds them to be relevant. Furthermore, Defendant has failed to justify its objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of PMK as to RFPs 1, 3, and 5-8.

 

RFPs 2, 10, 12-15

 

These RFPs seek a variety of information regarding Defendants policies and procedures. While some of these requests may be relevant, they all are overbroad as Plaintiff has not limited the requests to only documents regarding the same make, model, and year of the subject vehicle.

 

Therefore, the Court denies the Motion to Compel Deposition of PMK as to RFPs 2, 10, and 12-15.

 

RFPs 9, 11

 

These RFPs seek Documents regarding the Deponent’s job description and all material given to Deponent in preparation for the deposition. The Court finds this request is relevant as it shows the knowledge of the Deponent and the weight one should put on Deponent’s statements. Furthermore, Defendant has failed to justify its objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co., supra, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of PMK as to RFPs 9, 11.

 

RFP 16

 

This RFP seeks all Technical Service Bulletins for vehicles of the same year, make, and vehicle model as the subject vehicle. Since this request is relevant to whether Defendant was willful in its alleged failure to comply with the Act and because the request is tailored to vehicles of the same year, make, and vehicle model as the subject vehicle, the Court finds that it is relevant to the instant matter. (Krotin v. Porsche (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 294, 302-03.) Furthermore, Defendant has failed to justify its objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co., supra, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of PMK as to RFP 16.

            The Court considered the moving papers.

 

RULING

           

The Court grants the Motion to Set Aside Default.

           

BACKGROUND

           

On December 15, 2023, Plaintiff Gabriella Emma Cagliari filed the complaint against Rebecca Minda Zimmerman, alleging a single cause of action for conversion arising from the alleged conversion of a 2023 Jeep UT.

 

On June 21, 2024, The Court entered the default of Defendant.

 

On December 10, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Default.

 

No Opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect…¿ [The application]¿shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §¿473 subd. (b).) “‘[W]hen relief under section 473¿is¿available, there is a strong¿public¿policy¿in¿favor¿of granting relief and allowing the requesting party his or her day in court…[Citation.]” (Rappleyea v. Campbell¿(1994) 8 Cal. 4th 975, 981-82.) 

¿ 

Although a trial court has the discretion to vacate the entry of a default or subsequent judgment, this discretion may be exercised only after the party seeking relief has shown that there is a proper ground for relief and that the party has raised that ground in a procedurally proper manner, within any applicable time limits.”¿(Cruz v.¿Fagor¿America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.) “The defendant must … demonstrate a satisfactory excuse for not responding to the original action in a timely manner.”¿(Id.¿at 504.) Moving parties have the initial burden to prove excusable neglect by a preponderance of competent evidence.¿(Kendall v. Barker¿(1988) 197 Cal.App.3d 619, 624.)

¿ 

Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 473 subd. (d) provides in relevant part that “[t]he court may,¿upon motion of the injured party…set aside any void judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473 subd. (d.))¿ 

 

Code Civ. Proc., section 473.5 permits the Court to set aside a default and default judgment when the service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action. Code Civ. Proc., section 473.5 requires the motion to be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by the party's avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5.)¿The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of the following:¿ 

¿ 

1) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or¿¿ 

2) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. ¿¿ 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Defendant requests that the Court set aside the default under Code Civil Procedure section 473.5, as Defendant argues they were never served the Summons and Complaint. In the alternative, Defendant asks the Court to set aside the default under Code Civil Procedure section 473 subdivision (b).

 

            Plaintiff filed no opposition to this motion.

 

“The filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper. However, the presumption arises only if the proof of service complies with the applicable statutory requirements.” (Floveyor International Ltd. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 795.) “Evidence Code section 647 provides that a registered process server’s declaration of service establishes a presumption that the facts stated in the declaration are true.” (Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.)

 

 

            Defendant has filed an affidavit stating that she was never served the Summons and Complaint. (Zimmerman Decl. ¶ 7.) However, Plaintiff has filed a Proof of Summons of Service that states Defendant was served by Rachel Ruddock on February 17, 2024, at 11:24 am at 4712 E. 2nd Street, Long Beach, CA, 90803. While Defendant has not overcome the presumption that the proof of service was improper, A party may seek relief from default when the party lacked actual notice of the lawsuit in time to defend against the action and the party seeks relief within a reasonable time not to exceed two years from entry of default. (Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5.) Relief from default may be granted even if service was proper if the party had no actual notice of the action. (Ramos v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1434, 1444-1445.) Where there is any doubt about setting aside a default, such doubt should be resolved in favor of setting aside the default. (Rosenthal v. Garner (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 891, 898.) Therefore, because Defendant states, in their declaration, that they lacked actual knowledge in time to defend the action, Defendant is entitled to relief from default. (Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5; Ramos, 223 Cal.App.4th 1434, 1444-1445.)

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Vacate Default. Defendant has 20 days to file a responsive pleading.
