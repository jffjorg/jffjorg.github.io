DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV18546    Hearing Date: July 15, 2024    Dept: B

Maria Salgado, et al. V. SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA EDISON COMPANY

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:         July 15, 2024                          Trial Date:       January 13, 2025

Department:                B                                             Case No.:        21STCV18546

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Crown Castle Fiber LLC 

Responding Party:      Plaintiffs Maria Salgado & Alexis Banuelos

 

 

BACKGROUND

           

On May 17, 2021, Maria Salgado and Alexis Banuelos (Plaintiffs) filed a Complaint against Southern California Edison Company, alleging a sole cause of action of wrongful death in negligence. This case stems from a motorcycle accident that took place on December 28, 2019, at the intersection of Florence Avenue and Crofton Avenue in the City of Bell California. (Complaint, ¶¶ 4-5.) There, Ismael Banuelos Quintero (Decedent) was operating his motorcycle when he struck the curb, was ejected off of the motorcycle, struck a “guy wire”, suffered fatal injuries, and died after arriving at the hospital. (Complaint, ¶ 7.)

 

            On August 4, 2022, Plaintiffs amended their initial Complaint to substitute Doe 1 for Crown Castle Fiber, LLC (Defendant) the company that installed the guy wire at issue. The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment (the Motion). Plaintiffs opposed the Motion; no reply was filed.      

 

Tentative] Ruling

           

Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Legal Standard

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

            “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

 

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Analysis

           

The moving party’s initial task here is to present facts and evidence to negate an element of the cause of action presented by Plaintiffs. As explained in more detail below, Defendant negates both the duty and breach element of the sole cause of action, thereby shifting the burden to Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs have failed to show a triable issue of material fact with regard to duty and breach, therefore, Defendant’s Motion is granted.

 

            Duty

            “The elements of a cause of action for wrongful death are a tort, such as negligence, and resulting death.” (Lopez v. City of Los Angeles (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 675, 685.) In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

            The first element addressed by Defendant is the element of duty. Defendant points to Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 (Rowland) which sets the standard for whether a duty is owed. Rowland lays out that whether a duty is owed is determined by weighing the following seven factors: (1) the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff; (2) the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury; (3) the closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered; (4) the moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct; (5) the policy of preventing future harm; (6) the extent of the burden to the defendant and the consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for breach; and (7) the availability, cost and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.

 

 

 

(1)   Foreseeability

            As it pertains to foreseeability, ordinarily, foreseeability is a question of fact for the jury, however it may be decided as a question of law only if under the undisputed facts there is no room for a reasonable difference of opinion. (See generally, Rest. 2d Torts, §453.)

 

            Here, Defendant demonstrates that Defendant and its contractors installed the subject guy wire. (UMF 7.) Additionally, the guy wire was a required component of the utility pole under applicable regulations from the California Public Utility Commission’s (CPUC), General Order 95. (UMF 9.) Although it is disputed as to whether the guy wire was covered, there is no allegation that the guy wire was installed contrary to CPUC regulations. In fact, Defendants argue that the guy wire was installed as per requirements by CPUC’s regulations. Plaintiffs contend that the guy wire being placed immediately next to the roadway poses a foreseeable risk of harm to motorists, however, Plaintiffs provide no proof to support this contention.    

 

            Plaintiffs attempts to analogize to Bigbee v. Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. (1983) 34 Cal.3d 49 (Bigbee) are unpersuasive. In Bigbee, a telephone booth user was injured when the booth was struck by an automobile who had jumped the curb. The Supreme Court of California reversed a grant of summary judgment for the telephone company who installed the booth, holding that there was a triable issue of material fact as to whether there was a foreseeability of risk for the user, even when the booth was 15 feet from the side of a major thoroughfare. The facts here are distinguishable. Decedent was the driver, and as the evidence from both parties shows, Decedent lost control of the motorcycle prior to being ejected and hitting the guy wire. (See Plaintiff’s Compendium of Evidence, Exhs. C & D; Defendant’s Index of Evidence, Exhs. D.) Additionally, in Bigbee it is unclear whether the telephone company was following any governmental regulations on the placement of phone booths, whereas here, CPUC governs how and where guy wires are installed. Bigbee is simply inapplicable.   

 

            Plaintiffs argue that it was foreseeable for a pedestrian or a motorist to be injured by the guy wire’s installation. However, no more or less foreseeable than a motorist jumping the curb to collide with a tree, building, or bus stop.

                             

(2)   Degree of Certainty

            The second factor is the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury. It is clear that Plaintiff suffered an injury, but this factor is not dispositive. It must be assessed with all other factors together.

 

(3)   Closeness of Connection Between Defendant’s Conduct & the Injury

            The third factor is the connection between Defendant’s alleged breach and the injury. Here, Defendant has provided evidence that the guy wire was only installed because it was required by CPUC regulations. (Declaration of Saad Khalid, hereinafter, Khalid Decl., ¶¶ 10-13.) There is no evidence nor allegations that Defendant installed the guy wire improperly or contrary to CPUC regulations. Accordingly, there is no connection between Defendant’s conduct and the injury here.  

 

(4)   Moral Blame

            When there is no connection between Defendant’s conduct and Plaintiffs injury, there is no rational for placing moral blame.

 

(5)   Preventing Future Harm

            Similarly, with no connection between the conduct and the harm, preventing future harm is a non-issue. Defendant contends that guy wires are placed on countless utility poles as per CPUC’s regulations, and as CPUC has seen no reason to change the manner in which they are installed because there is no showing of harm, thus there is no need to attempt to prevent future harm.  

 

(6)   Burden to Defendant and Consequences to the Community

            Defendant contends that there are utility poles and guy wires on every boulevard and non-residential street throughout the state. Defendant argues that a sudden finding that public utility providers needed to move or change the guy wires attached to utility poles to prevent injury because a motorist lost control of their motorcycle would be impractical and costly. The Court agrees.  

 

            Clearly, the Rowland factors weigh in favor of imposing no duty on Defendant. With no duty, the cause of action must fail.

 

            Breach

            Additionally, without a breach of duty, there is no cause of action. Plaintiffs contend that Defendant breached their duty of care by failing to inspect the guy wire to be sure it was properly maintained. However, the Declaration of Saad Khalid, a Supervisor of Field Operations, Maintenance & Inspections at Crown Castle Fiber, LLC notes that no repairs were performed on this specific guy wire, for pole number 4452100E, because no repairs nor maintenance were necessary for that pole number. (Khalid Decl., ¶ 14.) Plaintiffs evidence to the contrary is unpersuasive. Plaintiffs present the Declaration of Nestor E. Valencia a former City Councilman, who states that during his time as a Councilman, he was aware that Sothern California Edison was not doing an adequate job of maintaining their equipment. (Declaration of Nestor E. Garcia, ¶ .) However, it is unclear how this relates to the specific guy wire alleged to have caused Decedent’s injuries and death.                    

 

 

CONCLUSION

            Accordingly, as Defendant has successfully negated the elements of both duty and breach, the Court need not address causation. Crown Castle Fiber, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

 

On May 6, 2022, Plaintiffs Mario Fernando Velasquez (“Velasquez”) and Reynaldo Garcia (“Garcia”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), filed this action against Defendants Virgie Robrigado Binasahan (“Virgie”), Exekiel R. Binasahan (“Exekiel”), and Jayvee R. Binasahan for damages arising from a motor vehicle accident that occurred on November 25, 2021.

 

On March 25, 2024, the Court granted Defendant Virgie’s motions to compel discovery directed at Plaintiff Velasquez, and ordered Plaintiff Velasquez “to serve verified responses to Defendant Virgie’s (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for inspection and production of documents, set one, without objections, within twenty (20) days.” (See March 25, 2024 Minute Order at pg. 2.) Additionally, the Court ordered “Plaintiff Velasquez and/or his counsel are ordered to pay sanctions to Defendant Virgie, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $565.17, within twenty (20) days.” (Id. at pg. 3.)

 

On March 26, 2024, the Court granted Defendant Virgie’s motions to compel discovery directed at Plaintiff Garcia and ordered Plaintiff Garcia “to serve verified responses to Defendant Virgie’s (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for inspection and production of documents, set one, without objections, within twenty (20) days.” (See March 26, 2024 Minute Order at pg. 2.) Additionally, the Court ordered “Garcia and/or his counsel are ordered to pay sanctions to Defendant Virgie, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $565.17, within twenty (20) days.” (Id. at pg. 3.)

 

Defendants Virgie and Exekiel (collectively, “Defendants”) now move for the Court to impose terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Garcia on the grounds that Plaintiff Garcia has failed to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024 order. Alternatively, Defendants request the Court to impose $455.34 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Garcia.

 

No opposition has been received.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Legal Standard

 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Ibid.) “[C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.” (Ibid.) Where discovery violations are “willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid.) A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions. (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) Where discovery sanctions are requested against a party, there must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful. (Ibid.) 

 

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.” (Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702.) A trial court’s order to impose terminating sanctions will be reversed only if it “was arbitrary, capricious, or whimsical.” (Id. at 702.)  “[W]here a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Id. at p. 702.) Trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders. (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.) Terminating sanctions are warranted when a party’s lack of compliance with the discovery process has caused the opposing party prejudice. (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc., supra, 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 989.)

 

Discussion

 

            Terminating Sanctions

 

As advanced in Defendants’ motion, Plaintiff Garcia has failed to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024 Order. The Court ordered Plaintiff Garcia to provide verified responses and without objections to Defendants’ discovery requests within 20 days of service of the order on October 24, 2023. (See March 26, 2024 Minute Order at pg. 2.) Furthermore, the Court ordered Plaintiff Garcia and/or his counsel to pay $565.17 in monetary sanctions. (Id. at pg. 3.) Defendants gave notice of the Court’s order on March 28, 2024. (Motion at pg. 4; Babadjanian Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. A.) To date, Plaintiff Garcia has neither provided responses to the subject discovery nor paid the awarded monetary sanctions. (Id. at ¶¶ 7-10.)

 

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff Garcia’s conduct is in clear violation of the Court’s March 26, 2024 Order. Because Plaintiff Garcia has chosen not to file an opposition, he has failed to explain why he has been unable to comply with the Court’s order, and as a result, the Court finds that Plaintiff Garcia has willfully misused the discovery process by this lack of diligence. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 787 [“Lack of diligence may be deemed willful in the sense that the party understood his obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply.”])

 

Nevertheless, the Court is mindful that an order for terminating sanctions must not be made lightly and any sanctions must be tailored to the discovery violation. (Creed-21, supra, 18 Cal.App.5th at 702; Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at 992.) Thus, the Court must take an incremental approach to discovery sanctions. The Court therefore finds that terminating sanctions are not appropriate at this time.

 

Accordingly, the Court denies Defendants’ request for terminating sanctions.

 

            Monetary Sanctions

 

Alternatively, Defendants argues that the Court should impose further monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Garcia in the amount of $455.34. (Motion at pg. 7.)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure, § 2023.030 provides that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  A misuse of the discovery process “include[s] . . . [d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.”  (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at 991.) 

 

Based on Plaintiff Garcia’s violation, the Court does find that monetary sanctions are appropriate against Plaintiff Garcia and his counsel of record for their failure to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024 Order. In this instance, Defendants seek monetary sanctions in the amount of $455.34, consisting of two hours preparing the instant motion at a rate of $160.17 per hour. (Babadjanian Decl. ¶ 11.) This amount is reasonable and shall be awarded in full.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court denies Defendants’ motion for terminating sanctions. Alternatively, the Court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanction against Plaintiff Garcia and his counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the amount of $455.34. Payment shall be tendered to Defendants’ counsel within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

The Court shall provide Plaintiff Garcia a second opportunity to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024. Plaintiff Garcia is ordered to serve verified responses to Defendant Virgie’s (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for inspection and production of documents, set one, without objections, within twenty (20) days of receipt of this order. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(a),(b), 2031.300(a),(b).)

 

Defendants to give notice.
