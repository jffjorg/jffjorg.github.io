Case Number: 19STCV21240    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: B

Plaintiff Tammy Lynn Gardner’s Requests for Default Judgment

Defendants: Laura Rabelo and Marcos Guereca

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Tammy Lynn Gardner’s request for default judgment against Defendants Laura Rabelo and Marcos Guereca is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a May 6, 2019, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiff Tammy Lynn Gardner (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendants Laura Rabelo (“Rabelo”), Marcos Guereca (“Guereca”), and Does 1 to 10 (collectively “Defendants”) negligently operated their motor vehicle, colliding with Plaintiff’s motor scooter and causing serious injuries thereof.

On June 18, 2019, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants alleging motor vehicle negligence and negligent entrustment.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On October 21, 2024, Defendants entered default.

On October 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendants.

LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.)

ANALYSIS

Defendant Rabelo

Plaintiff seeks monetary damages against Rabelo in the amount of $203,440.87, representing $181,483.07 in general and special damages, $21,282.80 in prejudgment interest, and $675.00 in costs.

Plaintiff’s request is denied for the following deficiencies:

1. Plaintiff did not file a copy of the Statement of Damages served upon Defendants. Additionally, the amount of principal damages sought in JUD-100 ($181,483.07) is more than that in Plaintiff’s CIV-100 ($180,808.07).

2. Plaintiff requests interest but does not provide interest calculation. (CRC 3.1800(a)(3).)

3. Plaintiff is suing Defendant Guereca on the sole basis that he owns and entrusted the involved vehicle to Rabelo. Accordingly, Plaintiff must provide proof of Guereca’s ownership pursuant to Cal. Veh. Code § 17151(a).

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment has been denied for deficiencies on three separate occasions. Accordingly, the Court dismisses this action for failure to prosecute pursuant to Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 583.410 and CRC 3.110(h).

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Defendants Laura Rabelo and Marcos Guereca is DENIED and the action is dismissed without prejudice pursuant to CCP § 583.410 and CRC 3.110(h).

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

I. BACKGROUND

On October 16, 2020, Plaintiff Sharon Smith (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants California Highway Patrol (“Defendant”), Pearl Rodney (“Rodney”), and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiff alleges that, on April 18, 2020, Rodney, acting within the scope of his employment with Defendant, ran a red light and collided with the vehicle in which Plaintiff was a passenger. (First Amended Complaint [“FAC”] ¶¶ 11-15.) Plaintiff alleges serious injuries as a result.

On May 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed their FAC alleging four causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) vicarious liability; (3) motor vehicle negligence; and (4) negligence pursuant to Cal. Gov. Code § 820(a).

On August 2, 2023, Defendant filed the instant demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC.

On August 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed their opposition. Defendant filed their reply that same day.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

II. DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S FAC

Defendant CHP moves for an order sustaining its demurrer to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action in Plaintiff’s FAC without leave to amend, and to dismiss the FAC with prejudice because each cause of action fails to allege sufficient facts. (Notice of Demurrer, p. 2:4-9.)

III. LEGAL STANDARDS

A demurrer is a pleading that may be used to test the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in the complaint. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10.) There are two types of demurrers – general demurrers and special demurrers. (See McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 77.)

General demurrers can be used to attack pleadings for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. (CCP § 430.10(e); McKenney, 167 Cal.App.4th at 77.) Such demurrers can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable; evidence or extrinsic matters are not considered. (CCP §§ 430.30 and 430.70; Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the Court admits “all material facts properly pleaded” and “matters which may be judicially noticed,” but does not

consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].” (Blank, 39 Cal.3d at 318.) It gives these facts “a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Ibid.). At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) "If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence." (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.) Special demurrers can be used to attack the pleadings on grounds that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible, or in a contract case, for failure to allege whether a contract is oral or written. (CCP § 430.10(f).)

Moreover, CCP § 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §¿430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is

absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue. (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227 [“Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment. (Citation.) Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given."].)

IV. DISCUSSION

Requests for Judicial Notice

CHP’s July 12, 2023, request for judicial notice of the Notice of Ruling Re: Sharon Smith’s Petition for Relief from Gov. Code § 945.4 heard on February 28, 2023, in Department 16 of Stanley Mosk Courthouse, is granted pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code §§ 452-454.

Plaintiff’s August 8, 2023, requests for judicial notice of Executive Orders N-35-20, N-71-20, and N-08-21, issued by Governor Gavin Newsom, are granted pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code §§ 452-454

Meet and Confer Requirement

Defense counsel declares that, on May 24, 2023, they emailed a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel regarding this matter. (Brown Decl. ¶ 2.) Counsels discussed these issues via telephone but were unable to reach an agreement. (Ibid.) Accordingly, the Court finds Defendant satisfies the meet and confer requirement under CCP § 430.41(a).

Defendant’s Argument

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s FAC fails to allege facts stating a legal claim because Plaintiff admits to their untimely filing of a claim against a government entity pursuant to Cal. Gov. Code § 911.2. (FAC ¶ 5.) Under Gov. Code § 945.4, no personal injury suit for money or damages may be brought against a government entity until a written claim is presented to and acted upon by the Government Claims Program (“GCP”). (Gov. Code § 945.4.) The claim must

be presented no later than six months after accrual of the cause of action. (Gov. Code § 911.2(a).) However, a claimant may apply to the GCP for late claim relief. (Gov. Code § 911.4.) If claimant’s application is denied, then they may petition the court for an order for relief from the presentation requirement of § 945.4. Here, Plaintiff admits to presenting an untimely claim pursuant to § 911.2. (See FAC ¶ 5.) Accordingly, Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s FAC on the grounds that her claim is barred by § 911.2.

Plaintiff’s Petition for Relief from § 945.4

On February 4, 2022, Plaintiff filed a petition for relief from the presentation requirement of Gov. Code § 945.4. Notably, this petition is under a different case number with the Court, Case No. 21STCP03992. (Notice of Demurrer Exh. A [notice of ruling on Plaintiff’s petition].) On February 28, 2023, the Honorable Lia Martin in Department 16 of Stanley Mosk Courthouse denied Plaintiff’s petition. (Ibid.) The notice of ruling states that Plaintiff failed to demonstrate denial of a claim under § 945.4. (Ibid. at p. 3.) Instead, Plaintiff simply stated that the GCP did not respond to her claim. (Ibid.)

On March 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion for reconsideration on their petition. The motion is set to be heard on July 17, 2025, in Department 3 of Stanley Mosk.

Plaintiff argues that the Court should refrain from ruling on the instant demurrer since it rests entirely on the timeliness of her claim pursuant to Gov. Code § 945.4, which will turn on Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration in 21STCP03992. In reply, Defendant “contends that plaintiff’s late claim petition should be resolved prior to proceeding with this action.” (Reply p. 2:1-2.)

The Court agrees. Ruling on the instant demurrer prior to resolution of Plaintiff’s late claim petition risks conflicting outcomes. Accordingly, the Court declines to rule on Defendant’s

demurrer at this time and stays the instant action, pending the hearing of Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration in 21STCP03992.

V. CONCLUSION

The instant action is stayed until Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration in Case No. 21STCP03992 is ruled upon. The OSC re Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration and the hearing on this demurrer are continued to [date].

Defendant to give notice.

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

TENTATIVE RULING

RUBI GARCIA, vs. MAY SHEUNG LUU, et al. Case No.: 21STCV19438 Hearing Date: OCTOBER 22, 2024

Defendants Dieu K. Luu and May Sheung Luu’s motion to compel the independent medical examination of Plaintiff Rubi Garcia is granted. Defendants’ request for sanctions is also granted in the reduced amount of $2,380.33.

Defendants Dieu K. Luu (“Dieu”) and May Sheung Luu (“May Sheung”)(collectively “Defendants”) move to compel the independent medical examination (“IME”) of Plaintiff Rubi Garcia (“Plaintiff”), contending that there is good cause for the motion because Plaintiff placed her physical injuries in controversy. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities, p. 1:5-14.)

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court …, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action …, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

Where any party seeks to obtain discovery by a physical examination, the party shall obtain leave of the court. (CCP § 2032.310(a).) The Court shall grant the motion only for good cause shown. (CCP§ 2032.320(a).)

Meet and Confer

Plaintiff originally had an IME scheduled for May 7, 2024, which they failed to attend. (Rodriguez Decl. ¶¶ 5-6, 8.) Defense counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding Plaintiff’s non-appearance later that day. (Ibid.; Exh. F

[meet and confer letter].) On August 7, 2024, Defense counsel further meet and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel via telephone. (Ibid. at ¶ 10; Exh. G.) As a courtesy, Defense counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel with three prospective dates for a rescheduled IME. (Ibid.; Exh. H [email].) Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (Ibid. at ¶ 11.) Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendants satisfy the meet and confer declaration requirement.

Separate Statement Requirements

A motion for “medical examination over objection” must be accompanied by a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(a).)

“A separate statement is a separate document filed and served with the discovery motion that provides all the information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses to it that are at issue.” (CRC Rule 3.1345(c).)

Defendants filed a separate statement compliant with CRC Rule 3.1345 on September 4, 2024. Accordingly, Defendants meet the separate statement requirement.

Good Cause

Defendants contend that there is good cause to compel Plaintiff’s IME because Plaintiff placed her physical injuries in controversy. In their response to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Plaintiff claims injuries to their arms, chest, breast, and legs, swelling, neck pain, headaches, and a right knee medical meniscus bucket handle tear. (Exh. A [Plaintiff’s Response to Form Interrogatories, Set One] No. 6.2.) Plaintiff also received orthopedic treatment in connection to her alleged injuries. (Ibid. at No. 6.4.)

Plaintiff did not object to Defendants’ noticed IME or oppose this motion. Without an IME, defendants cannot fully evaluate Plaintiff’s injuries and prepare a defense for trial. Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendants have shown good cause to compel Plaintiff's IME.

Required Specifications of the Proposed IME

A motion for an examination must specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. (CCP § 2032.310(b).)

Defendants provide a scheduled IME for December 10, 2024, at 9:30 a.m., to be conducted by Jonathan T. Nassos, M.D., at 5651 Sepulveda Blvd., Suite #201, Sherman Oaks, CA 91411. (Separate Statement p. 2.) Defendants identify Dr. Nassos as an orthopedic specialist. (Ibid.) Defendants state that the IME “will be conducted for the purposes of determining, evaluating, and assessing plaintiff’s physical condition and claims of injury in controversy in this action.” (Ibid. at 18-19.) The examination “will be consistent with the practices of orthopedic surgery in Southern California and consultations in connection with such practices.” (Ibid. at 25-26.) Defendants state that this exam may include orthopedic procedures and/or manipulations of the body. (Ibid.) Finally, the exam may include the taking of x-rays, CT scans, or MRIs as the performing physician deems necessary. (Ibid.)

Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendants provide the required specifications of the proposed IME.

Sanctions

Defendants move to impose monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $2,500.46, payable within 20 days from this hearing.

Here, sanctions are appropriate since Plaintiff’s failure to appear at the May 7, 2024, IME and failure to reschedule a subsequent examination constitutes an abuse of the discovery process under CCP § 2023.010.

The requested $2,500.46 consists of the following: 2.75 hours of work at a rate of $160.17 per hour, including .75 hours in anticipation for preparing a reply, and 1 hour for attending the hearing; a $60.00 filing fee; and a $2,000 non-appearance fee which Defense counsel incurred for Plaintiff’s initial IME. (Rodriguez Decl. ¶ 15.) Prior to Plaintiff’s initial May 7, 2024, Defense counsel put Plaintiff on notice of the $2,000 non-appearance fee. Accordingly, the Court finds this fee reasonable. However, seeing as Plaintiff did not oppose this motion, the Court reduces the proposed 2.75 hours of work to two. Therefore, the Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $2,380.33.

Conclusion

For the reasons set forth above, the motion to compel Plaintiff’s independent medical examination is GRANTED. Sanctions are awarded against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $2,380.33, to be paid within 20 days of this hearing.

Case Number: 21STCV29627    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: B

Plaintiffs Jonathan Sims and Natalie Sanaee’s Requests for Default Judgment

Defendant: Ovita Alexis Hines

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Jonathan Sims’s request for default judgment is GRANTED.

Plaintiff Natalie Sanaee’s request for default judgment is DENIED. Additionally, the Court sets an OSC re Sanctions against Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $250 on [date] for failing to correct identified deficiencies and to comply with CRC 3.110(h). Further deficient filings risk dismissal without prejudice pursuant to CCP § 583.410.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from an August 12, 2019, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiffs Jonathan Sims (“Sims”) and Natalie Sanaee (“Sanaee”)(collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege Ovita Alexis Hines (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) negligently operated their motor vehicle, colliding with Plaintiffs’ motor vehicle and causing serious injuries thereof.

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendant alleging motor vehicle negligence.

On October 30, 2023, Defendant entered default.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendant.

LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5)

declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.)

ANALYSIS

Sims seeks damages in the amount of $38,521.41, representing $37,675.00 in general and special damages and $846.41 in costs.

Defendant has failed to timely respond or appear. Sims’s default packet meets all statutory requirements under CCP § 585. Therefore, Sims’s request for default judgment against Ovita Alexis Hines is GRANTED.

Sanaee seeks damages in the amount of $12,275.00 in general and special damages.

Sanaee’s request for default judgment is denied because they served their Statement of Damages on Defendant after default was entered. Statement of Damages must be served at least 30 days prior to entry of default. (CCP § 425.11(c)-(d).) Additionally, Sanaee failed to file proof of service for their Statement of Damages.

Sanaee’s request for default judgment has now been denied for deficiencies twice. Accordingly, the Court sets an OSC re Dismissal and/or sanctions against Plaintiff’s counsel. Further deficient filings risk dismissal without prejudice pursuant to CCP § 583.410 and CRC 3.110(h).

Therefore, Sanaee’s request for default judgment against Ovita Alexis Hines is DENIED.

Moving parties are ordered to give notice.
