DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24AHCV00170    Hearing Date: November 8, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANTS DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

I.         INTRODUCTION

Plaintiffs Ting Zhao and Yong Sun (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Weixin Fu and Xian Ge (“Defendants”), alleging that the parties entered into a loan agreement which subsequently defaulted. Following the default, Plaintiffs obtained a judgment from a Chinese court, holding Defendants liable for RMB 18,855,071.00 yuan (approximately $2,620,981.35). Plaintiffs now seek recognition and enforcement of this foreign judgment in California.

Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC) on August 30, 2024. On September 30, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer challenging the FAC. Plaintiffs filed an opposition to the demurrer on October 28, 2024.

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, Defense counsel has filed a declaration stating that the meet-and-confer process was conducted by email. (Chen Decl., ¶ 2.) However, the Code requires that the meet-and-confer process be conducted in person, by telephone, or via video conference. Thus, Defense has not complied with this requirement. Nevertheless, while the meet-and-confer process may have been insufficient, this does not provide a basis for granting or denying the demurrer.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

A.    General Demurrer – Failure to State Facts Sufficient to Constitute a Cause of Action

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s FAC for Recognition of Foreign Judgment as it fails to state facts sufficient to support a cause of action. The Uniform Foreign-Country Money Judgments Recognition Act (“UFCMJRA”) authorizes the recognition of any foreign judgment that is final and conclusive.  See Manco Contracting Co. (W.L.L.) v. Bezdikian (2008) 45 Cal.4th 192, 195 [discussing former Uniform Foreign Money-Judgments Recognition Act, which the UFCMJRA replaced].  CCP section 1716 provides that “[e]xcept as otherwise provided in subdivisions (b), (c), (d), and (f), a court of this state shall recognize a foreign-country judgment to which this chapter applies.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 1716(a).  Pursuant to section 1715, the UFCMJRA applies to a foreign-country judgment to the extent that the judgment (1) grants or denies recovery of a sum of money, and (2) is final, conclusive, and enforceable under the law of the foreign country where it is rendered.  See id., § 1715(a).  For purposes of the UFCMJRA, a “foreign-country judgment” is defined as “a judgment of a court of a foreign country.”  See id., § 1714(b).  

Defendants contends that Plaintiffs’ FAC fails to allege sufficient facts to establish that the Chinese judgment is enforceable under Chinese law. (Demurrer, p.6) First, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have not met their burden, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1715(c), to establish that the foreign-country judgment is entitled to recognition. (Id.)  Second, Defendants contends that Plaintiffs allegations merely assert conclusions without factual support, which is insufficient to meet the requirements under UFCMJRA. Specifically, Defendants reference Article 269 Chinese civil law, suggesting that additional procedural steps may be necessary to render judgment enforceable under Chinese law. (Demurrer, p.9)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argues that the FAC meets the requirements for recognition of a foreign-country judgment, alleging that the Chinese judgment is final, conclusive, and enforceable. Plaintiffs’ claim that Defendants’ assertions are unsupported and that the FAC includes detailed allegations of compliance with Chinese law. (Opposition, p. 3.) Plaintiffs further submit that Defendants’ reliance on Article 269 of Chinese civil law is misplaced, arguing that Plaintiffs’ allegations satisfy the requirements for an enforceable judgment under Chinese law. (Opposition, pp. 3-4.)

Upon review, the Court finds that Plaintiffs has sufficiently alleged facts to meet the requirements for recognition under the UFCMJRA. Plaintiffs assert that, pursuant to Article 158 of the Civil Procedure Law of the People’s Republic of China, the Chinese judgment became effective, final, conclusive, and enforceable as of August 25, 2023. (FAC ¶ 24.) Additionally, Plaintiffs alleges that Defendants has not filed any appeal challenging the Chinese judgment up to the present date. (Id.)

At the pleading stage, Plaintiffs is required only to allege, not prove, the necessary elements for recognition. On its face, the FAC sufficiently alleges facts to establish that the judgment is final, conclusive, and enforceable under UFCMJRA. Accordingly, Defendants demurrer is overruled.

     IV.       ORDER

            The Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the FAC.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS' MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

I.                INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Linda Barajas (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendants HMRTJ LLC dba Villa Valencia Apartments, Gerald Tom, Ronald Tom, and Rodolfo Campos (“Defendants”), seeking damages arising from two slip-and-fall incidents at the Villa Valencia Apartments in Arcadia, CA. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to repair a broken rain gutter, which created a hazardous condition and ultimately led to Plaintiff’s injuries. Plaintiff brings claims for (1) premises liability and (2) general negligence.

On June 21, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint. The motion is unopposed.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civ. Proc. §436: “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:

(a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.

(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”

            Code Civ. Proc. §431.10:

“(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense.

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:

(1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.

(2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.

(3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”             

“‘In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.’ (Citation.)” Today IV’s Inc. v. Los Angeles County MTA (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.

            Civ. Code §3294:

(a) In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.

(b) An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.

(c) As used in this section, the following definitions shall apply:

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

III.           ANALYSIS

Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint, contending that several allegations are improper, immaterial, and irrelevant, warranting removal under California Code of Civil Procedure §§ 431 et seq., 435, 436, and 437. (Motion, pg.2) Defendants argue that the challenged allegations lack necessary factual support, are conclusory, and are insufficient to sustain claims for punitive and exemplary damages. Therefore, Defendants maintain that these allegations should be stricken from the complaint.

Defendants seek to strike the following: (1) page 3, paragraph 14a.(2), where Plaintiff requests punitive damages; (2) page 4, paragraphs L-3 and L-4, which allege “Willful Failure to Warn” and “Dangerous Condition of Public Property”; (3) page 5, paragraphs L-3 and L-4, which repeat the allegations of “Willful Failure to Warn” and “Dangerous Condition of Public Property”; and (4) the exemplary damages attachment on page 8, including EX-2, which asserts a “conscious disregard for the safety of the public and of Plaintiff.”

Page 3, paragraph 14a.(2)

Defendants seek to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages in Paragraph 14a(2), arguing that Plaintiff’s allegations do not satisfy the pleading requirements for punitive damages, which mandate specific factual support demonstrating malice, oppression, or fraud. The Court finds that Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages lacks the necessary factual foundation to establish malice, oppression, or fraud, as it consists only of conclusory statements without specific instances of intentional misconduct.

Therefore, the motion to strike Paragraph 14a(2) is GRANTED, and the punitive damages request is stricken.

Page 4, paragraphs L-3 and L-4 and page 5, paragraphs L-3 and L-4

Defendants moves to strike Plaintiff’s allegations of “Willful Failure to Warn” and “Dangerous Condition of Public Property” on pages 4 and 5, paragraphs prem L-3 and L-4, on the grounds that these claims lack necessary factual support. The “Willful Failure to Warn” claim is a statutory claim under Civil Code section 846. Consequently, Plaintiff must plead specific facts with reasonable particularity to satisfy the statutory elements of this claim (see Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal. App. 4th 612, 619). However, Plaintiff does not allege any facts showing intentional conduct or knowledge that serious injury would probably result, nor does Plaintiff allege reckless disregard for possible consequences. Therefore, the motion to strike this allegation is GRANTED.

Regarding the “Dangerous Condition of Public Property” allegation, Defendants argue this claim is conclusory and unsupported by any factual basis in the law. The Court agrees that the complaint lacks any factual support to establish a public property condition and finds this allegation similarly conclusory. As such, the motion to strike is GRANTED as to the “Dangerous Condition of Public Property” references in L-3 and L-4 on both pages 4 and 5.

 

Page 8 - exemplary damages attachment

Defendants seeks to strike the exemplary damages attachment in its entirety, including EX-2, which alleges that “Defendants’ lack of action after stating they would take action several times and being made aware of a tenant injury was a conscious disregard for the safety of the public and of Plaintiff.” The Court finds that while Plaintiff alleges that Defendants acted with a “conscious disregard for the safety of the public and of Plaintiff,” the complaint lacks sufficient factual detail to support this claim. To warrant exemplary damages, the claim must contain specific facts to demonstrate willful or malicious intent, beyond mere inaction or negligence. Here, Plaintiff’s allegations do not meet this standard as no facts are provided to show intentional conduct or malice required for exemplary damages.

The exemplary damages attachment is stricken from the complaint.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION & ORDER

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion to strike (1) page 3, paragraph 14a.(2), where Plaintiff requests punitive damages; (2) page 4, paragraphs L-3 and L-4, which allege “Willful Failure to Warn” and “Dangerous Condition of Public Property”; (3) page 5, paragraphs L-3 and L-4, which repeat the allegations of “Willful Failure to Warn” and “Dangerous Condition of Public Property” and (4) the exemplary damages attachment on page 8.

            Plaintiff has 20 days leave to amend.

            Defendants are to give notice.
