DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV02940    Hearing Date: January 23, 2025    Dept: H

Siddique v. SKC Construction, et al., Case No. 23PSCV02940

ORDER ON MOTIONS TO COMPEL

1.       Defendant/Cross-Complainant Business Alliance Insurance Company’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Wasif Siddique’s Responses to Form Interrogatories—Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide responses, without objections, to Business Alliance’s Form Interrogatories, Set No. One, within 30 days from the date of the notice of ruling. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $255.00 and are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

2.       Defendant/Cross-Complainant Business Alliance Insurance Company’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Wasif Siddique’s Responses to Special Interrogatories—Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide responses, without objections, to Business Alliance’s Special Interrogatories, Set No. One, within 30 days from the date of the notice of ruling. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $255.00 and are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling. 

3.       Defendant/Cross-Complainant Business Alliance Insurance Company’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Wasif Siddique’s Responses to Request for Production of Documents—Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide responses, without objections, to Business Alliance’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set No. One, within 30 days from the date of the notice of ruling. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $255.00 and are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Wasif Siddique (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

On or about November 9, 2015, Plaintiff entered into a written agreement with SKC Construction (“SKC”), wherein SKC agreed to perform a new home construction project (“Project”) at Plaintiff’s property located at 2502 Razzak Circle in Diamond Bar (“subject property”) for the fixed price of $1,214,800.00 (“Contract”). Plaintiff has paid SKC approximately $2,000,000.00 for the Project; however, the work remains incomplete and the work that has been completed is subpar. SKC has abandoned the Project. 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action against SKC, Sohail Khan (“Khan”), Business Alliance Insurance Company (“Business Alliance”) and Does 1-20 for: (1) breach of contract, (2) negligence, (3) fraud, and (4) recovery from contractor’s license board.

On August 6, 2024, Business Alliance filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against SKC, Khan and Roes 1-25 for: (1) indemnity, (2) reimbursement (declaratory relief), and (3) breach of contract 

On November 7, 2024, the Clerk entered default against SKC and Khan on Business Alliance’s cross-complaint.

Pending before the Court are Business Alliance’s motions to compel various discovery responses.

 

II.      Legal Standard 

Responses to requests for interrogatories and requests for production of documents are due 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).) “If a party . . . fails to serve a timely response . . . [t]he party [propounding the interrogatories or making the demand for production] may move for an order compelling response[s].” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).) “The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories [or a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling], unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300, subd. (c).) 

III.     Discussion

A.       Motion to Compel Form Interrogatories 

Business Alliance moves the Court for an order compelling Plaintiff to provide responses to Business Alliance’s Form Interrogatories, Set No. One. Business Alliance also moves the Court for an order awarding sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $255.00.

Case Number: 24PSCV03134    Hearing Date: January 23, 2025    Dept: H

Wasem v. Jacobo,
Case No. 24PSCV03134



ORDER ON MOTION
TO QUASH

Specially Appearing Defendant
Enrique Jacobo’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint is DENIED.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Leslie Yvonne Wasem (“Plaintiff”)
sustained injuries and damages in a September 21, 2022 motor vehicle accident
on the 605 freeway. 

On September 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint,
asserting causes of action against Enrique Jacobo (“Jacobo”) and Does 1-5 for:
(1) motor vehicle and (2) general negligence. 

On December 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed two
“Amendment[s] to Complaint,” wherein Rocio D. Jacobo was named in lieu of Doe 1
and Esmeralda Jacobo was named in lieu of Doe 2. 

Pending before the Court is Jacobo’s motion to quash
service of summons and complaint. 

II.      Legal
Standard

“A
defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any
further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a
notice of motion. . . (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of
jurisdiction of the court over him or her. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10.)

“When a defendant challenges the court’s personal
jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process the burden is on the
plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the
facts requisite to an effective service.” (Summers v. McClanahan (2006)
140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413 [quotation marks and citation omitted].)

III.     Discussion

Jacobo specially
appears and moves the Court for an order quashing service of the summons and
complaint.

Jacobo’s
motion is summarily denied. Although Jacobo represents, in his notice of
motion, that the instant motion is made on the grounds that service was
improper or incomplete, a review of his accompanying memorandum of points and
authorities reflects that Jacobo is instead arguing that he “was not the
registered owner of the vehicle and not involved in the collision,” that “[h]is
wife and daughter were the registered owners,” and that “[t]he summons and
complaint were served on an improper party.” (Motion, 3:27-4:2.)

These
arguments are not the proper subject of a motion to quash summons and complaint,
which challenges the validity of service, not whether or not a defendant
is a properly named party. Jacobo’s reliance Kline v. Beauchamp (1938)
29 Cal.App.2d 340 is misplaced; in that case, plaintiff filed a complaint
naming Dr. C. Beauchamp and Dr. George Simms as the sole defendants; there was
no reference therein to either fictitious defendants or defendants whose names
were unknown. Dr. Vernon T. Kelley was then served with the summons and
complaint in the case, which he moved to quash. The trial court granted
Kelley’s motion to quash and concurrently denied plaintiff’s motion for leave
to amend the complaint by alleging that the true name of Dr. George Simms was
Vernon T. Kelley and that Vernon T. Kelley was sometimes known as Dr. George
Simms. The appellate court affirmed the trial court’s order granting the motion
to quash, determining, inter alia, that “the proper procedure for attacking
the validity of a summons which has been served upon a person who is not a
party to an action is by a motion to quash the service thereof.” (Id. at
342.)



























































Jacobo’s reliance on Fuss
v. City of Los Angeles (1958) 162 Cal.App.2d 643 is also misplaced; there,
the appellate court affirmed the trial court’s order quashing service of the
summons on a corporation that had not been designated as a party to the action.
Jacobo, by way of contrast, is a named defendant in Plaintiff’s complaint.
