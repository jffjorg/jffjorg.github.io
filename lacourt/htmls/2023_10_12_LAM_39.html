DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV20862    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 39

Outsource, LLC v. Horizon Communications Technologies, Inc., et al.

Case No. 20STCV20862

Petition for Denial of Third-Party Claim

 

            Petitioner Outsource, LLC (“Petitioner”) petitions the Court for summary denial of the third-party claim by Sunwest Bank.  A judgment creditor may direct a levying officer to satisfy a writ of execution on property in which a third party holds an interest.  (Associated Oil Co. v. Mullin (1930) 110 Cal.App. 385, 392.)  The Court may determine the rights of the third party before the property is applied to satisfaction of the judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 720.010.)  The third-party claim constitutes the claimant’s pleading, and the judgment creditor's statement opposing a claim constitutes the creditor's pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 720.350, subd. (a).)  The third party must submit a copy of any writing on which a claim is based, including any security agreement and any financing statement.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 720.130, subd. (b), 720.230, subd. (b).)  The third party has the burden of proof at the hearing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 720.360.)

 

In Outsource, LLC v. Horizon Communications Technologies, Inc. (Cal. Ct. App., Mar. 23, 2023, No. B314438) 2023 WL 2605405, the Court of Appeal found that Sunwest Bank’s third-party claim to funds in a bank deposit account is not entitled to priority over Petitioner’s interest.  Thus, Sunwest Bank’s third-party claim has been adjudicated. 

 

In opposition, Sunwest Bank argues that the Court of Appeal’s decision does not preclude Sunwest Bank from filing a new third-party claim.  Sunwest Bank is incorrect.  As the Court of Appeal stated, “Sunwest asserts that the appropriate remedy is to remand the matter to give Sunwest an opportunity to conduct discovery to obtain evidence establishing that governing local law.  But Sunwest is not entitled to that second bite at the apple.”  (Outsource, LLC v. Horizon Communications Technologies, Inc. (Cal. Ct. App., Mar. 23, 2023, No. B314438) 2023 WL 2605405, at *6.)  This statement by the Court of Appeal, which is binding on this Court, resolves the issue.

 

Based upon the foregoing, the Court orders as follows:

 

1.         The Court grants Petitioner’s petition and denies Sunwest Bank’s third-party claim.  Judgment shall be entered in favor of Petitioner and the funds being held by the Santa Clara County Sheriff’s Department shall be released and disbursed to Petitioner.

 

2.         The Court signs the proposed order, lodged on September 18, 2023.

 

3.         Petitioner’s counsel shall provide notice and file proof of such with the Court.

Darren Fuller v. Jimmy Lee Hampton, et al.

Case No. 23STCV09279

Demurrer and Case Management Conference

BACKGROUND

 

            Plaintiff Darren Fuller (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Jimmy Lee and Ida Hampton (collectively, “Defendants”), asserting causes of action for quiet title, declaratory relief, unjust enrichment, and quantum meruit.  This action relates to two properties: (1) A property located at 15724 Brayton Street in Paramount, California 90723 (the “Brayton Property”); and (2) A property located at 137 West 111th Place in Los Angeles, California 90061 (the “111th Place Property”).  Defendants demur to each cause of action and move to strike the prayer for attorney’s fees.  The Court sustains the demurrer to the first and fourth causes of action (quiet title) without leave to amend.  The Court sustains the demurrer to the third, sixth, and seventh causes of action with leave to amend.  The Court overrules the demurrer to the second and fifth causes of action without prejudice to Defendants demurring to these causes of action in any amended complaint.  The Court grants the motion to strike with leave to amend.     

 

PLAINTIFF’S ALLEGATIONS

 

In 2000, Plaintiff wanted to purchase the Brayton Property but could not qualify for a loan.  (Complaint, ¶ 11.)  Therefore, Defendants, who are Plaintiff’s in-laws, purchased the property in their names.  (Complaint, ¶ 13.)  Plaintiff made the down payment and paid all closing costs, commissions, and fees for the Brayton Property.  (Complaint, ¶ 14.)  Since 2000, Plaintiff has paid all of the mortgage payments, taxes, insurance, utilities, and maintenance/repair costs for the Brayton Property.  (Complaint, ¶ 15.)  Plaintiff has his family have resided at the Brayton Property since 2000.  (Complaint, ¶ 16.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants agreed to deed the Brayton Property to him when he would be eligible to hold title in his own name, but failed to do so.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 13, 30.)      

 

Plaintiff also alleges as follows: In or about 1989, Defendant Jimmy Lee Hampton inherited the 111th Place Property, and in 2005, he conveyed the property to himself and his wife.  (Complaint, ¶ 39.)  Shortly thereafter, Plaintiff made an oral agreement with Defendants that he would rehabilitate the property, and in return, Defendants would sell him the property for $450,000.  (Complaint, ¶ 40.)  Plaintiff spent over $200,000 rehabilitating the property, and secured tenants, so the property produces income.  (Complaint, ¶ 42.)  Plaintiff also paid $5,000 as a down-payment.  (Complaint, ¶ 40.)  However, Defendants have not honored their agreement.  (Complaint, ¶ 43.)

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A.        Demurrer

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)  However, courts do not accept as true deductions, contentions, or conclusions of law or fact.  (Stonehouse Homes LLC v. City of Sierra Madre (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 531, 538.)  The general rule is that the plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts, not evidentiary facts.  (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)  “[D]emurrers for uncertainty are disfavored, and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.”  (Lickiss v. Fin. Indus. Regulatory Auth. (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1125, 1135.)  In addition, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, courts strictly construe a demurrer for uncertainty “because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.”  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Demurrers do not lie as to only parts of causes of action, where some valid claim is alleged but “must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained.” (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)  

 

            B.        Motion to Strike

 

Courts may, upon a motion, or at any time in their discretion, and upon terms they deem proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) Courts may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436, subd. (b).)  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., §¿437.)  

 

DISCUSSION

 

            A.        Quiet Title Claims – First and Fourth Causes of Action

 

            Plaintiff cannot maintain a cause of action for quiet title because he concedes he is not a legal owner of the property.  (Lewis v. Superior Court (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 1850, 1866.)  “[W]hatever interest [Plaintiff] might have is only equitable, and the holder of equitable title cannot maintain a quiet title action against the legal owner.”  (Ibid., citing Stafford v. Ballinger (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 289, 294-295.)  Therefore, the Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the first and fourth causes of action.  The Court denies leave to amend, as no amendment would be successful.

 

            B.        Unjust Enrichment Claims – Third and Sixth Causes of Action

 

            Plaintiff asserts unjust enrichment claims.  However, there is no independent cause of action for “unjust enrichment” in California.  (See, e.g., McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 387-388.)  Rather, it appears that Plaintiff is asserting causes of action for an oral breach of contract, and the Court will analyze these causes of action accordingly.

 

            1.         The Brayton Property

 

            Defendant argues that the statute of fraud bars claims with respect to the Brayton Property.  The statute of frauds provides that a contract that falls under it is invalid unless memorialized in writing, and signed by the party against whom the contract is to be enforced.  (Civ. Code, § 1624.)  In this case, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants agreed to hold title for the benefit of Plaintiff until Plaintiff qualified to hold title on his own.  In essence, Plaintiff alleges he had an option to purchase the property from Defendants.  Such an agreement is subject to the statute of frauds.  (See Robert T. Miner, M.D., Inc. v. Tustin Avenue Investors, LLC (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 264, 270.)  However, Plaintiff alleges that he paid the entire down payment to the home, the closing costs, all mortgage payments, property taxes, property insurance premiums, all and maintenance and repair costs.  Plaintiff also alleges that he was in possession of the property at all times.  Partial performance of an oral real property sale contract takes the contract out of the statute of frauds.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1972, subd. (a).)

           

            Defendants also argue that the statute of limitations bars this cause of action.  Under the delayed discovery rule, a claim does not accrue until “a plaintiff knew or should have known of the wrongful conduct at issue.”  (April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 832.)  In this case, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants agreed to deed the property to him when he would be eligible to hold title in his own name, so the statute of limitations would begin to run once Plaintiff knew or should have known that he was eligible to hold title in his own name.  The complaint does not allege any facts on this issue. 

 

            Based upon the foregoing, the Court sustains the demurrer to the third cause of action.  The Court grants leave to amend so Plaintiff may assert a breach of contract claim based upon the Brayton property and allege sufficient facts to demonstrate that the action is timely.    

 

            2.         The 111th Place Property

 

Defendants argue that the statute of fraud bars claims with respect to the 111th Place Property.  The statute of frauds provides that a contract that falls under it is invalid unless memorialized in writing, and signed by the party against whom the contract is to be enforced.  (Civ. Code, § 1624.)  In this case, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants agreed to sell him the 111th Place Property after he rehabilitated it.  In essence, Plaintiff alleges he had an option to purchase the property from Defendants upon completion of the work.  Such an agreement is subject to the statute of frauds.  (See Robert T. Miner, M.D., Inc. v. Tustin Avenue Investors, LLC (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 264, 270.)  However, Plaintiff alleges that he paid $5,000 as a down-payment and spent over $200,000 rehabilitating the property.  Partial performance of an oral real property sale contract takes the contract out of the statute of frauds.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1972, subd. (a).)

 

            Defendants also argue that the statute of limitations bars this cause of action.  Under the delayed discovery rule, a claim does not accrue until “a plaintiff knew or should have known of the wrongful conduct at issue.”  (April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 832.)  In this case, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants agreed to sell him the property once the rehabilitation work was completed.  However, the complaint does not allege any facts on that issue. 

 

            Based upon the foregoing, the Court sustains the demurrer to the sixth cause of action.  The Court grants leave to amend so Plaintiff may assert a breach of contract claim based upon the Brayton property and allege sufficient facts to demonstrate that the action is timely.   

 

C.        Declaratory Relief Claims – Second and Fifth Causes of Action

 

Plaintiff’s second and fifth causes of action seek a declaratory judgment concerning Plaintiff’s rights and duties with respect to the two properties under Code of Civil Procedure section 1060.  The Court agrees with Defendants: A declaratory relief action is inappropriate when a plaintiff has an adequate remedy on other causes of action at trial.  (See Hood v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 319, 324; California Ins. Guarantee Assn. v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1617, 1624.) 

 

            The declaratory relief statute should not be used for the purpose of anticipating and determining an issue which can be determined in the main action.  The object of the statute is to afford a new form of relief where needed and not to furnish a litigant with a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues.

 

(General of America Ins. Co. v. Lilly (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 465, 470.)  The Court has discretion to decline to issue a declaratory judgment under these circumstances.  (See AICCO, Inc. v. Insurance Company of North America (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 579, 590.) 

 

            However, at this point, the Court overrules the demurrer because it is unclear whether Plaintiff’s breach of contract claims will survive the next demurrer.  This order is without prejudice to Defendant demurring to these causes of action in the context of the first amended complaint. 

 

            D.        Quantum Meruit – Seventh Cause of Action

 

            Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action is for quantum meruit, which seeks recovery of the reasonable value of services rendered.  (See Pacific Bay Recovery, Inc. v. California Physicians’ Services, Inc. (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 200, 214.)  The statute of limitations for the claim is two years.  (See Iverson, Yoakum, Papiano & Hatch v. Berwald (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 990, 996.)  Plaintiff does not allege that he provided any services in connection with the 111th Place Property in the two years preceding the complaint.  Nor does he allege facts suggesting that the seventh cause of action is otherwise timely.  Therefore, the demurrer to the seventh cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            E.         Motion to Strike

 

            Defendants seek to strike the prayer for attorney’s fees.  Plaintiff alleges no basis for attorney’s fees in the complaint.  Therefore, the motion to strike is granted with leave to amend. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Based upon the foregoing, the Court orders as follows:

 

1.         The demurrer is sustained in part and overruled in part.

 

2.         The Court sustains the demurrer to the first and fourth causes of action without leave to amend. 

 

3.         The Court sustains the demurrer to the third, sixth, and seventh causes of action with leave to amend.  The Court prohibits Plaintiff from re-asserting causes of action for unjust enrichment but authorizes Plaintiff to assert causes of action for breach of contract.

 

4.         The Court overrules the demurrer to the second and fifth causes of action without prejudice to Defendants demurring to these causes of action in any amended complaint. 

 

5.         The Court grants the motion to strike with leave to amend.

 

6.         Plaintiff shall file a first amended complaint within thirty (30) days.

 

7.         The Court continues the case management conference to January 31, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  The Court authorizes Defendants to notice any demurrer and motion to strike regarding the first amended complaint for hearing on that date/time. 

 

8.         Defendants’ counsel shall provide notice and file proof of such with the Court.


Case Number: 23STCV11460    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 39

Paul Kim v. Airstream, Inc.

Case No. 23STCV11460

Motion to Stay

 

            Plaintiff Paul Kim (“Plaintiff”) filed this case under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act against Airstream, Inc. (“Defendant”).  Plaintiff purchased the Airstream in California.  However, Plaintiff signed a limited warranty with a forum selection clause that states:

 

“Exclusive jurisdiction for deciding legal disputes relating to alleged breach of express warranty and breach of implied warranties arising by operation of law as well as those relating to representations of any nature rests in the courts within the state of manufacture, which is Ohio.  Also, this limited warranty shall be interpreted and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of Ohio.  Any and all claims, controversies, and causes of action arising out of or relating to this limited warranty, whether sounding [in] contract, tort, or statute, shall be governed by the laws of the State of Ohio, including its statute of limitations, without giving effect to any conflict of law rules that would result in the application of the laws of a different jurisdiction.”

 

(Declaration of Rick March, Exh. A, p. 2.)

 

            The problem with this forum selection clause is that it applies Ohio law, “without giving any effect to conflict of law rules that would result in the application of the laws of a different jurisdiction.”  This is a problem because the Airstream was purchased in California, and the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act cannot be waived.  (See Civ. Code, § 1790.1.)  Recognizing this problem, Defendants have stipulated that the Ohio court shall “utilize the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act to adjudicate those allegations.”  (Declaration of James R. Robertson, ¶ 5.)  The Court grants the motion, conditioned on Defendant—and the Ohio court—applying the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act to this dispute.  The Court finds that striking the waiver provision cures the unconscionability. 

 

The remainder of Plaintiff’s arguments are not persuasive.  Per California law, a forum-selection clause is presumptively valid, and is enforceable unless the opposing party meets the “heavy burden” of proving enforcement would be unreasonable under the circumstances of the case.  (Bancomer S.A. v. Superior Court (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1450, 1457.)  Where a contract contains a forum-selection clause that requires litigation to proceed exclusively in one particular forum, the court will enforce the clause unless it is unreasonable.  (Animal Film, LLC v. D.E.J. Productions, Inc. (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 466, 471.)  “A clause is reasonable if it has a logical connection with at least one of the parties or their transaction.”  (Verdugo v. Alliantgroup, L.P. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 141, 147.)  Defendant manufactures its vehicles in Ohio.  As such, the forum-selection clause is reasonable.

 

            Plaintiff’s counsel suggests that his client did not freely and voluntarily agree to the forum selection clause because he did not read it.  Plaintiff’s declaration does not support that argument.  (Declaration of Paul Kim, ¶¶ 6-8.)  Clearly, he received the warranty and signed it.  (See Declaration of Rick March, Exh. A.)  The Court has considered Plaintiff’s counsel’s remaining arguments and finds none to be persuasive.

 

            Based upon the forgoing, the Court orders as follows:

 

            1.         The Court strikes the provision stating that Ohio law shall apply to this dispute, regardless of Ohio’s choice-of-law provisions.

 

            2.         The Court grants the motion on the condition that the Ohio court applies the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act in its entirety (e.g., the law governing what is a defect, the law governing what is a reasonable number of repair attempts, etc.)    

 

            3.         The Court advances and vacates all dates and stays this case.

 

4.         This order is without prejudice to a motion to lift the stay if the Ohio court refuses to apply the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act in its entirety.  (See Investors Equity Life Holding Co. v. Schmidt (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1376.)

 

5.         The Court sets an Order to Show Cause why this case should not be dismissed.  The hearing shall be on May 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  The Court orders Plaintiff’s counsel to file a status report on or before May 2, 2024.  The Court provides notice: If Plaintiff’s counsel fails to file a status report and fails to appear at the hearing, either remotely or in-person, absent good cause, the Court will assume this matter has been resolved in Ohio and will dismiss this case with prejudice.

 

6.         The Court’s clerk shall provide notice.
