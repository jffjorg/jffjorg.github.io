DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 PLEASE NOTE:    

The parties are encouraged to meet and confer concerning this tentative ruling to determine if there is an agreement to submit.  

Regardless of whether there is any such agreement, each party who wishes to submit must send an email to the Court at SSCdept30@LACourt.org indicating the party's intention to submit. 

Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the case number in the subject line of the email and in the body provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, or non-party.  

If a party submits but still intends to appear at the hearing, include the words "SUBMITS BUT WILL APPEAR" in the subject line of the email. 

If the Court does not receive emails from the parties indicating submission on this tentative ruling and there are no appearances at the hearing, the Court may, at its discretion, adopt the tentative as the final order or place the motion off calendar. 

Unless all the parties have submitted, the Court will hear argument from any party that appears at the hearing and wishes to argue. The Court may change its tentative as a result of the argument and adopt the changed tentative as the final order at the end of that hearing, even if all the parties are not present. 

Be advised that after the Court has posted/issued a tentative ruling, the Court has the inherent authority to prohibit the withdrawal of said motion and may adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court.     


Case Number: 19STCV21811    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: 30

EARL HACKETT, JR. vs ISB TRANSPORT LTD., et al.

Motion to Continue the Trial Date


TENTATIVE

Defendant’s motion to continue trial and all related deadlines is DENIED.  Moving party is ordered to give notice.

DISCUSSION

Given the age of the case, the currently scheduled discovery, and the prejudice to the opposing side, the Court finds that no good cause exists to continue the trial date. Enough time exists for all parties to be ready for trial. Further, both sides are not equally enthusiastic about mediating. Thus, the Court puts little weight on the possibility of mediation as ground to continue the trial in this aged case.

Case Number: 20STCV24350    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: 30

GILBERT DORSEY, AN INDIVIDUAL vs ROBERTO TAPIA GUZMAN, AN INDIVIDUAL, et al.

Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories
Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories
Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents

TENTATIVE

Defendant's Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories, and Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents are granted.  As to each request, Plaintiff is ordered to serve verified responses, without objections, within 20 days. Requests for sanctions are denied. Moving party to give notice. 

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff did not respond to Defendant Bryant's discovery demands served on October 17, 2022. Bryant also states he granted Plaintiff a thirty-day extension to serve responses, reached out to Plaintiff multiple times, and attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff. Still, Plaintiff failed to serve responses. A party waives objections to a discovery request when it does not serve a timely response to the request.  CCP 2030.290(a). As the motions are unopposed, the Court declines to award sanctions.

TINNA JONSDOTTIR vs HONGSHI SUN, et al.

Motion for Order Requiring Plaintiff's Mental Examination

TENTATIVE

Motion for Order Requiring Mental Examination of Plaintiff is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to sit for mental examination within 30 days of notice of ruling.  Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION

Intervenor Indian Harbor Insurance Company moves to have Plaintiff submit to a mental examination by Ari Kalechstein, Ph.D.

Plaintiff’s complaint includes emotional distress and depression since the date of the accident in 2019. As indicated in her deposition, Plaintiff is making a claim for damage to her psychological or psychiatric condition. Thus, Intervenor moves under CCP § 2032.310 to have Plaintiff submit to a mental examination performed by Ari Kalechstein, Ph.D. This examination is to “assist counsel and the trier of fact in understanding the origin, nature, and severity of plaintiff’s alleged difficulties/impairment vis-à-vis cognition, emotion, and/or behavior.” (Motion 9: 21-23.)

In Opposition, Plaintiff argues that a neuropsychological examination will violate Plaintiff’s privacy rights and is unlikely to lead to admissible evidence. Specifically, Plaintiff argues that her cognitive ability “has not been placed in controversy, and thus, a neuropsychological exam would violate her right to privacy by forcing her to…reveal private information…that is not necessary to Defendant’s needs in conducting litigation.” (Opp. 6: 23-26.) The type of exam requested by Intervenor is unnecessary, invasive, and is wholly unrelated to the current matter. However, Plaintiff would be amenable and stipulate to a standard psychological examination.

While it is obvious that Plaintiff's IQ is not relevant, the Court finds that a neuropsychological examination is appropriate to address the breadth of complaints that the Plaintiff has made regarding her mental status post-accident. “The only statutorily authorized justification for ordering a mental examination is that the ‘mental condition’ of the examinee is ‘in controversy.’” (Doyle v. Superior Court (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1878, 1886.) In Doyle, the Court found that because the plaintiff did not allege any current mental injury, a mental examination was not relevant. Here, however, the FAC indicates that Plaintiff’s injuries “have caused, and continue to cause Plaintiff physical, mental and nervous pain, suffering and anguish…” (FAC ¶¶ 10, 17, 23.) Thus, her mental state is directly in controversy. While Plaintiff claims that a neuropsychological examination is evasive, not all of the examinations may be administered if Plaintiff's history does not indicate them. (Reply 3: 2-5.) Therefore, given the fact that Plaintiff has indicated a claim for continuing mental pain, as well as the evidence that Plaintiff utilized psychiatrist in the past as a result of the June 2019 accident (Exhibits 3-4, filed 10/6/2023), the Court finds that a neuropsychological exam is proper.

TIMOTHY MOSES, AN INDIVIDUAL vs LA OLYMPIC HOTEL, UNKNOWN ENTITY, et al.

Motion to Quash Service of Summons


TENTATIVE

The Motion to Squash Service of Summons and Complaint is GRANTED.  Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION

Defendant asserts that the proof of service filed with the Court is false.

Firstly, Defendant states that although his home address is 2796 Creston Drive in Los Angeles, the home is part of a gated community that is difficult to access. (Declaration of James Tuggle, hereinafter, “Tuggle Dec.”, ¶ 8.) Defendant states that neither himself nor any other occupant of the home was notified of a visitor at the times and dates stated on the proofs of service, which are: 8:58pm on February 7, 2023 and 8:58am on February 12. (See Motion, Exh. 1.) Second, Defendant claims the description of the person served within the proofs of service is inaccurate. The proof of service describes Defendant as an 80-year-old Caucasian male, weighing 160 pounds, standing at 5 feet 11 inches, and having white hair. (Id.) Defendant states that he is heavier than 160 pounds, stands at 5 feet 6 inches, and has gray hair. (Tuggle Dec., ¶ 10.)

Third, Defendant argues that in addition to failing to serve him personally, Plaintiff made no effort to use other manners of service like serving Defendant at his place of business at 725 S Westlake Ave., Los Angeles, CA 90057, where a business manager would have been present to accept service. (Tuggle Dec., ¶ 7.) Finally, it appears that Plaintiff waited over a year to attempt service, and Defendant states that the only manner in which he found out about the instant case was the Request for Entry of Default mailed to his home on May 22, 2023. The instant Motion was filed three days later, with no opposition following.

“When a motion to quash is properly brought, the burden of proof is placed upon the plaintiff to establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence.” Aquila, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 568. Here, Plaintiff has filed no opposition and thus does not meet its burden to this regularly noticed motion.

CLIFSHEENA YEARWOOD vs LULA MAE MOUTON, et al.

Motion to Compel IME of Plaintiff

TENTATIVE

The Motion to Compel Plaintiff to submit to a physical examination is GRANTED. Plaintiff must submit to a physical examination within 30 days. Request for Sanction is DENIED.  Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Mouton moves to compel Plaintiff to comply with a demand for a physical examination. An examination was scheduled for May 17, 2023, with Dr. Nick Shamie, M.D., an orthopedic surgeon. However, Plaintiff did not appear for the exam. Defendant argues that good cause exists to comply compliance because Plaintiff’s failure to attend the examination has “prevented preparation for trial” and the defense “cannot analyze the possibility of settlement.” (Motion 4: 13-16.)

As of October 26, 2023, no opposition has been filed.

The Court finds that good cause exists to compel Plaintiff to submit to a medical examination. The complaint alleges that Plaintiff sustained serious bodily injuries due to the metal security gate falling on top of Plaintiff. (Comp. ¶ 19-21.) As Plaintiff has put her physical well-being in controversy for this matter, the Court finds that a physical examination is appropriate. Therefore, based on CCP § 2032.250, Plaintiff must attend a medical. Defendant attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff to resolve the issue, specifically as to why Plaintiff failed to attend the May 2023 examination, but it was not resolved. (Dec. Leeper, ¶ 7 Ex. D.) Thus, Defendant is in compliance with CCP § 2032.250. As this motion is unopposed, the Court declines to award sanctions.

Case Number: 23STCV12034    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: 30

KRISTOPHER KATALARIS vs BIRD RIDES, INC., et al.

Motion to Compel Arbitration

TENTATIVE

The Motion to Compel Arbitration filed by Defendant Bird Rides, Inc., is DENIED.  Moving party to give notice. 

DISCUSSION

The parties’ contentions surround whether the arbitration agreement (the “Agreement”) is enforceable because Plaintiff was a minor when he agreed to the terms of the Agreement. The following facts are uncontested by the parties: (1) Plaintiff was a minor when he entered the Agreement (Opposition Papers, 3:3-12.), (2) Plaintiff misrepresented his age (Motion, 2:5-6), and (3) Plaintiff executed the Agreement in his father’s name. (Id.)

Defendant’s Motion is silent as to whether the Agreement is enforceable against a minor who misrepresented their age upon executing the Agreement. Defendant makes clear that they have met their burden of (a) demonstrating the existence of an arbitration agreement and (b) establishing that the opposing party refuses to arbitrate. However, the question of whether the Agreement is enforceable against a minor who misrepresented his age and misrepresented the identity of the person assenting to the contract, still remains.

Plaintiff’s contentions on this inquiry are three-fold. First, Plaintiff argues CIV § 1556 which states: “All persons are capable of contracting, except minors, persons of unsound mind, and persons deprived of civil rights.” Plaintiff concedes that there was a binding arbitration agreement, however, Plaintiff argues that he did not have the requisite competency to enter into the Agreement because of his age. Second, Plaintiff argues that the fraud, which Plaintiff concedes he perpetrated, voids the Agreement, citing Duffens v. Valenti (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 434, 448. Third, Plaintiff argues that the Agreement cannot be enforced against the father because there was no authorization for the minor to enter into the Agreement using the father’s name, nor did the father assent to the Agreement. The Court agrees with all three contentions and denies the instant Motion.

Principally, CIV § 1556 is clear that minors do not have competency to enter into a contract. The question is whether the misrepresentations made by the minor Plaintiff changes this analysis. The Court believes they do not. (See Williams v. Leon T. Shettler Co. (1929) 98 Cal.App. 282, 283; Lee v. Hibernia Sav. & Loan Soc. (1918) 177 Cal. 656, 660; and 1 Witkin, Summary 11th Contracts § 27 (2023) [“It has been held in California that no estoppel arises against the minor by reason of his or her misrepresentation of age.”].)
