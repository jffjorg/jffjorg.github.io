[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO LIFT ARBITRATION STAY

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 17, 2024

 

On July 5, 2022, the Court ordered the parties to arbitration and stayed the entire action pending the conclusion of the arbitration.

On September 20, 2024, Plaintiff Autumn Yancey-Estime filed a motion to lift the stay and proceed in court because Defendant Tom Harrison waived his right to proceed in arbitration.

In a consumer arbitration that requires the drafting party pay certain fees and costs, the drafting party is in material breach of the arbitration agreement if the fees or costs required to continue the arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days after the due date.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98, subd. (a)(1).)  If the drafting party materially breaches the arbitration agreement and is in default, the consumer may unilaterally elect to withdraw the claim from arbitration and proceed in court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98, subd. (b).)

A defendant may be in material breach of the arbitration agreement even though the delay in payment was inadvertent, brief, and did not prejudice the plaintiff.  (Espinoza v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 761, 771.)  Section 1281.97 “statutorily defines a material breach as a matter of law to be the failure to pay anything less than the full amount due by the expiration of the statutory grace period.”  (Gallo v. Wood Ranch USA, Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 621, 644.)

Plaintiff argues that Defendant is in material breach of the arbitration agreement by failing to timely pay the arbitration fees.  On September 22, 2023, JAMS issued a notice of hearing and an invoice with a balance of $88,200.00, due upon receipt.  (Horacek Decl. ¶ 4 & Ex. A.)  Following the notice of bankruptcy filed by former defendants Vanguard Management Group LLC and David Guc, JAMS entered a Notice of Administrative Stay.  (Horacek Decl. ¶ 5.)  Defendant did not pay the fees, so on August 14, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel wrote a letter withdrawing Plaintiff from arbitration.  (Horacek Decl. ¶ 6 & Ex. B.)  As of September 19, 2024, the arbitration fees remained unpaid.  (Horacek Decl. ¶ 7 & Ex. C.)

Defendant did not file a full opposition, but he did file a declaration.  Defendant states that his former co-defendants Vanguard Management Group LLC and David Guc filed for bankruptcy on October 20, 2023, which automatically stayed all proceedings.  (Harrison Decl. ¶ 3.)  He is “informed and believe[s] that the arbitration hearing dates were then vacated by JAMS, the arbitration was placed on an administrative stay and that no fees were due to be paid until 90 days before new arbitration hearing dates were set by JAMS.”  (Harrison Decl. ¶ 3.)  According to Defendant, no fees are due and a conference with the arbitrator is scheduled for November 4, 2024.  (Harrison Decl. ¶¶ 3-4.)

It is undisputed that Defendant failed to pay the September 22, 2023 invoice.  Vanguard Management Group LLC’s and David Guc’s bankruptcy proceedings did not relieve Defendant from this obligation.  “Significantly, ‘the automatic stay of judicial proceedings against a debtor in bankruptcy does not apply to nondebtor codefendants.’  [Citation.]”  (Higgins v. Superior Court (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 973, 979.)  And regardless of any bankruptcy stay, the June 27, 2024 Status Conference Report indicated that the bankruptcy proceedings had ended because the matters were either discharged or closed.

Because Defendant materially breached the arbitration agreement, Plaintiff’s motion to lift the stay and proceed in this court is GRANTED.

On September 12, 2024, Defendant California L&M Investments, Inc. filed a motion to bifurcate Plaintiff Jose Mendez’s request for punitive damages.

The Court may order a separate trial of any cause of action or any separate issue in furtherance of convenience or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (b).)  The Court may also order the trial of an issue to precede the trial on another issue.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 598.)  “The court shall, on application of any defendant, preclude the admission of evidence of that defendant’s profits or financial condition until after the trier of fact returns a verdict for plaintiff awarding actual damages and finds that a defendant is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud in accordance with [Civil Code] Section 3294.”  (Civ. Code, § 3295, subd. (d).)

Plaintiff “does not take issue with the idea of bifurcation and having a punitive damages phase generally,” but “it seems like a bit of overkill.”  (Opposition at p. 1.)  According to Plaintiff, Defendant appears to own a substantial number of buildings where Plaintiff claims to have worked during his employment, and he argues that “any order of bifurcation must be carefully crafted so as not to prohibit testimony regarding Defendant’s ownership of said buildings, even if it might implicate the financial state of the company.”  (Id. at pp. 1-2.)

As Defendant notes, “[Plaintiff’s] concerns are focused on controlling testimony, which are issues better raised in motions in limine.”  (Reply at p. 2.)  Defendant also contends that Plaintiff has no evidence about Defendant’s ownership of properties, so “any such evidence is mere speculation and not subject to this motion.”  (Reply at pp. 2-3.)

Because bifurcation is mandatory under these circumstances, the motion to bifurcate punitive damages is GRANTED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING IN PART AND OVERRULING IN PART DEMURRER

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 17, 2024

 

On March 1, 2023, Plaintiff PMH Laboratory, Inc. filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company and Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross.  The FAC alleges (1) violation of California’s Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”); (2) negligence; (3) unjust enrichment; (4) quantum meruit; (5) goods and services rendered; (6) money had and received; (7) open book account; (8) account stated; (9) violation of Health and Safety Code section 1342.2; and (10) declaratory and injunctive relief.  Plaintiff later stipulated to the dismissal of its tenth cause of action.

On January 19, 2024, Defendants each filed a demurrer.  The demurrers are identical aside from the names of the parties.

Plaintiff filed an omnibus opposition, and each Defendant filed a reply.  On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Omnibus Surreply.  Plaintiff did not seek leave of court to file this Surreply.  The Court reviewed the Surreply and concludes it would not alter the outcome on this matter in any event. Accordingly, the Court orders the Surreply STRICKEN for not being filed in conformity with the law or a court order.

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of two documents that were referenced in the FAC.  (Exhibits 1-2).  Because the FAC refers to these items, they are appropriate matters for judicial notice.  (Align Technology, Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 956 fn. 6.) The request is granted.

Defendants also request that the Court take judicial notice of two documents published on the California Department of Managed Health Care’s (“DMHC”) public website (Exhibits 3-4).  The Court may take judicial notice of DMHC’s acts as “[o]fficial acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments . . . of any state.”  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (c); Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 484 (Arce).)  The request is granted.

Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of the DMHC’s All Plan Letter (“APL”) regarding APL 23-021 – Payment of COVID Claims for COVID-19 Tests.  The request is granted.

DISCUSSION

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)  “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.”  (Arce, supra, 181 Cal.App.4th at p. 556.)

A.        There Is No Private Right of Action for Violation of Section 1342.2 (Ninth Cause of Action).

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot bring a claim for violation of Health and Safety Code section 1342.2 because it does not provide for a private right of action.  (Demurrer at pp. 5-6.)

“[T]he Knox–Keene Act expressly authorizes the DMHC to enforce the statute and does not include a parallel authorization for suits by private individuals, [but] private individuals can bring suit under the UCL for violations of the Knox–Keene Act.”  (Blue Cross of California, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 1237, 1250.)

Plaintiff argues that “[t]he Court should recognize §1342.2’s implied cause of action.”  (Opposition at p. 15.)  This is contrary to binding authority.

The demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

B.        Plaintiff Sufficiently Alleges Violation of the UCL (First Cause of Action).

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot plead that their conduct was unlawful or unfair under the UCL.  (Demurrer at p. 8.)

California’s UCL includes any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading advertising.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.)  The UCL embraces “anything that can properly be called a business practice and that at the same time is forbidden by law.”  (Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 180.)  “By proscribing any unlawful business practice, section 17200 borrows violations of other laws and treats them as unlawful practices that the unfair competition law makes independently actionable.”  (Ibid.; see Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969 [“Virtually any law can serve as the predicate for a section 17200 action.”].)

According to Defendants, “[d]espite PMH’s generalized allegation that it posted its ‘cash price,’ the judicially-noticeable facts say otherwise.”  (Demurrer at p. 8; see id. at pp. 3-4.)  Defendants cite Plaintiff’s website, but the Court cannot take judicial notice of the truth of the contents of the document.  Defendants argue that “PMH does not (and cannot) allege that its full, billed charges equate to 125% of Medicare.”  (Id. at p. 9.)  The Court cannot decide this issue on this demurrer.

Defendants also argue that Plaintiff impermissibly seeks damages.  (Demurrer at p. 9.)  Plaintiff seeks, in part, “preliminary and permanent injunctive relief that orders Defendants to cease this unfair competition,” as well as restitution  (FAC ¶ 112.)

The demurrer is overruled on this ground.

C.        Plaintiff Sufficiently Alleges Negligence (Second Cause of Action).

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants[’] conduct, including payment of certain claims for COVID Testing and Services submitted by PMH established a duty of care.”  (FAC ¶ 115.)  Defendants breached this duty “by denying payment and underpaying on claims submitted to them by PMH for COVID Testing and Services.”  (FAC ¶ 116.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendants either assumed a duty of care by “pay[ing] certain claims” submitted or was “under a statutory duty to comply with” the CARES Act and Section 1342.2 fails.  (Demurrer at p. 10.)  Defendants argue that “a payor does not have a general duty not to underpay.”  (Ibid.)

“[H]ealth care service plans owe a duty of care to noncontracting emergency service providers in entering into their initial delegation contracts with IPAs or other RBOs.”  (Centinela Freeman Emergency Medical Associates v. Health Net of California, Inc. (2016) 1 Cal.5th 994, 1019.)  At this demurrer stage, the Court cannot determine whether Defendants owed a duty.

The demurrer is overruled on this ground.

D.        Plaintiff May Plead a Claim For Unjust Enrichment Third Cause of Action).

Plaintiff alleges, “Under the belief that claims for COVID Testing and Services would be fully paid for and reimbursed by Defendants as required by federal and state law, PMH provided and continued to provide COVID Testing and Services to patients insured by Defendants, thereby conferring a benefit to Defendants.”  (FAC ¶ 125.)

Defendants argue that “California law does not recognize an independent cause of action for unjust enrichment.”  (Demurrer at p. 10.)

“The elements of an unjust enrichment claim are the ‘receipt of a benefit and [the] unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.’  [Citation.]”  (Peterson v. Cellco Partnership (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1593.)  Unjust enrichment is not a cause of action, but a court may construe a cause of action for unjust enrichment as a cause of action for quasi-contract seeking restitution.  (Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 231.)

The demurrer is overruled on this ground.

E.        Plaintiff Sufficiently Alleges Quantum Meruit and Goods & Services Rendered (Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action).

Defendants argue that Plaintiff “does not and cannot allege that Anthem requested or benefited from the COVID testing services at issue.”  (Demurrer at p. 11.) 

“To recover on a claim for the reasonable value of services under a quantum meruit theory, a plaintiff must establish both that he or she was acting pursuant to either an express or implied request for services from the defendant and that the services rendered were intended to and did benefit the defendant.”  (Ochs v. PacifiCare of California (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 794.)

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants knew and understood at all times that PMH was undertaking to perform beneficial COVID Testing and Services for individuals insured by them and that such COVID Testing and Services were not free.”  (FAC ¶ 128.)  Plaintiff “has enriched Defendants by performing requested services for the individuals insured by Defendants.”  (FAC ¶ 129.)  Additionally, “Defendants through their conduct, including through the payment of certain claims for COVID Testing and Services submitted by PMH, requested that PMH perform and continue to perform COVID Testing and Services for Defendants’ benefit,” and Plaintiff “performed COVID Testing and Services pursuant to Defendants’ implied request.”  (FAC ¶¶ 132-133.)  At the pleading stage, this sufficiently alleges Defendants’ benefit.

The demurrer is overruled on this ground.

F.         Plaintiff Does Not Sufficiently Allege Money Had and Received (Sixth Cause of Action).

“A cause of action for money had and received is stated if it is alleged the defendant ‘is indebted to the plaintiff in a certain sum “for money had and received by the defendant for the use of the plaintiff.”’  [Citation.]”  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff “fails to plead facts to show that Anthem received any money “which belongs to” PMH – let alone identify a specific sum of money.”  (Demurrer at pp. 12-13.)

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants, through payments made by their insureds, received money that was intended, in part, to be used for the benefit of PMH, an out-of-network health care provider that provided COVID Testing and Services to the insureds.”  (FAC ¶ 137.)  But Plaintiff does not allege a specific sum of money.

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.

G.        Plaintiff Does Not Sufficiently Allege Open Book Account or Account Stated (Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action).

An open book account is a detailed statement constituting the principal record of one or more transactions between a debtor and a creditor arising out of a contract or some fiduciary relation, showing the debits and credits, entered in the regular course of business, and kept in a reasonably permanent form and manner.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 337a.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff does not allege a fiduciary relationship or that Plaintiff was keeping an “account” or that Defendants agreed that any account reflected Anthem’s “indebtedness.”  (Demurrer at p. 14.)

Plaintiff alleges that it and Defendants “had financial transactions with each other involving the payment for health care services performed by PMH for the benefit of Defendants and their insureds.”  (FAC ¶ 141.)  Plaintiff kept an electronic account of the debits and credits, and Defendants owe an amount of not less than approximately $14,320,439.92.  (FAC ¶¶ 142-143.)  This does not allege a fiduciary relationship or Defendants’ agreement to its indebtedness.

The demurrer to the seventh cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

Similarly, on the eighth cause of action for account stated, Plaintiff does not allege an agreement on the amount due.  (See Opposition at p. 14; FAC ¶¶ 146-147.)  The demurrer to the eighth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer to the ninth cause of action is SUSTAINED with no leave to amend.

The demurrer to the sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

The demurrer is otherwise OVERRULED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEMURRER

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 17, 2024

 

On March 21, 2023, Plaintiff Isabel Rendon filed this action against Defendant Nice Kicks Holdings, LLC.

On May 31, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer.  The demurrer generally argues that Plaintiff is not a bona fide patron who was denied access to a brick-and-mortar location, and Plaintiff cannot allege intentional conduct.

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendant’s request for judicial notice of Plaintiff’s discovery responses in different actions (Motion, Exs. A-C) and orders and filings in other cases (Motion, Ex. E; Reply, Exs. A-B) is denied.  The documents are irrelevant to this action.

Defendant’s request for judicial notice of its locations listed on its website (Exhibit D) is denied.  Information on websites can be reasonably subject to dispute and are not proper subjects for judicial notice.  (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 888-889; see also Huitt v. Southern Cal. Gas Co. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1586, 1605 fn. 10.)

DISCUSSION

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)  “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.”  (Arce ex rel. Arce v. Kaiser Found. Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 556.)

Plaintiff’s sole cause of action is for violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act.  Under the Act, business establishments must provide people with disabilities with equal access to their accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, and services.  (Civ. Code, § 51.)  “A plaintiff can recover under the Unruh Civil Rights Act on two alternate theories: (1) a violation of the ADA (§ 51, subd. (f)); or (2) denial of access to a business establishment based on intentional discrimination.”  (Martinez v. San Diego County Credit Union (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 1048, 1059 (Martinez I).)

A.        Plaintiff Fails to Allege a Violation of the ADA.

The Complaint alleges that Defendant “owns and operates Nice Kicks retail locations in California,” and those locations constitute places of public accommodation.  (Complaint ¶ 9.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant’s website “is a service, privilege, and advantage and accommodation of Defendant’s services and physical retail locations” and “a service, privilege, advantage, and accommodation that is heavily integrated with these physical retail locations, and provides advertising and marketing information helpful to influencing consumers to visit and shop at its physical retail locations.”  (Complaint ¶ 13; see Complaint ¶¶ 10-11.)  Because of the website’s access barriers, Plaintiff has been deterred from visiting Defendant’s physical locations.  (Complaint ¶¶ 7, 24.)

However, Plaintiff’s Unruh violations are based on “Defendant’s denial of full and equal access to its Website, and therefore its products and services offered thereby.”  (Complaint ¶ 4; see Complaint ¶¶ 7, 24.)  “Plaintiff genuinely wants to avail herself of Defendant’s goods and services as offered on Defendant’s Website.”  (Complaint ¶ 8.)  The accessibility barriers prevented her from “effectively browsing for Defendant’s products, amenities and services, privileges, advantages, and accommodations that exist online unlike sighted users,” and she has been deterred “on a regular basis from accessing the Website.”  (Complaint ¶¶ 23-24.) 

A website cannot constitute a “place of public accommodation” in the context of Title III of the ADA.  (Martinez v. Cot’n Wash, Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 1026-1053 (Martinez II).)  “Under current law, we cannot read this phrase as including retail websites without any connection to a physical space.  The statutory language does not include a category that encompasses such websites . . . .”  (Id. at p. 1032.)

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant operates retail locations in California, “including a location at 862 S. Main St., Los Angeles, California.”  (Complaint ¶ 9.)  However, Plaintiff resides in Madera County, California.  (Complaint ¶ 7.)  There is no nexus between Plaintiff’s allegations about her use of the website and Defendant’s physical space.

Plaintiff also alleges that “Defendant is a retail business that: (1) offers services to the public via its Website; (2) has a discrete, standalone location or identity via its Website; and (3) provides significant amounts of information to consumers on its Website, which is equivalent to or even more information than what would be typically provided by either an on-site proprietor or its employees.”  (Complaint ¶ 11.)  Accordingly, it appears that Plaintiff cannot allege that Defendant is a “place of public accommodation” with a nexus to any physical space in the context of Title III without contradicting the Complaint’s allegations of a “discrete, standalone location or identity via its Website.”

The demurrer is sustained on this ground.

B.        Plaintiff Fails to Allege Intentional Discrimination.

“[A]bsent an ADA violation, the Unruh Act requires allegations supporting ‘“willful, affirmative misconduct”’ [citation] with the specific intent ‘to accomplish discrimination on the basis of [a protected trait].’  [Citation.]”  (Martinez II, supra, 81 Cal.App.5th at p. 1036.) 

The Complaint does allege that Defendant “has constructed a Website that is inaccessible to Plaintiff, knowingly maintains the Website in this inaccessible form, and has failed to take adequate actions to correct these barriers even after being notified of the discrimination that such barriers cause.”  (Complaint ¶ 28.)  Accepting these allegations as true, they are insufficient to establish the required intentional discrimination.  A plaintiff “cannot establish [a defendant’s] intent to discriminate by showing only that its website does not allow visually impaired individuals the same access available to those who are not visually impaired (i.e., a disparate effect of a neutral structure), [so] it follows that [the defendant’s] failure to address this disparate effect likewise cannot establish [the defendant’s] intent to discriminate”  (Martinez II, supra, 81 Cal.App.5th at p. 1036.)  This remains true even when the defendant fails to adequately respond to complaints about the discriminatory effects.  (Id. at p. 1037.)

The demurrer is sustained on this ground.

C.        Conclusion

The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.
