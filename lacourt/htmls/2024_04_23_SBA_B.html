DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV03393    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

MARLENE THOMAS,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22STCV03393

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

AEOLUS AIR CHARTER, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          April 23, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Marlene Thomas

Responding Party:                  Defendant Aeolus Air Charter, Inc.

Motion to Compel the Deposition of Aeolus PMQ (Categories 33-66) and Production of Documents

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED.  Defendant Aeolus is ordered to produce a person(s) most qualified as to Categories 47, 56-61, and 64.  Defendant’s objections lack merit.

Defendant is ordered to serve code-compliant supplemental responses as to their purported “amended” responses as to Nos. 9-11, 16-17, and 24-34 within twenty days.  As to No. 36, defendant is ordered to produce a privilege log within twenty days.

At the person most qualified deposition, defendant is ordered to produce documents responsive to Nos. 14-15, and 18 (as to Ryan Thomas only).

BACKGROUND

            On January 27, 2022, plaintiff Marlene Thomas, ind. and as successor in interest to Ryan Thomas, filed a wrongful death and survival action against Aeolus Air Charter, Inc. for (1) negligence, (2) products liability – strict, and (3) products liability – negligence.  

On November 29, 2022, the case was transferred from the PI Hub to Dept. M.

On December 27, 2022, the case was deemed related to 22VECV01878.

On January 27, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s motion to amend complaint to add punitive damages and add parties.

On January 30, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC adding Let’s Jett, Inc., Conner Jadwin, and Dave Ventrella as defendants.

            On January 9, 2024, the court denied defendant Conner Jadwin’s motion to quash education records.

            On March 26, 2024, the court denied defendants’ motion for summary adjudication.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            CCP §2025.450(a) provides:  “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”

CCP §2025.450(b) provides, “A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1)        The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2)        The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

CCP §2025.230 states:  “If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Marlene Thomas requests that the court compel the deposition of defendant Aeolus Air Charter, Inc.’s Person Most Qualified (“PMQ”) on Categories 47, 56-61, and 64, and Request for Production of Documents, Nos. 9-11, 14-15, 16-17, 18, 24-30, 31, 33, 34, and 36.

            Plaintiff explains that on March 1, 2024, plaintiff took the deposition of Aeolus/Let’s Jett’s current Director of Operations, Brian Gaines.  During that deposition, plaintiff inquired about the meaning of a document that referenced a “risk severity matrix” and “safety risk assessment,” which had been produced earlier.  Plaintiff asserts that Gaines testified that the document in question reflected a Safety Risk Assessment matrix, known as a Flight Risk Assessment Tool (“FRAT”).  Plaintiff contends that a FRAT is a tool used to assess the risk of a given flight, variable by variable, and ensure that appropriate plans are employed where necessary to mitigate risks, or in situations where the risk is too high for a given flight, ensure that the flight is not released altogether.  Although Gaines was unaware whether a FRAT assessment was done for the subject flight, he testified that the subject flight would have scored a 33, which would have fallen in the red risk, or “no-go zone,” and the flight would have been cancelled. 

            Plaintiff further explains that on March 5, 2024, plaintiff served a deposition notice on defendants for the PMK (Categories 33-66) for March 20, 2024, regarding multiple new topics plaintiff learned about in recent depositions, including categories relating to Aeolus’ use of the FRAT, the FRAT matrix in place at the time of the subject flight, and FRAT completed for the subject flight.  On March 14, 2024, defendant objected on the grounds that plaintiff had already deposed Aeolus’ CEO Conner Jadwin and that a further deposition was harassing, burdensome, and oppressive, and that the date was unilaterally selected.  On March 18, 2024, although counsel met and conferred, which resulted in eliminating several categories of testimony and document requests, the parties were unable to come to an agreement on the remainder of categories and their corresponding requests for production.

            Plaintiff argues that defendant’s objections have no merit and do not constitute grounds to refuse to produce a witness.  Plaintiff contends that she seeks to conduct a deposition “only on categories pivotal to liability and punitive conduct” of defendants, based on new information.  Plaintiff also asserts that counsel did inquire about the FRAT system as to two deponents but that they did not have requisite knowledge of the systems in place at the time of the subject flight.  Plaintiff further argues that good cause exists for the production of documents as set forth in the separate statement and Hunter Norton declaration, including that defendant has not produced a privilege log.

            In opposition, defendant contends that the parties did not meet and confer as to the categories and document requests.  Defendant asserts that the need to take the deposition of an Aeolus PMQ on the subject of FRAT is “based solely on the failure of plaintiff to ask the question that they frame in their motion,” that is, what, if any, criteria were added to the matrix as a result of the accident.  Further, defendant argues, the “new” topics are not new and a PMQ deposition “does not warrant the time, expense, and disruption to Let’s Jett’s business.”  Defendant also asserts that defendants searched JetInsight and no pre-flight forms were completed in connection with the subject flight, and that the subject flight was the only flight N605TR undertaken while the aircraft was leased to Aeolus.

            In reply, plaintiff asserts that counsel sufficiently met and conferred.  See further declaration of Andrew Owen.  Plaintiff also reiterates that the PMQ topics are necessary, and that a declaration would not suffice in place of a deposition.  Further, defendant has not shown that any of the categories are unduly burdensome or oppressive.  As for the categories involving FRAT, plaintiff reiterates that the deponent Gaines did not have knowledge.  As to the “Volaris” category, plaintiff asserts that testimony was unclear whether Volaris was the source of a pre-flight risk assessment tool utilized at the time of the subject incident, “which bears further discovery.”  As to JetInsight categories, defendant’s opposition contains representations that are not admissible, including that they caused a search to be conducted.  To the extent that Aeolus completed no such pre-flight forms for the subject flight, plaintiff argues that it is entitled to explore how, and why that occurred in deposition.  Plaintiff also asserts that defendant’s “responsive” separate statement fails to address plaintiff’s substantive arguments related to each objection and that defendant’s amended responses are not code-compliant.

            The categories at issue are:

            47.  Your company’s use of Volaris, including Volaris safety management system guidance, manuals, flight risk assessment tools, pre-flight risk assessments, on or before the

date of the subject flight.

56. Aeolus’s policies and procedures regarding the use of any Flight Risk Assessment Tool (FRAT) on or before the date of the subject flight.

57. The FRAT rubric and categories in place on the date of the subject flight.

58. The FRAT form completed for the subject flight.

59. Any and all pre-flight forms completed, or information entered, through JetInsight for the subject flight.

60. Any and all pre-flight forms completed, or information entered, through JetInsight for

flights in 2021.

            61. Any communications about the FRAT form completed for the subject flight.

64. Your use of IPreFlight, also referred to APG, for any flights on the Challenger 605.

 

The court finds that defendant’s objections lack merit.

 

The requests for production at issue are:

Nos. 9-11:  personnel files of Connor Jadwin, Takashi Yanagi, David Ventrella, seeking information relating to the competence of current and former Aeolus executives to run an air charter service, interview and hire pilots, and competence to write policies and procedures regarding same.

In their response to the separate statement, defendants state that they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

Nos. 14-15: documents relating to company’s use of Volaris, prior to 2023 and on or before the date of fatal crash.

Plaintiff asserts that defendants can point only to a SMS manual, “that has nothing to do with Volaris,” which is not responsive.

Nos. 16-17:  documents on Aeolus’ quarterly safety meeting and board meeting minutes.

In their response to the separate statement, defendants state that they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

No. 18: documents reflecting capital contributions to company. 

In their response, defendants assert that if plaintiff seeks capital contributions to establish whether Ryan Thomas was a managing agent, then the request should be narrowed, and that “all other contributions are irrelevant and barred by third parties right to privacy.”

Plaintiff asserts that to the extent defendants claim that Ryan Thomas was a partner, or owner of the company, “evidence of other capital contributions is relevant to such classification.”

Nos. 24-29:  documents concerning Aeolus’ use of any flight risk assessment tool, or FRAT, whether through JetInsight or another program at or around time of subject flight.

In their response to the separate statement, defendants state that they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

Plaintiff asserts that defendants’ response that they completed a search relating to pre-flight and FRAT forms existent at the time of the subject incident is “evasive, contradictory to its own officer’s prior testimony.”  Plaintiff contends that defendants’ opposition allude to factors being “added” to the matrix following the subject flight, “which necessarily requires a pre-existing matrix to exist.”

In their response to the separate statement, defendants state that they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

As to No. 31, in their response to the separate statement, defendants contend that they have produced all training programs on several occasions, but that as it deals with training programs filed with, and approved by the FAA, they do not exist because the training programs were not filed with the FAA.  Thus, they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

As to No. 33, in their response to the separate statement, defendant states that in regard to flight plans filed with the FAA forth with the Challenger 605, the crew filed it, not defendants, and defendants do not have it.  Thus, they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

No. 34:  documents relating to Aeolus’ use of IPreFlight, or any flights on the Challenger 605. 

In their response to the separate statement, defendant states that in regard to flight plans filed with the FAA forth with the Challenger 605, the crew filed it, not defendants, and defendants do not have it.  Thus, they “amend their previous responses.”  They state that they have made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and that they are unable to comply with the request because the requested documents have never existed.

No. 36:  privilege log to extent Aeolus claims privilege.

Defendants acknowledge that they have not provided one.

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel

 

The court considered the moving papers.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED. The court orders that the attorney is relieved as counsel of record for plaintiff Maria Garza De Montes, effective upon the filing of the proofs of service of the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” (Judicial Council form MC-053) upon the client.

BACKGROUND

On July 28, 2023, plaintiff Maria Garza De Montes filed a complaint against Los Angeles World Airports, Los Angeles International Airport, City of Los Angeles, County of Los Angeles, State of California, and California Department of Transportation premises liability based on dangerous condition.  She alleges that on August 11, 2022 she was ascending an escalator when she slipped on an unknown object and/or substance and/or liquid causing her to fall.

On November 29, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC.

LEGAL STANDARD

The court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.  See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal. App. 2d 398.

CRC Rule 3.1362 (Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel) requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)).

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff’s attorney Vanessa Fantasia, Esq. seeks to be relieved as counsel of plaintiff.

            Counsel states in her declaration that “[o]ur office began having issues communicating with” plaintiff and that there has been an irremediable breakdown in attorney-client communication.

            Counsel states that she confirmed within the past 30 days that the client’s address is current by mail, return receipt requested.  Counsel has not shown a copy of the return receipt. 

            The court notes that the hearing was continued to allow counsel to file a supplemental declaration to show further proof of confirmation of the client’s address.

            On April 9, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel filed a supplemental declaration that on March 26, 2024, plaintiff was served via U.S. Certified Mail to plaintiff’s last known address, and that her address was confirmed by USPS signature confirmation service.

The court finds that the attorney submitted a declaration establishing that the service requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, have been satisfied.  The court also finds that the attorney has shown sufficient reason why the motion to be relieved as counsel should be granted.

            The motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED. The court orders that the attorney is relieved as counsel of record for plaintiff Maria Garza De Montes, effective upon the filing of the proofs of service of the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” (Judicial Council form MC-053) upon the client.

Moving counsel is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
