DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV18013    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 27, 2023               TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Michelle Bernardino et al. v. American Honda Motor Co.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV18013           

 

DEMMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT; MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant American Honda Motor Co.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Michelle and Anthony Bernardino.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         06/01/22: Complaint filed.

·         01/24/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on June 1, 2022. Plaintiffs purchased a 2019 Honda Pilot which they allege developed defects in the infotainment, electrical, transmission, and engine systems.

 

Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action for fraudulent concealment and moves to strike portions of the First Amended Complaint.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

            Plaintiffs shall have 30 days leave to amend the Complaint.

 

            Defendant’s Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Complaint is MOOT.

 

//

 

//

DISCUSSION:

 

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

 

            Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action for fraudulent concealment in the First Amended Complaint.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of David Chaffin attached to the demurrer states that the parties met and conferred on March 6, 2023 regarding this demurrer and the simultaneous motion to strike, but were not able to reach an agreement. (Declaration of David L. Chaffin ISO Demurrer ¶ 3.) The Court therefore finds that Defendant has satisfied the statutory meet and confer process.

 

Plaintiffs’ Requests for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiffs request that the Court take judicial notice of several appellate and federal district court opinions. As these materials are not relevant to the Court’s ruling, Plaintiffs’ requests are DENIED. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 (“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Analysis

 

            Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action for fraudulent concealment for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)  

 

The elements of fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) intent to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) the plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of fact. (Hambridge v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.)  

 

1.      Damages

 

            Defendant first argues that Plaintiffs may not assert this cause of action because, although Plaintiffs allege that the infotainment systems 2018 and 2019 Honda vehicles, including 2019 Honda Pilots are prone to failure, they do not allege at any point that any defects in the infotainment system have actually arisen, and thus, Plaintiffs have not adequately alleged any damages as the result of conduct by Defendant. A review of the lengthy allegations of the fifth cause of action reveals that this contention is correct. (See generally FAC ¶¶ 48-90.) Indeed, Plaintiffs base their fraudulent concealment claim on an argument that they have been injured because the infotainment system “may” fail. (FAC ¶ 80.) Under the law, Plaintiffs need not allege that the product is defective to establish a claim for fraudulent concealment, because a claim for fraudulent concealment impugns Defendant’s conduct. (Khan v. Shiley Inc. (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 848, 857.) Plaintiffs have also alleged damages in that they have alleged that they would not have purchased the vehicle had they known of the propensity of the infotainment system to fail. (FAC ¶ 89.) The Court therefore finds this contention to be without merit.

 

2.      Representations

 

            Defendant next argues that Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action fails because they do not allege any misrepresentations by Defendant or its agents. This contention neglects that Plaintiffs allege a cause of action for fraudulent concealment, not ordinary fraudulent misrepresentation. Defendants have therefore failed to demonstrate that the fifth cause of action is defective on this basis.

3.      Duty to Disclose

 

Defendant next argues that it had no duty to disclose any defect because it had no special or transactional relationship with Plaintiff.

 

There are four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; or (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material fact.” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) A duty to disclose may also arise when a defendant possesses or exerts control over material facts not readily available to the plaintiff. (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1198, 1199.)  

 

“Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1384.)  

 

            Defendant first argues that this cause of action fails because, although the First Amended Complaint contends that Defendant had exclusive knowledge of and actively concealed material facts from Plaintiff, the First Amended Complaint does not allege a transactional relationship between the parties. Ordinarily, a duty to disclose absent a fiduciary relationship “presupposes the existence of [a] relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 311.) Defendant contends that because Defendant is the manufacturer of Plaintiff’s vehicle, there was not a “direct dealing” between the parties that gives rise to a duty to disclose. (See Bigler-Engler, supra, at 311.) Defendant is mistaken.

 

            Although a manufacturer does not have a transactional relationship with the “public at large,” (Bigler-Engler, supra, at 311.) a vendor does have a duty to disclose material facts “not only to immediate purchasers, but also to subsequent purchasers when the vendor has reason to expect that the item will be resold.” (OCM Principle Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 835, 859-60.) Here, the essential allegation is that Plaintiffs are not a member of the public at large, but a subsequent purchaser of a product manufactured by Defendant. Existing precedent does not require Plaintiffs to allege any further relationship with the manufacturer of the vehicle exhibiting the defects giving rise to this action.

 

            Defendant next argues that the First Amended Complaint does not adequately allege that Defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts or actively concealed them from Plaintiffs. At the outset, Defendant argues that allegations as to other Honda vehicles are irrelevant because they do not pertain to Plaintiffs’ vehicle. Not so, as the theory advanced in the First Amended Complaint is that the defects identified are common to all Honda vehicles from 2018 and 2019, including the Subject Vehicle. (See generally FAC ¶¶ 45-90.) That said, as Defendant correctly observes, Plaintiffs allege that publicly available customer complaints should have put Defendant on notice of defects in its 2018 and 2019 series of vehicles, relying on complaints dating back to 2017. (See FAC ¶ 60.) Since these complaints are alleged to be publicly available, as Defendant argues, they could not have contained or evidenced Defendant’s exclusive knowledge of any material facts. Plaintiffs also allege, however, that Defendant put out advertising materials as of November 2018 on which Plaintiffs relied that discussed the infotainment system and made no mention of the alleged defects. (FAC ¶¶ 11-12.) Representations in marketing materials may be actionable if Defendant did not in fact entertain such opinion of the system’s quality given its knowledge of the system defects. (See Pacesetter Homes Inc. v. Brodkin (1970) 5 Cal.App.3d 206, 211.) While the theory of misleading advertising materials is alleged, the Court agrees that these allegations do not describe the representations which Defendant purportedly made with any specifics, which is required on a claim for fraud. Even a claim for fraudulent concealment still requires that a “representation was made to which the alleged concealment was contrary.” (Daugherty v. American Honda Motor Co. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 834.)

 

            As the First Amended Complaint does not plead any representations with sufficient specifics to determine what was concealed, the Court is forced to conclude that Plaintiffs have not stated facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for fraudulent concealment.

 

Leave to Amend

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Plaintiffs have not stated the means by which the complaint might be amended to cure the defects in this cause of action. However, as the defects arise from a lack of specificity, the Court will exercise its discretion to grant Plaintiffs leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

 

            As the Court has sustained the demurrer with leave to amend, Defendant’s Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Complaint is MOOT.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

            Plaintiffs shall have 30 days leave to amend the Complaint.

 

            Defendant’s Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Complaint is MOOT.

 

            Moving Party to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 27, 2023               TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Shenghua Entertainment, et al. v. CineLou Films, LLC, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV34604           

 

MOTION FOR ORDER REQUIRING PLAINTIFFS TO FURNISH SECURITY

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants After Dark Films, LLC, CSS Entertainment, LLC, California Maple Holdings, LLC, and Courtney Solomon.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Shenghua Entertainment and Shenghua Entertainment Communication Co., Ltd.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         10/28/22: Complaint filed.

·         07/20/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract and unjust enrichment. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants breached their obligations arising from a 2016 loan agreement to finance production of several film projects and a 2018 settlement agreement intended to resolve some of the disputes arising from those projects.

 

            Defendants After Dark Films, LLC, CSS Entertainment, LLC, California Maple Holdings, LLC, and Courtney Solomon move for an order requiring Plaintiffs to furnish security for attorney’s fees and costs expected in this action.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendants After Dark Films, LLC, CSS Entertainment, LLC, California Maple Holdings, LLC, and Courtney Solomon’s Motion for Order Requiring Plaintiffs to Furnish Undertaking is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiffs are ordered to file an undertaking with this Court in the amount of $360,000 within 30 days of this order.

DISCUSSION:

 

            Defendants After Dark Films, LLC, CSS Entertainment, LLC, California Maple Holdings, LLC, and Courtney Solomon move for an order requiring Plaintiffs to furnish security for attorney’s fees and costs expected in this action.

 

Legal Standard for Requiring Security

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1030(a), a defendant sued by an out-of-state plaintiff “may at any time apply to the court by noticed motion for an order requiring the plaintiff to file an undertaking to secure an award of costs and attorney’s fees which may be awarded in the action or special proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. §1030(a).) To prevail on a motion for undertaking, the defendant must show that: (1) the plaintiff resides out of state or is a foreign corporation; and (2) there is a “reasonable possibility” that the defendant will obtain a judgment in the matter. (Code Civ. Proc. §1030(b).) A defendant need not show that there is no possibility of the plaintiff prevailing—only that it is reasonably possible that defendant will prevail. (Baltayan v. Estate of Getemyan (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1427, 1432.)  Additionally, the motion must be accompanied by a supporting affidavit or declaration that sets forth the nature and amount of the costs the defendant has incurred and expects to incur. (Code Civ. Proc. §1030(b).) Finally, “the determinations of the court under this section have no effect on the determination of any issues on the merits of the action . . . and may not be given in evidence nor referred to in the trial of the action or proceeding.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §1030(f).) 

 

Defendants’ Evidentiary Objections to Declaration of Yu Wie-Chung

 

            Defendants object to the Declaration of Yu Wie-Chung offered in support of the opposition to this Motion. The Court declines to rule on these objections because, as stated below, the Yu Declaration is made in English, without a statement of translation, despite Plaintiffs’ averment that Mr. Yu does not speak English. (FAC ¶ 19, Declaration of Daryl Lee ISO Opp. ¶ 4.) The Court therefore finds the Yu Declaration not credible, and refuses to consider it further without considering Defendants’ objections to specific statements in the declaration. 

 

Defendants’ Evidentiary Objections to Declaration of Daryl Lee

 

            Defendants also object to the Declaration of Daryl Lee in support of the opposition. The Court rules on these objections as follows:

 

            Objection Nos. 2-3: OVERRULED. Does not lack foundation or personal knowledge. Remaining objections go to weight, not admissibility.

 

            As the remaining evidence to which Defendants object is not material to this ruling, the Court declines to rule on the remaining objection. 

 

Foreign Corporate Plaintiffs

 

            The First Amended Complaint expressly states that both Plaintiffs are corporations organized under the laws of Taiwan and with a principal place of business in Taiwan. (FAC ¶¶ 1-2.) It is therefore undisputed that both Plaintiffs in this action are foreign corporations.

 

Reasonable Possibility of Prevailing

 

The moving Defendants contend that they have a reasonable possibility of prevailing on each of the three causes of action asserted against them. Plaintiffs pled three causes of action in the First Amended Complaint: a claim for breach of contract arising from a 2018 Settlement Agreement (FAC ¶¶ 38-43), a second claim for breach of contract arising from a 2016 Loan Agreement, (FAC ¶¶ 44-49), and a claim for unjust enrichment that is derivative of the first two causes of action. (FAC ¶¶ 50-53.) Plaintiffs concede that the potential liability of these Defendants arises solely from the theory that the moving Defendants are alter egos of Cinelou Films (with respect to the first cause of action) and Comedian Productions (with respect to the second cause of action.) (Opposition pp.4:20-5:2.)

 

The moving Defendants request that Plaintiffs be ordered to furnish security because, assuming arguendo that the allegations of breach against Cinelou and Comedian are true, Defendants nevertheless have a reasonable possibility of defeating Plaintiffs’ alter ego claim, which would consequentially defeat all of Plaintiffs’ claims against them.

            To hold an owner of a corporation personally liable for the acts of the corporation as an alter ego of the owner, “[f]irst, there must be such a unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its equitable owner that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist. Second, there must be an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.” (Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 539.) “Among the factors to be considered in applying the doctrine are commingling of funds and other assets of the two entities, the holding out by one entity that it is liable for the debts of the other, identical equitable ownership in the two entities, use of the same offices and employees, and use of one as a mere shell or conduit for the affairs of the other. [citations] Other relevant factors . . . include inadequate capitalization, disregard of corporate formalities, lack of segregation of corporate records, and identical directors and officers.” (Id. at 538-39.)

 

            Defendants’ principal evidence in support of their position is a declaration from Defendant Solomon, in which he admits that he is the founder of Defendants After Dark Films, LLC, CSS Entertainment LLC, Cinelou Films, LLC, Comedian Production, LLC, and California Maple Holdings, LLC. (Declaration of Courney Solomon ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-6.) Defendant Solomon states that he is not an owner, member, or stakeholder of Atmosphere Entertainment MM, Inc. and has no involvement with its corporate filings or bank accounts. (Solomon Decl. ¶ 7.) Defendant Solomon has also provided copies of the Articles of Organization for After Dark, CSS, Cinelou, Comedian, and California Maple Holdings. (Solomon Decl. Exhs. A-D, F.) Greg S. Bernstein is listed as the organizer and agent for After Dark (Id. Exh. A). Defendant Solomon is listed as the organizer for CSS and Cinelou, but the Articles for CSS name Ryan D. Nowicki as agent for service of process, while Cinelou names Ian Brereton as its agent for service. (Id. Exhs. B, C.) Ian Brereton is also listed as the agent for Comedian Productions, but the organizer is listed as Scott Karol, who is also the organizer and agent for California Maple Holdings. (Id. Exhs. D, F.) Comedian and California Maple Holdings also list the same address for their offices, but no others. (Id.) These records demonstrate that there was limited overlap of offices and employees, and Defendant Solomon’s declaration also provides some evidence of identical directors, officers, and ownership. Defendant Solomon also states, however, that the various corporate entities maintain separate bank accounts and do not commingle their funds with Defendant Solomon’s personal funds. (Solomon Decl. ¶ 9.) Defendant Solomon also flatly denies diverting company funds for personal benefit or holding himself out as personally liable for the debts of any of the corporate Defendants. (Id.)

 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants’ evidence is not sufficient because the relevant portions of Defendant Solomon’s declaration consist of little more than bare denials that any of the relevant factors for an alter ego determination exist. The Court is not persuaded. Defendants have offered a categorical refutation of Plaintiffs’ factual contentions under penalty of perjury.  Plaintiffs provide no authority in support of their position that such a declaration is insufficient, nor do they offer any suggestion of what other evidence Defendants would need to refute a claim that Defendants did not respect the corporate form.

 

            Plaintiffs also offer declarations of their own which they claim shows that the entity Defendants were undercapitalized, that they commingled debts and obligations, and operated from the same offices. However, much of Plaintiffs’ arguments rest on the Declaration of Yu Wie-Chung, who is alleged in the First Amended Complaint to be fluent only in Mandarin, not in English, and for whom Daryl Lee declares he served as a translator. (FAC ¶ 19, Declaration of Daryl Lee ISO Opp. ¶ 4.) Mr. Yu’s Declaration is written entirely in English with no statement of translation, despite these verified allegations and statements under penalty of perjury, and Plaintiffs offer no explanation of this discrepancy. The Court therefore disregards the Yu Declaration as evidence that is  not credible. The Lee Declaration, which faces no such barrier, states that Defendant Solomon “conveyed to Yu that Cinelou Films, LLC . . . and After Dark were facing financial turmoil, teetering on the brink of bankruptcy,” and that Solomon “emphasized their personal commitment” to Plaintiffs. (Lee Decl. ¶ 5.) In reply, Defendants argue that, notwithstanding their other objections to this evidence, the Lee Declaration is insufficient because it only provides a generalized description of purported representations by Defendants, without the specific language used nor any indication of the date, time, or place where the statements were made.

 

            The Court concurs with Defendants’ assessment of the evidence. Defendant Solomon offered an unequivocal and specific denial that he or the entity Defendants engaged in conduct that would support Plaintiffs’ alter ego claim. Although the evidence presented by Defendants indicates some overlap in offices and employees, that overlap is not so substantial that the Articles of Organization on their face warrant a conclusion that Defendants could not prevail on the alter ego claim. By contrast, the only admissible evidence offered by Plaintiffs consists of vague descriptions by Mr. Lee which would not suffice to prove a unity of interest between the entities. The Court therefore finds that the moving Defendants have demonstrated a reasonable possibility of prevailing on the alter ego claim, and, therefore, a reasonable possibility of prevailing in the action.

 

Amount of Security

 

            Defendants request that Plaintiffs be required to furnish security in the amount of $960,085, which they contend accounts for the total costs and fees they expect to incur. Plaintiffs object to this request as excessive. Attorney Jeremiah Reynolds states that Defendants have incurred through July of 2023 a total of $53,083 in fees and $3,506 in costs. (Declaration of Jeremiah Reynolds ISO Mot. ¶ 9.) Attorney Reynolds states that he expects Defendants will need to take three depositions outside of the United States, which will, at a conservative estimate, incur costs of at least $18,855, plus $2,730 in travel costs. (Id. ¶¶ 5-6 Exhs. 2-3.) Defendants also anticipate at least eight depositions inside the United States, including depositions of named individuals Defendant Solomon, Mark Canton, Freddy Bush, and Scott Carol, as well as employees of Cinelou and agents of Sony Pictures, which they estimate will cost $5,000 per deposition, for a total of at least $40,000. (Reynolds Decl. ¶ 7.) Further, Defendants anticipate the need for at least one expert to testify to the commercial performance of the motion pictures at issue in this case and their connection to Defendants’ alleged gross mismanagement. (Id. ¶ 8.) Finally, Attorney Reynolds provides a breakdown of the expected attorneys fees to be incurred throughout the remainder of the litigation, with both high and low estimates of the fees incurred. (Id. ¶ 9.) That said, no breakdown of the anticipated hours is provided, despite Attorney Reynolds stating that he bills at a rate of $895 per hour, while his associate bills only $460 per hour. (Id.)

 

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendants have not adequately justified their claims for the amount of fees and costs expected, and that, in any event, they could not recover all fees and costs incurred because the 2016 loan agreement does not contain an attorney’s fees provision, even though the 2018 Settlement Agreement does. With respect to the latter contention, the Court is not persuaded because, as this motion demonstrates and as Plaintiffs contend, the single issue to be litigated is whether these Defendants are the alter egos of the other named Defendants. Thus, fees and costs incurred to disprove that contention would be reasonably incurred in litigation of the 2018 Settlement Agreement as well, and therefore expressly permitted under Civil Code section 1717.

 

            As to Plaintiffs’ challenge to the adequacy of Defendants’ fee request, the Court agrees with Plaintiffs. In particular, Attorney Reynolds provides no breakdown of the hours expected to be incurred, especially with respect to trial preparation, which lists nonspecific anticipated motions and post-trial motions and submissions. (Reynolds Decl. ¶ 9.) The Court also observes that the Declaration does not explain how the estimate of $960,085 was reached beyond it being a “conservative estimate of the fees and costs.” The Court is not convinced of the accuracy of this estimate, and concludes that a more appropriate amount would be a total of $360,000.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendants After Dark Films, LLC, CSS Entertainment, LLC, California Maple Holdings, LLC, and Courtney Solomon’s Motion for Order Requiring Plaintiffs to Furnish Undertaking is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiffs are ordered to file an undertaking with this Court in the amount of $360,000 within 30 days of this order.

 

            Moving Parties to give notice.
