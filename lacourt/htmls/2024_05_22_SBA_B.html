DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02078    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

EXPOIT LLC,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV02078

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

FARADAY & FUTURE INC. et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                       May 22, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff expoIT

Responding Party:                  none

Motion for Summary Judgment or Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

            The court considered the moving papers. No opposition papers were filed.

RULING

            Summary adjudication is GRANTED as to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

BACKGROUND

            On June 27, 2023, plaintiff filed its complaint against defendant for (1) breach of contract; (2) open book account; (3) account stated; (4) goods sold and delivered; and (5) money had and received. Plaintiff alleges that it supplied defendant with computer goods which defendant has refused to pay for.

            On August 15, 2023, defendant filed its answer.

            On February 15, 2024, plaintiff filed the instant motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication.

            No opposition has been filed.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843. “A party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” CCP § 473c subd. (a)(1). “The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” CCP § 473c subd. (c).

Where the plaintiff has also moved for summary judgment that party has the burden of showing there is no defense to a cause of action. CCP § 437c, subd. (a). That burden can be met if the plaintiff “has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action.” CCP § 437c, subd. (p)(1). If the plaintiff meets this burden, it is up to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” S.B.C.C., Inc. v. St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 383, 388.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff moves for summary judgment as to the action or for summary adjudication against defendant on its first cause of action for breach of contract.

            First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract Against Defendant

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.

As to the first element—the existence of a contract—plaintiff sets forth in the separate statement that plaintiff agreed to provide defendant with computer products and defendant agreed to pay plaintiff $8,038.83, $1,087.52, and $189,753.02 for the products, as evidenced by invoice numbers 542266-1, 542112-1, and 543071, respectively. Separate Statement, p. 2, UMF Nos. 1-3. Furthermore, plaintiff’s evidence provides that defendant does not deny that the three invoices constitute or evidence enforceable contracts that are binding on defendant. Id. p. 2, UMF No. 4. Based on the foregoing, the court finds that plaintiff has established the existence of a valid contract between plaintiff and defendant.

As to the second element—plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance—plaintiff sets forth in the separate statement that it performed its obligations to defendant by delivering to defendant the computer products described in the three invoices. Id. p. 4, UMF Nos. 13-15. Plaintiff’s evidence also provides that defendant used the subject computer products in the ordinary course of defendant’s business and that defendant acknowledged that it has not paid plaintiff in connection with the three invoices. Id. p. 5, UMF Nos. 19-20. The court finds plaintiff makes a prima facie showing that it performed under the contract based on the evidence presented.

As to the third element—defendant's breach—plaintiff sets forth in the separate statement that defendant breached the contract with plaintiff by failing to pay plaintiff the $8,038.83, $1,087.52, and $189,753.02 owed to plaintiff per the three invoices. Id. pp. 5-6, UMF Nos. 21-23. As set forth above, defendant acknowledged that it owes plaintiff these amounts. Id. pp. 5-6, UMF Nos. 20, 24-26. Defendant does not dispute that it failed to pay plaintiff for the subject computer products. Id. p. 6, UMF Nos. 27. Based on the foregoing, the court finds that plaintiff makes a prima facie showing that defendant breached the contract between plaintiff and defendant.

Plaintiff has also demonstrated the fourth and final element—the resulting damages to the plaintiff—given the evidence already put forth by plaintiff. Specifically, plaintiff has been damaged in the total amount of $198,879.37 due to defendant’s failure to pay plaintiff for the subject computer products. Id. p. 7, UMF Nos. 31.

            Accordingly, the court finds that plaintiff has satisfied its initial burden of showing there is no defense to the first cause of action for breach of contract by proving each element of the claim.

            The burden shifts to defendant to present a triable issue of material fact. Defendant offers no opposition to the motion and, as such, has not presented evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact.[1] Plaintiff is therefore entitled to judgment as a matter of law as to the first cause of action for breach of contract against defendant.

ORDER

            Summary adjudication is GRANTED as to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

RULING

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. 

BACKGROUND

            On October 19, 2023, Plaintiff Mohammad Zarenezhad filed a complaint against Defendant Hyundai Motor America for violations of the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, express warranty and violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.  Plaintiff alleges causes of action for (1) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(d); (2) violation of Civil Code §1793.2; (3) violation of Civil Code §1793.2; (4) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability (Civil Code §1791.1, 1794, 1795.5); and (5) violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

 

            Plaintiff alleges that he entered into a warranty contract with Defendant on April 5, 2020 relating to a 2020 Hyundai Elantra.  Plaintiff alleges the vehicle manifested defects within the warranty period.  Plaintiff alleges Defendant failed to either repurchase or replace the vehicle after failing to cure the defects after a reasonable number of attempts. 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “A written provision in any [] contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract or transaction, or the refusal to perform the whole or any part thereof, or an agreement in writing to submit to arbitration an existing controversy arising out of such a contract, transaction, or refusal, shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.”  9 U.S.C. § 2. 

            Under California and federal law, public policy favors arbitration as an efficient and less expensive means of resolving private disputes. (Moncharsh v. Heily & Blasé (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 8-9; AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333, 339.) Accordingly, whether an agreement is governed by the California Arbitration Act or the Federal Arbitration Act, courts resolve doubt about an arbitration agreement’s scope in favor of arbitration. (Moncharsh, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 9; Comedy Club, Inc. v. Improv West Associates (9th Cir. 2009) 553 F.3d 1277, 1284; see also Engalla v. Permanente Med. Grp., Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72 (“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability [citation] and a requirement that an arbitration agreement must be enforced on the basis of state law standards that apply to contracts in general.”).)  “[U]nder both the FAA and California law, ‘arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.’” (Higgins v. Superior Court (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1247.)

            The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.  (Engalla, supra, 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.)  A petition to compel arbitration must allege both a “written agreement to arbitrate” the controversy, and that a party to that agreement “refuses to arbitrate” the controversy. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)   The existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate involves general contract principles and state law governs disposition of that question.  (Stirlen v. Supercuts, Inc. (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1547.)  It then becomes plaintiff’s burden, in opposing the motion, to prove by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to her opposition. (Id..) 

            “Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 requires a trial court to grant a petition to compel arbitration ‘if the court determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists.’”  (Avery v. Integrated Healthcare Holdings, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 50, 59 (Avery), quoting Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2).) Accordingly, “when presented with a petition to compel arbitration, the court’s first task is to determine whether the parties have in fact agreed to arbitrate the dispute.” (Id., at p. 59.)

            Generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting rulings on common issues. (Id.; see also Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218-219.) “In these summary proceedings the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Avery, supra, 218 Cal.App.4th at p. 59; accord Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)

           

DISCUSSION

            Parties’ Positions

            Defendants ask that the Court compel arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims based on (1) the arbitration agreement found in the warranty for the subject vehicle (“Warranty Booklet”) and/or (2) the Retail Sale Contract (“Sale Contract”) signed by Plaintiff, requiring that Plaintiff arbitration any disputes concerning the condition or purchase of the Vehicle by binding arbitration. 

            Defendant argues Plaintiff’s claims are subject to the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”).  Defendant argues the arbitration agreement in the Warranty Booklet expressly states that it will be governed and interpreted under the FAA.

            Defendant argues it has standing to enforce the arbitration agreement in the Warranty Booklet, because the warranty explicitly states it is being made contingent on the binding arbitration agreement.  Defendant argues Plaintiff expressly sued on the Warranty Booklet, he purchased his vehicle in California, and that repairs have been performed under the Warranty Booklet. Defendant argues it therefore has standing to enforce the arbitration agreement. 

            Defendant argues it was the direct provider of the warranty and Plaintiff is suing it for the obligations contained therein.  Defendant argues it is also entitled to enforce the agreement based on the doctrine of equitable estoppel. Defendant argues that, if Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is unenforceable because it is not signed by him, Plaintiff is equitably estopped from avoiding the arbitration agreement contained in the Warranty Booklet on such grounds, given that Plaintiff is simultaneously suing on the Warranty Booklet.

            In the alternative, Defendant asks that the Court compel arbitration pursuant to the Sale Contract.  Defendant argues it is entitled to sue under the Sale Contract even though it was not a party thereto or named therein, because the Warranty Booklet presumes the existence of the Sale Contract.  Defendant argues Plaintiff’s claims fall within the disputes subject to the Sale Contract’s arbitration agreement. 

            Finally, Defendant argues it has not acted inconsistently with a desire to arbitrate.  Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish that it has waived its right to arbitrate.  Defendant argues the only actions it has taken in this litigation is filing of an Answer and this Motion and service of limited discovery. 

            Defendant asks that the Court stay this action while the motion is pending.  Defendant asks that the Court stay the action pending completion of arbitration if the motion is granted.

            Defendant argues the Court is not bound by the holding in Ochoa v. Ford Motor Co., which held that a nonsignatory auto manufacturer could not compel arbitration pursuant to a sales or lease agreement.  Defendant argues there is a split in authority and asks that the Court side with Felisilda, which found that a nonsignatory manufacturer could compel arbitration pursuant to a sales or lease agreement.

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues the Supreme Court has held that the Court cannot fashion special rules to favor arbitration.  Plaintiff argues Defendant fails to present any evidence of mutual assent to the arbitration provision.  Plaintiff argues Defendant fails to present any evidence that the Warranty Booklet was presented to him prior to purchasing the vehicle, that Plaintiff read the Warranty Booklet, that Plaintiff read the arbitration provision or that Plaintiff consented to it.  Plaintiff argues there is no evidence of contract formation. 

            Plaintiff argues warranty law only imposes obligations on the seller, not the buyer.  Plaintiff argues silence or failure to reject the arbitration provision does not constitute consent.  Plaintiff argues there is no evidence that he ever affirmatively consented to the arbitration agreement and equitable estoppel cannot apply absent such evidence.  Plaintiff argues he was completely unaware of the arbitration agreement prior to the sale. 

            Plaintiff argues that, separately, the Warranty Booklet is unconscionable.  Plaintiff argues the Warranty Booklet is procedurally unconscionable, because there is no evidence that it was ever presented to Plaintiff prior to purchase of the vehicle, nor is there evidence that Plaintiff was ever informed of the existence of the arbitration provision.  Plaintiff argues the provision is in the middle of the booklet, does not require any signature and does not require any initials. 

            Plaintiff argues the arbitration provision is also substantively unconscionable, because on public policy grounds.  Plaintiff argues arbitration provisions are unenforceable on public policy grounds if they impede enforcement of unwaivable statutory rights.  Plaintiff argues his rights under the Song Beverly Act are unwaivable.  Plaintiff argues his claims therefore cannot be shunted to arbitration.

            In reply, Defendant argues that under the FAA the arbitration agreement should be enforced.  Defendant argues the agreement to arbitrate is not unconscionable.  Defendant argues even an unsigned agreement to arbitrate is enforceable.  Defendant argues the arbitration provision is not buried, as it is on the first page of the Hyundai Warranty Information section and it is also provided in the easy to read Table of Contents.  Defendant argues the heading of the arbitration provision is capitalized and in boldface text.  Defendant argues there is also no substantive unconscionability.  Defendant argues the terms of the arbitration are equally applied, allows Plaintiff to choose the location of JAMS in Plaintiff’s city or county of residence, provides that Defendant is obligated to pay all fees for any arbitration except for the initial filing fee of $250 and there is no fee-shifting provision stated therein. 

            Defendant reiterates that Plaintiff is barred by the doctrine of equitable estoppel from asserting that the arbitration agreement is unenforceable.  Defendant argues Plaintiff is suing on the Warranty agreement and attaches it to the complaint. 

            Defendant argues the Court should follow Felisilda and compel arbitration pursuant to the Sale Contract if it does not compel arbitration pursuant to the Warranty Booklet. 

            Defendant establishes the existence of an enforceable arbitration agreement in the Warranty Booklet

            Plaintiff’s complaint is based on a warranty contract entered into with Defendant on April 5, 2020.  (Complaint, ¶6.)  A copy of the warranty contract is attached as Exhibit A to the Complaint.  (Complaint, Ex. A.)  The warranty contract is the “2020 Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information,” the identical Warranty Booklet that is the basis of Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration  (Id.).  In fact, Exhibit A to the Plaintiff’s complaint contains the very arbitration provision under which Defendant is seeking to compel arbitration.  (Complaint, Ex. A, p. 3, 12-13.)  Based on Plaintiff’s own complaint allegations, he agreed to the arbitration agreement contained in the Warranty Booklet when he “entered” into the “warranty contract” on April 5, 2020.

            For this reason, Plaintiff is also equitably estopped from denying that he agreed to the contract based on lack of a signature.  Plaintiff’s own complaint allegations are confirmation of consent.  Plaintiff is standing on the contract, which includes an arbitration provision.  “[U]nder equitable estoppel principles and in appropriate factual circumstances, a signatory to a[n] agreement containing an arbitration clause may be compelled to arbitrate its claims against a nonsignatory when the relevant causes of action rely on and presume the existence of the contract.”  (Boucher v. Alliance Title Co. Inc. (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 262, 269; see also Rowe v. Exline (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1287 (plaintiff’s claims against nonsignatory directors who were sued as alter egos of corporation were subject to arbitration based on arbitration provision contained in plaintiff’s agreement with corporation; plaintiff could not sue directors as alter egos of corporation then at the same time disclaim their right to compel arbitration pursuant to arbitration agreement with corporation.) 

            Moreover, the arbitration provision also states in bolded, all caps that, “BY ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS.”  (Willette Dec., Ex. B, p. 14.)  Plaintiff alleges that the defects were covered by the warranty, and she presented the vehicle to Defendant for repair a reasonable number of times per the warranty.  (Complaint, ¶¶14-15.) 

            Plaintiff argues that a warranty agreement does not create obligations on the buyer, only the seller.  Plaintiff argues that in Norcia v. Samsung Telecommunications Am., LLC (9th Cir. 2017), 845 F.3d 1279, 1284, based under similar facts, a warranty booklet that contained an arbitration provision was deemed unenforceable for lack of consent.  Norcia is distinguishable.          Norcia did not discuss the applicability of equitable estoppel.  Significantly, the plaintiff in Norcia did not bring any claims for breach of warranty.  (Id. at 1283.)  Plaintiff in Norcia was therefore not attempting to sue on the warranty agreement, while also disclaiming the enforceability of the arbitration provision contained within the warranty agreement.  There was therefore no occasion to discuss equitable estoppel. 

            The arbitration provision discussed in Norcia was also distinguishable from the arbitration agreement presented here.  Unlike Defendant’s arbitration agreement, the arbitration agreement in Norcia did not make warranty benefits contingent upon agreement to the arbitration agreement, nor did it state that acceptance of the warranty benefits was an agreement to be bound by the arbitration provision.  (Norcia, supra, 845 F.3d at 1282.) 

            Under the Warranty Booklet’s arbitration agreement, Plaintiff’s presentation of the vehicle to Defendant multiple times for repair was a manifestation of assent to the arbitration agreement.  Defendant is not relying solely on the Plaintiff’s failure to opt out of the arbitration provision, unlike the defendant in Norcia. 

            Defendant establishes the existence of an appliable arbitration agreement. 

            Plaintiff fails to establish that the arbitration agreement is unconscionable

            “[G]enerally applicable contract defenses, such as ... unconscionability, may be applied to invalidate arbitration agreements without contravening” the FAA’ or California law. (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 246.) “The party opposing arbitration has the burden of proving the arbitration provision is unconscionable.” (Szetela v. Discover Bank (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1099.)

            “The prevailing view is that procedural and substantive unconscionability must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability.  But they need not be present in the same degree. Essentially a sliding scale is invoked which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves.  In other words, the more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.”  (Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1243–1244 (quoting Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 114).)

            “The procedural element addresses the circumstances of contract negotiation and formation, focusing on oppression or surprise due to unequal bargaining power.  Oppression occurs where a contract involves lack of negotiation and meaningful choice, surprise where the allegedly unconscionable provision is hidden within a prolix printed form.  When the contract is a contract of adhesion imposed and drafted by the party with superior bargaining power, the adhesive nature of the contract is evidence of some degree of procedural unconscionability. However, the fact that an agreement is adhesive is not, alone, sufficient to render it unconscionable.”  (Malone v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1551, 1561.)

            “The term contract of adhesion signifies a standardized contract, which, imposed and drafted by the party of superior bargaining strength, relegates to the subscribing party only the opportunity to adhere to the contract or reject it.”  (Armendariz, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 113.)  Thus, a form agreement presented on a preprinted form and offered on a take-it-or-leave-it-basis would render it a contract of adhesion.  (Baltazar, supra, 62 Cal.4th at 1245.)  Where a contract of adhesion is presented but there is no element of surprise or oppression, the Court must be “particularly attuned” to a claim of unconscionability, but the agreement is “not subject[ed] to the same degree of scrutiny as contracts of adhesion that involve surprise or other sharp practices.”  (Id.) 

            “The circumstances relevant to establishing oppression include, but are not limited to (1) the amount of time the party is given to consider the proposed contract; (2) the amount and type of pressure exerted on the party to sign the proposed contract; (3) the length of the proposed contract and the length and complexity of the challenged provision; (4) the education and experience of the party; and (5) whether the party's review of the proposed contract was aided by an attorney.”  (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 126.)

            “Substantive unconscionability arises when a contract imposes unduly harsh, oppressive, or one-sided terms.¿(Armendariz, supra,¿24 Cal.4th at p. 113 (provision in adhesion contract will not be enforced if it “does not fall within the reasonable expectations of the weaker or adhering party or if, in context, it is unduly oppressive or unconscionable); Ajamian, supra, 203 Cal.App.4th at p. 797)  “The substantive unconscionability of an arbitration agreement is viewed in the context of the rights and remedies that otherwise would have been available to the parties.” (OTO, supra, 8 Cal.5th at p. 137.)

            Plaintiff fails to establish that the arbitration agreement is unconscionable.  The procedural unconscionability is moderate.  The arbitration agreement in the Warranty Booklet is a form agreement.  However, it was not presented on a take or leave it basis, as it allows the purchaser to opt-out of the arbitration provision within 30 days.  (Willette Dec., Ex. B, p. 14.)

            Moreover, the arbitration provision is not “buried.”  The Table of Contents located on page 3 of the Warranty Booklet identifies in all caps, “BINDING ARBITRAITON FOR CALIFORNIA VEHICLES ONLY, 13.”  On page 13, the arbitration provision is not hidden and is in regular font, preceded by a heading and warning in all caps stating, “BINDING ARBITRATION FOR CALIFORNIA VEHICLES ONLY…PLEASE READ THIS SECTION IN ITS ENTIRETY AS IT AFFECTS YOUR RIGHTS.”  Then, at the end of the section, in bolded, capital letters is a statement informing  the purchaser, “YOUR WARRANTY IS MADE SUBJECT TO THE TERMS OF THIS BINDING ARBITRATION PROVISION, BY ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS.  IF YOU DO NOT AGREE WITH THESE TERMS, PLESE CONTACT US…WITHIN THIRTY DAYS OF YOUR PURCHASE OR LEASE TO OPT-OUT OF THIS ARBITRATION PROVISION.”  (Willette Dec., Ex. B, p. 14.)

            Plaintiff fails to identify any substantive unconscionability.  There are no terms that are one sided or unduly harsh.  Plaintiff and Hyundai are entitled to compel arbitration.  (Id. at p. 13.)  Hyundai is responsible for payment of all JAMS fees except the initial filing fee.  (Id. at p. 13.)  The arbitration was to be held in the city or county of Plaintiff’s residence.  (Id.).  Plaintiff’s only argument in favor of substantive unconscionability is that his Song Beverly rights are unwaivable.  Plaintiff, however, fails to identify any aspect of the arbitration agreement or proposed arbitration agreement that would amount to a waiver of Plaintiff’s Song Beverly rights or an impediment thereto.

            Plaintiff fails to establish any grounds to find the arbitration agreement unenforceable.

            Defendant’s request to arbitrate under the Sale Contract is therefore moot[1]

            Defendant’s request to compel arbitration pursuant to the arbitration agreement contained in the Warranty Booklet is granted.  Defendant’s alternative request that the Court compel arbitration pursuant to arbitration agreement contained in the Sale Contract is therefore moot. 

ORDER

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.


[1] Had Defendant’s request to arbitrate under the Warranty Booklet failed, its attempt to compel arbitration pursuant to the Sale Contract would have been denied.  The Court finds the reasoning of the Ford Warranty Motor Cases persuasive.  There is currently a split in authority regarding whether a nonsignatory manufacturer may rely on equitable estoppel to compel arbitration of Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Claims based on an arbitration provision contained in the vehicle sale or lease contract.  The issue is currently under review by the California Supreme Court.            

 

While Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486, 495 answered this question in the affirmative, a line of more recent cases have disagreed with its reasoning.   These cases include Davis v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 825, 319 Cal.Rptr.3d 517, 527-528 (noting Supreme Corut review of issue and exercising discretion to follow Ford Motor Warranty Cases and rejecting Felisilda); Yeh v. Supr. Ct. (2023) 95 Cal.App. 5th 264, Kielar v. Supr. Ct. (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, 619-620 (review granted Oct. 25, 2023), Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal.Ap.5th 958, 968-971 (review granted Sep. 20, 2023), Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, 1333-1336 (review granted July 19, 2023 S279969), Ngo v. BMW of North America, LLC (2022) 23 F.4th 942, 946 and Jurosky v. BMW North America, LLC (2020) 441 F.Supp.3d 963.   

 

As explained in Kielar, “[i]n affirming the superior court's decision to order the entire case to arbitration based on the doctrine of equitable estoppel, a panel of this court explained ‘the sales contract was the source of the warranties at the heart of this case.’ (Citation omitted)  We join Ford Motor and Montemayor in disagreeing with this statement and concluding equitable estoppel does not apply in this situation.”  (Kielar, supra, 94 Cal.App.5th at 619.) 

 

The reasoning of the Ford Motor Warranty Cases line of cases is persuasive.  Plaintiff’s Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Claims are not “inextricably intertwined” with the Lease Agreement.  The mere fact that there would be no express or implied warranties to sue upon “but for” the Lease Agreement is insufficient to equitably estop Plaintiff from refusing to arbitrate its Song Beverly Consumer Warranty and negligent repair claims against Defendant.

RULING

BACKGROUND

            This is an elder abuse case. On October 31, 2023, plaintiff Gloria Caldwell, by and through her Power of Attorney, Terrie Baird, filed this action against defendants Driftwood Healthcare Center LLC d/b/a Driftwood Healthcare Center (“Driftwood”), Citrus Wellness Center, LLC, Ensemble Healthcare, LLC, Rechnitz Citrus, GP, and Does 1 through 50, alleging causes of action for negligence, violations of the Elder and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act, and violations of resident rights. On April 24, 2024, plaintiff dismissed Citrus Wellness Center, LLC, Ensemble Healthcare, LLC, and Rechnitz Citrus, GP from this action.

            On April 12, 2024, plaintiff moved for trial preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36. On May 9, 2024, Driftwood opposed the motion. Plaintiff did not reply.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings: (1) The party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole. (2) The health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party's interest in the litigation.” Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).

Unless the court otherwise orders: (1) A party may file and serve a motion for preference supported by a declaration of the moving party that all essential parties have been served with process or have appeared. (2) At any time during the pendency of the action, a party who reaches 70 years of age may file and serve a motion for preference.” Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (c).

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

            The court DENIES Driftwood’s request for judicial notice. “A party requesting judicial notice of material under Evidence Code sections 452 or 453 must provide the court and each party with a copy of the material. If the material is part of a file in the court in which the matter is being heard, the party must: (1) Specify in writing the part of the court file sought to be judicially noticed; and (2) Either make arrangements with the clerk to have the file in the courtroom at the time of the hearing or confirm with the clerk that the file is electronically accessible to the court.” Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1306, subd. (c). Driftwood

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

            The court OVERRULES Driftwood’s evidentiary objections. Code of Civil Procedure section 36.5 makes clear that the moving party’s attorney may provide a declaration based on information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party in support of a motion for trial preference. Code Civ. Proc., § 36.5.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff moves for trial preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36 on the grounds that she is 86 years old, has a substantial interest in this action as a whole, and currently has health problems of arthritis, dementia, and Type 2 diabetes mellitis. Plaintiff also contends that she is under hospice care, which requires certification by a licensed physician that she is terminally ill. Plaintiff contends these factors make it substantially unlikely she will survive before this case is brought to trial if preference is not granted.

            In opposition, Driftwood contends there is no evidence to support plaintiff’s claim that her health warrants trial preference. Driftwood contends the declaration of Janine A. Mitchell submitted in support of the motion is improper because Ms. Mitchell is not the attorney for plaintiff and she did not attest to plaintiff’s prognosis. Driftwood also contends none of the evidence submitted indicate the risk of imminent death, plaintiff’s prognosis, nor her purported hospice certification by a licensed physician. Driftwood states that the document relied upon is a discharge summary from Torrance Memorial Medical Center on January 26, 2023, but it does not provide plaintiff’s prognosis or a certification for hospice care. Driftwood further cites to plaintiff’s verified discovery responses indicating that she has not been seen by a physician or hospice provider. Additionally, Driftwood argues that a grant of trial preference would violate its due process rights by shortening the discovery period in a complex action like this one and shortening Driftwood’s time to file a motion for summary judgment.

            The court finds that plaintiff is entitled to preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36. Plaintiff has clearly shown that she is over the age of 70 and that she has many health challenges which make it likely that she would not survive before this case is brought to trial. Mitchell Decl., ¶¶ 5-8. While the court agrees with Driftwood that the discharge summary plaintiff submitted does not conclusively establish she is in hospice care, see Mitchell Decl., Ex. 1, that is not necessary to warrant preference. Plaintiff must only prove that she is at least 70 years old and that she has health problems that may prejudice her interests in the litigation. See Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a). The court finds plaintiff has satisfied moving burden. Once this burden is met, preference is mandatory. “[S]ubdivision (a) of section 36 is mandatory and absolute in its application in civil cases whenever the litigants are 70 years old.’ [Citation.]” Swaithes v. Superior Ct. (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1082, 1086. As for the adverse impact on Driftwood’s ability to conduct discovery and serve a motion for summary judgment, those interests acquiesce to the mandatory nature of trial preference. See Id.
            Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS the motion. The court will set a trial date within 120 days of the hearing on this order.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED. The court will set a trial date within 120 days of the court’s order at the hearing on this motion

Hearing Date:                          May 22, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Jenna Nicole Velazquez

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Jean Paul Hernan Solis Linares

Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Response to Special Interrogatory No. 2 is GRANTED.  However, Plaintiff is only required to provide responses for the time frame of 5-years before the incident to the present.  No sanctions are awarded. 

BACKGROUND

            On November 14, 2023, Plaintiff Jean Paul Hernan Solis Linares filed a complaint against Defendant Jenna Nicole Velazquez alleging (1) Motor Vehicle; and (2) general negligence.  On October 30, 2022, Plaintiff was sitting in his parked vehicle when Defendant negligently rear-ended him.  Defendant was allegedly negligently operating his vehicle at an unsafe speed.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §2030.300

(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

            (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

            (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

            (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

(2) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.

(d) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(e) If a party then fails to obey an order compelling further response to interrogatories, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of, or in addition to, that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

            If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories and RFAs. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (Wolcher) (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221; Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Sup.Ct. (Stendell) (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

            Parties’ Positions

            Defendant Velazquez moves to compel responses to Special Interrogatory No. 2.  Defendant argues Plaintiff asserted meritless objections based on privacy and referred Defendant to documents previously produced regarding Plaintiff’s treatment for injuries necessitated by the accident with Defendant.  Defendant argues Plaintiff waived any objection based on privacy by filing this action and alleging physical injuries to multiple parts of his body.  Defendant argues Special Interrogatory No. 2 is not asking Plaintiff to produce medical records for the past 10 years.  Defendant argues Special Interrogatory No. 2 asks Plaintiff to identify those medical providers who treated him for the past 10 years.      

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues the motion must be denied, because Defendant failed to file a separate statement as required under CRC Rule 3.1345.  Plaintiff also argues the objection based on privacy is meritorious.  Plaintiff argues Defendant is not entitled to information that is irrelevant to Plaintiff’s injury claims.  Plaintiff argues Defendant is therefore limited to discovery regarding treatment received as a result of this subject incident and prior treatments to the same body parts or conditions Plaintiff claims were injured as a result of the incident.  Plaintiff argues the injuries he claims does not justify requiring him to identify all the health care providers he has seen in the past 10 years. Plaintiff argues Defendant is not entitled to go on a fishing expedition involving his medical history, nor is it sufficient to speculate that the doctors with which Plaintiff has treated have relevant medical records. 

            In reply, Defendant argues it is Plaintiff’s burden to establish that the privacy objection is meritorious. Defendant argues he is not seeking the substantive medical records, only the identity of Plaintiff’s medical providers.  Defendant argues Plaintiff has claimed injury to numerous parts of his entire body.  Defendant argues restricting the interrogatory to those body parts is unrealistic.  Defendant argues he has also cured the failure to file a separate statement by filing a CRC Rule 3.1345 Separate Statement on reply.

            Failure to file CRC Rule 3.1345 Separate Statement not fatal

            Defendant is only seeking to compel further response to a single interrogatory.  Defendant sets forth the full text of the interrogatory in the memo of points and authorities and Plaintiff’s response is included as Exhibit B to the Motion.  (Motion, Sasaki Dec., Ex. B, p. 4.)  There is no prejudice to Plaintiff as a result of Defendant’s failure to file a CRC Rule 3.1345 and the Court can readily determine the discovery request and response at issue, as well as the reasons to compel further. 

            Plaintiff fails to justify his privacy objection, but the Court agrees that a 10-year time frame is overbroad

            “In Hill, we established a framework for evaluating potential invasions of privacy. The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.  The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy. A court must then balance these competing considerations.”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552 (citing Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 35).)

            “Whether a legally recognized privacy interest is present in a given case is a question of law to be decided by the court.  Whether plaintiff has a reasonable expectation of privacy in the circumstances and whether defendant's conduct constitutes a serious invasion of privacy are mixed questions of law and fact. If the undisputed material facts show no reasonable expectation of privacy or an insubstantial impact on privacy interests, the question of invasion may be adjudicated as a matter of law.”  (Hill, supra, 7 Cal.4th at 40.)

            “In general, the court should not proceed to balancing unless a satisfactory threshold showing is made. A defendant is entitled to prevail if it negates any of the three required elements. A defendant can also prevail at the balancing stage. An otherwise actionable invasion of privacy may be legally justified if it substantively furthers one or more legitimate competing interests. (Hill, supra, 7 Cal.4th at 40.)  Conversely, the invasion may be unjustified if the claimant can point to “feasible and effective alternatives” with “a lesser impact on privacy interests.”  (County of Los Angeles v. Los Angeles County Employee Relations Com. (2013) 56 Cal.4th 905, 926.)

            (1)  Legally protected interest.  Plaintiff undisputedly has a legally protected interest in his medical history and the persons he has seen for medical treatment in the past 10 years. 

            (2)  Reasonable expectation of privacy.  “The extent of a privacy interest is not independent of the circumstances.  Even when a legally cognizable privacy interest is present, other factors may affect a person's reasonable expectation of privacy. For example, advance notice of an impending action may serve to limit an intrusion upon personal dignity and security that would otherwise be regarded as serious.  In addition, customs, practices, and physical settings surrounding particular activities may create or inhibit reasonable expectations of privacy.  A ‘reasonable’ expectation of privacy is an objective entitlement founded on broadly based and widely accepted community norms.  Finally, the presence or absence of opportunities to consent voluntarily to activities impacting privacy interests obviously affects the expectations of the participant.”  (Hill, supra, 7 Cal.4th at 36-37 (athletes who voluntarily choose to participate in college athletics have diminished expectation of personal privacy in his or her bodily condition, such that drug testing programs routinely survive Fourth Amendment “privacy” challenges).) 

            “In determining whether one has waived the right of privacy by bringing suit, our Supreme Court has noted that although there may be an implicit partial waiver, the scope of such waiver must be narrowly, rather than expansively construed, so that plaintiffs will not be unduly deterred from instituting lawsuits by fear of exposure of private activities.  An implicit waiver of a party's constitutional rights encompasses only discovery directly relevant to the plaintiff's claim and essential to the fair resolution of the lawsuit.”  (Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1014 (citing Vinson v. Supr. Ct. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 842 (quoting Britt v. Supr. Ct. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 859).)

            Given Plaintiff’s response to Form Rog 6.2 identifying his injuries as headaches and injury to the left side of his neck, left side of his ack, his left ribs, left abdomen, left should, left pal, left wrist, left thigh and left knee, Plaintiff fails to establish a reasonable expectation of privacy over the identity of his medical providers.  As acknowledged by Plaintiff, his privacy right over any information is waived if put at issue by this litigation.  (Britt v. Sup. Ct. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 864; Vinson v. Sup. Ct. (1987) .)  Plaintiff has put prior injury to nearly his entire body at issue by claiming the injuries he lists in his response to Form Rog 6.2.  At the very least, Defendant is entitled to conduct discovery into Plaintiff’s medical history to determine if some of Plaintiff’s claimed injuries were preexisting.

            However, a time frame of 10 years is overbroad.  Plaintiff’s alleged injuries does not put at issue 10-years worth of medical treatment.  Plaintiff’s responses should be limited to 5-years prior to the incident to the present. 

            (3)  Threatened intrusion that is serious.  Plaintiff fails to demonstrate a serious invasion of privacy.  Defendant rightly points out that he is only seeking the identity of Plaintiff’s medical providers and from that information, he will cull those providers he believes will have relevant information tot his action.  Moreover, any threatened invasion of privacy can be addressed with a standard protective order.

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatory No. 2 is granted.  The relevant time frame, however, is limited to five years prior to the incident to the present.   

            Sanctions

            Defendant does not include a request for sanctions in the Notice of Motion.  Defendant’s only reference to sanctions is in the conclusion wherein he asks that Plaintiff “pay appropriate monetary sanctions.”  Defendant’s request for sanctions fails to comply with CCP §2023.040, which requires that the sanctions request be included in the Notice of Motion and the Notice identify the type of sanctions, the amount of sanctions and the persons against whom the sanctions are requested.  Moreover, Defendant only prevailed in part on his interrogatory.  For these reasons, no sanctions are awarded.    

ORDER

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Response to Special Interrogatory No. 2 is GRANTED.  However, Plaintiff is only required to provide responses for the time frame of 5-years before the incident to the present.  Plaintiff is ordered to serve further responses within 20 days.  No sanctions are awarded. 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion to Set Aside Default

 

            The court considered the moving papers. No oppositions papers were filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            On December 19, 2023, Michael Hecht filed his complaint against Joseph Arnao and Mina Arnao asserting a single cause of action for motor vehicle. On January 10, 2024, plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint substituting Sophia Mei Arnao for Doe 1.

            Default was entered as to defendants on April 10, 2024.

            On April 24, 2024, defendants filed the instant motion to set aside default.

            No opposition has been filed. 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Per CCP § 473, subdivision (b), a court may relieve a party or his counsel from a dismissal against him because of his “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” When a party seeks relief “no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” CCP § 473. And when such relief is available, “there is a strong public policy in favor of granting relief and allowing the requesting party his or her day in court.” Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 981-982, internal quotations omitted.

 

DISCUSSION

            In support of defendants’ motion to set aside, defendants provide the declaration of their attorney, K. Robert Gonter, Jr.

Counsel declares, in relevant part, the following. On January 25, 2024, defendant Joseph Arnao notified his insurance carrier that plaintiff served defendants with the Summons and Complaint. Gonter Decl. ¶ 5. On April 17, 2024, the insurance carrier forwarded the Complaint to defense counsel. Id. ¶ 7. On April 19, 2024, defense counsel called plaintiff’s counsel to see if he would grant an extension to file a responsive pleading, which was rejected; at that time, the court’s docket reflected plaintiff’s request for entry of default had been rejected. Id. ¶ 9. On April 22, 2024, defense counsel received a rejection for an answer filed on April 19, 2024, because a request for entry of default had been filed on April 10, 2024. This was the first time the defense learned of the default entry. Id. ¶ 10.

            Here, the court is inclined to grant the motion based on the inadvertent mistake by defendants’ insurer and counsel to timely file an answer. As provided above, on January 25, 2024, defendant Joseph Arnao communicated with his insurer that defendants were served with the Summons and Complaint, but the insurer did not forward such to defense counsel until April 17, 2024. Defense counsel received this file a week after plaintiff filed the requests for default, and counsel only learned of the request for default being filed when the answer was rejected on April 22, 2024. Consequently, defense counsel moved promptly to file the instant motion, which is timely. Further, no opposition is presented.

 

ORDER

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.
