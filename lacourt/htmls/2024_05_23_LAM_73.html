DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV10614    Hearing Date: May 23, 2024    Dept: 73

05/23/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

WEAVER, et al. v. LANCE JAY ROBBINS PALOMA PARTNERSHIP, et al. (23STCV10614)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:           James Fazio III (Gordon Rees Scully Mansukhani, LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:          Lisa Salisbury (Salisbury Group, Inc.)

 

MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA (filed 01/05/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to quash.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 20, 2023, Plaintiffs H. Duane Weaver (“Weaver”) and Negative Asset Limited Partnership filed this action against Defendants Lance Jay Robbins Paloma Partnership, 7th St. Associates, Inc. and Lance Jay Robbins (“Robbins”).

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (2) Breach of Contract; (3) Abuse of Control; (4) Appointment of Receiver; (5) Declaratory Relief; (6) Accounting; (7) Violation of Inspection Rights; (8) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and/or Retention; and (9) Negligence.

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff Weaver and Defendant Robbins formed a partnership in the 1980s to acquire, own and operate a 58-unit apartment building located at 15 Paloma Avenue, Venice, California 90291. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed in their duty to properly manage and operate the subject property pursuant to the partnership agreement. Defendants had hired Concord Real Estate Service, Inc. (“Concord”) to manage the subject property.

 

On January 5, 2024, Defendants filed its instant motion to quash, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs’ subpoena to Concord should be quashed as overly broad and unduly burdensome

·       Concord is Defendants’ property management company for the property that is the subject of this dispute, 15 Paloma Avenue, Venice, California 90291.

·       The subpoena contains 31 requests for virtually every document and communication in Concord’s possession related to Defendants’ real property that is the subject to this lawsuit spanning an 8-plus year period from January 2015 to the present.

·       As property managers, it is not Concord’s practice to store and prepare documents of this nature on file for years. In order to identify and gather all communications, financial records, invoices, receipts, employee and payment records, and all other documents requested by the Subpoena, many people at Concord would need to manually and electronically search innumerable inboxes and sent items folders, bank records, and virtually the entire accounting department and many outside parties including but not limited to countless city agencies and third party vendors.

·       Concord conservatively estimates it would take at least one hundred and twenty hours if not considerably longer for Concord to search for, gather and process all records requested by the Subpoena, in addition to an inestimable amount of time waiting for responses and documents from city agencies and third party vendors.

 

 

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The subpoena asks for relevant information because one of the key issues in the lawsuit is the condition and mismanagement of the subject property. Defendants’ decision to hire Concord is a key issue because there is a cause of action Negligent Hiring, Supervision and/or Retention

·       The subpoena is narrow in scope and time

·       The records requested are not unduly burdensome

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiffs’ subpoena is overly broad and unduly burdensome.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a) provides: 

 

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person. 

 

(Code of Civ. Proc., § 1987.1(a).)

 

B.     Discussion

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ subpoena to Concord should be quashed as overly broad and unduly burdensome.

 

Defendants assert the subpoena is overly broad because it contains 31 requests for “virtually every document and communication” in Concord’s possession related to the subject property spanning an 8-plus year period from January 2015 to the present. (Motion, p. 4.)

 

However, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ requests are overly broad. Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges Negligent Hiring, Supervision and/or Retention of Concord as Defendants’ property management company of the subject property. The Complaint alleges “Concord was unfit or incompetent at acting as a Professional Property Management company and maintaining accounting records.” (Compl., ¶ 78.) Thus, Plaintiffs limit the majority of their requests to within the time period that Concord was property management of the property. Plaintiffs also limit the subject matter of the documents it seeks to documents that should routinely be kept by property managers, such as financial records and employment files. The Court does not find Plaintiffs’ subpoena is overly broad.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiffs’ subpoena is unduly burdensome. Defendants argue Concord does not maintain all documents related to each property in conveniently organized file folders so “Concord would need to manually and electronically search innumerable inboxes and sent items folders, bank records, and virtually the entire accounting department and many outside parties including but not limited to countless city agencies and third party vendors.” (Goldstein Decl., ¶ 3.) In support, Defendants submit the declaration of Joe Goldstein, Concord’s Chief Operating Officer who estimates “it would take at least one hundred and twenty hours if not considerably longer for Concord to search for, gather and process all records requested by the Subpoena.” (Goldstein Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

However, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ subpoena is unduly burdensome. Generally, objections of “undue burden” must be supported by “evidence showing the quantum of work required….” (West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417 (“West Pico”).) “The trial court must … consider not only the stated objections to the interrogatories, but also the interrogatories themselves, as well as the pleadings, and the contentions of the interrogating party as to the purpose and validity of the interrogatories.” (See Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 18-19, fn. omitted.) The court “retains the discretion to weigh the burden of compliance against the likelihood of producing helpful information, to avoid duplicative production, and to narrow demands if appropriate to balance the reasonable concerns of both parties. [Citations.]” (Volkswagen of America, Inc. v. Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1497, italics added.) Here, Plaintiffs’ requests specify the witnesses it seeks Concord’s communications with or the documents it requests such that Concord could likely search for these in its emails and software. Further, the Court finds Plaintiffs’ requests would produce relevant information that outweighs Defendants’ burden of compliance.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ Motion to Quash Subpoena is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Motion to Quash Subpoena is DENIED.


Case Number: 23STCV10684    Hearing Date: May 23, 2024    Dept: 73

05/23/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

MCGAUSA LLC v. MICHELLE LIU (23STCV10684)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant/moving party:  Daniel Goodkin (Goodkin Law Group, APC)

Counsel for Defendant/Cross-Complainant/opposing party:  Cheryl Turner (Turner & Associates)

 

demurrer TO FIRST AMENDED CROSS-complaint (filed 3/15/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint’s fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

Cross-Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 11, 2023, Plaintiff McGausa LLC (“McGausa) filed this action against Defendant Michelle Liu (“Liu”). This action arises from a landlord and commercial tenant dispute.

 

On March 8, 2024, Liu filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) against McGausa alleging the following causes of action: (1) breach of lease covenants; (2) breach of covenant restricting transfer; (3) nuisance; and (4) harassment.

 

The FACC alleges that McGausa, tenant, entered into a lease agreement with Liu, the owner of the subject property. According to the FACC, McGausa breached the lease by: (1) failing to pay rent when due; (2) failing to pay the rent increases; (3) by using the premises to operate a cigar lounge and renting the space out for parties instead of using it as a community workspace; (4) subletting the premises without Liu’s consent; and (5) failing to pay the increase in insurance premiums. (FACC, ¶¶ 10-16.) The FACC alleges that Liu sustained damages as a result of these breaches. (FACC, ¶ 16.)

 

On March 15, 2024, McGausa filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike, arguing the FACC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for each of its causes of action. McGausa also moves to strike portions of the FACC’s damages. Liu filed an opposition. McGausa filed a reply.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Timeliness of Opposition

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Liu failed to timely file her opposition.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). A court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.)  The appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and his or her opposition to the motion on the merits is a waiver of any procedural defects or irregularities in a motion.  (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697.)

 

Here, Liu’s opposition was due by April 10, 2024. Liu did not file and serve her opposition until April 19, 2024. However, the hearing was continued to May 23, 2024, and McGausa filed a reply addressing Liu’s opposition on the merits. Therefore, there has been no prejudice to McGausa and the Court will consider the motions on the merits. 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he discussed the grounds of the Demurrer and Motion to Strike with Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone and email. (Shakouri Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

C.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

D.     First and Second Causes of Action: Breach of Covenants of Contract and Breach of Covenant Restricting Transfer

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.”  (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)  In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.”  (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-99.)

 

The FACC alleges that the parties entered into a lease agreement. The subject lease is attached as an exhibit to the FACC. According to the FACC, McGausa breached the lease by: (1) failing to pay rent when due; (2) failing to pay the rent increases; (3) by using the premises to operate a cigar lounge and renting the space out for parties instead of using it as a community workspace; (4) subletting the premises without Liu’s consent; and (5) failing to pay the increase in insurance premiums. (FACC, ¶¶ 10-16.) The FACC alleges that Liu sustained damages as a result of the breach. (FACC, ¶ 16.) The Court finds Liu has sufficiently alleged a cause of action for breach of the lease.

 

McGausa argues that the lease states smoking was permitted on the premises. Therefore, McGausa contends that it did not breach the lease and Liu cannot prevail on this cause of action. “Exhibits to a complaint are given precedence over the contrary allegations in the complaint itself.” (Vallejo Development Co. v. Beck Development Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 929, 946.) Paragraph 1.8 of the lease provides that agreed use of the premises includes: “workspace, office and permitted smoking, and related activities.” (FACC, Ex. A, p. 1.) Yet, since the parties dispute the terms of the lease and what is included in “permitted smoking,” the Court does not find this as grounds to sustain the demur to this cause of action at this stage. The FACC also alleges other actions taken by McGausa that constituted breaches of the lease, such as its failure to pay rent, which McGausa does not address.  

 

McGausa also asserts that Liu hasn’t provided support for her alleged damages. However, McGausa’s dispute over Liu’s claimed damages is also not adequate grounds for demurring to this cause of action as Liu is not required to present extrinsic evidence in support of damages at this time.  

 

McGausa further demurs to the second cause of action stating that Liu has offered no evidence to support her allegation that McGausa sublet the leased premises without Liu’s consent. However, extrinsic evidence is not necessary at this stage. The Court finds Liu has sufficiently alleged a cause of action.

 

Based on the foregoing, McGausa’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

E.     Third Cause of Action: Nuisance

 

The elements for a private nuisance cause of action are: (1) interference with use and enjoyment of plaintiff’s property that is (2) substantial and (3) unreasonable. (Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-63.) Plaintiff must plead and prove interference with plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of the property – i.e., defendant’s activity must be “of such a nature, duration or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.” (San Diego Gas & Elec. Co. v. Sup. Ct. (Covalt) (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938.) 

 

The FACC alleges that “in or about 2020 or 2021, McGausa changed the permitted uses of the Premises from a workspace, office and business incubator, to a cigar lounge and a rental space for parties and events in violation of the lease and without the Liu’s consent. By conducting a cigar lounge and a party rental location, McGausa produced and caused noxious odors from excessive smoke, loud music, the accumulation of excessive trash and loitering around the Building.” (FACC, ¶ 23.)

 

McGausa demurs to this cause of action on the basis that: (1) smoking was permitted pursuant to the lease and (2) Liu was not a tenant of the building and therefore did not suffer any effects of the alleged nuisance.

 

The Court does not find McGausa’s arguments persuasive. As discussed above, the parties dispute the term of the lease as to whether using the premises as a cigar lounge constituted “permitted smoking.” Further, nuisance involves the interference with the plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of the property. Even if Liu was not a commercial tenant of the building, McGausa’s conduct, which includes producing and causing “noxious odors from excessive smoke, loud music, the accumulation of excessive trash and loitering,” could still interference with Liu’s use and enjoyment of the property as the owner of the building.

 

Accordingly, McGausa’s demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

F.      Fourth Cause of Action: Harassment

 

The elements for a hostile work environment harassment are: (1) plaintiff belongs to a protected group; (2) plaintiff was subject to unwelcome harassment; (3) the harassment complained of was based on membership in the protected class, (4) the harassment complained of was sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment; and (5) respondeat superior.  Further, for hostile work environment harassment to be actionable, it must be sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment… (Fisher v. Penninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 608.)   

 

In determining what constitutes sufficiently pervasive harassment, the courts have held that acts of harassment cannot be occasional, isolated, sporadic, or trivial, rather the plaintiff must show a concerted pattern of harassment of a repeated, routine or a generalized nature.  (Fisher, supra, 214 Cal.App.3d at pp. 610-611.) 

 

The FACC alleges McGausa has subjected Liu “to harassing conduct because of her gender, ancestry, race and age by denying her full and equal advantages and privileges by discriminating against her where the motivating reason is because of her gender, race, ancestry and age.” (FACC, ¶ 29.)

 

The Court does not find these allegations sufficient to show harassment by McGausa. Besides these conclusory allegations that McGausa harassed Liu, Liu does not allege how McGausa’s conduct was based on Liu’s membership in the protected classes.

 

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the FACC’s fourth cause of action for harassment with leave to amend.

 

G.    Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

McGausa requests the Court to strike portions of the FACC as follows:

1.     FACC, page 9, prayer for relief, ¶ 3: “For termination of the lease”

2.     FACC, page 4, ¶ 13, line 4: “Cross-Complainant has suffered damages in the sum of approximately $1,052,550, such being the difference between the fair rental value of the premises and the rent for which the Premises were leased to Cross-Defendant.”

3.     FACC, page 9, prayer for relief, ¶ 4: “For pain and suffering for the Third and Fourth Cause of Action”

4.     FACC, page 9, prayer for relief, ¶ 5: “For medical expenses for the Third and Fourth Cause of Action”

5.     FACC, page 9, prayer for relief, ¶ 6: “For statutory fines and penalties for the Third and Fourth Cause of Action.”

 

McGausa argues paragraph 3 in the FACC’s prayer for relief that states “for termination of the lease” should be stricken because Liu did not file an unlawful detainer complaint. McGausa asserts that Liu must file an unlawful detainer complaint if she is attempting to evict McGausa. However, Liu contends in her opposition that she is not asserting any request for the return of possession of the premises. Regarding whether Liu served notice or has given McGausa the opportunity to cure is extrinsic evidence not considered on a motion to strike. Thus, the Court finds that Liu is warranted in including termination of the lease in the FACC’s prayer for relief.

 

Next, McGausa argues that Liu has not provided support for her claim for damages in the sum of approximately $1,052,550. (FACC, ¶ 13.) However, as discussed above, Liu is not required to provide evidence in support of her claim for damages at this stage. Thus, the motion to strike as to paragraph 13 of the FACC is denied.

 

Finally, McGausa moves to strike damages in paragraphs 4, 5, and 6 of the prayer for relief because Liu cannot prove her fourth cause of action for harassment. As discussed above, the Court sustained Liu’s fourth cause of action. McGausa does not address the fact that Liu is also seeking these damages for her third cause of action for nuisance. The Court overruled McGausa’s demur to Liu’s third cause of action. Damages for discomfort, annoyance, and mental distress suffered by the plaintiff as the result of a nuisance are recoverable, but not merely as an alternative to or to the exclusion of damages for depreciation of the plaintiff’s property in rental value. (Spaulding v. Cameron, (1954) 127 Cal. App. 2d 698, 706.) Further, exemplary or punitive damages may be recovered in a nuisance case. (Hassoldt v. Patrick Media Group, Inc., (2000) 84 Cal. App. 4th 153, 168.) Therefore, the Court does not strike Liu’s requests for damages as they are recoverable for her third cause of action.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint’s fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Cross-Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.

IN THE MATTER OF DANIELLE L. RICHTER, TRUSTEE OF THE DANIELLE L. RICHTER REVOCABLE TRUST DATED JULY 20, 2016 AS AMENDED, et al. v. SOCAL DESIGN & BUILDING INC. (24STCP00880) 

Counsel for Petitioner/moving party:  Michele Seltzer (Seltzer Legal Group, P.C.)

 

PETITION FOR RELEASE OF PROPERTY FROM MECHANICS LIEN (filed 03/20/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Petition for Release of Property from Mechanics Lien is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 20, 2024, Petitioners In the matter of Danielle L. Richter, Trustee of the Danielle L. Richter Revocable Trust dated July 20, 2016 as Amended; Glenn R. Richter, Trustee of the Glenn R. Richter Revocable Trust dated July 10, 2012 as Amended (“Petitioners”) filed a petition against Respondent Socal Design & Building Inc., dba M-Cubed Design and Building aka M-Cubed (“Respondent”) for release of property from lien.

 

No opposition or other response has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

“The claimant shall commence an action to enforce a lien within 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien. If the claimant does not commence an action to enforce the lien within that time, the claim of lien expires and is unenforceable.” (Civ. Code, § 8460.) If a claimant has not commenced an action to enforce the mechanics lien within the time provided in Civil Code section 8460, the property owner may petition the court for an order releasing the property from the lien. (Id., § 8480, subd. (a).) “A release order does not bar any other cause of action or claim for relief by the claimant, other than an action to enforce the claim of lien that is the subject of the release order.” (Id., subd. (b).) A petition for a release order may be joined with a pending action to enforce the lien claim. (Id., subd. (c).)

 

Civil Code section 8484 states: A petition for a release order shall be verified and shall allege all of the following:

(a) The date of recordation of the claim of lien. A certified copy of the claim of lien shall be attached to the petition.

(b) The county in which the claim of lien is recorded.

(c) The book and page or series number of the place in the official records where the claim of lien is recorded.

(d) The legal description of the property subject to the claim of lien.

(e) Whether an extension of credit has been granted under Section 8460, if so to what date, and that the time for commencement of an action to enforce the lien has expired.

(f) That the owner has given the claimant notice under Section 8482 demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien and that the claimant is unable or unwilling to do so or cannot with reasonable diligence be found.

(g) Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.

(h) Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien.

 

At least 10 days before petitioning for a release order, the owner must give the claimant a notice demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien claim. The notice must comply with the requirements of Civil Code section 8100 et seq. and state the grounds for the demand. (Id., § 8482.)

 

Civil Code section 8486 states:

(a) On the filing of a petition for a release order, the clerk shall set a hearing date. The date shall be no more than 30 days after the filing of the petition. The court may continue the hearing only on a showing of good cause, but in any event the court shall rule and make any necessary orders on the petition not later than 60 days after the filing of the petition.

(b) The petitioner shall serve a copy of the petition and a notice of hearing on the claimant at least 15 days before the hearing. Service shall be made in the same manner as service of summons, or by certified or registered mail, postage prepaid, return receipt requested, addressed to the claimant as provided in Section 8108.

 

Civil Code section 8488 states:

(a) At the hearing both (1) the petition and (2) the issue of compliance with the service and date for hearing requirements of this article are deemed controverted by the claimant. The petitioner has the initial burden of producing evidence on those matters. The petitioner has the burden of proof as to the issue of compliance with the service and date for hearing requirements of this article. The claimant has the burden of proof as to the validity of the lien.

(b) If judgment is in favor of the petitioner, the court shall order the property released from the claim of lien.

(c) The prevailing party is entitled to reasonable attorney's fees.

 

B.     Discussion

 

Petitioners allege they are the owners of real property (the “Property”) located at and commonly known as 1375 North Wetherly Drive, Los Angeles, CA 90069. (Petition, ¶ 1.) Respondent is a corporation engaged in the business activity of general contracting, and Respondent claims to have contributed to the work of improvement on the property. (Petition, ¶ 2.)

 

Petitioners request that the court release the mechanic’s lien recorded on October 13, 2019. Petitioners attached a copy of the claim lien. (Petition, Ex. A.) The claim of lien was in the amount of $58,245.00 for “not limited to labor, materials, construction management, administrative costs, engineering services, design consulting, material selections, travel time, and more” alleged to have been furnished by Respondent for a work of improvement. (Petition, ¶ 3.)

 

Respondent failed to bring an action to enforce the lien within 90 days after recordation. Petitioners sent respondent an informal letter on or about February 5, 2024, asking them to withdraw the lien. (Petition, ¶ 6.) Respondent responded on or about February 16, 2022 stating it had “indeed refrained from initiating a lawsuit to foreclose on the aforementioned mechanics lien.” (Petition, ¶ 6.) On February 21, 2024, Petitioners sent Respondent a written demand to remove the lien by overnight delivery. (Petition, ¶ 7.) On or about February 22, 2024, Respondent responded via email that they acknowledge it’s been over 90 days since they first recorded the lien and that it’s no longer enforceable.” (Petition, ¶ 8.) Respondent has not executed a release of the lien. (Petition, ¶ 5.) The Court finds Petitioners have complied with Civil Code section 8484.

 

Petitioners filed a proof of service indicating they served Respondent the petition papers by personal service on April 23, 2024. Petitioners also served Respondent the notice of the hearing. Thus, Petitioners have satisfied their service requirements.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Petition for Release of Property from Mechanics Lien.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Petition for Release of Property from Mechanics Lien is GRANTED.
