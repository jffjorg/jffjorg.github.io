SUBJECT:                 DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE

MOVING PARTY:   Defendant Brandy Blaske

RESP. PARTY:         No responding party as of 9-4-24

 

ORAL TENTATIVE RULING

            Defendant Brady Blaske’s Demurrer to Plaintiff Michael Allen Longmire’s first and only cause of action within his First Amended Complaint  for breach of contract is OVERRULED, without prejudice. 

 

            Defendant Brady Blaske (“Blaske”) demurs to Plaintiff Michael Allen Longmire’s (“Longmire”) FAC, however the Court has no record of a FAC being filed. Blaske references an allegedly filed FAC in the demurrer, so it appears Longmire served Blaske with the FAC, but never filed with the Court. Blaske did not include the FAC filing date in her demurrer, nor did she attach a copy of the FAC to the demurrer.

 

            The Court notes that there is no motion to strike filed, and no motion to strike is mentioned in the caption of the motion, however the hearing scheduled for 9-10-24 is a Demurrer with Motion to Strike. Since there is no motion to strike mentioned or filed alongside or within the demurrer the Court cannot rule on any motion to strike.

SUBJECT:                 MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION—DEFENDANT WINDSOR HAYSAC HOLDINGS, LLC

MOVING PARTY:   Plaintiff Ronny Graham

RESP. PARTY:         Defendant Windsor Haysac Holdings, LLC

 

TENTATIVE RULING

            Plaintiff Ronny Graham’s Motion to Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to Defendant Windsor Haysac Holdings, LLC’s 2nd, 4th, 12th, 15th, and 16th Affirmative Defenses, and DENIED as to Defendant Windsor Haysac Holdings, LLC’s 13th, and 20th Affirmative Defenses. Defendant withdrew their 2nd, 4th, 12rh, 15th and 16th affirmative defenses via stipulation. (Schroeder Decl., ¶ 17.) Plaintiff withdrew their motion for summary adjudication as to the 20th affirmative defense within their reply. (See Reply, p. 1:9–12.) Plaintiff did not meet their burden to show that there are no triable issues of material fact as to the 20th affirmative defense of offset.

 

            Plaintiff Ronny Graham’s Motion to Summary Adjudication is CONTINUED as to Defendant Windsor Haysac Holdings, LLC’s 23rd Affirmative Defenses for Binding Arbitration Agreement until ________ pursuant to CCP § 437c(h) Defendant submits an affidavit along with their Opposition explaining and describing specific facts and evidence needed to be obtained which are essential to opposing the motion. (Schroeder Decl., ¶¶ 18, 19.) Defendant declares there is reason to believe these facts exist. (Id.., ¶ 20.) Defendant declares the reason they need additional time is due to pending subpoena responses ((Id.., ¶ 21.)

 

REASONING

Code of Civil Procedure §437c(f)(1) permits a Plaintiff to move for summary adjudication as to “one or more affirmative defenses” that have “no merit” as to any cause of action, and the court shall grant the motion “only if it completely disposes” of the defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

 

In motion for summary adjudication of a defense “[t]he plaintiff bears the initial burden to show there is no triable issue of material fact as to the defense and that he or she is entitled to judgment on the defense as a matter of law. In so doing, the plaintiff must negate an essential element of the defense, or establish the defendant does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain evidence needed to support the defense.” (See's Candy Shops, Inc. v. Superior Court (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 889, 899–900.) “The responding party's incomplete answers can be used to satisfy the moving party's burden on summary judgment to show there is no evidence of a triable issue of material fact” (See Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2010) § 8:1050.10.)

 

Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto. The defendant or cross-defendant shall not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)

 

“(h) If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just. The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (h).)

 

            The party opposing summary judgment need only submit a good-faith affidavit explaining that “(l) the facts to be obtained are essential to opposing the motion, (2) there is reason to believe such facts may exist, and (3) the reasons why additional time is needed to obtain these facts.” (Chavez v. 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal. App. 4th 632, 643). “The party seeking the continuance must justify the need, by detailing both the particular essential facts that may exist and the specific reasons why they cannot then be presented.” (Ibid.) Upon such a showing, a continuance is “virtually mandated.” (Bahl v. Bank of America (2001) 89 Cal. App. 4th 389, 395; see also Denton v. City and Cty. o San Francisco (2017) 16 Cal. App. 5th 779, 791 [describing § 437c(h) as a “mandatory basis” for continuance].)

 

 

I.                2nd, 4th, 12th, 15th, 16th and 20th Affirmative Defenses

 

On 3-27-23, Defendant Windsor Haysac Holdings, LLC (“Windsor”) withdrew their 2nd, 4th, 12th, 15th and 16th Affirmative Defenses via an email from Windsor’s counsel to Plaintiff Ronny Graham’s (“Graham”) counsel. (See Schroeder Decl., ¶ 17, Ex. B.) Thus, the Court finds that Graham’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the 2nd, 4th, 12th, 15th and 16th Affirmative Defenses is GRANTED.

 

In the Graham Reply, Graham conceded that there are facts supplied by Windsor which “could potentially support” the affirmative defense and thus Graham “is not maintaining the request that the Court [sic] rule on this Affirmative Defense.” (Reply, p. 1:9–12.) Thus, Graham’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the 20th Affirmative Defense is DENIED.

 

II.             13th Affirmative Defense for Offset - DENIED

 

Defendant has a right to assert the affirmative defense that they are “entitled to an offset in “the proportionate amount attributable to the employer's negligence up to the amount of the ... benefits paid.” (Engle v. Endlich (1992) 9 Cal.App.4th 1152, 1166.)

 

“A medical malpractice defendant may introduce evidence of any amount payable as a benefit to the plaintiff as a result of the personal injury pursuant to the United States Social Security Act, any state or federal income disability or worker's compensation act, any health, sickness or income-disability insurance, accident insurance that provides health benefits or income-disability coverage, and any contract or agreement of any group, organization, partnership, or corporation to provide, pay for, or reimburse the cost of medical, hospital, dental, or other health care services.” (Cuevas v. Contra Costa County (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 163, 172.)

 

In the Reply, Graham argues that the 13th affirmative defense for Offset should be summarily adjudicated because this matter involves a sexual assault that Windsor “allowed to occur while [Graham] was a resident in [Windsor’s] skilled nursing facility (“SNF”),” and not caused by Windsor’s “rendering of professional services, but by the failure to protect’ Graham from harm. (Reply, p. 2:11–17.) Graham argues that because of this distinction MICRA does not apply, since MICRA only applies “in an action for personal injury against a health care provider based upon professional negligence” (Ibid.; Civ. Code, § 3333.2, sub. (a).)

 

Graham does not provide any authority for this interpretation of the MICRA statute.   In fact, there is authority to negate this argument. “Neither our case law nor the language of MICRA suggests that the possibility of professional discipline or criminal liability necessarily places a health care provider's actions outside “the scope of services for which [he or she] is licensed” or “within any restriction imposed by the licensing agency or licensed hospital.” (Lopez v. Ledesma (2022) 12 Cal.5th 848, 865.) MICRA has applied to an action “against a psychiatrist who compelled his patient to submit to sexual intercourse,” since the conduct arose out of the “course of the psychiatric treatment he was licensed to provide.” (Ibid., citing Waters v. Bourhis (1985) 40 Cal.3d 424, 436.)  “MICRA defines professional negligence as “a negligent act or omission to act by a health care provider in the rendering of professional services.” (Gutierrez v. Tostado (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 786, 790. [finding that MICRA applies where the breach of a duty arises out of professional negligence, as opposed to arising out general negligence which does not trigger MICRA].)

 

The allegations of the complaint appear to be based on a breach of a professional duty by the defendants. Graham does not allege or argue that Windsor had a general obligation to the public to keep Graham safe from the alleged sexual assault, but only that a duty was breached in reference to Windsor’s professional duties as an SNF. (See Compl., ¶¶ 8, 34.) Additionally, the general allegations show that the claim against Windsor arises out of their omission to act during the rendering of their services, thus placing this squarely within the MICRA professional negligence definition.

 

Additionally, Graham argues the offset doctrine is not applicable to the instant matter, as Windsor is just conflating “it with the principle of comparative negligence,” another affirmative defense that Windsor stipulated to withdrawing. However, the affirmative defense of offset is applicable to MICRA lawsuits through the allowable introduction of evidence “of any amount payable as a benefit to the plaintiff as a result of the personal injury.” (Cuevas, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th at p. 172.)

 

Graham does not provide any other evidence or facts to negate any elements of the offset affirmative defense. Thus, Graham does not meet their burden to show there is no triable issue of material fact as to the 13th affirmative defense. Graham’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the 13th affirmative defense is DENIED.

 

 

III.           23rd Affirmative Defense for Binding Arbitration Agreement - CONTINUED

 

Graham shows that on 10-26-23 when Windsor filed the Answer to the Complaint Windsor asserted the affirmative defense that Graham may have executed an arbitration agreement which “may have been in full force and effect at the time of all treatment rendered by Defendants.” (SSUF, ¶ 44; 12-26-23 Plaintiff’s Evidence In Support of MSA (“Evidence”), Ex. 2.). Graham shows that Windsor provided only objections to the discovery requests concerning the facts in support of the alleged arbitration agreement based on the Motion to Stay filing. (See SSUF, ¶¶ 45–48; Evidence, Ex. 3, 4, 5, 6.) Graham argues that Defendant has provided only objections to discovery, and not provided any evidence of the arbitration agreement during discovery, and thus under Union Bank, the non-moving party’s discovery responses’ are insufficient to demonstrate there was a triable issue concerning the correctness of their claims. (Motion, pp. 8–9; see Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 580–581 [Moving party may rely on non-moving factually devoid discovery responses to shift burden of proof as to whether there are triable issues of fact].)

 

However, the situation here is factually different from the Union Bank scenario. In Union Bank the non-moving party’s response to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1 was that they “believed” evidence existed to support the denials or objections to the discovery requests, whereas here, Windsor’s response to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1 was an objection to answering as there was a motion to stay filed. (Union Bank, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 580; SSUF, ¶ 48, Evidence, Ex. 6.) The Motion to Stay was denied on 4-18-24, and Windsor declares that “extensive meet and confer conferences and discussions regarding the discovery disputes” were held, followed by Windsor “substantively amend[ing] its responses to Plaintiff’s Form and Special Interrogatories, Set One, along with Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, which prompted further meet and confer discussions and correspondence between the parties.” (Schroeder Decl., ¶¶ 12–16.) Windsor declares on 8-26-24, Windsor subpoenaed Graham’s medical records from Kaiser Permanente, which “are believed to contain an applicable arbitration agreement that governs this matter.” (Schroeder Decl., ¶¶ 18.)

 

Windsor supplied an affirmative defense stating the possibility of an arbitration agreement and provided amended discovery responses stating the same. (Schroeder Decl., ¶¶ 4, 20; Ex. C.) Additionally, Windsor has provided a declaration stating that the alleged arbitration agreement governs this matter, and they are awaiting the response from their Kaiser subpoena to retrieve the alleged arbitration agreement. (Schroeder Decl., ¶¶ 20–22, Ex. C, D.) Thus, Windsor has met their burden under CCP § 437(c)(h) to obtain a continuance in order to receive the alleged essential discovery requests from Kaiser which possibly contains the arbitration agreement at issue.
