DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV07450    Hearing Date: November 1, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Tax Costs

Miguel Ernesto Ortega Bautista, An Individual v. Michelle Janice Glaser Weiner, An Individual, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Michelle Janice Glaser-Weiner
Responding Party: Plaintiff Miguel Ernesto Ortega Bautista

Tentative Ruling: GRANT in part

BACKGROUND

This is a car accident case in which Plaintiff filed a complaint alleging negligence against Defendant. (Declaration of Jennifer L. Russell [“Russell Decl.”] ¶ 2.) Plaintiff’s Section 998 offer was for $600,000. (Hickey Decl. at ¶ 11, Ex. 4.) On August 13, 2024 a jury returned verdict over three (3) times that of $2,045,371.00. (Id.) Plaintiff filed a memorandum of costs for $1,001,622.62. 

After the complaint was filed on March 4, 2019, the parties engaged in written exchange of discovery. (Hickey Decl. at ¶¶ 3, 4.) Within the First Set of Request for Production of Documents (“RFPDs”), Defendant was provided with all of the medical records and medical billing for all treatment through November 2, 2019. On April 2, 2020, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel to provide additional medical records, including medical appointments with Dr. Samimi on January 31, 2020 and March 13, 2020 as well as an April 2, 2020 letter from Dr. Samimi advising that Plaintiff was scheduled to undergo shoulder surgery on April 13, 2020. (Hickey Decl. at ¶ 7, Ex. 2.) Also attached to the email was an MRI report from a February 27, 2020 right shoulder MRI study. (Id.) Contained in the email from Plaintiff’s counsel, a CCP Sect. 998 offer for $600,000 was served as well as a time sensitive settlement demand for $500,000, which was set to expire on Friday, April 10, 2020. On April 14, 2020 (one day after the surgery was originally scheduled), defense counsel wrote a letter requesting a 30-day extension to respond to the 998 offer. (Hickey Decl. at ¶ 9, Ex. 3.) On April 23, 2020 Plaintiff’s counsel emailed defense counsel notifying that there had been a defect in the previous 998 offer, which would be withdrawn. (Id. at ¶ 11, Ex. 4.) Attached to the April 23, 2020 email was a new 998 offer set to expire on May 28, 2020.

Plaintiff underwent right shoulder surgery on May 19, 2020. (Hickey Decl. at ¶ 12.) On May 26, 2020 (2 days before the expiration of Plaintiff’s 998 offer to Defendant and a week after Plaintiff’s surgery), Defendant served a §998 in the amount of $125,001. (Id. at ¶ 13, Ex. 5.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant objects to the entire memorandum of costs and argues the Court should strike Plaintiff’s entire memorandum of costs unless Plaintiff submits documentation of all costs. Additionally, Defendant argues the Court should tax the following specific costs: 

• Costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 998: The Court should tax $33,254.10 for expert witness fees (Item No. 8), and $887,081.54 for prejudgment interest (Item No. 16). Plaintiff’s Code of Civil Procedure section 998 offer is invalid. 
• Item No. 4: The Court should tax $11,239.04 for deposition costs. The depositions of John Weiner and Sarah Glasser Weiner were unnecessary. 
• Item No. 5: The Court should tax $21,903.76 for service of process fees, because the amount is excessive. 
• Item No. 9: The Court should tax $14,988.25 for transcripts, unless Plaintiff can show the Court ordered transcripts. 
• Item No. 12: The Court should tax $10,500.00 for models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits, because the amount is excessive. 
• Item No. 16: The Court should tax $1,495.87 for other costs, because they were not reasonably necessary to the litigation. 
Defendant specifically raises two issues with respect to the Section 998 offer: that it was not in good faith, and that the offer is invalid because it did not attach a release. (Motion to Tax, p. 5.) 

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues that the Section 998 offer was valid and enforceable, that Plaintiff had a substantial basis to reasonably believe his 998 offer was made in good faith, and is entitled to reimbursement of the full amount of each item listed in his verified cost bill. (Opposition, p. 12.)

REPLY

Defendant argues the Section 998 offer was in bad faith because Defendant lacked sufficient information to assess whether the offer and whether it was reasonable. (Reply, p. 2.) Defendant further argues the Section 998 offer was invalid because it lacked a release.

ANALYSIS

I. Section 998 Validity

A Section 998 offer is not necessarily invalid if it does not attach a release. An offer that requires the offeree to release all claims between the parties in the current action is effective under Section 998 (Auburn Woods I Homeowners Association v. State Farm General Insurance Company (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 717.) However, an overbroad release that extended beyond the scope of the litigation may render an offer invalid. (Ignacio v. Caracciolo (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 81.)

The Section 998 Offer here is invalid. K.M. v. Grossmont Union High School Dist. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 717, is instructive here, as the court held that Section 998 offers were invalid because they required the parties to “execute a settlement and release providing that Plaintiff will satisfy all liens, execute a Civil Code section 1542 waiver, and there will be no admission of liability,” without attaching a settlement and release agreement. (K.M. v. Grossmont Union High School Dist. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 717, 775.) Specifically, the court found the District's section 998 offer to be invalid because it did not attach the written agreement or describe the terms in any meaningful detail, leaving plaintiffs “to guess at what terms” the might be required. (Id.)  Although K.M. was decided after the subject Section 998 offer was made, is nevertheless instructive in its application of the rule established in Sanford v. Rasnick (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1121, 1131-1132.

Here, Plaintiff’s Section 998 offer specifically states: “Upon acceptance of this Offer to Compromise by Defendant, MICHELLE JANICE GLASER WEINER, Plaintiff, MIGUEL ERNESTO ORTEGA BAUTISTA, shall agree to execute a release with a waiver of all costs normally pursuant to C.C.P. 1032, et. seq. and dismiss all Defendants, including Defendant JOHN WEINER.” (Motion to Tax Costs, Exh. C.) Like in K.M., the Section 998 offer by Plaintiff here is rendered invalid not because a mere failure to attach a release, but rather because of its vague description of what the terms of the release entailed.  Specifically, Defendant would necessarily have to guess what all “normally pursuant” costs are included by the release. 

II. Good Faith

“[T]o qualify as a good faith offer, it must be ‘realistically reasonable under the circumstances of the particular case and must merely carry with it some reasonable prospect of acceptance.” (Arno v. Helinet Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1019, 1024 (citations omitted).) [emphasis added] In determining whether a 998 offer was made in good faith, courts must consider if the offer was “within the ‘range of reasonably possible results at trial, considering all of the information the offeror knew or reasonably should have known[,]’” and whether there was sufficient information that the “offeree knew or reasonably should have known, to assess whether the ‘offer [was] a reasonable one’, such that the offeree had a fair opportunity to intelligently evaluate the offer[.]’” (Licudine v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2019) 30 Cal.App.5th 918, 924-925 (citations omitted).)

Here, Plaintiff obtained a verdict higher than the 998 offer of $600,000, establishing a prima facie case that the offer was reasonable and made in good faith, and the offeror is eligible for costs as specified in Sect. 998. (Barber v. Perez (2008) 166 Cal. App. 444, 450; Covert v. FCA USA, LLC (2022) 73 Cal.App.5th 821; Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1484; Santantonio v. Westinghouse Broadcasting Co., Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 102, 121-124 (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 570, 577); Rouland v. Pacific Specialty Ins. Co. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 280, 289.) Here, a jury returned verdict over three times the value of Plaintiff’s Section 998 offer at $2,045,371, creating a presumption that Plaintiff’s 998 Offer was reasonable and made in good faith. The burden is therefore on Defendant to prove that the 998 offer was made in bad faith. 

To satisfy this burden, Defendants are required to specifically make a record of particular facts that were missing that were material to the validity of the 998 offer. (Barber, supra, 166 Cal. App. at 450; Arno, supra, 130 Cal.App.4th at 1027.)  Here, Defendant contends and the Court agrees that it could not fairly evaluate Plaintiff’s 998 offer because Plaintiff had yet to have his shoulder surgery.  Indeed, Plaintiff’s operative 998 offer was made on April 23, 2020, but Plaintiff’s surgery was not done until May 19, 2020.  While it is true that Plaintiff’s counsel indicated that the surgery was scheduled, the surgery had not been done and the cost of the surgery had yet to be incurred.  At the time the offer was made, Plaintiff had only completed conservative treatment and Dr. Kurzweil opined that no surgery was necessary.  While it is true that Defendant did not request an extension of time to respond to the offer, the Court does find that the timing of the offer, for lack of a better term, was unfair.  Only Plaintiff knew whether he would proceed with the surgery.  Defendant could not fairly incorporate the value of the case until Plaintiff actually did have the surgery. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court alternatively finds the Section 998 offer to be invalid because it was not made in good faith.   

III. Memorandum of Costs

CCP Section 1032 provides that a prevailing party may recover their costs. (CCP § 1032.) CCP Section 1033.5(a) itemizes allowable costs; CCP Section 1033.5(b) itemizes costs that are not allowed. Subsection (c) provides that costs shall be necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount, and that items of cost not mentioned in the statute are recoverable in the court’s discretion. 

“If items on a memorandum of costs appear to be proper charges on their face, those items are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services are proper and necessarily incurred. [citations.] The burden then shifts to the objecting party to show them to be unnecessary or unreasonable.” (Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Family Services (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 675, 693.) “In ruling upon a motion to tax costs, the trial court's first determination is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face. ‘If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show [the costs] to be unnecessary or unreasonable.’ [citation.] (Foothill-De Anza Community College Dist. v. Emerich (2007 ) 158 Cal.App.4th 11, 29.)   

“[T]he mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a ‘proper objection’ to an item, the necessity of which appears doubtful, or which does not appear to be proper on its face. [citation.] However, ‘[i]f the items appear to be proper charges, the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the defendant [citations], and the burden of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable is upon the [objecting party].’ ” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.) The award of expert witness expenses where there is a failure to accept a pretrial 998 offer is discretionary, not automatic. (Santantonio v. Westinghouse Broadcasting Co., Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.4th 102, 121-24; Rouland v. Pacific Specialty Ins. Co. (2013) 220 Cal.4th 280, 289.) The expert fee recovery under Code of Civil Procedure §998 is limited to the reasonable fees and the claiming party has the burden to prove reasonableness. (Michelson v. Camp (1999) 72 Cal.4th 955, 974.)

a. Expert Witness Fees & Prejudgment Interest

Based on the Court’s finding that the Section 998 offer here is invalid, Defendant’s motion is proper with respect to $887,081.54 for prejudgment interest. The Court finds it is appropriate to tax $33,254.10 for expert witness fees (Item No. 8), and $887,081.54 for prejudgment interest (Item No. 16.)

b. Deposition Costs

In determining the need for the deposition costs, incurred, the Court must consider the issue “from the pretrial vantage point of a litigant” who does not yet know all of the testimony or issues that will be presented at trial. (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 132.) Plaintiff explains:

“As the driver of the vehicle, Plaintiff took Ms. Glasser-Weiner’s deposition and videotaped it. John Weiner was the registered owner of the vehicle that Mrs. Glasser-Weiner was driving at the time of the crash, but the extent of his involvement was not yet fully known to Plaintiff and his deposition was therefore necessary. Id. Finally, Plaintiff attempted to take the deposition of Sarah Glasser-Weiner, Defendant’s daughter, who was a passenger in the vehicle when the crash occurred. Id. The nature and extent of what Sarah Glasser-Weiner was clearly relevant to Plaintiff, particularly when Defendant was contesting liability.” 
(Opposition, p.17.)

The Court is satisfied with Plaintiff's explanation, and finds these costs are recoverable.

c. Service of Process Fees

Plaintiff seeks $21,903.76 for service of process fees. Defendant argues that amount is excessive because many of the witnesses were served multiple times. The Court finds this argument unavailing, and therefore that Plaintiff may recover. It is well-established that “[t]he amount actually incurred in effecting service of process by a registered process server is specifically recoverable under section 1033.5(a)(4)(B).” (Citizens for Responsible Development v. City of West Hollywood (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 409, 506.)

d. Transcript Costs

Plaintiff seeks $14,988.25 for transcripts. The Court finds Defendant’s motion is appropriate with respect to this expense. “[T]he fees of the court reporter for the preparation of a daily transcript, used for the trial, are not a proper item of costs unless such transcript has been expressly ordered by the court.” (Walton v. Bank of California, Nat. Assoc. (1963) 218 Cal.App.2d 527, 547-548.) The cost of transcripts is not recoverable if the transcripts were not ordered by the Court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(b)(5).) All amounts paid by a party for transcripts not made at public expense “shall be taxed as costs in the case.” (Govt. Code § 69953.)

e. Models, Enlargements, and Photocopies of Exhibits

Plaintiff seeks $10,500.00 for models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits. The Court finds that Plaintiff may recover for these costs as “reasonably helpful to aid the trier of fact.” (Cristler v. Express Messenger Systems, Inc. (2002) 171 Cal.App.4th 72, 90-91.) 

f. Other Costs

Plaintiff seeks $452.11 for postage. Postage fees are not recoverable. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(b)(3).)
Plaintiff seeks $243.42 for attorney/messenger service. Defendant’s arguments to strike Plaintiff’s $435.00 expense for a DME observer and $365.34 for an audio transcription are unconvincing. The Court exercises its discretion to award these costs.
In the opposition, Plaintiff provides sufficient documentation to support the $243.42 messenger service costs. (Hickey Decl. at ¶ 25(e), Ex. 18.)

RULING

The Court thus GRANTS IN PART Defendant’s motion to tax costs with respect to the following: $33,254.10 for expert witness fees (Item No. 8), $14,988.25 for transcripts (Item No 9), $887,081.54 for prejudgment interest (Item No. 16), and $452.11 for postage. The Court DENIES Defendant’s motion with respect to Plaintiff’s remaining costs.

Motion for New Trial

Miguel Ernesto Ortega Bautista, An Individual v. Michelle Janice Glaser Weiner, An Individual, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Michelle Janice Glaser Weiner
Responding Party: Plaintiff Miguel Ernesto Ortega Bautista
Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND

This is an automobile accident case wherein Plaintiff claims personal injuries. This matter went to trial. The jury returned a verdict for: $73,871.00 in past economic damages; $71,500.00 in future economic damages; $900,000.00 in past noneconomic damages; and $1,000,000.00 in future noneconomic damages.

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues the Court should order a new trial because the past and future noneconomic damages are excessive, as Plaintiff was able to return to his pre-accident activities. Defendant also argues a new trial is justified because of errors that took place during the trial that resulted in a miscarriage of justice, including overruling Defendant’s objection to Plaintiff’s special verdict form and sustaining Plaintiff’s objections to photos that Defendant attempted to publish for impeachment. (Motion for New Trial, p. 3.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s claim that the Court erred in overruling her objection to Plaintiff’s special verdict form is meritless, and moreover that Defendant failed to show any prejudice from the exclusion of improper impeachment photographs. (Opposition, p. 1-4.) 

REPLY

Defendant maintains that the Court erred in overruling Defendant’s objection and sustaining Plaintiff’s objection, and that the jury granted excessive non-economic damages. (Reply, p. 2-3.)

ANALYSIS

I. Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff submits five joint objections to the declarations of Juror No. 4 and Juror No. 5, submitted in support of Defendant’s Motion for New Trial. Plaintiff’s objections are overruled.
 
II. Motion for New Trial

A motion for new trial is a creature of statute; . . .” (Neal v. Montgomery Elevator Co.¿(1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1194, 1198.) A movant must satisfy Code of Civil Procedure §§ 657 and 659. Under Code of Civil Procedure § 657, a motion for new trial may be granted if there is any:¿

[¶] 1. Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury, or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial. [¶] 2. Misconduct of the jury; and whenever any one or more of the jurors have been induced to assent to any general or special verdict, or to a finding on any question submitted to them by the court, by a resort to the determination of chance, such misconduct may be proved by the affidavit of any one of the jurors. [¶] 3. Accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against. [¶] … [¶] 5. Excessive or inadequate damages. [¶] 6. Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law. [¶] 7. Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application.¿¿¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 657.)¿¿¿

Granting a new trial is not appropriate in this case, as Defendant fails to show an excessive verdict, that liability was improperly determined, or an error by the Court committed error.

With respect to the damages award, Seffert v. Los Angeles Transit Lines (1961) 45 Cal.2d 498, is instructive here, as the Supreme Court notes that “[t]he amount to be awarded is ‘a matter on which there legitimately may be a wide difference of opinion.’” (Seffert at 508.) Seffert emphasizes that verdicts should not be disturbed “unless it is so grossly disproportionate to any reasonable limit of compensation warranted by the facts that it shocks the court’s sense of justice and raises a presumption that it was the result of passion and prejudice.” (Id.) 

Applying Seffert, the Court finds that the jury verdict does not approach the level of excess under the circumstances in this case to give rise to a presumption of passion or prejudice by the jury. Here, there was credible testimony that Plaintiff would suffer from limitations relating to his injury and may need ongoing medical care for the rest of his life.  Given his age and testimony about how he has moderated several activities because of his injuries, the Court does not find that the award is grossly disproportionate to any reasonable limit or shocks the Court’s sense of justice.  

The two juror declarations provided by Defendant do not meaningfully undermine the validity of the jury deliberations in this case. Rather, the declarations acknowledge and establish that there was deliberation on noneconomic damages. However, the declarations do not evidence misconduct in this case.

Defendant relies on Neal v. Montgomery Elevator Co. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1194 to argue that the Court held that including language from jury instructions on a special verdict form was error. (Motion for New Trial, p. 7–8.) However, Defendant here mischaracterizes the reasoning in Neal. The court in Neal reasoned that “The jury in this case was provided with correct instructions and we see no reason why they had to be repeated in detail in the special verdict form” to support the conclusion that a new trial was not justified by the special verdict form’s lack of substantive instructions where the jury had already been instructed on the relevant law. (Neal v. Montgomery Elevator Co. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1194, 1199.) Neal does not, however, indicate that special verdict forms must avoid repeating correct legal elements that appeared in prior substantive jury instructions. 

The Court additionally finds it did not abuse its discretion by sustaining Plaintiff’s objection to Defendant’s photographs. Evidence Code § 352 empowers courts to exclude evidence if its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will create a substantial danger of undue prejudice, confusing the issues, or misleading the jury (People v. Anderson (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 851.) The balancing of evidence under this statute is undertaken on a sliding scale, where the more substantial the probative value of the evidence, the greater the danger of one of the excluding factors must be to warrant exclusion (People v. Castaneda-Prado (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 1260.). 

The trial court's discretion on the issue of admitting evidence is broad. (Meeks v. Autozone, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 855.) In this case, the Court entertained oral arguments on the issue of whether Defendant’s photographs represented impeachment evidence — and ultimately ruled that they were not impeachment and therefore were inadmissible. (Hickey Decl. at ¶3, Ex.2.) The Court here exercised its discretion to exclude the photographs, as they did not meaningfully contradict prior testimony. Even still, assuming the photographs were erroneously omitted, Defendant here does not establish how the Court’s decision caused prejudice to Defendant beyond harmless error. The Court therefor finds a new trial is not justified in this case.

RULING

Defendant’s motion for a new trial is DENIED.


Case Number: 19STCV28377    Hearing Date: November 1, 2024    Dept: T

Demurrer

Clive Duvall, an individual v. Lillian Horwitz, an individual, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Estate of Lillian Horwitz, Deceased ("The Estate")
Responding Party: Plaintiff Clive Duvall
Tentative Ruling: SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

BACKGROUND 

This matter arises from a car accident on August 14, 2017, wherein Plaintiff was injured in a motor vehicle accident between him and Decedent Lillian Horwitz ("Decedent"). (First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶ 9.) Decedent passed away on September 10, 2018. (FAC, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff is specifically seeking recovery against two of Decedent's insurance policies in effect at the time of the accident. (FAC., ¶¶ 18-19, 21.) The original complaint was filed on August 12, 2019. (FAC, ¶ 23.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

The Estate argues Plaintiff is not entitled to maintain the present action under Probate Code §§ 550 et seq. as the statute of limitations for an action under Probate Code §§ 550 et seq. expired on February 8, 2021. (Demurrer, p. 4.) The Estate therefore argues the Court should sustain demurrer without leave to amend as the sole cause of action within the complaint is barred by the applicable statute of limitations. The Estate also argues that the relation back doctrine does not apply because Plaintiff cannot show that he was genuinely ignorant that Decedent's claims were covered by insurance at the time he filed the Complaint. (Demurrer, p. 7.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues the demurrer should be overruled because the action is timely filed against Decedent, specifically because the substitution of the estate as defendant constitutes a “continuance” of the action and is not subject to the one-year limitations period in Probate Code § 551 and relates back to the original date of filing. (Opposition, p. 5.)

REPLY

The Estate argues that Plaintiff never properly commenced an action against Decedent or the Estate because Decedent passed away almost a year before Plaintiff filed the present lawsuit — therefor Plaintiff could not "continue" the action against Decedent because it was not pending at the time of her death. (Reply, p. 2.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Judicial Notice 

As an initial matter, the Court takes judicial notice of Plaintiff’s declaration, the declarations of his prior and current counsel, and of his own ex parte filing. (Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibits 1 through 4.) The Estate’s objections are accordingly overruled.

II. Pertinent Facts and Procedural Events

The following facts and events are drawn from the First-Amended Complaint and this Court’s docket.

The subject collision occurred on August 14, 2017.  Lillian Horwitz and Nathan Horwitz owned and Lillian Horwitz was driving the vehicle and that collided with Plaintiff’s vehicle.  Unbeknownst to Plaintiff, Lillian Horwitz died on September 10, 2018.  Plaintiff filed complaint naming Lillian and Nathan Horwitz on August 12, 2019, two days before the statute of limitations expired.  

Based on the date that Lillian Horwitz passed away, the last day to file a claim against the Estate of Lillian Horwitz was September 10, 2018.  C.C.P. § 366.2. 

Defendant Nathan Horwitz filed his answer on March 27, 2020.  Even though the matter had been pending for more than a year, Plaintiff’s counsel claims she first learned that Lillian Horwitz died sometime in November 2020. [Panah Decl. filed March 9, 2023]  There is no explanation as to why it took so long to discover this fact.  

At least three months after Plaintiff counsel admits to learning that Lillian Horwitz passed away, the time to file a Probate Code § 550 action against the Estate of Lillian Horwitz (accounting for the tolling provided under Emergency Rule 9) expired on February 8, 2021.  
On July 14, 2022, the Court dismissed Defendant Lillian Horwitz for failure to file a proof of service.  At this point in time, the only named defendant was Nathan Horwitz.  On October 1, 2022, Nathan Horwitz died.  

On February 2, 2023, more than two years after learning that Lillian Horwitz had died and more than four years after the death, Plaintiff amended his complaint to name the Estate of Lillian Horwitz, naming the estate as Doe 1.  Plaintiff never filed a proof of service relating to the estate.  

The parties stipulated to add the Estate of Nathan Horwitz as a defendant under Probate Code § 550 on February 16, 2023.  Of note, the stipulation stated, “Nathan Horwitz was the owner of the vehicle Lillian Horwitz was driving at the time of the subject accident, and he was not in the vehicle at the time the subject accident.”  This language would appear to have no operative effect other than to establish that any claims against the estate would be limited to $15,000 under Vehicle Code § 17151.

On March 28, 2023, the Court granted a motion to dismiss by the Estate of Lillian Horwitz.  In responses to a request for clarification, by way of an April 14, 2023 minute order, the Court stated “Specially Appearing Doe Defendant 1 – Estate of Lillian Horwitz, Deceased’s Motion to Dismiss by Quashing the February 14, 2023 Service of Summons and Complaint (filed 03/03/2023) Upon It Because of Untimely Service was heard and granted by the Court on 03/28/2023.  

In a March 28, 2023 ruling on the Motion to Dismiss, Judge Feeney stated:

“Here, there is no basis for relating the Doe Amendment back to the filing date of the original complaint.  Here, Plaintiff had actual knowledge of Lillian Horowitz’s [sic] death before the statute of limitation ran around February 14, 2021.  At minimum, based on the ex parte application filed in this action, Plaintiff had knowledge of Lillian Horowitz’s [sic] death as of December 2020.”  

The Court finished its ruling by stating:  

“The case as to the Estate of Lillian Horowitz [sic] is now dismissed with prejudice both due to the failure to comply with the statute of limitations and failure to timely serve within the three-year period.”

On December 15, 2023, a different bench officer granted Plaintiff leave to file an amended complaint to name the Estate of Lillian Horwitz.  Notably, the Court did not attempt to accord the prior ruling finding a violation of the statute of limitation and the failure to timely serve the complaint or the finding that claims against the Estate did not relate back to the original complaint.  Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint on December 19, 2023.  

The Court granted Defendant Estate of Lillian Horwitz’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons on June 13, 2024.  However, after considering arguments made in a hearing on July 25, 2024 addressing Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Order of Court, this Court concluded that the most appropriate procedural approach to dealing with the issues presented was by way of a demurrer rather than a motion to quash.  Accordingly, the Court vacated its June 13, 2024 order and ordered Defendant to file a responsive pleading to the First Amended Complaint.  

III. Demurrer

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.)  
Plaintiff is pursuing this action under Probate Code § 550, et. seq. (FAC ¶ 17.) 

Probate Code §§ 550 et seq. allows a plaintiff to pursue an action against the insurance policy of a deceased defendant without having to initiate probate so long as the damages sought are within policy limits and plaintiff complies with a series of requirements, including service requirements and a specific statute of limitations for actions under these sections. 

Probate Code § 551 provides: “Notwithstanding Section 366.2 of the Code of Civil Procedure, if the limitations period otherwise applicable to the action has not expired at the time of the decedent’s death, an action under this chapter may be commenced within one year after the expiration of the limitations period otherwise applicable.”

Under Probate Code § 552(a), a summons for an action against a decedent’s insurance policy must be served on a person designated in writing by the insurer or, if none, on the insurer itself. Under Code of Civil Procedure § 583.210(a), the summons and complaint must be served upon a defendant within three years after the complaint is filed.

Statute of limitations

Any action seeking to recover under Probate Code § 550 et seq. must be commenced within one year after the expiration of the limitations period applicable, so long as the applicable limitations period had not expired at the time of Decedent’s death. (Prob. Code § 551.) The statute of limitations for Plaintiff’s causes of action, general negligence and motor vehicle negligence, is two years from the date of injury. (Code Civ. Proc. § 335.1.) 

Here, the subject accident took place on August 14, 2017. The limitations period for Plaintiff’s action would have expired on August 14, 2019. However, Decedent died on September 10, 2018, before the limitations period expired, therefore triggering Prob. Code § 551. Section 551 extended the statute of limitations applicable to Decedent to August 14, 2020, two and one years after the limitations period began to run. In addition, Emergency Rule 9(a) Related to COVID-19 is also applicable. The Rule provided that “Notwithstanding any other law, the statutes of limitations and repose for civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 26 1, 2020.” Consequently, 178 days need to be added to the calculation of the statute of limitations under § 551. Therefore, the statute of limitations to bring a cause of action limited to the insurance policy against Decedent was February 8, 2021. Defendant contends that the FAC, filed on December 19, 2023, is time-barred.

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that the FAC relates back to the action’s original filing date of August 12, 2019. “[W]hen a complaint is amended only to identify a party by its proper name, the gravamen of the complaint remains unaltered, and hence the later pleading relates back to the earlier pleading.” (Barrington, supra, 39 Cal.3d at 154; see also Burgos v. Tamulonis (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 757, 763.)

Plaintiff here relies on Burgos v. Tamulonis (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 757, 762-763 (“Burgos”)) to argue that where an action is timely filed against decedent, the substitution of the estate as defendant constitutes a ‘continuance’ of that action and relates back to the original date of filing.”  However, this reliance is misplaced. Plaintiff here — unlike in Burgos — has not filed any creditor claim and is not pursuing this action under Probate Code §§ 9000 et seq., but instead exclusively relies on Probate Code §§ 550 et seq. 

There are additional facts in this case that provide additional grounds to distinguish Burgos.  In Burgos, Plaintiff filed his personal injury complaint on December 10, 1991 and attempted to serve Defendant three times before completing substituted service on January 20, 1992.  Plaintiff’s counsel then notified the Defendant’s insurer that service had been affected.  On February 20, 1992, Plaintiff learned for the first time that Defendant had passed away on March 3, 1991.  Just over two weeks later, Plaintiff petitioned the probate department fore leave to file a late claim.  Leave was granted and Plaintiff filed a creditor’s claim just over a month later.  Plaintiff then promptly sought leave to name the personal representative of the defendant’s estate in the personal injury action.  The entire process took just over four months.

In analyzing the demurrer based on then Code of Civil Procedure § 353 (now Code of Civil Procedure § 366.2), the Burgos Court explained:

“What we are saying is that the legislative scheme encompasses exactly the type of situation that arose in this case where it is uncontroverted the creditor had no actual knowledge of the administration of the estate, did not even know of the death, and timely commenced the action (under both § 340, subd. (3) and § 353) naming Moscaritolo as defendant. This would be the expected course of action in any situation where there is no known administration of an estate, including the situation where there is never an administration of the estate.  Having timely commenced the action and having been granted leave to file the late claim against the estate, Burgos was entitled to continue pursuit of her cause of action by the standard method of amending the complaint to name Tamulonis as defendant in her representative capacity. This the trial court also properly allowed. ”  Id. at 762-763 (citation and footnotes omitted).

The Burgos Court also addressed concerns over endless limitations periods if Plaintiff’s were provided unlimited time to file amended complaints:

“Contrary to Tamulonis's contention made at oral argument, there is no open-ended period for actions or claims under the present scheme of law involving statutes for which administration has been commenced. Any open-ended possibility is precluded by a combination of rules including the requirement the action be filed within one year after the decedent's death (§ 353, now § 366.2), the rule that failure to file a claim bars the claim and action (Prob. Code, §§ 9002, subd. (b), 9351) and the late claim filing time limits, generally within one year of issuance of letters of administration, before a final distribution order and before the expiration of the one year after death limitation of section 353 (now § 366.2) for filing an action (Prob. Code, § 9103; see also Prob. Code, §§ 9053, 9392).”  Id. at footnote 7.

The facts in this case are starkly different than Burgos.  Here, there are no apparent attempts to ever serve Ms. Horwitz by any means.  Plaintiff’s counsel admits she first learned that Lillian Horwitz died sometime in November 2020. [Panah Decl. filed March 9, 2023]  At that time, Plaintiff had three months to amend the complaint to name the Estate of Lillian Horwitz and continue the action under Probate Code § 550.  Unlike the Plaintiff in Burgos who took affirmative steps to protect his rights in just two weeks, Plaintiff waited more than two years.  This Court cannot align these facts with Burgos, where the Court found that “the legislative scheme encompasses exactly the type of situation that arose in this case.”  To the contrary, Plaintiff slept on his rights and allowed critical deadlines to pass.  This conduct is not “what the legislative scheme encompasses.”  

Similarly, while the Burgos Court noted several rules precluding an “open-ended” limitations period, allowing Plaintiff to proceed against the Estate based on an amended complaint filed more than five years after Ms. Horwitz died and three years after learning of the death would be the equivalent of endorsing an endless limitations period.  

The Court is unpersuaded by the claims of estoppel.  While there may have been settlement negotiations suggesting that the insurance carrier was offering money over the $15,000 exposure faced by Mr. Horvitz, there are no allegations that Defendant’s insurers and attorneys misled Plaintiff about the need to protect his rights.  It is common for plaintiffs to file complaints to protect the statute of limitations while settlement discussions are ongoing and it is similarly common for parties to stipulate to extend the time to file responsive pleadings because settlement appears imminent.   

RULING 

This Court concludes that Judge Feeney was correct in ruling that there was no basis for relating the Doe Amendment back to the filing date of the original complaint and dismissing the claims against the Estate, with prejudice, “both due to the failure to comply with the statute of limitations and failure to timely serve within the three-year period.”  

This Court now reaches the same conclusions, vacates any past orders reaching contrary results, and sustains Defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend.

RULING

Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.

Motion for Summary Adjudication

Teresa Sorensen v. Charter Communications (CCI), Inc. dba Spectrum

Moving Party: Cross-Complainant Temple Israel of Hollywood 
Responding Party: Cross-Defendant Charter Communications 

Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND 

This is a trip and fall action. Plaintiff Teresa Sorensen (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint alleging negligence and premises liability against Defendants Charter Communications (“Charter”) and Temple Israel of Hollywood (“TIOH”). At the time of the incident, TIOH had leased the property to Charter, where Plaintiff was returning her cable equipment. Pursuant to their lease agreement, Charter is required to indemnify and defend TIOH from any claims arising out of Charter’s use or occupancy of the premises. On December 31, 2021, January 4, 2022, February 16, 2023, and May 19, 2023, TIOH tendered its defense to Charter, which has failed to respond to TIOH’s tender demands or defend TIOH in the action. (UMF #13-16). The pertinent language of the lease says as follows: 

“Except for Lessor’s gross negligence or willful misconduct, Lessee shall indemnify, protect, defend and hold harmless the Premises, Lessor and its agents, Lessor’s master or ground lessor, and Lenders, from and against any and all claims, loss of rents and/or damages, liens, judgments, penalties, attorneys’ and consultants’ fees, expenses and/or liabilities, arising out of the use and/or occupancy of the Premises by Lessee. If any action or proceeding is brought against Lessor by reason of any of the foregoing matters, Lessee shall upon notice defend the same at Lessee’s expense by counsel reasonably satisfactory to Lessor and Lessor shall cooperate with Lessee in such defense. Lessor need not have first paid any such claim in order to be defended or indemnified.” (UMF #9; Ex. 6 and 7 ¶ 8.7; emphasis added).

The lease was amended on April 10, 2012 to include, in part, a subrogation waiver, which states the following: 

“Neither Lessee nor Lessor shall be liable for loss or damage caused by fire or other perils covered or normally covered by insurance policies maintained or required to be maintained by this Lease by the other party with respect to the Premises, the Building or any personal property contained in the same, and each party on behalf of itself and any insurer, waives all rights of subrogation against the other with respect to those perils. This waiver of liability and subrogation shall apply regardless of the negligence of either party and shall not be limited by the amount of insurance coverage carried by either party. The provisions of this Section shall survive the expiration or earlier termination of this Lease.” (Charter’s Opposition, p. 3.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

TIOH argues Charter owes it a contractual duty to defend against Plaintiff’s claims, triggered when TIOH tendered its defense on December 31, 2021. TIOH argues the lease clearly obligates Charter to defend TIOH against Plaintiff’s claims, and asserts that granting this motion is necessary to prevent deprivation of TIOH’s contractual right to a defense. (Motion for Summary Adjudication, p. 5.) TIOH further argues Charter is not excused from its duty to defend by the subrogation waiver included in the amendment to the lease. (Motion for Summary Adjudication, p. 7.)

OPPOSITION 

Charter argues that the Court should not grant TIOH’s motion because TIOH has failed to properly authenticate the lease agreement it relies on to establish the duty. (Opposition, p. 4.) Further, Charter argues that the April 10, 2012 amendment to the lease subrogates Charter’s obligation to defend TIOH. (Opposition, p. 7.)

REPLY

TIOH argues that contractual duty to defend is a proper subject for summary adjudication, that substantial uncontradicted evidence authenticates the operative Lease, the subrogation waiver does not abrogate Charter’s duty to defend TIOH against Plaintiff’s claims, and Charter’s duty to defend may be summarily adjudicated based on Plaintiff’s allegations regardless of whether its distinct duty to indemnify is later triggered or exonerated. (Reply, p. 2.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Evidentiary Objections 

TIOH submits 10 objections to evidence supporting summary adjudication. TIOH’s objections are overruled. Additionally, Charter submits 4 objections to TIOH’s motion for summary adjudication. Charter’s objections are overruled.

II. Summary Adjudication

A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims of damages and issues of duty. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(f)(1).) "A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment." (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(f)(2).) Pursuant to CCP 437(f)(1) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”

Parties to a contract have great freedom to allocate responsibilities for each other's legal defense of third-party claims as they see fit, subject to public policy and established rules of contract interpretation. (Crawford v. Weather Shield Mfg., Inc. (2008) 44 Cal.4th 541.) In general, an agreement establishing one party's responsibility for the other's legal defense when a third-party claim is made against the latter is construed under the same rules as govern the interpretation of other contracts. (Id. at 552.) “Unless the parties' agreement expressly provides otherwise, a contractual indemnitor has the obligation, upon proper tender by the indemnitee, to accept and assume the indemnitee's active defense against claims encompassed by the indemnity provision.” (Id. at 555.)

In this case, the only cause of action that can be summarily adjudicated is TIOH’s sixth cause of action for duty to defend. The language of the lease agreement here plainly creates a duty to defend. (See Crawford v. Weather Shield Mfg., Inc. (2008) 44 Cal.4th 541, 549) (holding a subcontract gave rise to the immediate duty to defend against claims insofar as they concerned the party’s conduct regardless of any findings of negligence.) Specifically, the language of the contract states that “Lessee shall… defend… Lessor and its agents… from and against any and all claims, loss of rents and/or damages, liens, judgments, penalties, attorneys’ and consultants’ fees, expenses and/or liabilities, arising out of the use and/or occupancy of the Premises by Lessee.” (Plaintiff’s Index of Exhibits in Opposition to Summary Judgment, Exhibit A.) Plaintiff’s complaint against TIOH in this case implicates the language giving rise to a duty to defend under the original language of the lease agreement; indeed, Charter does not purport to argue otherwise, but instead argues it is not obligated to defend TIOH because of the subsequent April 2012 amendment to the lease. However, the amended language of the lease can only be reasonably interpreted to apply to damages caused to the property itself and has no bearing on the present motion. Summary adjudication is thus appropriate with respect to TIOH’s sixth cause of action for duty to defend.

However, TIOH’s remaining causes of action also cannot currently be summarily adjudicated, as the motion does not completely dispose of them, as even if liability is found, the cause of action damages would remain an issue that needs to be proven. The Court thus finds summary adjudication is not appropriate with respect to TIOH’s remaining causes of action.

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, TIOH’s motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED with respect to the sixth cause of action for duty to defend. TIOH’s motion for summary adjudication with respect to remaining causes of action are DENIED.

Motion for Summary Judgement

Jean-Pierre Boccara v. City Of Los Angeles, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Simon J. Thomas; Laura Sheppard
Responding Party: Plaintiff Jean-Pierre Boccara

Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND 

This is a trip and fall case wherein Plaintiff tripped on the sidewalk adjacent to an apartment complex containing Defendants’ unit. 

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendants argue they did not owe a duty to maintain the sidewalk because they lacked ownership or control over it. (Motion for Summary Judgement, p. 9.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues disputed facts exist as to defendants’ ownership and control of the land abutting the sidewalk. (Opposition, p. 9.) Plaintiff additionally argues there are disputed facts as to whether Defendants’ plants caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s fall and whether Defendants had notice.

REPLY

Defendants argue that nothing in Plaintiff’s opposition negates the arguments presented in the original motion for summary judgment: namely, that Defendants' entire argument has to do with ownership of the sidewalk where the incident is alleged to have occurred. (Reply, p.2.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Judicial Notice

As an initial matter, the Court takes judicial notice of the Los Angeles County Assessor Parcel Information for APN 5523005025, 646 Wilcox Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90004.

II. Summary Judgement

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.  

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998 (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Civil Code § 1714(a).) In order to impose liability on a property owner for a dangerous condition, the owner must have either actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition or have been able to discover the condition by the exercise of ordinary care. (See Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at 1206.) “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.) 

A. Defendants Meet their Initial Burden 

An abutting property owner will be responsible for the condition on a public sidewalk if that owner has performed construction or altered the sidewalk for the unique benefit or his property. (Sexton v. Brooks (1952) 39 Cal.2d 153, 157-158.) And the abutting landowner is liable for his or her own affirmative acts on the sidewalk that create a hazardous condition, such as by dropping waste or a slippery substance that a passerby may fall on. (Kopfinger v. Grand Central Public Market (1964) 60 Cal.2d 852, 857).

Defendants make a prima facie showing that they do not own the property outside of their unit and that they do not maintain the landscaping or perform any construction — relieving them of a duty to Plaintiff. Namely, Defendants’ expert George Mekhaiel reviewed property maps, tax maps, and personally surveyed the area identified as the location of the fall. (Mekhaiel Decl ¶4-6). He confirmed that this was a "'city sidewalk" by determining the City of Los Angeles owned the sidewalk. (Mekhaiel Decl. ¶7.) Defendants also attested not to exert control over the sidewalk. (Decl. of Simon Thomas ¶7; Decl. of Laura Sheppard ¶7).

B. Plaintiff Fails to Raise a Triable Issue of Material Fact

Plaintiff here fails to provide any evidence to show a triable issue may exist with respect to the issue of Defendants’ ownership of the property outside their area. Plaintiff relies on HOA Restrictions (Exhibit List, Exh. A) to argue common areas were owned by Defendants and that “it cannot be said as a matter of law that Defendants did not own or control the land abutting the sidewalk where Plaintiff tripped and fell.” (Opposition, p. 9.) While the HOA restrictions convey a fractional property interest in common areas, it does not show a triable issue regarding whether Defendants owned or controlled it.

Plaintiff’s only other evidence to suggest Defendants’ ownership is in the HOA interrogatory responses, where “Responding Party [HOA] contends that the adjacent property owners Simon Thomas and Laura Sheppard, had an obligation to notify Responding Party and the City of Los Angeles, of any condition that needed repairs.” (Wilshire Country Manor Owners Association’s Responses To Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, p. 19-20.) This is insufficient to bring into issue whether Defendants owned the property outside their unit. Rather, the response reflects obligations between Defendants and the HOA regarding providing notice of any issues in the HOA’s common areas.  

Based on the undisputed facts, the Court is not persuaded by Plaintiff’s argument that Defendants have a nondelegable duty to protect Plaintiff from harm.

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion for summary judgement is GRANTED.

Joey To vs Tesla, Inc. dba Tesla Motors, Inc.

Moving Party: Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Tesla”)
Responding Party: Plaintiff Joey To

Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

In August of 2021, Plaintiff purchased a new Model S from Tesla, accompanied by Tesla’s four-year, 50,000-mile basic warranty and eight-year, 100,000-mile Powertrain Warranty. Plaintiff alleges the car began experiencing significant problems that substantially impaired its use and which Tesla was unable to repair to conform to its warranties within a reasonable number of attempts. Plaintiff brought this action against Tesla alleging Breach of Express and Implied Warranty, as well as violation of the Song-Beverly Act § 1793.2. (See Complaint, Ex. 2 to Ameripour Decl.) 

In buying the car, Plaintiff signed a seven-page “Retail Installment Sale Contract” which included an arbitration agreement. (Kim Decl. ¶ 3 & Sale Contract, Exhibit 1 to Kim Decl.) The arbitration provision provides, in part:

“1. EITHER YOU OR WE MAY CHOOSE TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN US DECIDED BY ARBITRATION AND NOT IN COURT OR BY JURY TRIAL. 
2. IF A DISPUTE IS ARBITRATED, YOU WILL GIVE UP YOUR RIGHT TO PARTICIPATE AS A CLASS REPRESENTATIVE OR CLASS MEMBER ON ANY CLASS CLAIM YOU MAY HAVE AGAINST US INCLUDING ANY RIGHT TO CLASS ARBITRATION OR ANY CONSOLIDATION OF INDIVIDUAL ARBITRATIONS. 
3. DISCOVERY AND RIGHTS TO APPEAL IN ARBITRATION ARE GENERALLY MORE LIMITED THAN IN A LAWSUIT, AND OTHER RIGHTS THAT YOU AND WE WOULD HAVE IN COURT MAY NOT BE AVAILABLE IN ARBITRATION.

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action. […]” 
(Sale Contract, Ex. 1 to Kim Decl., page 7.)

The arbitration provision also includes language that Tesla “will pay your filing, administration, service or case management fee and your arbitrator or hearing fee all up to a maximum of $5,000, unless the law or the rules of the chosen arbitration organization require us to pay more.” (See Declaration of Luis A. Serrano Exhibit 1, ¶ 4.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Tesla argues the Court should stay Plaintiff’s claims and compel arbitration, asserting FAA and California Law require compliance of Plaintiff’s agreement to arbitrate with Tesla, as Plaintiff’s claims fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration clause. (Motion to Compel, p. 4-5, 11.) 

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues that the arbitration provision is unconscionable — both procedurally and substantively — and imposes terms that make arbitration more costly and onerous than proceedings in Superior Court. (Opposition, p. 2, 4-5.) 

REPLY

Tesla contends that the agreement to arbitrate is not unconscionable procedurally or substantively. (Reply, p. 2.) Tesla further emphasizes that California has a strong public policy in favor of arbitration. (Id. at 7.)

ANALYSIS

I. Judicial Notice 

The Court takes judicial notice of Plaintiff Joey To’s Complaint, filed on or about July 8, 2024. (Tesla Request for Judicial Notice, p. 2.)
 
II. Motion to Compel Arbitration

California law reflects a strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a relatively quick and inexpensive method for resolving disputes. To further that policy, California Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2 requires a trial court to enforce a written arbitration agreement unless one of three limited exceptions applies. Those statutory exceptions arise where (1) a party waives the right to arbitration; (2) grounds exist for revoking the arbitration agreement; and (3) pending litigation with a third party creates the possibility of conflicting rulings on common factual or legal issues.” (Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 959, 967 [citations omitted]; Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)  

In deciding a petition to compel arbitration, trial courts must decide first whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then determine the second gateway issue whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) The party seeking arbitration has the “burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842.)¿The trial court “sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Id.) General principles of contract law govern whether parties have entered a binding agreement to arbitrate. (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236; see also Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)  Whether there is a written agreement to arbitrate is a matter of contract, and courts must enforce arbitration contracts according to their terms. (Banc of California, National Association v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 357, 366.) 

Plaintiff here does not raise any arguments regarding the existence of the arbitration agreement, whether he signed the agreement, or the scope of the arbitration agreement.  Rather, he argues that the arbitration provision is unconscionable. As the parties do not dispute the existence or scope of the arbitration agreement, the Court will discuss whether the terms of the arbitration agreement are unconscionable.

A. Unconscionability

“[P]rocedural and substantive unconscionability must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability.” (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 102.) The courts invoke a sliding scale which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves, i.e., the more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to conclude that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa. (Id. at p. 114.) Plaintiff bears the burden of proving that the arbitration agreement is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable. This case thus turns on whether Plaintiff can prove that his arbitration agreement with Tesla is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable.

i. Procedural Unconscionability

“Procedural unconscionability focuses on the elements of oppression and surprise. [Citations] ‘Oppression arises from an inequality of bargaining power which results in no real negotiation and an absence of meaningful choice … Surprise involves the extent to which the terms of the bargain are hidden in a ‘prolix printed form’ drafted by a party in a superior bargaining position.’ [Citations]” (Roman v. Superior Court (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.) 

Here, Plaintiff has not provided sufficient evidence to establish procedural unconscionability. Plaintiff argues the contract was one of adhesion, presented on a "take it or leave it" basis that deprived him of meaningful opportunity to negotiate. (Opposition, p. 5.) However, Plaintiff’s characterization of the agreement is not borne out in the evidence on the record. Namely, the court in Sanchez explicitly refutes the argument that an agreement’s status as a contract of adhesion automatically constitutes procedural unconscionability; rather, the court emphasizes that the “manner in which the disputed clause is presented to the party in the weaker bargaining position....’” is determinative of procedural unconscionability. (Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 931.) Sanchez further explained that “there is no reason in this case to conclude that Plaintiff lacked power to bargain. In general, nothing prevents purchasers of ... vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that Plaintiff could not bargain in this case.” (Id.) The reasoning in Sanchez directly applies here — as there is no evidence that the arbitration clause was non-negotiable or that Plaintiff attempted to negotiate such terms and was denied.  (See Bolanos v. Khalatian (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1586 [holding that arbitration agreement was enforceable because the plaintiff’s declaration did not say that she could not read or understand the agreement and because she did not offer any evidence that she was forced or tricked into signing the agreement].) Plaintiff has not provided a declaration that he attempted to ask questions about or negotiate the arbitration agreement, nor that he was denied such requests.  

Plaintiff also argues that the arbitration agreement is procedurally unconscionable because it did not include a copy of the relevant arbitration rules. This argument is unavailing. The failure to attach or provide the AAA rules to the arbitration agreement is not, in itself, a sufficient ground to support a finding of procedural unconscionability.  For example, in Lane v. Francis Capital Management LLC (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 676, 690, the Court of Appeal concluded: “[T]he failure to attach a copy of the AAA rules did not render the agreement procedurally unconscionable. There could be no surprise, as the arbitration rules referenced in the agreement were easily accessible to the parties—the AAA rules are available on the Internet.” The arbitration agreement here explicitly draws attention to the American Arbitration Association rules and provides instructions to find them. Thus, there are no indications of procedural unconscionability.   

ii. Substantive Unconscionability

“Substantive unconscionability focuses on the actual terms of the agreement and evaluates whether they create ‘overly harsh’ or ‘‘one-sided’ results’ [Citations] that is, whether contractual provisions reallocate risks in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.  [Citation] Substantive unconscionability ‘may take various forms,’ but typically is found in the employment context when the arbitration agreement is ‘one-sided’ in favor of the employer without sufficient justification, for example, when ‘the employee’s claims against the employer, but not the employer’s claims against the employee, are subject to arbitration.’ [Citations]” (Roman, supra, 172 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1469-1470.) 

Plaintiff has not demonstrated the agreement as substantively unconscionable. Plaintiff argues that the arbitration agreement, by providing the party electing arbitration to “choose any of the following arbitration organization and its applicable rules,” is substantively unconscionable because it allows Tesla the choice of organization and applicable rules. (Opposition, p. 6.) In doing so, Plaintiff mistakenly relies on Chavarria v. Ralphs (9th Cir. 2013) 733 F.3d 916 (“Even if it were the case that Ralphs' policy does not guarantee that Ralphs will always be the party with the final selection, the selection process is not one designed to produce a true neutral in any individual case.”). Part of the issue in Chavarria was that the arbitration selection provision would always produce an arbitrator proposed by Ralphs and that the provision precluded institutional arbitration administrators like AAA or JAMS which had established rules and procedures to select a neutral arbitrator.  Here, Tesla’s arbitration agreement does not preclude the selection of neutral arbitrators at qualified institutions like AAA or JAMS.   

Plaintiff also argues that the arbitration cost provision is substantively unconscionable and would impose prohibitive costs on Plaintiff.  However, the arbitration agreement states: “We will pay your filing, administration, service or case management fee and your arbitrator or hearing fee all up to a maximum of $5000, unless the law or the rules of the chosen arbitration organization require us to pay more.” (See Sale Contract, Ex. 1 to Kim Decl., page 7, line 23-24.) As such, it is unclear how the arbitration cost provision is unconscionable, nor is it clear that arbitration would impose a higher financial burden on Plaintiff.

RULING

As there are no indications of procedural or substantive unconscionability, the motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.  The matter is stayed to permit the parties to proceed with arbitration.  The Court sets a Post-Arbitration Status Conference for October 7, 2025 at 8:30 a.m.
