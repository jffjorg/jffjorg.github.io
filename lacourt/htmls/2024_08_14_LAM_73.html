Dept. 73

CULBERSON-STOWERS, INC., et al. v. TOYOTA MOTOR CREDIT CORPORATION (22STCV25204)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs: Jonathan Micheals (MLG Attorneys at Law, APLC)

Counsel for Defendant: Brian Paino (Hinshaw & Culbertson LLP)

 

(1)   DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION (filed 2/09/2024) 

(2)   MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL AS TO PLAINTIFF RICHARD STOWERS (filed 7/09/2024) 

(3)   MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL AS TO PLAINTIFF CULBERSON-STOWERS, INC. (filed 7/09/2024) 

(4)   MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL AS TO PLAINTIFF CULBERSON-RENTAL AND LEASING CO., INC. (filed 7/09/2024) 

(5)   defendant’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories, set one AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed 1/08/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

The Motions to be Relieved as Counsel as to Plaintiffs Richard Stowers, Culberson-Stowers, Inc., and Culberson-Rental and Leasing Co., Inc. are GRANTED.

 

Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, is MOOT.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 4, 2022, Plaintiffs Culberstone-Stowers, Inc.; Culberson Rental and Leasing Co., Inc.; and Richard Stowers, filed a complaint against Defendant Toyota Motor Credit Corporation (“Defendant” or TMCC”) and Does 1-25, inclusive.  

 

On September 12, 2022, Defendant filed a demurrer to the complaint, which was rendered moot by Plaintiffs’ filing of the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on October 19, 2022. 

 

The FAC alleges the following causes of action:  

1.        Breach of Contract;  

2.      Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing;  

3.      Civil Conspiracy – Fraud;  

4.      Violation of California Commercial Code; and 

5.      Conversion

On March 7, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer as to the FAC’s third and fifth causes of action, and overruled as to the first, second, and fourth causes of action.

A.      Allegations in the First Amended Complaint

The FAC alleges the following.

Plaintiff Culberson-Stowers, Inc., was a family-owned car dealership in business for over 90 years. The Stowers Family, Mr. Dick Stowers and his wife, Dot Stowers, together with their son Richard W. Stowers, Jr., ran a new and used car dealership in Pampa, Texas (the “Dealership” and/or “Culberson-Stowers”). Plaintiff Culberson Rental and Leasing Co., Inc., owned the real property where the Dealership was located (the “Real Property”). (FAC, ¶¶ 9-11.)

Defendant is an automobile dealership creditor, engaged in the purchase, sale, finance, and service of consumer vehicles with the public. (FAC, ¶ 12.)

On or about February 9, 2017, Plaintiffs entered in an Inventory Loan and Security Agreement with Defendant. The Inventory Loan was secured against the Dealership inventory and other collateral. (FAC, ¶ 13.)

Plaintiffs subsequently delayed in making certain payments under the terms and conditions of the Inventory Loan. (FAC, ¶ 23.) II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment

 

a.      Legal Standard

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.  In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) 

 

“The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

c.       Discussion

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment of Plaintiffs’ remaining first, second and fourth causes of action arguing that Plaintiffs have failed to provide evidence establishing their damages, an essential element of all Plaintiffs’ causes of action.

 

The FAC alleges that given Plaintiffs’ breach of their payment obligations to Defendant, Defendant commenced a foreclosure sale on November 1, 2018, and sold collateral in ten categories: (1) accounts; (2) inventory; (3) equipment and fixtures; (4) contract rights; (5) intellectual property; (6) deposit accounts; (7) investment property; (8) negotiable collateral; (9) chattel paper; and (10) “[p]roceeds of the foregoing” (the “Collateral”). (FAC, ¶ 27.)

The FAC further alleges Defendant deprived Plaintiffs of “fair and rightful compensation from the sale” by selling the Collateral “at a grossly unfair and inadequate price.” (FAC, ¶¶ 42, 49.) Plaintiffs allege that before the Dealership was managed by 805 Hobart, Inc., the company that managed the Dealership prior to the closing, the value of the collateral was no less than $7,267,488.00. While 805 Hobart, Inc managed the Dealership, and while being monitored by TMCC, 805 Hobart, Inc. illegally and fraudulently appropriated the Dealership’s Collateral, thereby reducing its value by no less than $3,750,000.00. (FAC, ¶ 27.)

The FAC further alleges “at the time of the sale, the market value of the Collateral was not less than $3,500,000.00. However, TMCC sold the Collateral to Country II for $2,117,493; over $1,350,000.00 less than the Collateral was worth at the time of the sale, and over 5 million dollars less than what the Collateral was worth before 805 Hobart, Inc. began “managing” the Dealership with TMCC’s oversight.” (FAC, ¶ 29.)

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs do not possess needed evidence to support an essential element of their claims, specifically damages. Defendant asserts that it can rely on Plaintiffs’ factually devoid discovery responses to shift the burden to Plaintiffs to show that a triable issue of material facts exists. (Union Bank v. Super. Ct. (1995) 31 Cal. App. 4th 573, 590.) On April 14, 2023, Defendant propounded thirteen special interrogatories to Plaintiffs. (McClure Decl. ¶ 3.) On May 23, 2023, Plaintiffs served their responses, which consisted solely of boilerplate objections. (McClure Decl. ¶ 3.) On October 20, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel to provide further responses and Defendant’s request for sanctions. (Court’s 10/20/23 Minute Order.) The Court ordered Plaintiff to “respond to the interrogatories without objection and with code-compliant verified responses.” (Ibid.) Defendant asserts that Plaintiff served supplemental responses that remained evasive, did not comply with the California Code, and violated the Court’s order. Critically, Defendant contends that the supplemental responses did not contain substantive information about Plaintiffs’ purported damages. (McClure Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

For example, in response to Defendant’s interrogatories related to Plaintiffs’ damages, including the market value of the Collateral, how the market value was calculated, and an explanation for any discrepancies between the purported market value and what was paid for each unit, Plaintiff responded:

 

“At this time, Plaintiffs have no answer to this special interrogatory because Plaintiffs have not calculated the market value of each unit of collateral as of November 6, 2018. Such calculation must be made by one or more expert witnesses who have not yet performed the evaluation necessary to calculate the market value of the collateral. Plaintiffs will amend this response when the calculation has been performed.”

 

(McClure Decl., Ex. 4.)

 

In addition, in response to Defendant’s interrogatories asking Plaintiff Culberson to disclose its total compensatory damages and how it calculated these damages, Plaintiff responded:

 

“At this time, Plaintiffs have no answer to this special interrogatory because Plaintiffs have not yet calculated the sum of compensatory damages they seek to recover from TMCC in this action. Such calculation must be made by one or more expert witnesses who have not yet performed the evaluation necessary to calculate Plaintiffs’ compensatory damages. Plaintiffs will amend this response when the calculation has been performed.”

 

(McClure Decl., Ex. 4.)

 

The Court agrees that Plaintiffs’ discovery responses are factually devoid. Plaintiffs’ theory of damages is that Defendant sold the Collateral “at a grossly unfair and inadequate price.” (FAC ¶¶ 42, 49.) However, Plaintiff’s discovery responses fail to provide a calculation of the collateral or their damages. In Plaintiffs’ opposition they argue that Plaintiffs’ further discovery responses were compliant, and Plaintiffs produced thousands of responsive documents. (Oppos., p. 5.) However, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ argument persuasive. As stated above, Plaintiff specifically stated in its responses that Plaintiffs have not calculated the market value of each unit of collateral. The Court finds that the burden shifts to Plaintiffs.

 

Plaintiffs argue that they have shown a triable issue of material fact exists as to Plaintiffs’ damages claim. Plaintiffs contend that they have submitted competent evidence that the value of the collateral sold by Defendant in November 2018 greatly exceeded the proceeds that Defendant’s sale produced. In support, Plaintiffs submit the declaration of Plaintiff Richard Stower. Stower states “At the time of TMCC's sale of the collateral in November 2018, Plaintiffs held fifty-two new vehicles in its inventory, including twenty-four Toyota vehicles, eleven Chevrolet vehicles, eight Buick vehicles and nine GMC vehicles. The combined value of the fifty-two new motor vehicles in Plaintiffs' inventory at the time of TMCC's sale of the collateral was $1,734,647.35.” (Stower Decl., ¶ 4.) Stower further states that the assets other than the new vehicle inventory, which included tools, parts, machinery and other property, at the time of TMCC's sale of the collateral was no less than $1,428,750.00. (Stower Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

However, Stower’s purported calculations contradict Plaintiffs’ discovery responses that stated they were unable to calculate the value of the collateral without the assistance of an expert. (McClure Decl., Ex. 4.) A “party cannot create an issue of fact by a declaration which contradicts his prior discovery responses.”  (Shin v. Ahn (2007) 42 Cal.4th 482, 499, fn. 12, citing to D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 22.)  “In determining whether any triable issue of material fact exists, the trial court may, in its discretion, give great weight to admissions made in deposition and disregard contradictory and self-serving affidavits of the party.  (Preach v. Monter Rainbow (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1451.)”  (Benavidez v. San Jose Police Dept. (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 853, 860.)  To the extent deposition testimony or discovery responses are contradicted by a declaration in opposition to the summary judgment motion, the court must defer to the prior testimony or responses.  (Id. at p. 859.) Here, Plaintiffs have not provided an explanation as to the discrepancy and Plaintiffs have not provided evidence from an expert witness regarding the calculation of damages.

 

Moreover, the Court notes that even if Plaintiffs are able to show a difference in the value of the collateral at the time of sale and the amount that the collateral was sold for, this does not establish Plaintiffs’ damages claim, specifically that Defendant sold the Collateral “at a grossly unfair and inadequate price.” (FAC ¶¶ 42, 49.) A sale is not commercially unreasonable merely because “a greater amount could have been obtained by a collection, enforcement, disposition, or acceptance at a different time or in a different method from that selected by the secured party.” (Cal. Comm. Code § 9627(a).) A commercially reasonable disposition of collateral is presumed to be in good faith and at the greatest possible market rate. (Canadian Commercial Bank v. Ascher Findley Co. (1991) 229 Cal. App. 3d 1139, 1149.) Where a creditor fails to abide by this provision of the Commercial Code, the creditor “is liable for damages in the amount of any loss caused by a failure to comply with this division [Division 9, Secured Transactions].” (Commercial Code § 9625(b).) Plaintiffs have not produced evidence to support a finding that Defendant’s sale was not consistent with the requirements of Cal. Comm. Code § 9627(b). Plaintiffs have not established they possess evidence to support their damages claim, which is an essential element to each of Plaintiffs’ causes of action.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that Plaintiffs have carried their burden. Plaintiffs have not shown a triable issue of material fact exists as to Plaintiffs’ damages claim. Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On October 26, 2023, Plaintiff Donald Underwood (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Hertz Corporation (“Defendant”) asserting a cause of action for negligence.

 

Defendant filed a demurrer to the Complaint, which was sustained, with leave to amend, on February 1, 2024.

 

Plaintiff attempted to file a First Amended Complaint on February 20, 2024, which was rejected on February 28, 2024.

 

On March 19, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s request to file a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”), which was filed on March 19, 2024.

 

The SAC alleges the following. On August 19, 2023, Plaintiff was involved in a motor vehicle accident with Daniel O’Neal Kirk (“Kirk”). (SAC, p. 1.) Plaintiff alleges that Kirk was driving his vehicle on the wrong side of the road and collided with Plaintiff. (SAC, pp. 1-2.) Kirk had rented the vehicle from Defendant. (SAC, p. 2.) Plaintiff requested Defendant compensate him for the damage from the accident, however, Plaintiff was not compensated by Defendant. (SAC, p. 1.) The SAC appears to allege a cause of action for negligence.

 

On November 29, 2023, Defendant filed a demurrer and motion to strike portions of the SAC on the following grounds:

·       The cause of action for negligence fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

o   The SAC appears to attempt to assert a negligence cause of action against Hertz by asserting that Hertz was negligent for renting a vehicle to an unlicensed driver.

o   However, facts alleged in the Complaint conflict with the facts pled in the SAC. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff took a photograph of Kirk’s identification card and temporary paper driver’s license at the scene of the subject accident. (RJN, Exhibit A, Complaint, p. 4.) The SAC alleges that it was not a temporary paper driver’s license that Plaintiff photographed at the scene, but rather was the vehicle registration. (SAC, p. 2.)

o   According to the allegations of the Complaint, Mr. Kirk was a licensed driver. Hertz complied with the law applicable to car rentals.

·       Further, any bad faith cause of action fails because Plaintiff has not established the parties had a contractual relationship.  

·       Motion to Strike: Defendant asserts that the SAC contains references to code sections that are inapplicable and should be stricken.

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating she discussed the grounds of the demurrer and motion to strike with the pro per Plaintiff by telephone on April 11, 2024 but an agreement was not reached between the parties. (Jensen Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

C.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests the Court take judicial notice of the Complaint filed by Plaintiff in this lawsuit on October 26, 2023.

 

Evidence Code § 452(d) provides that judicial notice may be taken of, “Records of (1) any court of this state.” Evidence Code § 453 provides that, “The trial court shall take judicial notice of any matter specified in Section 452 if a party requests it and: (a) Gives each adverse party sufficient notice of the request, through the pleading or otherwise, to enable such adverse party to prepare to meet the request, and (b) Furnishes the court with sufficient information to enable it to take judicial notice of the matter.”

 

The Court grants Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

D.     Cause of Action for Negligence

 

Defendant demurs to the SAC’s cause of action for negligence on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

 

Negligence requires: (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiff to use due care; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richeld Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.) 

 

The SAC alleges Defendant rented it’s vehicle to Kirk, who did not have a driver’s license. (SAC, p. 3.) The SAC also alleges that Plaintiff had taken a picture of “what he believed was [Kirk’s] temporary paper driver’s license however was the vehicle registration.” (SAC, p. 2.) Thus, it appears Plaintiff is asserting a negligence cause of action by alleging that Defendant was negligent in renting a vehicle to an unlicensed driver.

 

However, Defendant asserts that the SAC’s allegations directly contradict Plaintiff’s initial Complaint. Plaintiff had alleged in his Complaint that Kirk had a “temporary paper driver’s license” that he displayed to Plaintiff at the scene of the accident. (Complaint, p. 2.) A temporary driver’s license is a valid driver’s license. (California Vehicle Code § 12506.) Thus, the allegations in the Complaint indicate that Kirk was a licensed driver, which contradicts the allegations in the SAC.

 

When a complaint contains allegations that are fatal to a cause of action, a plaintiff facing demurrer cannot avoid those defects simply by filing an amended complaint that omits the problematic facts or pleads facts inconsistent with those alleged earlier. (Banis Restaurant Design, Inc. v. Serrano (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1035, 1044.) Absent an explanation for the inconsistency, a court will read the original defect into the amended complaint, rendering it vulnerable to demurrer. (Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 743.) A subsequent complaint is considered a “sham” pleading if the amendment contains allegations that contradict the earlier pleading or that omit previously pled facts that rendered the initial complaint deficient. (Owens v. Kings Supermarket (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 379, 383-84.)

Here, Plaintiff did not file an opposition. “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion or demurrer is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.” (Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.” (Id.) Since Plaintiff did not file an opposition, he also fails to provide an explanation for the inconsistency. Absent an explanation, the Court reads the original defect into the SAC. Therefore, the Court does not find Plaintiff has pled facts establishing that Defendant was negligent relating to this accident or owed a duty to prevent harm to Plaintiff.

 

The Court notes that Defendant also argues that the SAC fails to state a bad faith cause of action.

The last page of the SAC does not explicitly state Plaintiff is asserting a bad faith cause of action, nevertheless, the Court does not find Plaintiff has alleged facts supporting that cause of action. “In addition to the duties imposed on contracting parties by the express terms of their agreement, the law implies in every contract a covenant of good faith and fair dealing” (Egan v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 809, 818), which requires that “neither party… do anything which will injure the right of the other to receive the benefits of the agreement” (Careau & Co. v. Sec. Pac. Bus. Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1393, internal quotations omitted). Here, Plaintiff does not allege he had a contractual relationship with Defendant.

 

The Court also does not grant leave to amend. “The plaintiff bears the burden of proving there is reasonable possibility of amendment.” (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Service (2000) 81 Cal. App. 4th 39, 43, 96 Cal.Rptr.2d 354, 356). There is no basis in law to grant leave to amend where, “the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Casterson v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 177, 190). Here, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition. Plaintiff has not carried his burden of showing a reasonable possibility of amendment.

 

Accordingly, the Court sustains Defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend.

 

E.      Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendant moves to strike the following from the SAC:

1.      Para. 1), p. 3, in its entirety, “In pursuant of U.S. Code § 914 - Art. 114. Endangerment - Reckless Endangerment. It is factual that Hertz had wrongful, reckless, and wanton disregard for the safety of persons or property by renting to Daniel O'Neal Kirk who had no driver's license.”

2.      Para. 2), p. 3, in its entirety, “In pursuant of California Code Regs. Tit. 22, § 71811 - Gross Negligence - Hertz' failed to exercise any care, or exercised so little care that it is apparent that they are indifferent to the consequences of his or her conduct and to the welfare of others. Hertz was aware that their behavior poses a substantial, unjustifiable risk of harm, and intentionally ignored risk when renting out a car to someone who was not legally authorized to rent, but also violated their own policy by renting to Daniel O'Neal Kirk.”

 

Defendant argues these allegations should be stricken because they have not been drawn in conformity with the law. Defendant asserts the allegations reference code sections that are inapplicable. The Court agrees.

 

U.S. Code § 914 – Art. 114, Endangerment – Reckless Endangerment, referenced on page 3 of the SAC, appears to refer to 10 USC 914: Art. 114, which is the Uniform Code of Military Justice. This code section from the Uniform Code of Military Justice has no relevance or application to the instant case involving a property damage claim from a motor vehicle accident.

 

Further, California Code Regs. Tit. 22, § 71811, referenced on page 3 of the SAC, provides, “Gross negligence means the failure of a person to exercise any care, or the exercise of so little care that it is apparent that the person is indifferent to the consequences of his or her conduct and to the welfare of others.” This code section is a definition relating to the California Certified Nurse Assistant Program, which is likewise not relevant to this matter.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to strike.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to strike.

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

TKEYAH CAMPBELL v. DARDEN RESTAURANTS, INC., et al. (24STCV05156)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Mark Joseph Valencia (Valencia & Cywinska, ALC)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party: Jesse Caryl (Bent Caryl & Kroll, LLP)

 

DEFENDANTS’ motion to compel arbitration (filed by Defendants Darden Restaurants, Inc. and GMRI, Inc. on 6/14/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of an employment dispute. On February 29, 2024, Plaintiff Tkeyah Campbell (“Plaintiff”), filed this action against Defendants Darden Restaurants, Inc.; GMRI, Inc.; Bernadette Last Name Unknown; Genesis Last Name Unknown; and Anthony Last Name Unknown (collectively “Defendants”).

 

Plaintiff brings the following seven causes of action against Defendants: (1) Hostile Work Environment Racial Harassment in Violation of Govt. Code § 12940(j); (2) Constructive Discharge in Violation of Public Policy; (3) Disparate Treatment Racial Discrimination in Violation of Govt. Code § 12940(a); (4) Retaliation in Violation Govt. Code § 12940(h); (5) Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code § 1102.5; (6) Failure to Prevent Harassment or Discrimination or Retaliation in Violation of Govt. Code § 12940(k); and (7) Civil Code §§ 51.7 & 52.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff worked as a food server for her joint employers, Defendants GMRI and Darden Restaurants, at the Olive Garden restaurant located at 430 Huntington Drive, Arcadia, CA 91006 from December 27, 2022 to July 2, 2023. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges that over the course of her employment she experience racial discrimination and harassment from other employees, including being called racist derogatory names and being threatened with physical violence based on her race. (Compl., ¶ 9.) Plaintiff complained to supervisors and management, which she alleges resulted in her being retaliated against, including being given fewer tables and customers to serve. (Compl., ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges management never investigated her complaints of racial harassment and she continued to experience harassment and retaliation, which led to her resignation. (Compl., ¶ 17.)

 

On June 14, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration arguing: Defendants presented Plaintiff with its Dispute Resolution Process (“DRP”) handbook during the initial new hire and on-boarding process; Plaintiff acknowledged that she read and understood the handbook and DRP agreement; and Plaintiff executed an acknowledgment agreeing to be bound by the DRP.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends the purported arbitration agreement is unenforceable because Plaintiff did not see the arbitration agreement

 

Defendants filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff executed the DRP and thus agreed to arbitrate her claims.

·       Plaintiff’s convenient contention that the link to the full DRP booklet that was embedded into the attestation she signed is simply not credible

·       Should the Court be inclined to deny Defendants’ Motion based on Plaintiff’s declaration, Defendants request that Plaintiff be subject to deposition on the limited issue of the parties’ arbitration agreement and her electronic signature to the agreement prior to the Court’s final determination to test the veracity of her declaration on the critical issue of whether the hyperlink to the full DRP booklet was in fact “dead.”

·       The arbitration agreement is procedurally and substantively enforceable.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A written agreement where the parties agree to arbitration is valid, enforceable and 

irrevocable unless there is a contract defense. (C.C.P. § 1281.) “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿¿(C.C.P. § 1281.2.)  

¿ 

“[T]he petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence…”¿¿(Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc.¿(2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.)¿¿“In determining whether an arbitration agreement applies to a specific dispute, the court may examine only the agreement itself and the complaint filed by the party refusing arbitration [citation]. The court should attempt to give effect to the parties' intentions, in light of the usual and ordinary meaning of the contractual language and the circumstances under which the agreement was made.”¿¿(Weeks v. Crow¿(1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.) “To determine whether a contractual arbitration clause requires arbitration of a particular controversy, the controversy is first identified and the issue is whether that controversy is within the scope of the contractual arbitration clause.”¿(Titolo¿v. Cano¿(2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 310, 316.) “Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration. The court should order them to arbitrate unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.”¿(California Correctional Peace Officers¿Ass'n¿v. State¿(2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

There is strong public policy in favor of both arbitration of disputes and resolving any doubts concerning the scope of arbitrable disputes in favor of arbitration.¿(Moncharsh¿v.¿Heily¿&¿Blase¿(1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 9.) Arbitration agreements should be liberally interpreted, and arbitration should be ordered unless the agreement clearly does not apply to the dispute in question.¿¿(Vianna¿v. Doctors’ Management Co.¿(1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1186, 1189.)¿ 

 

B.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of the Employment Arbitration Rules and Mediation Procedures of the American Arbitration Association (the “AAA Rules”).

 

Evidence Code §452(h) provides that a court may take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”  Additionally, “a court may take judicial notice of the fact of a document’s recordation, the date the document was recorded and executed, the parties to the transaction reflected in a recorded document, and the document’s legally operative language, assuming there is no genuine dispute regarding the document’s authenticity.”  (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 265.) Moreover, “[f]rom this, the court may deduce and rely upon the legal effect of the recorded document, when that effect is clear from its face.” (Id.)

 

The Court grants Defendants request for judicial notice.

 

C.      Existence of an Agreement to Arbitrate

 

Under California law, “[t]he burden of persuasion is always on the moving party to prove the existence of an arbitration agreement with the opposing party by a preponderance of the evidence ….” (Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 164-166.) 

 

“However, the burden of production may shift in a three-step process.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 165.) 

 

“First, the moving party bears the burden of producing ‘prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy.’ [Citation.]” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 165.) “The moving party ‘can meet its initial burden by attaching to the [motion or] petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the [opposing party’s] signature.’ [Citation.]” (Ibid.) “Alternatively, the moving party can meet its burden by setting forth the agreement’s provisions in the motion.” (Ibid.) “For this step, ‘it is not necessary to follow the normal procedures of document authentication.’ [Citation.] If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party does not dispute the existence of the arbitration agreement, then nothing more is required for the moving party to meet its burden of persuasion.” (Ibid.) 

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff was presented with the Dispute Resolution Process (“DRP”) handbook during her initial new hire and on-boarding process. Defendants contend that the DRP expressly provides that employment-related disputes, including claims for discrimination, are subject to mandatory and binding arbitration. Defendants further assert that the DRP is a valid and enforceable agreement, and dictates this matter be compelled to binding arbitration.

 

Defendants attached a copy of the DRP to the instant motion. (Baughn Decl., Ex. F.) However, the Court notes that the last page of the DRP titled “Dispute Resolution Process Acknowledgement”, does not include Plaintiff’s signature. (Baughn Decl., Ex. F, p. 17.) The Employee Signature line is left blank.

 

However, Defendants argue in their motion that Plaintiff acknowledged that she would be bound by the DRP agreement. During Plaintiff’s on-boarding process, Defendants assert Plaintiff signed an acknowledgement that states:

 

“This agreement contains the requirements, obligations, procedures and benefits of the Dispute Resolution Process (DRP). I acknowledge that I have received and/or have had the opportunity to read this arbitration agreement. I understand that this arbitration agreement requires that disputes that involve the matters subject to the agreement be submitted to mediation or arbitration pursuant to the arbitration agreement rather than to a judge or jury in court. I agree as a condition of my employment, to submit any eligible disputes I may have to the DRP and to abide by the provisions outlined in the DRP. I understand this includes, for example, claims under state and federal laws relating to harassment or discrimination, as well as other employment-related claims as defined by the DRP. Finally, I understand that the Company is equally bound to all of the provisions of the DRP.”

 

(Baughn Decl., Ex. E.)

 

Defendants attached the acknowledgment which bears Plaintiff’s electronic signature. Thus, Defendants have met their initial burden by attaching the DRP acknowledgment purportedly bearing Plaintiff’s signature. Under the prima facie standard articulated in Gamboa, the Court finds that the Defendants have met their burden of producing prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy between them and Plaintiff.  

 

“If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party disputes the agreement, then in the second step, the opposing party bears the burden of producing evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 165.) “The opposing party can do this in several ways. For example, the opposing party may testify under oath or declare under penalty of perjury that the party never saw or does not remember seeing the agreement, or that the party never signed or does not remember signing the agreement.” (Ibid. [emphasis added].) 

 

For example, in the 2021 case Gamboa, the Court of Appeal found that the plaintiff “met her burden on the second step by filing an opposing declaration, saying she did not recall the agreement and would not have signed it if she had been aware of it: ‘I do not remember these documents at all .... Had I been made aware of the existence of an arbitration agreement, and been explained its provisions, I would not have signed any such documents.’” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 167.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that she did not see the DRP at any time prior to filing this motion. Plaintiff declares the following under the penalty of perjury. “During my on-boarding with Olive Garden, which was handled electronically, I clicked on many documents during the on-boarding phase. Per Exhibit C of the Declaration of Amanda Baughn, which I am attaching as Exhibit 2, there is an activity report with dates and times that I purportedly signed documents.” (Campbell’s Decl., ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff further declares:

 

“15 rows from the top is an item entitled: “Dispute Resolution Process Book – English & Spanish Saved.” The date/time associated with reviewing this item is 7:45 pm, December 19, 2022. The next item reviewed was “Photo/Video Consent & Release – English and Spanish Saved” at 7:46 pm, December 19, 2022. Literally only one minute elapsed between the “Dispute Resolution Process Book” and “Photo/Video Consent and Release.” This supports the fact that I did not review the 17 Page Dispute Resolution Process Arbitration Agreement. If I were to review a 17-page contract, there is no way I would read, review, and comprehend it in less than a minute.” (Campbell’s Decl., ¶ 4.) 

 

Moreover, Plaintiff declares that she tried clicking on the link to view the DRP but the link did not work, so she never saw or reviewed the DRP. (Campbell’s Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

In Gamboa, the Court of Appeal held it did not need to “decide whether [the plaintiff] challenged the authenticity of her purported signature on the arbitration agreement” because “[i]t was enough that [the plaintiff] challenged the authenticity of the agreement by saying under penalty of perjury that she did not remember it.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 168.)  

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff has met her burden of producing evidence challenging the authenticity of the DRP by declaring under penalty of perjury that she was not given the opportunity to read the DRP and did not in fact see or review it.  

 

“If the opposing party meets its burden of producing evidence, then in the third step, the moving party must establish with admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. The burden of proving the agreement by a preponderance of the evidence remains with the moving party.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at pp. 165–166.) In Gamboa, the court stated “[i]n sum, once Gamboa produced evidence challenging the authenticity of the purported arbitration agreement, the Clinic was required to rebut the challenge by establishing by a preponderance of the evidence that the agreement was valid. The Clinic did not have to authenticate Gamboa's signature on the arbitration agreement. The Clinic could have met its burden in other ways, including a declaration from the Clinic’s custodian of records. But proferring [sic] no admissible evidence was insufficient.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 171.) 

 

Here, the Court does not find Defendants met their burden of rebutting Plaintiff’s challenge to the DRP. In Defendants’ reply, they claim that Plaintiff’s contention that the link to the full DRP booklet that was embedded into the attestation she signed is not credible, without providing support for this assertion. Defendants again point to the DRP acknowledgment purportedly bearing Plaintiff’s electronic signature. Defendants also argue that Defendants’ DRP handbook is available as a hyperlink as part of the application process, and not just the onboarding process. However, the Court does not find Defendants’ arguments persuasive. Plaintiff stated that she did not see the DRP at any time prior to filing this motion. Defendants do not produce evidence that could show that Plaintiff had reviewed and signed the DRP. As the Gamboa court stated, the burden is on Defendant to rebut Plaintiff’s challenge. The Court finds that the Defendants have not met their burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence the validity of the DRP.  

 

The Court notes that in Defendants’ reply, Defendants include a footnote requesting that Plaintiff be subject to deposition on the limited issue of the parties’ arbitration agreement and her electronic signature to the agreement prior to the Court’s final determination to test the veracity of her declaration on the critical issue of whether the hyperlink to the full DRP booklet was in fact “dead.” If Defendants are requesting an order from the Court, Defendants do not provide any support or authority for their request. Thus, the Court is not inclined to grant this request.

 

For the reasons set forth above, the Court denies the motion.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.
