TENTATIVE RULING RE:

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Recana Solutions LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Giuliano-Pagano Corporation dba Giuliano’s Bakery (“Giuliano’s Bakery”) and NG Trucking LLC (“NG Trucking”) (jointly, “Defendants”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) open book account, and (4) open book account.[1]

On January 24, 2022, Defendants filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) fraud, (4) declaratory relief, and (5) unfair business practice.

NG Trucking now moves for an order granting summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication in favor of NG Trucking against Plaintiff. Plaintiff opposes.

Legal Standard

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Ibid.)  

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) 

When a defendant seeks summary judgment or summary adjudication, he/she must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established; or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

A.    Allegations of the Complaint

In the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that on or about January 31, 2015, Plaintiff and

Giuliano’s Bakery entered into a written agreement whereby Plaintiff, among other things, agreed to provide temporary employees to Giuliano’s Bakery in exchange for agreed upon fees which would be invoiced to Giuliano’s Bakery on a monthly basis. (Compl., ¶ 6.) On or about January 17, 2020, Giuliano’s Bakery ceased making required payments of Plaintiff’s monthly invoices and, on January 29, 2020, sent a letter terminating the agreement with Plaintiff. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges that as a result of Giuliano’s Bakery’s default of payment of these invoices, there is now due and payable from Giuliano’s Bakery to Plaintiff the amount of principal and interest of $681,521.52. (Compl., ¶ 10.)

            Plaintiff further alleges that “Plaintiff and Defendant NG Trucking LLC also entered into an Agreement whereby Plaintiff provided temporary employees to Defendant NG Trucking LLC under the same terms and calculations as the agreement between Plaintiff and Giuliano’s. Defendant NG Trucking has failed to pay invoices per said Agreement in the amount now due from said invoices and interest thereon is $71,331.61. Interest on said amount shall continue to accrue.” (Compl., ¶ 13.)

            In the second cause of action against NG Trucking for “breach of oral contract,” Plaintiff alleges that “[b]y failure to pay the invoices and interest thereon, Defendant Giuliano’s breached its written contract with Plaintiff. Plaintiff has been damaged by said breach in an amount not fully ascertained but in excess of $80,807.71…” (Compl., ¶ 18.) In the fourth cause of action for open book account, Plaintiff alleges that “[w]ithin four (4) years last past, there has existed an open book account between Plaintiff and Defendant NG Trucking LLC, there is an outstanding balance on said open book account owned by NG Trucking to Plaintiff of $80,807.71 and said amount is now due and owning from said Defendant to Plaintiff.” (Compl., ¶ 21.)

B.     Second Cause of Action for Breach of Oral Contract

NG Trucking asserts that Plaintiff’s second cause of action for breach of oral contract is time-barred. In the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that it is a “Limited Liability company organized and existing under the laws of the state of Texas and is licensed in the State of California.” (Compl., ¶ 1.) NG Trucking cites to United Medical Management Ltd. v. Gatto (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1732, 1737-1738, where the Court of Appeal noted as follows:

 

“A foreign corporation which qualifies to transact intrastate business may also be subject to penalties for failure to pay state taxes. The corporate powers, rights and privileges of a foreign corporation may be forfeited for failure to pay state taxes. (Rev. & Tax. Code, § 23301.)…A foreign corporation whose rights have been forfeited for failure to pay state taxes may not prosecute or defend an action, nor appeal from an adverse judgment. (Reed v. Norman (1957) 48 Cal. 2d 338, 343 [309 P.2d 809].) A foreign corporation whose rights have been forfeited for failure to pay state taxes may be reinstated upon application to the Franchise Tax Board and payment of outstanding taxes, penalties and interest. (Rev. & Tax. Code, § 23305.) Upon payment, the Franchise Tax Board issues a certificate of revivor reinstating the corporation. (Rev. & Tax. Code, § 23305a.) The reinstatement of the corporation is without prejudice to any action, defense or right which accrued by reason of the forfeiture…‘If it pays its taxes and obtains a certificate of revivor during the pendency of an action, the corporation may be allowed to carry on litigation, even to the extent of validating otherwise invalid prior proceedings.’ (Benton v. County of Napa (1991) 226 Cal. App. 3d 1485, 1490 [277 Cal. Rptr. 541].) The revival of corporate powers validates any procedural act…taken on behalf of the corporation while its rights were forfeited…Substantive defenses, such as the statute of limitations, are not revived…”

NG Trucking asserts that at the time Plaintiff filed the Complaint in this action on November 12, 2021, Plaintiff’s corporate powers, rights and privileges were forfeited by the California Franchise Tax Board. NG Trucking provides evidence of a California Secretary of State “Certificate of Status” for Plaintiff, dated December 9, 2021. (NG Trucking’s RJN, ¶ 1, Ex. M, p. 10.) The Certificate of Status Provides that “[t]he records of this office indicate the Secretary of State forfeited the entity’s powers, rights and privileges on February 4, 2020, pursuant to the provisions of the California Corporations Code; the California Franchise Tax Board forfeited the entity’s powers, rights and privileges on February 1, 2021, pursuant to the provisions of the California Revenue and Taxation Code; and the entity’s powers, rights and privileges remain forfeited.” (Emphasis added.)

NG Trucking also indicates that on April 12, 2023, its counsel visited “https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/search/business” to conduct a business entity search of “Recana Solutions LLC.” (Diaz Decl., ¶ 13.) NG Trucking’s counsel states that “[a]fter clicking on the matching result, I then clicked on the button labeled ‘View history’” and that “[o]pon the expansion of a pop up window detailing various entry dates, I clicked on the tab ‘Expand All’ which expanded each of the entries on the pop up page.” (Diaz Decl., ¶¶ 14, 15.) NG Trucking’s counsel indicates that he “then printed a copy of the results, which [he]…attached as Exhibit N to the [Index of Exhibits]…” (Diaz Decl., ¶ 15, Ex. N.) NG Trucking’s Exhibit N includes an Item stating “FTB – Standing” changed from “Not Good” to “Good,” next to the date June 21, 2022. (Ibid.) NG Trucking asserts that Plaintiff accordingly “lacked capacity to both prosecute and maintain an action against NG Trucking from February 1, 2021, to June 21, 2022.” (Mot. at p. 12:23-24.)

NG Trucking asserts that the second cause of action for breach of oral contract is subject to the two-year statute of limitations set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 339(1). This provision provides in pertinent part, “[w]ithin two years: 1. An action upon a contract, obligation or liability not founded upon an instrument of writing…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 339(1).)

NG Trucking asserts that here, “[t]he latest time a breach occurred was January 28, 2020.” (Mot. at p. 9:21.) NG Trucking provides the Declaration of Gregory S. Ritmire, the President of Giuliano-Pagano Corporation (‘GPC’) and NG Trucking, Inc. (Ritmire Decl., ¶ 1.)[2] Mr. Ritmire states that “on or around January 28, 2020, [he] notified Alex Stiles, the principle for Recana Solutions, that [he] would be terminating the oral contract between NG Trucking and Recana Solutions, and the written agreement between GPC and Recana Solutions.” (Ritmire Decl., ¶ 15.) Mr. Ritmire references a January 28, 2020 email he sent to Mr. Stiles, which provides, inter alia, that “[a]s you know, the absence of your Worker’s Compensation insurance was discovered when our company was named in a Worker’s Compensation claim relating to one of your workers…In light of this material breach of our contract, we cannot continue to do business with you, at least until breach has been cured to our satisfaction and any damages resolve to our satisfaction. Therefore, this email will provide you written notice that we are discontinuing any further services from your company based upon this material breach...” (Ritmire Decl., ¶ 15, Ex. L.) 

NG Trucking also cites to the following testimony from Mr. Stiles deposition: “Q…am I correct that the NG Trucking relationship also ended in January of 2020? A. Correct.” (Diaz Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. G (Stiles Depo.) at p. 103:12-15.) Mr. Stiles was asked “Okay. And how did you receive notice of Giuliano’s’ termination of the contract with Recana?” to which he responded, “A. I believe it was e-mail.” (Id. at p. 104:4-6.) NG Trucking also cites to the following testimony: “Q. And was there ever any separate communication from NG Trucking about the termination, or you just understood that when they communicated they were terminating Giuliano’s, they were terminating NG Trucking as well? A. That’s correct, the second part.” (Id. at p. 104:19-24.)

NG Trucking asserts that “[s]ince there were no payments made by NG Trucking to Plaintiff following this date, and because Plaintiff alleges NG Trucking’s failure to pay the outstanding invoices, we can place the time of breach at the time Ritmire cancelled [sic] NG Trucking agreement. Accordingly, the two-year statute of limitations ran on January 28, 2022.” (Mot. at p. 13:20-24.) As set forth above, NG Trucking provides evidence that the December 9, 2021 Certificate of Status concerning Plaintiff provides, inter alia, that “[t]he records of this office indicate…the California Franchise Tax Board forfeited the entity’s powers, rights and privileges on February 1, 2021, pursuant to the provisions of the California Revenue and Taxation Code; and the entity’s powers, rights and privileges remain forfeited.” (NG Trucking’s RJN, ¶ 1, Ex. M, p. 10.) NG Trucking also states that the Secretary of State website indicates that Plaintiff’s corporate status was not revived by the Franchise Tax Board until June 21, 2021. (Diaz Decl. ¶¶ 13-15; Ex. N.) NG Trucking asserts that “Plaintiff’s corporate status was forfeited by the FTB as of February 1, 2021 and remained as such through to the time that Plaintiff filed the instant Complaint. Since Plaintiff did so while its rights and privileges were forfeited, Plaintiff was required to revive its status prior to January 28, 2022—the date the statute ran. The Secretary of State website clearly indicates that Plaintiff did not obtain revival status until June 21, 2022, which was more than five months since the statute ran.” (Mot. at p. 14:14-19.)

NG Trucking cites to City of San Diego v. San Diegans for Open Government (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 568, 579, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he statute of limitations is regarded as a substantive defense. If an action is commenced while the corporation is suspended, the corporation’s subsequent revival does not prevent the running of the statute of limitations; if the statute runs prior to revival, the corporation’s actions will be time-barred, even if the complaint would have otherwise been timely.”

In the opposition, Plaintiff does not dispute NG Trucking’s assertion that the latest time a breach occurred was January 28, 2020. Rather, Plaintiff asserts that “[b]y agreeing to the terms of the written agreement with [Plaintiff] and [Giuliano’s Bakery], NG Trucking is subject to the four-year statute of limitations for an agreement based on a written contract.” (Opp’n at p. 5:5-7.) In support of the opposition, Plaintiff submits the Declaration of Adam Stiles, Plaintiff’s President. (Stiles Decl., ¶ 1.) Mr. Stiles states that “[o]n or about January 31, 2015, [Plaintiff] and [Giuliano’s Bakery] entered into a written agreement which memorialized the parties’ business relationship.” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 3.) Mr. Stiles further states that “[i]n or about 2013, [Plaintiff] also entered into an agreement to provide temporary employees to work at NG TRUCKING, an affiliate of [Giuliano’s Bakery].” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 4.) Mr. Stiles states that “[t]he Terms of [Plaintiff’s] agreement with NG TRUCKING were to be the same as the written agreement between [Plaintiff] and [Giuliano’s Bakery’s]…” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 4.)

Plaintiff cites to Pietrobon v. Libarle (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 992, 994, where “Defendant Marc Libarle appeal[ed] from a judgment of the Sonoma County Superior Court in favor of plaintiff Renzo Pietrobon on plaintiff’s action against defendant for breach of a settlement agreement. Defendant contend[ed] there was no written settlement agreement and that the court erred in ruling that the four-year statute of limitations for an action upon a contract founded upon an instrument in writing…applied.” In Pietrobon, “[o]n May 11, 2000, defendant, an attorney, and plaintiff, a former client, entered into a settlement of plaintiff’s lawsuit against defendant in open court…The parties agreed to a stipulated judgment in the total amount of $55,000, and that defendant would pay plaintiff certain sums on a payment schedule over a two-year period…Counsel for defendant stated he would be reducing the stipulation to a writing. He drafted such a writing and delivered it to plaintiff for signature. Plaintiff signed the agreement on June 30, 2000. Defendant failed to sign the agreement.” (Ibid.)

The Court of Appeal found that “[t]he central question here is whether the four-year statute of limitations for a written contract (§ 337) applies in these circumstances. We believe that it does.” (Id. at p. 997.) The Pietrobon Court noted as follows:

 

“In Amen v. Merced County Title Co. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 528 [25 Cal. Rptr. 65, 375 P.2d 33] (Amen), the California Supreme Court held that an action against an escrow holder for failure to comply with escrow instructions was on a written contract, despite the failure of the escrow holder or its agent to sign the instructions…The instructions provided that any debts over a specified amount to be assumed by the plaintiff would be paid by the escrow holder out of the sale proceeds. The escrow was accepted by the escrow holder, but the instructions were never signed by the escrow holder or its agent…The buyers sued the escrow company for breach of the escrow contract when the escrow closed without payment out of the escrow of debts exceeding the specified amount…According to the Supreme Court, ‘[t]he contract may be ‘in writing’ for purposes of the statute of limitations even though it was accepted orally or by an act other than signing…’…‘When a party has agreed to the writing, there is no reason to invoke the two-year statute of limitations applicable to oral agreements. The four-year statute of limitations, unlike the statute of frauds, does not require that the writing be signed by the party to be charged. Had the Legislature meant to make a signature mandatory for a writing to qualify for the longer period of limitations it would have so provided as it did in section 360 of the Code of Civil Procedure, which requires that a new promise to perform under an old contract be signed to start the statute running anew. Plaintiff’s action was therefore on a written contract.’…” (Pietrobon v. Libarle, supra, 137 Cal.App.4th at p. 997 [emphasis omitted].)  

Plaintiff asserts that “[h]ere, NG Trucking agreed to the terms of the RECANA-GIULIANO’S written agreement and the four-year statute of limitations applies to [Plaintiff’s] contract action against NG Trucking.” (Opp’n at p. 7:5-6.) Plaintiff asserts that accordingly, the four-year statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 337, subdivision (b) applies. (Opp’n at p. 7:11-12.)[3]

But as noted by NG Trucking, “Plaintiff sued NG for breach of oral contract and open book account,” and “does not allege it ever entered into a written contract with NG.” (Reply at p. 2:18-19; 3:9-10.) The second cause of action is “as against NG Trucking for breach of oral contract.” As NG Trucking notes, “[t]he complaint serves to delimit the scope of the issues before the court on a motion for summary judgment, and a party cannot successfully resist summary judgment on a theory not pleaded.” (Whelihan v. Espinoza (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1566, 1576 [internal citation omitted.)

NG Trucking also asserts that Pietrobon v. Libarle, supra,137 Cal.App.4th 992 is distinguishable, as that case involves writings between the actual parties in dispute. Indeed, NG Trucking notes that here, there is no dispute that NG Trucking was provided a written agreement that it failed to sign. NG Trucking also asserts that Plaintiff’s reliance on Amen v. Merced County Title Co., supra, 58 Cal.2d 528 is misplaced, because here, “there was no unsigned writing adopted through performance…” (Reply at p. 11:18.) As discussed, Plaintiff alleges a cause of action for breach of oral contract, based on the allegation that “NG Trucking LLC also entered into an Agreement whereby Plaintiff provided temporary employees to Defendant NG Trucking LLC under the same terms and calculations as the agreement between Plaintiff and [Giuliano’s Bakery]…” (Compl., ¶ 13.)

NG Trucking also asserts that “by virtue of styling the second cause of action against NG as ‘Breach of Oral Contract’ Plaintiff concedes the agreement between Plaintiff and NG was not in writing and the statute of limitations specified in CCP § 339 applies.” (Reply at p. 3:18-20.) NG Trucking cites to Valerio v. Andrew Youngquist Construction (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 1264, 1271, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he admission of fact in a pleading is a ‘judicial admission.’ Witkin describes the effect of such an admission: ‘An admission in the pleadings is not treated procedurally as evidence; i.e., the pleading need not (and should not) be offered in evidence, but may be commented on in argument and relied on as part of the case. And it is fundamentally different from evidence: It is a waiver of proof of a fact by conceding its truth, and it has the effect of removing the matter from the issues. Under the doctrine of ‘conclusiveness of pleadings,’ a pleader is bound by well pleaded material allegations or by failure to deny well pleaded material allegations.” (Emphasis in original.)

In light of the foregoing, the Court finds that NG Trucking has met its burden of demonstrating that Plaintiff’s second cause of action is time-barred, and that Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact as to this cause of action.

C.     Fourth Cause of Action for Open Book Account 

NG Trucking asserts that the fourth cause of action for open book account fails because “Plaintiff will be unable to present any evidence that an open book account ever existed.” (Mot. at p. 14:28.)

NG Trucking cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 337a, subdivision (a), which provides that “[t]he term ‘book account’ means a detailed statement which constitutes the principal record of one or more transactions between a debtor and a creditor arising out of a contract or some fiduciary relation, and shows the debits and credits in connection therewith, and against whom and in favor of whom entries are made, is entered in the regular course of business as conducted by such creditor or fiduciary, and is kept in a reasonably permanent form and manner and is (1) in a bound book, or (2) on a sheet or sheets fastened in a book or to backing but detachable therefrom, or (3) on a card or cards of a permanent character, or is kept in any other reasonably permanent form and manner. A ‘book account’ does not include consumer debt.” NG Trucking also cites to Durkin v. Durkin (1955) 133 Cal.App.2d 283, 29, where the Court of Appeal found that “[t]his ‘Dealer’s Reserve Account’ does not bear the earmarks of an ‘open book account’ in the traditional sense of that term. The following definition has been quoted with approval: ‘The expression ‘outstanding and open account’ has a well-defined and well-understood meaning. In legal and commercial transactions it is an unsettled debt arising from items of work and labor, goods sold and delivered, and other open transactions, not reduced to writing, and subject to future settlement and adjustment. It is usually disclosed by the account books of the owner of the demand, and does not include express contracts or obligations which have been reduced in writing, such as bonds, bills of exchange, or promissory notes.’…An express contract, which defines the duties and liabilities of the parties, whether it be oral or written, is not, as a rule, an open account.” (Internal citations omitted.)

NG Trucking asserts that here, “the obligations of NG Trucking and Plaintiff are manifested solely out of their respective performances under their express, oral agreement.” (Mot. at p. 15:25-26.) In his supporting declaration, Mr. Ritmire states that “I acknowledge that [Plaintiff] has alleged the existence of a written agreement between [Plaintiff] and GPC, attached as Exhibit K to the Index of Exhibits…filed concurrently herewith,” and that “[t]o the best of my knowledge that is my signature indicated on that agreement.” (Ritmire Decl., ¶¶ 6-7, Ex. K.) Exhibit K is titled “Recana Client Services Agreement” and provides, inter alia, that “This Addendum…is made as of the day of January 31, 2015, by and between Recana Solutions, L.L.C. and Giuliano’s Bakery.” (Ritmire Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. K.) NG Trucking notes that this agreement contains a section concerning “Terms and Conditions of Payment” which provides, inter alia, that “An itemized invoice for temporary services, based on hours shown on Recana or Client time cards or associate detail reports from time and attendance systems, will be mailed weekly by Recana to Client. Such invoice is DUE AND PAYABLE WITHIN THIRTY (30) DAYS OF RECEIPT.” (Ibid., ¶ 4(A).)

NG Trucking also indicates that it propounded Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Plaintiff, including Form Interrogatory No. 50.1. (Diaz Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. C.) Form Interrogatory No. 50.1 provides in part, “50.1 For each agreement alleged in the pleadings: (a) identify each DOCUMENT that is part of the agreement and for each state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of each PERSON who has the DOCUMENT; (b) state each part of the agreement not in writing, the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of each PERSON agreeing to that provision, and the date that part of the agreement was made…” (Ibid.) Plaintiff’s response to NG Trucking’s Form Interrogatory No. 50.1(a) provides in pertinent part, “[t]he written agreement between Recana Solutions, LLC and Giuliano’s Bakery titled ‘Recana Client Services Agreement’ dated January 31, 2015...” (Diaz Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. D.) Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory No. 50.1(b) provides, “Plaintiff and NG Trucking, LLC entered into an oral argument [sic] whereby Plaintiff provided temporary employees to NG Trucking, LLC under the same terms as the January, 2015 written agreement between Plaintiff and Giuliano’s…” (Ibid.)

NG Trucking asserts that accordingly, “Plaintiff’s claim for open book account fails as a matter of law because Plaintiff has failed to meet its burden to show that an open book account ever existed. As the evidence shows, the parties entered into no agreement, express or implied by conduct, in contrast to the underlying services agreement.” (Mot. at p. 18:11-14.)

In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that it “has brought a proper action for breach based on its open book account with NG Trucking which is not inconsistent with its claim of breach of an express contract.” (Mot. at p. 3:6-8.) Plaintiff cites to Warda v. Schmidt (1956) 146 Cal.App.2d 234, 237, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[a] book account is created by the agreement or conduct of the parties thereto. The mere recording in a book of transactions or the incidental keeping of accounts under an express contract does not of itself create a book account. Such memoranda cannot be utilized under the guise of a book account as a device to extend the statute of limitations beyond the time it would run on the contractual obligation…However, the parties to a written or oral contract may, by agreement or conduct, provide that monies due under such contract shall be the subject of an account between them. In that event a cause of action arising therefrom is on the account and not on the underlying contract.” (Internal citations omitted.)

            Plaintiff asserts that in this matter, “[Plaintiff] has established that through the conduct of the parties, a book account was established in addition to the express agreement between the parties. The parties acted as having a book account relationship long before the written agreement.” (Opp’n at pp. 4:25-5:1.)

            In his supporting declaration, Mr. Stiles states that “[Plaintiff] began doing business with [Giuliano’s Bakery] on or around 2005 providing temporary employees to work at [Giuliano’s Bakery] facilities. From the inception of the parties’ relationship, [Plaintiff] and [Giuliano’s Bakery] agreed and conducted themselves in a manner in which all monies due to [Plaintiff] for the services it provided were the subject of an account between them.” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 2.) Mr. Stiles further states that “[o]n or about January 31, 2015, [Plaintiff] and [Giuliano’s Bakery] entered into a written agreement which memorialized the parties’ business relationship. The parties continued to treat the monies due to [Plaintiff] for the services it provided under the written agreement as an account. The written agreement memorialized this by containing specific provisions on ‘Past Due Accounts.’” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 3.) In addition, Mr. Stiles states that “[i]n or about 2013, [Plaintiff] also entered into an agreement to provide temporary employees to work at NG TRUCKING, an affiliate of [Giuliano’s Bakery].” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 4.)

Mr. Stiles asserts that “[w]hen [Giuliano’s Bakery] and [Plaintiff] entered into the agreement in 2015, NG TRUCKING agreed to be bound by the terms of the agreement, As a result, [Plaintiff] and NG TRUCING [sic] agreed and conducted themselves in a manner in which all monies due to [Plaintiff] for the services it provided were also the subject of an account between them.” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 4.) As set forth above, the Court sustains NG Trucking’s evidentiary objection to Mr. Stiles’s statement that “NG TRUCKING agreed to be bound by the terms of the agreement…” (See Evid. Objection No. 1, Stiles Decl., ¶ 4.) As noted by NG Trucking, Plaintiff does not allege that NG Trucking “agreed to be bound by” Plaintiff’s agreement with Giuliano’s Bakery. Rather, Plaintiff alleges that “Plaintiff and Defendant NG Trucking LLC also entered into an Agreement whereby Plaintiff provided temporary employees to Defendant NG Trucking LLC under the same terms and calculations as the agreement between Plaintiff and Giuliano’s.” (Compl., ¶ 13, emphasis added.) Similarly, Plaintiff’s response to NG Trucking’s Form Interrogatory No. 50.1(b) provides, “Plaintiff and NG Trucking, LLC entered into an oral argument [sic] whereby Plaintiff provided temporary employees to NG Trucking, LLC under the same terms as the January, 2015 written agreement between Plaintiff and Giuliano’s…”  (Diaz Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. D.) NG Trucking cites to Thompson v. Williams (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 566, 573-574, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[a]fter-the-fact attempts to reverse prior admissions are impermissible because a party cannot rely on contradictions in his own testimony to create a triable issue of fact. The assertion of facts contrary to prior testimony does not constitute substantial evidence of the existence of a triable issue of fact.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.) NG Trucking asserts that “NG’s oral agreement to be bound by the same or similar terms included in GPC’s contract with Plaintiff does not…add it as a party to that separate written agreement.” (Reply at p. 10:23-24.)

NG Trucking also asserts that Warda v. Schmidt, supra, 146 Cal.App.2d 234, relied upon by Plaintiff, is distinguishable. NG Trucking asserts that “[w]hat Plaintiff fails to mention is that the initial contract between the same parties in Warda was based on a written agreement and the open accounts claims were based on two oral agreements giving rise to the open accounts claim in which the court applied a four-year statute of limitation. In Wanda [sic], the plaintiff’s claim was solely on two counts: account stated and a book account – and not a claim for breach of an oral contract which then gave rise to a common count. Thus, the Wanda [sic] ruling is not applicable here, as it did not apply to two distinct legal entities, nor did it support an end run around the oral contract allegation...” (Reply at p. 7:11-18, emphasis omitted.) As set forth above, “[a]n express contract, which defines the duties and liabilities of the parties, whether it be oral or written, is not, as a rule, an open account.” (Durkin v. Durkin, supra,133 Cal.App.2d at p. 290.)

Indeed, in Warda, “[o]n May 22, 1947, plaintiff and defendant entered into a written contract whereby plaintiff was to do the plastering work on the 171 houses for which he was to be paid $ 500 per house for plastering the outside and $ 800 per house for plastering the inside…The contract was based upon the master plan. Its terms provided for installment payments of the amount due thereunder: 60 per cent was to be paid to plaintiff when the first coat of plaster was put on; 30 per cent upon completion of the second coat, and the remaining ten per cent when the wash was applied and the job completed.” (Warda v. Schmidt, supra, 146 Cal.App.2d at pp. 235-236.)

“Plaintiff complained to defendant about the extra plastering necessitated by the changes in the master plan. The parties entered into an oral agreement whereby defendant agreed to compensate plaintiff for the extra plaster work.” (Warda v. Schmidt, supra, 146 Cal.App.2d at p. 236.) In addition, “Defendant planned to build an additional 110 houses in Meadow Glen. In the latter part of 1947 he entered into an oral contract with plaintiff whereby he agreed to pay plaintiff the flat rate of $ 2.50 a square yard for the plaster work on these houses.” (Ibid.) “A notice of completion of the 171 houses was recorded on December 22, 1948. One for the 110 houses was recorded on February 25, 1949. Plaintiff commenced this action on January 2, 1953. The complaint filed on that day was in two counts: an account stated and a book account. Plaintiff sought to recover $ 38,658.60 which was alleged to be the balance owed to him by defendant as a result of the aforementioned events.” (Ibid.)  

The Warda Court found that “George Bryant, who was plaintiff’s foreman and bookkeeper at the time of the events here involved, testified that the installment-payment provisions of the May 22, 1947, contract were never followed and that all payments made by defendant to plaintiff were on account. Plaintiff testified likewise. Linea Miller, who was defendant’s bookkeeper at the time of the events here involved, was called as a witness by defendant. She testified that the payments made to plaintiff during this period were on account. The two oral contracts entered into between plaintiff and defendant in 1947 had no payment provisions. The trial court could properly infer that this was because payments thereunder were to be subject to the account already established between the parties. A transfer in writing of a credit of defendant with a third party to plaintiff expressly stated that it was received by plaintiff on the account of defendant.” (Id. at pp. 237-238.)

Here, Plaintiff does not appear to provide any evidence demonstrating how NG Trucking (as opposed to Giuliano’s Bakery), through its conduct, created a book account. As set forth above, in the Warda Court held that “the parties to a written or oral contract may, by agreement or conduct, provide that monies due under such contract shall be the subject of an account between them.” (Warda v. Schmidt, supra, 146 Cal.App.2d at p. 237.) Rather, Mr. Stiles states that “[f]rom the inception of the parties’ relationship, [Plaintiff] and [Giuliano’s Bakery] agreed and conducted themselves in a manner in which all monies due to [Plaintiff] for the services it provided were the subject of an account between them.” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 2, emphasis added.)  

Mr. Stiles also states that “[t]hroughout its business relationship, with [Giuliano’s Bakery] and NG Trucking, [Plaintiff] had an agreement with a factoring company under which [Plaintiff] assigned its accounts receivable for its accounts with [Giuliano’s Bakery] and NG Trucking to the factoring company in exchange for an advance on the total amount on the invoices submitted to [Giuliano’s Bakery] and NG Trucking.” (Stiles Decl., ¶ 5.)

NG Trucking notes that in his deposition, Mr. Stiles testified as follows: “Q. Did those agreements assign the accounts receivable that were between Giuliano’s and Recana Solutions or NG and Recana Solutions? Did it assign those accounts receivable to Vertex and then BAM and then Dare? A. Yes. Q. And Recana was -- received money in exchange for that assignment, correct? It’s basically an advance on the AR factor? A. Yes.” (Diaz Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. G (Stiles Depo.) at p. 56:9-18.) In the motion, NG Trucking asserts that “[d]uring the course of its business relationship with the Defendants, Plaintiff retained the services of Vertext and its successors, BAM Capital LLC and Dare Capital Partners LLC (collectively hereafter ‘Vertext’)…to handle its AR factoring,” and that the “credit arrangement with Vertext operated for the benefit of Plaintiff’s own business practices, and was incidental to the obligations of Plaintiff and NG Trucking under their express agreement.” (Mot. at pp. 16:26-28; 17:16-18.)  NG Trucking notes that “[a] book account is created by the agreement or conduct of the parties in a commercial transaction. Nonetheless, the mere recording in a book of transactions or the incidental keeping of accounts under an express contract does not of itself create a book account. Parties to a written or oral contract may, however, provide that monies due under such contract shall be the subject of an account between them.” (H. Russell Taylor's Fire Prevention Service, Inc. v. Coca Cola Bottling Corp. (1979) 99 Cal.App.3d 711, 728.) In the opposition, Plaintiff does not appear to dispute NG Trucking’s assertion that “Vertext’s role was purely incidental.” (Mot. at p. 17:1.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that NG Trucking has met its burden of demonstrating that Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is without merit, and that Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact as to this cause of action.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, NG Trucking’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

The Court orders NG Trucking to file and serve a proposed judgment of dismissal within 10 days of the date of this Order.¿¿

TENTATIVE RULING RE: 

 

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

 

Plaintiff LVNV Funding LLC (“Plaintiff”) requests entry of default judgment against Defendant Stephen C Dimon. Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $61,541.08, comprising $53,235.45 demanded in the Complaint, $5,817.42 in interest, $533.50 in costs, and $1,954.71 in attorney fees.   

The Court notes one defect with the submitted default judgment package. 

As set forth above, Plaintiff requests $5,817.42 in interest. However, as discussed in the Court’s October 13, 2023 minute order on Plaintiff’s previous request for default judgment, Plaintiff does not appear to allege in the Complaint that it seeks any interest. The Court notes that “[a] complaint or cross-complaint shall contain…the following:…(2) A demand for judgment for the relief to which the pleader claims to be entitled. If the recovery of money or damages is demanded, the amount demanded shall be stated.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10, subd. (a)(2).) “[I]n all default judgments the demand sets a ceiling on recovery.” (Finney v. Gomez (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 527, 534.) “A trial court has no jurisdiction to grant a plaintiff more relief against a defaulting defendant than is asked for in the complaint.” (Feminist Women's Health Center v. Blythe (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1641, 1675.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for default judgment without prejudice. Plaintiff to schedule a date for submitting a new default judgment package.

TENTATIVE RULING RE: 

 

PETITION TO CONFIRM MINOR’S COMPROMISE

           

            Background

            On May 10, 2018, Plaintiffs Elizabeth Noemy Zetino, Jessica Ivonne Zetino Morales, and Harlynn Amerylise Zetino Lopez, a minor, by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Elizabeth Noemy Zetino (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed the instant action against Defendants 15025 Saticoy Street, Inc. and Anza Management Company.

Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Habitability Complaint (“FAC”) on September 11, 2019 against Defendants 15025 Saticoy Street, Inc., Anza Management Company, and J.K. Residential Services, Inc. The FAC alleges causes of action for (1) violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, (2) tortious breach of the warranty of habitability, (3) private nuisance, (4) Business & Professions Code § 17200 et seq., and (5) negligence.

The parties have reached a settlement in this matter. Elizabeth Noemy Zetino as Petitioner for Claimant Harlynn Amerylise Zetino Lopez (“Petitioner”) now petitions for the Court’s approval of the settlement on behalf of the Claimant. 

On December 1, 2023, the Court issued a minute order in this action providing, inter alia, that “[h]ere, the petition is prepared on form MC-350 and is verified by the Petitioner. However, the Court notes that Item 14 of the petition (Form MC-350) is blank…Based on the foregoing, the Court intends to grant the petition and approve the compromise, subject to Petitioner filing a corrected Form MC-350 prior to the hearing that corrects the defect identified above, and subject to Plaintiffs’ examination of Petitioner at the hearing. On the Court’s own motion, the Hearing on Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise scheduled for 12/01/2023 is continued to 12/14/23 at 10:00 AM in Department 50 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse. The minor does not need to be present on the above continued date.” On November 30, 2023, Petitioner filed a revised petition.

            Discussion

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, § 3500.) The petition must be verified by the petitioner, must contain a full disclosure of all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise, and must be prepared on judicial council form MC-350. (California Rules of Court, rule 7.950.) 

Here, the petition is prepared on form MC-350 and is verified by the Petitioner. The defect previously identified by the Court has been corrected in the revised petition.

            Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court intends to grant the revised petition and approve the compromise, subject to Plaintiffs’ examination of Petitioner at the hearing. Petitioner is to give notice of this ruling.
