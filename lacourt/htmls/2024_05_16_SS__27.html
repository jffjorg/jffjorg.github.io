DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV06479    Hearing Date: May 16, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR GOOD FAITH DETERMINATION OF SETTLEMENT 

Hearing Date: 5/16/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV06479 NAKISA NOWZAR vs VIA MARINA TAHITI HOA, et al. 

Moving Party: Defendants Via Marina Tahiti HOA and Horizon Management Company

Responding Party: Defendant Personal Touch Cleaning and Maintenance

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: MOTION FOR GOOD FAITH DETERMINATION OF SETTLEMENT IS GRANTED.

 

Background

 

On February 18, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present premises liability case, stemming from an incident where Plaintiff slipped and fell on a water puddle in the garage of a condominium complex owned by Defendant Via Marina Tahiti HOA (“Via Marina”). Defendant Horizon Management Company ("Horizon Management") serves as the HOA management company for the subject property. Defendant Personal Touch Cleaning & Maintenance (“Personal Touch”) is contracted by Via Marina and Horizon Management (collectively referred to as "Settling Defendants") to provide cleaning and maintenance services for the condominium, including the garage. On May 30, 2023, HOA management and Via Marina reached a settlement with Plaintiff for $80,000. On October 20, 2023, the Settling Defendants filed their first motion for determination of good faith settlement without prejudice; that motion was denied because the Settling Defendants failed to provide sufficient evidence of a rough approximation of the total recovery. The Settling Defendants now again move the court for a determination of good faith settlement. Defendant Personal Touch opposes the motion, arguing that the settlement amount is grossly disproportional to the total value of the case.

 

Legal Standard

 

In an action involving two or more joint tortfeasors or co-obligors, when one tortfeasor or obligor enters into a settlement with the plaintiff, the other tortfeasors or obligors are entitled to a hearing on the issue of whether the settlement was entered into in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6(a).) Where a plaintiff settles with one of several joint tortfeasors or co-obligors without releasing the others, a determination of “good faith” discharges the settling defendant from liability to the other defendants for equitable contribution or comparative indemnity. (CCP § 877(a)-(b).) The amount paid by the settling defendant reduces the claim against the others (CCP § 877(a)), but a risk of prejudice remains because an unreasonably low settlement (i.e., with the “most culpable” tortfeasor) exposes the remaining defendants to a judgment exceeding their fair share of the liability. (See Bay Development, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1990) 50 Cal. 3d 1012, 1019-1020.) 

 

There is no precise yardstick for measuring the “good faith” of a settlement with one of several tortfeasors, but it must harmonize the public policy favoring settlements with the competing public policy favoring equitable sharing of costs among tortfeasors. (See Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.)

 

“A more appropriate definition of ‘good faith,’ in keeping with the policies of American Motorcycle and the statute, would enable the trial court to inquire, among other things, whether the amount of the settlement is within the reasonable range of the settling tortfeasor's proportional share of comparative liability for the plaintiff's injuries. This is not to say that bad faith is ‘established by a showing that a settling defendant paid less than his theoretical proportionate or fair share.’ [Citation.] Such a rule would unduly discourage settlements. ‘For the damages are often speculative, and the probability of legal liability therefor is often uncertain or remote. And even where the claimant's damages are obviously great, and the liability therefor certain, a disproportionately low settlement figure is often reasonable in the case of a relatively insolvent, and uninsured, or underinsured, joint tortfeasor.’ [Citation.] Moreover, such a rule would tend to convert the pretrial settlement approval procedure into a full scale mini trial [citation].

 

But these considerations do not lead to

the conclusion that the amount of the settlement is irrelevant in determining good faith. Rather, the intent and policies underlying section 877.6 require that a number of factors be taken into account including a rough approximation of plaintiffs' total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial. Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of nonsettling defendants. [Citation.]

 

Finally, practical considerations obviously require that the evaluation be made on the basis of information available at the time of settlement. ‘[A] defendant's settlement figure must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant's liability to be.’ [Citation.] The party asserting the lack of good faith, who has the burden of proof on that issue (§877.6(d)), should be permitted to demonstrate, if he can, that the settlement is so far ‘out of the ballpark’ in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute. Such a demonstration would establish that the proposed settlement was not a ‘settlement made in good faith’ within the terms of section 877.6.”

 

(Tech-Bilt, Inc., 38 Cal.3d at 499-500.)

 

Preliminary Matter

 

An order that the denial of a motion is made “without prejudice” allows the motion to be made at a later time, potentially without the need to show new facts, as is generally required by Section 1008. (Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial, Posthearing Procedures, Ch. 9(I)-E.) Courts often indicate that a motion is being denied “without prejudice” in order to signal that the matter is not yet ripe but that relief may later be available. (Id. at [9:338].) Some courts have suggested that denying a motion without prejudice “impliedly invites the moving party to renew the motion at a later date, when he can correct the deficiency that led to the denial.” (Farber v. Bay View Terrace Homeowners Assn. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1007, 1015; National Grange of Order of Patrons of Husbandry v. California Guild (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 706, 716 fn.10.) 

 

The Court denied the initial application without prejudice due to insufficient evidence of a rough approximation of the total recovery necessary to evaluate a good faith settlement. In the renewed motion, the settling defendants provided the requisite evidence and addressed all the Tech-Bilt factors. Consequently, the current motion has rectified the previous deficiency, and is procedurally proper.

 

Analysis

 

The Court examined the evidence presented by both parties and determined that liability may rest with both the settling defendants and Personal Touch. On one hand, evidence suggests that the settling defendants failed to repair the leaky pipes that allegedly caused the water damage. On the other hand, Personal Touch was contractually responsible for the cleaning and maintenance of the garage, which included mopping water, placing cones or "wet floor" signs, and reporting the presence of water to the property manager (Morales Depo. at 34:11-20, 37:22-38:1). There is evidence both supporting and contesting each party’s liability.

 

Personal Touch estimates its liability at approximately 25%, with the Settling Defendants' liability at around 75% (Opposition at pg. 11). While this approximation may be generous to Personal Touch (as one would expect given that Personal Touch is the one presenting this estimation of liability), the Court considers it reasonable based on the provided evidence.

 

Personal Touch alleges the total value of the case to be $250,000, based on Plaintiff’s §998 offer served on December 19, 2023. (Opposition at pg. 14). However, the Court notes that this offer was made after Plaintiff claimed $180,000 - $220,000 in future damages for potential lumbar surgery on November 2, 2023. The settlement agreement was entered into in May 2023, well before the future damages increased from approximately $60,000 to $200,000. Under Tech-Bilt, supra., the evaluation of the good faith of a settlement must be based on information available at the time of settlement. Therefore, the value of the case at the time of settlement should align more closely with Personal Touch’s own §998 offer of $30,001, served on October 25, 2023, before the drastic increase in future damages.

 

Considering Personal Touch’s estimation that the settling defendants are 75% liable and Personal Touch is 25% liable, the settlement value of $80,000 is almost three times the $30,001 §998 offer by Personal Touch. This closely matches Personal Touch’s estimation of the liability distribution between the parties.

 

Moreover, even accepting Personal Touch’s assertion that the total case value is $250,000 and the settling defendants are 75% liable, a settlement of $80,000 remains within a reasonable range. "A settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if found liable at trial" (Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at 499; Abbott Ford, Inc. v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 858, 874 ["In order to encourage settlements, it is quite proper for a settling defendant to pay less than his proportional share."]). Courts have found settlements to be in good faith with significantly lower settlement to value ratio than the present case.  See, e.g., Bay Development, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1990) 50 Cal.3d 1012, 1028 (finding a $30,000 settlement reasonable in a litigation involving a claim for $1 million in damages; Cahill v. San Diego Gas & Electric Co. (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 939, 969-970 ($25,000 settlement on a $40 million estimated value.)  Furthermore, based on the Court’s own experience, the ratio in the present case is reasonable even based on the total value and proportion of liability presented by Personal Touch.

 

Thus, the motion for good faith settlement is GRANTED.

MOTION TO REOPEN DISCOVERY 

Hearing Date: 5/16/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV16034 ERIC SLATTERY vs MAR PIZZA, INC., et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Mar Pizza and Maria De Los Angeles Sanchez

Responding Party: Plaintiffs 

Notice: Sufficient 

 

Ruling: MOTION TO REOPEN DISCOVERY IS GRANTED.

 

On May 13, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their complaint alleging injuries from a motor vehicle accident. Defendants filed their answers in May 2023. On February 23, 2024, the Court granted Defendants' ex parte application to continue the trial date from April 10, 2024, to August 19, 2024. Pursuant to the CCP, the Court did not extend the discovery cutoff date, as that must be done by stipulation or noticed motion. On March 5, 2024, Defendants moved the Court to extend the discovery cutoff date to align with the current trial date of August 19, 2024.

Defendants request an extension of the discovery cutoff date because they filed their answer in May 2023, thus having less than one year to conduct discovery. They request additional time to conduct discovery into Plaintiff's medical history.  Because Plaintiff Eric Slattery underwent major surgeries in December 2023, Defendants request further discovery, including an additional IME to assess the results of the surgery.

The Court finds Defendants’ requests reasonable, especially because Defendants were served only approximately one year ago and had less than one year to conduct discovery. The Court is usually inclined to allow parties up to two years, depending on the circumstances, in motor vehicle accident cases to conduct discovery. The Parties had far less time than that. Furthermore, Defendants' need to assess the results of Plaintiff’s recent surgeries in December 2023 is reasonable. Although Plaintiff disputes the appropriateness of the various exams proposed by Defendants, the Court will assess them upon Defendants’ fully briefed motions and not in the present motion. Nevertheless, The Court does find Defendants' overarching need to conduct discovery to assess the results of Plaintiff’s surgeries to be appropriate.

. Furthermore, the Court does not find Defendants to be tardy in requesting the discovery extension. Defendants brought the ex parte application on February 22, 2024, to continue the trial and discovery cutoff dates, just two months after learning about the surgeries. A delay of two months, while perhaps not ideal, is not tardy.

Based on the age of the case, the limited time Defendants had to conduct discovery, and the need to conduct further discovery into Plaintiff's recent surgeries, the Court grants the present motion. All pretrial deadlines are hereby adjusted to align with the new trial date of August 19, 2024.

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV26100 NATHAN AKHTARZAD, et al. vs MICHAEL MAIOLO

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/16/24

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV26100 NATHAN AKHTARZAD, et al. vs MICHAEL MAIOLO

Moving Party: Defendant Silvercar Inc.

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Nathan Akhtarzad and Lauren Davis

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SANCTIONS IS GRANTED

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Background

On July 15, 2021, Plaintiffs filed the present auto accident case alleging that on July 19, 2019, Plaintiffs Nathan Akhtarzad and Lauren Davis were involved in a collision with a vehicle owned by Defendant Silvercar and operated by Defendant Michael Maiolo. Silvercar now moves for summary judgment on the basis that Plaintiffs' claim against Silvercar is barred under the Graves Amendment, which precludes liability against rental car businesses. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

Legal Standard 

¿ 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)¿ 

¿ 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). "When the motion for summary judgment is supported by affidavits sufficient to sustain the motion,…the burden of showing that triable issues exist shifts to the party opposing the motion. [Citation.] A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture…but instead must produce admissible evidence raising a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)

 

Analysis

Under the Graves Amendment, an owner of a motor vehicle that rents or leases the vehicle shall not be liable under the law of any state, by reason of being the owner of the vehicle, for harm to persons or property that results or arises out of the use, operation, or possession of the vehicle during the period of the rental or lease, if: (1) the owner is engaged in the trade or business of renting or leasing motor vehicles; and (2) there is no negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner. (49 U.S.C. § 30106, subd. (a).) The Graves Amendment “shields owners of leased vehicles ‘engaged in the business or trade of renting or leasing motor vehicles’ from vicarious liability for the alleged negligence of their lessee’s drivers.” (Vargas v. FMI, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 638, 642.)

Defendant Silvercar has met its initial burden of demonstrating that Plaintiffs' claim against Silvercar is barred by the Graves Amendment. Silvercar is and at all times relevant to the captioned action was a business entity engaged in the business of renting motor vehicles to the public. (Milanez Decl., ¶ 2.) Silvercar rented the vehicle to Maiolo from July 18, 2019, through 10 p.m. on July 19, 2019. (Milanez Decl. ¶ 4; Rental Agreement between Silvercar and Maiolo, attached as Exhibit C to the Milanez Decl.) On July 19, 2019, at 4:20 PM, Defendant Maiolo was involved in an accident with Plaintiff while driving a rental car from Silvercar. Defendant Silvercar was not the operator of the vehicle at the time of the accident, Maiolo was not an employee or agent of Silvercar, and the accident was not caused by a malfunction or defect in the vehicle, nor was there any negligence on behalf of Silvercar that led to the accident. (Milanez Decl. and Sawyer Decl., Exhibit D.) Defendant has met its initial burden in establishing all the elements under the Graves Amendment.

Plaintiff failed to file an opposition or introduce any evidence raising a triable issue of fact. Thus, the present Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS UNDER CCP 128.7

Legal Standard

CCP section 128.7(b) provides that by signing and filing a pleading, an attorney “is certifying that to the best of the person’s knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, all of the following conditions are met: (1) [i]t is not being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation[;] (2) [t]he claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law[;] (3) [t]he allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery[;] (4) [t]he denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(b).) CCP section 128.7 authorizes the court to impose appropriate sanctions upon attorneys or parties that have violated subsection (b). (Id., § 128.7(c).)

CCP section 128.7 provides a 21-day safe harbor provision whereby the offending party can avoid sanctions by withdrawing the improper pleading. (See Galleria Plus, Inc. v. Hanmi Bank (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 535, 538.)  In pertinent part section 128.7, subdivision (c)(1) reads as follows: "A motion for sanctions under this section shall be made separately from other motions or requests and shall describe the specific conduct alleged to violate subdivision (b). Notice of motion shall be served as provided in Section 1010, but shall not be filed with or presented to the court unless, within 21 days after service of the motion, or any other period as the court may prescribe, the challenged paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation, or denial is not withdrawn or appropriately corrected. . . ." Section 1010 provides that a notice of a motion "must state when . . . the motion will be made."

A claim can be factually frivolous or legally frivolous. (See Peake v. Underwood (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 428, 440.) “A claim is factually frivolous if it is ‘not well grounded in fact’ and it is legally frivolous if it is ‘not warranted by existing law or a good faith argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law.’” (Id.) “In either case, to obtain sanctions, the moving party must show the party’s conduct in asserting the claim was objectively unreasonable.” (Id.) “A claim is objectively unreasonable if ‘any reasonable attorney would agree that [it] is totally and completely without merit.’” (Id.)  The Court further notes that even if a claim is frivolous, sanctions are not mandatory and should be made with restraint. (Id.)

Analysis

Defendant moves the court for sanctions on the basis that Plaintiffs' action/pleading against Defendant Silvercar is frivolous.

The Court finds that the moving party has complied with the safe harbor rule, as the notice and motion were served on Plaintiff on July 20, 2023, and the moving party waited at least 21 days before filing the present motion on August 16, 2023. The lawsuit against Silvercar is not legally frivolous, as Silvercar can be liable even under the Graves Amendment if it is independently liable in some way for the accident. From an objective perspective, it is reasonable for Plaintiff to file a lawsuit against the owner of the vehicle at the onset, in case the discovery process and further investigations uncover Silvercar’s independent negligence, such as negligent maintenance of the vehicle causing the accident.

However, if throughout this process, Plaintiff has not uncovered any facts supporting its allegations against Silvercar, then dismissal of its claims against Defendant would be appropriate. Plaintiff did not oppose Defendant’s motion for summary judgment nor Defendant’s motion for sanctions. Plaintiff failed to provide any indication that the present lawsuit is not factually frivolous by presenting evidence of Silvercar’s independent negligence to sustain its cause of action against Silvercar. Thus, the motion is granted.

Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $10,275.20. However, the Court notes that the motion for summary judgment was filed on April 7, 2023, and Plaintiff was provided formal notice of the motion for sanctions on July 20, 2023, well after the MSJ was filed. Although Defendant filed an informal notice, the safe harbor period does not start until a formal notice is served on Plaintiff. Therefore, even if Plaintiff had withdrawn its pleading and dismissed the matter during the safe harbor period starting on July 20, 2023, it would not have saved Defendant attorney fees in drafting the summary judgment motion. However, because Plaintiff did not dismiss Silvercar during the safe harbor period and after the motion for sanctions was filed, Silvercar is still required to attend the hearing on the two motions and incur additional attorney’s fees. Pursuant to the principle that sanctions under § 128.7 are not mandatory and should be made with restraint (Peake v. Underwood (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 428, 440), the Court exercises its discretion and sanctions Plaintiff’s attorney in the amount of $2,500.  Said sanction is to be paid within 20 days.

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/16/24

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV30547 ROBERT WILLIAMSON vs GLS US FREIGHT, INC.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Defendants GLS US FREIGHT AND FRANCISCO ESCALANTE

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: THE COURT WILL HEAR ARGUMENT ON THE MOTION FOR SANCTIONS 

 

Legal Standard

 

The Civil Discovery Act provides for an escalating and “incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc.¿(2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604.)¿Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to and commensurate with the misconduct, and they “should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc.¿(2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) “If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.” (Ibid.; see also, e.g.,¿Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.)¿“Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390 [citation omitted].)

 

The primary purpose of discovery sanctions is to obtain compliance with the Civil Discovery Act and the Court’s orders. It is not to punish. (Newland v. Super. Ct.¿(1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 613;¿Ghanooni v. Super Shuttle of Los Angeles¿(1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 256, 262.) A discovery sanction should not create a “windfall” for a party or place a party in a better position than it would have been if the opposing party had simply complied with its obligations under the Court’s orders and the Civil Discovery Act. (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co.¿(2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1194; see also 2 Weil & Brown, California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023), ¶¶¿8:2214-2220.)¿“[T]the trial court has broad discretion in selecting the appropriate sanction, and we must uphold the trial court's determination absent an abuse of discretion.” (Dept. of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 19.)

 

Discussion

On September 19, 2022, Plaintiff filed this case alleging injuries from a forklift accident due to Defendant Escalante's improper setup of various equipment. On March 19, 2024, the Court heard Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition and ordered Escalante to sit for his deposition and pay $2,000 in sanctions to Plaintiff within 20 days. Plaintiff alleges Defendant has not complied with these orders and now moves the Court for terminating, issue, evidentiary, and monetary sanctions. Defendant provided evidence of payment of the sanctions (by defense counsel) on April 9, 2024. However, the deposition of Defendant Escalante remains unresolved. This has been a longstanding issue between the Parties, as defense counsel lost contact with Escalante and could not produce him for the previously scheduled depositions, prompting a motion to compel deposition. Even after the Court issued an order for his deposition, defense counsel still was not able get in contact with Escalante, and his deposition remains outstanding.

Monetary sanctions have not addressed the discovery abuse and, given counsel’s numerous failed attempts in the past to reach Defendant Escalante, it appears that Defendant Escalante has abandoned his defense, failing to communicate with his own counsel and failing to file any document or appear when his counsel moved to withdraw as his counsel.  As such, an appropriate sanction is warranted.  The Court will discuss with the parties the appropriate sanction(s).
