DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22STCV12388    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 1, 2024                     JUDGMENT: July 25, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Renee Lotenero, et al. v. BMW of North America, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV12388           

 

MOTION TO TAX COSTS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Renee Lotenero and Steve Siegrist

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant BMW of North America, LLC

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/12/22: Complaint filed.

·         07/03/24: Jury verdict rendered.

·         07/25/24: Judgment on special verdict.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on April 12, 2022. Plaintiffs signed a Retail Installment Sales Contract to buy a 2017 BMW i3 which had electrical system defects. Plaintiffs sued the manufacturer, BMW of North America, LLC, for breach of warranty under the Song Beverly Act. A jury verdict was rendered on July 3, 2024 in favor of Plaintiffs and against Defendant.

 

Plaintiffs move to tax costs in the memorandum of costs filed by Defendant.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED. Item 16 of Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs is taxed in the amount of $3,095.30.  Defendant is, therefore, entitled to recover costs of $448.59.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiffs move to tax costs in the memorandum of costs filed by Defendant.

 

//

Legal Standard 

 

In general, the “prevailing party” is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs for suit in any action or proceeding. (Code Civ. Proc. §1032(b); Santisas v. Goodin (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 606; Scott Co. Of Calif. v. Blount, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1103, 1108.)  “Prevailing party” includes a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recovery any relief against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1032(a)(4).) Assuming the “prevailing party” requirements are met, the trial court has no discretion to order each party to bear his or her own costs of suit.  (Michell v. Olick (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1194, 1198; Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 129.)

 

Defendant’s claim for costs is grounded in an offer to Plaintiffs to compromise for $45,000 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 998. Plaintiffs did not accept that offer, and subsequently secured less than the value of that offer after a jury trial. Section 998(b) of the Code of Civil Procedure states, in relevant part:

 

(b) Not less than 10 days prior to commencement of trial or arbitration (as provided in Section 1281 or 1295) of a dispute to be resolved by arbitration, any party may serve an offer in writing upon any other party to the action to allow judgment to be taken or an award to be entered in accordance with the terms and conditions stated at that time. The written offer shall include a statement of the offer, containing the terms and conditions of the judgment or award, and a provision that allows the accepting party to indicate acceptance of the offer by signing a statement that the offer is accepted. Any acceptance of the offer, whether made on the document containing the offer or on a separate document of acceptance, shall be in writing and shall be signed by counsel for the accepting party or, if not represented by counsel, by the accepting party.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 998(b).) If an offer made by a defendant is not accepted and the plaintiff does not obtain a more favorable judgment or award, the defendant is entitled to recover costs from the time of the offer and may seek post-offer costs for expert witnesses who are not regular employees of any party, provided those costs were actually incurred and reasonably necessary to the trial. (Code Civ. Proc. § 998(c)(1).)

 

Allowable costs under Section 1033.5 must be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation, and must be reasonable in amount.  An item not specifically allowable under Section 1033.5(a) nor prohibited under subdivision (b) may nevertheless be recoverable in the discretion of the court if they meet the above requirements (i.e., reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount).  If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774.)  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.  (Ibid.) Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion.  (Ibid.)  However, because the right to costs is governed strictly by statute, a court has no discretion to award costs not statutorily authorized.  (Id.)  Discretion is abused only when, in its exercise, the court “exceeds the bounds of reason, all of the circumstances being considered.”  (Ibid.)   

 

Timeliness of Motion

 

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail or email, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(1).) Here, Defendant served the memorandum of costs by electronic service on August 9, 2024. (Memorandum of Costs POS.) This motion was filed and served on August 28, 2024. Accounting for the manner of service, the motion is timely brought.

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiffs challenge portions of Defendant’s request for “other” costs listed in Item 16 of Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs. Specifically, Plaintiffs contend that Defendant’s requests for $786 in transcript costs and $2,309.30 in travel & meal expenses are improper. Defendant, in opposition, concedes that the request for trial transcript costs is inappropriate, but disputes the challenge to travel and meal expenses.

 

Plaintiffs contend that Defendant’s request for $2,309.30 in travel and meal expenses is an improper cost not reasonably necessary to the costs of litigation. Although travel expenses to attend depositions are expressly authorized under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(a)(3)(C), other travel expenses do not enjoy such esteem. Moreover, courts have found that routine expenses for local travel are not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, and therefore are not recoverable under section 1033.5(c). (See Ladas v. Cal State Auto Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 775-76.) Plaintiffs assert that it was Defendant’s choice to use counsel from San Francisco, rather than local counsel, and thus have not shown the travel costs to be reasonably necessary to the litigation.  Defendant’s support for securing these out-of-town travel expenses is not persuasive.  Its reliance on Chaaban v. Wet Seal, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 49, 59-60, does not support recovery of expenses for trial counsel’s travel from out of town to attend trial, because the expenditures reimbursed in that case covered travel expenses for depositions outside the forum and, thus, were compensable under Section 1033.5.  The other authorities cited are rulings by federal courts based on federal cost recovery standards and, thus, do not govern costs bills submitted in state court.  Under California law, Defendant is required to demonstrate that it was reasonably necessary to have counsel from the San Francisco office of Lewis Brisbois cover the trial, even though the firm has a large Los Angeles office with multiple attorneys who could have defended at trial.  Defendant has offered no evidence or argument that would allow the Court to find that such a standard has been satisfied.  Thus, the Court finds this cost item should be taxed.

 

The Court therefore finds that Item 16 of the Memorandum of Costs should be taxed in the amount of $3,095.30, based on the Parties’ agreement that the request for trial transcript costs is improper and Defendant’s failure to show that trial counsel’s travel expenses were reasonably necessary.   

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED. Item 16 of Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs is taxed in the amount of $3,095.30.  Defendant is, therefore, entitled to recover costs of $448.59.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on June 1, 2022. Plaintiffs purchased a 2019 Honda Pilot which they allege developed defects in the infotainment, electrical, transmission, and engine systems.

 

Defendant moves to transfer venue to Yuba County and to compel Plaintiffs’ depositions.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion to Transfer Venue

 

Defendant moves to transfer venue to Yuba County.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiffs request that the Court take judicial notice of Defendant’s Statement of Information filed with the California Secretary of State on June 28, 2024. Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice is DENIED as immaterial to the Court’s ruling. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 (“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Timeliness of Motion

 

            Plaintiff, in opposition, contends that Defendant’s motion to transfer venue is not timely because Defendant demurred to both the original Complaint and the First Amended Complaint and subsequently filed an answer, without moving to transfer venue. Under the plain language of Code of Civil Procedure section 396b subdivision (a), a motion to transfer must be brought by the Defendant “at the time he or she answers, demurs, or moves to strike, or, at his or her option, without answering, demurring, or moving to strike and within the time otherwise allowed to respond to the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 396b subd. (a).) Defendant indisputably did not do so, and Defendant’s assertion in reply that it did not waive any challenge to venue is unsupported by legal authority. Although Defendant cites to Owens v. Kings Supermarket (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 379, nothing in that opinion has any bearing on the issue of venue. The Court therefore finds that Defendant has waived its right to challenge venue.

 

Conclusion

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Transfer Venue is DENIED.

 

Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Defendant moves to compel depositions of Plaintiffs Michelle and Anthony Bernardino.

 

Multiple Motions

 

Multiple motions should not be combined into a single filing.¿(See¿Govt. Code,¿§ 70617(a)(4) [setting forth the required filing fee for each motion, application, or any other paper or request requiring a hearing];¿see¿also¿Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011)¿[“Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.”].)

 

Here, Defendant improperly moved to compel deposition of two different parties in one motion. Combining multiple motions under the guise of one motion with one hearing reservation manipulates the Court Reservation System and unfairly jumps ahead of other litigants. Moreover, combining motions to avoid payment of separate filing fees deprives the Court of filing fees it is otherwise entitled to collect. The Court therefore conditions its ruling on the payment of an additional $60 in filing fees within 10 days of this order.

 

Analysis

 

Defendant moves to compel depositions of Plaintiffs Michelle and Anthony Bernardino. Plaintiffs, in opposition, represent that they are willing to sit for deposition and do not challenge Defendant’s right to take their deposition. The Court therefore finds that Defendant is entitled to an order compelling these depositions and will require Plaintiffs to attend deposition on or before Thursday, October 31, 2024.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff Michelle Bernardino is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to sit for deposition by Defendant on Thursday, October 31, 2024, or on an earlier date mutually agreed upon by the parties.

 

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff Anthony Bernardino is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to sit for deposition by Defendant on Thursday, October 31, 2024, or on an earlier date mutually agreed upon by the parties.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Transfer Venue is DENIED.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff Michelle Bernardino is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to sit for deposition by Defendant on Thursday, October 31, 2024, or on an earlier date mutually agreed upon by the parties.

HEARING DATE:     October 1, 2024                     TRIAL DATE: June 10, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Martin Melendez-Murillo et al. v. AKG Trading (USA), Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV08596           

 

MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Martin Melendez-Murillo and Rufina Barajas

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendants Home Depot, USA, Inc., et al. (24 Defendants)

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/18/23: Complaint filed.

·         12/05/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Caesarstone USA, Inc.

·         12/12/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Global Stone Trading, Inc.

·         12/28/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Lowe’s Home Centers, LLC

·         04/02/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Arizona Tile, LLC.

·         05/14/24: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         06/27/24: Cross-Complaint filed by WilsonArt LLC

·         07/03/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Marbolis, Inc.

·         08/12/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Caesarstone Ltd.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a toxic tort action alleging that Plaintiff Melendez-Murillo developed silicosis and consequential injuries by breathing in dust from Defendants’ stone products generated by his work as a fabricator and polisher of stone countertops.

 

Plaintiffs move for trial preference pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivisions (d) and (e).

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiffs move for trial preference pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivisions (d) and (e).

Subdivision (d) of section 36 of the Code of Civil Procedure states that the Court, in its discretion, may grant a motion for preference “that is accompanied by clear and convincing medical documentation that concludes that one of the parties suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of survival of that party beyond six months, and that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 36(d).) If the motion is granted, the Court “shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 36(f).)

 

Plaintiff Martin Melendez-Murillo states that he was diagnosed with silicosis and progressive massive fibrosis in December 2022. (Declaration of Martin Melendez-Murillo ISO Mot. ¶1.) Plaintiff’s treating physician, Dr. Jane Fazio, M.D., states she last examined Plaintiff on March 20, 2024, and, at that time, his CT scans and pulmonary function tests show progression of his disease. (Declaration of Jane Fazio ISO Mot. ¶ 3.) Dr. Fazio states there is no known cure for Plaintiff’s condition, and the damage to his lungs is not reversible. (Id. ¶ 4.) Dr. Fazio further declares it is reasonably medically likely that Plaintiff will die within six months absent a lung transplant, but such a procedure is inherently risky, and there is no way of knowing if or when donor organs will become available. (Id. ¶ 5.) Dr. Fazio contends that, based on her knowledge of Plaintiff’s condition and her knowledge of typical outcomes for this type of illness, there is a substantial medical doubt of Plaintiff’s survival beyond six months. (Id. ¶ 9.)

 

Dr. Fazio’s declaration was not accompanied by any medical documentation. Moreover, while the Declaration of David R. Donadio in support of the motion purported to attach certain medical records for the Court’s review, it does not appear that those materials were ever presented to the Court. (See Declaration of David R. Donadio ISO Mot.) Given the “clear and convincing” standard for trial preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36(d), the Court considers those materials necessary, especially to the extent those records informed Dr. Fazio’s testimony.

 

The Court also observes that the numerous Defendants who have opposed this motion raise concerns regarding the course of discovery, identification and deposition of witnesses, and pre-trial dispositive motions (i.e., motions for summary judgment or adjudication). The Court believes it would be helpful for the Parties to meet and confer regarding these issues to explore what agreements could be reached to structure discovery and motion proceedings in the event preference is granted.

 

To that end, Plaintiff’s Motion for Trial Preference is CONTINUED to October 30, 2024, at 9:00 AM. Plaintiffs are directed to lodge the medical documentation referenced in the Donadio Declaration with the Court on or before Friday, October 4, 2024.

 

The Parties are also directed to meet and confer in person, telephonically, or by video conference regarding joint stipulations governing discovery and pre-trial motion proceedings in the event trial preference is granted. The Parties are ordered to file a Joint Statement setting forth the results of those efforts on or before Thursday, October 24, 2024.
