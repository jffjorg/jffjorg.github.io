DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03021    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

KASOWER PROPERTIES, LLC,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

BORIS DOBRIN, et al.,

Defendant.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  23VECV03021              107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] DECISION:

PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION       

 

 

Deny

 

I.                    BACKGROUND

 

On November 11, 2022, Plaintiff Kasower Properties, LLC (“Plaintiff”) entered into a commercial lease with Defendant the Testing Company, LLC (“the Testing Company”). (First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) ¶10.) Defendant Boris Dobrin executed a Guaranty of Lease agreement. (FAC ¶ 11.) The Testing Company defaulted on the lease. (FAC ¶14.)

 

On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Boris Dobrin alleging a single cause of action for breach of contract.

 

On September 18, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s application for a right to attach order, writ of attachment, and temporary protective order against Boris Dobrin in the amount of $113,737.00. (09/13/23 Minute Order.) The subject of the writ of attachment was Boris Dobrin’s property located at 25548 Kingston Court, Calabasas, CA 91302 (the “Subject Property”) and all of the deposit and investment accounts held by Boris Dobrin in California. (Right to Attach Order filed 10/02/23 p. 2.)

 

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Boris Dobrin, Zarina Dobrin, the Testing Company, Bellnorth Venture LLC, and Does 1 to 10, alleging five causes of action for breach of contract – lease, breach of contract – guaranty, avoidance and recovery of fraudulent transfers pursuant to California Civil Code section 3439.04(A)(1), avoidance and recovery of fraudulent transfers pursuant to California Civil Code section 3439.04(A)(2), and avoidance and recovery of fraudulent transfers pursuant to California common law. (FAC p. 1.)

On October 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for a temporary restraining order preventing Defendant Bellnorth Ventures, LLC (“Bellnorth”) from selling, transferring, hypothecating or encumbering the Subject Property. On October 30, 2024, this court held a hearing and granted the ex parte application, in part. (10/30/24 Minute Order.)

 

This court set a hearing on Plaintiff’s motion for preliminary injunction for November 12, 2024, ordered any opposition to be filed by November 4, 2024, and any reply to be filed by November 7, 2024.

 

On November 4, 2024, Bellnorth filed an opposition. On November 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

II.                    LEGAL STANDARD

 

An injunction may be granted “[w]hen it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 526.) A preliminary injunction may be granted upon an affidavit which shows the existence of sufficient grounds for the issuance of an injunction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 527(a).)

 

“[T]rial courts consider two interrelated questions in deciding whether to issue a preliminary injunction: (1) are the plaintiffs likely to suffer greater injury from a denial of the injunction than the defendants are likely to suffer from its grant; and (2) is there a reasonable probability that the plaintiffs will prevail on the merits.” (Robbins v. Superior Court (1985) 38 Cal.3d 199, 206.) “Plaintiff carries the burden of proof and persuasion on these issues.” (Drakes Bay Oyster Co. v. California Coastal Com. (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 1165, 1172.) “[A] court may deny a preliminary injunction either (1) on its finding irreparable injury will not result to the party seeking the injunction, or (2) that the party has failed to demonstrate a reasonable probability of success on the merits.” (Jessen v. Keystone Savings & Loan Assn. (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 454, 459, emphasis original.) “Regardless of the balance of interim harm, the preliminary injunction cannot be allowed to stand unless there is ‘some possibility’ [the party] will prevail on the merits of its action.” (Costa Mesa City Employees' Ass'n v. City of Costa Mesa (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 298, 309.)

 

III.                    THE ARGUMENTS

 

a.     Plaintiff’s Motion

 

Plaintiff seeks a preliminary injunction preventing Bellnorth from selling, transferring, hypothecating, or encumbering the Subject Property. (Motion p. 7.) Plaintiff states it filed and served its application for a right to attach order on August 15, 2023. (Motion p. 9.) At that time, the Subject Property was worth $5 to $6 million and was encumbered by a first position lien of $2.58 million and a tax lien with the Franchise Tax Board of $355,115.10. (Motion p. 9; Gizer Declaration ¶ 9.) On August 24, 2023, a title order was opened by Bellnorth and Boris Dobrin for a $600,000 loan. (Motion p. 9; Gizer Declaration ¶ 4.) On October 2, 2023, the court issued the right to attach order and order for issuance of right of attachment. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 5.) On October 31, 2023, Boris Dobrin executed a promissory note which increased the Bellnorth loan from $600,000 to $3,350,000. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 6.) On November 1, 2023, Bellnorth recorded a deed of trust encumbering the Subject Property. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 11.)

 

Plaintiff argues Bellnorth’s loan transaction documents indicate Bellnorth and Boris Dobrin engaged in a fraud designed to hinder Plaintiff’s ability to collect any judgment it receives on this case. (Motion pp. 10-11.) Plaintiff states: (1) “The Bellnorth Loan appears to be secured solely by the Subject Property and hard money lenders do not customarily make loans to individuals secured by a primary residence”; (2) “The combined loan to value ratio for the Bellnorth loan is at or above one-hundred (100) percent which greatly exceeds the standard for a hard money loan”; (3) “There are several preexisting liens that would be red flags to a bona fide hard money lender”; (4) “The Promissory Note does not meet hard money loan industry standards as it does not state the purpose of the loan and the interest rate is not clearly defined”; (5) “The Updated Lender’s Instructions to Escrow do not state the loan amount or lien position of the deed of trust and indicate that only $300,000 will be disbursed through escrow as ‘the last remaining balance’ of the loan” and “[it] is contrary to hard money lending industry customs and practices to disburse funds to a borrower outside of escrow”; (6) “Bellnorth’s name is spelled wrong throughout the loan documents”; (7) “The Updated Lender’s Instructions have different font sizes and types throughout”’; (8) “The Updated Lender’s Instructions agree that items 7-20 in the preliminary report are to remain in the final title policy even though the preliminary report only lists 13 items”; (9) “The Updated Lender’s Instructions state property taxes are to be paid current even though the preliminary report indicates that they are paid current”; (10) “A broker fee of $27,000 is being paid to DCM Capital Partners, LLC, which appears to be a Florida LLC that has not been in good standing since 2021 and there are no instructions to pay that entity in the escrow file”; and (11) “There is no public information on Bellnorth except that it is a Delaware LLC. It is not a licensed lender or licensed to do business in California.” (Motion pp. 11-12.) Plaintiff submits a declaration by a lending and real estate expert, James Hibert. (Hibert Declaration ¶ 1.) Hiber’s declaration discusses relevant customary hard money lending practices and states Bellnorth’s loan does not meet industry standards and does not appear to be a bona fide hard money loan. (Hibert Declaration ¶¶ 6-17.)

 

Plaintiff argues it is likely to succeed on the merits of the action because the deed of trust was recorded four weeks after this court issued the writ of attachment, and it secured a loan amount five times more than the original amount that “coincidentally took up any remaining equity in the Subject Property.” (Motion pp. 13-14.) Plaintiff argues “[t]he timing and amount of the loan alone strongly support that Dobrin intentionally engaged with Bellnorth to hinder Plaintiff’s ability to recover any judgment lien arising from adjudication in Plaintiff’s favor.” (Motion p. 14.) Plaintiff further argues the escrow documents and evidence Bellnorth’s loan is not a bona fide hard money loan support finding fraud. (Motion pp. 14-15.)

 

Plaintiff argues it will suffer irreparable harm because Boris Dobrin’s only material asset is the Subject Property and, if Bellnorth forecloses on the Subject Property, Boris Dobrin “is likely to claim insolvency when this matter is concluded and unable to pay any judgment in Plaintiff’s favor.” (Motion p. 16.)

 

Plaintiff also argues the balance of equities favors issuing an injunction. Plaintiff states Bellnorth would suffer no harm from an injunction because the Subject Property was fraudulently transferred, making the Bellnorth deed of trust void. (Motion p. 16.) Plaintiff further argues that if Bellnorth argues it is a bona fide lender, the only potential harm to Bellnorth is the temporary delay of sale. (Motion p. 16.)

 

b.     Bellnorth’s Opposition

 

Bellnorth argues its promissory note and deed of trust are legitimate, and the loan and deed of trust were recorded in good faith without knowledge of any alleged encumbrances. (Opposition p. 3.) Bellnorth states that before it recorded the deed of trust, a title report was prepared, which indicated the Subject Property was free of any material encumbrances, aside from the senior deed of trust, tax liens, and other minor liens. (Opposition p. 3; Munoz Declaration ¶¶ 9-11.)  Bellnorth argues that its “reliance on a clear title report is sufficient to demonstrate good faith.” (Opposition p. 4.) Bellnorth further states that Plaintiff failed to file the writ of attachment with the Los Angeles County Recorder, which could have ensured Bellnorth had notice of the attachment lien. (Opposition p. 5.)

 

Bellnorth also argues Plaintiff’s fraud allegations are speculative, unsupported, and do not establish a fraudulent scheme. (Opposition p. 5.) Bellnorth states Plaintiff’s claim Bellnorth is not a bona fide hard money lender is “baseless, lacking any supporting evidence from industry standards, and mischaracterizes the nature of private loans.” (Opposition pp. 5-6.) Bellnorth argues the combined loan-to-value ratio and nature of the collateral are guidelines, not requirements and “[p]rivate lenders, particularly those focused on asset-based lending, frequently offer loans based solely on collateral value rather than creditworthiness, particularly where a title report confirms ownership and priority liens.” (Opposition p. 6.) Bellnorth states it is not required to give Plaintiff a justification for the loan terms. (Opposition p. 7.)

 

Bellnorth argues Plaintiff has not shown it is likely to prevail on the merits because the claims do not involve a direct real property interest in the Subject Property. (Opposition p. 7.)

 

Bellnorth further argues Plaintiff cannot demonstrate irreparable harm because financial harm alone does not warrant injunctive relief. (Opposition pp. 7-8.) Bellnorth argues Plaintiff’s claims are speculative because Plaintiff has not received a recorded Notice of Sale for the Subject Property, no foreclosure sale is currently scheduled, and the Subject Property is not currently listed for sale. (Opposition p. 9.) Bellnorth argues “courts do not grant injunctions based on conjecture and unsubstantiated fears” and “Plaintiff’s fears about a potential sale are hypothetical at best, and do not rise to the level of immediate, irreparable harm required to justify a TRO.” (Opposition pp. 8-9.) Bellnorth also states that Plaintiff’s alleged harm is financial, and Plaintiff can be compensated with monetary damages. (Opposition p. 9.)

 

Finally, Bellnorth argues it “stands to suffer significant harm if enjoined from proceeding with its foreclosure, which was lawfully initiated following the Notice of Default recorded on July 15, 2024.” (Opposition p. 9.)

 

c.      Plaintiff’s Reply

 

Plaintiff states Bellnorth’s $1 million wire receipt leaves $2 million of the loan undocumented and unaccounted for. (Reply p. 4.)

 

Plaintiff also argues Bellnorth has proffered little evidence supporting its good faith defense. Plaintiff argues that “Bellnorth and Dobrin rushed to beat Plaintiff’s recording of its writ” and “Bellnorth and Dobrin knew this, which is why they sped up their process, increased the loan amount five-fold and only funded $300,000 through escrow.” (Reply p. 5.) Plaintiff also argues that “Bellnorth’s continued refusal to produce any evidence of complete disbursement of the loan proceeds only further supports Plaintiff’s case that the loan is fraudulent and Dobrin did not receive a reasonably equivalent value for the Deed of Trust.” (Reply p. 5.)

 

Plaintiff argues it has shown a likelihood of prevailing on the merits because an attachment creates a real property interest. (Reply p. 6.)

 

Plaintiff also argues that Bellnorth’s contention financial harm cannot warrant relief “misstates the findings and facts of its cited authority.” (Reply p. 8.) Plaintiff states that “Dobrin simply does not have the means to adequately compensate Plaintiff if Bellnorth’s foreclosure sale is effected” because “Dobrin and his wife have tax liens and several judgment liens recorded against them.” (Reply p. 8.) Plaintiff also states, “[t]he only assets of theirs that had equity was the Subject Property” and “[i]f it is foreclosed upon by Bellnorth’s fraudulent lien, Plaintiff will have no ability to collect on any monetary damage award it receives.” (Reply p. 8.)

 

Finally, Plaintiff states irreparable harm is irrelevant for a preliminary injunction and the balance of equities favors issuing an injunction because Bellnorth’s only potential harm is a temporary delay of sale while Plaintiff will lose the ability to collect any judgment against Dobrin. (Reply p. 10.)

 

IV.                     ANALYSIS 

 

a.     Injury

 

The court first considers which of the parties is likely to suffer greater injury upon the grant or denial of the injunction.

 

Plaintiff states it will suffer irreparable harm because if Bellnorth forecloses on the Subject Property, Boris Dobrin will claim insolvency and be unable to pay any judgment in Plaintiff’s favor. (Motion p. 16.) Plaintiff states Bellnorth’s only potential harm is a temporary delay in selling the property. (Motion p. 16.)   Bellnorth argues it will suffer significant harm because it lawfully recorded its interest but will be prevented from proceeding with its foreclosure. (Opposition p. 9.)

 

When a cause of action involves real property, the court may find that the party who stands to lose the property is more likely to suffer irreparable harm than the party seeking to sell the property. (Baypoint Mortgage Corp. v. Crest Premium Real Estate etc. Trust, (1985) 168 Cal.App.3d 818, 824.) At worst, if the party seeking to sell the property prevails at trial, “the worst that it will have suffered will have been some delay in foreclosure.” (Id.) In contrast, the party who stands to lose the property is more likely to suffer greater harm because it may lose its trust deeds and be chargeable with foreclosure expenses. (Id. at pp. 824-825.)

 

This cause of action originated because the Testing Company breached its contractual obligations under a lease. (FAC ¶ 29; Complaint ¶¶ 12-17.) Plaintiff’s first amended complaint added causes of action against Bellnorth and alleged “a fraudulent transfer was made by Defendants with the actual intent to hinder, delay or defraud Plaintiff and to avoid Plaintiff from being able to recover the amounts due and owing under the Lease and Guaranty, as amended, and to enforce the writ of attachment granted.” (FAC ¶ 37.) Thus, although this cause of action involves the Subject Property, other than its interest as Boris Dobrin’s potential creditor, Plaintiff alleges no interest in the Subject Property.

 

A “mere monetary loss is not irreparable in contemplation of the remedy of injunction unless there is an averment or a showing that [the] parties causing the loss are insolvent or in any manner unable to respond in damages.” (West Coast Constr. Co. v. Oceano Sanitary Dist. (1971) 17 Cal.App.3d 693, 700.)

 

Plaintiff argues that if the Subject Property is sold, Boris Dorbin will claim insolvency and will be unable to pay any judgment in Plaintiff’s favor. (Motion p. 16.) Plaintiff submits a declaration by its counsel stating that the Subject Property is valued at $5 to $6 million. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 8.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration further states that with the Bellnorth loan, the Subject Property’s total obligations are roughly $6.2 million. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 9.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration states that “if the Subject Property is foreclosed upon and sold for its current estimated value, Dobrin will be rendered insolvent and unable to pay any judgment rendered in Plaintiff’s favor.” (Gizer Declaration ¶ 9.)

 

Plaintiff has submitted evidence showing the obligations on the Subject Property total more than what the Subject Property is worth. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 9.) However, Plaintiff has submitted no evidence showing Boris Dobrin lacks other assets to pay the judgment.

 

Like Plaintiff, Bellnorth risks suffering monetary harm if it is prevented from selling the property.

 

Accordingly, because both parties risk suffering monetary harm, this court finds that Plaintiff has not shown it is likely to suffer greater injury upon the denial of the injunction.

 

b.     Reasonable Probability of Prevailing on the Merits

 

Next, this court considers whether there is a reasonable probability Plaintiff will prevail on the merits.

 

Plaintiff asserts a cause of action for fraudulent transfer under Civil Code section 3439.04. (Motion p. 13.) Section 3439.04(a) states:

 

(1)   A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is voidable as to a creditor, whether the creditor's claim arose before or after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred, if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation as follows:

 

(1)   With actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor.

 

(2)   Without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation, and the debtor either:

 

(1)    Was engaged or was about to engage in a business or a transaction for which the remaining assets of the debtor were unreasonably small in relation to the business or transaction.

 

(2)    Intended to incur, or believed or reasonably should have believed that the debtor would incur, debts beyond the debtor's ability to pay as they became due.

 

(Civ. Code § 3439.04(a).)

 

To determine whether the debtor acted with actual intent, the court may consider, “among other factors . . . any or all of the following:”

 

(1)   Whether the transfer or obligation was to an insider.

 

(2)   Whether the debtor retained possession or control of the property transferred after the transfer.

 

(3)   Whether the transfer or obligation was disclosed or concealed.

 

(4)   Whether before the transfer was made or obligation was incurred, the debtor had been sued or threatened with suit.

 

(5)   Whether the transfer was of substantially all the debtor's assets.

 

(6)   Whether the debtor absconded.

 

(7)   Whether the debtor removed or concealed assets.

 

(8)   Whether the value of the consideration received by the debtor was reasonably equivalent to the value of the asset transferred or the amount of the obligation incurred.

 

(9)   Whether the debtor was insolvent or became insolvent shortly after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred.

 

(10)         Whether the transfer occurred shortly before or shortly after a substantial debt was incurred.

 

(11)         Whether the debtor transferred the essential assets of the business to a lienor that transferred the assets to an insider of the debtor.

 

(Civ. Code § 3439.04(b).)

 

Plaintiff must show the debtor made a transfer or incurred an obligation. (Civ. Code § 3439.04(a).) Plaintiff filed its complaint against Boris Dobrin on July 10, 2023. The promissory note was executed on October 31, 2023, and Bellnorth’s deed of trust was recorded on November 1, 2023. (Gizer Declaration ¶¶ 6, 11.) Accordingly, Plaintiff submitted evidence Dobrin made a transfer or incurred an obligation.

 

Plaintiff must show the transfer was made “[w]ith actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor.” (Civ. Code § 3439.04(a).) As noted above, Plaintiff filed its complaint against Boris Dobrin on July 10, 2023, the promissory note was executed on October 31, 2023, and Bellnorth’s deed of trust was recorded on November 1, 2023. (Gizer Declaration ¶¶ 6, 11.) Thus, Plaintiff submitted evidence showing the transfer was made after the debtor was sued. (Civ. Code § 3439.04(b)(4).)

 

Moreover, the timing here supports Plaintiff’s claim that the transfer was made “[w]ith actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor.” (See Oiye v. Fox (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1059 [“The timing of defendant’s conveyance of his personal residence to a trust after he was arrested on charges of molestation may be indicative of an intent to protect his assets against creditors.”]) On August 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed its application for a right to attach order seeking an attachment against the Subject Property. (Gizer ¶ 3.) On August 24, 2024, a title company provided a preliminary title report to Bellnorth and Dobrin for a $600,000 loan, to be secured by the Subject Property. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 4.) The court issued a right to attach order and order for issuance of right of attachment on October 2, 2023, Boris Dobrin executed the promissory note on October 31, 2023, which increased the loan amount from $600,000 to $3,350,000, and Bellnorth recorded the deed of trust on November 1, 2023. (Gizer Declaration ¶¶ 5-6, 11.) Finally, Plaintiff submits evidence by way of an expert’s declaration indicating the Bellnorth loan does not meet industry standards and does not appear to be a bona fide hard money loan. (Hibert Declaration ¶¶ 6-17.) Accordingly, this court finds Plaintiff has submitted evidence showing “the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation . . . [w]ith actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor.” (Civ. Code § 3439.04(a)(2).)

 

Plaintiff must also show the “debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation . . . [w]ithout receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation.” (Civ. Code § 3439.04(a)(2).) Bellnorth’s Member and Manager, David Munoz, submits a declaration stating that Bellnorth disbursed $3,050,000.00 outside of escrow and $300,000 through escrow. (Munoz Declaration ¶ 3.) Munoz’s declaration further states that the “Promissory Note was an all-inclusive promissory note which incorporated the previous amounts loaned and disbursed to Boris Dobrin and Zarina Dobrin, including, but not limited to the wire transfer of $1,016,665.00, along with the final $300,000.00 disbursed to Boris Dobrin and Zarina Dobrin through escrow as set forth in the Lender’s Instructions.” (Munoz Declaration ¶ 3.) And, at the request of Boris Dobrin and Zarina Dobrin, Bellnorth deposited $1,016,665.00 into Boris Dobrin’s company, the Testing Company, “instead of to them individually.” (Munoz Declaration ¶ 3.)

 

The total loan amount was purportedly for $3,350.000.00. (Gizer Declaration ¶ 5.) Munoz’s declaration accounts for transfers totaling $1,316,665.00. (Munoz Declaration ¶ 3; Ex. 8.) This leaves more than $2 million dollars of the purported loan unaccounted for.  Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff has shown the “debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation . . . [w]ithout receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation.” (Civ. Code § 3439.04(a)(2).)

 

Plaintiff must also show Dobrin “[w]as engaged or was about to engage in a business or a transaction for which the remaining assets of the debtor were unreasonably small in relation to the business or transaction” or “[i]ntended to incur, or believed or reasonably should have believed that the debtor would incur, debts beyond the debtor's ability to pay as they became due.”(Civ. Code § 3439.04(a)(2).) Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration states that “[w]ith the Bellnorth loan encumbering the Subject Property further with a $3,350,000 deed of trust, assuming no further obligations, the total obligations on the Subject Property total roughly $6.2 million. Thus, if the Subject Property is foreclosed upon and sold for its current estimated value, Dobrin will be rendered insolvent and unable to pay any judgment rendered in Plaintiff’s favor.” (Gizer Declaration ¶ 9.)  Accordingly, although this court acknowledges its finding above that Plaintiff submitted no specific evidence showing Boris Dobrin lacks other assets, this court finds that Plaintiff has shown “the debtor . . . reasonably should have believed that the debtor would incur, debts beyond the debtor's ability to pay as they became due.” (Civ. Code § 3439.04(a)(2)(B).)

 

Thus, Plaintiff has shown it is likely to prevail on the merits. However, because Plaintiff has not shown it is likely to suffer greater harm upon the denial of the injunction, this court denies the Motion.

 

V.                    CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies the Motion.
