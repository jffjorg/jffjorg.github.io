Case Number: 21STCV13094    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 4, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV13094

MOTION: 

Motion for Final Approval of Class Action Settlement

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Ramiro Mata Moya

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Ramiro Mata Moya (“Plaintiff”) and Defendant Chipmasters Manufacturing, Inc. (“Defendant”) jointly seek final approval of the class action settlement in this matter. 

ANALYSIS

Before the Court finally approves a class action settlement, the court must ascertain the fairness of the proposed settlement using procedures such as in-chamber conferences, examination of documents or witnesses, and consideration of objections by class members or any other appropriate evidence.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.769, subd. (g).) 

Plaintiff submits the Declaration of Tracy Dantema (“Dantema”), who works as a case manager for ILYM Group (“ILYM”), the settlement administrator for this case.  Dantema states Defendant provided ILYM with information for 29 members of the class.  ILYM mailed a  notice the Court approved to those 29 class members.  The post office returned 3 notices to ILYM.  Of those, ILYM found forwarding information for one class member.  For two class members, ILYM could not find updated information.  (See Declaration of Tracy Dantema, ¶¶ 4-10.)  ILYM has not received any requests for exclusion, objections to, or disputes concerning the settlement.  (Dantema Decl. , ¶¶ 11-13.) 

The average payment from the class action settlement for a class member is $3,315.71, and the highest payment for a class member is $7,135.41.  (Dantema Decl., ¶ 16.)  The average payment for a class member under the Labor Code Private Attorneys’ General Act of 2004, or PAGA, is $43.10; while the highest payment is $101.39.  (Ibid.)  This evidence suggests the members of the class consider the settlement to be in their best interests.  The Court also finds Plaintiff’s counsel has presented evidence of his efforts in litigating this matter, which substantiate the fees Plaintiff’s counsel seeks.  (See Declaration of Alfredo Nava.)  Accordingly, the Court concludes the preliminarily approved settlement is fair and reasonable and grants final approval of the settlement.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 23STCV30791    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 4, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV30791

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Maira Flashey

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Maira Flashey (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel Defendant FCA US, Inc. (“Defendant”) to further respond to Requests for Production of Documents, set one (“RPD”), Nos. 8-10, 12-20, 26, 40-41, 43-47, and 51-57.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310.) 

In the instant case, Plaintiff alleges her 2022 Dodge Durango vehicle had defects Defendant was unable to repair within a reasonable number of attempts.  Plaintiff, therefore, contends Defendant is liable under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.  Plaintiff demanded Defendant produce documents related to Plaintiff’s vehicle and concerning the same make and model of that vehicle.  Plaintiff served the RPD on Defendant on February 6, 2024 by e-mail.  Defendant’s responses were due by March 11, 2024.  Defendant failed to timely respond without explanation.  As such, Defendant waived all objections.  Defendant then served unverified and untimely responses with objections to the RPD on April 6, 2024, but produced no documents. 

“Unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.”  (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.)  Defendant belatedly served a verification of its response on June 3, 2024, although it was purportedly executed on May 30, 2024.  However, by serving untimely responses, Defendant had waived all objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)  Accordingly, Defendant must further respond to the RPD.  Therefore, Defendant is to serve verified responses, without objections, to RPD Nos. 8-10, 12-20, 26, 40-41, 43-47, and 51-57 and produce responsive documents to Plaintiff within 30 days. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

DEPARTMENT

39

24STCV13779

MOTIONS

Demurrers to Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Gilsleider, McMahon, Molinelli & Phan, Jennifer E. Miller, Progressive Casualty Insurance Company, Progressive West Insurance Company, Progressive Select Insurance Company, United Financial Casualty, Parker, PLLC, and Michael R. Parker  

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Darryl Franklin and Joshua Franklin

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Darryl Franklin and Joshua Franklin (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Gilsleider, McMahon, Molinelli & Phan, Jennifer E. Miller, Progressive Casualty Insurance Company, Progressive West Insurance Company, Progressive Select Insurance Company, United Financial Casualty, Parker, PLLC, and Michael R. Parker (collectively, “Defendants”) in connection with an underlying lawsuit that was filed against Plaintiffs involving a motor vehicle accident.  Defendants Gilsleider, McMahon, Molinelli & Phan and Jennifer E. Miller (collectively, the “Gilsleider firm Defendants”) demur to Plaintiffs’ complaint.  Defendants Progressive Casualty Insurance Company, Progressive West Insurance Company, Progressive Select Insurance Company, and United Financial Casualty (collectively, the “Progressive Defendants”) separately demur to Plaintiffs’ complaint.  Defendants Parker, PLLC and Michael R. Parker (collectively, the “Parker Defendants”) demur to Plaintiffs’ complaint and move to strike portions of the complaint. 

Plaintiffs allege the Progressive Defendants insured Plaintiffs in connection with the motor vehicle collision which led to the underlying lawsuit.  The Progressive Defendants appointed the Gilsleider firm Defendants to represent Plaintiffs in that lawsuit.  The claimant in the underlying lawsuit made a policy limits demand against Plaintiffs, which Plaintiffs allege the Gilsleider firm Defendants and the Progressive Defendants improperly rejected.  The Progressive Defendants then appointed the Parker Defendants to represent Plaintiffs in the underlying lawsuit.  Plaintiffs contend this is because the Progressive Defendants wanted to use Plaintiffs’ case to investigate an expert (Michael Hayavi, M.D.) who was involved in a separate case and was also used by the claimant in the underlying lawsuit against Plaintiffs.  Plaintiffs eventually hired their own counsel, and the Progressive Defendants settled the underlying claim for three times more than the policy limits, which the Progressive Defendants satisfied.  As the motions substantially overlap, the Court considers them together.  Plaintiffs filed one omnibus opposition to the three sets of demurrers and the motion to strike.    

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d ¶767, 782.)

Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is for fraud against all Defendants.  A party must allege fraud with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.)  To state a case of action for fraudulent concealment, Plaintiffs must allege Defendants concealed or suppressed a material fact that Defendants were under a duty to disclose to Plaintiffs with the intent to defraud Plaintiffs; Plaintiffs were unaware of the fact and would not have acted in the same way if Plaintiffs knew of the concealed fact; and the concealment caused Plaintiffs to sustain damages.  (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 868.) 

Plaintiffs allege the Gilsleider firm Defendants and the Parker Defendants were Plaintiffs’ legal counsel and, as such, they owed fiduciary duties to Plaintiffs (Complaint, ¶ 58); the Progressive Defendants owed Plaintiffs duties of good faith and fair dealing as Plaintiffs’ insurer (id., ¶ 61); the Gilsleider firm Defendants “intentionally breached their duty of disclosure by, inter alia: [¶] Failing to include [Plaintiff] in communications with [the Progressive Defendants] regarding the . . . Lawsuit generally; [¶] Failing to keep [Plaintiffs] apprised of settlement proposals, and actively hiding those proposals; [¶] Failing to timely send [Plaintiffs] the Second Policy Limits Demand; and [¶] Failing to respond at all to [Plaintiffs’] request for advice regarding the Second Policy Limits Demand” (id., ¶ 63); the Parker Defendants “intentionally breached their duty of disclosure by, inter alia: [¶] Failing to include [Plaintiffs] in communications with [the Progressive Defendants] regarding the . . . Lawsuit generally; [¶] Refusing to allow [Plaintiffs] to join the February 2, 2023 ‘case review’ call;  [¶] Failing to keep [Plaintiffs] apprised of settlement proposals, and actively hiding those proposals; [¶] Failing to advise [Plaintiffs] regarding the flagrant conflict of interest inherent in representing [Plaintiffs] and [the Progressive Defendants] in the ISI Investigation and the Hayavi Major Case, or obtain [Plaintiffs’] informed written consent to that concurrent representation; and [¶] Failing to disclose the intent to use the . . . Lawsuit to further [the Progressive Defendants’] objectives in the ISI Investigation and the Hayavi Major Case” (id., ¶ 64); the Progressive Defendants “intentionally breached [their] duty of disclosure by, inter alia: [¶] Forbidding its counsel from including [Plaintiffs], joint clients, in communications regarding the . . . Lawsuit, . . . ; [¶] Failing to keep [Plaintiffs] apprised of settlement proposals, and actively hiding those proposals; [¶] Failing to timely send [Plaintiffs] the Second Policy Limits Demand; [¶] Failing to advise the [Plaintiffs] regarding the flagrant conflict of interest inherent in representing [Plaintiffs] and [the Progressive Defendants] in the . . .  Lawsuit while representing [the Progressive Defendants] in the ISI Investigation and the Hayavi Major Case; and  [¶] Failing [sic] disclose the intent to use the . . .  Lawsuit to further [the Progressive Defendants’] objectives in the ISI Investigation and the Hayavi Major Case” (id., ¶ 65. 

Plaintiffs further allege “[i]n failing to disclose the foregoing information, Defendants intended to deceive [Plaintiffs], and [Plaintiffs] were in fact deceived: while waiting for advice (which never came), [Plaintiffs] did not seek independent legal counsel regarding the Second Policy Limits Demand and did not object to the assignment of the . . . Lawsuit to [the Parker Defendants].  Any reasonable attorney would have explained the predictable ramifications of declining to accept the Second Policy Limits Demand and would have advised [Plaintiffs] to direct their counsel and carrier to accept the Second Policy Limits Demand.  Had [Plaintiffs] not been deceived, they would have timely sought independent counsel, and with that counsel, directed [the Progressive Defendants] to accept the Second Policy Limits Demand.  [The Progressive Defendants], if only out of self-interest (i.e., avoidance of bad faith liability), would then have accepted the Second Policy Limits Demand, and the . . . Lawsuit would have ended in May 2022.” (Complaint, ¶ 67). 

First Cause of Action: Fraud

In their demurrer, the Gilsleider firm Defendants argue Plaintiffs cannot prevail on the cause of action for fraud because Plaintiffs do not allege damages. They also contend they “cannot be the proximate cause of Plaintiffs’ alleged injuries, because: (1) [the Gilsleider firm] Defendants were not counsel of record at the time the matter settled; (2) Plaintiffs suffered no injuries, because [the Progressive Defendants] paid the full amount in settlement and legal defense cost, eliminating any adverse verdict; and (3) under well-settled California law [the Progressive Defendants], not [the Gilsleider firm] Defendants or Plaintiff[s], controlled the underlying litigation and had the right to determine when the underlying matter settled.”  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Defendants’ Demurrer, p. 8.)  As the Gilsleider firm Defendants point out, they are not liable for decisions made by subsequent counsel.  (Filbin v. Fitzgerald (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 154, 171-172.)  Further, because the Progressive Defendants defended Plaintiffs in the underlying action, the Progressive Defendants determined whether to settle that action.  (Pitzer College v. Indian Harbor Ins. Co. 8 Cal.5th 93, 108; Safeco Insurance Company v. Superior Court (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 782,789.)  Accordingly, Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to show any fraud by the Gilsleider firm Defendants caused Plaintiffs’ damages and, therefore, fail to state a cause of action for fraud against them. 

For the same reason, Plaintiffs’ cause of action for fraud against the Parker Defendants fails.  As the Progressive Defendants defended Plaintiffs in the underlying action, the Progressive Defendants controlled the litigation.  As such, Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to show any fraud by the Parker Defendants caused Plaintiffs’ damages.

Plaintiffs also fail to allege the Progressive Defendants’ alleged fraud caused Plaintiffs’ damages.  Because the Progressive Defendants decided when to settle the underlying claim, the Progressive Defendants’ alleged failure to inform Plaintiffs of information regarding the underlying claim could not have caused Plaintiffs’ damages.  

Second and Third Causes of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duties

To state a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duties, Plaintiffs must allege Defendants owed Plaintiffs fiduciary duties; Defendants breached those duties; and Plaintiffs sustained damages from that breach.  (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.)

As set forth above, Plaintiffs fail to allege any damages resulted from breach of fiduciary duties by the Gilsleider firm Defendants or the Parker Defendants.  Accordingly, Plaintiffs fail to state claims for breach of fiduciary duties against those Defendants.

Plaintiffs cannot state a claim for breach of fiduciary duties against the Progressive Defendants because insurers are not fiduciaries.  (Vu v. Prudential Property & Casualty Ins. Co. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1142, 1150-1151.)  The Court sustains the demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action for breach of fiduciary duties against the Progressive Defendants without leave to amend.

Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action: Bad Faith

To state a cause of action for bad faith, Plaintiffs must allege an insurer was obligated to Plaintiffs; the insurer unreasonably breached a duty under the insurance policy; and that breach caused economic damages to Plaintiffs.  (Love v. Fire Ins. Exchange (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 1136, 1151; Waters v. United Services Auto. Ass'n (1996) 41 Cal.App.4th 1063, 1079.)

Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to state a claim for bad faith against the Progressive Defendants.  “[T]he insurer must settle within policy limits when there is substantial likelihood of recovery in excess of those limits.  The duty to settle is implied in law to protect the insured from exposure to liability in excess of coverage as a result of the insurer's gamble—on which only the insured might lose.”  (Murphy v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1976) 17 Cal.3d 937, 941, internal citations omitted.)  “An unreasonable refusal to settle may subject the insurer to liability for the entire amount of the judgment rendered against the insured, including any portion in excess of the policy limits.”  (Hamilton v. Maryland Cas. Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 718, 725.)  Plaintiffs do not allege there is a judgment rendered against them beyond the policy limits of their policy with the Progressive Defendants.  As such, Plaintiffs do not allege a claim for bad faith against the Progressive Defendants. 

As Plaintiffs do not allege that there is a judgment against them beyond the policy limits of their policy with the Progressive Defendants, Plaintiffs must allege the Progressive Defendants “caused harm different from exposure to excess liability . . . .”  (J.B. Aguerre, Inc. v. American Guarantee & Liability Ins. Co. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 6, 13.)  Plaintiffs fail to allege any such harm.  While Plaintiffs contend Plaintiffs hired outside counsel, the Progressive Defendants are not liable for that cost unless their “tortious conduct reasonably compel[led] the insured to retain an attorney to obtain the benefits due under a policy . . . .”  (Emerald Bay Community Assn. v. Golden Eagle Ins. Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1096.)  As Plaintiffs concede, the Progressive Defendants provided Plaintiffs with a defense to the underlying action and settled the action on Plaintiffs’ behalf.  Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to show Plaintiffs had to hire counsel to obtain the benefits of their policy from the Progressive Defendants.  Likewise, Plaintiffs’ claim the delay in settlement distressed Plaintiffs cannot be their only basis for damages.  (Continental Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 69, 85.)

Plaintiffs’ fourth and fifth causes of action against the Gilsleider firm Defendants and the Parker Defendants fail as a matter of law.  Attorneys are not liable for insurance bad faith.  (Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, 576.)  The Court sustains the demurrer to the fourth and fifth causes of action against the Gilsleider firm Defendants and the Parker Defendants without leave to amend.

Sixth and Seventh Causes of Action: Legal Malpractice

As set forth above, Plaintiffs fail to allege any damages from breach of fiduciary duties by the Gilsleider firm Defendants or the Parker Defendants.  Accordingly, Plaintiffs fail to state claims for legal malpractice against them.

Plaintiffs do not allege the Progressive Defendants are attorneys or law firms.  As such, the Progressive Defendants are not liable for legal malpractice.  (Merril v. Rsrv. Ins. Co. (1973) 34 Cal.App.3d 858, 880.)  The Court sustains the demurrer to the sixth and seventh causes of action without leave to amend.

Motion to Strike

As the Court sustains the Parker Defendants’ demurrer, the motion to strike is moot.  However, the Court notes Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to support an award of punitive damages.  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Plaintiff does not allege any specific facts that constitute malicious conduct, which could support an award of punitive damages.  (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶ 158.)  Accordingly, Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to support an award of punitive damages. 

Plaintiffs also fail to allege facts to support an award of attorney fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1021.5, which provides, “Upon motion, a court may award attorneys' fees to a successful party against one or more opposing parties in any action which has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest if: (a) a significant benefit, whether pecuniary or nonpecuniary, has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons, (b) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement, or of enforcement by one public entity against another public entity, are such as to make the award appropriate, and (c) such fees should not in the interest of justice be paid out of the recovery, if any.”  In the instant case, Plaintiffs do not seek enforcement of a right that will benefit the public.  Rather, Plaintiffs seek enforcement of their own rights.  Thus, Plaintiffs do not allege facts to show they are entitled to attorney fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 1021.5.  

Plaintiffs also fail to allege they are entitled to disgorgement of attorney fees.  The attorney fees the Parker Defendants received for work with which Plaintiffs are dissatisfied are not damages.  (Orrick Herrington & Sutcliffe v. Superior Court (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1052, 1058.)

The Parker Defendants also correctly point out Plaintiffs cannot recover emotional distress damages absent other economic damages.  (Continental Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 69, 85.)

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court sustains the demurrers to the second, third, sixth, and seventh causes of action by the Progressive Defendants and to the fourth cause of action by the Gilsleider firm Defendants and the Parker Defendants without leave to amend.  The Court otherwise sustains the demurrers as to the other causes of actions asserted against the Defendants with leave to amend.

Sustaining the demurrers render the motion to strike moot.

Plaintiffs are to file an amended complaint within 10 days of notice of this order. 

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
