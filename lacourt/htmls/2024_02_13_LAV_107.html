DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03385    Hearing Date: February 13, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

PARVANEH AZMOON,

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

MUFG UNION BANK N.A,

 

Defendant.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  23VECV03385                 107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

Demurrer - with Motion to Strike (CCP 430.10)

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

The Demurrer is sustained without leave to amend, and the Motion to Strike is denied as moot.

I. BACKGROUND

 

A. Factual

 

The following facts are drawn from the First Amended Complaint (as defined below).

 

On September 26, 2017, Parvaneh Azmoon (“Plaintiff”) obtained a home equity line of credit (the “Mortgage”) on her residential real property (the “Property”) from MUFG Union Bank, N.A. (“MUFG”), of which U.S. Bank National Association (“Defendant”) became the successor by merger. The Mortgage was secured by a Deed of Trust, which was recorded in second position against the Property.

 

In July 2020, Plaintiff applied to MUFG for a loss mitigation modification. MUFG denied the application, determining that Plaintiff was ineligible because she did not have sufficient income. Plaintiff appealed. MUFG denied Plaintiff’s appeal.

 

In October 2021, Plaintiff again applied for a loss mitigation modification, and again, MUFG denied the application for the same reasons. In December 2021, MUFG approved Plaintiff for a repayment plan, but the payments thereunder were “twice as much” as her existing payments.

 

Plaintiff applied two more times for a loss mitigation modification—in January 2022 and in the summer of 2022. Each time, MUFG determined Plaintiff to be ineligible therefore and denied the application.

 

On April 20, 2022, Plaintiff was notified that her line of credit was being frozen due to unsatisfactory credit performance.

 

On October 17, 2022, Plaintiff was notified that her debt was being accelerated. At the time, she was less than 90 days delinquent.

 

In December 2022, Plaintiff ran her own credit report, which showed an average credit score of 719.66.

 

B. Procedural

 

On August 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against MUFG alleging one cause of action for violations of Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq. Defendant demurred and filed a motion to strike. Plaintiff opposed the demurrer and the motion to strike, and Defendant replied to the opposition to the motion to strike.

 

On October 25, 2023, this court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend and found the motion to strike thereby mooted.

 

On November 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “First Amended Complaint” or the “FAC”). On January 11, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and a motion to strike (the “Motion to Strike”). On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition (the “Opposition”) to the Demurrer, and on February 5, 2024, Defendant filed a reply to the Opposition. Plaintiff did not oppose the Motion to Strike.

 

II. MOVING PARTY’S GROUNDS FOR THE DEMURRER

           

Defendant demurs to the FAC on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against it.

           

III. ANALYSIS

 

The Demurrer

 

                        Demurrers Generally

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

“[T]he pleadings set the boundaries of the issues to be resolved at summary judgment. A plaintiff cannot bring up new, unpleaded issues in his or her opposing papers.” (Oakland Raiders v. National Football League (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 621, 648 (internal citations and quote marks omitted).) The same is true of pleadings subject to a demurrer. “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.” (Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 482.)

 

                        Cal. Bus. & Prof. C. § 17200 a/k/a The Unfair Competition Law

 

Cal. Bus. & Prof. C. § 17200 et seq. (the “UCL”) “broadly prohibits ‘any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising….’ Because the statute is framed in the disjunctive, a business practice need only meet one of the three criteria to be considered unfair competition. Therefore, an act or practice is ‘unfair competition’ under the UCL if it is forbidden by law or, even if not specifically prohibited by law, is deemed an unfair act or practice.” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1359 (internal citations omitted).)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s actions are each of unlawful, unfair, and fraudulent: unlawful because of its loan modification policies, unfair because those actions included “negligent servicing and blaming Plaintiff for their own mistakes,” and fraudulent because MUFG’s failure to "give Plaintiff a determination for a prolonged period of time” put Plaintiff “reasonably under the impression that she would be granted alternatives to foreclosure.” (Opposition p. 5.)

 

                        “Unlawful” Criterion

 

“An ‘unlawful’ business practice or act within the meaning of the UCL is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Bernardo v. Planned Parenthood Federation of America (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 322, 351.) “By proscribing ‘any unlawful’ business practice, [the UCL] borrows violations of other laws and treats them as unlawful practices that the unfair competition law makes independently actionable.” (Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 180.) A plaintiff may “fail[] to meet her burden of stating and substantiating a legally sufficient claim of unlawfulness” by “fail[ing] to identify any statutory, regulatory or decisional law that [the defendant] allegedly violated.” (Bernardo v. Planned Parenthood Federation of America, supra, at 352 (citing Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88).)

 

Here, Plaintiff fails to meet her burden of stating and substantiating a legally sufficient claim of unlawfulness because the FAC does not identify any law that Defendant violated other than the UCL.

 

“Unfair” Criterion

 

As this court held in its October 25, 2023 ruling on the prior demurrer in this case, “unfair” in the context of consumer cases under the UCL may be determined by one of three tests: either the narrower test set out by Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 186–87, which Defendant urges us to apply, or the broader tests described by Camacho v. Automobile Club of Southern California (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1394, 1401–03. The Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. test requires that “any finding of unfairness to competitors under [the UCL] be tethered to some legislatively declared policy or proof of some actual or threatened impact on competition.” (supra, at 86–87.) In other words, “unfair” “means conduct that threatens an incipient violation of an antitrust law, or violates the policy or spirit of one of those laws because its effects are comparable to or the same as a violation of the law, or otherwise significantly threatens or harms competition.” (Id. at 87.) The first Camacho v. Automobile Club of Southern California test comprises three factors: “(1) whether the practice, without necessarily having been previously considered unlawful, offends public policy as it has been established by statutes, the common law, or otherwise—whether, in other words, it is within at least the penumbra of some common-law, statutory, or other established concept of unfairness; (2) whether it is immoral, unethical, oppressive, or unscrupulous; (3) whether it causes substantial injury to consumers (or competitors or other businessmen).” (supra, at fn. 10 (quoting People v. Casa Blanca Convalescent Homes, Inc. (1984) 159 Cal.App.3d 509, 530).) The second Camacho v. Automobile Club of Southern California test borrows from the factors that define unfairness under section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act and requires “(1) the consumer injury must be substantial; (2) the injury must not be outweighed by any countervailing benefits to consumers or competition; and (3) it must be an injury that consumers themselves could not reasonably have avoided.” (Id. at 1403.) Plaintiff improperly relies on an unpublished case, Zuniga v. Bank of America, N.A., 2014 WL 7156403 (C.D. Cal. Dec. 9, 2014), to argue for application here of the second Camacho v. Automobile Club of Southern California test. (FAC ¶ 49.)

 

Moreover, in its October 25, 2023, this court held that “[a]bsent a legal duty or statutory duty, [a] complaint does not show that the business practice is actionable under the unfair[ness] [criterion].” (Notice of Ruling Exh. “A” p. 2.) And, this court held that, per the California Supreme Court, “a lender has no statutory duty of care to treat a loan modification with due care for a [home equity line of credit] loan nor does a lender have any legal duty to ‘process, review, and respond carefully and completely to Plaintiff's loan modification applications.’” (Ibid. (citing Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 922, reh'g denied (June 1, 2022)).) However, “a lender does owe a duty to a borrower to not make material misrepresentations about the status of an application for a loan modification.” (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 68.) “It is foreseeable that a borrower might be harmed by an inaccurate or untimely communication…about the status of a loan modification application, and the connection between the misrepresentation and the injury suffered could be very close.” (Id. at 69.)

 

The FAC fails to plead any facts that show unfair acts by Defendant under the UCL under any of the three tests. Plaintiff argues that the FAC pleads “MUFG’S business acts and practices, includ[ing], but [] not limited to, the following: (1) prolonged loan modification review to generate unwarranted fees; (2) seeking to collect, and collecting, various improper fees, costs and charges, that are either not legally due under the mortgage contract or California law, or that are in excess of amounts legally due; (3) mishandling borrowers’ loan documentation, and (4) dual-tracking.” (Opposition p. 5.) However, contrary to this contention, the FAC does not plead any facts about fees, so we do not consider those contentions. As far as “mishandling borrowers’ loan documentations,” the FAC alleges that Defendant denied Plaintiff’s applications for loan modifications and related appeals multiple times without providing her sufficient explanation as to why these denials were being made, from which she infers that the denials were based on “incorrect” grounds and “false numbers” that “disregard[ed] facts about Plaintiff’s true income and [net present value] numbers.” (FAC ¶ 28, 37, 44.) The FAC argues that “the information provided to Plaintiff was certainly misleading and not consistent as to the status of the loan modification and what she was supposed to do to satisfy the lender’s demands.” (FAC ¶ 47.) However, the FAC does not plead any facts to show that Defendant was obligated to provide a more specific explanation for its denials nor that Defendant was obligated to approve her loan modification based on the numbers that Plaintiff provides. Plaintiff does not allege any material misrepresentations of fact made by Defendant, nor a causal relationship between any untimeliness of her loan modification applications and her injury suffered. We therefore fail to find that these allegations as pled meet any of the tests for unfairness under the UCL. “Dual tracking” refers to the prohibited practice by a mortgage lender of simultaneously proceeding with a foreclosure while also considering the borrower’s application for a loan modification or other alternatives to foreclosure. (Valbuena v. Ocwen Loan Servicing, LLC (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 1267, 1269 (citing Cal. Civ. C. § 2923.6).) While dual tracking does violate the UCL, Cal. Civ. C. § 2923.6 refers only to first lien mortgages. (Cal. Civ. C. § 2923.6; Majd v. Bank of America, N.A. (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 1293, 1302.) It is therefore inapplicable here.

 

Moreover, the FAC does not allege any legal duty or statutory duty owed to Plaintiff by Defendant.

 

“Fraudulent” Criterion

 

“The “fraud” [criterion] of [the UCL] is unlike common law fraud or deception.” Podolsky v. First Healthcare Corp. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 632, 647.) “In order to state a cause of action under the fraud prong of the UCL a plaintiff need not show that he or others were actually deceived or confused by the conduct or business practice in question.” (Schnall v. Hertz Corp. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 1144, 1167 (quoted by Buller v. Sutter Health (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 981, 986).) “Instead, it is only necessary to show that members of the public are likely to be deceived.” (Podolsky v. First Healthcare Corp., supra, at 648 (emphasis added).)

 

Here, Plaintiff argues she has pled fraudulent conduct because of her own reasonable beliefs from and about Defendant’s conduct, but her own belief is not an element of fraud under the UCL. The FAC does not plead any facts to show that members of the public are likely to be deceived, so it does not plead any facts to show fraudulent acts by Defendant under the UCL.

 

                        Standing

 

“[O]nly plaintiffs who have suffered actual damage may pursue a private UCL action. A private plaintiff must make a twofold showing: he or she must demonstrate injury in fact and a loss of money or property caused by unfair competition.” (Peterson v. Cellco Partnership (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1590.) To show economic injury from unfair competition, a plaintiff may, by example, “(1) surrender in a transaction more, or acquire in a transaction less, than he or she otherwise would have; (2) have a present or future property interest diminished; (3) be deprived of money or property to which he or she has a cognizable claim; or (4) be required to enter into a transaction, costing money or property, that would otherwise have been unnecessary.” (Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310, 323.) A plaintiff has standing to sue under the UCL when a bank forecloses on his home while he has a loan modification application pending because the loss of the home is clear injury in fact and the possibility that the plaintiff could have proven eligibility to modify his loan is sufficient causation. (Majd v. Bank of America, N.A., supra, at 1304.) However, a borrower continuing to make payments on a loan does not constitute detrimental reliance because the borrower already had the obligation to make those payments. (Lueras v. BAC Home Loan Servicing, LP, supra, at 79, 82.) Moreover, “[p]laintiffs cannot establish standing to pursue a UCL claim based on expenses incurred in order to bring their UCL claim. If they could, the requirement that individuals show they lost money or property ‘as a result’ of the challenged practice in order to have standing to sue under the UCL would be meaningless.” (Robinson v. HSBC Bank USA (2010) 732 F.Supp.2d 976, 988.)

 

Here, Plaintiff fails to show sufficient economic injury to have standing to sue under the UCL. Plaintiff has not, for example, lost her home through foreclosure. Rather, Plaintiff alleges that, had she received the loan modification she applied for and for which she alleges she was wrongfully denied, she would have paid less in monthly payments and interest, plus she would have avoided the costs of bringing this suit. (FAC ¶ 48.) However, her continuing to pay the agreed-to payments and interest on her loan is not an economic injury. And, Plaintiff cannot rely upon her costs of bringing the suit to establish standing. Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to show sufficient economic injury to establish standing under the UCL.

 

                        Leave to Amend

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

 

Here, we do not see any possibility that Plaintiff can cure her lack of standing to sue under the UCL.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer without leave to amend.

 

The Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)

 

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for monetary damages and disgorgement of profits on the grounds that such cannot be recovered from a cause of action for violations of Cal. Bus. & Prof. C. § 17200 et seq.

 

“[T]he remedies available to private individuals for violation of the UCL are limited to restitution and injunctive relief; damages cannot be recovered.” (Graham v. Bank of America, N.A. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 609–10.) Disgorgement of profits is only available as a remedy under the UCL to the extent that it is restitutitonary and the plaintiff has an ownership interest in the money it seeks to recover from defendants. (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1147–49.)

 

The monetary damages and non-restitutionary disgorgement of profits that Plaintiff seeks are therefore not available remedies to Plaintiff in this action. However, because we have sustained the Demurrer, the Motion to Strike is rendered moot.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to Strike as moot.

 

III. Conclusion

 

Therefore, the Demurrer is sustained without leave to amend, and the Motion to Strike is denied as moot.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

                 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel is GRANTED. 

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

 

Plaintiffs’ attorney Bleau Fox, a Professional Law Corporation (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel.

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs filed this action on August 22, 2023, stating claims for (1) negligence, (2) premise liability, (3) unsafe conditions, and (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

Counsel filed these motions on January 10, 2024. No opposition was filed.

 

Legal Standard

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (CCP § 284(2)).  Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.         Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051));

2.         A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under CCP §284(2), is brought instead of filing a consent under CCP §284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052));

3.         Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4.         The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if withdrawal would prejudice the client.  (Rules Prof. Conduct, Rule 3-700(A)(2); Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial, 9:385.2 (2008); 1 Witkin Cal. Pro., Attorneys §72 (2008).)  The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice.  (Mandell v. Superior Court, (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.)  The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.  (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.)  A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship (or personality clash) is grounds for withdrawal.  (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client.  (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 554, 559.)   

 

Discussion

 

The motion satisfies the procedural requirements. Specifically, as it relates to notice under CRC Rule 3.1362(a), a notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-051). Here, that is complied with and the hearing date on the notices is correct (February 13, 2024). Next, under the requirement of a Declaration under CRC Rule 3.1362(c), the motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1).

The declaration in this case provides three reasons: (1) a “breakdown in attorney-client communication,” (2) failure by client to “cooperate with his counsel,” and (3) failure by client to “fulfil his financial obligations.” The reasons provided are identical on both declarations. (The declaration Form MC-052 lists the wrong hearing date.) Furthermore, under the service requirement pursuant to CRC Rule 3.1362(d) and LASC Local Rule 4.35, the notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service or mail. If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

(1)The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

(2)The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.

 

As used in this rule, "current" means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011(b) applies.

 

When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40.  (LASC Local Rules, Rule 4.35.)

 

In the current case, the declaration states that the motion was served at the client's current electronic address, which was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion. It attaches (to the Notices of Motion) an attachment purporting to prove that the electronic mailing address was confirmed on January 9, 2024, but that attachment shows an email from a Sergey Vershinin, who is not party to this case, and does not explain any relationship between Sergey Vershinin and either Plaintiff.

 

Also, as it pertains to the requirement of a proposed order and CRC Rule 3.1362(e), the proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. In this case, the hearing date on proposed orders is incorrect. The proposed orders have the date of hearing therefore as February 9, 2024, but it is actually set for February 13, 2024. The proposed orders also do not list the Case Management Conference set for February 13, 2024, though they do list the prior Case Management Conference. Trial is not yet set.

 

The court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client, and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.) Here, no trial date has been set and there is no prejudice to either side.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel is ordered to serve a copy of the order granting leave to withdraw on the client and all parties who have appeared in the action or proceeding. Counsel will be relieved upon filing proof of service on the client of the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel--Civil (Judicial Council form MC-053).

 

Plaintiff’s counsel is ordered to give notice of the ruling on the motion to be relieved. Counsel for Plaintiff is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.
