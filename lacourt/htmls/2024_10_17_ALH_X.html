DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 22AHCV00270    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: X


PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO GARNISH WAGES

Plaintiff has been unsuccessful in collecting on the judgment. Plaintiff moves to garnish Defendant’s spouse’s wages.  No opposition is filed.

The case involves former business partners with ownership of defendant limited liability company FAKK LLC which operated as a scuba dive shop business known as the Zen Dive Company. The matter went to arbitration and the hearing was conducted on March 11-15 and 18, 2024. On June 13, 2024, the arbitrator issued a Final Award. (6/25/24 Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award, Attachment 8(c).)      

Plaintiff’s motion to garnish the wages of Defendant’s spouse is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

“An earnings withholding order may not be issued against the earnings of the spouse of the judgment debtor except by court order upon noticed motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 706.109.)  “Except for an earning assignment order for support, the earnings of an employee shall not be required to be withheld by an employer for payment of a debt by means of any judicial procedure other than pursuant to this chapter.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 706.020.)  “Both the wage garnishment law and the attachment law protect wages from creditors.  The wage garnishment law provides the exclusive judicial procedure by which a judgment creditor can execute against the wages of a judgment debtor, except for cases of judgments or orders for support.  [Citation.]  It limits the amount of earnings which may be garnished in satisfaction of a judgment and establishes certain exemptions from earnings which may not be garnished.  [Citation.]”  (California State Employees’ Assn. v. State of California (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 374, 377.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 706.050 sets forth express maximum limits on the amount of disposable earnings of an individual judgment debtor that are subject to a garnishment order. 

 

Plaintiff is the Judgment Creditor and seeks an order permitting him to garnish the wages of Defendant/Judgment Debtor’s spouse, Heather Wilson.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 695.020 provides that community property is subject to enforcement of a money judgment as provided in the Family Code.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 695.020(a).)  Family Code section 910 provides that “[e]xcept as otherwise expressly provided by statute, the community estate is liable for a debt incurred by either spouse before or during marriage, regardless of which spouse has the management and control of the property and regardless of whether one or both spouses are parties to the debt or to a judgment for the debt.”  (Fam. Code, § 910(a).) 

 

Judgment Creditor presents a Petition for Dissolution of Marriage regarding Karim Hamza and Heather Wilson’s divorce proceedings.  (Cameli Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.)  According to the petition’s Statistical Facts, the date of marriage was March 25, 2010 and the date of separation was May 15, 2024.  (Cameli Decl., Exh. A, ¶ 3.)

 

Judgment was entered for Judgment Creditor on July 31, 2024 against Judgment Debtor in a total sum of $341,461.38.  (7/31/24 Judgment; Sheik Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.)  Judgment Creditor’s counsel declares Plaintiff has been unsuccessful in his attempts to collect the judgment (Sheik Decl., ¶ 3), and no amount has been paid on the judgment (Sheik Decl., ¶ 4).

 

Family Code section 903, subdivision (a) provides that in the case of a contract, a debt is incurred “at the time the contract is made” and subdivision (b) provides that in the case of a tort, a debt is incurred “at the time the tort occurs.”  (Fam. Code, § 903, subds. (a)-(b).)

 

Here, Hamza’s debt pursuant to the Judgment was incurred at the time he committed the torts, namely assault in 2021 and defamation throughout 2021-2023, which is during the time Hamza and Wilson were married.  (Cameli Decl., ¶ 11.)  Since Hamza and Wilson were married during this time, the debt becomes a community debt.

 

The Court finds this evidence demonstrates the spousal relationship between Judgment Debtor and Wilson during the time the debt was incurred, such that an earnings withholding order may be issued against Wilson’s earnings.

 

Therefore, Judgment Creditor is entitled to a court order garnishing the wages of Heather Wilson.  The Court will issue an earnings withholding order against Wilson’s earnings. 

 

Plaintiff’s motion to garnish the wages of Defendant’s spouse is GRANTED.


Case Number: 22AHCV00600    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: X


DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to the complaint’s third cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment.

 

BACKGROUND

This is a lemon law action which arises from Plaintiff’s lease of a 2020 Honda Accord.  On August 23, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant American Honda Motor Co. asserting causes of action for Violation of Song-Beverly Act and Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment.  Plaintiff alleges their vehicle had a sensing defect, which Defendant knew about and concealed.

 

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the third cause of action is DENIED.

 

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

As to each claim, defendant must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  Once defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to plaintiff to show a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection no. 1 to Defendant’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“SSUMF”) ¶ 20 is overruled.

 

Analysis

First, Honda’s memorandum exceeds the page limit at 24 pages.  Plaintiff did not previously seek leave to exceed the page limit.  “In a summary judgment or summary adjudication motion, no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 20 pages.”  (CRC, Rule 3.1113(d).)  “A memorandum that exceeds the page limits of these rules must be filed and considered in the same manner as a late-filed paper.”  (CRC, Rule 3.1113(g).)

 

Honda moves for summary adjudication on the third cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment.  Honda argues Plaintiff cannot establish a special or transactional relationship, active concealment, or fraud damages, and that the claim is barred by the economic loss doctrine.

 

The elements of a claim for fraudulent concealment are: “(1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose that fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damages as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.”  (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.)  As it is a species of fraud and deceit, such claim must be plead with specificity.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 [specificity means pleading the who, where, when, what, and how].)  But this rule for fraud by concealment, unlike fraud by an affirmative misrepresentation, must be applied differently in the case of non-disclosure because it is difficult to allege “who” or “how” or “by what means” something was not disclosed or “when” or “where” a statement was not made.  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Imp. System & Planning Ass’n, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  Because of the nature of such fraud and the fact such facts supporting concealment are more likely to be known by defendant, less particularity is required.  (Id.)

 

Concealment

Defendant contends one reason Plaintiff cannot prevail on this cause of action is because Plaintiff has failed to come forward with evidence of active concealment by Defendant which affected his decision to purchase the subject vehicle.  Defendant argues that, in order to prevail on this claim, a plaintiff must prove that, prior to the purchase of the vehicle, the manufacturer was aware of a defect that it was either unwilling or unable to fix.  (Santana v. FCA US, LLC (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 334, 344-45.)

 

In support of its position, Defendant relies on excerpts from Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s discovery requests.  (See SSUMF ¶¶ 4-7.)  Prior to purchasing the Subject Vehicle, Plaintiff did not review and rely upon any sales literature related to the Subject Vehicle.  (SSUMF ¶ 4.)  He did review the AHM internet web page to look at safety features in Honda vehicle (SSUMF ¶ 5), but those were the only web pages Plaintiff looked at related to his purchase of the Subject Vehicle (SSUMF ¶ 6).  And the only newspapers and magazines Plaintiff looked at related to his purchase of the Subject Vehicle were the classified ads in the L.A. Times.  (SSUMF ¶ 7.)

 

Factually devoid discovery responses can be an evidentiary basis in support of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.)  As Plaintiff provided responsive documents in connection with Defendant’s Requests for Production, Defendant may properly assert that the responses are factually devoid so as to meet its burden under the summary adjudication standard.  The burden therefore shifts to Plaintiff to establish a triable issue of fact.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff offers evidence from his deposition testimony that he did in fact communicate with a Honda representative.  Plaintiff submits that Defendant had exclusive knowledge of the severity of the subject vehicle’s sensing defect through numerous similar consumer complaints.  (AMF ¶ 6.)  Prior to purchasing the Subject Vehicle, no employees of Defendant, nor any representatives at the selling dealership Sierra Honda, disclosed any information to Plaintiff about the defective Sensing System.  (AMF ¶ 1; SSUMF ¶ 15.)  In purchasing the Subject Vehicle, Plaintiff’s main considerations was safety for his family and himself.  (AMF ¶ 2.)  This information was vital to Plaintiff’s purchase of the Subject Vehicle and Plaintiff relied on the information Honda produced on its website and from its sales representatives about the safety of the vehicle.  (AMF ¶ 1; UMF ¶ 26 as disputed.)  However, Plaintiff’s vehicle was distributed to him with an underlying sensing defect that Defendant was unable to repair. (AMF ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiff has presented evidence that Defendant was aware of the defects.  (AMF ¶ 6; Tran Decl., ¶ 5.)  According to paragraph 5 of the Tran Declaration:

 

On August 25, 2023, Defendant provided further responses to Plaintiff’s First Set of Production of Documents request.  In response to Request for Production No. 45, Defendant produced four-hundred and ninety-five (495) pages of internal communications with its customers regarding similar complaints that Plaintiff experienced with the Subject Vehicle.  For brevity sake, Plaintiff will only include the further responses but can and will produce the full 495 pages of confidential documents upon request of the Court.  A true and correct copy of Defendant’s August 25, 2023 further responses to Request for Production of Documents is attached hereto as Exhibit 2.

 

Further, Plaintiff demonstrates that he interacted with and received information from a sales representative at a Honda dealership prior to purchasing the subject vehicle. 

 

Plaintiff therefore meets his burden to establish that a triable issue of fact exists with respect to the issue of active concealment. 

 

Damages

Defendant also contends that Plaintiffs cannot prevail on this cause of action because Plaintiff has not established damages from an alleged fraudulent concealment.

 

The standard measure of fraud damages is the difference between the actual value of that with which the defrauded person has parted and the actual value of that which he received.  (Civ. Code § 3343.)  Costs of repair is not the proper measure of damages, but has some probative worth on the issue.  (See, e.g., Central Mut. Ins. Co. v. Schmidt (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 671, 676-77.)

 

Defendant bases its position, that Plaintiff offers no evidence of the market value of the subject vehicle, on Plaintiff’s deposition.  (See SSUMF ¶¶ 19-22.)  Plaintiff does not have any opinion as to the fair market value of the Subject Vehicle on the day he bought it.  (SSUMF ¶ 19.)  Plaintiff also does not have any opinion as to how much the Subject Vehicle has diminished in value because of Plaintiff’s warranty concerns.  (SSUMF ¶ 20.)  Plaintiff has never gone on Kelley Blue Book online to see what the value of the Subject Vehicle is.  (SSUMF ¶ 21.)  Plaintiff has not performed any valuation of the Subject Vehicle. (SSUMF ¶ 22.)

 

Defendant has offered evidence that shows that Plaintiff cannot establish damages for fraudulent concealment.  The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to establish a triable issue of fact. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff asserts he would not have purchased the vehicle had the sensing defects been disclosed.  Therefore, Plaintiff argues, the purchase price of the vehicle is a reliance damage.  Further, Plaintiff would not have insured or registered the vehicle absent Defendant’s alleged fraudulent concealment.  Thus, Plaintiff argues these are additional damages.  Specifically, Plaintiff spent $268.00 to register the subject vehicle with the Department of Motor Vehicles.  (AMF ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff incurred further expenses associated with the purchase of the Subject Vehicle including document processing charge of $80.00, $2,361.41 sales tax, $30.00 Transfer charge, $161.00 vehicle license fees, $165.00 registration fee, $8.75 California tire fees. (AMF ¶ 8.)  Plaintiff asserts he brought his vehicle in for repairs on multiple occasions, but the dealership technicians were unable to fix the issues.  (AMF ¶ 4.)  Plaintiff anticipates his expert will testify as to the diminution in value and its effects on Plaintiff’s use, value, and safety in the subject vehicle caused by the defective Sensing System. (Tran Decl., ¶ 3)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff is able to show the difference between the actual value with which he has parted and the actual value of that which he received, because he declares that he brought his vehicle in for repairs on multiple occasions.  The costs of repair have probative worth as to the issue of value.

 

Plaintiff has established a triable issue of fact as to whether Plaintiff incurred any damage as a result of the defect in his vehicle.

 

Economic Loss Rule

Defendant contends that this cause of action is barred under the economic loss doctrine. 

 

Under the economic loss rule, “[w]here a purchaser’s expectations in a sale are frustrated because the product he bought is not working properly, his remedy is in contract alone, for he has suffered only ‘economic losses.’”  (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 988.)  The economic loss rule “hinges on a distinction drawn between transactions involving the sale of goods for commercial purposes where economic expectations are protected by commercial and contract law, and those involving the sale of defective products to individual consumers who are injured in a manner which has traditionally been remedied by resort to the law of torts.”  (Id.)  Simply stated, the economic loss rule “prevents the law of contract and the law of tort from dissolving one into the other.”  (Id.)

 

The Robinson Helicopter case carved out a narrow exception to that rule, where defendant makes affirmative misrepresentations on which a plaintiff relies, leading to a claim for fraud.  The two requirements for the exception to apply are: (1) the defendant must have made affirmative misrepresentations upon which the plaintiff relied; and (2) the affirmative misrepresentations must “expose a plaintiff to liability for personal damages independent of the Plaintiff’s economic loss.”  (Robinson Helicopter, supra, 34 Cal.4th at 993.)

 

Defendant argues this very narrow exception does not apply to this case, because Honda made no affirmative misrepresentations and because Plaintiff has not suffered personal damages.

 

Plaintiff relies on Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, which extended the ruling in Robinson Helicopters to fraudulent concealment claims.  Although Dhital is currently on review by the California Supreme Court and has no precedential value, the Court finds its analysis of Robinson Helicopters persuasive.  Dhital points out that the tort claims considered by the Supreme Court in Robinson Helicopters were premised on a duty that is either “completely independent of the contract or arises from conduct which is both intentional and intended to harm.’”  (Robinson Helicopter, supra, 34 Cal.4th at 990.)  Because “that independence is present in the case of fraudulent inducement (whether it is achieved by intentional concealment or by intentional affirmative misrepresentations), because a defendant’s conduct in fraudulently inducing someone to enter a contract is separate from the defendant's later breach of the contract or warranty provisions that were agreed to,” the economic loss rule should not apply.  (Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at 841.)  Here, plaintiffs’ fraudulent inducement claim alleges presale conduct by Honda (concealment) that is distinct from Honda’s alleged subsequent conduct in breaching its warranty obligations.  Thus, the economic loss rule does not bar such claims. 

 

As a result, Defendant has failed to show that Plaintiff cannot prevail on this cause of action based on the economic loss doctrine.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the third cause of action is DENIED.

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

 

Plaintiff is the prevailing party in this case and moves for an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $28,336.00 (plus $1,925 in anticipated attorney’s fees) against Defendants.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees in the total amount of $28,721.00.

           

Analysis

 

Attorney Fees Under Civil Code Section 5975, Subdivision (c)

 

“The Davis-Stirling Act governs an action to enforce the recorded covenants and restrictions of a common interest development.  Civil Code section 5975 provides that the CC&Rs may be enforced as equitable servitudes and that in an action to enforce the governing documents, the prevailing party shall be awarded reasonable attorney’s fees and costs.”  (Almanor Lakeside Villas Owners Assn. v. Carson (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 761, 773; Civ. Code, § 5975, subd. (c).)

 

“The Davis-Stirling Act does not define ‘prevailing party’ or provide a rubric for that determination.”  (Ibid.)  Rather, California courts have found that the test for prevailing party is “a pragmatic one, namely whether a party prevailed on a practical level by achieving its main litigation objectives.”  (Ibid.)

 

Civil Code section 5975 provides: “In an action to enforce the governing documents, the prevailing party shall be awarded reasonable attorney fees and costs.”  Plaintiff prevailed at trial and was deemed the prevailing party.  Thus, Plaintiff is entitled to an award of attorney fees and costs.  Attorney fees under Civil Code section 5975 are appropriate.

 

Reasonableness of Attorney Fees Sought

“The determination of what constitutes a reasonable fee generally “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the hourly rate.”  (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 154.)

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)  “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of value of professional services rendered in his court.”  (Ibid.)  An award of attorney fees may also be based on counsel’s declarations.  (Raining v. Data Corp. v. Barrenechea (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1375.)  The court finds Defendant’s counsel’s hourly rates reasonable.

 

The attorney bears the burden of proof as to reasonableness of any fee claim.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(c)(5).)  This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered.  (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.)  “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence.  General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.”  (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 488, quoting Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys., Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.)

 

Plaintiff has substantiated its attorney fees request with the billing records of work performed.  (Moreno Decl., ¶ 8; Exh. 1.)  Plaintiff is claiming $28,336.00 in attorney fees so far, plus an anticipated $1,925.00 for 5 hours of work at $385 per hour in connection with reviewing the opposition, preparing a reply, and appearing at the hearing.

 

No opposition was filed, so the Court only awards 1 hour of anticipated work at $385 for attending the hearing of this motion.  The Court will award the requested $28,336.00 in attorney’s fees plus $385 for attending the hearing.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion and awards $28,721.00 in attorney’s fees.

Case Number: 23AHCV02482    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: X

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT

Defendants demur to the entire complaint, the latter of which alleges various violations related to the foreclosure of a property.

 

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the third cause of action and SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the first, second, fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action.

Plaintiff is to file an amended Complaint within 20 days of mailing of a notice of ruling.

Factual Allegations

Kato obtained a mortgage loan on the subject property from JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. on July 15, 2016 for $477,367, with a maturity date of August 1, 2046.

 

The deed of trust was subsequently assigned three times: from JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. to Bayview Loan Servicing, LLC on August 5, 2020, from Bayview Loan Servicing, LLC to Nationstar Mortgage, LLC on August 16, 2022, and from Nationstar Mortgage, LLC to VMRTG DEFENDANT on March 22, 2023.

 

On May 30, 2023, a notice of default and election to sell under the deed of trust was recorded, and on August 30, 2023, a notice of trustee’s sale was recorded, with the sale date set for October 5, 2023.  The subject property was Kato’s additional property, which he had intended to use as a senior assisted living home, but after his wife’s passing, he decided to rent it out.

 

Kato alleges that he did not receive any notices regarding the foreclosure, and was unaware that the loan had been transferred multiple times, still believing it was owned by JP Morgan Chase.

 

The Complaint alleges eight causes of action: violations of Civil Code Sections (1) 2923.5; (2) 2924(a)(1); (3) 2924.9; (4) wrongful foreclosure; (5) violation of Business and Professions Code 17200 et seq.; and (6) violation of Civil Code Section 3412.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant’s unopposed request for judicial notice is GRANTED as to all exhibits, Exhs. A to I. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.) The Court will take judicial notice of the recorded property documents and accepts the fact of their existence, but not the truth of their contents. (See Herrera v. Deutsche Bank Nat'l Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 137.)

 

Legal Analysis

“The function of a demurrer is to test the sufficiency of the complaint as a matter of law.”  (Holiday Matinee, Inc. v. Rambus, Inc. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1413, 1420.)  A complaint “is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550 (“Doe”)), but the plaintiff must set forth the essential facts of his or her case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint [the] defendant with the nature, source and extent” of the plaintiff’s claim.  (Doheny Park Terrace Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Truck Ins. Exchange (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1076, 1099.)

 

Legal conclusions are insufficient.  (Id., at pp. 1098–99.)  The Court “assume[s] the truth of the allegations in the complaint, but do[es] not assume the truth of contentions, deductions, or conclusions of law.”  (California Logistics, Inc. v. State of California (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 242, 247.)  A demurrer lies only for defects appearing on the face of the complaint or from matters of which the court must or may take judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40; see Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

 

1.     Civil Code § 2923.5

 

Civil Code section 2923.5 provides, in relevant part:

A mortgage servicer, mortgagee, trustee, beneficiary, or authorized agent shall not record a notice of default pursuant to Section 2924 until both of the following:

 

(A) Either 30 days after initial contact is made as required by paragraph (2) or 30 days after satisfying the due diligence requirements as described in subdivision (e).

(B) The mortgage servicer complies with paragraph (1) of subdivision (a) of Section 2924.18, if the borrower has provided a complete application as defined in subdivision (d) of Section 2924.18.

 

With regards to the “contact” required by (A) above, the mortgage servicer “shall contact the borrower in person or by telephone in order to assess the borrower’s financial situation and explore options for the borrower to avoid foreclosure.”  (Civ. Code § 2923.5(a)(2).)

 

“By its terms, section 2923.5 operates substantively on lenders.  They must do things in order to comply with the law.”  (Mabry v. Superior Court (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 208, 222.)  However, even though the law requires the mortgage servicer to do certain things, if those actions are not taken, the borrower’s remedy is extremely limited.  Under the plain language of section 2923.5, “the only remedy provided is a postponement of the [foreclosure] sale before it happens.” (Id. at 235.)

 

Here, the Complaint alleges Defendants failed to comply with the requirements of section 2923.5(a)(2) before recording a Notice of Default on the plaintiff's property on May 30, 2023.  Specifically, Plaintiff claims that Defendants failed to advise him in writing within 30 days that his Deed of Trust had been transferred or assigned to a third party, as required by 15 U.S.C. § 1641(g).  Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants failed to contact him in person or by telephone to assess his financial situation and explore options to avoid foreclosure, and failed to schedule a subsequent meeting within 14 days as mandated by section 2923.5(a)(2), before recording the Notice of Default.

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff lacks standing to invoke any statutory rights or protections under the Homeowner Bill of Rights (“HBOR”) because such protections apply only to first lien mortgages and deeds of trust against “owner-occupied residential real property”  (Dem., pp. 8-9 (citing Civ. Code, § 2924.15(a)(1)(A)).)  For these purposes, ‘owner-occupied’ means that the property is the principal residence of the borrower and is security for a loan made for personal, family, or household purposes.”  (Civ. Code, § 2924.15(a)(1)(B).)  Defendants assert Plaintiff is not the “borrower” for the purposes of HBOR, and thus lacks standing to assert HBOR claims.

 

Section 2920.5 subsection (c)(1) defines “borrower” as “any natural person who is a mortgagor or trustor and who is potentially eligible for any federal, state, or proprietary foreclosure prevention alternative program offered by, or through, his or her mortgage servicer.”  (Civ. Code, § 2920.5(c)(1).)

 

The Court notes that page 2 of RJN, Exhibit A (the Deed of Trust recorded on July 15, 2016), lists under “Definitions” that “Borrower” is David Kato and Tamara Kato.  (RJN, Exh. A.)  Thus, it appears to the Court that Plaintiff David Kato was a borrower.

 

However, the demurrer makes a strong argument regarding the purposes of the loan that Plaintiff obtained.  In the complaint, Plaintiff admits, “PLAINTIFF’s intentions were to make this a senior assisted living home to help care for about 10 seniors that needed to have stability in their living situation” and “PLAINTIFF did not reside at this address.”  (Compl., ¶¶ 16-17.)  Plaintiff’s admission demonstrates that this property was not owner-occupied and that the loan was commercial and not made for “personal, family, or household purposes.”  As such, Plaintiff lacks standing to assert HBOR claims.  The demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

2.     Civil Code § 2924(a)(1)

 

As a preliminary matter, the “owner-occupied residential real property” requirement under Civil Code section 2924.15 does not expressly apply to Civil Code section 2924.9.

 

The cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 2924(a)(1) relies entirely on the assertion that no Substitution of Trustee was recorded with the Los Angeles County Recorder office which renders the Notice of Default and Notice of Trustee’s Sale void.

 

Civil Code section 2924(a)(1) provides: “The trustee, mortgagee, or beneficiary, or any of their authorized agents shall first file for record, in the office of the recorder of each county wherein the mortgaged or trust property or some part or parcel thereof is situated, a notice of default.”

 

The Court notes that RJN, Exhibit F is a Substitution of Trustee recorded on May 15, 2023, in the official records of Los Angeles County as Document Number 20230315043.

 

Therefore, without other points or arguments regarding a violation of section 2924(a)(1), the demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

3.     Civil Code § 2924.9

 

In relevant part, Section 2924.9 requires, within five business days after recording a notice of default,” a mortgage services that offers one or more foreclosure prevention alternatives to “send a written communication to the borrower that includes all of the following information: (1) That the borrower may be evaluated for a foreclosure prevention alternative or, if applicable, foreclosure prevention alternatives. (2) Whether an application is required to be submitted by the borrower in order to be considered for a foreclosure prevention alternative. (3) The means and process by which a borrower may obtain an application for a foreclosure prevention alternative.” (Civ. Code §, 2924.9(a)(1)-(3).)

 

The complaint alleges no such written communication was sent following the May 30, 2023 recording of a Notice of Default on the property.  (Compl., ¶ 36.)

 

The demurrer argues Plaintiff is not a borrower and thus this code section is inapplicable. As explained previously, the Court deems Plaintiff to be a borrower because of his designation as a “Borrower” in the Deed of Trust. (RJN, Exh. A.)

 

Plaintiff has adequately alleged he received no communications from Defendants after the Notice of Default was recorded.  Plaintiff alleges he was “staying in his home when the Notice of Default was issued, and received no mail or messages.” (Compl., ¶ 36.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff has adequately pled a violation of section 2924.9.  The demurrer to this cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

4.     Wrongful foreclosure

 

Wrongful foreclosure “is an equitable action to set aside a foreclosure sale, or an action for damages resulting from the sale, on the basis that that the foreclosure was improper.”  (Sciarratta v. U.S. Bank Nat'l Ass'n (2016) 247 Cal. App. 4th 552, 561.)  The elements of the tort of wrongful foreclosure are as follows:

 

(1) the trustee or mortgagee caused an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale of real property pursuant to a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust; (2) the party attacking the sale...was prejudiced or harmed; and (3) in cases where the trustor or mortgagor challenges the sale, the trustor or mortgagor tendered the amount of the secured indebtedness or was excused from tendering.

 

(Id. at 561-562.)  Defendant challenges Plaintiff’s wrongful foreclosure claim on each of the three elements.

 

Defendant first argues that Plaintiff failed to plead facts sufficient to show an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale.  The complaint alleges a claim for wrongful foreclosure based on the alleged violations of HBOR.  (Compl., ¶ 41.)

 

As to prejudice, the complaint’s allegation that plaintiff “suffered prejudice or harm as a result of the wrongful foreclosure trustee sale” is insufficient to meet this element.  (Compl., ¶ 43.)

 

As to tender, Plaintiff alleged he is excused from the tender requirements.  (Compl., ¶ 44.) However, Plaintiff provides no more allegations to support this conclusory statement.

 

Because the complaint fails to allege facts demonstrating prejudice or how Plaintiff is excused from tender, the demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

5.     California Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq.

California’s unfair competition law (“UCL”) prohibits any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq.)  A claim may be brought under the UCL by a person who has suffered injury in fact and has lost money or property as a result of unfair competition.  (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17204).  To establish standing under the UCL, a plaintiff must (1) establish a loss or deprivation of money sufficient to qualify as injury in fact, i.e., economic injury, and (2) show that the economic injury was the result of, i.e., caused by, the unfair business practice that is the gravamen of the claim.  (See Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310, 337.)

 

Plaintiff alleges violations of the unfair, unlawful, and fraudulent prongs of the UCL as a result of Defendant’s alleged HBOR violations.

 

In Jenkins v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 497, 522-523, the court affirmed the dismissal of a UCL challenge to foreclosure where the plaintiff’s default caused the foreclosure.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff lacks standing to maintain a UCL claim based on the alleged unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts.  The demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

6.     Cancellation of Instruments – Civil Code § 3412

 

The sixth cause of action seeks cancellation of the recorded Notice of Default, Notice of Trustee’s Sale, and Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale.  (Compl., ¶¶ 62, 64.)

 

Under Civil Code section 3412, a court may order the cancellation of a written instrument if “there is a reasonable apprehension that if left outstanding it may cause serious injury to a person against whom it is void or voidable.”  In an action to cancel a certain instrument, “it is essential to allege the facts affecting the validity and invalidity of the instrument which is attacked.”  (Kroeker v. Hurlbert (1940) 38 Cal.App.2d 261, 266.) “Cancellation of an instrument is essentially a request for rescission of the instrument.”  (Deutsche Bank Nat’l Tr. Co. v. Pyle (2017) 13 Cal. App. 5th 513, 523.)

 

Here, the complaint alleges Plaintiff “has a reasonable belief that the Notice of Default, instrument no. 20230349457 and Notice of Trustee’s Sale, instrument no. 20230578462 and Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale are voidable or void ab initio.”  (Compl., ¶ 62.)  It also alleges Plaintiff has a “reasonable apprehension that if these voidable or void ab initio recorded written instruments is left outstanding, they may cause serious injury to” Plaintiff.  (Compl., ¶ 63.)  However, the complaint does not include any factual allegations explaining how or why the Notice of Default, Notice of Trustee’s Sale, and Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale are void or voidable. “A plaintiff must provide facts, ‘not mere conclusions, showing the apparent validity of the instrument designated, and point out the reason for asserting that it is actually invalid.”  (Santana v. BSI Fin. Servs., Inc. (S.D. Cal. 2020) 495 F.Supp.3d 926, 950 quoting Ephraim v. Metropolitan Trust Co. of California (1946) 28 Cal.2d 824, 833.)

 

Based on the above, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.
