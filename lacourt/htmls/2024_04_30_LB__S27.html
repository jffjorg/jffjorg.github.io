DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20LBCP00239    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

On 10/16/20, the Labor Commissioner commenced 20LBCP00248 by filing a petition to have the Clerk enter judgment based on the final order of the Labor Commissioner.  The same day, the Clerk entered judgment.  20LBCP00249 and 20LBCP00239 were commenced in a similar manner; notably, the parties refer to the three actions as “related,” but there is no official order deeming them related.  This order applies equally to each of the three actions on calendar today.

 

The Labor Commissioner commenced the actions by way of petition.  The petitions were filed on behalf of the real party in interest, Sarbjeet Singh.  The petitions sought to have judgment entered against the respondents, including Baljeet Bansal (00248), Balwinder Kaur (00249), and SG Nanda Agency, Inc. (00239).

On 5/10/23, the Court heard and denied Respondent, Bansal’s motion to remove encumbrances.  The motion was made on the ground that the judgment had been satisfied.  Specifically, he contended the parties settled the action, and he had made all payments per the settlement.  Singh opposed the motion, taking the position that the settlement payments were not timely, and that a provision in the settlement made clear that the underlying labor commissions findings could be reduced to a judgment in the event a settlement payment was not timely made. 

 

The Court denied the motion, finding that (a) there was no authority presented in the moving papers in support of the relief sought, and (b) Bansal had not given notice of the motion to the labor commissioner, and (c) Bansal had not provided sufficient evidence establishing that all settlement payments had been timely made.   

 

2.     Applications to Set Aside Judgments

On 4/12/24, the labor commissioner filed applications to set aside the judgments in each of the three above cases.  The applications are denied for the following reasons:

 

First and foremost, there are no points and authorities with any of the applications.  There is no notice of motion, no statement of the grounds for the relief sought, and no authority presented to support the relief sought. 

 

Second, the applications all consist solely of the Declaration of Max Norris, an attorney with the labor commissioner’s office.  Norris declares that the judgments should be set aside because it has been determined that the judgments were entered erroneously, as the parties settled the maters with the settlement fully consummated.  As noted above, whether or not settlements have been fully consummated was the subject of prior court proceedings.  Notably, Singh opposes the applications.  The moving papers are entirely devoid of evidence concerning the terms of the settlements, payments on the settlements, etc.  Absent evidence establishing that the judgments were erroneously entered, the Court cannot grant the relief requested.

 

The applications are denied.  The ruling is without prejudice to the labor commissioner’s right to file properly noticed motions, with scheduled hearing dates, and including all relevant evidence and authorities in support of the relief sought. 

 

The labor commissioner is ordered to give notice. 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.

1.     Background Facts

On 10/16/20, the Labor Commissioner commenced 20LBCP00248 by filing a petition to have the Clerk enter judgment based on the final order of the Labor Commissioner.  The same day, the Clerk entered judgment.  20LBCP00249 and 20LBCP00239 were commenced in a similar manner; notably, the parties refer to the three actions as “related,” but there is no official order deeming them related.  This order applies equally to each of the three actions on calendar today.

 

The Labor Commissioner commenced the actions by way of petition.  The petitions were filed on behalf of the real party in interest, Sarbjeet Singh.  The petitions sought to have judgment entered against the respondents, including Baljeet Bansal (00248), Balwinder Kaur (00249), and SG Nanda Agency, Inc. (00239).

On 5/10/23, the Court heard and denied Respondent, Bansal’s motion to remove encumbrances.  The motion was made on the ground that the judgment had been satisfied.  Specifically, he contended the parties settled the action, and he had made all payments per the settlement.  Singh opposed the motion, taking the position that the settlement payments were not timely, and that a provision in the settlement made clear that the underlying labor commissions findings could be reduced to a judgment in the event a settlement payment was not timely made. 

 

The Court denied the motion, finding that (a) there was no authority presented in the moving papers in support of the relief sought, and (b) Bansal had not given notice of the motion to the labor commissioner, and (c) Bansal had not provided sufficient evidence establishing that all settlement payments had been timely made.   

 

2.     Applications to Set Aside Judgments

On 4/12/24, the labor commissioner filed applications to set aside the judgments in each of the three above cases.  The applications are denied for the following reasons:

 

First and foremost, there are no points and authorities with any of the applications.  There is no notice of motion, no statement of the grounds for the relief sought, and no authority presented to support the relief sought. 

 

Second, the applications all consist solely of the Declaration of Max Norris, an attorney with the labor commissioner’s office.  Norris declares that the judgments should be set aside because it has been determined that the judgments were entered erroneously, as the parties settled the maters with the settlement fully consummated.  As noted above, whether or not settlements have been fully consummated was the subject of prior court proceedings.  Notably, Singh opposes the applications.  The moving papers are entirely devoid of evidence concerning the terms of the settlements, payments on the settlements, etc.  Absent evidence establishing that the judgments were erroneously entered, the Court cannot grant the relief requested.

 

The applications are denied.  The ruling is without prejudice to the labor commissioner’s right to file properly noticed motions, with scheduled hearing dates, and including all relevant evidence and authorities in support of the relief sought. 

 

The labor commissioner is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV00207    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Michelle Sampson filed this action against Defendant, Theresa Gereaux for libel, slander, and defamation arising out of statements made during the course of the parties’ employment-based relationship.  Plaintiff filed her complaint on 2/03/23.  Defendant filed an answer on 3/16/23.

 

2.     Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

a.     Initial Note

Defendant filed this motion on 1/26/24, setting it for hearing on 8/01/24.  On 2/16/24, at Defendant’s request, the Court advanced the motion for hearing on 4/30/24.  Defendant gave notice of the ruling on 2/20/24.  To date, Plaintiff has not filed opposition to the motion.  The Court will not consider untimely opposition to the motion. 

b.     Standard on Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings 

CCP §438 states, in relevant part:  “(b)(1) A party may move for judgment on the pleadings. . . . (c)(1) The motion provided for in this section may only be made on one of the following grounds:  (A) If the moving party is a plaintiff, that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.  (B) If the moving party is a defendant, that either of the following conditions exist: . . . . (ii) The complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” 

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings “has the purpose and effect of a general demurrer.”  (Smiley v. Citibank (S.D.), N.A. (1995) 11 Cal. 4th 138, 146 (citation omitted)).  “[T]he trial court generally confines itself to the complaint and accepts as true all material facts alleged therein.  As appropriate, however, it may extend its consideration to matters that are subject to judicial notice.  In this, it performs essentially the same task that it would undertake in ruling on a general demurrer.” (Id. (citations omitted)).

 

c.     Meet and Confer

CCP §439 requires a party moving for judgment on the pleadings to meet and confer prior to filing the motion.  Defense Counsel declares she sent two meet and confer letters to Plaintiff, both of which asked Plaintiff to contact her to set up a time to meet and confer telephonically.  Plaintiff did not respond to either letter.  Defendant therefore satisfied the meet and confer requirement prior to filing the motion. 

 

d.     Analysis

Plaintiff, at ¶7 of her complaint, indicates she is suing for defamation, libel (sic), and slander. 

 

Defamation is an invasion of the interest in reputation. It may be libel (written) or slander (spoken).  The tort involves (a) a publication that is (b) false, (c) defamatory, and (d) unprivileged, and that (e) has a natural tendency to injure or that causes special damage.  (Civil Code §§45, 46; Rest.2d, Torts §§558, 559; see Smith v. Maldonado (1999) 72 Cal.A.4th 637, 645.) 

 

Plaintiff, at ¶4 of her complaint, provides details concerning a substitute teaching assignment she was hired for at West High School.  Notably, nothing in ¶4 indicates Defendant said or wrote anything about Plaintiff in connection with the assignment.  At ¶5, Plaintiff alleges, “Defendant has written falsehoods and told lies in an effort to get Plaintiff fired.”  Plaintiff fails, however, to allege what Defendant said, what Defendant wrote, to whom the statements were made, or any other details about the statements. 

 

Absent details about the alleged publication(s), the complaint fails to state a cause of action.  The motion for judgment on the pleadings is therefore granted. 

 

e.     Leave to Amend

It is not up to the Court to figure out how the complaint can be amended to state a cause of action.  Rather, the burden is on the plaintiff to show in what manner he or she can amend the complaint, and how that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading.  Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349; Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.

 

A plaintiff may not amend as a matter of right, and in order to be in a position to complain upon appeal of an abuse of discretion in sustaining a demurrer without leave to amend, he must ask permission to amend, unless it is made to appear that he had no opportunity to ask such permission.  Williams v. Hinckley (1930) 109 Cal.App. 574, 576.

 

In light of the lack of opposition, Plaintiff has necessarily failed to show how she would amend if given leave to do so.  The motion is therefore granted without leave to amend.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV00974    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jesus and Diana Renteria filed this action against Defendants, Pinner Construction Co., Inc.., et al. on 6/06/23.  Plaintiffs’ operative complaint is their First Amended Complaint, which they filed before any pleading challenge was heard, on 6/09/23.  The FAC includes causes of action for general negligence, premises liability, and loss of consortium.  The FAC alleges the defendants, and each of them, provided negligent drawings, plans, specifications, safety instructions, and designs for Plaintiff to use in constructing a building.  As a result, the building Plaintiff was working on collapsed, causing him to sustain grave injuries.  His wife sues for loss of consortium. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel

Plaintiffs propounded RPDs, FROGs, and SROGs on Defendant, Kern Steel Fabrication, Inc. on 8/04/23.  Defendant obtained extensions of time to respond, and ultimately served unverified responses to SROGs and RPDs on 12/11/23.  Defendant had not, as of the filing of the moving papers, served responses to FROGs.

 

Plaintiffs seek an order compelling Defendant to serve responses to FROGs, without objections, and to serve verifications to the RPDs and SROGs.  Defendant, in opposition to the motion, indicates it provided responses to FROGs with the opposition and will provide verifications prior to the due date for the reply.  Plaintiffs, in reply, indicate they have still not received verifications.  To the extent the request for verifications is not rendered moot prior to the hearing, the motion is granted.  CCP §§2030.290(a), (b), 2031.300(a), (b).  Defendant must serve verifications within ten days.

 

Plaintiffs also seek an order compelling Defendant to produce documents responsive to RPDs 10 and 15.  Defendant, in its unverified response to these RPDs, indicated it would produce all responsive documents.  Plaintiffs contend it failed, however, to do so.  Defendant, in opposition, contends it provided all responsive documents, but Plaintiffs, in reply, contend Defendant did not do so.  To the extent the documents have not been provided, the motion to compel is also granted.  CCP §2031.320(a).  Defendant must produce responsive documents within ten days.

 

Plaintiffs seek imposition of monetary sanctions.  They seek sanctions in the amount of $1061.65 against Defendant and its attorney of record to compensate for attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in making this motion.  The request is both reasonable and fully supported, and is granted in full.  CCP §§2030.290(c), 2031.300(c), 2031.320(b). 

 

Plaintiffs also seek imposition of a $1000 penalty against Defense Counsel per CCP §2023.050(a), which provides:

Notwithstanding any other law, and in addition to any other sanctions imposed pursuant to this chapter, a court shall impose a one-thousand-dollar ($1,000) sanction, payable to the requesting party, upon a party, person, or attorney if, upon reviewing a request for a sanction made pursuant to Section 2023.040, the court finds any of the following:

(1) The party, person, or attorney did not respond in good faith to a request for the production of documents made pursuant to Section 2020.010, 2020.410, 2020.510, or 2025.210, or to an inspection demand made pursuant to Section 2031.010.

(2) The party, person, or attorney produced requested documents within seven days before the court was scheduled to hear a motion to compel production of the records pursuant to Section 2025.450, 2025.480, or 2031.320 that is filed by the requesting party as a result of the other party's, person's, or attorney's failure to respond in good faith.

(3) The party, person, or attorney failed to confer in person, by telephone, letter, or other means of communication in writing, as defined in Section 250 of the Evidence Code, with the party or attorney requesting the documents in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning the request.

 

Plaintiff contends Defense Counsel is subject to this sanction because verifications were promised but not provided, and also because responsive documents were promised but not provided.  Defendant, in opposition to the motion, contends additional sanctions should not be awarded because there were no false or misleading statements made.  The standard above, however, is simply that the party failed to act in good faith to provide responses to an inspection demand.  Plaintiffs made the required showing in the moving papers, and Defendant did not meaningfully respond to the showing in opposition, instead basically conceding the failure to timely respond and failing to provide any justification for the failure.  The request for this sanction is therefore also granted. 

 

Defendant and its attorney of record, jointly and severally, must pay sanctions to Plaintiffs, by and through their attorney of record, in the total amount of $1061.65, within twenty days.  Defense Counsel must pay an additional sanction of $1000 to Plaintiffs, by and through their attorney of record, also within twenty days. 

 

3.     Motion for Leave to Amend

Plaintiffs reserved a hearing on a motion for leave to amend for 4/30/24.  It appears the motion is moot, as Plaintiffs filed their operative Third Amended Complaint, pursuant to stipulation and order, on 4/04/24.  The Court therefore takes the hearing on the motion for leave to amend off calendar.  

 

4.     OSC and CMC

Case Number: 23LBCV01474    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Carlos Freddis Peraza filed this action against Defendant, Total Terminals International, LLC for damages arising out of an automobile accident that occurred on Defendant’s property. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFAs

Plaintiff propounded RFAs on Defendant and Defendant responded.  The parties met and conferred, and Defendant served supplemental responses.  Plaintiff, believing the responses to remain deficient, filed this motion to compel further responses. 

 

The RFAs that are at issue on this motion are all variations on the same theme – each RFA asks Defendant to admit that Plaintiff suffered a certain type of injury or damage as a result of the subject collision.  Defendant, in its initial responses, objected on the grounds that the RFAs called for expert medical opinion, sought work product information, and was premature.  Defendant then went on to state that it could neither admit nor deny the requests due to the status of discovery.  Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter, and the letter contended the expert and work product objections lacked merit.  Defendant, after receiving the letter, served supplemental responses.  The supplemental responses omitted the expert and work product objections, but continued to state the premature objection, and continued to substantively indicate the responses could be neither admitted nor denied due to the status of discovery. 

 

Plaintiff filed this motion without meeting and conferring after receiving the supplemental responses.  Defendant contends the motion should be denied due to failure to meet and confer.  Plaintiff, in reply, contends further meet and confer was not necessary and would not have resolved the issues.  Plaintiff contends he met and conferred after receiving the initial responses, and the supplemental responses continued to include meritless objections, such that further meet and confer was not necessary.  The problem with Plaintiff’s position is that Plaintiff’s meet and confer correspondence, sent after receipt of the original responses, did not mention the “premature” objection or the substantive responses at all.  The meet and confer letter mentioned only the expert and attorney work product objections.  Defendant, therefore, had no reason to anticipate Plaintiff would take issue with the supplemental responses, which omitted the only two objections Plaintiff had addressed in the meet and confer correspondence. 

 

CCP §2033.290(b)(1) requires parties to meet and confer prior to filing a motion to compel further responses to RFAs.  Because Plaintiff failed to meet and confer on the issue that forms the basis of this motion, the motion is denied.

 

The Court notes that Defendant does not seek imposition of sanctions with the opposition papers, and therefore no sanctions are imposed.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
