DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV30451    Hearing Date: June 10, 2024    Dept: 39

Background

On September 16, 2022, Plaintiff Marta Lopez filed her initial complaint against

her former employer, SCPMG (Defendant), and non-employers Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (“KFH”) and Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (“KFHP”) (collectively, Defendants).

 

In the initial complaint, plaintiff alleged causes of action for wrongful termination in violation of public policy, age discrimination, race discrimination, and failure to prevent discrimination. All four causes of action stemmed from allegations that plaintiff’s former manager concocted false reasons to terminate plaintiff’s employment so that Plaintiff could be replaced with “a younger, full time social worker for significantly less [pay] than what plaintiff was earning…” (Complaint, ¶ 25.) After the Defendants filed a demurrer and motion to strike, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint on November 18, 2022.

 

On March 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for leave to amend to file Second Amended Complaint. On May 28, 2024, Defendant filed the opposition. On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed her reply.

 

Judicial Notice

Defendant seeks judicial notice for the following documents:

·       Plaintiff’s Complaint filed in this case on September 16, 2022

·       Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint filed in this case on November 18, 2022

·       Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint, filed in this case on December 16, 2022

·       Minute Order dated February 7, 2023 in this case

·       SCPMG’s Motion for Summary Judgment Or In The Alternative, Summary Adjudication of Issues filed in this case on January 10, 2024

·       Minute Order dated March 8, 2024 in this case

·       Minute Order dated April in this case

·       Exhibit K to SCPMG’s Motion for Summary Judgment

The copies of documents provided by Defendants are judicially noticeable under Evidence Code sections 451 and 452. The court takes judicial notice of the specified documents, though not necessarily the facts stated therein.

 

Legal Standard

“A court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) Such amendment may occur “at any time before or after commencement of trial.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) The court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendments of the pleadings. (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.)¿ “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)[1] The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Rule 3.1324(b).)¿

Discussion

A.              Merits

Plaintiff moves for leave to file a Second Amended Complaint to add new causes of action for Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code §1102.5 and Retaliation in Violation of Health & Safety Code §1278.5. Plaintiff also includes the changes to the factual allegations.

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s motion does not comply with Rule 3.1324. Defendant also argues Plaintiff unreasonably delayed in seeking leave to amend, and Plaintiff’s new cause of action for retaliation is based on facts Plaintiff knew from the outset of the case. Moreover, Defendant asserts the proposed amendment to include a new cause of action for retaliation would be futile because Plaintiff failed to allege sufficient facts to state a valid claim in any event. Additionally, Plaintiff’s attempt to reinsert two non-employer defendants in defiance of the trial Court’s order dismissing those non-employer defendants from the case must be rejected. Defendant adds it would be prejudicial to grant the motion while a motion for summary judgment is scheduled to be heard.

As an initial matter, the court notes Plaintiff satisfied the requirements of Rule 3.1324 in this instance. (See Mot. at p. 5; Cervantes Anderson Decl., ¶¶3,11,12, and 14.) If the party seeking the amendment has needlessly delayed, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.¿ (See Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)¿ Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.¿(Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿¿

The court finds Plaintiff has not needlessly delayed the amendment because she explains she discovered the facts upon which the amendment is based during a February 2024 deposition. (Cervantes Anderson Decl., ¶¶ 10, 11.) Given the discovery issues the parties have chronicled in this litigation, Plaintiff’s representations in this regard are not unreasonable.  Plaintiff’s counsel also notes she was her ill mother’s caretaker which resulted in the late depositions. Plaintiff filed the instant motion on March 7, 2024.  Therefore, the amendment was not needlessly delayed. Moreover, even if there were a delay, Defendant failed to provide evidence it would be prejudiced by Plaintiff being permitted to amend the complaint at this juncture of the litigation.  For instance, Defendant only states it will be forced to reopen Plaintiff’s deposition, take additional discovery, and be denied the time to seek resolution through a motion for summary judgment. Admittedly, allowing Plaintiff to file an amended complaint could impact the case as Defendant suggests. However, at this point in the litigation, that does not overcome providing Plaintiff the opportunity to conform her complaint in keeping with discovery which is still open.

Conclusion

Therefore, the court grants Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend First Amended Complaint. Plaintiff is ordered to file the Second Amended Complaint within 10 days of this Order and to give notice of the court’s ruling.

MOTION

Defendant Chris Tolles (“Defendant”) moves for judgment on the pleadings on the complaint filed by Plaintiff Virginia Hoge (“Plaintiff”).  Defendant Google, LLC removed the action to federal court.  The United States District Court dismissed Plaintiff’s claims to the extent Plaintiff premised them on federal law, and remanded Plaintiff’s remaining claims for cyber spying and cyber harassment based on California law.  (See Notice of Remand, filed February 26, 2024.)

 

ANALYSIS

A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer, but may be made after the time to demur has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).)  “Like a demurrer, the grounds for the motion [for judgment on the pleadings] must appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Civic Partners Stockton, LLC v. Youssefi (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1005, 1013.)  In ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “[a]ll allegations in the complaint and matters upon which judicial notice may be taken are assumed to be true.” (Rippon v. Bowen (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1313.)

Plaintiff alleges, “Beginning in 2010 the Plaintiff began to be stalked, harassed and spied on online by trolls - hired abusers who post anonymously - connected with [Defendant], CEO of Topix.com.  This crime was facilitated and supported by Eric Schmidt, CEO of Google.”  (Complaint, p. 2.)  Plaintiff further alleges Defendant “hired the trolls to attack the Plaintiff.”  (Ibid.)  These are the only allegations in Plaintiff’s complaint pertaining to any liability on Defendant’s part. 

While Plaintiff asserts multiple causes of action in her complaint, Plaintiff fails to indicate which causes of action Plaintiff asserts against Defendant.  In a complaint, “[e]ach separately stated cause of action . . . must specifically state: [¶] (1) Its number (e.g., “first cause of action”); [¶] (2) Its nature (e.g., “for fraud”); [¶] (3) The party asserting it if more than one party is represented on the pleading (e.g., “by plaintiff Jones”); and [¶] (4) The party or parties to whom it is directed (e.g., “against defendant Smith”).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.112.)  As Plaintiff fails to specify which causes of action Plaintiff asserts against Defendant and which allegations about Defendant Plaintiff claims support those causes of action, Plaintiff’s complaint is uncertain.  Thus, the court grants Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings but provides Plaintiff an opportunity to amend.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings on Plaintiff’s complaint is granted with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 10 days of the notice of this order.  Defendant is ordered to provide such notice and to file proof of service thereof.

Defendant Google LLC (“Defendant”) demurs to the complaint filed by Plaintiff Virginia Hoge (“Plaintiff”).  Defendant previously removed the action to federal court.  The United States District Court dismissed Plaintiff’s claims to the extent Plaintiff premised them on federal law, and remanded Plaintiff’s remaining claims for cyber spying and cyber harassment based on California law.  (See Notice of Remand, filed February 26, 2024.)

 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Plaintiff alleges, “Beginning in 2010 the Plaintiff began to be stalked, harassed, and spied on online by trolls - hired abusers who post anonymously - connected with Chris Tolles, CEO of Topix.com.  This crime was facilitated and supported by Eric Schmidt, CEO of Google.”  (Complaint, p. 2.)  These are the only allegations in Plaintiff’s complaint pertaining to liability on Defendant’s part.  While Plaintiff asserts multiple causes of action in her complaint, Plaintiff fails to indicate which causes of action Plaintiff asserts against Defendant.  In a complaint, “[e]ach separately stated cause of action . . . must specifically state: [¶] (1) Its number (e.g., “first cause of action”); [¶] (2) Its nature (e.g., “for fraud”); [¶] (3) The party asserting it if more than one party is represented on the pleading (e.g., “by plaintiff Jones”); and [¶] (4) The party or parties to whom it is directed (e.g., “against defendant Smith”).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.112.)  As Plaintiff fails to specify which causes of action Plaintiff asserts against Defendant and which allegations about Defendant Plaintiff claims support those causes of action, Plaintiff’s complaint is uncertain.  The demurrer to the complaint is, therefore, sustained, but Plaintiff is given the opportunity to amend the complaint.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 10 days of the notice of this order.  Defendant is ordered to provide such notice and to file proof of service thereof.
