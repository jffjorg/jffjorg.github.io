DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV19261    Hearing Date: January 3, 2025    Dept: 73

1/03/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

AKLYAN, et al. v. GALPIN JAGUAR LINCOLN-MERCURY, INC., et al. (23STCV19261)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs:  Iman Alamdari (Shegerian & Associates, Inc.)

Counsel for Defendant Ford: Sarah Nakamoto (Snell & Wilmer LLP)

 

DEFENDANT FORD’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFFS’ FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORD’S FIRST SET OF FORM INTERROGATORIES, SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, AND REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed on 05/01/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Ford’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan and Yelena Malkhasyan’s further responses to (1) Form Interrogatories, Set One, (2) Special Interrogatories, Set One, and (3) Requests for Production, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan and Yelena Malkhasyan shall provide verified responses, without objections, within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant Ford’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiffs in the reduced amount of $3,842.50. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant Ford within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 11, 2023, Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan, individually and as successor-in-interest to Arthur Aklyan, deceased; and Yelena Malkhasyan (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed this action. On January 24, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint against Defendants Galpin Jaguar Lincoln-Mercury, Inc. d/b/a Galpin Premier Collection; Ford Motor Company (“Ford”); the State of California; and California Department of Transportation.

 

The FAC alleges the following. On August 28, 2022, Arthur Aklyan was involved in a fatal collision while driving northbound on the I-5 highway, south of Sheldon Street, Sun Valley, California 91352, in a 2018 Lincoln MKZ, California License Plate No. 8EQC405, Vehicle Identification No. 3LN6L5E90JR615845 (the “SUBJECT MKZ”). The Subject MKZ contained inherent defects in design and manufacturing. Arthur Aklyan faced an emergency braking situation, which resulted in a collision, while using the Subject MKZ and each of its component parts as intended or in a reasonably foreseeable manner. However, had the Subject MKZ’s ruptured and/or structurally weak front brake jounce hoses and loose steering wheel retaining bolt been repaired, the collision would have been prevented or its severity lessened. Plaintiffs assert the following causes of action:

1.      Strict Liability/Product Defect

2.      Negligence/Product Defect

3.      Dangerous Condition of Public Property

 

On May 1, 2024, Defendant Ford filed the Motion to Compel Plaintiffs’ Further Responses to Ford’s First Set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production, and request for sanctions. No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

 

Here, Defendant Ford’s counsel declares that she met and conferred telephonically and via correspondence with Plaintiffs’ counsel regarding the allegedly deficient responses. (Nakamoto Decl., ¶ 5-24.) The Court finds that counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) 

 

It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

 

Furthermore, to the extent there is any doubt in whether these records should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) 

 

C.      Discussion

 

Defendant Ford moves to compel Plaintiffs’ further responses to Ford’s first set of discovery: (1) requests for production; (2) special interrogatories; and (3) form interrogatories, general.

 

On September 18, 2023, Defendant Ford propounded its first set of discovery upon Plaintiffs. (Alamdari Decl., ¶ 3, Exhs. 2-7.) On October 5, 2023, Ford granted Plaintiffs a 60-day extension of time to respond to its first sets of written discovery, making Plaintiffs’ responses due on December 19, 2023. (Nakamoto Decl., ¶ 4.) On or about December 21, 2023, Ford sent Plaintiffs a letter stating that it had not received Plaintiffs’ responses, that Plaintiffs waived its objections, and that Plaintiff was to provide verified responses, without objections, on or before January 5, 2024. (Alamdari Decl., ¶ 4.) The parties met and conferred, and Defendant Ford provided an extension until January 26, 2024 for Plaintiffs to provide responses. (Alamdari Decl., ¶ 5.) On January 26, 2024, Plaintiffs served objection-only responses to all Defendants to preserve its objections. Plaintiffs provided notice that it would work to provide substantive responses by February 12, 2024. (Alamdari Decl., ¶ 6.) On February 23, 2024, Plaintiffs served further responses to Ford’s written discovery, with objections. (Alamdari Decl., ¶ 6.) On April 22, 2024, Plaintiffs served their document production to Ford’s first sets of requests for production. On May 1, 2024, Ford filed its instant motion to compel Plaintiffs to provide further responses to its first sets of form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for production, without objections, and associated requests for sanctions.

 

Ford asserts that further responses are required as Plaintiffs waived all objections to Ford’s first sets of written discovery by failing to serve timely responses. Thus, per Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.290(a) and 2031.300(a), Plaintiffs waived all objections to first sets of written discovery, including those based on privilege and work product. On July 11, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ Motion for Relief from Effect of Late Served Response.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiffs have failed to oppose the motion. If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) Therefore, Plaintiffs have not met their burden of justifying any of their objections.

 

Further, “a party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion…is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.” (Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.” (Id.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Ford’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan and Yelena Malkhasyan’s further responses to (1) Form Interrogatories, Set One, (2) Special Interrogatories, Set One, and (3) Requests for Production, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan and Yelena Malkhasyan shall provide verified responses, without objections, within 30 days. 

 

D.     Sanctions

 

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.) 

 

Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, subd. (a).) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, subd. (d).) Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 provides that a court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery even if no opposition was filed.  

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiffs engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to provide code compliant responses to Defendant Ford’s discovery requests. Ford requests $5,565 in sanctions for counsel’s work on this motion, including 14.5 hours spent on the instant motion, and an anticipated 6.5 additional hours to review the opposition, prepare a reply and appear at the hearing, at the rate of $265 an hour. The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced total amount of $3,842.50 (14.5 hours of attorney time at the rate of $265 an hour).

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant Ford’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan and Yelena Malkhasyan’s further responses to (1) Form Interrogatories, Set One, (2) Special Interrogatories, Set One, and (3) Requests for Production, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiffs Garen Aklyan and Yelena Malkhasyan shall provide verified responses, without objections, within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant Ford’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiffs in the reduced amount of $3,842.50. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant Ford within 30 days of the date of this order.

MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH RULE 2.257 OF THE CALIFORNIA RULES OF COURT (filed by Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc. on 10/17/2024)

Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Rule 2.257 of the California Rules of Court is GRANTED.

2.      Violation of California Homeowner Bill of Rights

3.      Violation of Foreclosure Consultancy Laws

The FAC alleges the following. Defendants consist of individuals and entities in the mortgage and hard money lending business who conspired with each other to make high-interest and high-fee loans to Plaintiff - loans that they knew and intended that Plaintiff could not pay back so that they could seize the Plaintiff’s property located at 1095 South Genesee Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90019 (“Property”) for a small fraction of its value. ·       Plaintiff’s Complaint filed September 14, 2023, his First Amended Complaint filed May 30, 2024, and his Objection to Declaration of Non-Monetary Status filed on October 15, 2024 fail to comply with Rule 2.257.

·       Plaintiff’s “/s/” signature is not compliant with Rule 2.257(b)(1) because it is not “unique to the declarant, capable of verification, under the sole control of the declarant, and linked to data in such a manner that if the data are changed, the electronic signature is invalidated.”

 

On December 4, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc.’s Motion to allow Plaintiff to file an opposition.

 

On December 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Prior to the filing of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint, Plaintiff reviewed the Complaint and First Amended Complaint. Plaintiff’s signature in the verification was inserted by Plaintiff’s attorney, Tariq S. Hasan.

·       The Plaintiff has not signed a printed form of the document (Complaint and First Amended Complaint) and, therefore, does not have a physically signed verification of the Complaint or the First Amended Complaint to provide to Defendant. (Decl. Hasan. ¶ 4.)

·       Plaintiff states he is willing to physically sign a new verification for the Complaint and First Amended Complaint and file these new physically signed verifications with the Court. (Decl. Hasan. ¶ 6.).

 

On December 27, 2024, Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc. filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s opposition admits that Plaintiff did not comply with Rule 2.257 with respect to the verifications accompanying the Complaint and First Amended Complaint in this matter.

·       The Court may “at any time in its discretion…[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” CCP § 436. An unverified quiet title complaint (and the instant pleading seeks to quiet title) is not drawn in conformity with the laws of this state of the rules of court and is therefore subject to being stricken. (CCP § 761.020.)

·       Defendant submits that because Plaintiff has failed to comply with (and cannot comply with) Rule 2.257, the First Amended Complaint should therefore be stricken on the Court’s own motion.

 

II. DISCUSSION

Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc. (“FCI”) moves this Court for and order compelling Plaintiff’s compliance with Rule 2.257 of the California Rules of Court. FCI asserts that Plaintiff’s Complaint filed September 14, 2023, his First Amended Complaint filed May 30, 2024, and his Objection to Declaration of Non-Monetary Status filed on October 15, 2024 fail to comply with Rule 2.257.

Rule 2.257(b) provides in relevant part that:

When a document to be filed electronically provides for a signature under penalty of perjury of any person, the document is deemed to have been signed by that person if filed electronically provided that either of the following conditions is satisfied:

(1) The declarant has signed the document using an electronic signature and declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the state of California that the information submitted is true and correct. If the declarant is not the electronic filer, the electronic signature must be unique to the declarant, capable of verification, under the sole control of the declarant, and linked to data in such a manner that if the data are changed, the electronic signature is invalidated; or

(2) The declarant, before filing, has physically signed a printed form of the document. By electronically filing the document, the electronic filer certifies that the original, signed document is available for inspection and copying at the request of the court or any other party.

FCI asserts that Plaintiff’s Complaint filed September 14, 2023, his First Amended Complaint filed May 30, 2024, and his Objection to Declaration of Non-Monetary Status were filed by Plaintiff’s attorney. Plaintiff’s signatures on these documents consist of the symbols “/s/ Daniel A. Bond.” (Exs. 1-3.) However, Plaintiff’s “/s/” signature is not compliant with Rule 2.257(b)(1) because it is not “unique to the declarant, capable of verification, under the sole control of the declarant, and linked to data in such a manner that if the data are changed, the electronic signature is invalidated.” FCI contends that Plaintiff’s counsel represented that Plaintiff had never signed a printed form of the documents. (Cronin Decl., ¶ 6.) Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff has failed to comply with Rule 2.257.

In Plaintiff’s opposition, he states that prior to the filing of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint, Plaintiff reviewed the Complaint and First Amended Complaint and Plaintiff’s signature in the verification was inserted by Plaintiff’s attorney, Tariq S. Hasan. (Decl. Hasan. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff also contends he has not signed a printed form of the document (Complaint and First Amended Complaint) and, therefore, does not have a physically signed verification of the Complaint or the First Amended Complaint to provide to Defendant. (Decl. Hasan. ¶ 4.)

 

Therefore, Plaintiff failed to comply with Rule 2.257 with respect to the verifications accompanying the Complaint and First Amended Complaint in this matter.

 

Accordingly, Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Rule 2.257 of the California Rules of Court is GRANTED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant FCI Lender Services, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Rule 2.257 of the California Rules of Court is GRANTED.

(2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 843-44, “Dhital”, review granted Feb. 1, 2023, 523 P.3d 392. On Dec. 18, 2024, the California Supreme Court dismissed the pending appeal in Dhital v. Nissan N. Am., Inc. (S277568).

o   Plaintiff has alleged (1) misrepresentation/concealment (Complaint, ¶¶ 22, 58), (2) knowledge of falsity (Id., ¶¶ 21, 60a), (3) intent to induce reliance (Id., ¶¶ 61-62), (4) justifiable reliance (Id., ¶¶ 26, 62-63), and (5) damages (Id., ¶¶ 16, 36, 64-65).

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant sent an email to Plaintiff’s counsel on October 21, 2024, regarding the issues raised in the demurrer, requesting a telephonic conference, and requesting an extension of time to file a responsive pleading. (Brown Decl., ¶ 3.) Defendant’s counsel states Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (Brown Decl., ¶ 4.) While the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, failure to meet and confer is not a proper ground for overruling a demurrer. (CCP § 430.41(a)(4).) Thus, the Court continues on the merits of the motion. 

 

C.      Third Cause of Action: Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(A)(3)

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for failure to allege sufficient facts to constitute a valid cause of action.

 

Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.2 reads, in relevant part: 

 

Every manufacturer of consumer goods sold in this state and for which the manufacturer has made an express warranty shall: 

 

[. . .] 

 

Make available to authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period. 

 

(Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.2(a)(3).) 

 

The Complaint states, in relevant part:

 

“In violation of Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3), Defendant FCA failed to make available to its authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period. Plaintiff has been damaged by Defendant FCA's failure to comply with its obligations pursuant to Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3), and therefore brings this Cause of Action pursuant to Civil Code section 1794.

 

Defendant FCA's failure to comply with its obligations under Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3) was willful, in that Defendant FCA knew of its obligation to provide literature and replacement parts sufficient to allow its repair facilities to effect repairs during the warranty period, yet Defendant FCA failed to take any action to correct its failure to comply with the law. Accordingly, Plaintiff is entitled to a civil penalty of two times Plaintiff's actual damages, pursuant to Civil Code section 1794(c).”

 

(Compl., ¶¶ 50-51.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not alleged facts related to the Subject Vehicle’s repair history other than, “Although Plaintiff presented the Vehicle to Defendant FCA's representative in this state, Defendant FCA and its representative failed to commence the service or repairs within a reasonable time and failed to service or repair the Vehicle so as to conform to the applicable warranties within 30 days, in violation of Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (b). Plaintiff did not extend the time for completion of repairs beyond the 30-day requirement.” (Compl., ¶ 45.) The Third Cause of Action contains only conclusory allegations with virtually no facts at all to support the claims alleged. (See Gutierrez v. Carmax Auto Superstores California (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 1234, 1247-1248). As a result, there is no factual basis alleged for Plaintiff's claim that he was "damaged by Defendant's failure to comply with its obligations pursuant to Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3)." (Compl., ¶ 50.) 

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. 

 

D.     Fifth Cause of Action: Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment

 

Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action for failure to meet the heightened pleading standard for fraud and failure to adequately state a cause of action.

 

“[T]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact; (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff; (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.)¿ 

 

Ordinarily, ”fraud must be pleaded specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.). “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, where, to whom, and by what means” the alleged fraud occurred. (Id.) The purpose of the particularity requirement is to “separate meritorious and nonmeritorious cases, if possible in advance of trial.” (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.)¿¿¿¿ 

 

Some cases, however, conclude that this standard is less stringent when the defendant already has “ ‘full information concerning the facts of the controversy.’ ” (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 217, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in Californians for Disability Rights v. Mervyn's, LLC (2006) 39 Cal.4th 223, 227.) Relaxation of the specificity requirement is particularly appropriate in a¿concealment¿case.¿ Unlike intentional misrepresentation, which requires some affirmative representation or promise, a fraudulent¿concealment¿is the absence of something, the suppression of a fact. (Civ. Code, section 1710.)¿¿ 

 

The courts have ruled that less specificity as to fraud claims is required if it appears from the nature of allegations that defendant must necessarily possess full information, or if the facts lie more in the knowledge of opposing parties. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384-1385 [“it does not appear necessary to require each of the 38 plaintiffs to allege each occasion on which an agent of either defendant could have disclosed …. Surely defendants have records of their dealings with the plaintiffs”] accord Bushell v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 915, 931 [“plaintiffs did not have to specify the … personnel who prepared these documents because that information is uniquely within … [defendant’s] knowledge”].) “‘[T]he courts should not ... seek to absolve the defendant from liability on highly technical requirements of form in pleading. Pleading facts in ordinary and concise language is as permissible in fraud cases as in any others, and liberal construction of the pleading is as much a duty of the court in these as in other cases.’” (Appollo Capital Fund, LLC v. Roth Capital Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 226, 242.)¿ 

 

Defendant argues that the Fifth Cause of Action is not pled with the requisite particularity as it contains no allegations concerning the content of the omitted facts, Plaintiff’s justifiable or actual reliance, and noneconomic damages. Defendant further argues that there is no transactional relationship between the parties as Defendant did not sell the car to Plaintiff and, as such, there is no duty to disclose. Defendant cites to Mel Clayton Ford v. Ford Motor Co. (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 46, 56 (“Mel Clayton Ford”) and presents that Mel Clayton Ford holds that automakers are not vicariously liable for the independent wrongs of a dealership. Defendant further presents that Plaintiff has not alleged the required ratification by a corporate entity as Plaintiff’s Complaint lacks any allegations regarding how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were made, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, the plaintiff must allege the names of the persons who made the representations, their authority to speak on behalf of the corporation, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when the representation was made. Defendant further presents that Plaintiff has failed to allege what information was concealed, that the economic loss rule bars Plaintiff’s fraudulent concealment claim, that there was no duty of the rights and obligations assumed under the express limited warranty, the purported injury was contemplated by the parties when the warranty was provided, and Plaintiff’s claim seeks tort recovery for a breach of contract duty. 

 

Plaintiff argues that in cases claiming fraud through non-disclosure, it is not practical to allege facts showing how, when and by what means something did not happen; the pleadings must set forth the substance of the statements at issue, that the particularity requirement of fraud is relaxed when it is apparent from the allegations that the defendant necessarily possesses knowledge of the facts. Plaintiff contends that the Fifth Cause of Action is sufficiently pled under Dhital v. Nissan N. Am., Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828 (“Dhital”). Plaintiff further argues that a transactional relationship is not required in this action and directs the parties and the Court to Bader v. Johnson & Johnson (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 1094, 1132 (“Bader”). Plaintiff presents that the engine defect is a material fact, that it poses safety risks, and that the defect arose during the warranty period. Plaintiff argues that his claim is not barred by the economic loss rule and the Rattagan v. Uber Technologies, Inc., (2024) 17 Cal.5th 1 (“Rattagan”) test does not apply.  

 

In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has conceded that each element in a fraud cause of action must be pleaded with specificity and argues that Plaintiff’s Complaint fails in this respect. Specifically, Defendant argues: (1) Plaintiff’s own citation to Dhital is self-defeating as Plaintiff relies on the conclusory contention that the facts are within Defendant’s knowledge; (2) there is no fiduciary or transactional relationship between the parties; (3) Plaintiff’s Opposition fails to address the pleading requirements of factual specificity, such as what fraud was committed and by whom; (4) Rattagan bars Plaintiff’s fraud by omission claim; (5) Plaintiff’s fraud claim arises exclusively from a breach of the warranty contract; and (6) Plaintiff’s reliance on Dhital is misplaced as Plaintiff does not allege any legal agency between Defendant and the unidentified selling dealership. 

 

The Court emphasizes that Dhital states: “Plaintiffs alleged that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan’s authorized dealerships are its agents for purposes of the sale of Nissan vehicles to consumers.” (Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at 844 [emphasis added].) Here, unlike Dhital, Plaintiff’s Complaint has no discussion regarding an authorized dealership for the sale of the Subject Vehicle. Regarding how Plaintiff attained the vehicle, Plaintiff alleges only that he “entered into a warranty contract with Defendant FCA regarding a 2022 Jeep Compass, vehicle identification number 3C4NJDBB6NT169927 (hereafter "Vehicle"), which was manufactured and/or distributed by Defendant FCA.” (Compl., ¶ 9.) Further, Plaintiff only references Defendant’s agents in the capacity of repairs. (See Complaint ¶¶ 39, 41, 45, 48.) References are unclear as to “FCA’s sales representatives” as FCA is a manufacturer and not a dealer and the Complaint includes discussion of non-FCA sales representatives. (See Complaint ¶ 63 and compare with Complaint ¶¶ 22-23.) Thus, as alleged, there is no transactional relationship between the parties.  

 

Rattagan addresses exceptions to the economic loss rule. Specifically, Rattagan goes provides a further analysis than Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979 (“Robinson”) which held that only fraud claims based on affirmative misrepresentations and only when the tortious conduct exposed a plaintiff to risk of liability for personal damages independent of the plaintiff‘s economic losses creates an exception to the economic loss rule. Rattagan provides, in relevant part: 

 

Can a plaintiff assert an independent claim of fraudulent concealment in the performance of a contract? The answer to this question is also yes. A plaintiff may assert a tort claim for fraudulent concealment based on conduct occurring in the course of a contractual relationship, if the elements of the cause of action can be established independently of the parties‘ contractual rights and obligations and the tortious  conduct exposes the plaintiff to a risk of harm beyond the reasonable contemplation of the parties when they entered into the agreement. 

 

Within the context of the broader independent tort principles previously summarized (see discussion at pp. 26–27, ante), our answer encapsulates steps one and three of the required analysis: (1) a court must ascertain the full scope of the parties' contractual agreement; and (3) the court must determine whether the plaintiff can establish all elements of the tort independent of the rights and duties assumed by the parties under the contract. Step two is satisfied here because, as discussed post, the independent tort duty to refrain from engaging in fraudulent conduct is well established by statute and common law. 

 

(Rattagan, supra, 17 Cal.5th 1, 38.)  

 

Plaintiff concedes that the warranty at issue is a contract. (See Complaint ¶ 9.) As such, Rattagan is applicable to the instant action.  

 

Plaintiff has included the warranty at issue which reads, in relevant part:  

 

The Basic Limited Warranty covers the cost of all parts and labor needed to repair any item on your vehicle when it left the manufacturing plant that is defective in material, workmanship or factory preparation. There is no list of covered parts since the only exceptions are the items listed in “section 2.1 C”. These warranty repairs or adjustments, including all parts and labor connected with them, will be made by an authorized dealer at no charge, using new or remanufactured parts. 

 

(Complaint, Exh. A, p. 6.)  

 

This Court’s analysis needs only discuss the first element. The warranty contract does not arise independently of the parties’ contractual rights and obligations and, as such, Plaintiff’s claim is barred by the economic loss rule.  

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. 

 

DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint’s third and fifth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.
