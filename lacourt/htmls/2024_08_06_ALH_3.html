On April 5, 2024, defendant Sunbelt Rentals, Inc. (“Sunbelt”) filed a motion for an order compelling plaintiff Ivan Cervantes (“Plaintiff”) to serve further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Two, Nos. 80, 83, 86, 89, 92, and 95. Sunbelt also requested an order imposing monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and counsel in the amount of $5,440. On May 14, 2024, the Court granted Sunbelt’s motion and imposed sanctions.

Plaintiff served further responses on May 17, 2024. On July 10, 2024, Sunbelt filed this motion for issue and evidentiary sanctions or, in the alternative, for an order compelling further responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 80, 83, 86, 89, and 92. Sunbelt also requests an award of monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,645.

The discovery requests at issue ask Plaintiff to state “all facts” supporting certain aspects of his Third Cause of Action for negligence against Junior Steel Company (“JSC”). Special Interrogatory Nos. 83 and 86 request Plaintiff to state all facts supporting the claims that JSC had a duty to inspect the forklift and jib extension and ensure they were properly attached and secured, and that JSC had to a duty to ensure the forklift and jib extension were safe and ready to use. Special Interrogatory Nos. 89 and 92 request that Plaintiff state all facts to support his contention that he relied on the expertise and training of JSC’s personnel to inspect and determine whether the jib extension and forklift were attached, secured, safe, and ready to use. Plaintiff’s further response to these five interrogatories was identical:

When Defendant Sunbelt Rentals, Inc. (“Sunbelt”) delivered a forklift to the jobsite where Plaintiff Ivan Cervantes was working, Sunbelt failed to secure the jib extension to its forklift at the required three points of securement. Plaintiff’s Third Cause of action for Negligence is based on a “theory that Plaintiff’s employer Junior Steel Company had a duty to inspect the forklift that Sunbelt had delivered. Such an inspection, had Junior Steel employed someone with the expertise to conduct one, could have determined that Sunbelt had negligently delivered the forklift in a

dangerous, partially assembled matter. At the time Plaintiff filed his cause of action against Junior Steel, he had a good-faith belief that the evidence adduced in discovery would show that 1) Junior Steel had personnel with the ability to conduct such an inspection

on duty, and 2) that expert and non-expert testimony would establish that Junior Steel could not rely on Sunbelt performing its work in a safe and professional fashion. Because Plaintiff dismissed this cause of action, he has not conducted the discovery that would establish these facts one way or the other. In any event,

it is for the jury to determine whether Junior Steel was negligent and to what extent their actions contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

(Trowbridge Decl., Ex. M.)

Sunbelt argues that this response remains evasive because Plaintiff filed the claim alleging JSC was liable after deposing JSC’s employees in 2022.  (Trowbridge Decl., ¶ 26.) Plaintiff also served written discovery on JSC on or about February 2, 2022. (Trowbridge Decl., ¶ 27.) Therefore, the “anticipated” discovery referred to in Plaintiff’s further responses appears to be a pretense to avoid providing a more detailed answer.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that this motion is untimely, which the Court briefly addresses. Sunbelt’s motion to compel was initially due on July 3, 2024. Plaintiff’s counsel granted Sunbelt a week extension to file its motion to compel, making the deadline July 10, 2024.  Therefore, Sunbelt’s motion, which was filed on July 10, 2024, is timely.

Plaintiff also argues that its responses are Code-compliant because he cannot be forced to identify specific witnesses which support a contention. Plaintiff relies on a case which held that a party could not be compelled to identify which percipient witnesses would be called at trial because such information was work product. (City of Long Beach v. Superior Court (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 65, 73.) This is not an analogous situation because Plaintiff is not being asked to identify witnesses at trial, but to identify the facts underlying his allegations regarding JSC’s purported obligations. The Court separately adds that Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 provides that by presenting a pleading to the court, an attorney “is certifying that to the best of the person’s knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under all the circumstances,” that “[t]he allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd.(b)(3).) Therefore, there must be some reasoning behind Plaintiff’s decision to file an amended complaint with a cause of action against JSC.

The Court therefore GRANTS Sunbelt’s motion and affords Plaintiff one more opportunity to supplement his responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 80, 83, 86, 89, and 92. The Court imposes monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,440, consisting of 6 hours at defense counsel’s hourly rate of $230 and a $60 filing fee. Further responses are to be served and sanctions are to be paid within 10 days of the date of this Order. Subsequent failure to provide supplemental responses may result in issue or evidentiary sanctions regarding Plaintiff’s reliance on JSC’s employees to inspect or ensure that forklift and jib extension were safe to use, although the Court notes that such a sanction would not preclude Plaintiff from proving at trial that Sunbelt was also negligent.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANTS TY LABBE AND LIZA GONZALEZ’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

August 6, 2024

 

I.            INTRODUCTION

This action was filed by plaintiffs Gerald Wachel (“Mr. Wachel”) and Gina Wachel (“Ms. Wachel”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) on December 30, 2021. Plaintiffs filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on April 14, 2023. Defendants Ty Labbe (“Mr. Labbe”) and Liza Gonzalez (“Ms. Gonzalez”) (collectively, “Defendants”) bring this motion for summary adjudication of Plaintiffs’ Second Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”) as to Ms. Wachel, Plaintiffs’ Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action for Defamation, and Plaintiffs’ Sixth Cause of Action for Negligence Per Se.  

II.          FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs and Defendants are next-door neighbors, sharing a common property boundary line. (Defendants’ Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) No. 1.) On January 8, 2021, Mr. Wachel opened his garage door from his car using a remote electronic device and his cat, Kale, ran out. (UMF Nos. 2-3.) At the time, Mr. Labbe and his son, Ty Labbe, Jr., were in their driveway with their pit bull, Lucy. (UMF No. 4.) Lucy ran and chased Kale. (UMF No. 4.) Mr. Wachel then kicked Lucy to get her to release Kale. (UMF No. 5.) Mr. Labbe and Mr. Wachel then became engaged in a heated verbal argument during which Mr. Labbe stated that he thought Mr. Wachel was a pedophile “spying on little boys and girls.” (UMF No. 8.) Months later, on or around April 8, 2021, Mr. Labbe filed a police report accusing Mr. Wachel of poisoning Lucy. (UMF No. 29.)   

III.        LEGAL STANDARD

“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

IV.        EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The Court declines to rule on Defendants’ evidentiary objections to the Declarations of Gina Wachel and Gerald Wachel because the evidence objected to was not material to the disposition of this motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (q).)

V.          DISCUSSION

A.   Second Cause of Action for IIED

An IIED claim includes the following elements: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903.) “Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ [Citation.]” (Ibid.) “It is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff, or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiffs allege as part of their second cause of action for IIED that Defendants intended to cause them emotional distress, or acted with a reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress, by “scream[ing] in the face of the [Plaintiffs]”, calling Mr. Wachel a pedophile in public while neighbors were within earshot, and threatening them with physical harm.

Defendants argue that summary adjudication of Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for IIED as to Ms. Wachel is appropriate because her emotional distress did not result from any outrageous conduct and Ms. Wachel’s emotional distress was caused by events which Defendants had no control over. (Motion, pp. 8-9.) Defendants emphasize that Ms. Wachel was not present during the verbal altercation between Mr. Wachel and Mr. Labbe on January 8, 2021, and had only watched a video of the events. Therefore, Mr. Labbe did not engage in conduct that was directed towards Ms. Wachel on January 8, 2021.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants fail to address the TAC’s allegations that after January 8, 2021, Mr. Labbe filed false police reports against Plaintiffs and “took affirmative action to encourage the [wrongful] prosecution of [Plaintiffs].” (TAC, ¶ 31.) Plaintiffs allege that as a result of Defendants’ actions, the Los Angeles County District Attorney filed criminal charges against Plaintiffs on March 12, 2021. (TAC, ¶ 35.)  Although these factual allegations are “incorporated” into the Second Cause of Action using boilerplate language, the TAC does not allege that these false police reports were intended to cause emotional distress. Instead, the Second Cause of Action only alleges that “Defendants intended to cause the Wachels emotional distress by screaming loudly that Mr. Gerald Wachel was a pedophile and by threatening to harm the Wachels.” Therefore, Plaintiffs fail to raise a triable issue of fact to show that the alleged conduct was intentionally “directed” towards Ms. Wachel.

Accordingly, the motion for summary adjudication of the Second Cause of Action for IIED as to Ms. Wachel is GRANTED.

B.   Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action for Defamation

Defamation is the intentional publication of a statement of fact that is false, unprivileged and has a natural tendency to injure or cause special damages. (Grenier v. Taylor (2015) 34 Cal.App.4th 471, 486). Rhetorical hyperbole, vigorous epithets, lusty and imaginative expressions of contempt and language used in a loose, figurative sense will not support a defamation action, “[f]alse statements that accuse the plaintiff of criminal conduct are defamatory on their face.” (Ibid.) Whether a published statement is actionable fact or nonactionable opinion is to be decided by the court as a matter of law. (Baker v. Los Angeles Herald Examiner (1986) 42 Cal.3d 254, 260.)

Here, Plaintiffs allege that Mr. Labbe is liable for defamation because he called Mr. Wachel a pedophile while Defendants argue that Mr. Labbe’s description of Mr. Wachel as a pedophile was mere opinion made in the middle of a heated exchange. The statements made by Mr. Labbe on January 8, 2021, included: “Matter of fact, why don’t you get your camera out of my yard. It’s kind of creepy. It’s disturbing.” (UMF No. 8.) When Mr. Wachel replied, “Oh well”, Mr. Labbe said, “It’s disturbing. I think you are a pedophile.” (Id.) Mr. Labbe followed up by saying, “Yes, spying on little boys and girls.” (Id.) Based on the totality of their interaction, Mr. Labbe and Mr. Wachel were obviously engaged in “mudslinging” and exchanging insults, but loudly claiming that someone is a pedophile serves as an insult when it is meant to be a statement of fact.

Therefore, Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication of the Fourth Cause of Action is DENIED.

Defendants also argue that Plaintiffs’ defamation claim fails insofar as they are predicated on statements made to the La Verne Police Department because those reports are privileged publications. A privileged publication or broadcast is one made “[i]n any (1) legislative proceeding, (2) judicial proceeding, (3) in any other official proceeding authorized by law, or (4) in the initiation or course of any other proceeding authorized by law and reviewable pursuant to Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 1084) of Title 1 of Part 3 of the Code of Civil Procedure”, subject to a few exceptions. (Civ. Code, § 47, subd. (b).) Any communication between a person and a law enforcement agency is not privileged if it is one “in which the person makes a false report that another person has committed, or is in the act of committing, a criminal act or is engaged in an activity requiring law enforcement intervention, knowing that the report is false, or with reckless disregard for the truth or falsity of the report.” (Civ. Code, § 47, subd. (b)(4).) Where the facts and circumstances under which a defamatory publication is made are not disputed, the question of privilege is one of law. (Swaffield v. Universal Ecsco Corp. (1969) 271 Cal.App.2d 147, 163.)

Here, Mr. Labbe filed a police report on April 8, 2021, after suspecting that Mr. Wachel poisoned Lucy. Defendants argue that their police report was privileged because they had valid suspicions. Mr. Labbe declares that he believed that Lucy had been poisoned because she was acting lethargic and Defendants found green pellets in their backyard near a common wall with Plaintiffs. (Labbe Decl., ¶ 6.) There is no evidence that the green pellets were actually poison. Also, there is no evidence that Mr. Labbe knew Lucy had ingested the pellets before making the report. Mr. Labbe actually made the report before he had even taken Lucy to the veterinarian for care. Therefore, the Court concludes that the statement made to the police about poisoning Lucy was made in reckless disregard especially given that the detective Mr. Labbe contacted was the detective assigned to another case in which Mr. Wachel was accused of committing animal cruelty against Lucy. Accordingly, the statements made by Mr. Labbe to the police about Lucy being poisoned are not privileged. The motion for summary adjudication of the Fifth Cause of Action is DENIED.

C.   Sixth Cause of Action for Negligence Per Se

Last, Defendants move for summary adjudication of Plaintiffs’ Sixth Cause of Action for negligence per se. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants violated Civil Code section 3342. (TAC, ¶ 79.)

In Plaintiffs’ opposition brief, they withdraw the Sixth Cause of Action. (Opp., p. 3, n. 1.) Therefore, Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication of the Sixth Cause of Action is GRANTED. 

VI.    CONCLUSION

          In light of the foregoing, the motion for summary adjudication as to the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action is DENIED. The motion for summary adjudication as to the Second and Sixth Causes of Action is GRANTED.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On June 7, 2024, the petition for writ of mandate brought by Sean E. Rogers (“Petitioner”) was heard and granted in part. The Court ordered respondent Pasaca Capital, Inc. (“Pasaca”) to allow Petitioner to inspect and copy certain corporate records (“June 7 Order”).

On June 24, 2024, Pasaca filed this motion for a stay of proceedings pending the appeal of the June 7 Order. Petitioner filed an opposition brief on July 8, 2024. In the opposition brief, Petitioner noted that he would file a supplemental declaration but that counsel was unable to obtain Petitioner’s signature prior to the filing date of the opposition brief because of the July 4th holiday weekend.

Pasaca filed a reply brief on July 15, 2024.

On July 25, 2024, Petitioner lodged conditionally under seal a supplemental declaration which attached a copy of a stock redemption agreement executed between Pasaca and its former CEO as Exhibit A. On August 5, 2024, Pasaca filed an ex parte application to strike Petitioner’s supplemental declaration on the grounds that it is inexcusably untimely and irrelevant. Pasaca also filed a motion to seal the unredacted supplemental declaration and the redemption agreement.

The supplemental declaration was filed 17 days after Petitioner’s opposition brief but Petitioner offers no reason for the untimeliness of this filing. Accordingly, the Court, in its discretion, refuses to consider it. (CRC 3.1300, subd. (d).)

Pasaca moves for an order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5, but the applicable statute appears to be section 918 because 918.5 only applies to stays where a judgment debtor has another action pending on a disputed claim against a judgment creditor. The Court thus treats this motion as one brought under section 918, which provides the trial judge with the discretion to “stay the enforcement of any judgment or order, whether or not an appeal will be taken and whether or not a notice of appeal has been filed.” (C.C.P. 918(a), (c).)

Pasaca moves for an order staying enforcement of the June 7 Order pending the resolution of its anticipated appeal. Pasaca argues it will be irreparably harmed due to the nature of the June 7 Order if Petitioner accesses sensitive information to which the court of appeal later determines he has no right. Pasaca also argues that a stay should be issued in this action to prevent Petitioner from “attempting to use Pasaca’s financial records against Pasaca in [a] separate legal proceeding.” (Motion, pp. 10-11.)      

Pasaca states that it is amenable to stipulating to an expedited briefing schedule on a priority basis with the Court of Appeal in order to mitigate any prejudice that could result from a stay.

Pasaca’s arguments are persuasive. The June 7 Order dealt with a novel issue of unsettled law and if Petitioner were able to access records he would not otherwise have had access to under Nevada Law, it would render Pasaca’s appeal effectively useless.  Petitioner’s claims of prejudice are unsubstantiated and do not address the fact that Pasaca has agreed to an expedited briefing process. Petitioner also offers no legal authority for his request that Pasaca post an undertaking. Accordingly, the motion for a stay is GRANTED.
