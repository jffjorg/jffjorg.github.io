DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22STCV04378    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 7, 2024                     TRIAL DATE: March 11, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         G&P Group, Inc, v. City National Bank

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV04378           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff G&P Group, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant City National Bank

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         02/03/22: Complaint filed.

·         01/05/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         06/08/23: Cross-Complaint filed.

·         07/07/23: First Amended Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract stemming from a commercial banking agreement. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant placed Plaintiff into a distressed loan program without justification and offered overdrafts to Plaintiff only to abruptly discontinue them, forcing Plaintiff out of business.

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to special interrogatories propounded to Defendant.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order. 

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED

 

//

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to special interrogatories propounded to Defendant.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to an interrogatory when the court finds that: “(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete[;] (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate[; or] (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Timing:

 

            A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

Plaintiff served the special interrogatories at issue on this motion on April 14, 2023. (Declaration of Damion Robinson ISO Mot. ¶ 2.) Defendant served its initial responses on May 31, 2024. (Id. Exh. 1.) Defendant provided supplemental responses on August 1, 2024. (Id. ¶ 19; Exh. 7.) Plaintiff moved to compel further responses on September 3, 2024, less than 45 days after the supplemental responses were served. This motion is therefore timely.

 

Meet and Confer

 

A party making a motion to compel further responses must also include a declaration stating facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally the issues presented by the motion before filing the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

 

The Declaration of Damion Robinson attached to the moving papers describes the extensive efforts the parties undertook to informally resolve various issues regarding the special interrogatories that are the basis of this motion, including multiple meet-and-confer exchanges and supplemental responses which ultimately did not resolve the outstanding dispute. (Robinson Decl. ¶¶ 2-10; Exhs. 3-6.) The Court observes, however, that the only issues discussed in the meet-and-confer correspondence, as provided by Plaintiff, are improper or vague references to documents with respect to Interrogatories Nos. 11, 14, and 16, an inadequate objection to Interrogatory No. 18, and an evasive response relying on a vague claim of privilege. (See Id.) Plaintiff’s counsel did not identify further deficiencies with Interrogatory No. 16, as referenced in the moving papers, and did not identify No. 17 as a basis for the motion at all. The Court therefore finds that Plaintiff did not adequately meet and confer with Defendant before bringing this motion. Nevertheless, the Court will rule on the merits of the motion in the interest of an expeditious resolution of this dispute.

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 11, 14, and 16 through 18.

 

1.      Special Interrogatory No. 11

 

Special Interrogatory No. 11 is a follow-up interrogatory to Interrogatory No. 10. The latter request asked Defendant to state all facts supporting its contention, as alleged in the Cross-Complaint, that Plaintiff agreed to be bound by the Account Agreement and Disclosures with respect to the checking account Plaintiff opened in January of 2020. (Robinson Decl. Exh. 1. No. 10.) Interrogatory No. 11 asked Defendant to identify—by stating the nature of the document, information, or thing, its date, title or subject, general substance, author, sender, and all recipients—all documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things evidencing the facts stated in response to Interrogatory No. 10. (Separate Statement p. 2:5-17.)

 

Defendant’s response, as amended, asserts a series of boilerplate objections based on relevance, privilege, work-product doctrine, privacy, subpart/compound interrogatories, undue burden, and premature demand for expert analysis and opinions. (SS p.3:18-26.) Defendant then states, subject to those objections, that:

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.230, the answer to this interrogatory can be obtained from the monthly account statements provided to G&P in the ordinary course of business and produced again in this litigation, and numerous documents signed and/or consented to by G&P in the ordinary course of its business and dealings with CNB, described in the response to the preceding interrogatory, including Business Account Agreement, Account Agreement and Disclosures, City National Bank Treasury Management Services Disclosure and Agreement, CNB Treasury Net Form, Online and Mobile Banking Service Agreement, Monthly Account Statements for G&P’s business checking account at CNB, Outgoing Wire Transfer Request forms, Supersedure Agreement (Business), Account Agreement Email (as defined in the preceding interrogatory), and emails from G&P using the email address liz@mrnature.com. Additionally, the Declarations of Anne Lopez and Christina Harder in support of Supplemental Brief in Further Support of Motion for Judicial Reference and the exhibits thereto evidence these facts.

 

(SS p.3:28-4:12.)

 

            At the outset, Defendant’s opposition does not address any of the objections asserted, and the Court therefore finds them without merit. As to the substance of the response, Defendant contends that the response is sufficient because the documents referenced were also described in the response to Interrogatory No. 10 in greater detail. The Court is not persuaded. The purpose of this interrogatory, as stated by Plaintiff, was to obtain a list of documents which Defendant relies upon as evidence, as would be necessary to conduct discovery of those documents or to prepare an exhibit list for trial. Defendant’s response frustrates that purpose. Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling a further more detailed response.

 

2.      Special Interrogatory No. 14

 

Special Interrogatory No. 14 is also a follow-up interrogatory. The previous request asked Defendant to identify by date and nature each default by Plaintiff under any loans, credit facilities, or other extensions of credit by Defendant. (Robinson Decl. Exh. 1. No. 13.) Interrogatory No. 14 then asked for Defendant to identify, as above, all documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things evidencing the facts stated in response. (SS p.5:12-23.)

 

Defendant’s amended response asserted similar boilerplate objections before stating that the answer to the interrogatory could be obtained from (1) a Revolving Note dated June 26, 2015, attached to the First Amended Cross-Complaint; (2) a series of Loan Revision Agreements and Forbearance Agreements attached to the First Amended Cross-Complaint; (3) the Security Agreement, Business Account Agreement and Disclosures, and Treasury Management Services Disclosures and Agreement attached to the First Amended Cross-Complaint, (4) an email transmitting the Account Agreement; (5) an Overdraft Line Agreement attached to the First Amended Cross-Complaint; (6) a Term Note-Interest Included and a Notification of Closure of Deposit Account attached to the Cross-Complaint, and (7) unspecified account statements, notices of default, and communications. (SSp.6:20-7:15.)

 

Once again, Defendant’s opposition does not address the objections asserted, and the Court therefore finds them without merit. Further, the substance of the responses is likewise insufficient for the same reasons as the response to Interrogatory No. 11. Although Defendant’s listing of documents is more comprehensive and the enumerated documents appear responsive to the interrogatory by their titles, the reference to “account statements” “notices of default,” and “communications with G&P about these matters” are inadequate on their face. Plaintiff requested a list of documents with details sufficient to identify those documents. Plaintiff is entitled to a response which satisfies that request.

3.      Special Interrogatory No. 16

 

Special Interrogatory No. 16 asked Defendant to state the date, nature, and amount of each disbursement, interest charge, fee, or service charge that comprises any part of Defendant’s claimed damages. (SS p. 8:7-9.) Defendant’s amended response asserted the same set of boilerplate objections and then listed the same loan documents, notices, and histories referenced with respect to Interrogatory No. 14, in addition to Defendant’s ongoing attorney’s fees and costs. (SS p. 9:3-26.)

 

Defendant contends that its response is sufficient because the nature of the interrogatory inherently requires a compilation or summary of existing records and the burden or expense of making that compilation would be substantially the same for either party.  Based on these assertions, Defendant argues its list of documents should be considered permissible under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230. The Court does not find that explanation credible. Defendant’s own First Amended Cross-Complaint alleges damages “in an amount not less than $602,430,” plus interest, fees, and charges accruing after the date of the operative pleading. (FAXC Prayer No. 1.) That number did not simply emerge from the ether: it was calculated by Defendant at some point, and therefore Defendant has already performed the bulk of the compilation or summary in preparing that estimate. Plaintiff is therefore entitled to a complete and straightforward response to this interrogatory.

 

4.      Special Interrogatory No. 17

 

Special Interrogatory No. 17 asked Defendant to state all interest rates applicable to each loan, credit facility, or extension of credit alleged in the First Amended Cross-Complaint. (SS p.10:20-22.) Defendant again asserted boilerplate objections in response before identifying documents attached to the cross-complaint which contain the respective interest rates and stating the interest rates for the Line of Credit Note as amended, the Business Overdraft Line Agreement, and the Refinanced SBA Loan. (SS p.10:23-11:21.) Plaintiff contends that this response is incomplete because it did not identify the interest rate on the Account Agreement. Defendant does not address this response except to argue that a further response should not be compelled because Plaintiff did not meet and confer on this issue. Plaintiff’s failure to meet and confer does not cure the deficiency in Defendant’s response, and Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling a further response.

 

5.      Special Interrogatory No. 18

 

Special Interrogatory No. 18 asks Defendant to state all reasons that it has not applied the collateral posted by Plaintiff to cover the balance owed under Plaintiff’s Line of Credit. (SS p.12:1-6.) Defendant’s response asserted objections based on attorney-client privilege and the work product doctrine and claimed there was no non-privileged answer to the response. (SS p.12:14-20.) Defendant’s opposition merely stands on this response without explanation for why the answer to this question is privileged except for the assertion that the decision is “based on legal advice.” Defendant’s response is insufficient. A party who claims a privilege has the burden to establish the preliminary facts supporting the claim of privilege. (Costco Wholesale Corp v. Superior Court (2009) 47 Cal.App.3d 818, 825.) Defendant’s unsupported assertion does not carry this burden. Even so, as Plaintiff states in reply, while a privilege objection may shield attorney-client communications from disclosure, it does not excuse Defendant from providing the business reasons for its decisions.  Defendant must provide its reasons in a verified response whether the reasons amount to reliance on advice of counsel or otherwise.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendant and its counsel for the evasive responses to these interrogatories.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions on any attorney engaging in the misuse of the discovery process by requiring that attorney to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300(d) requires the Court to impose sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

            Here, the Court is not inclined to award sanctions, as Plaintiff did not meet and confer regarding all issues presented in the motion, and did not meet and confer at all with respect to one interrogatory. The need for haste in timely presenting the motion does not excuse Plaintiff’s initial failure to present all issues in an informal attempt to resolve the disputes, especially where Plaintiff contends that the supplemental responses did not cure the original deficiencies.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order. 

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

DISCUSSION:

 

Cross-Defendants demur to the First Amended Cross-Complaint in its entirety.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the (cross-) complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the [cross-] complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of David Tarlow in support of the demurrer states that counsel for the parties met and conferred on June 3, 2024 regarding the issues raised in this demurrer, but were not able to reach a resolution of this issue. (Declaration of David Tarlow ISO Dem. ¶ 3.) Attorney Tarlow does not state the form in which this meeting took place, but the included email correspondence, dated May 30, 2024, contains an invitation for a telephone call, which is an acceptable method under section 430.41(a), to discuss the issues raised. (Id. Exh. B.) The Court therefore finds that Cross-Defendants have satisfied their statutory meet and confer obligations.

 

First Cause of Action: Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. § 1030(a))

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the first cause of action for violation of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            To assert a claim under the CFAA, a plaintiff must plead that the defendant “(1) accessed a protected computer (2) without authorization or exceeding such authorization that was granted, (3) knowingly and with intent to defraud, and thereby (4) furthered the intended fraud and obtained anything of value, causing (5) a loss to one or more persons during any one-year period aggregating at least $5,000 in value.” (LVRC Holdings v. Brekka (9th Cir. 2009) 581 F.3d 1127, 1132.) “Loss” means “any reasonable cost to any victim, including the cost of responding to an offense, conducting a damage assessment, and restoring the data, program, system, or information to its condition prior to the offense.” (18 U.S.C. § 1030(e)(11).)

 

            Cross-Defendants first argue that the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act preempts this cause of action. This contention is contrary to the plain language of the Supremacy Clause of the United States Constitution. (U.S. Const. Art. VI § 2.) When federal law conflicts with state law, it is the state law that is “without effect.” (Maryland v. Louisiana (1981) 451 U.S. 725, 746.) The first cause of action is not preempted by California law under this basic legal principle.

 

            Cross-Defendants also argue that the First Amended Cross-Complaint does not adequately allege damages because it simply alleges damages in excess of $5,000 without specifying the source. (FAXC ¶ 84.) Cross-Defendants draw the inference that this claim is grounded in the allegations that Cross-Defendants employed stolen data to start a competing business, rather than damage to Cross-Complainant’s computer systems. Cross-Defendants may not rely upon such inferences in challenging the pleadings by demurrer, as all allegations must be taken as true and the pleadings must be construed in the light most favorable to the non-moving party. Cross-Defendants’ challenges to the allegation of damages are without merit.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Second Cause of Action: Violation of Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (Penal Code § 502)

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the second cause of action for violation of the Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (CDAFA) for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            The CDAFA is California’s counterpart to the federal CFAA, and claims under the CDAFA are substantially identical to claims under the CFAA, except that California does not impose a minimum damage threshold. (Meta Platforms Inc. v. Brandtotal Ltd., (N.D. Cal. 2022) 605 F.Supp.3d 1218, 1260.) Thus, to state a claim under this statute, a plaintiff must plead that the defendant “(1) accessed a protected computer (2) without authorization or exceeding such authorization that was granted, (3) knowingly and with intent to defraud, and thereby (4) furthered the intended fraud and obtained anything of value, causing (5) a loss to one or more persons[.]” (LVRC Holdings v. Brekka (9th Cir. 2009) 581 F.3d 1127, 1132.)

 

            Cross-Defendants contend that the second cause of action is also preempted by the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act (CUTSA). The CUTSA’s preemption provision is codified in Civil Code section 3426.7, which states, in relevant part, that:

 

(a) Except as otherwise expressly provided, this title does not supersede any statute relating to misappropriation of a trade secret, or any statute otherwise regulating trade secrets.

 

(b) This title does not affect (1) contractual remedies, whether or not based upon misappropriation of a trade secret, (2) other civil remedies that are not based upon misappropriation of a trade secret, or (3) criminal remedies, whether or not based upon misappropriation of a trade secret.

 

(Civ. Code § 3426.7.) Applying this provision, the Court of Appeal has interpreted the CUTSA as preempting common law misappropriation claims. (K.C. Multimedia Inc. v. Bank of Am. Tech. & Operations, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 939, 954.) The CUTSA “does not displace noncontract claims that, although related to a trade secret misappropriation, are independent and based on facts distinct from the facts that support the misappropriation claim.” (Angelica Textile Servs. Inc. v. Park (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 495, 506 [aggregating cases].)

 

            Cross-Defendants’ preemption argument as to this cause of action is grounded entirely, in the moving papers, on Henry Schein, Inc. v. Cook, an unpublished 2017 opinion from the Northern District of California. (Henry Schein v. Cook (N.D. Cal. 2017) 2017 WL 783617.) This opinion is of little persuasive value, as the District Court merely concluded that the plaintiff’s claim under Penal Code section 502 could not survive if the facts supporting the misappropriation claim were excised, without first analyzing whether Penal Code section 502 could be preempted by the CUTSA in the first place. (Id. at *5.) Indeed, several other courts, faced with silence from our Court of Appeal on the issue, have found that the CUTSA cannot preempt claims under Penal Code section 502. (See Heieck v. Fed. Signal Corp. (C.D. Cal. 2019) 2019 WL 6873869 at *4-5 [Section 502 is a criminal statute containing criminal remedies and is therefore subject to subdivision (b)(3) of Civil Code section 3426.7]; Synopsis v. Ubiquiti Networks Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2018) 313 F.Supp.3d 1056, 1074-75 [no case law explicitly addressing preemption of statutory claims].) As the District Court for the Southern District of California stated in 2016, “it is illogical to think that the California legislature enacted a computer crime provision and deliberately included a civil remedy that would in turn be preempted by other legislation authorizing civil protection for trade secrets.” (Regents of the Univ. of Cal. v. Aisen (S.D. Cal. 2016) 2016 WL 4097072 at *8.) Although Cross-Defendants’ reply brief offers additional unpublished District Court opinions which found that section 502 claims for civil remedies are preempted, the Court finds these rulings to be considerably less persuasive than the sentiment expressed in Aisen. Indeed, a cursory examination of the history of these two statutes reveals that the Uniform Trade Secrets Act was enacted in 1984 (Stats. 1984 Ch. 1724 § 1), while section 502 was enacted in 1987 (Stats. 1987 Ch. 1499 § 3.) This sequence of events strongly suggests that the Legislature did not intend for section 502 to be preempted by the CUTSA. The Court therefore declines to adopt Cross-Defendants’ interpretation with respect to the second cause of action, and instead finds that CUTSA does not preempt statutory claims.

 

            Cross-Defendants also contend that the second cause of action inadequately pleads damages for the same reason discussed above with respect to the first cause of action. For the same reasons, the Court finds that contention unpersuasive here as well.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Third Cause of Action: Violation of Stored Communications Act (18 U.S.C. § 2701(a)(1))

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the third cause of action for violation of the Stored Communications Act.

 

            To plead a claim under the Stored Communications Act, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant “intentionally [accessed] without authorization a facility through which an electronic communication service is provided […] and thereby obtain[ed], alter[ed], or prevent[ed] authorized access to a wire or electronic communication while it is in electronic storage in such system[.]” (18 U.S.C. § 2701(a).) “Electronic storage” for the purposes of this statute means “(A) any temporary, intermediate storage of a wire or electronic communication incidental to the electronic transmission thereof; and (B) any storage of such communication by an electronic communication service for purposes of backup protection of such communication.” (18 U.S.C. §§ 2510(17); 2711(1).)

 

            Cross-Defendants assert that the third cause of action is inadequately pled because the Cross-Complaint does not clearly allege what kinds of communications were accessed or where they were stored, and therefore does not allege access of the kinds of communications contemplated by the Stored Communications Act. This contention is specious. The First Amended Cross-Complaint plainly lists the materials which were accessed from Cross-Complainant’s internal databases and cloud servers. (FAXC ¶¶ 63-65.) Cross-Defendants’ challenge to this cause of action is without merit.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Conversion

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for conversion for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) “‘Money cannot be the subject of a cause of action for conversion unless there is a specific, identifiable sum involved, such as where an agent accepts a sum of money to be paid to another and fails to make the payment.’ A ‘generalized claim for money [is] not actionable as conversion.’” (PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 395 [internal citations omitted].)

 

Cross-Defendants contend that this cause of action is inadequately pled because the fourth cause of action alleges conversion of pecuniary assets without identifying a specific sum of money which was stolen. (FAXC ¶ 98.) Not so. The Cross-Complaint plainly alleges examples of specific transactions using company funds which Cross-Complainant alleges were improper, such as payment of rent and deposits for an apartment and unilateral, unauthorized wage increases. (FAXC ¶¶ 55-56.) For this reason alone, Cross-Defendants’ challenge to the fourth cause of action is without merit.

 

Cross-Defendants also argue that the fourth cause of action is preempted by the CUTSA to the extent it is grounded in the theft of trade secrets. As this contention does not address all of the allegations on which the fourth cause of action is premised, it is insufficient to sustain a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the fourth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Trade Libel

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for trade libel for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            Trade libel “is the publication of matter disparaging the quality of another’s property, which the publisher should recognize is likely to cause pecuniary loss to the owner.” (City of Costa Mesa v. D’Alessio Investments, Inc. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 358, 376.) The statement constituting trade libel must be pled with specificity, if not verbatim. (Kahn v. Bower (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 1599, 1612.)

 

            Cross-Defendants contend that the only specific allegation of “trade libel” deals with an alleged statement that the CEO of Cross-Complainant, David Sibelman, “was under criminal investigation.” (FAXC ¶ 75.) Cross-Defendants argue that this allegation does not support a claim of trade libel because it did not concern the products or services of Cross-Complainant itself. Not so. The Cross-Complaint expressly alleges that Mr. Sibelman was under investigation “in relationship to his business at OTS LLC.” (Id.) Construed in the light most favorable to Cross-Complainant, this is an allegation of defamatory statements pertaining to Cross-Complainant as a business, and is therefore sufficient to maintain a claim for trade libel.

 

            Cross-Defendants also assert that this cause of action is time-barred because it is premised on statements which occurred in or around October 2022 (FAXC ¶ 9) and therefore falls outside the one-year statute of limitations in Code of Civil Procedure section 340 for defamation claims. Cross-Defendants are incorrect: the statute of limitations for trade libel claims is two years. (Guess Inc. v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 473, 479.) As both versions of the Cross-Complaint were filed within two years of the alleged statement, this cause of action is not barred by the statute of limitations.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Cross-Defendants contend that this cause of action is premised on the theft of trade secrets and is therefore preempted by the CUTSA. However, as Cross-Complainant states, Cross-Defendants are also alleged to have deleted, i.e., destroyed, confidential business information, and converted company assets to make unauthorized purchases and enrich themselves. (FAXC ¶¶ 54-56, 66-67.) Given these additional allegations unrelated to the theft of trade secrets, the Court cannot find the sixth cause of action to be preempted in its entirety.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the sixth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the seventh cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets under the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            To plead a claim for misappropriation of a trade secret, a plaintiff must allege, fittingly, the existence of a trade secret and its misappropriation. (Civ. Code § 3426.1.(b).) The plaintiff “should describe the subject matter of the trade secret with sufficient particularity to separate it from matters of general knowledge in the trade or of special knowledge of those persons who are skilled in the trade.” (Diodes Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal.App.4th 244, 252.)

 

            Cross-Defendants assert that the Cross-Complaint does not allege the trade secrets which were misappropriated with sufficient particularity. The Court disagrees. The Cross-Complaint alleges that Cross-Defendants have taken, inter alia, client lists, contract templates, payroll reports, marketing materials, and business development plans. (FAXC ¶ 65.) Although, as Cross-Defendants state in reply, customer lists are not necessarily trade secrets (see, e.g., Morlife Inc. v. Perty (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1514, 1521), materials such as payroll reports and business development plans appear on their face to fall within the scope of “trade secrets.” Cross-Defendants’ dismissal of the allegations as “broad, catch-all terms” is not persuasive. The allegations are sufficient to distinguish the materials identified from matters of general knowledge in the trade or of special knowledge to those skilled in the trade.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the seventh cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Eighth Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the eighth cause of action for breach of contract for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must plead the contract, the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, Defendant’s breach, and finally the resulting damage. (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458.)

 

Cross-Defendants contend that the Cross-Complaint does not allege that Cross-Complainant performed or is excused from performing on the contracts on which it is premised. A review of the First Amended Cross-Complaint reveals that Cross-Complainants are correct.

 

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Ninth Cause of Action: Fraudulent Inducement

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the ninth cause of action for fraudulent inducement for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            “The elements of fraud that will lead to a tort action are: (a) misrepresentation; (b) knowledge of falsity; (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant[s] to understand fully the nature of the charge made. (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Ibid.) “[G]eneral and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) When the facts are necessarily in the possession of the defendant, less specificity is required, since the purpose of the specificity requirement is in part to provide notice to the defendant of the basis for the claim. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)

 

            Cross-Defendants contend that the ninth cause of action for fraudulent inducement is deficient because it does not allege any misrepresentation with sufficient particularity. The ninth cause of action, asserted exclusively against Halen Kadel, alleges that Cross-Defendant Kadel “withheld material information” from Cross-Complainant, including, but not limited to his past criminal conduct. (FAXC ¶¶ 129-130.) The failure to disclose information is properly encompassed within the tenth cause of action for fraudulent concealment, which is also asserted against this Cross-Defendant. The Court therefore concurs with Cross-Complainant that the ninth cause of action is inadequately pled because it does not allege any affirmative misrepresentations.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the ninth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Tenth Cause of Action: Fraudulent Concealment

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the tenth cause of action for fraudulent concealment for failure to allege facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            The elements of fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) intent to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) the plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of fact. (Hambridge v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.) Fraudulent concealment claims are subject to a lighter specificity requirement than other fraud claims. As the Court of Appeal explained, "it is harder to apply this rule to a case of simple nondisclosure. 'How does one show "how" and "by what means" something didn't happen, or "when" it never happened, or "where" it never happened?'” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.) When the facts are necessarily in the possession of the defendant, less specificity is required, since the purpose of the specificity requirement is in part to provide notice to the defendant of the basis for the claim. (Id.) However, even a claim for fraudulent concealment still requires that a “representation was made to which the alleged concealment was contrary.”

 

            Cross-Defendants contend the tenth cause of action does not allege that Cross-Defendants Kadel and Gillenwater had any duty to disclose any of the facts alleged. A duty to disclose arises when (1) the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant has exclusive knowledge of material facts; (3) when the defendant actively conceals material facts; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but suppresses some material facts. (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) Cross-Defendant Kadel is alleged to have served as Cross-Complainant’s Chief Operating Officer, and, therefore, as a fiduciary of Cross-Complainant. (FAXC ¶ 13.) The Cross-Complaint also alleges that Cross-Defendant Gillenwater had exclusive knowledge of information which Cross-Complainant alleges is material, including the true value of proposed corporate partners (FAXC ¶¶ 44-45, 48) and Kadel and Gillenwater’s history of criminal activity and financial misconduct. (FAXC ¶ 50-51.) Cross-Defendants’ assertion that Cross-Complainant “never asked” about this information is beside the point: the Cross-Complaint alleges that information was withheld which was purportedly material to Cross-Complainant’s business decisions. Cross-Complainant has adequately alleged facts demonstrating that the Cross-Defendants had a duty to disclose.

 

            As Cross-Defendants’ challenges to the tenth cause of action are without merit, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the tenth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Eleventh Cause of Action: Constructive Fraud

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the eleventh cause of action for constructive fraud for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. This cause of action is duplicative of the tenth cause of action on its face, as it is premised on Cross-Defendant Kadel’s concealment of information. Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Twelfth Cause of Action: Unfair Competition

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the twelfth cause of action for unfair competition for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Business & Professions Code section 17200 prohibits any business acts or practices which are unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent. (Bus & Prof Code § 17200.) As Cross-Complainant has alleged multiple causes of action including multiple statutory violations and fraud claims which survive demurrer, the twelfth cause of action is not deficient as a matter of law.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the twelfth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Thirteenth Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the thirteenth cause of action for negligent misrepresentation for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. As Cross-Complainants have successfully alleged a cause of action for fraudulent concealment, i.e., a species of intentional misrepresentation, the Court finds this challenge to be without merit.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the thirteenth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fourteenth Cause of Action: Theft (Penal Code § 496)

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the fourteenth cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Penal Code section 496 states, in relevant part:

 

(a) Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment.

 

(c) Any person who has been injured by a violation of subdivision (a) . . . may bring an action for three times the amount of actual damages, if any, sustained by the plaintiff, costs of suit, and reasonable attorney’s fees.

 

(Penal Code § 496 (a), (c).)

 

            Cross-Defendants argue that the Cross-Complaint does not allege that any of the Cross-Defendants had actual knowledge of any property that was stolen. This contention is incorrect. The Cross-Complaint directly alleges that Cross-Defendants stole Cross-Complainant’s records, and, in particular, that Cross-Defendants Melissa Kadel and Myranda McCurdy stole those materials at the direction of the other Cross-Defendants. (FAXC ¶¶ 61-66.)

 

            Nor is the Court persuaded by Cross-Defendants’ contention that this cause of action is preempted by the CUTSA. As discussed above with respect to the second cause of action, the Court does not construe the CUTSA as preempting statutory claims. Cross-Defendants’ challenges to this cause of action are therefore without merit.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the fourteenth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fifteenth Cause of Action: Declaratory Relief

 

            Cross-Defendants demur to the fifteenth cause of action for declaratory relief for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            To state a claim for declaratory relief, a party must allege (1) a proper subject for declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations. (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909.)

 

            Cross-Defendants contend that the fifteenth cause of action is deficient because it does not allege any facts supporting the contention that an actual controversy exists. The Court concurs. The Cross-Complaint merely pleads the bare elements of a declaratory relief claim without identifying what rights and obligations Cross-Complainant wishes to address.

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the fifteenth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Whether Cross-Claims are “Otherwise Barred”

 

            Cross-Defendants also assert that the cross-claims are otherwise barred because Cross-Complainant’s CEO, Mr. Sibelman, failed to present a cross-complaint in a separate action filed against him specifically. Cross-Complainant’s CEO is not a party to this Cross-Complaint. Cross-Defendants’ argument is therefore without merit.

 

Uncertainty

 

            Although Cross-Defendants purport to demur to the Cross-Complaint as uncertain, they do not specifically argue that the Cross-Complaint is uncertain beyond a bare assertion to that effect with respect to the fifth cause of action. As should be apparent from the Court’s lengthy examination of the Cross-Complaint, the pleadings are not uncertain.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Cross-Complainant has not clearly stated how the Cross-Complaint might be amended to cure the deficiencies identified. However, as the defects arise either from the failure to plead specific information (as in the eighth and fifteenth causes of action) or allegations which are duplicative of the fraudulent concealment claim (as in the ninth and eleventh causes of action), the method of curing these defects is readily apparent. The Court will therefore permit Cross-Complainant to amend the pleadings to allege missing information and to set forth any theories of misrepresentation beyond the mere concealment of material information.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the eighth, ninth, eleventh, and fifteenth causes of action and otherwise OVERRULED.

 

            Cross-Complainant shall have 30 days leave to amend the Cross-Complaint.

HEARING DATE:     October 7, 2024                     TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Jenna Lawrence v. Eureka Restaurant Group, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV20283           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Eureka Restaurant Group, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Jenna Lawrence

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a sexual harassment and employment discrimination action that was filed on August 23, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that she was sexually harassed by a colleague and terminated in retaliation for her complaints and for requiring accommodations for a disability.

 

Defendant moves to compel arbitration of this dispute.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendants move to compel arbitration of this dispute pursuant to an arbitration agreement.

 

Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of the Court’s April 9, 2024 Order granting Plaintiff’s motion to set aside a stipulation to arbitrate. Defendant’s request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

//

 

Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

Under California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Blake v. Ecker (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 728, 741 (overruled on other grounds by Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094).) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate, and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)

 

However, as to the burden of production, rather than persuasion, courts have articulated a three-step burden shifting process:

 

First, the moving party bears the burden of producing “prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy.” [citation] The moving party “can meet its initial burden by attaching to the [motion or] petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the [opposing party’s] signature.” [citation] Alternatively, the moving party can meet its burden by setting forth the agreement’s provisions in the motion. [citations] For this step, “it is not necessary to follow the normal procedures of document authentication.” [citation] If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party does not dispute the existence of the arbitration agreement, then nothing more is required for the moving party to meet its burden of persuasion.

 

If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party disputes the agreement, then in the second step, the opposing party bears the burden of producing evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement. [citation] The opposing party can do this in several ways. For example, the opposing party may testify under oath or declare under penalty of perjury that the party never saw or does not remember seeing the agreement, or that the party never signed or does not remember signing the agreement. [citations]

 

If the opposing party meets its burden of producing evidence, then in the third step, the moving party must establish with admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. The burden of proving the agreement by a preponderance of the evidence remains with the moving party. [citation].

 

(Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165-66.) An electronic record or signature is attributable to a person if it was the act of the person. (Civ. Code § 1633.9(a).) The act of the person may be shown in any manner. (Id.) As described by the Court of Appeal, “the burden of authenticating an electronic signature is not great.” (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 844.)

 

            Defendants have provided a copy of an Arbitration Agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant which appears to bear Plaintiff’s digital signature dated March 30, 2022. (Declaration of Shiri Fishman ISO Mot. ¶¶ 7-8; Exh. A.) Plaintiff does not dispute the genuineness of the agreement. The Court therefore finds that Defendant has demonstrated that Plaintiff signed an agreement to arbitrate.

 

Applicability of FAA

 

            Defendants argue that the FAA governs the arbitration agreement at issue.

 

An arbitration clause is governed by the FAA if the agreement is a contract “evidencing a transaction involving commerce.” (9 U.S.C. § 2.) Courts “broadly construe” this phrase, because the FAA “embodies Congress’ intent to provide for the enforcement of arbitration agreements within the full reach of the Commerce Clause.” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Pers., Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1286.)

 

Here, the Agreement expressly states that arbitration under the agreement shall be conducted “under the Federal Arbitration Act.” (Fishman Decl. Exh. A ¶ 2.) The Court therefore finds that the Federal Arbitration Act applies to this agreement.

 

Scope of the Arbitration Agreement

 

            Defendants contend that the scope of the Arbitration Agreement covers Plaintiff’s substantive claims. Plaintiff does not dispute this contention.

 

            “The scope of arbitration is a matter of agreement between the parties.” (See, e.g., Ericksen, Arbuthnot, McCarthy, Kearney & Walsh, Inc. v. 100 Oak Street (1983) 35 Cal.3d 312, 323.) “A party can be compelled to arbitrate only those issues it has agreed to arbitrate.” (Perez v. U-Haul Co. of California (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 408, 419.)

 

            As it is undisputed that the Agreement covers Plaintiff’s claims, the Court need not proceed further except to adopt the agreement of the parties as to the scope of the agreement as the conclusion of the Court.

 

Exclusion of Sexual Harassment Claims from Arbitration

 

            The primary dispute on this motion is that Plaintiff contends her claims are excluded from arbitration under the recent amendments to the Federal Arbitration Act.

 

            On March 3, 2022, the President signed the “Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021” into law. (Pub. L. No. 117-90, 136 Stat. 26, codified in 9 U.S.C. §§ 401, 402.) The effect of this provision is to preclude arbitration of disputes relating to conduct alleged to be sexual harassment under applicable law, including state law. (9 U.S.C. §§ 401, 402.) Section 3 of the Act states that it applies “to any dispute or claim that arises on or after the date of enactment of this Act.” (Pub. L. No. 117-90, 136 Stat. 26 § 3.) The plain language of the statute states that “at the election of the person alleging conduct constituting a sexual harassment dispute or sexual assault dispute […] no predispute arbitration agreement […] shall be valid or enforceable with respect to a case which is filed under Federal, Tribal, or State law and relates to the sexual assault dispute or the sexual harassment dispute.” (9 U.S.C. 402(a).) A sexual harassment dispute means “a dispute relating to conduct that is alleged to constitute sexual harassment under applicable Federal, Tribal, or State law.” (9 U.S.C. § 401(4) [emphasis added].)

 

            Plaintiff’s complaint alleges that she was discriminated against for taking disability leave (Complaint ¶¶ 5-6) and, critically, that she was retaliated against and ultimately terminated for reporting the harassing conduct of another employee (¶¶ 10-14.) Thus, the Complaint, on its face, alleges conduct constituting sexual harassment and a dispute relating to that conduct. Defendant’s assertion, as offered in both its moving papers and its reply, that Plaintiff’s claims are not subject to the act because she was not the victim of the harassment is unsupported by any legal authority and contrary to the plain, broadly worded language of the Ending Forced Arbitration Act. Defendant’s contention that the remaining claims should be severed is also contrary to the plain language of the statute, which states that the case, not the claim or dispute, is excluded from arbitration. (9 U.S.C. § 402(a).) Defendant offers no authority which empowers the Court to presume that Congress meant something other than the plain language of the statute.

 

            The Court therefore finds that Plaintiff’s complaint is excluded from arbitration under the Ending Forced Arbitration Act.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

CASE:                         Troy Williams v. Ralphs Grocery Co., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 24STCV06750           

 

DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Ralphs Grocery Co., The Kroger Company, and Kirk Reynolds

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Troy Williams

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         03/18/24: Complaint filed.

·         08/23/24: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for defamation and unfair business practices. Plaintiff was terminated in December 2010 for allegedly stealing six bottles of lemonade. In 2012, Plaintiff filed the action Willams v. Ralphs Grocery Co. LASC Case No. BC495977, which concluded with a jury verdict in Plaintiff’s favor, with judgment entered on January 11, 2024. Plaintiff alleges that, during the pendency of that proceeding, Defendants continued to defame him by communicating to others that he was a thief.

 

Defendants demur to the first amended complaint in its entirety.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the fourth cause of action and otherwise OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendants demur to the first amended complaint in its entirety.

 

//

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Defendants’ counsel states that the parties met and conferred on September 6, 2024, regarding the deficiencies in the operative pleading, but could not resolve this dispute. (Declaration of Joshua A. Waldman ISO Dem. ¶ 5.) The Court therefore finds that Defendants have satisfied their statutory meet and confer obligations.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of (1) Plaintiff’s claim in the action Williams v. The Kroger Co. LASC Case No. BC495977 (the “Prior Action,”) (2) Plaintiff’s Worker’s Compensation Claim; (3) the Notice of Ruling on the Motion for Summary Adjudication in the Prior Action; (4) the Court of Appeal Opinion in the Prior Action; (5-11) the Official Reporter’s Transcripts for the jury trial in the prior action beginning August 17, 2023 and continuing through September 19, 2023; (12) the Judgment in the prior action; (13) Plaintiff’s Notice of Filing Partial Satisfaction of Judgment in the Prior Action; (14) Plaintiff’s opposition to Defendants’ Motion for Sanctions in this action; and (15) Trial Exhibit 351A from the Prior Action. Defendants’ requests are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of (1) portions of the September 7, 2023 trial transcript from the Prior Action, and (2) the Court’s July 16, 2024 Ruling on the Motion for Sanctions. Plaintiff’s Requests are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

Res Judicata (First through Third Causes of Action)

 

Defendants demur to all four causes of action on the basis that they are barred by res judicata. Defendants contend these claims are grounded in the same primary right that was litigated to a jury verdict in the prior action. As the parties litigated this issue quite thoroughly in the context of Defendants’ Motion for Sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, the Court will not belabor the point.  For the reasons explained in the Court’s July 16, 2024 ruling, Plaintiff’s claims against Ralphs and its employees, to the extent they are grounded in publications made before the entry of the jury’s verdict in the prior action, are barred by the doctrine of res judicata. (Plaintiff’s RJN Exh. 3.) However, since the First Amended Complaint also alleges ongoing publications in general terms (FAC ¶¶ 24-29), that conclusion does not immediately defeat Plaintiff’s claims in this action which are premised on continuing defamation, compelled self-defamation, and emotional distress.  

 

As Defendant’s reliance on the prior jury verdict is the sole basis for the challenge to the first cause of action for defamation, second cause of action for compelled self-defamation, and third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, Defendants’ Demurrer to the first three causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Unfair Competition

 

            Defendants also demur to the fourth cause of action for unfair competition on the basis that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

California Business and Professions Code § 17200 prohibits any “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.” Ultimately, “an ‘unfair’ business practice occurs when that practice ‘offends an established public policy or when the practice is immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous or substantially injurious to consumers.” (Davis v. Ford Motor Credit Co., LLC (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 581, 585.) “Unlawful” practices are business practices forbidden by any law.” (Olszewski v. Scripps Health (2003) 30 Cal.4th 798, 827.) “Unfair” practices must also be tethered to some underlying constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provision. (Scripps Clinic v. Superior Court (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 917, 940.)

 

            Defendants first argue that the fourth cause of action is barred by the litigation privilege, as codified in Civil Code section 47(b), to the extent it is grounded in Defendants’ alleged suppression of evidence in the prior action. In response, Plaintiff contends that the fourth cause of action is not premised on the concealment of evidence, but on Defendants’ refusal to issue a retraction of their prior publications, which has resulted in reputational harm, lost business, and other economic losses. (FAC ¶ 56.) As Defendants note, Plaintiff asserted the opposite argument in his response to the Motion for Sanctions, and the Court did not find it to be persuasive at that time. (See Defendants’ RJN Exh. N; Plaintiff’s RJN Exh. 3.) Plaintiff’s contention here fares no better, as his argument brings the fourth cause of action squarely within the scope of the Court’s res judicata finding by characterizing this claim as grounded in reputational injury from publications made before the jury verdict.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            Here, Plaintiff has conceded that the fourth cause of action is premised on claims which were conclusively litigated in the prior action. The Court therefore finds that the fourth cause of action cannot be cured by amendment because it is barred by the doctrine of res judicata.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the fourth cause of action and otherwise OVERRULED.
