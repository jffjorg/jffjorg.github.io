Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 20STCV46671    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

annette turner , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

aids healthcare foundation , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

20STCV46671

 

 

Hearing Date:

May 9, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion for summary adjudication

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation

 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiff Tammy Davis

Motion for Summary Adjudication

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS 

The court declines to rule on defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation’s evidentiary objections, filed on May 2, 2024, because those objections are directed to evidence that is not material to the court’s disposition of this motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (q).)

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court grants defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation’s request to take judicial notice of the Third Amended Complaint filed in this action by plaintiffs Annette Turner and Tammy Davis on December 28, 2022.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

 

LEGAL STANDARD

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant or cross-defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action       . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “Once the defendant or cross-defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)  “When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Id. at p. 467; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order granting summary adjudication in its favor and against plaintiff Tammy Davis (“Plaintiff”) on Plaintiff’s first cause of action for sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9.

“Civil Code section 51.9 . . . covers sexual harassment in certain business relationships outside the workplace, including the relationship between a plaintiff and a landlord or property manager.”  (Ramirez v. Wong (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1480, 1487.)  “A person is liable in a cause of action for sexual harassment under [section 51.9] when the plaintiff proves” three elements set forth by statute.  (Civ. Code, § 51.9, subd. (a).)  First, the plaintiff must show that “[t]here is a business, service, or professional relationship between the plaintiff and defendant . . . .”  (Civ. Code, § 51.9, subd. (a)(1).)  Such a relationship may exist between a plaintiff and his or her landlord or property manager.  (Civ. Code, § 51.9, subd. (a)(1)(D).)  Second, the plaintiff must show that “[t]he defendant has made sexual advances, solicitations, sexual requests, demands for sexual compliance by the plaintiff, or engaged in other verbal, visual, or physical conduct of a sexual nature or of a hostile nature based on gender, that were unwelcome and pervasive or severe.”  (Civ. Code, § 51.9, subd. (a)(2).)  Third, the plaintiff must show that he or she “has suffered or will suffer economic loss or disadvantage or personal injury, including, but not limited to, emotional distress or the violation of a statutory or constitutional right, as a result of the conduct described in paragraph (2)” of subdivision (a), section 51.9.  (Civ. Code, § 51.9, subd. (a)(3).)

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of showing that the first cause of action for sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9 has no merit because Defendant has not shown that the element of sexual harassment on the part of Defendant cannot be established.    

Defendant has moved for summary adjudication on the ground that (1) it did not directly violate Civil Code section 51.9[1] and (2) it cannot be held vicariously liable for a violation of Civil Code section 51.9 based on the conduct committed by Tony Rositas (“Rositas”).  (Mot., p. 6:20-22.)  Specifically, Defendant contends that, “[b]ecause the alleged sexual harassment/assault was committed by Rositas, the question is whether there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether [Defendant] may be held vicariously liable for a violation of Section 51.9 based on Rositas’ alleged sexual harassment/assault of [Plaintiff].”  (Mot., p. 9:15-17.)  However, Defendant did not address all legal theories underlying the first cause of action.

Plaintiff bases the first cause of action on two legal theories: (1) the sexual assault committed by nonmoving defendant Rositas (TAC ¶¶ 108, 114, 116-117), and (2) the creation of “a hostile environment based on sex” by Defendant (TAC ¶ 107).  As to the creation of a hostile environment based on sex, Plaintiff has alleged (1) that Defendant knew that its female tenants were unsafe upon receiving reports of sexual harassment at the hands of its “male tenants” (TAC ¶ 113) and (2) two instances in which Plaintiff was subjected to sexual harassment by two male tenants as follows: (i) tenant L.C. informed Plaintiff that he “fantasize[d] about” her in a graphic and sexual manner (TAC ¶ 90), and (ii) tenant G.R. “tri[ed] to rub himself onto [Plaintiff’s] behind” (TAC ¶ 93, subd. (b)).  Thus, it appears that Plaintiff has alleged the creation of a hostile environment based on sex in violation of Civil Code section 51.9 based, at least in part, on Defendant’s failure to take action against the male tenants that sexually harassed Plaintiff.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1048 [Civil Code section 51.9 provides a remedy for hostile environment form of sexual harassment if conduct is pervasive or severe].)  

Defendant has presented evidence and argument in support of its contention that it cannot be held vicariously liable for the sexual assault and harassment of Plaintiff by Rositas under Civil Code section 51.9.  Defendant, however, did not argue or present evidence showing that Plaintiff cannot establish the element of the existence of a hostile environment form of sexual harassment based on the sexual harassment of Plaintiff committed by Defendant’s male tenants as alleged in the Third Amended Complaint.  Moreover, Defendant did not submit evidence establishing that Plaintiff has limited the basis of the first cause of action to the conduct committed by Rositas (e.g., Defendant did not submit Plaintiff’s discovery responses establishing that this cause of action was so limited).  

Thus, Defendant has challenged only a portion of the first cause of action for sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9, which is improper.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1) [“A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action . . . .”] [emphasis added].)  Because the court cannot grant a motion for summary adjudication as to only portion of a cause of action, the court denies Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action for sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9.  (Ibid.)

ORDER

            The court denies defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation’s motion for summary adjudication.

The court orders plaintiff Tammy Davis to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

defendant’s motion to sever unlawful detainer complaint from civil complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Artem Gerashchenkov     

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion to Sever Unlawful Detainer Complaint from Civil Complaint

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

On September 29, 2023, the court issued an order granting the ex parte application filed by plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe (“Rowe”) and ordering that (1) this unlimited civil action filed by Rowe (Case No. 23STCV14562) (the “Civil Action”) and the limited civil action for unlawful detainer filed by defendant Artem Gerashchenkov (“Gerashchenkov”) (Case No. 23IWUD01124) (the “Unlawful Detainer Action”) are related cases, (2) the Unlawful Detainer Action shall be assigned to Department 53, and (3) the Civil Action and the Unlawful Detainer Action are consolidated for all purposes.  (Sep. 29, 2023 Minute Order, p. 1.)  On March 5, 2024, the court issued an order reclassifying the Unlawful Detainer Action (which was filed as a limited jurisdiction civil case) as an unlimited civil case.  (Mar. 25, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)

Gerashchenkov now moves the court for an order severing the Unlawful Detainer Action from the Civil Action on the grounds that the Second Amended Complaint, filed by Rowe in the Civil Action on March 6, 2024, (1) removes Gerashchenkov as a party and (2) does not assert claims involving title to the real property at issue.

The court denies Gerashchenkov’s motion to sever, without prejudice, because the court finds that the appropriate remedy would be for the court to reconsider its prior orders deeming the cases related, consolidating the cases, and reclassifying the Unlawful Detainer Action as an unlimited civil action in light of the new facts and circumstances presented.

First, as noted by Gerashchenkov, Rowe’s First Amended Complaint, filed July 14, 2023, named Gerashchenkov as a defendant, but Rowe’s Second Amended Complaint, filed March 6, 2024, does not name him as a defendant.  (SAC ¶¶ 2-3 [alleging defendants are Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing and Newrez, LLC (collectively, “Civil Defendants”)].)

Second, Rowe’s First Amended Complaint included a first cause of action for quiet title which sought to quiet title to the real property that is the subject of the Unlawful Detainer Action in favor of Rowe.  (FAC ¶¶ 94-105; Unlawful Detainer Compl., ¶ 1 [alleging entitlement to real property known as 2735 W. 94th Street, Inglewood, California].)  But, in the Second Amended Complaint, Rowe does not allege a cause of action to quiet title and does not seek to quiet or adjudicate title to that property.  Instead, in the Second Amended Complaint, Rowe alleges that the wrongful conduct allegedly committed by Civil Defendants caused her to lose that property and seeks to recover only damages against Civil Defendants.  (SAC ¶¶ 4 [alleging that Rowe’s mother purchased the real property located at 2735 W. 94th Street, Inglewood, California], 56; SAC Prayer, ¶¶ 1-5.)  Moreover, the Second Amended Complaint concedes that Rowe lost title to the property.  (SAC ¶ 68 [alleging that mismanagement of the property loan resulted in “loss of equity and loss of the title of the Property in foreclosure”].)   

Based on the new facts and circumstances discussed above, the court finds that it is appropriate, and exercises its discretion, to set an Order to Show Cause why the court should not (1) reconsider and vacate (i) its September 29, 2023 order deeming the Unlawful Detainer Action and Civil Action related, (ii) its September 29, 2023 order reassigning the Unlawful Detainer Action to Department 53 at the Stanley Mosk Courthouse, (iii) its September 29, 2023 order consolidating the Unlawful Detainer Action and Civil Action for all purposes, and (iv) its March 5, 2024 order reclassifying the Unlawful Detainer Action as a civil unlimited case, and (2) issue a new order (i) finding that the Unlawful Detainer Action and Civil Action are not related, (ii) denying the request to consolidate the Unlawful Detainer Action and Civil Action for all purposes, and (iii) leaving the Unlawful Detainer Action as a limited unlawful detainer case.  (Paul Blanco’s Good Car Company Auto Group v. Superior Court (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 86, 97 [“As a general rule, trial courts have inherent jurisdiction to reconsider, vacate, or otherwise modify their interim orders”]; State of California v. Superior Court (Flynn) (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 94, 100 [“Even without a change of law, a trial court has the inherent power to reconsider its prior rulings on its own motion at any time before entry of judgment”].)

ORDER

The court denies defendant Artem Gerashchenkov’s motion to sever.

            The court sets for hearing on June 18, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., in Department 53, an Order to Show Cause why it should not (1) reconsider and vacate (i) its September 29, 2023 order deeming case numbers 23STCV14562 and 23IWUD01124 related, (ii) its September 29, 2023 order assigning case number 23IWUD01124 to Department 53 at the Stanley Mosk Courthouse, (iii) its September 29, 2023 order consolidating case numbers 23STCV14562 and 23IWUD01124 for all purposes, and (iv) its March 5, 2024 order reclassifying the unlawful detainer case (Case No. 23IWUD01124) as a civil unlimited case, and (2) issue a new order        (i) finding that case numbers 23STCV14562 and 23IWUD01124 are not related, (ii) denying the request to consolidate case numbers 23STCV14562 and 23IWUD01124 for all purposes, and (iii) leaving case number 23IWUD01124 as a limited unlawful detainer case.

            Any response to the Order to Show Cause shall be filed no later than nine court days before the hearing.

 

            The court orders defendant Artem Gerashchenkov to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with the demurrer and motion to strike.  No reply papers were filed.

DEMURRER TO ANSWER

Plaintiff Emilio Reyes (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order sustaining his demurrer to each of the 24 affirmative defenses alleged by defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas (“Defendants”) in their answer to Plaintiff’s Third Amended Complaint.

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the first through third, fifth, seventh through 10th, 13th through 19th, 21st, 26th, 27th, 28th, and 33rd affirmative defenses because Plaintiff did not specifically address those affirmative defenses in his supporting memorandum of points and authorities and therefore has not met his burden to show that those defenses do not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense or are uncertain.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subds. (a), (b).)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the fourth affirmative defense of statute of limitations because it states facts sufficient to constitute a defense since Defendants have pleaded that Plaintiff’s action is barred by the statute of limitations set forth in Code of Civil Procedure sections 335.1, 338, and/or 340.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.20, subd. (a), 458; Martin v. Van Bergen (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 84, 91 [statute of limitations is properly alleged by pleading the specific section and subdivision].)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the sixth affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff is “barred” from seeking recovery of damages based on his failure to mitigate damages.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 232; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 11th affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s claims are “barred” by the doctrine of unclean hands.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 237; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court sustains Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 12th affirmative defense of laches because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense since Defendants have pleaded this defense as a terse legal conclusion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (a); Answer, ¶ 238 [“The Plaintiff’s claims are barred by laches”]; Quantification Settlement Agreement Cases (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 758, 812-813 [affirmative defenses must not be pled as terse legal conclusions].)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 20th affirmative defense of sovereign immunity because (1) it states facts sufficient to constitute a defense since Defendants have pleaded that Plaintiff’s action is barred pursuant to the tribal sovereign immunity doctrines, and (2) Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s claims are “barred” pursuant to those doctrines.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subds. (a), (b); Answer, ¶ 246; Demurrer, pp. 3:12-18, 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 22nd affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s action is “barred” due to his own affirmative misconduct.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 248; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 23rd affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s causes of action are “barred” by the equitable doctrine of waiver.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 249; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 24th affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff is “barred” from obtaining the relief sought because Plaintiff consented to the acts about which he complains.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 250; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 24th affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff is “barred” from obtaining the relief sought because Plaintiff ratified the acts about which he complains.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 251; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 29th affirmative defense because (1) it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Defendants have specifically pleaded that Plaintiff’s claims are barred pursuant to the privileges set forth by Civil Code sections 47, subdivisions (a) through (e), and 48, subdivision (a), and (2) Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s claims are “barred” under those statutes.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subds. (a), (b); Answer, ¶ 255; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 30th affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s claims are “barred” because the statutes relied on by Plaintiff violate Defendants’ statutory and constitutional rights.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 256; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 31st affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s claims are “barred” because Defendants’ statements were true.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 257; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 32nd affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff’s claims are “barred” because Plaintiff cannot demonstrate actual malice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 258; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

The court overrules Plaintiff’s demurrer to the 34th affirmative defense because Plaintiff has not shown that this defense is ambiguous or unintelligible on the ground that it states that Plaintiff is “barred” from bringing this action because Defendants’ conduct was protected under the First Amendment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20, subd. (b); Answer, ¶ 260; Demurrer, p. 4:7-11.)

MOTION TO STRIKE ANSWER

Plaintiff moves the court for an order (1) striking Defendants’ answer in its entirety, or (2) in the alternative, striking each affirmative defense alleged by Defendants.

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to strike Defendants’ answer in its entirety because (1) Defendants’ answer is not irrelevant, false, improper, and (2) Plaintiff has not shown that the answer was not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state or a court order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.)

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to strike each affirmative defense alleged in Defendants’ answer because Plaintiff has not shown (1) that all of the affirmative defenses are false, improper, or irrelevant, or that they were not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state or an order of the court, (2) that, as to the second, fifth, eighth, ninth, 24th, 25th, 2th, 30th, 31st, and 34th affirmative defenses, Defendants have improperly combined multiple defenses into one defense, and (3) that Defendants have improperly alleged duplicate defenses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Mot., pp. 5:10-18, 6:18-21.)

 

ORDER

            The court overrules plaintiff Emilio Reyes’s demurrer to defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas’s first through 11th and 13th through 34th affirmative defenses.

            The court sustains plaintiff Emilio Reyes’s demurrer to defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas’s 12th affirmative defense.

            The court denies plaintiff Emilio Reyes’s motion to strike.

            The court grants defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas leave to file a First Amended Answer no later than 7 days from the date of this order that cures the deficiencies in the 12th affirmative defense set forth in this ruling. 

            The court orders defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
