DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19PSCV00586    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: H

Wang v. BB Wells Investment Inc., et al. (19PSCV00586)

ORDER

Defendants James Cheung’s, BB Wells Investment Inc.’s and Protec Investment Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

Background

Plaintiff Kirby Wang Wang (“K. Wang”) alleges as follows:

K. Wang and his mom, Cher Wang (“C. Wang”), had been the owners of the property located at 24332 Delta Drive, Diamond Bar, CA (the “subject property”) since 1989. In about July 2017, K. Wang fell behind on his first mortgage loan with Wells Fargo. There were 5 loans on the subject property at that time; the first two were with Wells Fargo and the latter three were with private lenders. K. Wang wanted to reinstate the first loan and pay off the third, fourth, and fifth loans. In 2017, K. Wang was introduced by Ginny Lin (“Lin”), an acquaintance and real estate agent, to James Cheung (“Cheung”). Cheung is a hard money lender and the principal of Protec Investment Inc. (“Protec”). Cheung agreed to lend K. Wang a $170,000 loan at 18% annual interest, all due within one year and secured by the subject property. 

In about September 2017, K. Wang signed a real estate listing agreement with Faith Global Realty Group, a company that belonged to Lin, for about $980,000.00. Lin suggested that K. Wang should sell the subject property due to the loan maturity and to avoid default and foreclosure. Lin never procured any acceptable offers. When the $170,000 loan matured on about July 17, 2018, Cheung demanded $250,000.00 from K. Wang to pay off the loan. In the fall of 2018, Cheung told C. Wang that Cheung and Protec would reduce the interest rate from 18% to 9%; no documents were provided reflecting this change, however.

On October 18, 2018, Cheung recorded an assignment of the deed of trust to BB Wells Investment Inc. (“BB Wells”), a company Cheung controls. The assignment was signed by Cheung as President of Protec, even though Protec had already been dissolved. On November 15, 2018, a Notice of Default was recorded. On May 10, 2019, BB Wells foreclosed on the subject property.

BB Wells subsequently filed an unlawful detainer action against K. Wang and his father, who were forced to vacate the subject property in July 2019. During the unlawful detainer case, a second promissory note with different language and a different interest rate was produced. K. Wang had never seen or signed the second promissory note and believes the first page of the first and second promissory notes were swapped.

On June 27, 2019, K. Wang and C. Wang brought this lawsuit against BB Wells, Protec, and Cheung. In their initial complaint, the plaintiffs asserted several causes of action in connection with the $170,000 loan, including negligent and reckless misrepresentations, constructive fraud, and unfair business practices. On July 31, 2019, BB Wells filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against K. Wang, C. Wang, and Roes 1-100 for declaratory relief and abuse of process. 

On April 8, 2021, K. Wang filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting causes of action against BB Wells, Protec, Cheung, Lin, First American Financial & Investment Corp. (“First American”), and Does 1-60 for: 

1.       Fraud (against all defendants and Does 1-60);

2.       Constructive Fraud (against Lin, First American, and Does 1-60);

3.       Conspiracy to Commit Fraud (against all defendants and Does 1-60);

4.       Negligent Misrepresentation (against Cheung, Protec, and Does 1-60);

5.       Violation of Federal Truth in Lending Act (against Cheung, Protec, BB Wells, and Does 1-60);

6.       Rescission (against Cheung, Protec, BB Wells, and Does 1-60);

7.       Wrongful Foreclosure (against Cheung, BB Wells, Protec, and Does 1-60);

8.       Breach of Fiduciary Duty (against Lin, First American, and Does 1-60); and

9.       Unfair Business Practices (against all defendants and Does 1-60). 

On September 27, 2023, Protec and Cheung filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint asserting causes of action against K. Wang and Does 1-10 for fraud or deceit based on intentional misrepresentations, fraud or deceit based on concealment, fraud or deceit based on false promises, fraud or deceit based on negligent misrepresentations, breach of contract, and breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. 

The Final Status Conference is set for October 1 2024. Trial is set for October 15, 2024. 

Pending before the Court is Cheung, BB Wells, and Protec’s motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication. For the reasons stated herein, the Court grants the motion for summary judgment.  

Legal Standard

“A party may move for summary judgment. . . if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(1).) 

“The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

“On a motion for summary judgment[/adjudication], the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) “A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) 

“A defendant or cross-defendant has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action. . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action. Once the defendant. . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

Discussion 

Cheung, BB Wells and Protec (together, “Moving Defendants”) move the Court for summary judgment in their favor and against K. Wang as to all causes of action and claims for damages in the Operative Complaint brought against Moving Defendants. In the alternative, Moving Defendants seek summary adjudication that there is no merit to the causes of action brought against Moving Defendants.[FN1] 

Prior Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication 
 
At the outset, the Court has been made aware through the parties’ briefing that on February 9, 2021, the previously assigned judge (The Honorable Peter Hernandez) denied Moving Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment as to K. Wang, denied Moving Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication as to all but one issue, and granted Moving Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication as to one issue.[FN2] Moving Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment or, Alternatively, Summary Adjudication as to K. Wang was directed at the causes of action in the original complaint filed June 27, 2019, which asserted causes of action for (1) Misrepresentations, (2) Constructive Fraud, (3) Voiding of Contract, (4) Unfair Business Practices, (5) Accounting and (6) Quieting Title to Real Property.

While not specifically referenced by either party, the Court is mindful that “[a] party shall not move for summary judgment based on issues asserted in a prior motion for summary adjudication and denied by the court unless that party establishes, to the satisfaction of the court, newly discovered facts or circumstances or a change of law supporting the issues reasserted in the summary judgment motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(2).)

The instant motion is directed at the Operative Complaint filed April 8, 2021, which was filed pursuant to stipulation and asserted causes of action for (1) Fraud, (2) Constructive Fraud, (3) Conspiracy to Commit Fraud, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Violation of Federal Truth in Lending Act, (6) Rescission, (7) Wrongful Foreclosure, (8) Breach of Fiduciary Duty and (9) Unfair Business Practices; the second and eighth causes of action are not asserted against Moving Defendants and the fourth cause of action is asserted against Cheung and Protec only. 

The Court has reviewed Judge Hernandez’s February 9, 2021 ruling, as well as the Notice of Motion filed July 7, 2020 with respect to Moving Defendants’ prior Motion for Summary Judgment or, Alternatively, Summary Adjudication. The Court determines that Moving Defendants’ instant motion does not violate the restrictions set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (f)(2), notwithstanding the fact that both the original complaint and the Operative Complaint contain causes of action for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, rescission/voiding of contract and unfair business practices, because Judge Hernandez elected to treat the previous motion as a motion for judgment on the pleadings with respect to these causes of action. (See Page 6 [“All these contentions essentially argue that the pleading are [sic] insufficient to put Defendants on notice as to the claim alleged against them. Thus, this argument is equivalent to a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings. Thus, the court shall treat this issue as a motion for judgment on the pleadings rather than a motion for summary judgment. . .”]; see also, Page 7 [“Defendants reiterate the arguments made above for the First and Second Causes of Action. However, the court already address this [sic] as not a sufficient basis to grant summary judgment, and instead allowed Plaintiffs to correct this defect. Thus, the court will grant Plaintiffs an opportunity to correct this defect. . .;” and “Defendants contend that this cause of action ‘piggybacks’ on the same causes of action discussed above. . . The court has already addressed these other issues by denying summary adjudication on them. . .”].) 

Accordingly, the Court will proceed to the merits of the motion.  

Opposing Separate Statement 

The Court observes that K. Wang’s opposing separate statement does not comply with California Rules of Court rule 3.1350, subdivision (f), which provides, in relevant part, as follows:

“The Separate Statement in Opposition to Motion must be in the two-column format specified in (h).

(1)      Each material fact claimed by the moving party to be undisputed must be set out verbatim on the left side of the page, below which must be set out the evidence said by the moving party to establish that fact, complete with the moving party's references to exhibits.

(2)      On the right side of the page, directly opposite the recitation of the moving party's statement of material facts and supporting evidence, the response must unequivocally state whether that fact is ‘disputed’ or ‘undisputed.’ An opposing party who contends that a fact is disputed must state, on the right side of the page directly opposite the fact in dispute, the nature of the dispute and describe the evidence that supports the position that the fact is controverted. Citation to the evidence in support of the position that a fact is controverted must include reference to the exhibit, title, page, and line numbers. . .” (Emphasis added). 

K. Wang purports to “dispute” material fact nos. 6, 11, 20, 21, 26, 32, 35, 44, 46, 53, 59, 62, 63, 65, 66, 67, 69, 76, 77, 82, 85, 87, 89, 91, 93, 96, 98, 100, 102, 105, 108 and 110-112, but fails to identify the nature of the dispute. Additionally, while K. Wang purports to “dispute” material fact nos. 16-18 (and part of no. 27), he fails to cite to any supporting evidence. K. Wang also fails to cite Moving Defendants’ evidence in the left-hand column of his opposing separate statement. 

Next, the Court notes that K. Wang’s opposing separate statement contains evidentiary objections, which is improper. California Rules of Court rule 3.1354 expressly states that “objections must not be restated or reargued in the separate statement.” (See also, Hodjat v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 1, 9 [“interposing objections into the separate statement defeats the goal of allowing the trial court to quickly and efficiently determine what particular piece of evidence is admitted and what is not”].) The Court declines to consider any evidentiary objections contained in K. Wang’s opposing separate statement on this basis. 


Request for Judicial Notice 

Accompanying Moving Defendants’ motion is a Request for Judicial Notice. A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court grants Moving Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice and takes judicial notice of Exhibit Nos. 1, 2, 15, 18, 20-24, 31, 34, 36-43, 48, 53, 57, 59 and 60.

Merits                  

Moving Defendants seek summary judgment as to every cause of action brought against them in the Operative Complaint.[FN3] The Court addresses the causes of action in turn. 

First and Third Causes of Action (i.e., for Fraud and Conspiracy to Commit Fraud, Respectively) 

“The elements of the tort of fraudulent misrepresentation are: (1) representation; (2) falsity; (3) knowledge of falsity; (4) intent to deceive; and (5) reliance and resulting damages (causation).” (Cooper v. Equity Gen. Insurance (1990) 219 Cal.App.3d 1252, 1262.) 

“[T]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248 [quotations and citation omitted].)

K. Wang’s first cause of action is comprised of one count of fraudulent misrepresentation and one count of fraud and deceit based on concealment. More specifically, K. Wang first alleges that at the time Protec issued the $170,000.00 loan in July 2017, Cheung represented to him that the only payment he would have to make was a balloon payment after one year for a total of $200,600. (FAC, ¶ 34.) K. Wang alleges that Cheung knew this statement was untrue at the time it was made, “as evidenced by Defendants’ later demand of $259,507.71 in the Notice of Default and $309,166.96 at the foreclosure sale,” and that K. Wang was unaware of the falsity of the statement and relied on it, causing him damage. (Id.)

K. Wang next alleges that sometime between July 2017 and July 2020, Defendants secretly fabricated the second Promissory Note with changed terms in Paragraphs 3-5, along with a changed interest rate, without his knowledge or consent, even though they knew they had a legal duty to disclose these changes to him. He further alleges that he could not have discovered the suppression of these adverse material facts despite care and diligence and that he suffered damages. (Id., ¶ 35.)

Moving Defendants contend that K. Wang cannot establish that Cheung’s statement in Paragraph 34 was false at the time it was made. The Court agrees. It is undisputed that default on any other senior or junior loan or payment of taxes was grounds for default pursuant to Paragraph 8, subdivision (h) of the $170,000 Note (Moving Defendants’ Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) No. 29); that changing the business purpose of the loan was grounds for default pursuant to Paragraph 8, subdivision (a) of the $170,000 Note (UMF No. 29); that, in the event of default, Protec could raise the annual percentage rate to 24% during the period K. Wang was in default pursuant to Paragraph 9, subdivision (c) of the $170,000 Note and could sell the property pursuant to subdivision (d) (UMF No. 30); that the Protec Deed of Trust (“DOT”) permitted Protec to protect the collateral for the Protec DOT by making payments to senior lenders if K. Wang failed to do so (UMF No. 31); that on February 1, 2018, Protec made a second payment to Wells Fargo in the amount of $8,910 to bring the Wells Fargo first lien current after K. Wang missed another two months of mortgage payments (UMF No. 33); that the first lien Wells Fargo monthly payment was in excess of $4,000; that from July 2017 to May 2019 (a total of 22 months), K. Wang did not make any payment to Wells Fargo but knew that Protec had made that foregoing payments to Wells Fargo to prevent Wells Fargo from foreclosing on the property (UMF No. 34); and that Cheung wired $37,302 to Wells Fargo on October 1, 2018 (UMF No. 45), which Cheung represents was to stop the Wells Fargo Trustee’s Sale set for the following day. (Cheung Decl., ¶ 42, Exh. 35.)

Further, while K. Wang purports to dispute material fact no. 32 (i.e., that “[o]n November 2, 2017, Plaintiff sent Cheung a text message informing him that Plaintiff was 3 months in arrears on the Wells Fargo loan, and he asked Protec to make the payment. . .”), on the basis that it “misstates the document” (i.e., Moving Defendants’ Exhibit 27), a review of Moving Defendants’ Exhibit 27 reflects a text message exchange where K. Wang, at 11:11 a.m. on November 2, 2017, texted Wells Fargo’s phone number, the loan number, and the property address before stating “Amt is 12,399.63 without late fees which the rep said is ok to pay first—thanks for your help we are gonna get an appraisal done in few days per ginny and lower price as well ASAP.” This statement supports Moving Defendants’ material fact no. 32, particularly when read in light of the undisputed material fact that the first lien Wells Fargo monthly payment was in excess of $4,000. 

K. Wang, moreover, does not explain the nature of his dispute as to material fact no. 35 (i.e., that “[i]n order to protect its security interest in the Property when Plaintiff failed to make required payments to Wells Fargo, BB Wells as a junior lien holder made mortgage payments to Wells Fargo for a total of approximately $100,000. This is known as an ‘advance to senior debt,’ and the amount was added to Plaintiff’s outstanding balance to Protec as allowed for under Provision 3 of the Protec DOT”) or as to material fact no. 59 (i.e., that “[t]he itemization as of May 10, 2019, shows that Plaintiff owed an outstanding balance of $326,573 to BB Wells”). The foregoing evidence reflects that K. Wang’s outstanding debt increased due to K. Wang’s failure to make payments. 

Moving Defendants also assert that K. Wang cannot establish damages with respect to the second Promissory Note. Again, the Court agrees. K. Wang never made any payments on any loan and by the time BB Wells foreclosed on the second promissory note (the 10% note), it also could have foreclosed on the initial promissory note (the 18% note). While K. Wang contends that “the falsified 10 Percent Note ties in to Defendants’ failure to comply with the Truth in Lending Act” (Opp., 10:17-18)[FN4], K. Wang’s Trust in Lending Act (“TILA”) cause of action fails, for the reasons set forth below in the Court’s discussion of the fifth cause of action.

Finally, K. Wang’s third cause of action likewise fails, inasmuch as it is derivative of his failed first cause of action.
  

Fourth Cause of Action (i.e., Negligent Misrepresentation)[FN5]

“The elements of negligent misrepresentation are (1) the misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damage.” (Apollo Capital Fund, LLC v. Roth Capital Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 226, 243.) 

K. Wang alleges, with respect to the fourth cause of action, that Cheung “made an assertion of untrue fact in that all Plaintiff had to pay was $170,000 plus interest at time of maturity; that, when Defendants (i.e., Protec and Cheung only) made this representation to him, they lacked reasonable ground for the belief, with an intent to induce him to rely upon that representation; and that he, in fact, relied on this representation by entering into the loan transaction to his detriment and later faced an “exorbitant amount of charges” and was damaged. (FAC, ¶ 46.) 

Moving Defendants assert that K. Wang cannot establish that Cheung’s statement in Paragraph 46 was made without a reasonable ground for believing it was true at the time it was made. The Court agrees for the same reasons set forth above as to the fraudulent misrepresentation action. 
  

Fifth Cause of Action (i.e., for Violation of Federal Truth in Lending Act [“TILA”]) 

K. Wang asserts that Moving Defendants violated TILA by failing to make certain disclosures and provide certain documents to him “before [he] undertook the loan transaction with CHEUNG and PROTEC.” (FAC, ¶ 55.)

As an initial matter, the Court notes that it is undisputed that pursuant to paragraph 8, subdivision a, of the $170,000 Note, changing the business purpose of the loan was a ground for default. (UMF No. 29.) It thus appears that the $170,000 Note is not subject to TILA at all. (See, e.g., Mauro v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (E.D.N.Y. 2010) 727 F.Supp.2d 145, 155 [loans for business purposes are exempt from TILA coverage].)

Even if it were assumed that the loan to K. Wang was a consumer loan, Moving Defendants assert that they are entitled to adjudication of this cause of action in their favor because, inter alia, they are not “creditors” under TILA. The Court agrees. Section 1602(g) of Title 15 of the United States Code provides, in relevant part, that “[t]he term ‘creditor’ refers only to a person who both (1) regularly extends, whether in connection with loans, sales of property or services, or otherwise, consumer credit which is payable by agreement in more than four installments or for which the payment of a finance charge is or may be required, and (2) is the person to whom the debt arising from the consumer credit transaction is initially payable on the face of the evidence of indebtedness or, if there is no such evidence of indebtedness, by agreement.” It further provides that “[a]ny person who originates 2 or more mortgages . . . in any 12-month period or any person who originates 1 or more such mortgages through a mortgage broker shall be considered to be a creditor.” 15 U.S.C. § 1602(g). 

It is undisputed that Cheung did not individually extend credit of any kind in 2016 nor 2017. (UMF No. 22.) It is also undisputed that BB Wells, a California corporation incorporated in 2017, did not extend credit of any kind in 2016 or 2017 and had no involvement of any kind with K. Wang until June 2018, when K. Wang entered into an Equity Purchase Agreement with BB Wells. (UMF No. 23.) Further, K. Wang does not explain the nature of his dispute as to material fact nos. 20 (i.e., “Protec was a California corporation incorporated in 1989. Protec did not regularly extend consumer credit in 2016 nor 2017. Protec made two business purpose loans and zero consumer loans in 2016.”) and 21 (i.e., “Protec made two loans in 2017. One of the loans was to a limited liability company for a development project. The other loan was the $170,000 loan to Plaintiff.”). 

While K. Wang argues that Protec was a creditor under section 1602(aa), K. Wang has not brought a cause of action under Home Ownership and Equity Protection Act (“HOEPA”), as discussed below. Further, while K. Wang asserts that Protec is a “creditor” pursuant to 12 C.F.R. section 226.2(a)(17)(v) (explaining, in part, that “[a] person regularly extends consumer credit if, in any 12-month period, the person originates more than one credit extension that is subject to the requirements of § 226.32 or one or more such credit extensions through a mortgage broker”), K. Wang does not offer up any evidence that the requirements of section 226.32 applied.

Moving Defendants also argue that K. Wang’s TILA cause of action is time-barred. The Court agrees.

Section 1640(e) of Title 15 of the United States Code provides, in relevant part, that “any action under this section may be brought . . . within one year from the date of the occurrence of the violation” and that “[a]ny action under this section with respect to any violation of section 1639, 1639b, or 1639c of this title may be brought . . . before the end of the 3-year period beginning on the date of the occurrence of the violation.”

Here, any failure to make the required disclosures occurred at the time the loan documents were signed, which occurred on July 24, 2017. (UMF No. 12.) Plaintiff’s lawsuit was filed on June 27, 2019, nearly two years later.

Although K. Wang, in opposition, asserts that the three-year statute of limitations applies, based on the purported applicability of 15 U.S.C. section 1639, the Court notes that the Operative Complaint does not allege any violation of section 1639, which falls under HOEPA.[FN6] (See, e.g., Cunningham v. Nationscredit Financial Servs. Corp. (7th Cir. 2007) 497 F.3d 715, 717; Lopez v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, Nat. Ass’n (E.D. Cal. 2012) 2012 WL 2953065, at *5 [“Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege facts demonstrating that her mortgage qualifies for protection under HOEPA. Even assuming the truth of Plaintiff’s allegations, her failure to sufficiently allege that her mortgage falls under HOEPA protection subjects the claim to dismissal”].) “On a motion for summary judgment, the issues are framed by the pleadings since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond.” (Scolinoa v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 635, 641.) 

The Court declines to address Moving Defendants’ other arguments made as to the TILA cause of action as they are unnecessary to resolve the instant motion. Summary judgment is granted as to this cause of action. 

 

Sixth Cause of Action (i.e., for Rescission)

K. Wang’s sixth cause of action is predicated on Civil Code section 1689, subdivision (b)(1), which allows a party to a contract to rescind the contract “[i]f the consent of the party rescinding, . . . was given by mistake, or obtained through . . . fraud, or undue influence, exercised by or with the connivance of the party as to whom he rescinds.” 

Moving Defendants assert that K. Wang’s sixth cause of action is barred because K. Wang did not and could not tender at any time before the property was sold. The Court agrees.

“One who desires to cancel a contract on the ground of fraud or mistake must tender back whatever he has received prior to the commencement of the action, offer to place his contractee in statu quo and he must allege such tender or offer in his complaint.” (Williams v. Smith (1954) 127 Cal.App.2d 607, 612; Civ. Code § 1691(b) [“to effect a rescission a party to the contract must. . . [r]estore to the other party everything of value which he has received from him under the contract or offer to restore the same upon condition that the other party do likewise. . .”].)

K. Wang does not explain the nature of his dispute as to material fact no. 62 (i.e., “Plaintiff never requested a payoff amount from Protec or Cheung or BB Wells, never communicated any notice of tender or intent to tender, and never offered to return the principal money lent to Plaintiff or any portion thereof. From the date of Protec’s loan to Plaintiff onward, Plaintiff never paid a single lender on the Property.”). K. Wang concedes that, as of the time of the May 2019 foreclosure, he did not have $170,000 “to pay off the $170,000 loan/principal.” (Response to Material Fact No. 57.) Accordingly, the Court grants Moving Defendants’ motion for summary judgment as to the sixth cause of action. 

 

Seventh Cause of Action (i.e., for Wrongful Foreclosure) 

“[W]here a mortgagee or trustee makes an unauthorized sale under a power of sale he and his principal are liable to the mortgagor for the value of the property at the time of the sale in excess of the mortgages and liens against said property.” (Munger v. Moore (1970) 11 Cal.App.3d 1, 11 [emphasis added]).

It is undisputed that K. Wang testified that the value of the property was around $950,000 to $1,050,000 at the time of foreclosure in 2019. (UMF No. 64.) Further, Cheung attests that a property tax bill on the property from the Los Angeles County Assessor’s Office appraised the property to have a value of $1,003,700 as of May 10, 2019, which he appealed, and that an April 13, 2021 re-appraisal of the property value conducted by the County of Los Angeles Appeals Board reduced the property value to $813,000.00. (Cheung Decl., ¶ 61, Exh. 53.)

It is also undisputed that K. Wang gifted C. Wang 50% of the ownership of the property on March 13, 2019 (UMF No. 50), that C. Wang filed a Chapter 13 bankruptcy proceeding on March 14, 2019 (UMF No. 51), and that C. Wang, as a co-owner of the property, listed in her bankruptcy schedules that the property had a value of $1,100,000 and an outstanding balance of $869,000 from the two Wells Fargo Liens (UMF No. 52). K. Wang does not explain the nature of his dispute as to material fact 66 (i.e., “Given the value range maximum of $1,100,000, and the debt on the Property of $1,139,861, Plaintiff could not have been damaged by the foreclosure because the debt exceeded every valuation in the record”). While K. Wang argues that the property was ultimately sold for $1.44 million, what matters is the valuation of the property at the time of the foreclosure. 

Because the total liens on the property exceeded the value of the property at the time of the sale, K. Wang cannot demonstrate he incurred any damages. The Court thus grants Moving Defendants’ motion for summary judgment as to this cause of action. 

 

Ninth Cause of Action (i.e., for Unfair Business Practices) 

Business and Professions Code section 17200 “prohibits practices that are unfair, unlawful or fraudulent.” (Countrywide Financial Corp. v. Bundy (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 234, 256.) “While the scope of conduct covered by the UCL is broad, its remedies are limited. A UCL action is equitable in nature; damages cannot be recovered.” (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1144.) Under the UCL, “prevailing plaintiffs are generally limited to injunctive relief and restitution.” (Id. [quotations and citation omitted].)

Moving Defendants assert that K. Wang’s ninth cause of action fails because damages are not an available remedy under Business and Professions Code section 17200 and because, while K. Wang does not claim a remedy of restitution, he made no payments that could be restored. The Court agrees. Again, the Operative Complaint is devoid of any requests for injunctive relief and/or restitution. (See FAC, ¶¶ 72-77 and Prayer.)

Accordingly, the Court grants Moving Defendants’ motion for summary judgment.

Case Number: 24PSCP00070    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: H

In re Donald Karlin, Case No. 24PSCP00070

ORDER

The hearing on Petitioner Don Karlin’s Amended Petition to Modify Victim Restitution is CONTINUED to November 15, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. Petitioner is directed to file a supplemental brief by November 1, 2024 addressing whether a probate was opened for Theresa Karlin’s estate and the disposition of that estate; whether anyone other than Petitioner would have any interest in the restitution order; and whether Theresa Karlin ever assigned or left her interest in the restitution order to anyone other than Petitioner. Counsel for Petitioner is to provide notice.

Introduction

In 2022, following a burglary at the Karlin home involving the theft of a large sum of cash, a criminal court issued an order requiring offender David Escoto to pay restitution in the amount of $129,660 to Theresa Karlin, Petitioner’s wife(Bassirian Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1; Karlin Decl. ¶ 3.) Theresa Karlin passed away in 2012. Following her death, the California Victim Compensation Board (the “Board”) initially continued to issue checks to Petitioner by manually changing the name on the check to Petitioner’s name, rather than Theresa Karlin. (Karlin Decl., ¶ 4.) However, Petitioner was informed in 2021 that the Board’s legal division had determined it did not have legal authority to change the names on checks received from the California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation. (Id.) The balance owed under the restitution order is $109,000. (Id. ¶ 3.)

Petitioner now petitions this Court for an order modifying the beneficiary of the restitution order issued in Case No. KA057115, a 2002 criminal matter against Escoto, so that Petitioner is the beneficiary, rather than Theresa Karlin.

 

Legal Standard

“[I]n every case in which a victim has suffered economic loss as a result of the [criminal] defendant’s conduct, the court shall require that the defendant make restitution to the victim or victims in an amount established by court order, based on the amount of loss claimed by the victim or victims or any other showing to the court” (Pen. Code § 1202.4, subd. (f).) “A restitution order imposed pursuant to subdivision (f) shall be enforceable as if the order were a civil judgment.” (Pen. Code § 1202.4, subd. (i).)

“[T]rial courts have the authority to deem restitution orders money judgments. The plain language of section 1214 equates a restitution order to a civil judgment and articulates how such orders can be enforced within the criminal courts, but if a civil court is asked to convert such a restitution order into a civil judgment, . . . it is not error for it to do so.” (Nationwide Ins. Co. of America v. Tipton (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1355, 1358; see also People v. Harvest (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 641, 647 [i.e., “(V)ictim restitution is a civil remedy which does not require a criminal forum The governing statutes specify that an order for victim restitution may originate from criminal court, but expressly recognize that such an order shall be enforceable as a civil judgment. It therefore contemplates that subsequent enforcement efforts may occur outside the context of the criminal law.” (quotations and citations omitted)].)

 

Discussion

In support of his petition, Petitioner has submitted a declaration from Petitioner, a copy of the email he received from the Board, a copy of Theresa Karlin’s death certificate, and a copy of the October 22, 2022 order in Case No. KA057115 requiring Escoto to pay restitution in the amount of $129,660.00. (Bassirian Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1; Karlin Decl. ¶¶¶ 5, 6, 7, Exhs. 1-3.) However, before ruling on the petition, the Court would like additional information about Theresa Karlin’s estate and whether there are other individuals who may have an interest in the restitution order. Specifically, the Court requests that Petitioner advise the Court (1) whether or not a probate was ever opened for Theresa Karlin’s estate and, if so, the disposition of that estate, (2) whether anyone other than Petitioner would have any interest in Theresa Karlin’s restitution order, and (3) whether Theresa Karlin ever assigned or left her interest in the restitution order to anyone other than Petitioner.

Accordingly, the Court will continue the hearing on the petition to November 15, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. Petitioner is directed to file and serve his supplemental brief addressing the foregoing issues by no later than November 1, 2024. Counsel for Petitioner is to provide notice. 

Case Number: KS021326    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: H

The Court is inclined to grant the Motion for Distribution to Funds on Deposit with the Court, filed by Claimant Celeste Osborne, Administrator and Heir to the Estate of Ernest Osborne. At the hearing on September 12, 2024, the Court requests that Claimant’s Counsel address whether notice was provided to the lienholders of the three liens identified in Attachment 11b to the Petition Filed by MTC Financial Inc.

 

Background

On May 30, 2018, MTC Financial Inc. (“MTC”) filed a “Petition and Declaration Regarding Unresolved Claims and Deposit of Undistributed Surplus Proceeds of Trustee’s Sale” (“Petition”). MTC was the trustee under the deed of trust executed on April 19, 2006 and recorded on April 25, 2006 which encumbered the real property commonly known as 1525 Rama Drive in La Puente (the “subject property”). The subject property was the subject of a trustee’s sale that was held on September 21, 2016. MTC’s Petition sought a determination of claims to surplus proceeds. 

On June 8, 2018, an “Order to Deposit Surplus Proceeds Pursuant to Civil Code § 2924j(c) & (d)” (“June 8, 2018 Order”) was entered.

On July 2, 2018, a “Notice of Deposit” was filed, wherein MTC advised that it had submitted the surplus proceeds deposit of $30,969.29 pursuant to the Court’s June 8, 2018 Order.

On October 3, 2018, the Court ordered that surplus proceeds in the amount of $30,969.29 remain deposited with the superior court until such time as they are ordered part of the trial court operations fund per Government Code § 68084.1 or escheated to the State.


Discussion 

Claimant Celeste Osborne, Successor In Interest and Administrator to Estate of Ernest Osborne (“Claimant”), moves for an order distributing the funds on deposit with the Court in the amount of $30,969.29.

Request for Judicial Notice

Turning first to Claimant’s Request for Judicial Notice, a court may take judicial notice “of official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].)

With that standard in mind, the Court grants the request for judicial notice as to: Exhibit 2 (Joint Tenancy Grant Deed recorded July 11, 1984 and Affidavit—Death of Joint Tenant recorded December 3, 2012); Exhibit 5 (Certificate of Death for Ernest Osborne); and Exhibit 6 (“Petition and Declaration Regarding Unresolved Claims and Deposit of Undistributed Surplus Proceeds of Trustee’s Sale” filed May 30, 2018 in this case). The Court denies the request for judicial notice as to: Exhibit 1 (page entitled “Search for Unclaimed Property” taken from http://www.sco.ca.gov/search_upd.html); Exhibit 3 (Declaration of Claimant); Exhibit 4 (Copy of Claimant’s California driver’s license); and Exhibit 7 (Declaration of David M. Brandon, Esq.).


Claimant’s Motion        

Civil Code section 2924k(a), which governs the priority in which claimants must be paid after a foreclosure sale, provides that the proceeds be distributed in the following order: “(1) To the costs and expenses of exercising the power of sale and of sale . . . (2) To the payment of the obligations secured by the deed of trust or mortgage which is the subject of the trustee’s sale. (3) To satisfy the outstanding balance of obligations secured by any junior liens or encumbrances in the order of their priority. (4) To the trustor or the trustor’s successor in interest. In the event property is sold or transferred to another, to the vested owner of record at the time of the trustee’s sale.”

Passage of property to the state by escheat is “not favored by the law and is ‘to be avoided wherever possible.’” (Estate of Johnson (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 917, 921.) Code of Civil Procedure section 1351 provides that “all money or other property deposited in the State Treasury under the provisions of this title, if not claimed by the person entitled thereto within five years from the date of such deposit, shall become the property of the State by escheat” pursuant to proceedings commenced under Code of Civil Procedure section 1410.

The Court maintains ancillary jurisdiction to distribute funds on deposit. (Lord v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1946) 27 Cal.2d 855.) Further, “[j]urisdiction of the court over the parties and the subject matter of an action continues throughout subsequent proceedings in the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.50, subd. (b).) 

Here, Claimant is the biological daughter and sole heir of Ernest Osborne, who passed away in 2015. Ernest Osborne was the owner of the subject property before the foreclosure sale and the only person listed on the title to the subject property. Ernest Osborne was widowed when he died and had no will, and there are no pending probate petitions in this jurisdiction or any other jurisdiction regarding Ernest Osborne. (Claimant Decl., ¶¶ 1-5; Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”), Exhs. 2, 5.) Accordingly, Claimant is a successor in interest and administrator of Ernest Osborne’s estate, as defined in Probate Code section 13006.  

Following the foreclosure sale in 2016, $30,969.29 in proceeds remaining from the foreclosure sale were deposited with the Court. (Claimant Decl., ¶ 7.) There is no evidence that any escheat proceedings have taken place pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1410, and there is no indication that the superior court has escheated the funds and published notice pursuant to Government Code section 68084.1. subdivision (a). In fact, the funds remain on deposit with the superior court. (Brandon Decl., ¶ 2.)

No opposition has been filed by any entities and/or individuals listed on the accompanying proof of service.

Based on the foregoing facts, the Court is inclined to grant the petition. That said, at the hearing on September 12, 2024, the Court requests that Claimant’s counsel address whether notice of MTC’s petition was provided to certain lienholders referenced in Attachment 11b to the Petition and if not, its bearing (if any) on the instant motion. Specifically, counsel should address whether notice was provided to the lienholders of the following three liens identified in Attachment 11b to the Petition: (1) lien recorded November 28, 1989 as Instrument No. 89 1902708 naming Barclays American/Financial, Inc., a North Carolina Corporation as beneficiary, (2) lien recorded on April 16, 2002 as Instrument No. 02 0888278 naming Deborah Russell, an unmarried woman as to an undivided 100.00000000% interest as beneficiary and (3) lien recorded April 16, 2022 as Instrument No. 02 0888279 naming Sandy Cape Management L.L.C. as to an undivided 100.00000000% interest as beneficiary). (See RJN Exh. 6). Although the Court’s October 3, 2018 ruling states that “[t]he Trustee has identified the following potential claimants: 1) Secretary of Housing and Urban Development; and 2) prior owner, Ernest Osborne; and 3) three liens recorded on the property” and that “[a]ll potential claimants were given notice of the surplus funds,” the proof of service accompanying the Petition does not reflect that service of the Petition was made on the aforementioned lienholders.
