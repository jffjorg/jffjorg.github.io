DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation. 



           


Case Number: 20STCV24898    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date: June 27, 2024

Case Name: Reed v. Salinas, et al.

Case No.: 20STCV24898

Matter: Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

Moving Party: Plaintiffs David and Connie Reed

Responding Party: Defendants Cal Homes Group, Inc., Eddie Salinas, and HVMJ, LLC

Ruling: The Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is denied.


Moving party to give notice.


If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly 

encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect rather than in person due to the COVID-19 pandemic. 


On July 1, 2020, Plaintiffs David Reed and Connie Reed filed the operative Complaint for (1) breach of contract and (2) fraud against Defendants Cal Homes Group, Inc. and Eddie Salinas.  Plaintiffs allege that the parties reached a written settlement agreement through mediation whereby Defendants promised to pay Plaintiffs $15,000 in connection with a purchase of a property that contained certain material defects, but Defendants failed to pay the agreed upon amount when it became due per the settlement agreement.

On April 22, 2021, a default judgment was entered against Defendants Cal Homes Group, Inc. and Eddie Salinas.

On August 31, 2021, the default judgment was amended to include HVMJ, LLC as a Defendant having alter ego liability.  

On January 5, 2022, the Court vacated the amended default judgment on the basis of Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5.  

On January 6, 2022, Defendants Cal Homes Group, Inc., Eddie Salinas, and HVMJ, LLC filed an Answer asserting a general denial and fifteen affirmative defenses.  

Plaintiffs now seek judgment on the pleadings with respect to the Answer, contending that the affirmative defenses cannot defeat Plaintiffs’ breach of contract and fraud claims.  To support their arguments, Plaintiffs point the Court to its rulings on their (a) application for default judgment, (b) motion for charging order, and (c) motion to amend the default judgment.  In those rulings, the Court stated that the evidence supported that Defendants were alter egos and that Salinas used HVMJ to avoid the obligations incurred by him and Cal Homes.  

“A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings on the ground that the complaint does not state facts sufficient to state a cause of action against that defendant. A motion for judgment on the pleadings ‘is equivalent to a demurrer . . . .’ Leave to amend ‘is properly denied if the facts and nature of plaintiffs’ claims are clear and under the substantive law, no liability exists.’ ”  (Templo v. State¿(2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 730, 735.)  

While the Court can take judicial notice of its rulings, it cannot necessarily take judicial notice of the truth of the matters therein, particularly where those matters are currently disputed by Defendants.  Importantly, the rulings at issue pertain to when Defendants had defaulted and could not dispute the assertions made by Plaintiffs.  Those findings cannot now be used to defeat an operative pleading.

Thus, the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is denied.  The Requests for Judicial Notice are granted.   The objections are overruled.

On December 7, 2023, Plaintiff Teresa Martinez filed the operative Complaint for (1) FEHA discrimination, (2) FEHA failure to prevent discrimination, (3) FEHA failure to engage in the interactive process, (4) FEHA failure to provide reasonable accommodations, (5) FEHA retaliation, (6) FEHA harassment, (7) FEHA failure to prevent harassment, (8)-(10) violations of Lab. Code §§ 98.6, 1102.5, 6310, and (11) wrongful termination.   

Defendants Goodwin Procter LLP and D’Juan Parker demur to all causes of action for uncertainty and failure to state sufficient facts.  They argue that the Complaint consists only of legal conclusions without any actual facts pleaded.

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, and “treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn v. Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  It is error “to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if the plaintiff shows there is a reasonable possibility any defect identified by the defendant can be cured by amendment.”  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist.¿(1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

Defendants are mostly correct.  The only causes of action that have factual support are the sixth and seventh causes of action relating to harassment.  The Complaint adequately alleges that Plaintiff was called a “little bitch” and “stuck up white bitch.”  Factual issues remain, and, thus, the Demurrer is overruled as to the sixth and seventh causes of action.

The Demurrer is sustained as to all other causes of action, with 20 days leave to amend.  This is because there are no actual facts pleaded to support such claims.

  1. Confirm/Vacate Award

Mark Perry seeks to confirm an arbitration award in his favor issued by arbitrator Hon. Glen M. Reiser (Ret.) on January 8, 2024.  Gibson, Dunn, & Crutcher LLP (“GD”) seeks to vacate this award.  

The award at issue requires GD to pay Perry $557,371.55.  It also finds that Perry is a retired—as opposed to a resigned—partner and that Perry is entitled to retirement payments after he stops competing with GD.  

During the arbitration proceedings, GD had contended that Perry was a resigned partner that could not receive periodic retirement payments and that even if Perry had retired, he is not entitled to these payments because he competed with GD in the first instance by joining another firm, Weil.   

Code Civ. Proc. § 1285 provides in relevant part, “Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.”

Code Civ. Proc. § 1285.4 states, “A petition under this chapter shall: (a) Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement. (b) Set forth the names of the arbitrators. (c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.”

Code Civ. Proc. § 1286 states, “If a petition or response under this chapter is duly served and filed, the court shall confirm the award as made, whether rendered in this state or another state, unless in accordance with this chapter it corrects the award and confirms it as corrected, vacates the award or dismisses the proceeding.”

“The trial court has but four courses of conduct once a confirmation proceeding is initiated: 1) to confirm, 2) to correct and confirm, 3) to vacate, 4) to dismiss, where a respondent is not a party to the award or is not bound by the arbitration agreement.” (United Bhd. of Carpenters etc., Loc. 642 v. Demello (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 838, 840.)

GD seeks to vacate the subject award because (1) the arbitrator exceeded his powers when, for a summary disposition motion, he legally erred in finding that Perry was a retired  partner entitled to payment when there were, at minimum, material disputes and (2) the arbitrator ignored relevant evidence for the dispositive motion and refused to hold an evidentiary hearing in which GD would attempt to show both (a) evidence of resignation and (b) extrinsic evidence supporting that the firm’s partnership agreement entirely precluded periodic retirement payments if competition ever occurred.  

Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.2(a) provides in relevant part that “the court shall vacate the award if the court determines any of the following: . . . (4) The arbitrators exceeded their powers and the award cannot be corrected without affecting the merits of the decision upon the controversy submitted . . . . (5) The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced . . . by the refusal of the arbitrators to hear evidence material to the controversy . . . .”

GD’s contentions boil down to the assertion that the arbitrator committed legal error in granting Perry’s summary disposition motion and then refusing an evidentiary hearing.  Notably, however, “errors of law committed by the arbitrator, no matter how gross, are not grounds for challenging the arbitrator's award under California law. [ ] [Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase (1992) 3 C4th 1, 11, 10 CR2d 183, 188; Richey v. AutoNation, Inc. (2015) 60 C4th 909, 916, 182 CR3d 644, 648-649; Baize v. Eastridge Cos. (2006) 142 CA4th 293, 300-302, 47 CR3d 763, 768-769]”.  (Cal. Prac. Guide Alt. Disp. Res. Ch. 5-J.)  Further, there was no requirement to hold an evidentiary hearing when the parties agreed to dispositive motions under the applicable procedural rules.

GD contends that “the parties’ agreement expressly circumscribes the arbitrator’s powers: “The arbitrator’s authority and jurisdiction shall be limited to determining the dispute in arbitration in conformity with law, to the same extent as if such dispute were to be determined as to liability and remedy by a court without a jury.” (Ex. 1.1 § 3, italics added.) This language shows that the parties intended that the arbitrator’s legal errors be subject to judicial review.”

“[T]o take themselves out of the general rule that the merits of the award are not subject to judicial review, the parties must clearly agree that legal errors are an excess of arbitral authority that is reviewable by the courts. . . . we emphasize that parties seeking to allow judicial review of the merits, and to avoid an additional dispute over the scope of review, would be well advised to provide for that review explicitly and unambiguously.”  (Cable Connection, Inc. v. DIRECTV, Inc. (2008) 44 Cal.4th 1334, 1361.)  

GD’s argument lacks merit because the subject agreement does not unambiguously indicate that there will be review of legal errors or the merits of a ruling.  It merely states that the arbitrator is to act in conformity with the law.  This does not indicate anything as to the level of review available. Indeed, this case is similar to Christensen v. Smith (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 931, 936–37 in which the court of appeal found that the following provision did not provide for greater review than ordinary: “ ‘[t]he arbitrator shall be a retired judge or justice . . . who shall render an award in accordance with substantive California Law.’ ”  Similar to here, the court reasoned as follows: “This language provides no hint the parties contemplated appellate review of the merits. . . . We discern no ambiguity in the agreement. The language here . . . simply does not convey that the parties resolved to contravene the ‘general rule’ of arbitral finality (DirecTV, supra, 44 Cal.4th at p. 1361, 82 Cal.Rptr.3d 229, 190 P.3d 586) by subjecting each other to the time and expense of further court proceedings and the arbitrator to appellate policing.”  (Ibid.)  GD also emphasizes the phrase, “to the same extent as if such dispute were to be determined as to liability and remedy by a court without a jury”, but this too merely refers to the scope of power that the arbitrator himself will have.  Again, there is nothing to indicate heightened review.  

The Court finds that there is no basis to review for legal errors; that there is nothing to indicate that the arbitrator refused to consider material evidence for the dispositive motions; and that it was not improper to refuse a full evidentiary hearing instead of ruling on a dispositive motion that had been agreed to.  Thus, the Petition to Vacate Award is denied.

Further, the Petition to Confirm Award is granted because Perry has attached Reiser’s award, as well as the underlying agreement to arbitrate.  (Petition to Confirm, Exhibits 4b, 8c; Code Civ. Proc. § 1285.4.)   


  1. Seal & Unseal

On February 20, 2024, the Court granted GD’s ex parte application to publicly redact certain portions of the Petition to Confirm Award while awaiting an ultimate ruling on a sealing order. 

GD now seeks to seal (a) its petition to vacate and associated documents and (b) Perry’s petition to confirm.  GD’s Motions primarily relate to “the Firm’s Partnership Agreement, correspondence and testimony about the Firm’s management, structure, and retirement benefits, and argument and expert analysis that would reveal the same.”  GD contends that numerous provisions of its partnership agreement constitute trade secrets given GD’s efforts geared at maintaining confidentiality and the fact that this provides an economic benefit.  GD contends that the disclosure of such information could damage it because there is fierce competition among large law firms and the information could be used by competitors to undercut GD in recruiting talented attorneys.  

GD also seeks to seal information relating to other former partners, their settlement agreements, and their circumstances of departure from GD.  GD further seeks to seal private financial information as to Perry and Weil, including Weil’s retirement benefits.  

Two anonymous former partners of GD seek to unseal the Petition to Confirm Award.  Alternatively, they seek to have the documents unsealed only as to them, given that they are privy to GD’s internal workings as former partners.  

A motion to unseal is somewhat premature here because the Court never truly sealed documents in this matter.  That is the inquiry for this hearing.  It’s true that on February 20, 2024, the Court issued a minute order as follows: “The Court orders Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award Filed on 01/19/2024 sealed pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.550.”  However, the Court’s intention was merely to grant GD’s ex parte application to provide further lodged redactions that were inadvertently omitted while GD awaited an actual ruling on its Motions to Seal.  

In any case, if the Court were to enter a sealing order of some kind, that would ripen the instant Motion to Unseal or allow a substantially similar motion to be heard later.  Therefore, the Court, in the exercise of its discretion, will entertain the former partners’ arguments at this time. 

GD argues that the former partners must arbitrate this dispute.  This lacks merit because this dispute relates to whether Court documents should be sealed and that is clearly not a matter for an arbitrator.  

The Court notes that the issue of sealing has now eclipsed the underlying issue of confirming an award.  There is now at least one article that focuses on this sealing dispute.  (See Ex-Gibson Dunn Partners Battle Firm Over Sealed Records, https://www.law360.com/articles/1827622/ex-gibson-dunn-partners-battle-firm-over-sealed-records.)  The sealing dispute is also discussed by Reuters.  (See Litigator Mark Perry wins ruling in pay fight with ex-firm Gibson Dunn; https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/litigator-mark-perry-wins-ruling-pay-fight-with-ex-firm-gibson-dunn-2024-05-09/.)

Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open to public review.  (Cal. Rules of Court 2.550(c); see also Cal. Const., art. I, § 3, subd. (b)(1) [“The people have the right of access to information concerning the conduct of the people’s business”].)  “The court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d); see also NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1217-1218.)

“A party or member of the public may move, apply, or petition, or the court on its own motion may move, to unseal a record. . . . In determining whether to unseal a record, the court must consider the matters addressed in rule 2.550(c)-(e). [ ] The order unsealing a record must state whether the record is unsealed entirely or in part. If the court's order unseals only part of the record or unseals the record only as to certain persons, the order must specify the particular records that are unsealed, the particular persons who may have access to the record, or both. . . .”  (Cal. Rules of Court 2.551(h).)

GD has established an overriding confidentiality interest with respect to portions of its partnership agreement that the Court finds to be a trade secret or other property information.  Specifically, the Court refers to Sections 6.03(c), 7.04(c), 8.02(c), 9.01, and Schedules A-E (to the extent even attached) of the 2018 partnership agreement.  If there is not a sealing order, GD’s business may be impacted in detrimental fashion, and no less restrictive means exist to protect the confidentiality interests at issue.

All documents specifically discussing or referencing the aforementioned sections are to be sealed.  This includes substantially similar provisions from other iterations of partnership agreements that have been provided.  

The Court will also allow the names of former partners to be redacted, as well as the terms of the confidential settlements to which they were parties.  

The Court will further redact Perry’s dividend statement and documents pertaining to Weil.

On the other hand, GD’s requests are not otherwise narrowly tailored.  A sea of documents are at issue, and redactions directed at information other than what was stated above are unwarranted.  For example, there is no basis to redact the remainder of the 2018 partnership agreement, including, but not limited to, its recitals, or the provision that states that retired partners (to some extent) forfeit their payments if they compete; most party communications; the subject arbitration agreement; or supposed “private” information about Perry, including that he earned $60 million over his career and had a lucrative 401k.  Indeed, most of this information is unremarkable, and there is no overriding interest that would protect it.  The use of four layers of confidentiality provisions is not alone sufficient.  GD should remember that a settlement is not at issue, and it was GD’s choice not to abide by the arbitrator’s decision, triggering a public lawsuit.  It was also GD’s decision to file a 1,600-page declaration.  

The Court’s ruling means that most redactions from the arbitrator’s award must be removed.  Documents that have been sealed only to the extent specified herein should be provided by GD within 30 days for insertion on the docket. 

The Motions to Seal are granted in part as set forth herein.

The Court has considered the former partners’ arguments in making its decision on GD’s motions.  Thus, the Motion to Unseal is denied as moot.
