ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT’S PERSON MOST KNOWLEDGEABLE

 

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a Song-Beverly Act case. Plaintiff Lucero Ambriz (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) violated the Song Beverly Act’s Breach of Express Warranty, Implied Warranty, and Section 1793.2.

Plaintiff now moves to compel the deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”).

///

///

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On May 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act- Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of Song Beverly Act- Breach of Implied Warranty; and (3) Violation of The Song Beverly Act Section 1793.2.

On June 30, 2023, Defendant filed its answer.

On February 5, 2024, the parties stipulated to a protective order, which this Court approved.

On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s PMK (“Motion”).

On July 29, 2024, Defendant filed its Opposition.

On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Reply.

On August 7, 2024, this Court continued the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion, issued its Song-Beverly Act Addendum, and ordered the parties “further meet and confer BY TELEPHONE, IN PERSON, OR REMOTE VIDEO PLATFORM and file a Joint Status Update on or before Wednesday, September 4, 2024. Written correspondence shall not be acceptable except to memorialize what occurred in the conference.”

On August 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s PMK.

On September 4, 2024, the parties filed a Joint Trial Setting Conference Statement which was silent as to their meet and confer efforts despite the Court’s order.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a)[1] provides that, “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action… without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it…, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.410(a), provides that, “[a]ny party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with [the notice requirements in Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2025.210 through 2025.290] waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.”

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.290, which provides for a limit of seven hours of total testimony of a witness, does not apply to the deposition of a person designated as the person most qualified to be deposed. (See Code. Civ. Proc. § 2025.290(b)(5).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

A motion set forth under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450 must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration or, when the deponent does not attend the deposition, a “declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).)

Plaintiff noticed the deposition of Defendant’s PMK on January 5, 2024, and again on March 8, 2024. (See Declaration of Daniel Gopstein filed June 25, 2024 (“Gopstein Decl.”), ¶¶ 25, 27.) The parties set the deposition for June 19, 2024. (See Declaration of Samantha Geraghty filed July 29, 2024 (“Geraghty Decl.”), ¶¶ 5-7.) Defendant served responses to Plaintiff’s notice on June 12, 2024. (See Geraghty Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff served a “meet and confer” letter on Defendant on June 19, 2024. (See Gopstein Decl., ¶ 29; Geraghty Decl., ¶ 9.)

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion on June 25, 2024, before Defendant responded to his meet and confer letter. (See Geraghty Decl., ¶ 10.) Defendant attempted to set a deposition date of November 1, 2024, after the filing of the instant Motion, but, after an initial acknowledgement, Plaintiff did not respond. (See Geraghty Decl., ¶¶ 11-18.)

Having observed the parties’ poor attempts at meeting and conferring, the Court issued a Minute Order, dated August 7, 2024, continuing the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion, issuing its Song-Beverly Act Addendum, and ordering the parties “further meet and confer BY TELEPHONE, IN PERSON, OR REMOTE VIDEO PLATFORM and file a Joint Status Update on or before Wednesday, September 4, 2024.” (Minute Order dated August 7, 2024, (emphasis in original).)

The parties did not do as the Court directed. Instead, Plaintiff filed a third amended notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMK, setting the deposition for March 19, 2025. (See Plaintiff’s Third Amended Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s PMK.) Because the parties did not do as this Court asked, the Court is unsure why Plaintiff’s third notice was filed with this Court. Have the parties met and conferred and decided upon a mutually agreeable date and the Motion is now moot? Is this notice further proof of Defendant’s alleged lack of cooperation and “stonewalling”? Plaintiff provides no explanation.

At any rate, the Court finds Plaintiff is not entitled to an order compelling the deposition of Defendant’s PMK. Plaintiff’s single meet and confer letter,[2] abject failure to obey this Court’s August 7th directive, and what appears to be a general disinterest in actually setting a deposition date demonstrate Plaintiff is not entitled to this Court’s intervention.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion is DENIED.

V.             SANCTIONS

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(g)(1) provides: “[i]f a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

Plaintiff did not request sanctions for his Motion until his Reply, where he requested $3,175.00 in discovery sanctions.

Plaintiff did not prevail on his Motion, nor did he properly request sanctions in his Notice of Motion.

Defendant did not request sanctions.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for discovery sanctions.

VI.          CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s PMK.

The Court also DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

DATED:      September 25, 2024

Judge, Superior Court

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This case stems from Plaintiff Rachel Waddy’s (“Plaintiff”) claim against General Motors Financial Company, Inc. (“Defendant”), alleging breach of contract, violations of multiple provisions under the Business & Professions Codes[1], violation of Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, as well as several tort claims. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant agreed to finance her purchase of a 2015 Ford Focus (“Vehicle”), and when she was unable to make payments due to the COVID-19 Pandemic, Defendant offered her a financial hardship program, postponing the monthly payments for six months. Despite this agreement, Plaintiff further claims that Defendant repossessed the Vehicle and proceeded to list it for auction. (See Complaint ¶¶ 6-17.)         

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On June 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging (1) Violation of Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act; (2) Conversion; (3) Violation of California Commercial Code Section 9609; (4) Trespass to Chattels; (5) Violation of Consumer Legal Remedies Act (Cal. Business & Professions Code §§ 1750 et seq.), and (6) Business & Professions Code Section 17200.

On August 2, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

On March 4, 2024, Defendant filed a Substitution of Attorney and Scott J. Hyman became the attorney of record for Defendant.

On August 2, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion.

Plaintiff did not file an opposition to this Motion.  

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

As a general rule, “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect…” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 473 (a)(1).) Additionally, the court “in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars…” (Id.) However, a motion for leave to amend may be denied if the moving party makes “no showing whatever that such leave should be granted ‘in furtherance of justice’ or on any other ground.” (Waxman v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County, 246 Cal. App. 2d 668, 672 (1966).) The court may deny a motion for leave to amend an answer to a complaint when “the proposed amended answer raised new issues of fact,” as this would necessitate a continuance “to allow plaintiff time to investigate the factual issues thus raised for the first time.” (Moss Estate Co. v Adler, 41 Cal. 2d 581, 585 (1953).) Finally, under the Rules of Court “[a] failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion.” (Rules of Ct., rule 8.54 (c).)

IV.     ANALYSIS

          Defendant properly asserts in its Memorandum of Points and Authorities that the proposed amendment will not prejudice the Plaintiff and is in the interest of justice. Defendant’s proposed amended answer seeks to add a single affirmative defense under Bus. & Prof. Code §7507.13(b). Unlike Moss Estate where the court denied the defendant’s motion to amend their answer due to new factual issues that would have delayed the trial, the affirmative defense in this case does not present new facts. (Moss Estate, 41 Cal. 2d 581, 585.) Plaintiff alleges in her Complaint that the repossession of the Vehicle was conducted by “Lightning Recovery.” (See Complaint ¶ 12). Additionally, in its Memorandum of Points and Authorities, Defendant argues it did not repossess the Vehicle, but that “Lightning Recovery” did. (See Defendant’s Memorandum of Points and Authorities). Since both parties apparently agree the repossession was carried out by Lightning Recovery, no further discovery or investigation is necessary, and the trial schedule, including the discovery cut-off date, does not need to be altered.

As stated previously, California Rules of Court Rule 8.54 (c) states that when a motion is unopposed it “may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion.” (Rules of Ct., rule 8.54 (c).) Given that Plaintiff has not filed an opposition in this matter, rule 8.54(c) provides the court with additional grounds to grant Defendant’s motion, as the absence of opposition can be interpreted as implied consent.

IV.     CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion for Leave Amend Answer to Complaint. Defendant shall file and serve its Proposed Amended Answer on or before September 26, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER SUSTAINING LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY’S DEMURRER WITH LEAVE TO AMEND AND DENYING LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY’S MOTION TO STRIKE

 

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

This case arises out of a personal injury dispute between Plaintiff Patrick Kennedy (“Plaintiff”), and Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“LACMTA”), Matthew Barrientos (“Barrientos”), and Jorge Martinez (“Martinez”). In its Complaint, Plaintiff alleges two causes of action which include (1) Damages for personal injuries against public entities and employees and (2) intentional tort. (See Complaint, pg. 4, 6) The first cause of action alleges multiple injuries and damages as a direct and proximate result of the negligence, carelessness, and unlawful conduct of Defendants. (See id., pg.5: 1-3.) The second cause of action alleges that Defendants, with the intent to harm or offend Plaintiff, willfully touched him without his consent, causing harm and that a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the same. (See id., pg.6.)

LACMTA now seeks to demurrer to Plaintiff’s entire Complaint for being uncertain and devoid of fact such that LACMTA is not able to respond.

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Complaint, alleging (1) Damages for personal injuries against public entities and employees against LACMTA and (2) intentional tort against Does 1–5.

On August 9, 2024, LACMTA filed the instant demurrer with motion to strike.

Plaintiff has not filed an Opposition to either. Nor has Plaintiff filed a Proof of Service as to the other two named defendants.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

A.       Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[1] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007 (citation omitted); see also Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.”  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App. 3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).  

B.      Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (See id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (See id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (See id. at § 437.)

Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(a).) “Malice” is defined as conduct intended to cause injury to a person or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (See Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. Cal., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (See id.) “Fraud” is an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known by defendant, with intent to deprive a person of property, rights or otherwise cause injury. (See id.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.)

To succeed on a motion to strike punitive damages allegations, it must be said as a matter of law that the alleged behavior was not so vile, base, or contemptible that it would not be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228-1229.) The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. (See Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.) 

IV.     MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), emphasis added.) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

          In their declaration, LACMTA attests to their effort to meet and confer with Plaintiff through correspondence regarding the Complaint’s deficiencies. (See Demurrer, pg. 6; 10-11.) LACMTA made three attempts by contacting Plaintiff’s Counsel to discuss the deficiencies and request an amended complaint. (See id., 16-17.)  Plaintiff did not respond to either attempt or file an amended complaint. (See id., 13-14, 17-18, 21-22.) The failure to meet and confer is mitigated by Plaintiff’s counsel’s failure to respond in any way to Defendant’s attempts. The Court will rule on Defendant’s Motion in the interest of judicial efficiency. However, the Court cautions Plaintiff’s counsel this Court expects strict compliance with all meet and confer requirements going forward and that failure to comply may subject Plaintiff and/or his counsel to sanctions as appropriate.

V.       ANALYSIS 

LACMTA appears to be demurring to both causes of action. However, Plaintiff only alleges its first cause of action against LACMTA. Therefore, the Court will only address the demurrer as to that cause of action. The Court will then address LACMTA’s Motion to Strike (“Motion”).

A.             Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action Is Defectively Pled.

Plaintiff’s first cause of action is entitled “Damages for personal injuries against public entities and employees,” but the substance of the allegations lead this Court to conclude Plaintiff intends to allege negligence against LACMTA.

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.” (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.) “Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1714(a).)

Here, LACMTA argues Plaintiff’s Complaint is devoid of fact and fails to specifically plead any elements of any of the causes of action Plaintiff claims against LACMTA. (See Demurrer, p. 4:4-5.) Plaintiff’s Complaint, LACMTA argues, does not provide the reasons that give rise to liability and therefore fails to provide facts that demonstrate the alleged wrongful acts by LACMTA. (See id., 6-9.) LACMTA argues that the confusing nature of the Complaint deems them unable to determine the facts of the incident, the claims that are being brought, or of their liability. (See id., 11-13.) LACMTA further argues because of this they are unable to understand what it is being sued for, it cannot determine which issues need to be admitted or denied, and therefore cannot provide an appropriate response. (See id., 13-15.)

The Court agrees with LACMTA, albeit not entirely. Although Plaintiff’s first cause of action specifies the date and bus line on which he was allegedly commuting, it does not adequately plead all of the elements of negligence. More specifically, Plaintiff’s first cause of action does not allege LACMTA owed any duty whatsoever to him or that it breached such duty. It simply alleges the negligence was the “sole and proximate cause of the accident giving rise to the action herein.” (Complaint, p. 4:25.)[2] Plaintiff’s approach leaves his first cause of action devoid of the facts necessary to meet even California’s liberal notice pleading standard and is defectively uncertain.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS LACMTA’s Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

B.             LACMTA’s Motion to Strike Inappropriate

Here, LACMTA seeks to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations as they are not recoverable from public entities. (See Motion to Strike, pg. 3: 6-7.)

The Court acknowledges that punitive damages against public entities are expressly prohibited under California Government Code § 818. The Court agrees that Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations would not be recoverable from LACMTA, a public entity. However, because the Complaint alleges two causes of action, one of which is against LACMTA only and the other of which is entirely against Doe Defendants and because the Court has sustained the demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action, thereby temporarily dismissing LACMTA from the action pending Plaintiff’s amendment, it would be inappropriate to grant the Motion because that would deny Plaintiff the opportunity to pursue punitive damages in connection with its second cause of action.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion to Strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations.

VI.     CONCLUSION

          Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court SUSTAINS LACMTA’s Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for negligence WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

In addition, the Court DENIES LACMTA’s Motion to Strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations.

The Court grants Plaintiff leave to file his amended Complaint on or before October 18, 2024.
