DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV23696    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 9-10-24

Case # 22STCV23696 

Trial Date: 6-17-24 

 

TERMINATING SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant, Law Offices of L.A. Law Inc.

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant/Cross-Complainant, Shahrzad Shahili

 

RELIEF REQUESTED 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories (set one)

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION 

Plaintiff Law Offices of L.A. Law Inc. alleges Defendant Shahrzad Shahili failed to pay for outstanding legal services rendered in association with a marital dissolution. Plaintiff alleges an outstanding balance of $32,555.57.

 

On July 21, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint for Breach of Contract, Quantum Meruit for Services Rendered, Account Stated, Book Account, and Fraud. Defendant answered the complaint on October 17, 2022, and filed a cross-complaint against F. Bari Nejadpour, Susan Winchester, and Law Offices of L.A. Law, Inc. on October 18, 2022, for Professional Negligence Legal Malpractice, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Fraud and Deceit, Breach of Contract, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

On December 15, 2022, the court denied the motion to strike the one day after the answer filed cross-complaint. On January 30, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the cross-complaint with leave to amend. A first amended cross-complaint for Professional Negligence Legal Malpractice, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Fraud and Deceit, Breach of Contract, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, was filed on February 27, 2023.

 

On April 20, 2023, the court struck the demurrer to the first amended cross-complaint. On May 30, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the first amended cross-complaint.

 

On June 20, 2023, the second amended cross-complaint for Professional Negligence Legal Malpractice, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Fraud and Deceit, Breach of Contract, and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress was filed. On September 21, 2023, the court overruled the demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action, and sustained the demurrer to the second amended cross-complaint for the first, second and fifth causes of action.

 

On November 20, 2023, the third amended cross-complaint for Professional Negligence Legal Malpractice, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Fraud and Deceit, and Breach of Contract, was filed. On January 16, 2024, the court sustained the demurrer to the Professional Negligence Legal Malpractice, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and Breach of Contract, causes of action without leave to amend, and overruled the demurrer to the fourth cause of action for fraud and deceit. Cross-Defendants answered the third amended cross-complaint on February 15, 2024.

 

RULING: Denied.

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant, Law Offices of L.A. Law Inc. presents a motion for terminating sanctions only, with no alternative request for issue, evidentiary, or monetary sanctions. The motion comes due to the failure to comply with the order compelling further responses to Form Interrogatories (set one), numbers 15.1 and 17.1 from Defendant/Cross-Complainant, Shahrzad Shahili. Defendant/Cross-Complainant submitted a declaration presenting a June 28, 2024, order of disbarment of F. Bari Nejadpour. The declaration represents LA Offices of LA Law, Inc. ceased operations on July 1, 2024. Defendant/Cross-Complainant therefore challenges the motion on grounds of lack of standing. Defendant/Cross-Complainant challenges any imposition of terminating sanctions as overly drastic. Plaintiff in reply challenges of a lack of any meaningful meet and confer efforts. Plaintiff defends its right to proceed with the case, and challenges the order for disbarment as inadmissible hearsay. Plaintiff maintains the responses to Form Interrogatory 15.1 were “intentionally improper” thereby supporting the motion. Finally, Plaintiff challenges any request for monetary sanctions by Defendants.

Rosenthal (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 96, 118-119 citing Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793; Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 613.) “The discovery statutes thus ‘evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.’ (Citation.) Although in extreme cases a court has the authority to order a terminating sanction as a first measure (Citations), a terminating sanction should generally not be imposed until the court has attempted less severe alternatives and found them to be unsuccessful and/or the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective.” (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604.) To avoid sanctions, the burden of proving that a discovery violation was not willful is on the party on whom the discovery was served. (Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250, 252- 253.)

 

The court adheres to a policy of robust discovery in order to facilitate the parties’ presentation of their best, thereby allowing a fair prosecution and defense of the action. In cases of extreme practice, the court will follow the progression of evidentiary and issue sanctions, with terminating sanctions only reserved for the most extreme of cases, as well established in the standard. The court hesitantly imposes terminating sanctions, where the result leads to a potential windfall for the prevailing party and an effective “avoidance of a decision on the merits.” (See Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1194.)

On September 25, 2023, Rio Hondo Community College District filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff for Breach of Written Construction Contract, Breach of Written Settlement Agreement, Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, Money Damages and Civil Penalties for Violations of California False Claims Act, Making or Using False Records of Statements to Obtain payment or Approval of False Claims; and Conspiracy to Violate the False Claims Act.

RULING

Demurrer: Overruled in Part/Sustained with Leave to Amend in Part.

Defendants Del Terra Real Estate Services, Inc. dba Del Terra Group and Luis Rojas (Del Terra) submit a demurrer to the first, second, third, and sixth causes of action in the second amended complaint for Intentional Interference with Contract, Negligence, Violations of Business and Professions Code section 17200, and Taxpayer Action for Temporary and Permanent Injunctions Setting Aside Contracts between RHCCD and Del Terra. Defendants move on grounds of the statute of limitations, and lack of sufficient facts to allege “viable” causes of action. Plaintiff Pioneer Construction Co., Inc. (Pinner) in opposition cite back to the prior order of the court overruling the demurrer and maintain the subject arguments continue to lack merit. Del Terra in reply reiterates the statute of limitations, the lack of viability, and flaws in the operative complaint.

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law . . . .” ’ ” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 [“[U]nder our liberal pleading rules, where the complaint contains substantive factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled or plaintiff given leave to amend.]

 

On February 29, 2024, the court overruled the demurrer to the Intentional Interference with Contract, Violations of Business and Professions Code section 17200, and Taxpayer Action for Temporary and Permanent Injunctions Setting Aside Contracts between RHCCD and Del Terra causes of action, when they were numbered as third, sixth, and tenth causes of action. While Plaintiff notes the prior order overruling the demurrer certain appellate districts considering the question of successive demurrers on previously overruled causes of action allow such practices. “The objecting party is acting properly in raising the point at his first opportunity, by general demurrer. If the demurrer is erroneously overruled, he is acting properly in raising the point again, at his next opportunity. If the trial judge made the former ruling himself, he is not bound by it.” Ion Equipment Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 877; Carlton v. Dr. Pepper Snapple Group, Inc. (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 1200, 1211; Pacific States Enterprises, Inc. v. City of Coachella (1993) 13 Cal. App. 4th 1414, 1420 (footnote 3).) The Second Appellate District, which governs this court, however, appears to treat such a practice as an improper motion for reconsideration barring a showing of new or different facts. (Bennett v. Suncloud (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 91, 96-97.)

 

Following all of these cases however was a change in the Code of Civil Procedure which states: “A party demurring to a pleading that has been amended after a demurrer to an earlier version of the pleading was sustained shall not demur to any portion of the amended complaint, cross-complaint, or answer on grounds that could have been raised by demurrer to the earlier version of the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (b).) The court adheres to the new state wide standard presented in the Code of Civil Procedure, and declines to seek distinction from prior out of district appellate district authority.

 

Even if the court considered any and all new arguments, the court adheres to the Second Appellate District authority under the reconsideration standard, whereby the new demurrer would require reexamination and consideration of new or different facts. Other than renumbered causes of action, the demurrer lacks any identification of altered allegations or newly presented arguments.

 

A review of the prior order and demurrer shows no challenge on the statute of limitations. The court will not now consider argument over the statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (b).) The demurrer is overruled as to this new argument.

 

The court also previously found three of the challenged causes of action were properly pled, as to intentional interference with contract, violations of Business and Professions Code section 17200, and taxpayer temporary and permanent injunction injunctions setting aside contracts between RHCCD and Del Terra. A comparison of the challenged allegations in the first and second amended complaint show no material changes between the pled causes of action. The court finds the restated arguments offer no new argument justifying reconsideration. The court also finds no erroneous ruling upon second presentation thereby justifying sua sponte reconsideration.

 

The demurrer is overruled as the first, third, and sixth causes of action, with the parties directed to the February 29, 2024, order of the court for reference. “If a demurrer is overruled as to a cause of action and that cause of action is not further amended, the demurring party preserves its right to appeal after final judgment without filing a further demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (g).)

 

2nd Cause of Action: Negligence

Del Terra challenges the subject claim on grounds of failure to state an independent cause of action due to the incorporation of the prior claims without any specific distinction for the negligence elements. Del Terra also introduces a new argument regarding a bar under the economic loss rule, due to a lack of distinction between the tort and contractual damages claims. Plaintiff counters the negligence claim is well pled based on a sufficient showing of duty between the parties. The court previously sustained the demurrer to the instant cause of action, and will therefore address previously raised arguments. Because the newly pled complaint now incorporates allegations regarding the economic loss rule, the court will also consider the new economic loss rule position. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (b).)

 

“To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care, (2) the defendant breached that duty, and (3) the breach proximately caused the plaintiff's damages or injuries.” (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 62.) The first amended complaint incorporated the prior allegations and then conclusively alleged the negligence standard without any factual context other than incorporation. [First Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 36-40.]

 

In opposition, Plaintiff cites to paragraph 12 of the second amended complaint, as well as paragraphs from the third cause of action for intentional interference with contract which are in no way incorporated into the previously filed identified cause of action. Paragraph 12 in the second amended complaint identifies a long list of dates involving alleged prior unlawful conduct by the Del Terra defendants. The actual negligence cause of action itself also now includes a allegation of a duty of care based on the “administering” of the contract, which led to “economic loss.” [Sec. Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 43.]

 

The court finds a list of alleged prior criminal conduct lacks support for a finding of a basis of duty unless Plaintiff relies on the allegation regarding Del Terra became the designated contract manager. Paragraph 14 of the operative complaint continues:

 

“PCC is informed and believes and based thereon alleges that in or about January 2018, Del Terra and Luis Rojas, and each of them, formulated a scheme to intentionally delay the Project and earn themselves hundreds of thousands – if not millions – of dollars of additional compensation. This scheme consisted of interfering with the Project’s required plan revision and submission process. The scheme was successfully carried out as the interference delayed the Project by 926 days and, as contemplated, resulted in Del Terra earning itself almost $1 million in additional compensation for its work on the Project.”

 

The allegations continue with articulation of the manifestation of said “scheme” [Sec. Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 15-24], until September 23, 2022, when “Del Terra completely abandoned its most basic obligations as the Project’s construction manager by refusing to respond to PCC’s telephone calls and emails. This refusal prevented a close out of the Project, prevented processing of final change orders, and prevented release of PCC’s retention.” [Id., ¶ 25.]

 

While the economic loss rule presents a potential basis of new challenge, the court again cannot determine a basis of duty between the parties. Del Terra was brought in as the construction manager on behalf of the Rio Hondo parties, not Plaintiff. Furthermore, the demurrer and opposition cite to inapplicable portions of the second amended complaint. The review of the actual operative sections again leads to a conclusion questioning any basis of duty or reasonable reliance. Without an articulated basis of duty, the court declines to make an advisory opinion on potential economic loss rule consideration in that said argument still assumes some form of duty between the parties. (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 922.)

 

The demurrer is sustained on this cause of action.

 

In summary, demurrer is sustained as to the negligence cause of action, and overruled on the remainder. Plaintiff may not add any new causes of action or allegations to the previously existing claims. (Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023.) Any new causes of action added without leave of court may be subject to a motion to strike. If Plaintiff elects to forego filing an amended pleading, the Del Terra defendants are ordered to answer the remaining complaint within 10 days of the lapse of the amendment deadline.

 

“In response to a demurrer and prior to the case being at issue, a complaint or cross-complaint shall not be amended more than three times, absent an offer to the trial court as to such additional facts to be pleaded that there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured to state a cause of action. The three-amendment limit shall not include an amendment made without leave of the court pursuant to Section 472, provided the amendment is made before a demurrer to the original complaint or cross-complaint is filed.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (e)(1).) The court has now twice reviewed the negligence cause of action.

RULING: Overruled.

Request for Judicial Notice: Granted.

·         The court can take judicial notice of the existence of the documents, but not the content for any truth of the matter asserted.

·         The court takes judicial notice of the filing of the government claim and contents only. Gong v. City of Rosemead (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 363, 369 (footnote 1).)

 

Defendants Rio Hondo Community College District (Rio Hondo), Marilyn Flores (“Flores”), Kristal Orozco (“Orozco”), Anais Medina Diaz (“Diaz”), Oscar Valladares (“Valladares”), Rosaelva Lomeli (“Lomeli”) and Vicky Santana (“Santana”) submit a demurrer to the fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action in the second amended complaint for Taxpayer Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Taxpayer action for Waste, and Taxpayer Action for Temporary and Permanent Injunctions Setting Aside Contracts between RHCCD and Del Terra. Defendants collectively challenge the second amended complaint on grounds of a prematurely filed complaint, to wit, filed before any action was taken on the claim; the subject action alleges causes of action beyond the scope of the issues presented in the claim; the claim letter omits the name of the Trustee defendants; the claim period on the fourth of action is now time barred; all causes of action are uncertain; and, the individual trustee defendants are immune from any personal liability under the Government Claims Act.

 

Plaintiff in opposition contends the Government Tort Claims Act in no way bars the tax payer claims, thereby excusing any compliance requirements with the claim requirements. Even if the Government Tort Claims Act applies, Plaintiff maintains sufficient compliance with the notice requirements under substantial compliance doctrine due to both a rejection of the claim before any appearance in the action, and through notice sufficiently apprizing defendants of all claims. Plaintiff denies any time bar as the damages continue to accrue. Plaintiff denies any uncertainty and immunity. Plaintiff alternatively requests leave to amend.

 

Defendants in reply reiterate the arguments in the demurrer, including application of the Government Tort Claims Act to taxpayer claims; lack of substantial compliance doctrine to excuse the premature filing of the complaint less than 45 days from the date of the claim; non-compliance with the Government Claims Act; uncertainty; and immunity. Defendants also deny any tolling of the statute of limitations, and maintain leave to amend would be futile.

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law . . . .” ’ ” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 [“[U]nder our liberal pleading rules, where the complaint contains substantive factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled or plaintiff given leave to amend.]

 

Premature Filing

Defendants challenge the filing of the complaint only seven days after the submission of the claim. The argument depends on the request for judicial notice of a copy of the purported claim with a received date of May 15, 2023. [Req. Jud. Not., Ex. 2.] The opposition offers no objection or dispute the submitted date of the claim, and instead challenges the applicability of the Government Tort Claims Act.

 

“Except as provided in Sections 946.4 and 946.6, no suit for money or damages may be brought against a public entity on a cause of action for which a claim is required to be presented … until a written claim therefor has been presented to the public entity and has been acted upon by the board, or has been deemed to have been rejected by the board...” (Gov. Code, § 945.4.) “A claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property or growing crops shall be presented … not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.” (Gov. Code, § 911.2, subd. (a).)

 

“If the board fails or refuses to act on an application within the time prescribed by this section, the application shall be deemed to have been denied on the 45th day or, if the period within which the board is required to act is extended by agreement pursuant to this section, the last day of the period specified in the agreement.” (Gov. Code, § 911.6, subd. (c).)

 

The fourth and fifth causes of action allege the following damages claims: “As a result, the taxpayers have suffered at least the following damages: (a) all monies paid to Del Terra; (b) the loss of use of the facility resulting from the delays caused by Del Terra; (c) the monies that RHCCD will be forced to pay PCC and its subcontractors as a result of delays to the project; and (d) attorney and claims consultants’ fees paid by RHCCD to defend additional claims regarding the Project delays.” While perhaps phrased as a tax payer recovery, subsection (c) regarding money paid to “PCC and its subcontractors” clearly anticipates some form of (collateral) recovery. Said allegation at a minimum implicitly seeks recovery of damages notwithstanding the intentional omission of explicitly seeking said recovery from the government entity directly. The court finds the situation from the pled distinction of non-sought funds directly from the government entity in no way renders the improperly spent taxpayer funds paid back to Plaintiff relieves Plaintiff from the claim filing requirement. (Stanson v. Mott (1976) 17 Cal.3d 206, 224; Gates v. Superior Court (1986) 178 Cal.App.3d 301, 307; see Minsky v. City of Los Angeles (1974) 11 Cal.3d 113, 117; Escamilla v. Department of Corrections & Rehabilitation (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 498, 506.)

 

As noted above, the claim was received on May 15, 2023. The complaint was filed on May 22, 2023, which is less than 45 days from the claim expiration date. The action was therefore prematurely filed. (Lowry v. Port San Luis Harbor District (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 211, 219.) The court within in its discretion can dismiss the case based on an inability to amend the complaint to correct the defect, (Id. at p. 221 [“[Plaintiff’s] noncompliance with the Act cannot be cured by amending the complaint to allege [it] complied.”].) Later cases considering the impact of premature filing however allow for substantial compliance doctrine to avoid overly technical application of the statute where no showing of prejudice to the government entity appears. (Malear v. State (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 213, 221-222.) The filing of the first amended complaint on October 13, 2023, both constituted a superseding pleading, and therefore informed Defendants of all claims. (Ibid.) The court overrules the demurrer on prematurity grounds.

 

Scope of Claim

On the scope of the claims, Defendants challenge the claim as not reflective of the items addressed in the claim letter. Specifically, the letter addresses the purported wrongful conduct of the Del Terra defendants, but nothing references the taxpayer claims under Government Code section 526a. Defendants also challenge the lack of any identification of the individual defendants.

 

“The claim [] need not specify each particular act or omission later proven to have caused the injury. (Citation.) A complaint's fuller exposition of the factual basis beyond that given in the claim is not fatal, so long as the complaint is not based on an ‘entirely different set of facts.’ (Citation.) Only where there has been a ‘complete shift in allegations, usually involving an effort to premise civil liability on acts or omissions committed at different times or by different persons than those described in the claim,’ have courts generally found the complaint barred. (Citation.) Where the complaint merely elaborates or adds further detail to a claim, but is predicated on the same fundamental actions or failures to act by the defendants, courts have generally found the claim fairly reflects the facts pled in the complaint. (Citation.)” (Stockett v. Association of Cal. Water Agencies Joint Powers Ins. Authority (2004) 34 Cal.4th 441, 447; Plata v. City of San Jose (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 736, 749.) “If a plaintiff relies on more than one theory of recovery against the [governmental agency], each cause of action must have been reflected in a timely claim. In addition, the factual circumstances set forth in the written claim must correspond with the facts alleged in the complaint; even if the claim were timely, the complaint is vulnerable to a demurrer [or motion for judgment on the pleadings] if it alleges a factual basis for recovery which is not fairly reflected in the written claim.” (Fall River Joint Unified School Dist. v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 431, 434.)

 

The claim itself categorically identifies the subject matter as one for “failing to comply with California law and the terms of the contract.” The claim continues with the allegations of alleged misconduct in bringing in the Del Terra parties for project management, but contends said source of conduct was due to the “criminal tactics” of the Del Terra parties. Plaintiff therefore seeks to allege a claim of vicarious tort liability as well, but identified as the interference with contract damages claims. [Req. Jud. Not., Ex. 2.]

 

In considering the sufficiency of the claim, the court may consider the legal foundation for the claims, but cannot render granular distinctions specifically analyzing the factual basis of support for the three challenged causes of action based on the claim letter and operative pleading. (Plata v. City of San Jose, supra, 74 Cal.App.5th at p. 749; Fall River Joint Unified School Dist. v. Superior Court, supra, 206 Cal.App.3d at p. 434.) Again, the claim letter specifically identifies a contract claim on which the court finds no relationship to a taxpayer suit. Nevertheless, in alleging the wrongful conduct by the Del Terra defendants and the purported influence resulting in the identified vicarious liability tortious interference with contract claims, the court considers the basis of a taxpayer claim.

 

“[T]he powers of municipal officers are well defined. Their modes of procedure in all matters of expenditure are pointed out with particularity. They are given by law a legal adviser, and, if not, are fully empowered to employ one. There is no occasion whatsoever for their taking any step without such advice. There is no reason for their ever making any illegal expenditure of the public's moneys. To countenance the making by these officials of an illegal expenditure in one case is to open wide the door for like expenditures in every case.” (Stanson v. Mott (1976) 17 Cal.3d 206, 223–224.) “A taxpayer in his representative capacity can sue a municipality only in cases involving fraud, collusion, ultra vires, or a failure on the part of the governmental body to perform a duty specifically enjoined.” (Silver v. City of Los Angeles (1961) 57 Cal.2d 39, 40-41.)

 

While not an exact match, the claim categorically identifies allegedly improper conduct by the District acting through its trustees. Given such contracts are paid with taxpayer funds provided to the District to manage under its authority, any and all claims can arise for both recovery of personal damages, as well as a taxpayer claim. For purposes of the demurrer, the court therefore finds the later articulated claim for taxpayer relief sufficiently arises from the claim against both the district and the trustees. The demurrer is overruled on this basis. In ruling on the subject issue for purposes of the demurrer, however, nothing precludes later, factual consideration. (Plata v. City of San Jose, supra, 74 Cal.App.5th at p. 749.)

 

Time Barred

Defendants contend the “claim” on the fourth cause of action for Taxpayer Breach of Fiduciary Duty is barred. The position depends on a finding that any purported breach of fiduciary duty occurred no later than January 2018, when Del Terra became the project manager. Defendants also reference a “Settlement Agreement” on January 15, 2021. [Sec. Amend. Comp., ¶ 22.] Plaintiff in opposition contend the taxpayer liability claim actually accrued in 2022, when the Del Terra parties were awarded a new contract with a 14% premium increase over project value. [Sec. Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 24, 55.]

 

The argument depends on the claim filing deadlines, which were previously found premature, but timely with the filing of the first amended complaint. The court therefore declines to find the cause of action barred under this standard.

 

The demurrer otherwise lacks actual address of the underlying, applicable statutes of limitations for the individual cause of action, which range as long as four years for the breach of contract claim. Plaintiff on the other hand presents discussion of delayed discovery yet the operative complaint lacks any such allegations of specific dates. [Sec. Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 24, 55.]

 

Assuming a potential accrual date “sometime in or around 2022,” an argument can be made for no lapse of the statute of limitations assuming a potential four year statute of limitations. The court declines to infer any and all dates based on the limited legal support and unfound allegations of dates, notwithstanding the parties citation to the operative complaint along with other unclear sections. [See Sec. Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 18, 23, 24, 55.]

 

Given the lack of sufficiently supported argument and the burden on Defendants to challenge the operative allegations, the court overrules the demurrer. Again, the ruling only applies to the subject demurrer, and in no way bars later, more articulated consideration of the issue.

 

Sufficiency and Government Immunity

Defendants challenge the sufficiency of the challenged causes of action, and maintain immunity applies.

 

The court previously overruled the demurrer on sufficiency and immunity. Defendants present no basis for reconsideration or justification for not raising any potentially new arguments. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (b); Bennett v. Suncloud (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 91, 96-97.) The demurrer is overruled.

 

In summary, the demurrer is OVERRULED in its entirety.

 

Due to the concurrent demurrer of the Del Terra parties, should Plaintiffs not file a third amended complaint, the court orders defendants to answer the second amended complaint within 40 days of the Del Terra demurrer order (e.g. 30 days leave to amend was granted on the negligence cause of action, with a 10 day period to answer upon the lapse of the amendment deadline, if applicable). If Plaintiffs elect to proceed with a third amended complaint, Defendants are also ordered to answer said complain within 10 days unless Plaintiff adds any new unauthorized addition of new causes of action or allegations subject to a motion to strike or potential demurrer.

 

Moving Defendants to give notice to all parties.
