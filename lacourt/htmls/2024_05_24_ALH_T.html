DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV00377    Hearing Date: May 24, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Deposition and Request for Sanctions

Moving Party:                         Defendant Ronald Smothers

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Jason Mota

BACKGROUND

On April 12, 2024, Defendant filed this motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition and an order imposing sanctions. On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On May 17, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure, section 2025.450 provides that “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it . . . the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(a).) “Any party served with a deposition notice . . . waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.” (C.C.P. § 2025.410.)

CCP section¿2025.450(b) provides:¿“A motion under subdivision (a)… shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”¿ (Id., § 2025.450(b).)  

If a motion under CCP section¿2025.450(a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Id., § 450(g)(1).) 

DISCUSSION

On March 1, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with a notice of taking his deposition, which was scheduled for May 23, 2023. (Mullen Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. A.) At Plaintiff’s request, the deposition scheduled on May 23, 2023, was rescheduled to August 7, 2023. (Id., ¶ 3; Exh. B.) Again, at Plaintiff’s request, the deposition scheduled on August 7, 2023, was taken off calendar. (Id., ¶ 4.) On October 11, 2023, Defendant unilaterally rescheduled Plaintiff’s deposition to October 25, 2023, because Plaintiff did not respond to Defendant’s multiple attempts to obtain a new deposition date, and served notice on Plaintiff to appear for deposition. (Id., ¶ 4; Exh. C.) The deposition did not go forward as Plaintiff’s counsel had lost communication with Plaintiff and, as a result, filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. (Id., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s Motion was granted on November 27, 2023. (Id., ¶ 6.)

Defendant argues that Plaintiff decided to pursue the case again in March 2024. Plaintiff’s counsel associated back into the case on March 11, 2024. (Id., ¶ 7.) The court denied Defendant’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions on March 25, 2024. On March 27, 2024, counsel for both parties agreed to schedule Plaintiff’s deposition for April 12, 2024, and Defendant served another notice of taking Plaintiff’s deposition. (Id., ¶ 9; Exh. D.) On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff requested to move the deposition to April 11, 2024, and Defendant served yet another notice of taking Plaintiff’s deposition on Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 10; Exh. E.)

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff appeared via Zoom, along with Plaintiff’s counsel Jeffrey Bloeser. Defendant Smothers and Defendant’s counsel Amy Mullen also appeared. (Id., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff’s counsel refused to move forward with the deposition because Defendant was in attendance via Zoom. Defendant had his camera off and was on mute. Plaintiff refused to move forward with the deposition unless Defendant logged off. (Id.) Defendant Smothers is a party to this action and is entitled to be present for the deposition. (Id.) Due to Plaintiff’s refusal, an Affidavit of Non-Appearance was taken by the Court Reporter. (Id., Exh. F.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s Counsel had a breakdown in communication, thus Plaintiff’s counsel was unable to move forward with the First Noticed deposition scheduled for May 23, 2023 or the Second Noticed deposition scheduled for August 7, 2023. (Bloeser Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff then reached out to his former Plaintiff’s counsel and explained he had done military service and did not provide his attorney’s office sufficient contact information. As a result of this explanation, Plaintiff’s counsel substituted back into the case and resumed the litigation. (Id., ¶ 5.)

Plaintiff was scheduled to appear at his deposition on April 11, 2024, but was surprised that Defendant was making an appearance, and thus he did not feel comfortable moving forward with his deposition. (Id., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff’s counsel suspended the deposition and relayed that it would have been common courtesy to allow Plaintiff’s counsel to prepare Plaintiff for the presence of the Defendant, however would be willing to “resume the deposition or schedule for a later date.” (Id., ¶ 8.) Defendant’s counsel was unwilling to resume or reschedule and already decided she was going to file a motion to compel. (Id., ¶ 9; Exh. 2.)

As Plaintiff was properly served with the notice of deposition, did not object under Section 2025.410, and failed to proceed with it, the motion to compel Plaintiff to appear for deposition is granted.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear for deposition within 20 days of this order.

Defendant requests $660 in sanctions under Section 450(g)(1). As the motion is granted, the request for sanctions is also granted, because Plaintiff has not shown why the imposition of sanctions would be unjust or any substantial justification in his refusal to proceed with the deposition.

First, the Court notes that Defendant was entitled to be present at the deposition. The Discovery Act does not specifically state who may or may not attend a deposition. But it recognizes that parties and their counsel have the right to be there. This is implicit in the statute dealing with protective orders which allows the court to exclude from a deposition “designated persons, other than the parties to the action and their officers and counsel.” (CCP § 2025.420(b)(12).) A party cannot be barred from attending another's deposition even on a showing that the deponent will feel “intimidated” by the party's presence at the deposition. (Willoughby v. Sup.Ct. (Lui) (1985) 172 CA3d 890, 892.) For this reason, on this record, the Court finds that the deposition was improperly suspended.

Although Plaintiff’s counsel has shown he met and conferred with defense counsel after the suspended deposition, the Court notes that Defendant has been attempting to take Plaintiff’s deposition since March 2023, for over a year. This case was filed in January 2022, yet Plaintiff’s deposition has still not been taken, almost two and a half years later. While Plaintiff’s counsel offers an excuse that Plaintiff was in military service, there is no reason why Plaintiff could not have communicated this to his counsel before leaving for military service, rather than stringing his counsel and the defense along for almost a year with no communication whatsoever.

Further, although Plaintiff’s counsel states that Plaintiff did not provide him with sufficient contact information before going to military service-- in connection with Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions, Plaintiff’s counsel stated that Plaintiff did not provide his contact information to his attorney’s office before going to miliary service. (3/25/24 Minute Order; Plaintiff’s 3/19/24 opposition.) In light of this history, defense counsel’s reluctance in setting another deposition of Plaintiff without court intervention was entirely reasonable. Defense counsel justifiably states she is not willing to expend the time and cost on another deposition without the reassurance of a Court order that Plaintiff will comply with the Discovery Act and provide his deposition testimony without meritless objections or other roadblocks.

As a result, the Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and counsel of record in the amount of $660 ($200 per hour, for 3 hours, plus $60 filing fee), jointly and severally, payable within 20 days to Defense counsel.

CONCLUSION

Defendant Ronald Smothers’ motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is GRANTED.  Plaintiff Jason Mota is ordered to appear for deposition within 20 days of Defendant giving notice of this order.

Plaintiff Jason Mota and counsel of record Jeffrey Bloeser of Downtown L.A. Law Group are ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $660, jointly and severally, to Defense counsel within 30 days of defendant giving notice of this order.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
