DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV21084    Hearing Date: August 14, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER

Hearing Date: 8/14/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV21084 GALINA KOPELEV vs CHURCHILL CONDOMINIUM ASSOCIATION

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Defendant HOA Organizers, Inc

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER IS REFERRED TO DISCOVERY REFEREE

 

Plaintiff, in pro per, seeks to compel further responses to Plaintiff's special interrogatories, set one and two; form interrogatories, set one; requests for admissions, set one; and requests for production, set one and two. As a preliminary matter, a single motion to compel further should be limited to one set of discovery responses. Plaintiff requests to compel two sets of discovery responses in each motion. For example, Plaintiff requests further responses to both form interrogatories and requests for admission in one motion. Combining multiple motions under the guise of one motion with one hearing reservation manipulates the Court Reservation System and unfairly jumps ahead of other litigants. Moreover, combining motions deprives the Court of filing fees it is otherwise entitled to collect. While the Court would forgive this technicality and consider the substance of the multiple requests within one motion, it does not address the substance herein due to procedural issues as discussed below.

CCP § 2031.310(b)(2) states the motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040; no declaration was attached to the motions.  Plaintiff is hereby advised that the Court will hold her to the same standards as a party with counsel – so, if a meet and confer statement is required, she is to provide one.

Having read the various positions in this case, the Court finds that Defendant’s request for the Court to appoint a discovery referee is appropriate and necessary.  Having reviewed Defendant’s proposed referee list, the Court appoints Ann Kough under the terms set forth by Defendant.  If for some reason, Judge Kough (Ret.) is not available, the Court appoints Gary Nadler. At the point that the $10,000 cap is reached, the parties shall no longer be subject to the referee appointment.  The motions at issue here are referred to the referee for recommendation – the Court believes that addressing these motions through a referee, who can spend time with the parties to address the various issues raised, will be the most beneficial use of a referee, thereby setting the stage for the litigation to continue most efficiently.  

The Court will further address these issues with the parties.

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

Hearing Date: 8/14/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV40586 STATE OF CALIFORNIA vs ARNOLDO RODRIGUEZ

Moving Party: Plaintiff State of California

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL IS GRANTED 

 

This consolidated workers' compensation subrogation and personal injury action arises from a three-vehicle, rear-end collision that occurred on November 20, 2019, in Los Angeles, California. The State of California filed its complaint on November 3, 2021, and Maria Hernandez Luna filed her complaint on November 12, 2021. The actions were consolidated on June 13, 2022. The parties have signed a stipulation to continue the trial to sometime in January 2025 due to a second mediation scheduled for November 2024 and counsel’s unavailability in December 2024. The Court finds good cause to continue the trial given that the mediation is a second effort and the mediator is unavailable until November. However, given the age of this case and that this will be the fourth trial continuance, no further continuances will be granted.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 8/14/23

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV00877 SHARITA RANDLESTON vs NICOLE LORRAINE

Moving Party: Defendant Kaiser 

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION IS GRANTED

 

Background

Defendant Nicole Linton worked as a nurse for Defendant AMN Healthcare,[1] who placed Plaintiff at Kaiser West LA as a temporary nurse. While in that role, Linton was not authorized to perform any work outside of Kaiser West LA or at any other Kaiser location.

At approximately 1:00 p.m. on August 4, 2022, Linton left in the middle of her shift without informing her supervisors. Linton was neither asked to leave the premises nor instructed to perform any errands or tasks by her supervisors. Linton drove away from the premises in her personal vehicle and allegedly caused a multi-vehicle collision over four miles away when she went through a red light. This collision led to several deaths, including Plaintiff’s daughter. Plaintiff is suing Defendant Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (“KFHP”) for (1) negligence, (2) wrongful death, and (3) negligent entrustment and negligent retention and supervision.

KFHP now moves the Court for summary adjudication on the causes of action for negligence and wrongful death, arguing that Linton was not acting within the scope of her employment at the time of the incident under the “going and coming rule.” KFHP also moves the Court for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s negligent entrustment claim, contending that Defendant KFHP did not own or possess the vehicle, an element Plaintiff must prove to prevail under this theory of recovery.

Legal Standard

        Motion for Summary Adjudication

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) 

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

 

Going and Coming Rule 

“‘Under the ‘going and coming’ rule, an employee going to and from work is ordinarily considered outside the scope of employment so that the employer is not liable for his torts.’”  (Halliburton Energy Services, Inc. v. Department of Transportation (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 87, 95-96 (quoting Hinman v. Westinghouse Electric Co. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 956, 961).)  “The rationale for the rule is that the employment relationship is suspended from the time the employee leaves work until he or she returns because an employee ordinarily renders no service to the employer while traveling.”  (Pierson v. Helmerich & Payne Intern’l. Drilling Co. (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 608, 618.)

Courts have recognized exceptions to the “going and coming” Rule.  (Marez v. Lyft, Inc. (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 569, 578; Lantz v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 298, 309; Halliburton Energy Services, Inc. v. Department of Transportation (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 87, 96.)   One such exception arguably applicable here is the “special risk” exception.

        Under the “special risk” exception, “an injury is compensable if, before entry upon the premises, an employee suffers injury from a special risk causally related to employment.”  (Santa Rosa Junior College v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 345, 353.)  “[T]he exception will apply (1) if ‘but for’ the employment the employee would not have been at the location where the injury occurred and (2) if ‘the risk is distinctive in nature or quantitatively greater than risks common to the public.’”  (Id. at 354 (quoting Gen. Ins. Co. v. Workers’ Comp. App. Bd. (Chairez) (1976) 16 Cal.3d 595, 600).) 

Evidentiary Ruling

The Court overrules Plaintiff's Objections Nos. 1-6. The Parties did not make any other objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)

Discussion

Defendant’s Initial Burden

Defendant KFHP’s evidence establishes the following:

·        During her assignment, Linton reported only to the West LA Hospital. (SSUMF ¶ 1.)

·        Nothing in Ms. Linton’s job description required her to perform any work outside of the West LA Hospital, and she was never asked or expected to do any such work. (Id. at ¶ 2.)

·        At no point prior to the collision had Linton been assigned to report to any other KFHP location. (Id. at ¶ 3.)

·        KFHP  did not provide Linton—and does not provide nurses generally—a vehicle, nor was Linton required to use a vehicle to accomplish her work at the West LA Hospital. (Id. at ¶¶ 4-6.)

·        KFHP did not require Linton to use any particular method of transportation to commute to and from the West LA Hospital. (Id. at ¶ 9.)

·        Defendant did not assign a company vehicle to Linton (SSUMF ¶¶ 4-6)

·        On August 4, 2022, Ms. Linton’s only responsibility at the West LA Hospital was the care of a few patients. (SSUMF ¶ 70.)

·        During the afternoon of August 4, around 1:00 p.m., Linton left the West LA Hospital without informing her supervisors that she was leaving. Neither Ms. Okorie nor Arturo Lasquete, the charge nurse who provided on-the-floor supervision, asked Linton to do anything off-site, and neither knew where she had gone. (Id. at ¶¶ 66-69.)

·        Linton typically worked three days a week, for a total of 36 hours. (Id. at ¶ 56.)

·        During the week of the collision, Linton’s first 12-hour shift was on August 1, 2022. (Id. at ¶ 57.)

·        After a day and a half off, Ms. Linton began her second 12-hour shift on August 3, which ended around 7:30 p.m. (Id. at ¶ 58.)

·        The next morning, her third shift started around 7:00 a.m. (Id. at ¶ 59.) Linton then worked just over six hours before abruptly leaving the West LA Hospital sometime after 1:00 p.m. (Id. at ¶ 60.)

·        After Linton left Kaiser West LA, she was not performing any work, duties, or errands for KFHP and her patients had to be reassigned to other nurses. (SSUMF ¶¶ 68, 70.)

·        The incident leading to this lawsuit occurred approximately four miles away from the Kaiser West LA Hospital, at the intersection of Slauson Avenue and La Brea Avenue in Los Angeles, at approximately 1:30 p.m., on August 4, 2022.

Through this evidence, Defendant KFHP met its initial prima facia burden to establish a defense under the goings and comings doctrine to the negligence and wrongful death causes of action.  Linton had left work at the time of the incident. She was not performing any errands or tasks as requested by Defendant, nor was she going to another Kaiser location as requested by her supervisor or required by her work. Defendant also showed that Linton had plenty of rest prior to her shift on the date of the accident where she left without telling her supervisor and got into the accident at issue by driving through a red light.

        Defendant also met its burden to establish that Plaintiff cannot meet at least one element of the negligent entrustment cause of actions: Defendant has presented evidence demonstrating that the car operated was owned and possessed solely by Plaintiff. Defendant did not assign a company vehicle to Plaintiff (SSUMF ¶¶ 4-6), nor did Defendant require Plaintiff to use any particular method of transportation for commuting (SSUMF ¶ 9).

Plaintiff’s Opposition

Coming and Going Rule

Plaintiff appears to argue that Defendant did not meet its burden regarding the special risk exception to the going and coming rule, relying on Purton v. Marriott International, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 499, in which the court held that an employer may be found liable for its employee's torts as long as the proximate cause of the injury occurred within the scope of employment.

Plaintiff first argues that Defendant did not meet its burden of proof because the evidence does not show where Linton was headed when she drove her car and got into an accident. This argument is not persuasive because Defendant has demonstrated that Linton was not asked to perform any off-site duties or errands by Defendant at the time of the incident. In fact, Defendant’s evidence establishes that contingency hires are not allowed to perform any off-site tasks or work for any other Kaiser locations. When Linton left the premises on the date of the incident, she did so without informing her supervisor, who consequently had no idea where she went. Although Defendant did not show where Plaintiff was headed, it did show that she was leaving work and was not performing any tasks for Defendant.

Plaintiff then draws a comparison to Purton v. Marriott International, Inc., supra, 218 Cal.App.4th 499, and argues that Linton’s supervisors, who are all educated, had expressed concerns relating to Plaintiff’s alleged underperformance both the day prior to and on the day of the incident. Despite this, not only was Linton allowed to leave the hospital without question, but her below-standard performance and failing mental state were not addressed prior to her departure.

This analysis overlooks the central point of Purton: the injury was caused by drinking alcoholic beverages, an activity that had become a customary incident of the employment relationship in that case. The allegation that Plaintiff’s employer did not prevent Plaintiff from leaving is irrelevant if the accident was not proximately caused by an 'outgrowth' of the employment. The risk of tortious injury must be "inherent in the working environment" or "typical of or broadly incidental to the enterprise [the employer] has undertaken.” (Depew v. Crocodile Enterprises, Inc. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 480, 488-89.)

Furthermore, the argument that Linton was allowed to leave the hospital without question, and her below-standard performance and failing mental state were not addressed prior to her departure, pertains to Plaintiff’s claims of negligent retention, supervision, and hiring, which are not the subjects of this  motion. The issue of vicarious liability is not focused on what should reasonably be avoided, but rather on the more or less inevitable toll of a lawful enterprise, as articulated in Hinman v. Westinghouse Electric Co. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 956, 960.

What Defendant has demonstrated is that Linton was working approximately three days a week and had a day and a half off prior to her shift on the date of the incident. Under a similar set of facts in Depew v. Crocodile Enterprises, Inc., supra., the court found an insufficient causal nexus between the employment and the resultant death. Specifically, the court ruled that a break of 16 hours between shifts, followed by six hours of work, does not constitute the type of excessive workload that makes falling asleep at the wheel and killing another driver "a generally foreseeable consequence" of operating a restaurant. Similarly, here, nothing inherent in the working environment or incidental to the enterprise, such as the working hours of the job, was the proximate cause of the incident.

Triable Issue of Fact

Plaintiff attempts to raise a triable issue of fact by pointing to the presence of a work cell phone as an indication that Linton was performing an errand or some work-related task for Defendant at the time of the incident. Once Defendant has met its initial burden by demonstrating that no work-related task was performed by Plaintiff at the time of the incident, the onus is on Plaintiff to present affirmative evidence. To raise a triable issue of fact, Plaintiff needs to set forth specific facts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) If Plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted. (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) Evidence that only gives rise to mere speculation is similarly ineffective to establish a triable issue of fact. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.) Nor can the opposing party create a triable issue by attacking the credibility of the moving party’s evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c.)

Without further evidence, the connection between the presence of a work phone and the allegation that Linton was performing an errand for Defendant is speculative and does not raise a triable issue of fact. Had there been a text message on the work cell phone from Plaintiff’s supervisor instructing her to perform certain errands, the situation might have been different. However, the mere presence of a work cell phone, without more, does not establish a triable issue of fact.

Furthermore, Plaintiff points to Defendant's alleged awareness of Linton’s shortcomings and mental instability the day prior to the incident. However, this does not raise a triable issue of fact for a finding of vicarious liability. In Purton v. Marriott International, Inc., the court established that an employee’s intoxication contributed to an accident because the consumption of alcohol occurred at a company event which had become a customary incident of the employment relationship. Similarly, to advance a comparable argument in this case, it must be shown that Linton's unusual behavior stemmed from conditions related to her employment.

In the moving papers, Defendant has demonstrated that Linton’s alleged strange behavior was not due to factors typically associated with employment, such as long, consecutive work hours. Consequently, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to present affirmative evidence that connects Linton's strange behavior to her employment conditions and shows how this behavior approximately caused the accident at issue.

Plaintiff has not done so. Plaintiff did not identify any specific aspect of Linton’s employment that could have contributed to her alleged strange behavior, nor show how such conditions would be considered an inherent outgrowth of her employment. Furthermore, there is no clear explanation of how these alleged strange behavior or poor work performance caused the traffic accident. Without addressing these crucial factors, Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact regarding Kaiser’s liability for negligence or wrongful death.

Job Abandonment

Even if an exception to the going and coming rule is found to be applicable, the employee may yet be found outside the scope of his employment if, at the time of the accident, he has completely abandoned his employer's business for personal reasons. (Felix v. Asai (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 926, 932–33; Loper v. Morrison (1944) 23 Cal.2d 600, 604-607.) To constitute an abandonment, however, the deviation or departure from the employer's business to pursue a personal errand must be substantial and complete. (Trejo v. Maciel, supra, 239 Cal.App.2d at p. 496.) A mere deviation for personal reasons will be insufficient. Where the employee may be deemed to be pursuing a business errand and a personal objective simultaneously, he will still be acting within the scope of his employment. In determining whether an employee has completely abandoned the pursuit of a business errand for a personal objective, a variety of relevant circumstances should be considered and weighed, such as the intent of the employee, the nature, time, and place of the employee's conduct, the work the employee was hired to do, the incidental acts the employer should reasonably have expected the employee to do, the amount of freedom allowed the employee in performing his duties, and the amount of time consumed in the personal activity. (Felix v. Asai (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 926, 932–33.)

Based on evidence submitted by Defendant, the court finds that defendant has met its burden in showing that Linton abandoned her job. First, Linton left abruptly in the middle of her shift, did not inform her supervisor, and as a result her supervisor had to have other nurses cover her station as a result of her departure. Second, Linton was not asked to perform any errands on the date of the incident, nor was she authorized to perform any off site nursing duties or work at any other kaiser locations. Plaintiff argues that there is no evidence indicating where Linton was going, why she left, how long she had been away from the West Los Angeles hospital, or what her intent was in leaving. While it is true that Defendant did not present any evidence regarding Linton’s actual intentions or destination, the evidence presented does demonstrate that she left Kaiser for reasons related to Kaiser.

Whether or not Plaintiff was still on the clock at the time of the incident or should receive payment for the time leading up to and as of the collision is not considered by the court in Felix v. Asai, nor has Plaintiff introduced any legal authority to demonstrate its relevance to the issue of whether Plaintiff abandoned her job. Based on the foregoing analysis, the court is not persuaded by Plaintiff’s arguments and has determined that Defendant has successfully met its burden. Plaintiff solely contests job abandonment on the basis that Defendant failed to meet its burden and did not attempt to raise a triable issue of fact on this matter.

Thus, Summary Adjudication as to the Plaintiff’s first and second cause of action is GRANTED.

Negligent Entrustment

Plaintiff does not contest Defendant’s postion as to Plaintiff’s theory of recovery for negligent entrustment. Defendant has presented evidence demonstrating that the car operated was owned and possessed solely by Plaintiff. Defendant did not assign a company vehicle to Plaintiff (SSUMF ¶¶ 4-6), nor did Defendant require Plaintiff to use any particular method of transportation for commuting (SSUMF ¶ 9). Plaintiff does not dispute this and did not raise a triable issue of fact. Therefore, summary adjudication is GRANTED as to this issue.
