DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV00335    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: W

NPI DEBT FUND I, LP v. ROBERT BRARD, et al.

 

PLAINTIFF’S motion for summary judgment

 

Date of Hearing:        February 23, 2024                                        Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        21VECV00335

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff NPI Debt Fund I, LP

Responding Party:     Defendants Belle Maison Partners, Inc. and Robert Brard

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff alleges Defendant Belle Maison Partners and Arch CBT SPE, LLC entered into an agreement by which Arch CBT SPE, LLC agreed to loan Defendant Belle some monies. Defendant Brard guaranteed the loan. Plaintiff further alleges Defendants defaulted on the loan and the loan was assigned to Plaintiff.

 

On March 11, 2021, Plaintiff NPI Debt Fund I, LP filed a complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Goods Sold and Delivered; (3) Book Account; and (4) Account Stated.

 

This action is related to 21VECV00329 (NPI Debt Fund I, LP v. Brard, et al.) and 21VECV00333 (NPI Debt Fund I, LP v. Property Cowboys, LLC, et al.)

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of the Deed of Trust on the property commonly known as 4511 Dulcinea Court, Woodlands Hills, CA 91364 (the “DOT”) securing the Loan Agreement between Defendant Belle Maison Partners, Inc. (“Belle”) and Plaintiff in the Official Records of Los Angeles County with Recorder Number 20180415419. (RJN, Exh. 4.)

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves the court for summary judgment on the complaint against Defendant Belle Maison Partners, Inc. and Robert Brard upon the ground there is no triable issue of material fact.

 

¿A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle him/her to judgment as a matter of law. (CCP § 437c(c).) The moving party must make an affirmative showing that he/she is entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition. (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742- 743.) Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.). When a plaintiff seeks summary judgment, he/she must produce admissible evidence on each element of each cause of action on which judgment is sought. (CCP § 437c(p)(1).) The moving party’s “affidavits must cite evidentiary facts, not legal conclusions or ‘ultimate’ facts” and the courts must construe the evidence in support of the opposing party, resolving any doubts in favor of the opposing party. (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639; Scalf, 128 Cal.App.4th at 1519; Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  

 

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets his or her initial movant’s burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.) Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial. (CCP § 437c(p).) The opposing party may not simply rely on his/her allegations to show a triable issue but must present evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162.) Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  

 

Plaintiff presents evidence a contract was made between Defendant Belle Maison Partners, Inc. (“Belle”) and third-party Arch CBT SPE, LLC on April 25, 2018 (the “Loan Agreement”). (UMF 1.) Defendant Brard signed a Guaranty Agreement on behalf of Defendant Belle. (UMF 2.) The Loan Agreement was secured by a Deed of Trust on certain real property. (UMF 3.) Arch disbursed $1,068,000.00 to Defendant Belle pursuant to the Loan Agreement. (UMF 4.) Thereafter, Arch assigned its interest under the Loan Agreement and DOT to Plaintiff on December 10, 2019. (UMF 5.) By virtue of the assignment, Defendants Bella and Brard became indebted to Plaintiff in the amount owed under the Loan Agreement and Guaranty. (UMF 6, 7.) Pursuant to the agreement, Defendant Belle was to make 12 equal payments of $10,680.00 between June 1, 2018 and May 1, 2019 and Defendant Belle was to pay back the entirety of the remaining loan balance (“Balloon Payment”) on May 1, 2019. (UMF 8, 9.) Payments made by Defendants are reflected in the written payment history. (UMF 10.)

 

Plaintiff further presents evidence that Defendants breached the Loan Agreement and Guaranty by failing to make the Balloon Payment and failing to date to pay the outstanding amounts owed under the Loan Agreement. (UMF 11, 12.) After crediting payments to Defendant Belle’s account, there remains a balance of $510,257.63 which has not been paid, not including interest. (UMF 13.)  Plaintiff has met its burden of proof. 

 

Defendants initially presented no evidence to oppose the motion for summary judgment.   In a separate untimely filing on February 13, they submit a single piece of evidence, the declaration of  Robert Brard.  That declaration cryptically states that prior to the loan, Brard set up a limited liability company.   The declaration does not properly authenticate an equally cryptic text message attached to his declaration, nor explain how this fragment of information negates any of the evidence set forth in the moving papers.  Plaintiff does not present any evidence in its opposition to the separate statement.   Based on this incomplete and inscrutable declaration, plaintiff argues that Belle was created solely for the purposes of acquiring title to the Property and executing the promissory note, the proceeds of which funded Belle’s acquisition of the Property. Defendants contend Belle had and has no other business except for its obligations to Plaintiffs and the related Loans; except for the Property, Belle had and has no other assets and no other liabilities; Belle has never generated any income or revenue; the Property has never generated any rental income or other revenue; any funds in the Belle bank account were deposited as the result occasional loans made to the company; Belle is and was exclusively controlled by Defendants; Belle has no employees or other depository accounts; Belle never conducted any other business; Plaintiffs knew the subject property was owner occupied. Defendants contend taken all together, these factors lead to the reasonably conclusion that Defendants were not “true guarantors” of the note but rather, were the principal obligors.  As such, the Guaranty is a sham. As the defacto borrowers, Defendants are entitled to the protections of California’s anti-deficiency statutes and usury laws and those statutes necessitate dismissal of the Complaint. None of these arguments are supported by evidence and so cannot create a triable issue of fact.  In  the separate statement, “[c]itation to the evidence in support of each material fact must include reference to the exhibit, title, page, and line numbers.” (Cal. R. Ct., 3.1350(d)(3); see also Zimmerman, Rosenfeld, Gersh & Leeds LLP v. Larson (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 1466, 1477 [“if it is not set forth in the separate statement, it does not exist.”'].) Failure to cite to evidence in the opposing separate statement is grounds for granting summary judgment. (Kojababian v. Genuine Horne Loans, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal. 3 App.4th 408.) Under CRC 3.1350(f)(2) citation to the evidence in support of the position that a fact is controverted must include reference to the exhibit, title, page, and line numbers.  Defendants have utterly failed to comply with these requirements.

 

Defendants separately raise argument that Plaintiffs seek damages from Defendants to which they are not legally entitled under California law. Defendants contend an interest rate in excess of 10% is usurious, and if a lender negotiates a loan at a usurious rate absent a qualified exemption, the agreement shall be void and the lender will have no action at law to recover any interest. (Civ. Code § 1916-2.) The Loan Agreement here references the loan as a “Promissory Note (Business Purpose Loan).”  Plaintiff has  presented undisputed evidence that plaintiff is a private money lender whose loans are exempt because arranged by a licensed California real estate Broker.   Defendants do not present any evidence to counter this information.   The court finds that the loan is not usurious as a matter of law.  

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. 


Case Number: 22VECV01458    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: W

KIMBERLY DAVIS, et al. v. PAIGE HEMMIS, et al.

 

DEFENDANT PAIGE HEMMIS' MOTION TO PARTIALLY QUASH DEPOSITION SUBPOENA FOR PRODUCTION OF BUSINESS RECORDS TO YAHOO, INC.

 

Date of Hearing:        February 23, 2024                 Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV01458

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis filed a third amended complaint against Defendants Paige Hemmis, Jason Short, Ameka Benton, Scott Fields, Hallmark Cards, Inc., Crown Media Holdings, Inc., Paige Hemmis, Inc., Aria Music Group, The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners Association, Melissa Improta, Jason Improta, and Compass California, Inc. for 1) Intentional Interference with Contract; 2) Negligence; 3) Trespass; 4) Violations of Civil Code § 1798.93; 5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6) Professional Negligence; 7) Unfair Business Practices; 8) Fraudulent Concealment; 9) Negligent Misrepresentation; 10) Fraud; 11) Slander of Title; 12) Constructive Fraud; 13) Wrongful Eviction; 14) Quiet Title; and 15) Conversion.

 

Plaintiffs allege about September 15, 2019, when Plaintiffs were absent from the subject property, Defendants Hemmis and Short unlawfully entered upon the subject property and remained in possession of the property until early July 2022. While Defendants Hemmis and Short were in possession of the property, Plaintiffs were engaged in a dispute with their mortgage lender, US Bank, related to a settlement agreement that was entered into to address the pay off of a mortgage loan. Plaintiffs further allege at some point prior to September 12, 2019, Defendants Hemmis, Short, Improta and Compass engaged in a conspiracy to attempt to steal title to the Plaintiffs’ home. On this point, Plaintiffs claim Hemmis and Short fraudulently forged a lease that purported to have Kimberly Davis' signature on it. As a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged. Plaintiff dismissed Defendants Ameka Benton and Scott Fields.

 

On March 24, 2023, this action was related to Kimberly Davis v. Nationstar, et al. (23VECV01125).

 

On August 18, 2023, Paige Hemmis and Jason Short filed a cross-complaint against Kimberly Ann Davis, Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta, and Jason Improta for breach of contract, money had and received, implied indemnification, express indemnification, and comparative contribution. Cross-Complainants dismissed their cause of action for express indemnification.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Paige Hemmis’ Motion to Partially Quash Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records to Yahoo, Inc. is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of several court documents in other cases as well as the cross-complaint and answer to cross-complaint filed in the instant action. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Paige Hemmis moves the court for an order partially quashing the deposition subpoena for production of business records issued by Plaintiffs to Yahoo, Inc. and seeks additional orders to protect Defendant from unreasonable and oppressive demands including violations of the right of privacy of the person. Defendant Hemmis also seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $7,120.00.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section¿1987.1(a)¿states, “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.¿ In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” 

 

A deposition subpoena may be quashed for:¿(1) defects in form or content of the subpoena (e.g., inadequate description of requested records); (2) defects in service of the subpoena (e.g., failure to satisfy the requirements of providing notice to consumer; (3) requesting¿production of records not within the permissible scope of discovery; and (4) being unjustly burdensome or oppressive.¿ (Weil & Brown,¿Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶¿8:598.)¿

 

Defendant Hemmis moves to partially quash the deposition subpoena on the grounds it is overly broad, not reasonably limited in its scope or duration and is unduly burdensome and harrassive. Defendant Hemmis contends the deposition subpoena seeks documents for an unlimited duration. However, the time period based on the allegations of the operative pleading would be, at most, from in or about August 1, 2019 until such time defendant Hemmis vacated the Property in or about July 2022 (although Defendant Hemmis believes it should be further limited to September 12, 2019 when the lease was executed).

 

In opposition[1], Plaintiff argues the IP address information is relevant because if any of Defendant Hemmis’ IP information at any point from January 1, 2018 to present matches either of the two email addresses that have now been linked to the kadavislaw1967@gmail.com email or the DocuSign certificate produced by Compass, that would be highly relevant to establishing that Defendant Hemmis was the one who sent e-mails, and forged, the electronically-signed lease. Moreover, Plaintiff contends Defendant Hemmis does not identify any harm, or privilege, that she has in preventing the Plaintiff from knowing the information sought in this subpoena.

 

The court finds the scope of the subpoena does go beyond Plaintiff’s allegations. Even with the Third Amended Complaint, the relevant allegations do not support discovery into the issue at hand beyond August 1, 2019 to July 31, 2022. Defendant does not dispute Plaintiff has presented evidence why the discovery into the IP Addresses sought is relevant; however, Plaintiff has not presented evidence as to why anything beyond July 2022 is relevant. Videos continuing to be posted by Hallmark’s social media account is not relevant to whether or not the IP addresses match the Google Account or the DocuSign certificate.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Paige Hemmis’ Motion to Partially Quash Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records to Yahoo, Inc. is GRANTED.

 

The request for sanctions is denied as both parties’ positions were substantially justified. 


  


Case Number: 23VECV03599    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: W

SYLVIA MORA v. ROCK STAR PROPERTIES LLC DBA AGWC ROCKIN' RESCUE, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        February 23, 2024                             Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03599

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Sylvia Mora

Responding Party:     Defendants Rock Star Properties, LLC, Ady Gil and Fabienne Origer

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 17, 2023, Plaintiff Sylivia Mora filed a complaint against Defendants Rock Star Properties, LLC dba AGWC Rockin’ Rescue, Ady Gil and Fabienne Origer for negligence, strict liability, and punitive damages. Plaintiff alleges on December 24, 2022, Plaintiff was a volunteer at Rock Star Properties, LLC dba AGWC Rockin’ Rescue (“Rescue”). While Plaintiff was sitting on the sidewalk near the Rescue, one of Rescue’s Labrador dogs, viciously attacked Plaintiff, causing severe injuries to Plaintiff’s person including to her back, neck, arms, wrist, and hand along with nerve damage and loss of functionality.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves the court for an order granting leave to amend the complaint filed in this action on August 17, 2023 to add “Ady Gil World Conservation” as a defendant in place of the fictitious name “DOE 1”.

 

A complainant may obtain leave from the trial court to amend his pleading beyond the number of amendments allowed under Code of Civil Procedure section 472 (a) by filing a noticed motion.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1324.)¿ The motion must be accompanied by a declaration stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) why the request was not made earlier.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1324 (b).)¿¿¿¿

¿¿¿ 

“Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”¿ (CCP § 576.)  In the absence of a showing of prejudice from the opposing side, the trial court ordinarily lacks discretion to deny a motion to amend a pleading.¿ (Honig v. Financial Corp. of America (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 960, 965.)

 

Plaintiff moves to add “Ady Gil World Conservation” as a defendant in place of the fictitious name “DOE 1” on the grounds the need for the amendment arose after the depositions of Defendants Ady Gil and Fabienne Origer, where it was discovered that “Ady Gil World Conservation” is the correct name of the entity involved regarding ownership, maintenance, and control of the animal rescue in question. Plaintiff contends when they did a search for AGWC, the Secretary of State website does not reveal “Ady Gil World Conservation,” which contributed to the initial misidentification of the Defendant at the initial filing of the complaint.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue nothing in Code of Civil Procedure section 473(a) appears to allow a party to substitute the name of an existing party and thus skirt the Doe amendment procedure outlined in Code of Civil Procedure section 474. Defendants further argue the real reason Plaintiff has decided to file a motion is to throw a potential roadblock to AGWC later challenging the issue as to whether Mora was indeed “ignorant of the true name of the defendant (AGWC)” as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 474. Defendants contend Plaintiff knew the identity of AGWC as early as April 9, 2018 because she signed a signed a document captioned “indemnity waiver, release of liability and assumption of risk” concurrent with a volunteer questionnaire in which AGWC is expressly listed.

 

The court grants the motion for leave to amend to add “Ady Gil World Conservation” as DOE 1. Leave to amend is generally liberally granted, provided there is no statute of limitations concern. (Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 411.) The court may also deny the plaintiff’s leave to amend if there is prejudice to the opposing party, such as delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation. (Id.) There are no statute of limitations issues presented. Defendants have not presented any evidence of any prejudice from the proposed amendment. Plaintiffs contend there will be no deposition necessary of Ady Gil World Conservation and the only discovery will be an initial set of written discovery which will be due by the discovery deadline.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend complaint is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 23VECV04468    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: W

TWONGTIP PUNLERTPATHANAKON v. MULHOLLAND SECURITY CENTERS, LLC DBA MULHOLLAND BRAND MANUFACTURING, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel arbitration pursuant to arbitration agreement

 

Date of Hearing:        January 23 and February 23, 2024  Trial Date:       None

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04468

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Twongtip Punlertpathanakon

Responding Party:     Defendants Mulholland Security Centers LLC, Abraham Ben David, and Avi ‘Coby’ Jacoby  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wrongful termination case. Plaintiff Twongtip Punlertpathanakon began working for Defendants Mulholland Security Centers LLC dba Mulholland Brand Manufacturing, Abraham Ben David, and Avi ‘Coby’ Jacoby in July 2022. Plaintiff alleges despite her work ethic, Defendants treated Plaintiff differently than the other employees because she was not Israeli. Plaintiff alleges despite her excellent sales records, Defendants would give preferred    job sites to Israeli workers who had less experience than her and worse sale records than her. After Plaintiff made complaints to Defendants about the racism she regularly experienced at work, Plaintiff was terminated. On October 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants for 1. Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy (FEHA); 2. Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy (Failure to Pay Owed Wages); 3. Discrimination and Disparate Treatment Based on National Origin; 4. Harassment; 5. Failure to Prevent Harassment; 6. Failure to Pay Wages Owed; 7. Failure to Pay Accurate Itemized Wage Statements; 8. Waiting Time Penalties; 9. Violation of Business & Professions Code section 17200 et seq.; 10. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; 11. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; and 12. Liquidated Damages for Complaint Wage Theft and Failure to Pay Wages Plus Interest.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Arbitration Pursuant to Arbitration Agreement is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In reply, Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of Declaration of Corey A. Pingle in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion to Stay, Compel Arbitration and Issue Sanctions and/or Reasonable Costs Against Defendants. The court GRANTS the request for judicial notice as to the existence of the exhibit.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff moves this court for an order to stay proceedings and compel arbitration pursuant to written agreement of the parties to arbitrate any controversy or claim.

 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1281.2(a)-(b).) “Because the existence of the agreement is a statutory prerequisite to granting the petition, the petitioner bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)¿ 

¿ 

“California law, ‘like [federal law], reflects a strong policy favoring arbitration agreements and requires close judicial scrutiny of waiver claims.’”¿ (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 31.) If the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until arbitration is completed.¿ (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)¿ 

 

Plaintiff contends on July 20, 2023, Plaintiff and Defendants entered into an agreement to arbitrate any and all employment disputes. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1.) The Arbitration Agreement provides: “In the event of a dispute between the parties over the execution of the attached contract, the parties will waive their right to litigate these issues in court and instead will resolve their dispute through binding arbitration in Canoga Park, California.” (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1, ¶1.) Pursuant to the terms of the Arbitration Agreement, Plaintiff made a demand of arbitration on April 13, 2023. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 2.) However, Defendants failed to comply by failing to make the necessary payment to American Arbitration Association. (Pingle Decl. ¶7.)

 

Defendants oppose the motion. The court takes the time to note it seems peculiar that Defendants, who themselves drafted the Arbitration Agreement, now seek court intervention to deem the agreement unconscionable. Although the court will, in good faith, analyze Defendants’ unconscionability agreement, Defendant's argument is an unusual one.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues the Arbitration Agreement is not enforceable because it was never signed by the parties. The court disagrees. “[T]he writing memorializing an arbitration agreement need not be signed by both parties in order to be upheld as a binding arbitration agreement.”  (Serafin v. Balco Properties Lrd., LLC (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 165, 176.)  “ ‘[I]t is not the presence or absence of a signature [on an agreement] which is dispositive; it is the presence or absence of evidence of an agreement to arbitrate which matters.’ [Citation.]  Evidence confirming the existence of an agreement to arbitrate, despite an unsigned agreement, can be based, for example, on ‘conduct from which one could imply either ratification or implied acceptance of such a provision.’  [Citation.]”  (Ibid.)  In the present case, while Defendants did not execute the Arbitration Agreement, Defendants authored the Arbitration Agreement, and printed the Arbitration Agreement upon Defendants’ letterhead, and produced the agreement to Plaintiff as a term of employment. The court further notes the Arbitration Agreement does not require a signature. (See Gorlach v. Sports Club Co. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1497, 1509 holding “where an arbitration agreement requires a signature to be effective, courts have found that unsigned arbitration agreements are unenforceable.” 

 

At the initial hearing on January 23, 2024, the court requested that plaintiff submit a further declaration, explaining his understanding of the agreements given to him to sign, what he executed, and whether he believed that he was bound by the agreement.  Plaintiff has now done so.  The court finds his declaration persuasive that he believed that he was executing electronically every one of the agreements sent to him to sign, placing his electronic signature at each prompt.  Even if his electronic signature does not appear on the arbitration agreement, as it does on some of the other pages, he did what he was asked to do by the prompts to assert his assent to every document sent to him. This is sufficient to establish an intent to be bound.  Even if unsigned by a representative of defendant, the fact that it was presented to plaintiff as part of the preemployment package by defendant, which the requirement that he review, sign and return, manifests an intent to be bound as well on the pat of the employer.  

 

Next, Defendants argue the arbitration clause has strong elements of substantive unconscionability. Defendant contends the agreement does not fulfill the basic requirements of an enforceable, valid arbitration agreement. Regarding arbitration costs, it allows the arbitrator to shift fees or costs to the prevailing party rather than placing the responsibility of payment of costs of arbitration exclusively on the employer. Moreover, it fails to provide for a “neutral” arbitrator; rather, it states that “the arbitration shall be conducted by one arbitrator” and fails to make the full range of statutory remedies available; it does not seem to permit any discovery, let alone, “adequate discovery;” it does not provide for a “written arbitration that will reveal the essential findings and conclusions on which the award is based.”

 

The court disagrees. Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element.” (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486.) An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both procedural and substantive unconscionability is shown. (Stirlen v. Supercuts, Inc. (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1533.) Defendant does not attempt to make a procedural argument. Even so, “[s]ubstantive unconscionability focuses on the actual terms of the agreement and evaluates whether they create ‘overly harsh’ or ‘‘one-sided’ results’ [Citations] that is, whether contractual provisions reallocate risks in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.  [Citation] Substantive unconscionability ‘may take various forms,’ but typically is found in the employment context when the arbitration agreement is ‘one-sided’ in favor of the employer without sufficient justification, for example, when ‘the employee’s claims against the employer, but not the employer’s claims against the employee, are subject to arbitration.’ [Citations].”  (Roman v. Superior Court (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469-1470.)  In determining whether an arbitration agreement is unconscionable, the Court considers whether the agreement: (1) provides for a neutral arbitrator; (2) provides for reasonable discovery; (3) requires a written award; (4) provides for the same remedies that otherwise would be available in court; and (5) does not require employees to bear costs unique to arbitration.  (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 102-103.) 

 

First, the Arbitration Agreement explicitly provides if this dispute is related to employment, the employer will bear the cost of arbitration. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1, ¶6.) Moreover, although it is silent as to discovery, express discovery language is not necessary because, “when parties agree to arbitrate statutory claims, they also implicitly agree, absent express language to the contrary, to such procedures as are necessary to vindicate that claim.” (Ramos v. Superior Court (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1042, 1062, quoting Armendariz, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 106.) As for a neutral arbitrator, although the agreement does not explicitly state ‘neutral’, it says one must be selected (with no limitations) and if the parties cannot agree to one, the arbitrator will be selected by the AAA. This seems to provide for a neutral arbitrator. Lastly, the Arbitration Agreement does provide the “parties agree to enter arbitration through the American Arbitration Association and abide by the Commercial Arbitration Rules of the American Arbitration Association. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1, ¶2.) The Commercial Arbitration Rules explicitly provide an award shall be in writing.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED.
