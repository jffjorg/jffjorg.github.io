DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV44865    Hearing Date: January 26, 2024    Dept: F49

Dept. F-49

Date: 1-26-24

Case # 20STCV44865

Trial Date: 4-22-24

 

SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTIES: Defendants Saugus Union High School District and Gina Nolte

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Solomon Perkins

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Summary Adjudication on the First Amended Complaint

·         1st Cause of Action: Negligence

·         2nd Cause of Action: Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention

·         3rd Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On February 13, 2020, Plaintiff Solomon Perkins, a student at Tesoro Del Valle Elementary School, a school campus within the territory of Defendant Saugus Union School District (Saugus Union), was an enrolled student on campus, when a fellow classmate, “Tobin,” reported to a staff member on campus that Plaintiff was carrying a gun in his backpack. According to the complaint, Plaintiff told Tobin he had a “Nerf-Slingshot gun” with him, which led to the misrepresentation of an actual gun.

 

Following the report, Plaintiff’s backpack was examined by school principal, defendant Gina Nolte. The search revealed no actual firearm or slingshot gun. Nolte subsequently contacted the Los Angeles County Sheriff, and the “mental health department.” According to the complaint, the mental health department “attempted to commit Plaintiff.”

 

Although Plaintiff’s mother, Thea Perkins, was apparently contacted and came to the school campus, prior to her arrival, the Sheriff’s Department interrogated Plaintiff. No other adult acting on Plaintiff’s behalf was present. Upon Thea Perkins arriving at campus, both Plaintiff and his mother were involuntarily detained on the premises, until they were both escorted off the premises by armed deputies.

 

On November 23, 2020, Plaintiff filed his original complaint with ten causes of action. On October 28, 2021, Plaintiff filed an eleven cause of action First Amended Complaint for Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Violation of California Education Code sections 200, 201, 220 and 260 – Discrimination on the Basis of Disability, Violation of Equal Protection Clause of the California Constitution, False Imprisonment, Violation of Americans with Disability Act, Violation of California Government Code section 11135 – Discrimination Based on Disability, Violation of 42 USC section 1983 – Violation of the Equal Protection Clause, Violation of 42 USC section 1983 – Deprivation of Civil Rights, and, Violation of 42 USC section 1983 –Monell Violations.

 

On December 4, 2023, this Court heard the motion for summary judgment filed by Defendants. The Court denied the motion in part, granted it in part, and continued the hearing on the first three causes of action for negligence; negligent supervision, training, and/or retention; and intentional infliction of emotional distress for further briefing.

 

Defendants filed their supplemental brief by the Court’s deadline on January 12, 2024. The deadline for Plaintiff’s brief was January 19. Plaintiff filed his supplemental brief late on January 22.

 

RULING: Granted for the First, Second, and Third Causes of Action

 

Defendant’s evidentiary objection to the Gina Nolte deposition:

Sustained on the basis that it assumes facts not in evidence and is not relevant

 

Defendants Saugus Union High School District, and Gina Nolte move for summary adjudication on the first through third causes of action for Negligence; Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention; and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress. Defendants, in their supplemental brief, argue that Defendants are immune from liability for the three causes of action. Plaintiff argues in his supplemental brief that Civ. Code § 47 does not apply, and Defendants’ Section 820.2 arguments should be disregarded.

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)”  (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also CCP § 437c(c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.” (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

1st Cause of Action, Negligence: Granted

2nd Cause of Action, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention: Granted

3rd Cause of Action, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: Granted

 

Defendants moves for summary adjudication on grounds that the conduct of Principal Gina Nolte of Tesoro Elementary School constitutes an action protected by litigation privilege thereby immunizing Defendants from liability for the Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention, and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress causes of action.

 

While the Court previously found that the litigation privilege in Civ. Code § 47 exists, the extent of its applicability to this case was unclear from the parties’ arguments.

 

Nolte states in declaration that while no gun was discovered in the backpack of Solomon, Nolte was unaware of whether Solomon otherwise had potential access to a firearm at home, and therefore called the Sheriff. School safety regulations require the reporting any potential threat of extreme violence. The decision was also based on the purported admission of Solomon regarding his admission to shoot everyone in his class, except for two friends, and confirmation from another classmate. [Declaration of Gina Nolte, ¶¶ 14-21, 23.]

 

The Court previously found that the 2020 version of Civ. Code § 47 was applicable at the time of the incident. Under the 2020 version, the litigation privilege was absolute and that no exceptions applied to the report that Nolte made to the Sheriff.

 

However, the three causes of action allege more than just conduct related to the police and mental health services report. Courts must “draw a careful distinction between a cause of action based squarely on a privileged communication…and one based upon an underlying course of conduct evidenced by the communication.” (White v. Western Title Ins. Co. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 870, 888.) “‘The distinction between communicative and noncommunicative conduct hinges on the gravamen of the action... [T]he key in determining whether the privilege applies is whether the injury allegedly resulted from an act that was communicative in its essential nature.’ (Citations.) Notably, ‘if the gravamen of the action is communicative, the litigation privilege extends to noncommunicative acts that are necessarily related to the communicative conduct.’ (Citations.) To show that the litigation privilege does not apply, the burden is on the plaintiff to demonstrate that ‘an independent, noncommunicative, wrongful act was the gravamen of the action.’” (Mireskandari v. Gallagher (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 346, 369 (internal quotation marks omitted).)

 

The original motion lacked arguments on the differences in articulated conduct underpinning the three challenged causes of action, while Plaintiff sought to distinguish the conduct based at least on part on the damages claims arising from conduct beyond the phone call itself. Paragraph 32 of the operative complaint presents a series of claims originating from the report of the fellow second grade student. The student statement triggered a search, phone call, and placement of Solomon into a separate space for questioning by the Sheriff.

 

The intent of the statute is to provide protection from potential reprisal and encourage the reporting of illegal, including threatening, activity. (Wise v. Thrifty Payless, Inc., supra, 83 Cal.App.4th at p. 1303; Braun v. Bureau of State Audits, supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1390–1391.) The court interprets the protection narrowly in that while communicative acts (e.g., the reporting) clearly constitutes a protected activity, the privilege in no way shields a party from all potential claims tangentially related damages. (Kimmel v. Goland (1990) 51 Cal.3d 202, 211 [“a review of the myriad cases that have applied section 47(2) to shield defendants from liability demonstrates that, without exception, the privilege has applied only to torts arising from statements or publications”]; Mancini & Associates v. Schwetz (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 656, 661 [“Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b) applies only to communicative acts and does not apply to tortious courses of conduct”].) The law interpreting privileged communications in no way excuses other claims of tortious conduct even if the communication initially prompts the pathway.

 

The Court must distinguish between the conduct of Principal Nolte and the subsequent conduct of the Sheriff in taking over the questioning. While Plaintiff seeks to assert separate damages as a result of the entire incident, such an argument lacks factual distinction between the conduct of Principal Nolte and the responding sheriffs. The conduct essentially constitutes allegations arising from the detention of a student pending a safety investigation and the flowing emotional distress of the incident. Under the gravamen of the complaint standard, the distinction between the search, detention and report to the Sheriff, and the subsequent assumption of the investigation by the Sheriff presents distinctive differences in the conduct between the principal and law enforcement. The Court found the distinction critical in that statutory and case law vests a school administrator with certain rights and privileges, especially in situations involving student well-being and safety.

 

The original motion and reply lacked arguments regarding both the potential extension of the absolute privilege standard to the moments leading to the turnover of Solomon to the Sheriff, as well as other protections arising from Nolte exercising rights as a principal of the school separate and apart from the absolutely privileged conduct of the report. The Court therefore invited supplemental briefing on the First, Second and Third Causes of Action regarding the extension of the litigation privilege and/or any other potentially applicable immunity. The Court sought briefing only on the conduct of Principal Nolte acting on behalf of Saugus Union High School District, regarding the search, questioning, and detention of Solomon, while Solomon was on the school campus as an enrolled student.

 

In their supplemental brief, Defendants argue that Civ. Code § 47 broadly applies to Principal Nolte’s actions preceding her call to the Sheriff because those acts were a necessary precedent to that call. Defendants also argue that Gov. Code § 820.2 and the federal Coverdell Teach Protection Act of 2011, 20 U.S.C. § 6731, also immunize Nolte.

 

Nolte’s report to the Sheriff was privileged. Mireskandari v. Gallagher (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 346, 369, as modified on denial of reh’g (Jan. 21, 2021), and Dwight R. v. Christy B. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 697, 702-03 stand for the proposition that acts done in preparation of a privileged act are also privileged. In Mireskandari, the Court of Appeal held that the act of forwarding medical records to a third party is communicating them and therefore the Civ. Code § 47 immunizes the defendants from liability. (Mireskandari, 59 Cal App.5th at 369-370.) Additionally, in Dwight, acts done in anticipation of official proceedings are protected activities. (Dwight, 212 Cal.App.4th at 710-11.)

 

Further, “‘if the gravamen of the action is communicative, the litigation privilege extends to noncommunicative acts that are necessarily related to the communicative conduct.... Stated another way, unless it is demonstrated that an independent, noncommunicative, wrongful act was the gravamen of the action, the litigation privilege applies.’ (Citation.)” (Jacob B. v. County of Shasta (2007) 40 Cal.4th 948, 957.) Finally, “because ‘the cause of action is based on a communicative act, the litigation privilege extends to those noncommunicative actions which are necessarily related to that communicative act.’ (Citation.)” (Id.)

 

The gravamen of the action was a communication act: Nolte’s report to the Sheriff. The litigation privilege would apply to this act. Based on the above cited cases, Nolte’s actions prior to making the report to the Sheriff, such as searching Plaintiff’s backpack, would be protected activities. Those activities were preparatory and were done in anticipation of official proceedings, which was, in this case, making the report to the Sheriff. Further, those acts necessarily related to the act of making the report to the Sheriff.

 

Additionally, the privilege would apply to all three of the causes of action at issue because “it is the gravamen of the cause of action rather than its designation that is controlling.” (McNair v. City and County of San Francisco (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 1154, 1171.) Because the gravamen of the first three causes of action was the call to the Sheriff, then the privilege would apply to all three causes of action.

 

The arguments in Plaintiff’s brief attempt to go beyond the scope of the supplemental briefing that the Court asked for. The Court requested supplemental briefing on Nolte’s actions prior to her making the report to the Sheriff. Instead, Plaintiff introduces new facts from Nolte’s deposition regarding what happened after the sheriffs left and how long Plaintiff was detained in Nolte’s office. Plaintiff also fails to provide any argument for how these new facts affect the application of Civ. Code § 47 to Defendants’ conduct prior to the report, which is what is at issue here. The Court also sustained Defendants’ evidentiary objections to Nolte’s deposition.

 

Next, Cal. Gov. Code § 820.2 gives public employees immunity from liability when their act or omission was the result of an exercise of discretion vested in them. Coverdell, 20 U.S.C. § 6731 et seq., also gives teachers and principals liability protection when their actions are in conformity with the law and were in furtherance of efforts to control, discipline, etc., a student. (20 U.S.C. § 7943.) Defendants argue that Nolte’s actions would also be immune from liability under these laws.

 

Plaintiff argues that the Court did not request brief on Gov. Code § 820.2. However, the Court had asked for briefing on any other potential immunity, beyond Civ. Code § 47. Plaintiff next argues that Section 820.2 is inapplicable. Plaintiff points to a distinction drawn between policy making or planning decisions on one side and ministerial or operational decisions on the other. Planning decisions are immune while operational decisions are not immune. (Perez-Torres v. State of Cal. (2007) 42 Cal. 4th 136, 142-43.) Plaintiff argues that Nolte’s actions were ministerial or operational and would therefore not fall under the immunity granted by Gov. Code § 820.2.

 

When determining whether a government official is liable for torts, an act is “ministerial” where law prescribes and defines duties to be performed with such precision and certainty as to leave nothing to the exercise of discretion or judgment but not where the act involves exercise of discretion or judgment. (See Glickman v. Glasner (1964) 230 Cal.App.2d 120.)

 

Per school policy, Nolte conducted a threat assessment after Plaintiff’s behavior was reported to her. (Nolte Decl., ¶¶ 15 and 17.) During her conversation with Plaintiff, he told her that he had stated that he was going to use a gun to kill everyone in his class. (Nolte Decl., ¶ 18.) Nolte called the Sheriff upon the advice of the school district superintendent, who advised her to do so based on district protocol. (Nolte Decl., ¶¶ 19-20.) This policy is related to the school policy on threat assessments. (Nolte Decl., ¶ 21.)

 

Based on this sequence of events, it does not appear that much was left to Nolte’s discretion. She was required to do the threat assessment and make the report to the Sheriff. Perhaps the only discretionary action was calling the superintendent. Otherwise, Nolte was following the duties prescribed to her by school district policies. This would make her decisions ministerial in nature. Because of this, immunity would not be available under Gov. Code § 820.2, though it would still be available under Civ. Code § 47.

 

Based on the foregoing, there is no triable issue of material fact for causes of action one through three because of the immunity granted to Nolte.

 

Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication is granted for the First, Second, and Third Causes of Action.

RULING: Granted

 

CCP § 708.510 authorizes the Court to issue an order directing a judgment debtor to assign to a judgment creditor or a receiver all or part of a right to payment due or to become due to the judgment debtor. The right to payment should be assigned only to the extent necessary to satisfy the creditor’s money judgment and that, where part of the payments are exempt, the amount of the payments assigned should not exceed the difference between the gross amount of the payments and the exempt amount. (CCP§708.510(c), (d), (e) and (f).)

 

The balance of the judgment accrues at a rate of 10% per annum. (CCP § 685.010(a).) The Court may order assignment of the right to payment in an amount necessary to pay off the judgment in full, including accrued interest.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court issues an order assigning Judgment Debtor’s right to payment from the above named entities to Judgment Creditor to satisfy the judgment of $50,110.41 and any interest that accrues until the judgment is paid in full.
