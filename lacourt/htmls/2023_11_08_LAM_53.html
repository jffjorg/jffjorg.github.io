MOVING PARTY:                Defendant Star Ford Lincoln

 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiff Arin Yaghoubi

Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court denies defendant Star Ford Lincoln’s request for judicial notice that was filed on November 3, 2023 (1) as an improper attempt to introduce new evidence in reply, and                (2) because rulings from other Superior Court departments are irrelevant to the issues presented by this motion.  (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537; Malek Media Group LLC v. AXQG Corp. (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 817, 825.)

 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS 

The court rules on plaintiff Arin Yaghoubi’s evidentiary objections, filed on October 25, 2023, as follows:

The court overrules Objections Nos. 1-8.

The court rules on defendant Star Ford Lincoln’s evidentiary objections, filed on November 3, 2023, as follows:

The court sustains Objections Nos. 2-4, 6-8, and 13.

The court overrules Objections Nos. 1, 5, 9-12, and 14-21.

LEGAL STANDARD

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant or cross-defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “Once the defendant or cross-defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)  “When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Id. at p. 467; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

DISCUSSION

Defendant Star Ford Lincoln (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order granting summary judgment in its favor and against plaintiff Arin Yaghoubi (“Plaintiff”) or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s eighth and ninth causes of action.  The court notes that, although Defendant has addressed the ninth cause of action first, the court addresses the causes of action alleged against Defendant in numerical order.

1.     Eighth Cause of Action for Negligence

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.”  (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.)

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of showing that the eighth cause of action for negligence has no merit on the ground that the economic loss rule bars this claim.     

“[T]he economic loss rule provides:¿ ‘ “ ‘[W]here a purchaser’s expectations in a sale are frustrated because the product he bought is not working properly, his remedy is said to be in contract alone, for he has suffered only “economic” losses.’ ”¿ (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 988 [citation omitted].)¿ Therefore, “[t]he economic loss rule requires a purchaser to recover in contract for purely economic loss due to disappointed expectations, unless he can demonstrate harm above and beyond a broken contractual promise.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ “Not all tort claims for monetary losses between contractual parties are barred by the economic loss rule.  But such claims are barred when they arise from – or are not independent of – the parties’ underlying contracts.”  (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 923.)

Here, Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for negligence does not “arise from” an underlying contract between Plaintiff and Defendant, even if Plaintiff only seeks recovery of economic damages.  (Sheen, supra, 12 Cal.5th at p. 923.)  Instead, Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant (1) had an independent duty to perform professional services in a good and workmanlike manner pursuant to Civil Code section 1796.5, and (2) Defendant breached that duty.  (SAC ¶ 92; Civ. Code, § 1796.5 [providing that any legal entity “which engages in the business of providing service or repair to new or used consumer goods has a duty to the purchaser to perform those services in a good and workmanlike manner”].)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff’s claim does not arise from, and is independent of, any contract between the parties and therefore finds that Defendant has not shown that the economic loss rule bars this claim.  (Sheen, supra, 12 Cal.5th at p. 923.)

The court finds that Defendant has met its burden of showing that the eighth cause of action for negligence has no merit because Defendant has shown that an element of the cause of action (damages) cannot be established.  (Peredia, supra, 25 Cal.App.5th at p. 687 [setting forth elements of claims for negligence].)  

Defendant has presented evidence showing that (1) Defendant did not charge Plaintiff for any of the repair visits; (2) Plaintiff received a free Lyft ride to his workplace after delivering the subject vehicle to Defendant on April 15, 2019 for repairs; (3) Defendant provided Plaintiff with a free rental vehicle for the April 22, 2019 and July 22, 2019 repair visits; and (4) Plaintiff did not seek medical treatment for symptoms he felt as a result of the carbon monoxide issue with the vehicle and is not claiming personal injury damages.  (Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) Nos. 14-16; Arguello Decl., ¶¶ 10, 12, 14; Def. Compendium of Evidence (“Def. COE”), Ex. 11, Form Interrogatory No. 10.1, p. 9:6-18 [“Plaintiff has not filed a personal injury claim and is not claiming personal injury damages”].)

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has met its burden to show that Plaintiff cannot establish that he suffered damages as a result of Defendant’s breach of duty.

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the element of damages.

First, the court notes that, aside from arguing that the economic loss rule does not bar his claim, Plaintiff has not set forth argument establishing that he has suffered damages as a result of Defendant’s breach of duty in his opposition brief.  Instead, Plaintiff appears only to argue that the issue of damages is a question of fact for the jury.

Second, Plaintiff does not dispute that Defendant did not charge Plaintiff in connection with the three repair visits, and his own evidence confirms that fact.  (UMF No. 14; Yaghoubi Decl., Exs. 1 [April 16, 2019 Invoice for $0], 2 [April 26, 2019 Invoice for $0], 3 [July 25, 2019 Invoice for $0].)

Third, although Plaintiff has stated in his declaration that he “was left without access to the Subject Vehicle for a total of over sixteen (16) days as a result of the repeat presentations for the same concerns that were never repaired[,]” Plaintiff does not dispute that (1) he was provided a free Lyft ride to his workplace after presenting the vehicle for repair on April 15, 2019, and (2) he was provided with a free rental vehicle for the April 22, 2019 and July 22, 2019 repair visits.  (Yaghoubi Decl., ¶ 13; UMF Nos. 15-16.)  Thus, Plaintiff has not presented any evidence showing that he was suffered damages as a result of his inability to use the vehicle.  

Finally, the court acknowledges that Plaintiff states in his declaration that he was damaged by (1) the continued exposure to noxious exhaust smells in the vehicle’s cabin, (2) the loss of the intended use of the vehicle, and (3) the benefit of the bargain sought in leasing the subject vehicle.  (Yaghoubi Decl., ¶ 14.)  However, the court finds that this evidence is insufficient to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the element of damages.

As to the exposure to noxious exhaust smells, Plaintiff expressly stated, in his discovery responses, that he (1) has not filed a personal injury claim, and (2) is not claiming personal injury damages.  (Def. COE Ex. 11, p. 9:6-18, Response to Form Interrogatory No. 10.1.)  As to the loss of the use of his vehicle, Plaintiff does not dispute, as set forth above, that he was provided use of other vehicles when he presented the vehicle to Defendant for repair, and has not sufficiently explained or established the damages suffered by his inability to use his vehicle.  (UMF Nos. 15-16.)  As to the alleged loss of the benefit of the bargain sought in leasing the subject vehicle, Plaintiff has not provided any facts in support of this assertion, which is conclusory and therefore does not constitute “substantial responsive evidence sufficient to establish a triable issue of material fact on the merits of [Defendant’s] showing.”  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the element of damages suffered as a result of Defendant’s alleged breach of duty.  (Peredia, supra, 25 Cal.App.5th at p. 687.)

            The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the eighth cause of action for negligence.

2.     Ninth Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

“‘A cause of action for breach of contract requires proof of the following elements: (1) existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) damages to plaintiff as a result of the breach.’”  (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402.)

The court finds that Defendant has met its burden of showing that the ninth cause of action for breach of contract has no merit because Defendant has shown that an element of the cause of action (the existence of a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant) cannot be established. 

Plaintiff has alleged, in support of his breach of contract cause of action against Defendant, that Defendant agreed to perform the requested repairs “both orally and in writing.”  (SAC ¶ 96.)  The court finds that Defendant has met its burden to show that Plaintiff cannot prove the existence of either an oral or written agreement between them.

As to the existence of an oral agreement, Defendant has submitted Plaintiff’s discovery responses to show that no such agreement exists.  In response to an interrogatory requesting information for, inter alia, “each part of [an agreement alleged in the pleadings] not in writing,” Plaintiff stated: “Currently unknown[.]”  (Def. COE Ex. 11, Form Interrogatory No. 50.1, pp. 54:9-14, 55:7.)  Thus, the court finds that Defendant has presented evidence showing that Plaintiff cannot establish the element of the existence of an oral contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590 [“a moving defendant may rely on factually devoid discovery responses to shift the burden of proof pursuant to section 437c, subdivision (o)(2)”].)

As to the existence of a written agreement, Defendant points to Plaintiff’s response to an interrogatory requesting the identification of documents that comprise the agreements alleged in the pleadings, in which Plaintiff identified the following documents: (1) “Warranty Booklet and Sale Contract/Lease Agreement,” (2) “Star Ford Repair Orders,” (3) “Montebello Ford repair orders[,]” and (4) “Galpin Ford repair orders.”  (Def. COE Ex. 11, Form Interrogatory No. 50.1, pp. 54:10-11, 55:1-2.)

First, Defendant has submitted evidence showing that (1) Plaintiff leased the subject vehicle from Ford of Montebello; (2) Defendant was not a party to that lease; and (3) Defendant did not issue a separate warranty for the subject vehicle apart from the manufacturer’s warranty, which was provided by Ford Motor Company.  (UMF Nos. 1-2; Def. COE Ex. 4, p. 1 [“California Motor Vehicle Lease Agreement” naming, as lessee, Plaintiff, and as lessor, “Ford of Montebello Inc.”]; Arguello Decl., ¶ 6 [“At the time Plaintiff leased the Vehicle, the Vehicle came with a manufacturer’s warranty[,]” and “Star Ford [i.e., Defendant] did not provide a written warranty of the Vehicle”].)  Thus, the court finds that Defendant has presented evidence showing that the “Warranty Booklet and Sale Contract/Lease Agreement” documents do not constitute written contracts between Plaintiff and Defendant.

Second, Defendant has submitted the three repair orders issued by Defendant to Plaintiff.  While the repair orders include language authorizing Defendant to perform the repair work specified thereon and granting Defendant the permission to operate the vehicle for the purpose of testing and/or inspections, they do not contain any written promise “to perform the requested repairs and to conform the vehicle to the terms of the warranty” as alleged in the Second Amended Complaint.  (Def. COE Exs. 7, p.1, 8, p. 1, 9, p.1; SAC ¶ 96.)  Thus, Defendant has shown that its written repair orders do not constitute written contracts between Plaintiff and Defendant.

Third, Defendant has presented evidence showing that it is not affiliated with and does not have any ownership in Galpin Ford, Sunrise Ford, or Ford of Montebello.  (Arguello Decl., ¶ 5.)  Thus, Defendant has shown that the repair orders issued by Ford of Montebello and Galpin Ford would not create a written contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (Ibid.) 

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the existence of a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.

First, Plaintiff contends that, on the reverse of one of Plaintiff’s repair orders, there exists a written contract between himself and Defendant.  Plaintiff does not quote the specific language that he argues has created contractual obligations on the part of Defendant in either his responsive separate statement or his opposition brief, though it appears that Plaintiff is relying on the document entitled “Ford Warranty Statement” that is attached to the April 22, 2019 repair order.  (Yaghoubi Decl., Ex. 2, p. 4.)  This warranty statement sets forth Ford Motor Company’s “New Vehicle Limited Warranty[,]” and references a “dealer” in certain provisions.  (Ibid.)  But this document (1) states that the warranty was provided by “Ford Motor Company[,]” i.e., not Defendant, and (2) does not contain any language creating a contractual obligation on the part of Defendant to repair the subject vehicle in accordance with Ford Motor Company’s warranty.  Thus, the court finds that this evidence is insufficient to show that there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether the repair orders constitute a written contract(s) between Plaintiff and Defendant.

Second, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s service advisor, Andrew Banales (“Banales”) “orally informed Plaintiff during each presentation that [Defendant] would perform the necessary diagnoses and repairs on the Subject Vehicle.”  (Opp., p. 15:8-9.)  However, Plaintiff did not present any evidence in support of this assertion.  Although Plaintiff has stated in his declaration that Banales represented “that he was aware of the exhaust odors in vehicles similar to the Subject Vehicle” and advised Plaintiff “that he would help with a solution[,]” Plaintiff did not present evidence (1) showing that Banales made specific oral promises that Defendant would conform the subject vehicle to Ford Motor Company’s warranty or to perform requested repairs (SAC ¶ 96), or (2) describing terms of any other oral contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (Yaghoubi Decl., ¶ 9.)  Thus, the court finds that this evidence is insufficient to show that there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether Banales’s statements created an oral contract.

            Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff did not meet his burden to “produce substantial responsive evidence sufficient to establish a triable issue of material fact on the merits of” Defendant’s showing that Plaintiff cannot establish the element of either an oral or written contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (Sangster, supra, 68 Cal.App.4th at p. 163.)

The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the ninth cause of action for breach of contract.

Because the court has granted summary adjudication in favor of Defendant on all of the causes of action alleged against Defendant in Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint, the court find that all of the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that Defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

ORDER

            The court grants defendant Star Ford Lincoln’s motion for summary judgment on plaintiff Arin Yaghoubi’s Second Amended Complaint.

            The court orders defendant Star Ford Lincoln to prepare, serve, and lodge a proposed judgment no later than 10 days from the date of this order.

            The court sets an Order to Show Cause re entry of judgment (as to defendant Star Ford Lincoln) for hearing on February 8, 2024, at 8:30 a.m., in Department 53.

The court orders defendant Star Ford Lincoln to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant and cross-complainant Core Construction and Development       

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendant and cross-complainant Core Construction and Development (“Core”) moves the court for an order determining that the settlement entered into by and between Core, on the one hand, and plaintiffs Greg Mooradian, Debra Mooradian, and the Greg and Debra Mooradian Family Trust (“Plaintiffs”), on the other hand, was made in good faith pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 877 and 877.6.  Core has agreed to settle this action with Plaintiffs for the total amount of $350,000, subject to the court’s determination that the settlement was made in good faith.  (McElroy Decl., ¶ 22.)

“[Code of Civil Procedure] Section 877.6 was enacted by the Legislature in 1980 to establish a statutory procedure for determining if a settlement by an alleged joint tortfeasor has been entered into in good faith and to provide a bar to claims of other alleged joint tortfeasors for equitable contribution or partial or comparative indemnity when good faith is shown.”¿ (IRM Corp. v. Carlson (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 94, 104.)¿ Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6, subdivision (a)(1) provides, in relevant part, that, on noticed motion, “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors . . . .”¿ “The party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof on that issue.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (d).)¿ 

When a motion for determination of good faith settlement is uncontested, a “barebones” motion that sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case, is sufficient to support a good faith determination.¿ (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.)¿¿¿ 

The court has not received opposition papers from any other party involved in this action contending that the settlement between Core and Plaintiffs was not made in good faith.  The court finds that (1) Core’s motion, accompanied by the declarations of Mark Hopkins, a retained expert on behalf of Core attesting to the liability of Core, and Conor H. McElroy, set forth the ground of good faith, and (2) the declaration of Conor H. McElroy sets forth a brief background of the case and other factors supporting a determination of good faith.  (Hopkins Decl., ¶¶ 2, 6-8; McElroy Decl., ¶¶ 10-17, 20-21, 24, 26-27.) 

The court therefore finds that Core’s motion and supporting evidence are sufficient to support a good faith determination and therefore grants Core’s motion.  (City of Grand Terrace, supra, 192 Cal.App.3d at p. 1261.)

ORDER

            The court grants defendant and cross-complainant Core Construction and Development’s motion for determination of good faith settlement.

            The court determines that the settlement entered into by and between defendant and cross-complainant Core Construction and Development, on the one hand, and plaintiffs Greg Mooradian, Debra Mooradian, and the Greg and Debra Mooradian Family Trust, on the other hand, is in good faith within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6, subdivision (a).

            All other joint tortfeasors or co-obligors are barred from any further claims against defendant and cross-complainant Core Construction and Development for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (c).)¿

The court orders defendant and cross-complainant Core Construction and Development to give notice of this ruling.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.
