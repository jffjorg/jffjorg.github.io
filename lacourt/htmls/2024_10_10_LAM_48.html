[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 10, 2024

 

Plaintiff Clarence Harrell seeks to compel further responses to Deposition Notices and Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”) from non-parties Francis Jose and Suzanne Stone, who are employees of Defendant City of Pasadena.

Plaintiff served a Second Amended Notice of Deposition of Francis Jose and RFPs for a November 2, 2023 deposition.  Defendant served objections on November 2, 2023.  On January 25, 2024, Defendant served supplemental responses.

Plaintiff served an Amended Notice of Deposition of Suzanne Stone and RFPs for a December 20, 2023 deposition.  Defendant served objections on December 20, 2023.

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed motions to compel further responses from Francis Jose and Suzanne Stone.  Defendant filed opposition on July 29, 2024—the day before the hearings.  The Court therefore continued the hearings.

A.        The Motion For Francis Jose is Untimely.

A motion to compel further deposition testimony or production must be made within 60 days of the completion of the record.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (b).)

The transcript for Jose’s deposition was finalized on November 28, 2023, making January 29, 2024 the motion deadline.  (Price Decl. ¶ 9.)  Plaintiff did not file this motion until March 8, 2024.

The Hearing on Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents in the Second Amended Notice of Deposition of Francis Jose is DENIED.

B.        Plaintiff Has Not Shown Good Cause for the Documents From Suzanne Stone.

RFP No. 13 requests “Each and every DOCUMENT containing each and every employee complaint and/or union grievance that YOU received and/or became aware of, about Greg Heltsley, between January 1, 2013 and Greg Heltsley’s last day of employment with the Defendant, City of Pasadena, including but not limited to each and every complaint and/or union grievance that James Lohse testified to having made during his deposition.  RFP No. 14 requests “Each and every DOCUMENT containing each and every investigative report or finding relating to each and every investigation that YOU undertook in connection with each and every employee complaint and/or union grievance made about Greg Heltsley, between January 1, 2013 and Greg Heltsley’s last day of employment with Defendant, City of Pasadena, including but not limited to each and every complaint and/or union grievance that James Lohse testified to having made during his deposition.”  In the Separate Statement, “Plaintiff is willing to limit the scope of these requests to only those complaints and/or union grievances filed by James Lohse as well as the investigations of Ms. Stone undertook in connection thereof.”

RFP No. 18 requests “Each and every DOCUMENT, including each and every COMMUNICATION, in whatever format, electronic or otherwise, reflecting each and every work-related complaint DEFENDANT received or became aware about PLAINTIFF’s supervisor.”  RFP No. 19 requests “Each and every DOCUMENT, including each and every COMMUNICATION, in whatever format, electronic or otherwise, reflecting each and every investigation concerning each and every work related complaint DEFENDANT received or became aware about PLAINTIFF’s supervisor.”  In the Separate Statement, “Plaintiff is willing to limit the scope of [these] request[s] to only those complaints and investigations relating to Francis Jose between March 1, 2015 and December 31, 2018.”  (See also Anglin Decl. ¶ 9.)

Plaintiff argues that these documents “would show whether other employees including James Lohse and/or others were also subject to the same violations and breaches of law and policy.  Complaints by others would show Defendant’s knowledge of the discriminatory conduct either through the acts or omissions of its supervisors such as Francis Jose and complaints made by others asserting the same types of complaints asserted by Plaintiff.”

On October 3, 2023, Elana Malekan filed a third amended complaint (“TAC”) against Rebeka Shadpour, Wealth Road Inc., and Jason Shadpour (Trustee of The JK Land Trust).  The TAC alleges (1) breach of lease; (2) fraud; (3) rescission and restitution; (4) breach of fiduciary duty; and (5) violation of Penal Code section 496.

On February 21, 2024, Rebeka Shadpour and Jason Shadpour (Trustee of The JK Land Trust) (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”) against ALE Solutions, Safeco Insurance Company of America, and Liberty Mutual Insurance Company (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”), as well as Elana Malekan.  The Cross-Complaint alleges (1) intentional misrepresentation; (2) negligent misrepresentation; (3) fraud by concealment; (4) contribution; (5) equitable indemnification; (6) implied contractual indemnification; (7) defamation per se; and (8) fraud.

On May 13, 2024, ALE Solutions filed a demurrer to the first six causes of action and motion to strike.

On May 31, 2024, Safeco Insurance Company of America and Liberty Mutual Insurance Company filed a demurrer to the first six causes of action.

Cross-Defendants’ arguments are substantially similar.  Accordingly, the Court will address them together without citations to page numbers.

DEMURRERS

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)

A.        Factual Background

Malekan alleges that her personal residence was damaged and she was forced to relocate.  (TAC ¶ 9.)  Pursuant to her insurance policy with Safeco, Malekan obtained authorization to rent a comparable residence, with Safeco paying the rent.  (TAC ¶ 9.)

Malekan entered into a lease with property owner Shadpour and landlord The JK Trust.  (TAC ¶¶ 10-11.)  Malekan informed Shadpour that the passcode for the property did not work, but she never received the correct passcode or a key, and she never obtained possession of the property.  (TAC ¶ 13.)  Malekan alleges that the lease was a sham.  (TAC ¶ 13.)

Cross-Complainants allege that Malekan’s temporary housing agreement was actually with Safeco and the other Cross-Defendants.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 15 & Ex. 1.)  At Malekan’s deposition, she testified that she orally notified Safeco and Cross-Defendants that she did not have access to the rented property.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 19.)  “[D]espite being put on notice by Malekan that she did not have access to the Subject Property,” Cross-Defendants never notified Cross-Complainants about the issue, concealed the information, and “continued to represent to Cross-Complainants that Malekan was in possession of the Subject Property.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 20.)

B.        The Misrepresentation Claims Are Time-Barred (First, Second, Third Causes of Action).

“‘In order for the bar of the statute of limitations to be raised by demurrer, the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.’”  (Geneva Towers Ltd. Partnership v. City of San Francisco (2003) 29 Cal.4th 769, 781.)

The statute of limitations for intentional misrepresentation and concealment is three years.   (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).)  The statute of limitations for negligent misrepresentation is two years.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 339.)

Cross-Defendants note that the latest alleged date was June 30, 2019, when the tenancy under the lease expired.  (See Cross-Complaint ¶ 17.)  Therefore, the filing of the Cross-Complaint on February 21, 2024 is after the two-year and three-year statutes of limitation for any misrepresentation and concealment during the lease, and these claims are time-barred.

Cross-Complainants argue that if the original complaint is filed before the statute of limitations runs on the cross-complaint, then the cross-complaint is timely.

“[A] statute of limitations is suspended or tolled as to a defendant’s then unbarred causes of action against the plaintiff arising out of the same transaction by the filing of the plaintiff’s complaint.”  (Electronic Equipment Express, Inc. v. Donald H. Seiler & Co. (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 834, 844; see ZF Micro Devices, Inc. v. TAT Capital Partners, Ltd. (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 69, 91 [“[T]he Supreme Court characterized the tolling doctrine as embracing all cross-claims by a defendant against the plaintiff, regardless of their relatedness to the claims asserted in the complaint.”].)

Cross-Defendants were not plaintiffs in the underlying complaint against Cross-Complainants.  They first became parties through the Cross-Complaint.  This tolling rule does not apply to Cross-Complainants’ claims against Cross-Defendants.

Cross-Complainants also argue that they newly discovered the information at Malekan’s January 26, 2024 deposition.  (See Cross-Complaint ¶ 19.)  “In order to rely on the discovery rule for delayed accrual of a cause of action, ‘[a] plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his claim would be barred without the benefit of the discovery rule must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.’  [Citation.]  In assessing the sufficiency of the allegations of delayed discovery, the court places the burden on the plaintiff to ‘show diligence’; ‘conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer.’  [Citation.]”  (Id. at p. 808.)  Cross-Defendants do not allege facts showing why they could not have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.

The demurrers to the first, second, and third causes of action are sustained.

C.        The Misrepresentation Claims Do Not Allege Sufficient Facts (First, Second Causes of Action).

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.  [Citations.]  The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true.  [Citations.]”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-231.)

“Causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud and, therefore, each element must be pleaded with specificity.”  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.)  “‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’  [Citation.]  A plaintiff’s burden in asserting a fraud claim against a corporate employer is even greater.  In such a case, the plaintiff must ‘allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.’  [Citation.]”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

Cross-Defendants argue that there are no particular facts about the misrepresentations.  Cross-Complainants allege only that Cross-Defendants “represented to Cross-Complainants that Malekan was in possession of the Subject Property.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 24, 34; see id. ¶ 20.)  There are no specific facts about the misrepresentations.

The demurrers to the first and second causes of action are sustained.

D.        The Concealment Claim Does Not Allege Sufficient Facts (Third Cause of Action).

Fraud based on concealment requires that “(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.”  (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310-311.)  An essential element of intentional concealment includes the duty to disclose, which must be based upon a transaction, or a special relationship, between plaintiff and defendant.  (Id. at p. 314.)  “There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.’”  (Id. at p. 311.)

Cross-Defendants argue that there are no particular facts and there was no duty owed to Cross-Complainant.  Safeco and Liberty were not parties to the Temporary Housing Agreement between ALE Solutions and Malekan.  (See Cross-Complaint, Ex. 1.)  All Cross-Defendants were not parties to the rental lease.  (See Cross-Complaint, Ex. 2.)  Cross-Complainants allege only that Cross-Defendants “never notified Cross-Complainants that this [Malekan’s inability to access the property] was an issue,” “concealed this information,” and “were in a business relationship” with Cross-Complainants.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 20, 43.)  There are no allegations about any duty owed by Cross-Defendants to Cross-Complainants.

The demurrers to the third cause of action are sustained.

E.        Cross-Complainants Cannot Allege Contribution (Fourth Cause of Action).

“A right of contribution can come into existence only after rendition of a judgment declaring more than one defendant jointly liable to the plaintiff.”  (Coca-Cola Bottling Co. v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1372, 1378; see also General Electric Co. v. State of Cal. ex rel. Dept. Pub. Wks. (1973) 32 Cal.App.3d 918, 925; Code Civ. Proc., § 875, subd. (a).)

Cross-Defendants note that they are not parties to Malekan’s underlying TAC, so there is not and will not be any judgment against them as joint tortfeasors with Cross-Complainants.

Cross-Complainants argue, without any citation to authority, that “[a]ny possible damages to Cross-Complainants are a result of Cross-Defendant’s actions or inactions and thus would make Cross-Defendant a joint tortfeasor responsible for any possible damages.”  There is still no basis for a future judgment in favor of Malekan and jointly against Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants.

The demurrers to the fourth cause of action are sustained without leave to amend.

F.         The Indemnity Claims Are Insufficiently Pleaded (Fifth, Sixth Causes of Action).

Equitable indemnity “applies only among defendants who are jointly and severally liable to the plaintiff.  [Citation.]  [J]oint and several liability in the context of equitable indemnity is fairly expansive.  We agree it is not limited to ‘the old common term “joint tortfeasor” . . . .’  It can apply to acts that are concurrent or successive, joint or several, as long as they create a detriment caused by several actors.”  (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc. v. Forcum/Mackey Construction, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852.)  “With limited exception, there must be some basis for tort liability against the proposed indemnitor.”  (Ibid.)

“Implied contractual indemnity is applied to contract parties and is designed to apportion loss among contract parties based on the concept that one who enters a contract agrees to perform the work carefully and to discharge foreseeable damages resulting from that breach.”  (Smoketree-Lake Murray, Ltd. v. Mills Concrete Construction Co. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1724, 1736.)  “‘[A] claim for implied contractual indemnity is a form of equitable indemnity subject to the rules governing equitable indemnity claims’ [citation]. . . . [A]n implied contractual indemnity claim, like a traditional equitable indemnity claim, is subject to the American Motorcycle rule that a party’s liability for equitable indemnity is based on its proportional share of responsibility for the damages to the injured party.”  (Prince v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1151, 1165.)  “Traditional equitable indemnity and implied contractual indemnity share a key feature that distinguishes them from express indemnity: unlike express indemnity, neither traditional equitable indemnity nor implied contractual indemnity is available ‘in the absence of a joint legal obligation to the injured party.’  [Citation.]  Under this principle, ‘“‘there can be no indemnity without liability,’”’ that is, the indemnitee and the indemnitor must share liability for the injury.”  (Jocer Enterprises, Inc. v. Price (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 559, 573.)

The Cross-Complaint seeks indemnity “[i]n the event that Cross-Complainants are held liable to Malekan, or to anyone else, for damages as a result of the incidents and occurrences alleged in Malekan’s Complaint.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 59.)  There is no basis for Cross-Defendants’ liability to Malekan, and thus no joint liability with Cross-Defendants.  Additionally, there is no contractual relationship between Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants.  The only contracts are between Malekan and ALE Solutions, and Cross-Complainants and Malekan.

The demurrers to the fifth and sixth causes of action are sustained without leave to amend.

MOTION TO STRIKE

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

ALE Solutions moves to strike the Cross-Complaint because Cross-Complainants did not have leave of Court to file it.

A party must file any cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint before or at the same time as the answer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50, subd. (a).)  ALE Solutions did not first file a complaint against Cross-Defendants.

Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the Court has set a date for trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50, subd. (b).)  The Court first set a trial date on January 7, 2022, but this Cross-Complaint was not filed until February 21, 2024.

Accordingly, Cross-Complainants were required to obtain leave of Court before filing their Cross-Complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50, subd. (c).)  The motion to strike is granted on this ground.

In the interest of judicial efficiency, the Court will not require Cross-Complainants to file a new motion for leave to file a cross-complaint.  The Court grants Cross-Complainants leave to file an amended cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants.

CONCLUSION

The demurrers are SUSTAINED.  The motion to strike is GRANTED.

Cross-Complainants are granted 30 days’ leave to amend the first, second, and third causes of action.  No leave to amend is granted for the fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action.

On March 4, 2022, Great Divide Insurance Company filed this action against Cannon Constructors South Inc. (“Cannon”) for damages in subrogation for general negligence.  The Complaint alleges that Cannon allegedly caused a back-up in a building’s plumbing system, resulting in substantial property damage for the insured, which was discovered on June 4, 2021.

On April 18, 2024, Cannon filed a first amended cross-complaint (“FACC”) against GPS Painting & Wallcovering (“GPS”), alleging (1) express indemnity; (2) implied indemnity; (3) equitable indemnity; (4) contribution; (5) declaratory relief re: duty to defend; (6) declaratory relief re: duty to indemnify; and (7) breach of contract.

On April 26, 2024, GPS filed a demurrer and motion to strike.  GPS demurs to the first, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action.  The request for judicial notice is denied as unnecessary because the FACC and its exhibits are already part of this case’s record.

DISCUSSION

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)

A.        The Claims Are Not Facially Time-Barred.

GPS argues that the first, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action are barred by the four-year statute of limitations for breach of written contract and/or the ten-year statute of repose for latent construction defect.  (Demurrer at pp. 13-16; see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 337, 337.15.)

“‘In order for the bar of the statute of limitations to be raised by demurrer, the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.’”  (Geneva Towers Ltd. Partnership v. City of San Francisco (2003) 29 Cal.4th 769, 781.)

The statute of limitations for breach of written contract is four years.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 337.)  The statute of limitations for construction defect is ten years.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 337.15.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 337.15’s ten-year limitation “begins when the individual contribution to the work of improvement has been substantially completed, rather than being tolled until the project is entirely completed.”  (Time for Living, Inc. v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All American Development Co. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 30, 36.)  “Actions barred after 10 years encompass both direct actions by the consumer against the contributor (§ 337.15, subd. (a)) and actions for indemnity against such contributor based upon his role in the project (§ 337.15, subd. (c)).”  (Id. at p. 36.)

GPS argues that the December 2020 purchase order does not itself contain an indemnity provision, and Cannon cannot rely on the parties’ prior agreements dated 2004 or 2010.  (Demurrer at pp. 13-14.)  This does not make the FACC facially time-barred.  The clock does not start when the contract is signed.  Rather, the limitations period begins when a cause of action accrues.  There could be no negligent construction, breach of contract, or failure to indemnify until the work on the underlying project occurred.  GPS’s subcontractor work was necessarily completed sometime between the December 2020 purchase order and the June 2021 discovery of the negligent construction work.  Accordingly, the causes of action accrued well within four years of both Great Divide filing this action (March 4, 2022) and Cannon filing its original cross-complaint against GPS (March 23, 2023).

The demurrer is overruled on this ground.

B.        The Claims Are Uncertain and Do Not Allege a Valid Basis for Express Indemnity.

GPS also argues that the first, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action are fatally uncertain because the allegations refer to indemnity provisions that do not apply to this underlying project.  (Demurrer at pp. 17-19.)

Cannon alleges that it is entitled to indemnity from GPS pursuant to the parties’ March 2010 Master Agreement.  (See FACC ¶¶ 11-14 & Ex. B.)  The 2010 Master Agreement states that Cannon intends to engage GPS to provide subcontractor work pursuant to purchase orders, which “will explicitly incorporate by reference all of the terms and conditions of this Agreement.”  Article 5.1 requires GPS to indemnify Cannon for all claims arising from GPS’s operations.

In December 2020, GPS approved a purchase order for Cannon’s work on the premises.  (FACC ¶ 14 & Ex. C.)  GPS argues that this purchase order does not provide a basis for indemnity because it does not explicitly incorporate the 2010 Master Agreement.  (Demurrer at pp. 13-16.)  Instead, “This Purchase Order is subject to and shall be governed by all of the provisions of the Master Subcontract Agreement between CANNON CONSTRUCTORS, INC. and the addressee dated 03/24/04.”  (FACC, Ex. C.)

Cannon argues that the parties’ 2004 Master Subcontract Agreement also contained an indemnity provision, and it automatically renews in the absence of a notice of termination.  (Opposition at pp. 4-5; see FACC ¶ 15 & Ex. D.)

Article 6.5 of the 2010 Master Agreement states that the “Agreement and any Purchase Order governed hereunder contains all of the covenants and provisions agreed upon by the Parties and supersede and nullify all prior negotiations, proposals or stipulations.”  The parties “shall not be bound by or be liable for, any statement, representation, promise or agreement not set forth herein.”  Accordingly, the 2004 Master Subcontract Agreement has been superseded and nullified by the 2010 Master Agreement.

The December 2020 purchase order (relevant to Great Divide’s claim in this action) does not “explicitly incorporate by reference all of the terms and conditions of this [2010 Master] Agreement.”  It is expressly governed by the 2004 Master Subcontract Agreement, which is no longer in effect.

Accordingly, there is no contractual basis for indemnity arising from this purchase order—and thus no basis for express indemnity, declaratory relief regarding express indemnity, or breach of contract.

The demurrer is sustained on this ground.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer to the first, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action is SUSTAINED.  Because Cannon has not shown any way to amend the claims to correct their deficiencies, no leave to amend is granted.

GPS is ordered to file an answer to the remainder of the FACC within 30 days.

On June 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Application for Order Directing Service of Summons on Defendant Corporation by Delivery of Process to Secretary of State.

If a corporation’s agent designated for service of process cannot with reasonable diligence be found at the address designated for personally delivering the process, and it is shown by affidavit that the domestic corporation cannot be served in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10, 415.20, 416.10, or 416.20, the court may order service on the Secretary of State.  (Corp. Plaintiff provides Declarations of Reasonable Diligence from its process servers.  (Application, Exs. B-C.)  On April 19, 2023, the process server attempted service on Kambiz Marashi (Agent for Service of Process, CEO, Secretary, CFO, and Director for Buy Zeeba Inc.) at 510 West 6th Street, Suite 728, Los Angeles, California 90014.  There was no answer/response.  She checked in with security who let her up, but the office door was locked and no one would answer.  On April 28, 2023, the agent was not in or available.  On April 30, 2023, the agent was not in or available and the office was closed.  On May 1 and 2, 2023, there was no answer and it was quiet.  On May 14, 2024, a different process server encountered a locked door and was informed by the front desk that “they work remotely.”  On May 16, 20, 22, and 23, 2024, there was no answer and the door was closed/locked.  The process server left a courtesy copy on May 23, 2024.

Plaintiff has shown that the agent designated for service of process (and sole officer and director) could not with reasonable diligence be found at the address designated for personally delivering the process. 

Accordingly, the Application for Order Directing Service of Summons on Defendant Corporation by Delivery of Process to Secretary of State is GRANTED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION FOR NUNC PRO TUNC ORDER

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 10, 2024

 

On September 25, 2023, Plaintiff Darral Scott filed this action against Defendants County of Los Angeles, Deputy Justin Sabatine, and Deputy Jacob Ruiz.

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for nunc pro tunc order.  Plaintiff asks that his complaint be deemed filed on September 15, 2023, which would render it timely filed.

Plaintiff’s counsel explains that they attempted to file the complaint and its accompanying documents on September 15, 2023.  (Ricketts Decl. ¶ 4.)  Counsel made an error on the Civil Case Cover Sheet Addendum when marking the reason for choosing the venue, which resulted in the entire filing being rejected.  (Ricketts Decl. ¶¶ 4-8.)

Defendants argue that the Complaint was properly rejected under Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (e)(4)(C) because it did not “comply with applicable filing requirements,” and Plaintiff did not timely file a corrected version.  (Opposition at pp. 4-5.)

“[T]he local superior court may not condition the filing of a complaint on local rule requirements.  Instead, so long as a complaint complies with state requirements, the clerk has a ministerial duty to file.  In legal effect, a complaint is ‘filed’ when it is presented to the clerk for filing in the form required by state law.”  (Carlson v. State of California Department of Fish & Game (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1268, 1270.)  “If a party that is required to provide a cover sheet under this rule or a similar local rule fails to do so or provides a defective or incomplete cover sheet at the time the party’s first paper is submitted for filing, the clerk of the court must file the paper.”  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.220(c).)  “The Judicial Council added subdivision (c) to former rule 982.2 (subsequently renumbered as 3.220) effective January 2002 to address the very problem that occurred in this case: ‘the refusal of some clerks to file an initial pleading because it is not accompanied by a cover sheet or the sheet is somehow defective.’”  (Mito v. Temple Recycling Center Corp. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 276, 280.)

The only defect in Plaintiff’s filing was an incorrect mark on the Civil Case Cover Sheet Addendum—a local form and local requirement.  The September 15, 2023 filing was improperly rejected for this error.  Accordingly, the motion for nunc pro tunc order is GRANTED.  The complaint is deemed filed on September 15, 2023.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEMURRER; GRANTING MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 10, 2024

 

On December 8, 2023, Plaintiff Carlice Etame filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant Inland Empire Health Plan, alleging (1) breach of contract; (2) medical negligence; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress “(IIED”); and (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress (“NIED”).

On February 8, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer and motion to strike.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendant’s request for judicial notice of IEHP’s 2024 Member Handbook is denied.  The Court disagrees with Defendant that it is an “official act” of a government agency and that “[n]otice is also proper because the document is available online and is ‘not reasonably subject to dispute and is capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.’”  Information on websites, even official governmental agency websites, can be reasonably subject to dispute and are not proper subjects for judicial notice.  (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 888-889; see also Huitt v. Southern Cal. Gas Co. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1586, 1605 fn. 10.)

The Court does, however, take judicial notice of the fact that Defendant is a not-for-profit Medicare-Medicaid public health plan.

DEMURRER

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)  “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.”  (Arce ex rel. Arce v. Kaiser Found. Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 556.)

A.        The FAC Does Not Allege Compliance With the Government Claims Act.

Under the Government Claims Act, no person may sue a public entity or public employee for money or damages unless a timely written claim has been presented to and denied by the public entity.  (County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1267.)  Absent an applicable exception, failure to timely present a claim for money or damages to a public entity bars a plaintiff from filing suit against that entity bars a plaintiff from filing a lawsuit against that entity.  (State of California v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1239.)

Defendant correctly notes that Plaintiff does not allege compliance with, nor exemption from, the Government Claims Act.  (Demurrer at pp. 7-8.)

The demurrer is sustained on this ground.

B.        The FAC Does Not Allege Any Statutory Basis for the Second, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action.

A public entity is not liable for an injury arising from an act or omission of the entity, a public employee, or any other person except as provided by statute.  (Gov. Code, § 815.)

Defendant argues that it is immune from the second, third, and fourth causes of action (medical negligence, IIED, and NIED), which lack a statutory basis.  (Demurrer at p. 10.)

The demurrer to the second, third, and fourth causes of action is sustained on this ground.

C.        The First, Second, and Third Causes of Action Lack Sufficient Facts.

Defendant argues that the first, second, and third causes of action are insufficiently pleaded.  (Demurrer at pp. 11-12.)

The first cause of action alleges breach of contract.  The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.  (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)  “A written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.”  (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.)  To plead a contract by its legal effect, a plaintiff must “allege the substance of its relevant terms.  This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.”  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff alleges that she “entered into a valid insurance contract, the Policy, with Defendant” and diligently paid all premiums, but Defendant “unreasonably and unjustifiably refused to fulfill its obligation to pay for medically necessary treatments for Plaintiff.”  (FAC at p. 10.)  This does not sufficiently allege the terms of the contract.

The second cause of action alleges medical negligence.  In a medical malpractice action, a plaintiff must establish the following elements: “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional’s negligence.  [Citations.]”  (Galvez v. Frields (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 1410, 1420.)  The FAC alleges that when Plaintiff’s daughter was treated at Loma Linda Children’s Hospital and the Center for Discovery, Defendant did not pay for some of her treatment, often requested her early release, did not provide adequate information, and forced the Center for Discovery to stop the treatment early.  However, Defendant provides insurance, not direct medical treatment.  (See, e.g., FAC at pp. 5, 8, 13, 15.)  Plaintiff alleges that she and Defendant “entered a formal health insurance /patient relationship, a confidential and fiduciary relationship.”  (FAC at p. 8.)  Plaintiff cannot allege medical negligence against an insurer who is not a medical provider.

The third cause of action alleges IIED.  “‘[T]o state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress a plaintiff must show: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’  [Citation.]  ‘Conduct, to be ‘outrageous’ must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’  [Citation.]”  (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.)  When a plaintiff does not suffer physical injury, the conduct must involve “extreme and outrageous intentional invasions of one’s mental and emotional tranquility.”  (Alcorn v. Anbro Engineering, Inc. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 493, 498.)  The FAC alleges that Defendant “did not care” about Plaintiff’s daughter’s treatment, refused to pay, went against doctor recommendations, and “stopped the treatment after we talked with them, intentionally inflicting emotional distress on parents and family.”  (FAC at pp. 15-16.)  These are not sufficient facts to allege extreme and outrageous conduct.

The demurrer to the first, second, and third causes of action is sustained on this ground.

MOTION TO STRIKE

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

Defendant moves to strike allegations relating to punitive damages.  A public entity is not liable for punitive damages or other damages imposed primarily for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.  (Gov. Code, § 818.)

The motion to strike is granted.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer is SUSTAINED.  The motion to strike is GRANTED.

Plaintiff did not file any opposition and therefore did not show any ability to amend the FAC to cure its deficiencies.  Accordingly, no leave to amend is granted.

This action is DISMISSED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 10, 2024

 

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs The People of the State of California and County of Los Angeles filed this action against Defendant Milton Joyner and others to abate a public nuisance and enjoin the operation and maintenance of an illegal cannabis dispensary.

Defendant filed an answer, and on September 16, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Defendant’s objections to Plaintiffs’ RJN Exhibit D are sustained.  Information on websites, even official governmental agency websites, can be reasonably subject to dispute and are not proper subjects for judicial notice.  (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 888-889; see also Huitt v. Southern Cal. Gas Co. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1586, 1605 fn. 10.)

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendant’s request for judicial notice is granted.

Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice is granted as to Exhibits A, B, and C.

DISCUSSION

A motion for judgment on the pleadings is the functional equivalent to a general demurrer.  (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.)  Like demurrers, motions for judgment on the pleadings challenge the legal sufficiency of the allegations, not their veracity.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  The Court “must accept as true all material facts properly pleaded, but does not consider conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or facts that are judicially noticed.”  (Stevenson Real Estate Services, Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Services, Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-1220.)

A.        The Complaint Sufficiently Alleges a Basis for Defendant’s Liability.

The Complaint alleges that Defendant is “owner of record and/or Beneficiary under a Short Form Deed of Trust and Assignment of Rents.”  (Complaint ¶ 7.)  An unlicensed cannabis dispensary operating under the name Compton Remedy was located at the property.  (Complaint ¶¶ 14-17.)  On May 16, 2023, Plaintiffs began contacting Defendant and Compton Remedy to inform them that they needed to take steps to ensure the illegal activity ceased.  (Complaint ¶ 18.)  Defendant’s attorney stated that Defendant would evict the cannabis business.  (Complaint ¶ 20.)

Defendant argues that he cannot be liable because he no longer owns or controls the subject property.  (Motion at p. 6.)  Through a grant deed dated October 30, 2023, Defendant granted the property to Sun Bay Group LLC.  (Defendant RJN, Ex. A.)

Plaintiffs seek, in part, civil penalties for violations of Los Angeles County Code sections 1.23.010 et seq., 22.140.134, 22.242.020 et seq.; Health and Safety Code section 11570 et seq.; and Business and Professions Code sections 17200, 26200 et seq.  These civil penalties may be imposed for each day that Defendant permitted a cannabis business to operate at the property.  (See Complaint ¶¶ 35, 47, 59, 71.)  The Complaint sufficiently alleges—and Defendant admits—that Defendant owned the property during some of the relevant time period.

The motion is denied on this ground.

B.        The Complaint Sufficiently Alleges Plaintiffs’ Standing.

Los Angeles County Code section  22.140.134 prohibits the operation of a marijuana business in the unincorporated portions of Los Angeles County.  Defendant argues that the property is in the incorporated areas of the City of Compton.  (Motion at p. 8.)

Plaintiffs allege that the property is “located in the unincorporated area of Los Angeles County at real property commonly known as 15320 Avalon Boulevard, Compton CA 90220.”  (Complaint ¶ 1.)  Although Compton is an incorporated city, “Compton” in the property’s address is not dispositive.  This is further supported by Defendant’s own grant deed, which indicates that the property (also known as “15320 Avalon Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90220”) is in the “Unincorporated area.”  (Defendant RJN, Ex. A.)

The motion is denied on this ground.

CONCLUSION

The motion for judgment on the pleadings is DENIED.
