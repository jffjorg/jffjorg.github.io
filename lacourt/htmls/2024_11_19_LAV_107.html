DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV02551    Hearing Date: November 19, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA 

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES 

NORTHWEST DISTRICT 

 

 

Kantresa Johnson et al., 

Plaintiffs. 

 

   v. 

 

TCT XVI, LLC and Does 1-50 

Defendants. 

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department 

    24VECV02551                             107 

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:        

Motion to Strike 

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]: Denied. 

 

 

  1. BACKGROUND¿ 

 

This is a landlord-tenant dispute. Plaintiffs Kantresa Johnson as individually and Guardian ad Litem for Aaron Johnson, Vanessa Johnson, and Marland Johnson¿(“Plaintiffs”) rent a unit located at 8504 Willis Ave. Panorama City, California, 91402 (the “Subject Property”). (Complaint ¶ 1.) Defendant TCT XVI, LLC is the owner and/or property manager of the Subject Property. (Complaint ¶ 14.) Plaintiffs entered a rental agreement for the Subject Property on or around December 2017. (Complaint ¶ 25.) On or around February 2023, Plaintiffs noticed toxic mold contamination in the Subject Property and notified management and/or their managing agents. (Complaint ¶ 26.) Defendants failed to remediate the conditions at the Subject Property. (Complaint ¶ 26.)  

On May 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1-50, alleging ten causes of action for breach of warranty of habitability: violation of Civil Code section 1941.1, breach of warranty of habitability: Health & Safety Code section 17920.3, breach of warranty of habitability: violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, negligence – premises liability – negligence per se, nuisance, intentional infliction of emotional distress, breach of contract, breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment, and unfair business practices: violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200, et. seq.  

 

On October 15, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint (the “Motion”). On November 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition, to which no reply was filed. 

¿ 

  1. PROCEDURAL MATTERS¿ 

¿ 

A. Timeliness of Filings¿ 

¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. (See also Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).)  

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion to consider an untimely pleading.¿ (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281-282.) 

 

Plaintiffs served Defendant with the complaint by personal service on June 4, 2024. (Proof of Service filed 06/06/24.) Defendant filed the Motion on October 15, 2024. October 15, 2024, is more than thirty days after June 4, 2024. The Motion is untimely under section 435, subdivision (b)(1), but this court exercises its discretion and considers the Motion. 

 

The Motion and Opposition were timely filed under section 1005, subdivision (b). 

¿ 

B. The Meet & Confer Requirement¿ 

¿ 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a motion to strike to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the motion to strike.  

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating she met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel by telephone and email prior to filing the Motion but they were unable to come to an agreement. (Mintesnot Declaration ¶ 2.)  

 

This court finds Defendant satisfied the meet and confer requirement. 

¿ 

  1. LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿ 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿ 

¿ 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.)¿ 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) To justify a punitive damages award, the defendant must be “guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”¿(Civ. Code., § 3294.) “He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff’s rights.” (Penner v. Falk (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 858, 867,) 

 

Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (c)(1) defines malice as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”   

 

 “A claim ‘for exemplary damage may be supported by pleading that the wrong was committed willfully or with a design to injure.’” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1055.) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)¿¿ 

¿ 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And, “’conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,’ within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].)¿ 

 

  1. DISCUSSION¿ 

¿ 

Defendant moves to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint referencing the punitive damages claim. (Notice of Motion pp. 2-3; Complaint ¶¶ 27, 31-32, 48, 80, 78, 83, 110, 116, 118, 119-122, 126, 132, 145, 155.) 

 

Defendant argues the complaint’s factual allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages because the “Complaint provides no factual basis to support the higher threshold required to warranty the imposition of punitive damages, and Defendants’ conduct as alleged in the Complaint is nothing more than conclusory. Additionally, Plaintiff’s allegations are so vague they are essentially meaningless for the purposes of pleading a claim for exemplary damages.” (Motion p. 6.)  

 

Plaintiffs argue they properly pled facts indicating Defendant’s malicious acts because the complaint alleges Defendant was aware of the toxic mold contamination but failed to abate it, Defendant did not notify Plaintiffs of the condition despite their knowledge of it, Defendant and their agents were aware that absent appropriate maintenance toxic mold contamination would develop, Defendant collected rent from Plaintiffs despite their knowledge of the mold contamination, and Defendant was provided adequate notice of the kind of conduct charged against them. (Opposition pp. 6-7.)  

 

In Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 920, a residential tenant “pleaded sufficient facts to support her prayer for exemplary damages” when her complaint both “alleged that defendant had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises including leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions” and “alleged that defendants ‘In maintaining said nuisance,… acted with full knowledge of the consequences thereof and the damage being caused to plaintiff, and their conduct was willful, oppressive and malicious.’”¿ 

 

Further, in Penner v. Falk, supra, 153 Cal.App.3d at p. 867, the Court of Appeal stated the pleadings adequately supported a punitive damages award because “[t]he pleadings sufficiently allege facts setting forth long existing physical conditions of the premises which portend danger for the tenants” and “[t]he pleadings also set out that respondents knew of those conditions for up to two years, had power to make changes, but failed to take corrective and curative measures.” The Court stated that “[i]f proven, these allegations would support an award of punitive damages.” (Id.)  

 

Plaintiffs allege that on or around February 2023, “Plaintiffs noticed toxic mold contamination in Subject Unit and immediately notified management and/or their managing agents, specifically, ‘Sauer’ of the toxic mold contamination in Plaintiffs’ Unit. Defendants and their agents and managers failed to remediate the uninhabitable conditions (specifically but not limited to, toxic mold contamination), at the Subject Property and Plaintiffs’ Unit.” (Complaint ¶ 26.) Plaintiffs also allege that “[o]n or around February 8, 2023, the Housing Authority of the [C]ity of Los Angeles inspector ‘JANET GALLARDO’ notified (inspection #CO360650) Defendants about the uninhabitable conditions of the Subject Unit and request[ed] the uninhabitable conditions to be resolved by March 02, 2023” but “[t]he uninhabitable conditions were not resolved for an extended period.” (Complaint ¶ 91.) And, Plaintiffs allege that “[o]n or around March 13, 2023, the Housing Authority of the [C]ity of Los Angeles inspector ‘SARAH SHOOSHANI’ notified (re-inspection #CO0363977) Defendants about the continuance uninhabitable conditions of the Subject Unit that was not abated and request the uninhabitable conditions to be resolved by March 28, 2023” but [t]he uninhabitable conditions was not resolved for an extended period.” (Complaint ¶ 92.) In sum, Plaintiffs allege Defendant was aware of the contamination by February 8, 2023, and the March 13, 2023, notification by the City of Los Angeles’ Housing Authority shows that Plaintiffs alleged Defendant knew the conditions were ongoing. Thus, like Stoiber v. Honeychuck, Plaintiffs allege Defendant had actual knowledge of the Subject Property’s defective condition. And, like Penner v. Falk, Plaintiffs state facts alleging long-existing physical conditions of the premises presenting a danger to the tenants. 

 

Plaintiffs further allege that “Defendants intentionally and willfully failed to remedy the issues raised by Plaintiffs by maintaining a company policy of apathy and/or denial. Defendants failed to remedy the toxic mold contamination at the Subject Property.” (Complaint ¶ 27.) Plaintiffs also allege that “Defendants aware of the toxic mold contamination at the complex affected multiple units and common areas for extended period, as prior and current tenants’ complaint about the toxic mold contamination in their units. Defendants failed to abate the toxic mold contamination from the Subject property and Subject Unit. More importantly, Defendants failed to disclose this material fact to plaintiffs, they failed to conduct reasonable inspections of the Subject [P]roperty, and they failed to take actions and abate the toxic mold contamination.” (Complaint ¶ 29.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege that “Defendants, and each of them, own other rental properties, and are experienced managers of residential property and are aware that absent appropriate maintenance of the toxic mold contamination serious uninhabitable conditions will develop that would seriously and materially affect the Plaintiffs’ quiet and peaceful enjoyment of the Subject Unit.” (Complaint ¶ 44.) Thus, like Stoiber v. Honeychuck, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant acted with knowledge of the consequences thereof and damages being caused to Plaintiffs. Further, like Penner v. Falk, these allegations indicate Defendant knew about the conditions but failed to take corrective measures.  

 

Finally, Plaintiffs allege that “Defendants’ actions and omissions were intentional, malicious, fraudulent and oppressive because they were done with a conscious disregard for the rights and safety of Plaintiffs, as well as other tenants’ subject to the same or similar uninhabitable conditions.” (Complaint ¶ 77.) Thus, like Stoiber v. Honeychuck, Plaintiffs allege Defendant’s conduct was willful, oppressive, and malicious. 

 

Defendant argues these allegations are conclusory. However, when read as a whole, Plaintiffs’ complaint alleges that Plaintiffs, on or around February 2023, notified management and/or their managing agents of the toxic mold contamination, inspectors from the City of Los Angeles’ Housing Authority notified Defendant about the uninhabitable conditions, Defendant as experienced managers were aware that toxic mold contamination would seriously affect Plaintiffs, but, despite that knowledge, Defendant failed to take action to remediate the contamination. (Complaint ¶¶ 26, 91-92, 44, 27.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege specific facts supporting the punitive damages award. 

 

Plaintiffs seek punitive damages for their implied warranty of habitability, nuisance, breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, and intentional infliction of emotional distress causes of action. (Opposition p. 4; Complaint ¶¶ 78, 116, 145, 155.) (While the Plaintiffs do not explicitly state they are seeking punitive damages in the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action section of the complaint, they generally seek punitive damages in the prayer for relief and state in their opposition that they are seeking punitive damages for the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action. (Opposition p. 4.)) 

 

Punitive¿damages¿. . . are recoverable for… the infliction of emotional distress.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th at p. 1055, internal citations omitted.) Punitive damages are also recoverable for an intentional tort nuisance cause of action. (Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d at p. 920.) Finally, punitive damages are available for a cause of action showing a “tortious breach of the warranty of habitability based on [a defendant’s] alleged intentional, malicious and outrageous conduct.” (Smith v. David (1981) 120 Cal.App.3d 101, 118 fn. 3.)   

 

“[W]hen the landlord has breached the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, but the tenant remains in possession of the premises, the tenant’s remedy is to ‘sue for breach of contract damages.” (Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 873, 902.) When the tenant remains in possession of the premises, “[t]here [is] no tort liability established that would entitle [them] to punitive damages.” (Id.)  

 

As to the eighth cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, Plaintiffs seek punitive damages by alleging that “Defendants’ conduct in continuously breaching the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment has been intentional, reckless, malicious and oppressive, thereby entitling Plaintiffs to recover punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial.” (Complaint ¶ 145.) Plaintiffs allege that they were “forced to vacate the subject property due to severe toxic mold contamination.” (Complaint ¶ 28.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege that they are no longer in possession of the premises and may, therefore, recover punitive damages. (See Ginsberg v Gamson, supra, 205 Cal.App.4th at pp. 898-898 [“[S]ome courts have implicitly or explicitly indicated a tenant may recover punitive damages in connection with a claim for breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, or identified the claim as a tort. However, all of those cases describe wrongful eviction claims. In other words, they are cases in which the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment is breached by a wrongful eviction.”]) 

 

  1. CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies the Motion.

  1. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

 

Defendant Jonathan Perlman (“Defendant”) is the former husband of Plaintiff David Pasternak’s (“Plaintiff”) daughter, Leah Pasternak (“Leah”). (First Amended Complaint “FAC” ¶ 10.) Leah and Defendant are obtaining a divorce. (FAC ¶ 10.)  

 

Between April 2015 and April 2023, Defendant and Plaintiff entered a series of agreements where Plaintiff agreed to pay Defendant’s mortgage payments, property taxes, utilities, and insurance and Defendant promised to repay Plaintiff “in cash or from sale proceeds” on a property located at 5187 Lindley Avenue, Tarzana, CA 91356 (the “Subject Property”). (FAC ¶¶ 10-11.) Defendant did not repay Plaintiff and the Subject Property “is now for sale [with] no escrow instructions to repay Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶¶ 10, 16.) 

 

On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 to 10, alleging six causes of action for breach of oral contract, fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, constructive trust, elder abuse, and unjust enrichment. 

 

On July 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Defendant and Does 1 to 10, alleging four causes of action for breach of oral contract, fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and elder abuse. 

 

On September 9, 2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint. On October 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On October 12, 2024, Defendant filed a reply. 

 

  1. PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS 

 

  1. Timeliness 

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿430.40(a).)  

 

Plaintiff submits a proof of service showing the first amended complaint was served on Defendant on July 8, 2024. (Proof of Service attached to FAC.) On August 8, 2024, Defendant filed a declaration in support of a thirty-day automatic extension to file the demurrer. Defendant then filed the demurrer on September 9, 2024. 

 

This court finds that the demurrer was timely filed. 

  1. Meet and Confer 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).) 

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating they contacted Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss the issues with the first amended complaint, but Defendant’s and Plaintiff’s counsel were unable to reach a resolution. (Nadel Declaration ¶ 2.)  

 

This court finds that Defendant satisfied the meet and confer requirement. 

 

  1. LEGAL STANDARD¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.) 

 

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).) A party may also demur because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) Further, a party may demur when the complaint alleges “an action founded upon contract” and “it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(g).)  

 

Demurrer for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.) 

¿ 

  1. DISCUSSION 

 

Defendant argues the first amended complaint is vague, it fails because it does not indicate whether the contract was written, oral, or implied by conduct, the fraud cause of action does not plead facts with particularity, the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action does not plead outrageous conduct, and the elder abuse cause of action does not detail when plaintiff became an elder. (Demurrer pp. 4-8.) 

 

Plaintiff’s opposition argues the first amended complaint sufficiently pleads fact supporting the four causes of action. (Opposition pp. 3-9.) 

 

  1. Breach of Contract 

 

  1. Cause of Action  

 

Defendant argues the first amended complaint does not meet the criteria for an oral or implied contract. (Demurrer p. 4.) 

 

The elements of a breach of contract claim are: (1) existence of a contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) resulting damage. (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N. Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) 

 

The first amended complaint alleges that “[o]n or about April 3, 2015[,] Plaintiff and Defendant entered into an oral agreement.” (FAC ¶ 30.) The first amended complaint also alleges that Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a second oral agreement in or about April 2020. (FAC ¶ 13.) Thus, the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges that the breach of contract claim is based on oral contracts.  

 

The first amended complaint alleges that the terms of the April 3, 2015, agreement between Defendant and Plaintiff were “1) Plaintiff would purchase [the Subject Property], 2) Defendant, his wife and their three (3) children would live there; 3) Defendant would maintain all debt on the house; 4) [the Subject Property] would be sold at a later date so that the Plaintiff can recoup his investment in [the Subject Property]; 5) [the Subject Property] sale proceeds would be used to repay Plaintiff for various credit(s) extended to the Defendant; [and] 6) until sold Defendant would hold [the Subject Property] in trust for Plaintiff, notwithstanding the characterization of its title.” (FAC ¶ 30.)  

 

The first amended complaint also alleges that, regarding the April 2020 agreement, “Defendant requested Plaintiff to ‘bail him out’ of debt several times” and “in exchange for Plaintiff’s payment of Defendant’s accrued debt, Plaintiff would be repaid by Defendant, or from the sale proceeds on [the Subject Property] when sold in the future.” (FAC ¶ 13.) Thus, the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges the existence of two contracts. 

 

The first amended complaint alleges that Plaintiff “has satisfied all covenants, conditions and performance of the agreement except those that are discharged or excused by defendants' material breaches.” (FAC ¶ 31.) Thus, the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges Plaintiff performed under the contracts or has an excuse for nonperformance. 

 

The first amended complaint also alleges that “Defendant has violated his promise to hold [the Subject Property] in trust for the Plaintiff by placing it for sale without escrow instructions to repay Plaintiff[,]” “Defendant has breached the agreement having failed to reimburse the Plaintiff on his initial investment for the purchase of [the Subject Property] at its present value of $1.3 million dollars (anticipated to be $1.5 million dollars by time of trial); by failing to reimburse the Plaintiff for all mortgage payments paid by the Plaintiff on [the Subject Property]; [and] by failing to reimburse the Plaintiff for all credit extended to the Defendant.” (FAC ¶¶ 32-33.) The first amended complaint further alleges that “Defendant is attempting to steal a property that is now worth over $1.3 million dollars for zero contribution on its original purchase, and failure to pay many mortgage payments and related expenses.” (FAC ¶ 34.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges that Defendant breached the contracts by failing to reimburse Plaintiff. 

 

Finally, the first amended complaint alleges that “[t]he Plaintiff continues to pay expenses and the mortgage to date without contribution by the Defendant and ha[s] been damaged in an amount not yet fully known but in an amount no less than $1.5 million dollars” and “Plaintiff has been damaged in an amount to be proven at trial but in an amount not less than $1.3 million . . .  plus interest according to proof for compelling Plaintiff to expend legal and attorneys fees to rectify the problem(s) directly caused by Defendant’s breach of the oral contract(s).” (FAC ¶ 36.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges damages resulting from Defendant’s breach. 

 

Thus, the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges a cause of action for breach of contract. 

 

  1. Statute of Limitations 

 

Defendant argues the first amended complaint contains “no allegations to explain why the claims of the financial transactions more than two (2) years before the filing of the lawsuit are not time barred.” (Demurrer p. 5.) 

 

An oral contract is governed by a two-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 339.)  

 

The first amended complaint alleges that “[i]n April 2023 Defendant and Leah separated. Defendant was removed from [the Subject Property] pursuant to the grant of a 5 year Domestic Violence Restraining Order. During this time period, and to the present, Defendant stopped paying the mortgage and other related debts to [the Subject Property].” (FAC ¶¶ 14-15.) The first amended complaint further alleges that “[the Subject Property] is now for sale and no escrow instructions to repay Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 16.)  

 

These allegations imply that Defendant breached the contract in April 2023. Plaintiff filed the complaint on June 5, 2024. Thus, the first amended complaint is not barred by the statute of limitations. 

 

  1. Statute of Frauds 

 

Defendant argues “many allegations of real property interest require some allegation of a written agreement, otherwise the Stat[u]te of Frauds (Cal. Civ. C. 1624) would apply” and the first amended complaint does “not explain why no interest the plaintiff claims exists was never supported by any writing.” (Demurrer p. 5.) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1624, subdivision (a)(3) provides “The following contracts are invalid, unless they, or some note or memorandum thereof, are in writing and subscribed by the party to be charged or by the party's agent . . . An agreement for the leasing for a longer period than one year, or for the sale of real property, or of an interest therein; such an agreement, if made by an agent of the party sought to be charged, is invalid, unless the authority of the agent is in writing, subscribed by the party sought to be charged.” 

 

The pleading alleges that “Defendant has violated his promise to hold [the Subject Property]” and “Defendant is attempting to steal a property that is now worth over $1.3 million dollars for zero contribution on its original purchase, and failure to pay many mortgage payments and related expenses.” (FAC ¶¶ 32-34.) Thus, on its face, the pleading alleges the contract was made to secure an interest in real property. However, Plaintiff has not alleged the existence of, nor submitted, a writing to satisfy the statute of frauds. (See Sterling v. Taylor (2007) 40 Cal.4th 757, 765 [“The statue of frauds does not require a written contract; a ‘note or memorandum . . . subscribed by the party to be charged’ is adequate.”]) 

 

Thus, the demurrer to Plaintiff’s first cause of action is sustained. 

 

  1. Fraud 

 

The elements of fraud are: (1) a misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent to defraud; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1060.) Fraud elements must be alleged with particularity. (Id.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 993.) 

 

The first amended complaint alleges that “[o]n or about April, 2020, Defendant made the fraudulent representation of material facts to the Plaintiff. Those representations were that in exchange for transferring title for [the Subject Property] to Defendant and LEAH, as a probate tool, the Parties would hold [the Subject Property] in trust for the Plaintiff. Defendant further fraudulently represented to the Plaintiff that [the Subject Property] would be sold in the future and the Plaintiff would be paid for its purchased investment and credit extended to the Defendant.” (FAC ¶ 39.)  The first amended complaint further alleges that “Defendant’s representations were in fact false. The truth was Defendant wanted the Plaintiff to purchase a house for his family to live in. Defendant’s plan was to continue to make sporadic payments on the mortgage and bills relying upon Plaintiff to be his bank. Defendant after gaining Plaintiff’s trust convinced the Plaintiff to convey [the Subject Property’s] title to Defendant and LEAH erasing Plaintiff’s name from title. Defendant concealed and suppressed these facts from the Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 40.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges Plaintiff made misrepresentations. 

 

The first amended complaint also alleges that “Defendant knew his representations to be false at the time when making said representation(s).” Thus, the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges that Defendant had knowledge of their falsity. 

 

The first amended complaint further alleges that “Defendant’s representations were made with the . . . intent to defraud and induce the Plaintiff to act as described herein[,]” and “[s]aid fraudulent acts were done in such a manner to intentionally induce Plaintiff to spend $745,000 to purchase [the Subject Property] to Defendant’s benefit and the Plaintiff’s financial detriment.” (FAC ¶¶ 39, 41.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges Defendant intended to defraud Plaintiff. 

 

The first amended complaint alleges that “Plaintiff acted in justifiable reliance upon the truth of Defendant’s representations” and Plaintiff was “induced to and did rely on the representations of Defendant[.]” (FAC ¶¶ 39, 43.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges Plaintiff justifiably relied on Defendant’s misrepresentations.  

 

Finally, although the first amended complaint alleges that “[s]aid fraudulent acts were done in such a manner to intentionally induce Plaintiff to spend $745,000 to purchase [the Subject Property] to Defendant’s benefit and the Plaintiff’s financial detriment” the first amended complaint does not clearly allege that the $745,000 to purchase the Subject Property are the resulting damages from Defendant’s fraud. 

 

Thus, because Plaintiff has not alleged resulting damages, the demurrer to Plaintiff’s second cause of action is sustained. 

 

  1. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress 

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action fails because it does not plead outrageous conduct. (Demurrer p. 7.) 

 

¿“The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) the defendant engages in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffers extreme or severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant's extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's extreme or severe emotional distress.” (So v. Shin (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 671.)¿ To be outrageous, the conduct must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.” (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.) 

 

The first amended complaint incorporates paragraphs 1 through 46 into the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action. (FAC ¶ 48.) Those paragraphs allege that Plaintiff purchased the Subject Property in April 2015 for $745,000 and Plaintiff and Defendant entered into an oral agreement in April 2015, where Plaintiff and Defendant would hold title to the Subject Property as joint tenants and Defendant would maintain all debt for the Subject Property. (FAC ¶¶ 11-12.) The first amended complaint also alleges that Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a second oral agreement in April 2020, where Plaintiff would pay Defendant’s debt and Plaintiff would be repaid by Defendant or repaid upon the sale of the Subject Property. (FAC ¶¶ 11, 13.) The first amended complaint further alleges that Defendant stopped paying the debts on the Subject Property, Plaintiff made the payments, “Defendant gained the benefit of retaining what became a 50-50 community property interest in [the Subject Property] with Leah in 2020” and the Subject Property “is now for sale [with] no escrow instructions to repay Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶¶ 15-16.) The first amended complaint alleges “Defendant is attempting to steal a property that is now worth over $1.3 million dollars for zero contribution on its purchase, mortgage and related expenses and maintenance paid by Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 18.) Next, the first amended complaint alleges “Defendant complained to Plaintiff that his grand children need a home to live in. It has been discovered that in truth, Defendant used his own children (i.e. needing a place to live) as an excuse and instrument to impose undue influence over elderly, retired, and caring Grandparent(s)” and further alleges that “Defendant executed a plan to rob his in laws of their hard earned money and life savings under the false premise that the Plaintiff was providing a benefit to the grandchildren.” (FAC ¶¶ 22.) And that “Defendant used his position and expertise as a psychological expert and licensed mental health care professional, to imposed undue influence upon the Plaintiff” and “Defendant falsely represented to the Plaintiff that transferring title in [the Subject Property] to Defendant and LEAH would be in their best interests.” (FAC ¶ 24.) Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “[o]nce Defendant convinced Plaintiff to transfer title from Plaintiff -Defendant Joint Tenants to Jonathan and Leah, community property, Defendant ‘still’ did not want to be obligated on the expense(s) that go with owning a home [and] Defendant continued to readily accept the benefit(s) of Plaintiff’s payments when Defendant was running short on money, which was often.” (FAC ¶ 25.) 

 

If true, these allegations demonstrate a callous disregard for Plaintiff, but that does not mean the allegations rise to the level as being “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.” (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc., supra, 129 Cal.App.4th at 1259; see also Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780-781 [“Here, the allegations in the first amended complaint consist of defendants misleading plaintiffs into believing they would be compensated in an amount¿that would allow them to retire if they continued to work for West Coast until the company was sold. While the allegations of defendants’ conduct if true, demonstrate a callous disregard for plaintiffs' professional and personal well-being, the alleged conduct as stated is not extreme or outrageous to support a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.”]) Accordingly, the first amended complaint does not sufficiently allege that Defendant’s conduct was outrageous. 

 

The first amended complaint alleges that Defendant’s actions “were intentional, extreme, and outrageous.” (FAC ¶ 48.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges Defendant acted with intent.  

 

The first amended complaint also alleges that “[a]s a direct legal and proximate result of such acts of Defendants, and DOES 1 through 10, and each of them, Plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress which has caused Plaintiff to sustain severe, serious and permanent injuries to [his] person[.]” (FAC ¶ 49.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges Plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress and Defendant’s conduct was the cause of Plaintiff’s severe emotional distress. 

 

Thus, because Plaintiff has not alleged that Defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct, the demurrer to Plaintiff’s third cause of action is sustained.  

 

  1. Elder Abuse 

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s elder abuse cause of action fails because it does not detail when plaintiff became an elder. (Demurrer pp. 4-8.) 

 

Financial elder abuse occurs when a person or entity: “(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. (2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. (3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(a).)  

 

“A person or entity shall be deemed to have taken, secreted, appropriated, obtained, or retained property for a wrongful use if, among other things, the person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains the property and the person or entity knew or should have known that this conduct is likely to be harmful to the elder or dependent adult.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(b).) “For purposes of this section, a person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property when an elder or dependent adult is deprived of any property right, including by means of an agreement, donative transfer, or testamentary bequest, regardless of whether the property is held directly or by a representative of an elder or dependent adult.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(c).)  An “’[e]lder’ means any person residing in [California], 65 years of age or older.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.27.)  

 

The first amended complaint alleges that “Plaintiff was 65 years of age or older at the time of Defendant’s conduct.” (FAC ¶ 54.) The first amended complaint further alleges that “Plaintiff is an individual residing in or doing business in the County of Los Angeles, State of California.” (FAC ¶ 1.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges that Plaintiff is an elder living in California. 

 

The first amended complaint also alleges that “[o]n or about April, 2020, Defendant made the fraudulent representation of material facts to the Plaintiff. Those representations were that in exchange for transferring title for [the Subject Property] to Defendant and LEAH, as a probate tool, the Parties would hold [the Subject Property] in trust for the Plaintiff. Defendant further fraudulently represented to the Plaintiff that [the Subject Property] would be sold in the future and the Plaintiff would be paid for its purchased investment and credit extended to the Defendant.” (FAC ¶ 39.) The first amended complaint further alleges that Plaintiff has made the associated payments, . . . Defendant gained the benefit of retaining what became a 50-50 community property interest in [the Subject Property] with Leah in 2020” and “[the Subject Property] is now for sale and no escrow instructions to repay Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 16.) Finally, the first amended complaint alleges that “Defendant represented to the Plaintiff a future sale would reimburse the Plaintiff for all credit extended to the Defendant. Plaintiff believed Defendant’s representations to be true when they were made, not knowing his secret intent was to breach the agreement upon transfer of title. Defendant never had any intention of holding [the Subject Property] in trust for Plaintiff. When Defendant represented - promised to hold [the Subject Property] in trust for Plaintiff that representation was false when made.” (FAC ¶ 28.) Thus, the first amended complaint alleges that Defendant retained Plaintiff’s real property with the intent to defraud Plaintiff. 

 

Accordingly, because the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges a financial elder abuse cause of action, the demurrer to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is overruled.  

  

  1. LEAVE TO AMEND 

¿ 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.)  

 

This court finds that there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiff can successfully amend the complaint and grants Plaintiff leave to amend. 

 

  1. CONCLUSION¿ 

¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer and grants Plaintiff leave to amend.
