The heading on your e-mail should contain the case name, number, hearing date, and that you submit. The message should indicate your name, contact information, and the party you represent. Please note, the above e-mail address is to inform the court of your submission on the tentative ruling. All other inquiries will not receive a response.

If there are no appearances by either side and no submission on the Court's tentative ruling, the matter will be placed OFF CALENDAR. 

Due to overcrowding concerns of COVID-19, all parties shall make every effort to schedule a remote appearance via LACourtConnect (https://my.lacourt.org/laccwelcome) for their next hearing. The parties shall register with LACourtConnect at least 2 hours prior to their scheduled hearing time.

Case Number: 23STLC06603    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Comer v. NJS Construction, Inc., et al.

MOTION TO VACATE DEFAULT

(CCP §§ 473(a)(1))


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Jack Saiege’s Motion to Vacate Entry of Default is GRANTED. DEFENDANT JACK SAIEGE IS TO FILE AND SERVE A RESPONSIVE PLEADING TO THE COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

 

                                                                                                                               

ANALYSIS:

 

On October 16, 2023, Plaintiff John Comer (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendants NJS Construction Inc (“Defendant NJS”) and Jack Saiege (“Defendant Saiege”). Following Defendant Saiege’s failure to file a responsive pleading, the Court entered his default on January 25, 2024.

 

Defendant Saiege filed the instant Motion to Vacate Entry of Default on March 25, 2024. Plaintiff filed an untimely opposition on April 8, 2024 and Defendant Saiege replied on April 16, 2024. The reply sought a short continuance to allow for a response on the merits. At the hearing on April 23, 2024, the Court continued the matter to May 29, 2024. (Minute Order, 04/23/24.) Defendant Saiege filed a substantive reply on May 3, 2024 and Plaintiff filed a supplemental declaration on May 15, 2024. Defendant Saiege then filed a supplemental reply on May 22, 2024.

 

To the extent Defendant Saiege objects to Plaintiff’s supplemental declaration, the Court finds he had an opportunity to respond to it in the supplemental reply such that there is no prejudice. Therefore, the Court will consider all the papers filed by the parties.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant Saiege moves for an order vacating the entry of default dated January 25, 2024 due to improper service of the Summons and Complaint. The Motion is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), which states that “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper . . . enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.” (Code Civ. Code, § 473, subd. (a)(1).) The Motion further relies on the service statute set forth at Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20 to argue that service did not comply with the requirements for substitute service.

 

“When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process ‘the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.’”  (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.) A proof of service containing a declaration from a registered process server invokes a rebuttable presumption affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return. (Cal. Evid. Code, § 647; see American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390.) The party seeking to defeat service of process must present sufficient evidence to show that the service did not take place as stated.  (See Palm Property Investments, LLC v. Yadegar (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 1419, 1428; cf. People v. Chavez (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1471, 1483 [“If some fact be presumed, the opponent of that fact bears the burden of producing or going forward with evidence sufficient to overcome or rebut the presumed fact.”].)  Merely denying service took place without more is insufficient to overcome the presumption.  (See Yadegar, supra, 194 Cal.App.4th at 1428.)

 

The proof of substitute service filed on January 2, 2024 is attested to by a registered process server thereby placing the burden of producing evidence overcoming the truth of the facts in the return upon Defendant Saiege. (Proof of Service, filed 01/02/24, ¶7.) The proof of substitute service attests that Defendant Saiege was sub-served by leaving the papers at 23705 Vanowen St., #152, West Hills, California on November 8, 2023 with Adrian P., the owner of the post office box business. (Id. at ¶¶4-5.) The proof of service also indicates that this sub-service was effectuated because “physical address unknown.” (Id. at ¶5(b)(3).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20, subdivision (a) allows for service at a defendant’s usual mailing address, if no physical address is known. Although Defendant Saige first argues that this usual mailing address cannot include a commercial mail receiving agency (“CMRA”) because the statute expressly excludes United States Postal Service post office boxes, the Court finds this exclusion does not encompass CMRAs. The statute, in subdivision (c) specifically refers to CMRAs but subdivision (a) uses the exact phrase “United States Postal Service post office box.” Given that the statute uses different phrases to refer to different types of post office boxes, it cannot be assumed that “United States Postal Service post office box” also includes a CMRA. Nor does the exclusion of CMRAs from the definition of United States Postal Service post office boxes render subdivision (c) of Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20 moot. Subdivision (c) is intended by its plain language to be an exception to the reasonable diligence requirement of subdivision (b): “Notwithstanding subdivision (b), if the only address reasonably known for the person to be served is a private mailbox obtained through a commercial mail receiving agency, service of process may be effected on the first delivery attempt . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20, subd. (b).) Subdivision (c) also does not render subdivision (a) moot because the former includes numerous requirements for service under Business & Professions Code section 17538.5. Those requirements do not exist for service accomplished pursuant to Code of Civl Procedures section 415.20, subdivision (a).

 

Therefore, Defendant Saiege must show either that the Vanowen Street address is not his usual mailing address or that Plaintiff does not know his physical address to overcome the presumption of proper service as stated in the proof of service. Defendant Saiege declares that, at all relevant times, he has resided in Simi Valley, California. (Motion, Saiege Decl., ¶2.) The Vanowen Street Address is the mailing address for Defendant NJS, Defendant Saiege’s construction company, as reflected by its Articles of Incorporation filed on March 21, 2016 and Statement of Information filed on February 1, 2024. (Motion, Saiege Decl., ¶¶4-7.) While the exhibits that purport to demonstrate this address are not attached to Defendant Saiege’s declaration, he reiterates that the post office box is rented on behalf of Defendant NJS to receive mail. (Id. at ¶8.) Defendant Saiege also declares that he does not use that location as an office for NJS or conduct any NJS business at the Vanowen Street address, other than collecting mail for the company. (Id. at ¶¶10-15.) This evidence is sufficient to demonstrate that the Vanowen Street address is solely for Defendant NJS, and not Defendant Saiege’s usual mailing address.

 

The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate that the Vanowen Street address is Defendant Saiege’s usual mailing address. Plaintiff argues that Defendant Saiege declares he has visited the Vanowen Street post office box once or twice a week since 2016. This, however, only shows that Defendant Saiege regularly checks the post office box for Defendant NJS’ mail. It does not demonstrate that Defendant Saiege uses the post office box for himself as an individual. Even if Defendant Saiege rents the Vanowen Street post office box in his name on behalf of Defendant NJS, as Plaintiff argues, this does not mean he uses it as his personal mailing address. Therefore, service at the Vanowen Street Address was not compliant with Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20, subdivision (a).

 

Subdivision (c) of the substitute service statute also allows for service on an individual at a CMRA, if it is the only address reasonably known. Plaintiff raised this statute in opposition to the Motion and argues that he did not reasonably know of any other address for Defendant Saiege because a request to the Department of Motor Vehicles for additional information requires the individual’s driver’s license number or date of birth, and Plaintiff does not have that information. Defendant Saiege’s reply responds that there are other ways to locate a person’s address but does not provide any other information regarding his reasonably known address. Plaintiff’s supplemental declaration further demonstrates that background checks he performed in April and May 2022 did not turn up any other address for “Jack Saiege” in California. (Supp. Decl., filed 05/15/24, pp. 1-2 and Exh. 1.) While this evidence is sufficient to demonstrate that no address other than the one located on Vanowen Street is reasonably known for Defendant Saiege, it does not demonstrate that all the other requirements for service under subdivision (c) have been met, as set forth in Business & Professions Code section 17583.5. Therefore, the Court cannot find that service on Defendant Saiege complied with Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20, subdivision (c).

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Jack Saiege’s Motion to Vacate Entry of Default is GRANTED. DEFENDANT JACK SAIEGE IS TO FILE AND SERVE A RESPONSIVE PLEADING TO THE COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

Case Number: LAM15K08573    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: 26

 

ACIC v. Edwin, et al.

MOTION FOR ASSIGNMENT AND TURNOVER ORDER

(CCP § 708.510)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Judgment Creditor American Contractors Indemnity Company’s Motion for Assignment Order is DENIED.

 

 

ANALYSIS:  

 

On July 10, 20215, Plaintiff American Contractors Indemnity Company (“Judgment Creditor”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Francis Edwin Azevedo (“Judgment Debtor) for breach of contract. On September 29, 2015, this Court entered default judgment against Judgment Debtor for the total amount of $12,112.49.

 

On March 20, 2024, Judgment Creditor filed the instant Motion for Assignment Order. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.510, subdivision (a), the moving statute, the Court may order the judgment debtor to assign to the judgment creditor or to an appointed receiver all or part of a right to payment due or to become due. The types of payments that can be assigned include wages due from the federal government if not subject to a withholding order, rents, commissions, royalties, patent or copyright payments, and insurance policy loan value. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (a).)

 

Relevant factors the Court may take into consideration when making an assignment order include the judgment debtor’s reasonable requirements if they are a natural person, payments the judgment debtor is required to make to satisfy other judgments and wage assignments, the amount remaining due on the judgment, and the amount to be received in satisfaction of the right to payment that may be assigned.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (c).) Construing all the applicable statutes together, the “assignment order” contemplated by Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.510, et seq. must include a court order that assigns a right to payment outright (not simply an order directing the judgment debtor to do so). 

 

Here, Judgment Creditor seeks an order (1) assigning payments made to Ed’s Custom Truck Bodies, Inc. (“Ed’s Custom”) to Judgment Creditor; (2) assigning payments made out of Ed’s Custom’s account(s) to Judgment Debtor to Judgment Creditor; (3) turning over any payments made by Ed’s Custom or out of Ed’s Custom’s account(s) to Judgment Debtor to Judgment Creditor; (4) restraining Judgment Debtor or any servant, agent, employee, attorney, or anyone else acting on his behalf or jointly with him from assigning or otherwise disposing of the right to payment; and (5) compelling Judgment Debtor to provide, file, and serve an accounting every other month as a means of monitoring Judgment Debtor’s activity and to ensure compliance with the Court’s orders. (Motion, pp. 1:24-2:18.)

 

On September 29, 2015, judgment was entered in Judgment Creditor’s favor and against Judgment Debtor in the amount of $12,112.49. (Motion, Murray Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.) Furthermore, Judgment Creditor has attempted to enforce its judgment but has only obtained $10,961.18 through bank levies and voluntary payments. (Id. at ¶ 3, Exh. 2.) The remaining interest amounts to $10,447.30. (Id. at ¶13 and Exh. 10.) Judgment Creditor also contends that it incurred post-judgment attorney’s fees and costs of $5,927.80. (Id. at ¶13.) Judgment Creditor subpoenaed Ed’s Custom’s bank records, which identify Judgment Debtor as the company’s CEO/Owner. (Id. at ¶11 and Exh. 8.) The records also show that wages are not paid from Ed’s Custom to Judgment Debtor but that cash and debit card withdrawals are made from the company’s bank account. (Id. at ¶12 and Exh. 9.) Judgment Creditor contends that these withdrawals appear to be Judgment Debtor taking money out of Ed’s Custom’s account for himself. (Id. at ¶12.)

 

This evidence does not demonstrate that the withdrawals from Ed’s Custom’s bank account are for Judgment Debtor to use for himself. Judgment Creditor does not identify any specific withdrawals and from what information it can be determined whether that money was used for the company or for Judgment Debtor as an individual. It can only speculate that Judgment Debtor pays himself from these withdrawals. The bank records only show that Judgment Debtor is the CEO/Owner of a company, has access to that company’s bank account, and makes sporadic withdrawals. The request to assign payments made to Ed’s Custom to Judgment Creditor, or to assign payments made out of Ed’s Custom’s account(s) to Judgment Debtor to Judgment Creditor, therefore, is denied.

 

Judgment Creditor also asks for an order turning over any payments made by Ed’s Custom or out of Ed’s Custom’s account(s) to Judgment Debtor to Judgment Creditor. Again, there is no evidence provided to show which payments were made by Ed’s Custom “to” Judgment Debtor in the sense that they were used by Judgment Debtor for himself, instead of for the company. Furthermore, section 708.510 does not provide for a turnover order. The statute only allows the Court to order assignment of “all or part of a right to payment due or to become due.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (a).) The request for a turnover order is also denied.

 

Finally, as there is no evidence of payments made by Ed’s Custom to Judgment Debtor are for his use as an individual and not for the company, the requests to restrain the assignment of payments or for an accounting are premature.

 

Conclusion

 

Judgment Creditor American Contractors Indemnity Company’s Motion for Assignment Order is DENIED.
