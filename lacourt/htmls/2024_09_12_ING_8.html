Case Number: 20TRCV00847    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 September 12, 2024

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   20TRCV00847

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Drakk Holdings, LLC v. PSIP SN Vermont LLC, et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant, PSIP SN Vermont, LLC

¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiff, Drakk Holdings, LLC

¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        January 27, 2025

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Summary Judgment

                                                (2) DRAKK’s objection to PSIP’s Notice of Motion to Quash Deposition Ruling

¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                  (1)  GRANTED.  The Court has several questions for DRAKK’s counsel noted below from its earlier tentative, but the evidence from the Zion Bank deposition has not raised a triable issue of material fact.   The Court is engaged in a jury trial, so oral argument will need to be focused and to the point as the Court has already heard extensive oral argument on this MSJ. 

                                                (2) DRAKK’s objection OVERRULED. The MTQ is mooted by the Zion Bank deposition having been taken

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿  

 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

On November 17, 2020, Plaintiff DRAKK Holdings, LLC (“DRAKK”) filed this action against Defendant, PSIP SN Vermont, LLC (“PSIP”). On September 29, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint. On November 30, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleging cases of action for: (1) Specific Performance/Express Written Contract; (2) Specific Performance/Promissory Estoppel; (3) Specific Performance/Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Promissory Estoppel and Money Damages; (5) Fraud & Deceit; and (6) Negligent Misrepresentation.¿ 

 

This case involves a dispute between Plaintiff DRAKK (buyer) and Defendant PSIP (seller), regarding a purchase and sale agreement (“PSA”) for real property. Plaintiff claims in its complaint that Defendant failed to complete the sale of its subdivided parcel under its PSA. On the other hand, Defendant asserts DRAKK failed to complete the purchase of the subdivided property because it did not have the funds in hand to pay the purchase price on time, and that DRAKK’s claims to “financial ability” to borrow or sell assets to get the funds at some future time is not legally sufficient to prove DRAKK was “ready and able” to purchase on time.

 

Defendant has filed a Motion for Summary Adjudication as to all six of the causes of action asserting that Plaintiff’s causes of action have no merit, that the necessary elements of each cannot be established, and there is a complete defense to each. Further, Defendant contends that because this Court can grant a Motion for Summary Adjudication to each of the causes of action that Summary Judgment can be granted on the grounds that the entire TAC has no merit, there are no triable issues of material fact, and PSIP is entitled to judgment against Drakk as a matter of law.

 

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On August 15, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion for Summary Adjudication and Summary Judgment. On March 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On March 22, 2024, Defendant filed a reply brief. The motion hearing was continued to March 14, 2024.

 

Further, on March 1, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion to Quash. On April 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On April 19, 2024, Defendant filed a reply brief. The original hearing date of the Motion to Quash was on March 14, 2024.

 

After posting a tentative ruling to grant the defense MSJ with areas for oral argument outlined, and after taking extensive oral argument at the March 14, 2024 hearing, this Court continued the hearing on the motion for summary judgment and the Motion to Quash Nonparty subpoenas to July 16, 2024.  In so doing, the Court ordered the parties to submit the first set of supplemental briefs, not to exceed five pages, on the narrow issues discussed by this Court at the hearing on March 14. II. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A.    Legal Standard

 

 A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411

 

            Further, where there has been no timely response to a Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300.) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion. Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required. 

 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Here, the Court notes that based on the moving papers, and lack of opposition brief, that Plaintiff has entirely failed to respond to the Moving Defendants’ propounded discovery. Accordingly, this Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s initial response to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s initial response to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s initial response to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. A.    Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300 provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that . . .”[a]n answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.”  (Code Civ. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3)).

(2)  If Plaintiff’s counsel did not telephonically agree to the extension to provide supplemental responses as well as Ford’s deadline to file its motion to compel further, why would Plaintiff’s counsel fail to respond detailing that such was never agreed to? Instead, the Court notes that the meet and confer evidence provided by Plaintiff is the exact same as that provided by Defendant Ford, detailing efforts on the part of Ford to meet and confer. Plaintiff has not filed any evidence such as a reply from Plaintiff’s counsel disputing any of the communications or agreements allegedly reached telephonically between the parties.   Depending on Plaintiff’s responses, the Court is inclined to find that Ford sufficiently met and conferred in good faith prior to the filing of this motion to compel further.   

 

NOTE:   Given the volume of cases pending between the counsel’s respective offices, some consolidation and coordination of discovery efforts will obviously need to be required.  Further, some level of cooperation will be required on a going-forward basis, and a recognition of the fact that because of the sheer number of cases pending between the parties, they should consider sample or universal extension agreements when there are limited deponents or verifiers of discovery responses available, or where the handling attorney is on vacation or in trial.   For example, the responding party might be automatically granted a standard 21-day additional period of time to object and otherwise respond, in order to avoid the receipt of a multitude of objections-only responses.  As a practical matter, the responding party must assert objections only if a requested extension to respond is not granted, which results in more discovery motions, more hearings, more consumption of non-productive time, and greater animosity between counsel.  There IS a better way, which the Court encourages the parties to consider going forward.  Further, if a party seeks to rely on a “document dump” as the way it intends to answer interrogatories, the Court discusses below what CCP section 2030.230 requires to answer interrogatories by reference to produced documents.

Clearly the questions are relevant to Plaintiff’s case as they specifically ask Plaintiff to provide the facts on which she bases each allegation in the complaint. The complaint is filled with just that – allegations. As such, it is an improper and evasive response to point Ford to allegations when Ford is seeking facts now that the parties have been able to engage in discovery.  A party seeking punitive damages limited only by the financial condition of the defendant and under current SCOTUS precedent up to 9 times the economic damages should expect discovery requests as to the factual basis for a punitive damages cause of action. 

 

            As to Special Interrogatories Nos. 25, 27, 29, 31, 33, 35, 37, 39, 40, 42, 44, 46, 48, 50, 52, 54, 56, 58, 60, and 62, Ford asked Plaintiff to identify the documents that support the facts identified in the above discussed interrogatories seeking Plaintiff’s material facts.  Instead of providing complete responses listing the documents provide, even if already provided, with the bates-stamped identifiers, Plaintiff merely refers Defendant generally to the documents produced in connection with Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Requests for Production, Set One, to the extent applicable.

 

            Plaintiff’s responses are not sufficient responses to any of the interrogatories seeking the corresponding relevant documents. In the multitude of documents produced in lemon law cases, Plaintiffs as well as Defendants are required to provide direct responses to the discovery and to specify the exact documents that speak to the interrogatory. As such, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion as to these interrogatories as well.  CCP Section 2020.230 provides a detailed process for answering an interrogatory by reference to produced documents.  It states as follows: 

“If the answer to an interrogatory would necessitate the preparation or the making of a compilation . . . or summary of or from the documents of the party to whom the interrogatory is directed, and if the burden or expense of preparing or making it would be substantially the same for the party propounding the interrogatory as for the responding party, it is a sufficient answer to that interrogatory TO REFER TO THIS SECTION AND TO SPECIFY THE WRITINGS FROM WHICH THE ANSWER MAY BE DERIVED OR ASCERTAINED. This specification shall be in sufficient detail to permit the propounding party to locate and to identify, as readily as the responding party can, the documents from which the answer may be ascertained.”  (capitalization and emphasis added.) 

The Court’s interpretation of Section 2030.230 is that for each interrogatory as to which a full and complete answer is found and contained in produced documents, the responding party shall, separately for each interrogatory, list the Bates numbers of the produced documents from which the full and complete answer to the interrogatory may be found.  Thus, different interrogatories will reference different Bates numbers.  This avoids the unsavory practice of a “document dump” where hundreds or thousands of pages are provided without any effort to identify the subset of the produced documents that are responsive to each different interrogatory. 

            Lastly, Ford notes that Special Interrogatories Nos. 57 and 59 ask Plaintiff to list all damages “suffered as a result of Ford’s failure to disclose material facts.” In Plaintiff’s response, Ford notes that Plaintiff only makes a vague reference that Plaintiff is “entitled to maximum amount of damages allowed by the law.”  This is an insufficient response. The thrust of the interrogatory is to require plaintiff to specify the extra-contractual damages claimed in addition to non-economic damages which are subject to the jury’s determination based on the evidence and their common sense, without any formula or any fixed standard in making that determination.  Ford’s motion is GRANTED and Plaintiff must provide a more complete response, verify what elements of economic damages are sought and in what amount beyond the refund of purchase price and the incidental damages permitted under the Song-Beverly Act, plus any categories of non-economic damages.

 

C.    Sanctions

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, subdivision (d), “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).) Here, Ford has requested the Court impose monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the amount of $560. However, Ford’s counsel does not indicate what criteria were used in coming up with this amount. Instead, Ford’s counsel merely seeks $500 in attorney work done on the motion and $60 for the filing fee. Although the amount is reasonable, the Court notes that Defense counsel’s declaration needs to break down how the amount was calculated.

 

Sanctions will be discussed further at the hearing during oral argument after the Court has a bigger picture of the meet and confer efforts. Nonetheless, the Court’s inclination is to find the amount requested by Defendant to be reasonable.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Ford’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED. Ford’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is tentatively granted pending oral argument on the meet and confer issues. Plaintiff’s further responses are due to Defendant Ford on or before October 11, 2024.

 

Ford is ordered to give notice.
