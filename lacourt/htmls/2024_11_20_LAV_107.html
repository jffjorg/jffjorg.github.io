DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV01169    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Dana Drabinsky,

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

4629 Fulton, LLC, et al.

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   24VECV01169                       107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

Motion to Strike

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]: Granted.

 

Grant with leave to amend.

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

This is a landlord-tenant dispute. Plaintiff Dana Drabinsky (“Plaintiff”) leased an apartment located at 4629 Fulton Avenue, Sherman Oaks, 91423 (the “Subject Property”). (First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) ¶¶ 14-15.) Defendants M West Holdings, 4629 Fulton, LLC, Polaris Property Management, LLC, Cushman & Wakefield, Inc. and Defendant Doe 1 Pinnacle Property Management Services California Corp. owned, controlled, and/or managed the Subject Property. (FAC ¶¶ 1-6, 14.) Plaintiff was exposed to mold at the Subject Property for two years and, on March 24, 2022, Plaintiff suffered an electric shock while using the shower. (FAC ¶¶ 18, 27.)

On March 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants 4629 Fulton, LLC, M West Holdings, LLC, Cushman & Wakefield, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield Global Services, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield of California, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield U.S., Inc., and Does 1 to 100, alleging three causes of action for negligence, premises liability, and negligent hiring, supervision, training, and retention.

On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the complaint substituting Pinnacle Property Management Services California Corp. for Doe 1.

On May 24, 2024, Defendants and Cross-Complaints 4629 Fulton, LLC and M West Holdings, LLC filed a cross-complaint against Polaris Property Management, LLC, Cushman & Wakefield, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield Global Services, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield U.S., Inc., Pinnacle Property Management, LLC, and Roes 1 to 20, alleging five causes of action for equitable indemnity, express indemnity, contribution, negligence, and declaratory relief.

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed requests to dismiss Defendants Cushman & Wakefield of California, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield, Global Services, Inc., and Cushman & Wakefield U.S., Inc., and the Defendants were dismissed from the complaint.

On June 27, 2024, Defendants 4629 Fulton, LLC and M West Holdings, LLC filed requests to dismiss Defendants Cushman & Wakefield of California, Inc., Cushman & Wakefield, U.S., Inc., Cushman & Wakefield, Global Services, Inc., and Polaris Property Management, LLC, and the Defendants were dismissed from the cross-complaint.

On July 12, 2024, Defendants Cushman & Wakefield, Inc. and Pinnacle California Corp. dba Pinnacle Property Management Services California Corp. (Doe 1) (erroneously sued as Doe 1 Pinnacle Property Management Services California Corp.) moved to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint.

On August 19, 2024, this court granted the motion to strike and granted Plaintiff leave to amend the complaint. (08/19/24 Minute Order p. 6.)

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint.

 

On October 11, 2024, Defendants Cushman & Wakefield, Inc. and Pinnacle California Corp dba Pinnacle Property Management Services California Corp (Doe 1) (erroneously sued as Doe 1 Pinnacle Property Management Services California Corp.) (“Defendants”) filed a motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s first amended complaint (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed no opposition. There is a stipulation (filed 10/18/24) between Plaintiff and Defendants 4629 Fulton, LLC and M West Holdings, LLC where Plaintiff agreed to withdraw the punitive damages claim, without prejudice, against Defendants 4629 Fulton, LLC and M West Holdings, LLC.

II.              PROCEDURAL MATTERS 

 

A. Timeliness of Filings 

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).) When service is made electronically, “[a]ny period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response” is extended by two court days. (Code Civ. Proc, § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

 

Plaintiff served Defendants with the first amended complaint electronically on September 9, 2024. Defendants filed the Motion on October 11, 2024, which is thirty days plus two court days after September 9, 2024. Further, October 11, 2024, is more than sixteen court days before the November 20, 2024, hearing.

 

Thus, the Motion was timely filed.

 

B. The Meet and Confer Requirement 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a motion to strike to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the motion to strike.

 

Defendants’ counsel submits a declaration stating he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel via written correspondence and telephone, but Defendants’ counsel and Plaintiff’s counsel were unable to reach an agreement. (Salas Declaration ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

This court finds Defendants satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

III.            LEGAL STANDARD 

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).) 

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.)

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “[P]roof of negligence, even gross negligence, or recklessness is insufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.” (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.) To justify a punitive damages award, the defendant must be “guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code., § 3294.)

 

Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) Oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(c)(2).) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).)

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of ‘oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,’ within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].) 

 

An employer shall not be liable for punitive damages based on the acts of an employee,
“unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civ. Code, § 3294(b).)

 

IV.            DISCUSSION 

 

Defendants move to strike the allegations, references to, and the prayer for punitive damages in the first amended complaint, arguing that Plaintiff fails to allege any facts supporting a claim for punitive damages. (Notice of Motion pp. 1-2.)

 

The first amended complaint alleges that Plaintiff moved into the Subject Property on or about May 20, 2017. (FAC ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that shortly after she moved into the Subject Property, she noticed the shower had low water pressure and rusty/yellow water. (FAC ¶ 17.) Plaintiff contacted Defendants and Defendants had the showerhead replaced, but the water was still rusty/yellow. (FAC ¶ 17.) Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants then negligently hired an unfit plumber” who “did not install the new valve per the Building code such that replacement was not a single-handle valve” and “Defendants’ plumber left a live electrified conduit very close to and/or touching the shower connections.” (FAC ¶ 17.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendants did not inspect the plumber’s work. (FAC ¶ 17.) Plaintiff alleges that for the next two years, the shower valve leaked and caused mold to grow, causing Plaintiff to be exposed to mold. (FAC ¶ 18.)  On or about May 7, 2019, Plaintiff noticed a shower leak in the apartment and notified Defendants. (FAC ¶ 19.) Later, Defendants hired another plumber to fix the shower. (FAC ¶ 22.) On or about May 30, 2019, Plaintiff told Defendants they needed to check the apartment for mold and asbestos. (FAC ¶ 24.) Defendants hired someone to check for mold and asbestos, and the person refused to share the results with Plaintiff. (FAC ¶ 24.) And “[o]n June 6, 2019, Defendants said the testing for mold and asbestos was fine but refused to share the results with Plaintiff” and “[b]etween June 6, 2019 and July 11, 2019, despite Plaintiff’s repeated requests for the testing results, Defendants refused to produce it.” (FAC ¶ 25.)  Plaintiff alleges that “[b]etween the time Plaintiff moved into the property until March 24, 2022, Defendants repeatedly conducted unpermitted renovations and repairs in and around Plaintiff’s apartment unit with full knowledge of the risk of serious bodily injury/death that could result to Plaintiff and others due to the unpermitted and unsafe renovations and repairs.” (FAC ¶ 26.) And “[o]n March 24, 2022, Plaintiff suffered an electric shock as she attempted to use the shower because Defendants had recklessly electrified the shower in violation of basic safety rules.” (FAC ¶ 27.)

 

Under the punitive damages claim section, Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s experienced property managers/property owners, Defendants knew of the substantial risk of death/injury that would result from performing serious repairs/renovations in and around Plaintiff’s apartment.” (FAC ¶ 45.) Plaintiff further alleges that Defendants, in 2019, “hired an attorney to reimburse Plaintiff . . . for the times [Plaintiff] was unable to live at the [Subject] Property because of Defendants’ negligence.” (FAC ¶ 46.) Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants, at the behest of Defendants’ principals and senior executives, continued to implement Defendants’ dangerous policy of intentionally skirting building safety codes to save money when repairing and maintaining the [Subject] Property.” (FAC ¶ 48.) Plaintiff also alleges “Defendants and their principals and senior executives knew from the prior deadly hazard Defendants had created at the [Subject] Property in 2019 that skirting building safety code requirements and hiring unqualified and inexpensive repair and maintenance personnel would needlessly expose the tenants and the public to deadly hazards, such as asbestos and/or electrocution.” (FAC ¶ 51.) Finally, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants “continued to recklessly endanger its tenants through intentionally shoddy repairs at the [Subject] Property between 2019 and 2022[,]” “misled Plaintiff into believing that Defendants had learned from their prior despicable behavior and that the repairs Defendants performed after hiring counsel were done properly[,]” and “never warned Plaintiff that the shoddy work Defendants performed in repairing/renovating the [Subject] Property would lead to the permanent and catastrophic injuries that Plaintiff suffered.” (FAC ¶¶ 52-54.)

 

As noted in this court’s August 19, 2024, Minute Order, Plaintiff’s negligent repair allegations, such as Defendants’ hiring workers who performed the work negligently, amount to negligence or at most gross negligence, which is insufficient for punitive damages. (08/19/24 Minute Order p. 5.)

 

Plaintiff also alleges Defendants refused to produce the mold and asbestos test results. However, a complaint must “include specific factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious[.]” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1193.) Plaintiff’s factual allegations in the first amended complaint regarding Defendants’ withholding the test results are identical to the complaint’s allegations. And, in this court’s August 19, 2024, Minute Order, this court concluded that Plaintiff had not set forth specific facts supporting the elements for fraud. (08/19/24 Minute Order p. 5.)

 

Plaintiff’s allegations under the punitive damages claim are also insufficient to support punitive damages claim. Plaintiff alleges Defendants hired counsel to reimburse Plaintiff for when Plaintiff was unable to live at the property and to settle claims resulting from their reckless endangerment of their tenants. (FAC ¶¶ 46, 52.) But hiring counsel to reimburse Plaintiff for when Plaintiff was unable to live at the property does not show that Defendants acted with malice, oppression, or fraud.  Further, Plaintiff does not tie Defendants’ hiring counsel in 2019 to settle claims resulting from Defendants’ reckless endangerment of their tenants to the factual allegations in Plaintiff’s first amended complaint – namely that Defendants did not ensure the plumbers performed their work properly and Defendants did not share the asbestos and mold results with Plaintiff. (FAC ¶¶ 17, 24-27.) Finally, Plaintiff’s remaining allegations under the punitive damages section are insufficient because they do not set forth specific facts supporting the punitive damages claims.

 

V.              LEAVE TO AMEND

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, ‘leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.’” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.)

 

Even though this is the second motion to strike, this court finds there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiff can cure the defect and grants Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

VI.            CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants the Motion and grants Plaintiff leave to amend within thirty (30) days’ notice of this order.

I.                BACKGROUND

Plaintiff GEJ Construction, Inc., dba Cali Remodeling (“Plaintiff”) entered a written agreement with Defendant Tonee Evans (“Defendant”) where Plaintiff agreed to provide construction services and Defendant agreed to pay Plaintiff $153,400.00. (Complaint ¶¶ 1, 8.) Defendant subsequently requested additional services totaling $30,000.00. (Complaint ¶ 9.) Plaintiff provided various credits, leaving a $157,000.00 balance. (Complaint ¶ 10.) Defendant paid Plaintiff a $1,000.00 downpayment and Plaintiff performed the services. (Complaint ¶¶ 11-12.) Defendant failed to pay Plaintiff the remaining balance of $156,000.00. (Complaint ¶¶ 11-13.)

 

On August 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 to 10, alleging five causes of action for breach of contract, quantum meruit, open book, account stated, and breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

On October 14, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel arbitration (the “Motion”) and a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants GEJ Construction, Inc., dba Cali Remodeling, RLI Insurance Company, and Roes 1 to 25.

 

On November 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Motion.

 

On November 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an answer to Defendant’s cross-complaint.

 

On November 12, 2024, Defendant filed a request to dismiss Cross-Defendant RLI Insurance Company from the cross-complaint and RLI Insurance Company was dismissed.

 

On November 13, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

 

II.             DISCUSSION

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professionals, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) “[T]he right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.” (Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Inv. Brokerage Co. v. Hock Inv. Co. (1998) 68 Cal. App.4th 83, 88.)

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413; Provencio v. WMA Securities, Inc., 125 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1031.) The court is empowered by California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.)

   

“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971–72.) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld ‘unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.’” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.) Notwithstanding that strong policy, the United States Supreme Court has held, “[A]rbitration is a matter of contract and a party cannot be required to submit to arbitration any dispute which he has not agreed so to submit.” (AT & T Technologies, Inc. v. Communications Workers of America (1986) 475 U.S. 643, 648.)

 

The hearing and determination of a petition to compel arbitration “in the manner and upon the notice provided by law for the making and hearing of motions” generally means that “the facts are to be proven by affidavit or declaration and documentary evidence with oral testimony taken only in the court's discretion.” (Sonic-Calabasas A, Inc. v. Moreno (2013) 57 Cal.4th 1109, 1157.)

 

  1. Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

Defendant submits the contract between Defendant and Plaintiff (the “Agreement”). (Ex. 1; Evans Declaration ¶ 3.) The Agreement contains an arbitration provision, which states:

 

ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES: ANY CONTROVERSY OR CLAIM ARISING OUT Of OR RELATED TO THIS CONTRACT, OR THE BREACH THEREOF, SHALL BE SETTLED BY BINDING ARBITRATION IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE CONSTRUCTION INDUSTRY ARBITRATION RULES OF THE AMERICAN ARBITRATION ASSOCIATION, AND JUDGMENT UPON THE AWARD RENDERED BY THE ARBITRATOR(S) MAY BE ENTERED IN ANY COURT HAVING JURISDICTION THEREOF. CLAIMS WITHIN THE MONETARY LIMIT OF THE SMALL CLAIMS COURT SHALL BE LITIGATED IN SUCH COURT AT THE REQUEST OF EITHER PARTY, SO LONG AS BOTH PARTIES LIMIT THEI R RIGHT TO RECOVERY TO THE JURISDICTION OF THE SMALL CLAIMS COURT. ANY CLAIM FILED IN SMALL CLAIMS COURT SHALL NOT BE DEEMED TO BE A WAIVER OF THE RIGHT TO ARBITRATE, AND IF A COUNTER CLAIM IN EXCESS OF THE JURISDICTION OF THE SMALL CLAIMS COURT IS FILED IN THE- MUNICIPAL OR SUPERIOR COURT, THEN THE PARTY ALING IN SMALL CLAIMS COURT MAY DEMAND ARBITRATION PURSUANT TO THIS PARAGRAPH.

 

NOTICE: BY INITIALIZING IN THE SPACE BELOW YOU ARE AGREEING TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE ARISING OUT OF THE MATTERS INCLUDED IN THE 'ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES' PROVISION DECIDED BY NEUTRAL ARBITRATION AS PROVIDED BY CALIFORNIA LAW AND YOU ARE GIVING UP ANY RIGHTS YOU MIGHT POSSESS TO HAVE THE DISPUTE LITIGATED IN A COURT OR JURY TRIAL BY INITIALIZING IN THE SPACE BELOW YOU ARE GIVING UP JUDICIAL RIGHTS TO DISCOVERY AND APPEAL, UNLESS THOSE RIGHTS ARE SPECIFICALLY INCLUDED INTHE 'ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES' PROVISION. IF YOU REFUSE TO SUBMIT TO ARBITRATION AFTER AGREEING TO THIS PROVISION, YOU MAY BE COMPELLED TO ARBITRATE UNDER THE AUTIJORITY OF THE BUSINESS AND PROFESSIONS CODE OR OTHER APPLICABLE LAWS. YOUR AGREEMENT TO THIS ARBITRATI ON PROVISION IS VOLUNTARY.

 

(Ex. 1 p. 10.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant has met their burden of proving a valid arbitration agreement exists.

 

  1. Plaintiff’s Arguments

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant relinquished their right to arbitration because “Plaintiff reached out to Defendant numerous times, over an 8-month period, trying to have the payment issues resolved to no avail. If Defendant believed she had a viable claim, this should have been resolved prior to Plaintiff initiating his Complaint. Plaintiff had waited long enough to find alternatives to a civil lawsuit, but Defendant failed to respond. This is Defendants further attempt to delay payment to Plaintiff.” (Opposition p. 3.) Plaintiff also argues Defendant relinquished their right to arbitration because Plaintiff filed a cross-complaint and “Defendant[‘]s counsel never reached out to Plaintiff’s counsel to initiate arbitration or even discuss the matter.” (Opposition pp. 3-4.)

 

Plaintiff cites Saint Agnes Medical Center v. Pacificare of California (2003) 31 4th 1187, which lists factors that the court should consider to determine whether a party has waived the right to arbitrate. Saint Agnes was overruled by Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 562, 582 fn. 4 (“To the extent they are inconsistent with our analysis, we overrule St. Agnes Medical Center v. PacifiCare of California, supra, 31 Cal.4th 1187, 8 Cal.Rptr.3d 517, 82 P.3d 727 and overrule or disapprove opinions cited in St. Agnes or relying upon our decision in that case.”) Plaintiff cites no specific authority supporting their argument that Defendant relinquished the right to arbitrate by failing to resolve the claim prior to the complaint being filed and by filing a cross-complaint.

 

  1. Waiver

 

The court may deny a motion to compel arbitration if it determines that “[t]he right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2(b).)  “To establish waiver under generally applicable contract law, the party opposing enforcement of a contractual agreement must prove by clear and convincing evidence that the waiving party knew of the contractual right and intentionally relinquished or abandoned it.” (Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 562, 584.) “Under the clear and convincing evidence standard, the proponent of a fact must show that it is ‘highly probable’ the fact is true.” (Id.) “The waiving party’s knowledge of the right may be ‘actual or constructive.’” (Id.) “Its intentional relinquishment or abandonment of the right may be proved by evidence of words expressing an intent to relinquish the right or of conduct that is so inconsistent with an intent to enforce the contractual right as to lead a reasonable factfinder to conclude that the party had abandoned it.” (Id.) “The waiver inquiry is exclusively focused on the waiving party's words or conduct; neither the effect of that conduct on the party seeking to avoid enforcement of the contractual right nor that party's subjective evaluation of the waiving party's intent is relevant.” (Id. at p. 585.)

 

In Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc, the Supreme Court of California held that the party seeking to compel arbitration was aware of its right to compel arbitration because it submitted a declaration showing the moving party required all employees to sign agreements providing for binding arbitration, the moving party provided the plaintiff with the signature page of the arbitration agreement before the plaintiff filed the suit, and in its answer to the complaint, the moving party asserted that the arbitration agreement barred the suit. (Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc., supra, 16 Cal.5th at p. 586.) The Court also held that the moving party intentionally abandoned its right to arbitrate because, “[r]ather than moving to compel arbitration at the outset of the case” the moving party “answered the complaint and propounded discovery requests, suggesting it did not intend to seek arbitration.” (Id.) The moving party also requested a jury trial on its initial case management conference statement – filed three months after the plaintiff filed the complaint - and left the box indicating it was “willing to participate” in arbitration blank. (Id.)  Finally, the moving party posted jury fees. (Id.) Thirteen months after plaintiff filed the complaint, the moving party sought to compel arbitration. (Id. at p. 587.)  The Court held that the moving party waived its right to arbitration because “being fully aware of its right to compel arbitration, [the moving party] chose not to do so for 13 months, affirmatively indicated its intent to pursue a jury trial rather than arbitration, and actively engaged in discovery — words and conduct markedly inconsistent with an intent to arbitrate.” (Id.)

 

First, the record shows that Defendant was aware of its right to compel arbitration at the outset of this case, given Defendant moved to compel arbitration at the outset of this case. (See Motion; Ex. 1.)

 

Second, the record does not show that Defendant intentionally relinquished or abandoned its right to compel arbitration. Plaintiff filed the complaint August 16, 2024, and the summons on the complaint was filed September 11, 2024. On October 14, 2024, Defendant filed the Motion and cross-complaint. Thus, unlike Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc., there is no evidence here that Defendant participated in litigation and discovery for months before seeking to compel arbitration or represented they did not intend to compel arbitration.  In contrast, Defendant immediately moved to compel arbitration at the outset of this case.

 

Plaintiff also argues it “reached out to Defendant numerous times, over an 8-month period, trying to have the payment issues resolved to no avail[,]” “[i]f Defendant believed she had a viable claim, this should have been resolved prior to Plaintiff initiating his Complaint[,]” and “Plaintiff had waited long enough to find alternatives to a civil lawsuit, but Defendant failed to respond.” (Opposition p. 3.) To the extent Plaintiff is arguing that Defendant waived the right to compel arbitration because Defendant failed to move to compel arbitration before Plaintiff filed this action, Plaintiff cites no authority supporting that proposition. Moreover, this court finds Plaintiff’s argument is meritless Plaintiff submits no evidence showing Defendant’s words or conduct demonstrate Defendant intended to relinquish the right to compel arbitration. (See Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc., supra, 16 Cal.5th at p. 585)

 

Thus, this court finds that Defendant did not waive the right to compel arbitration.

 

III.           CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants Defendant’s Motion.
