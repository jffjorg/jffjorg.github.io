DEPT:

HEARING DATE:

October 16, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV28134

MOTIONS: 

(1)   Motion to Compel Production of Documents From Charles Gruver Pursuant to Deposition Subpoena

(2)   Motion to Compel Production of Documents to Deposition of Joseph Enayati

MOVING PARTY:

(1)-(2) Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY:

(1)   Charles Gruver, M.D.

(2)   Joseph Enayati, M.D.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 26, 2024, Uber Technologies, Inc. (“Uber”) filed the instant motions seeking orders compelling nonparties Charles Gruver, M.D. (“Dr. Gruver”) and Joseph Enayati, M.D. (“Dr. Enayati”) to comply with certain requests for production in Uber’s deposition subpoenas.

 

The doctors filed their oppositions on October 2, 2024.

 

On October 9, 2024, Uber filed an omnibus reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.010.) A deposition subpoena may command either: (1) only the attendance and testimony of the deponent, (2) only the production of business records for copying, or (3) the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.020.)

 

A service of a deposition subpoena shall be affected a sufficient time in advance of the deposition to provide the deponent a reasonable opportunity to locate and produce any designated documents and, where personal attendance is commanded, a reasonable time to travel to the place of deposition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.220, subd. (a).) Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require a deponent who is a resident of California to: personally appear and testify, if the subpoena so specifies; to produce any specified documents; and to appear at a court session if the subpoena so specifies. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.220, subd. (c).) A deponent who disobeys a deposition subpoena may be punished for contempt without the necessity of a prior order of the court directing compliance by the witness. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.240.) A motion to compel compliance with a deposition subpoena must be made within 60 days after completion of the deposition record, the date objections are served, or the date specified for production, and be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd., (b); Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032-1033.)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a) states, “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.2, subdivision (a) states, in relevant part, “. . . in making an order pursuant to motion made . . . under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification . . . .”

 

“In general, ‘any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) To meet this standard, a party seeking to compel production of records from a nonparty must articulate specific facts justifying the discovery sought; it may not rely on mere generalities. [Citation.] In assessing the party’s proffered justification, courts must keep in mind the more limited scope of discovery available from nonparties.” (Board of Registered Nursing, supra, 59 Cal.App.5th at 1039.)

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

            The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has been met. (Declarations of Nicholas D. Brauns, filed on August 26, 2024 (“Braus Decl.”), ¶ 8 [testifying that Uber’s counsel and counsel for the nonparties met and conferred on several occasions regarding the requests for production at issue in the subpoenas but the parties were unable to resolve the dispute].)

 

SEPARATE STATEMENT

 

            Uber has also satisfied the separate statement requirement. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a) [requiring motions to compel the production of documents at a deposition to be accompanied by a separate statement]; Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 131 [“Under the plain meaning rule, the Act contemplates that discovery conducted by way of a business records subpoena is a ‘deposition’”]; Separate Statements filed on August 26, 2024 [outlining the requests for production at issue, the nonparties responses to those requests, and why further responses are required].)

 

DISCUSSION

 

This action arises from an automobile collision. The operative Complaint alleges that on or about August 2, 2019, Plaintiffs Kenny Reese (“Reese”) and Michele Bias were passengers in Defendant John Doe’s vehicle when the latter collided with two other defendants’ vehicle.

 

Uber now moves to compel Drs. Gruver and Enayati to produce certain documents pursuant to deposition subpoenas, arguing that those doctors treated Reese for pain management on a medical lien-basis. (Braums Decl., ¶ 2 [testifying Uber served the nonparties with the subpoenas on February 22, 2024]; Exhibits B – copies of the subpoenas.) According to defense counsel, the two doctors are affiliated with one another, LA Pain and Beverly Hills Pain & Spine (“LA Pain”) and Beverly Hills Advanced Pain and Spine (“BHAPS”). (Braums Decl., ¶ 2.) Uber previously moved for an order compelling BHAPS to comply with Uber’s subpoena seeking “[a]ny and all lien agreements or contracts between [BHAPS] and Downtown L.A. Law Group in the last 10 years.” (Separate Statement, filed June 20, 2024, p. 1:25-27.) The Court granted the motion in part, ordering BHAPS to only produce redacted lien information for the two years prior to Reese’s referral. (Minute Order, filed October 7, 2024 (“October 7 Minute Order”), p. 6.)

 

The Court has reviewed Uber’s separate statements and notes that the discovery at issue (i.e., request for production of documents (“RPD”) Nos. 3-9, 11, 15-34, 38-48, 54-73, and 75) are identical.

 

According to Uber, the RPDs seek records relevant to Reese’s damages; specifically, the reasonable value of medical services provided to Reese. (Braums Decl., ¶ 13.)

 

In their oppositions, Drs. Gruver and Enayati argue the Court should deny the motions for the following reasons. First, Uber failed to give consumer notices to the patients whose information is being requested. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (b) [“Prior to the date called for in the subpoena duces tecum for the production of personal records, the subpoenaing party shall serve or cause to be served on the consumer whose records are being sought a copy of the subpoena duces tecum . . .”].) Second, the subpoenas seek irrelevant information. Third, the subpoenas violate the doctors’ and their patients’ rights to privacy. The doctors ask the Court to impose sanctions on Uber for bringing what they deem to be frivolous motions.

 

Uber makes the following arguments in reply. First, it was impossible for Uber to comply with the consumer notice requirement because Uber did not know the identity of other patients other than Reese and, in any event, patient privacy can be protected by redacting patient identifying information. Second, the information requested for is relevant to Uber’s assessment of Reese’s damages. Third, any privacy interests the doctors have in the requested records are outweighed by Uber’s need for the discovery. In addition, a protective order can be issued to protect the doctors’ privacy. Finally, sanctions are inappropriate because Uber has brought the motions in good faith.

 

As an alternative to granting the motions in full, Uber argues in its reply that the Court should issue an order narrowing the scope of the RPDs to two years before and two years after Reese’s referral to BHAPS.

 

“In a tort action for economic damages, the California Supreme Court has held an award of past medical expenses is limited to the lesser of (1) the amount paid or incurred and (2) the reasonable value of the services rendered.” (Qaadir v. Figueroa (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 790, 797 (“Qaadir”).)

 

“[E]vidence of a medical bill is relevant to prove or disprove the ‘paid or incurred’ prong of past medical damages if it can be established the bill is actually paid or incurred.” (Qaadir. supra, 67 Cal.App.5th at p. 798.)

 

In addition, “[r]eferral evidence [i.e., evidence that a party was referred by his attorney to lien-physicians] … [is also] relevant to the question of the reasonable value of the lien-physicians’ medical care because it may show bias or financial incentives on the part of the lien-physicians.” (Qaadir. supra, 67 Cal.App.5th at p. 808.)

Here, with the exception of the RPDs discussed below, the Court finds that all other RPDs seek information that goes beyond seeking documents relevant to showing bias in Reese’s referral and the medical bills that Reese actually paid or incurred. For example, some RPDs ask the doctors to produce (1) all documents of instances in which the LA Pain “has commenced collections actions or litigation against a patient for non-payment of bills” (RPD No. 18), and (2) all liens for healthcare services rendered by LA Pain (No. 71) regardless of whether those documents have anything to do with Reese. Uber has not provided a sufficient basis for those requests.

 

However, the Court finds that RPDs Nos. 6, 34, 44-47[1], 66, 68-69, and 75 seek relevant documents. For example, RPD No. 6 asks the doctors to produce all documents LA Pain has used to develop Reese’s billing and RPD No. 46 asks them to produce all documents of incentives or discounts LA Pain offers for patient referrals by attorneys. The Court finds those documents are relevant to determining the value of the medical services LA Pain provided to Reese and the issue of bias with regard to Reese’s referral to LA Pain. The Court notes that, according to Uber’s separate statements, the doctors did not respond or object to RPD Nos. 44, 45, 46, 66, 68, and 69. The doctors objected to RPD Nos. 6, 34, 47, and 75 on various grounds, including that some requests are vague and seek to intrude on the doctors’ and nonparties’ rights to privacy. However, the Court has not found any of those requests vague, and the doctors have not explained how those specific requests will invade theirs and others’ rights to privacy. To the extent the doctors are concerned the documents produced will contain identifying information of other patients other than Reese, the doctors can redact that identifying information. Like BHAPS, the doctors have “not pointed to authority that redacting the personal identification information would still require consumer notices.” (October 7 Minute Order, p. 5, the third full paragraph.)

 

Accordingly, Uber’s request to compel the doctors’ requests for production to RPD Nos. 6, 34, 44-47, 66, 68-69, and 75 is granted but limited to two years before and after Reese was referred to LA Pain. The request to compel production of all other RPDs is denied.

 

The doctors argue that Uber should bear the cost of producing the documents. BHAPS’s General Counsel Craig Johnson testifies that it will cost approximately $20,000 to $30,000 for the doctors to locate and produce the RPDs. (Motions, Declarations of Craig Johnson, ¶ 4.)

 

Uber argues that the $20,000 to $30,000 estimate lacks foundation and the Court agrees. Johnson does not discuss how he arrived at that number. In addition, since the subpoenas seek the same documents from the same entity (LA Pain) it is unlikely that it will cost $40,000 to $60,000 in total for the doctors to produce the documents. The Court has also denied the request to compel many of the original requests for production in the subpoenas. Therefore, it is questionable whether Johnson’s estimate is accurate.

 

Accordingly, the Court will grant the doctors’ request for an order reimbursing their reasonable and actual costs of production contingent upon them providing Uber an estimate with sufficient information.

 

Drs. Gruver and Enayati’s requests for monetary sanctions against Uber is denied. Uber acted with substantial justification in bringing the motions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (f)(1)(B).)

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, the Court grants in part the motions to compel Charles Gruver, M.D. and Joseph Enayati, M.D. to comply with Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.’s deposition subpoenas. The doctors are ordered to produce redacted Request for Production of Documents, Nos. 6, 34, 44-47[2], 66, 68-69, and 75 limited to two years before and after Plaintiff Kenny Reese was referred to LA Pain and Beverly Hills Pain & Spine.

 

Defendant shall give notice of the Court’s order and file a proof of service of such.

MOTIONS: 

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Maliyah McKnight’s Counsel

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

BACKGROUND

 

            Plaintiff Maliyah McKnight’s (Plaintiff) counsel of record, Corral Law PC, Laiken Law Group and Attorneys Nathalie E. Corral and Ashley A. Laiken (Counsel), moves to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff. Counsel contends relief is necessary because there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship and communications.

 

            No opposition has been filed for this motion.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

To be granted relief as counsel, counsel must comply with California Rules of Court (CRC) 3.1362. Even where grounds for termination exist, attorneys seeking to withdraw must comply with the procedures set forth in California Rule of Professional Conduct (CRPC) 3.700 and are subject to discipline for failure to do so. CRPC 3.700(B) lists various grounds for mandatory withdrawal. 

 

An attorney's right to terminate the attorney-client relationship and withdraw from a case is not absolute. (See Vann v. Shilleh (1975) 54 Cal.App.3d 192, 197; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398.) The decision whether to grant or deny an application for withdrawal is within the court's discretion, and it does not abuse that discretion by denying the application on the ground that the attorney's withdrawal would work injustice upon a third party. (Hodcarriers, Bldg. and Common Laborers Local Union No. 89 v. Miller (1966) 243 Cal.App.2d 391.)

 

The rules have been liberally construed to protect clients. (Vann v. Shilleh, supra, 54 Cal.App.3d 192.) An attorney, either with client's consent or court's approval, may withdraw from a case when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to client's interests; however, an attorney “shall not withdraw from employment until the member has taken reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the rights of the client, including giving due notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel, complying with rule 3-700(D), and complying with applicable laws and rules.” (CRPC 3.700(A)(2).) A lawyer violates his or her ethical mandate by abandoning a client (Pineda v. State Bar (1989) 49 Cal.3d 753, 758 759), or by withdrawing at a critical point and thereby prejudicing the client’s case. (CRPC 3.700(A)(2); Vann v. Shilleh, supra.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Counsel has filed forms MC-051 and MC-052 and has lodged with the Court a copy of the proposed order on form MC-053 as required.  (Cal Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.)  Counsel states the instant motion is filed for the following reason: “Plaintiff and Counsel have encountered a breakdown in the relationship and communications. Therefore Plaintiff’s counsel wishes to withdraw pursuant to California Rules of Professional Conduct 3-700(c) and 3-700(b).” (MC-052.)

 

            On September 11, 2024, the Court denied the previous motion for relief because proof of service was defective, and it was unclear how a Lexis Nexis search would sufficiently confirm Plaintiff’s address. Therefore, the Court ordered Counsel to attempt the methods in item 3b(2) of MC-052 and mark them accordingly in a future motion to be relieved. However, the current motion was not corrected, and “Lexis Nexis search” was listed as the method used to confirm the Plaintiff’s address.

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES the motion to relieve counsel.  

 

Counsel shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file proofs of service of such.

MOTIONS: 

Motion for Order Compelling Defendant Target Corporation to Produce Court Ordered TruCase Reports

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiffs Brayden Medina Molina and Joo Hye Song

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendant Target Corporation

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs Brayden Medina Molina and Joo Hye Song (“Plaintiffs”) move for an order compelling Defendant Target Corporation (“Defendant”) to produce additional TruCase reports. Plaintiffs also seek monetary sanctions. Defendant opposes and Plaintiffs reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

            On August 8, 2024, the Court denied in part Plaintiff’s motion to have its expert conduct an inspection of Defendant’s TruCase system. However, the Court ordered the following: “the Court orders Target to produce a person most knowledgeable as to records contained in the TruCase system regarding stabbings and brandishings with knives taken from a Target store at the time of stabbing or brandishing, in any Target store within the United States for five years prior to the incident. This deposition shall occur within 15 days and Target shall produce all documents 2 days prior to the deposition.” (Min. Order, 8/8/24.)

 

            Following this order, the parties decided not to proceed with a deposition, but instead agreed on certain search terms for the TruCase system and allowed Defendant to produce the results. (Motion, 3.) The parties agreed that all results would be produced, which were placed in an Excel sheet. Once Plaintiff identified certain incidents from this sheet, Defendant agreed to produce the applicable TruCase report. Defendant produced the Excel sheet on August 23, 2024, which contained 3,000 results, and on August 30, 2024, produced the TruCase reports for the incidents Plaintiffs had identified.

 

            Plaintiffs now bring this motion, arguing they have subsequently identified 20 more incidents from the Excel sheet, and want Defendant to produce the applicable TruCase reports.[1]

 

            In opposition, Defendant argues that TruCase only stores the raw data of each incident; as a result, the Excel sheet reflects the form of the data within TruCase. (Opp., 2; Deck Decl. ¶ 3.) Furthermore, Defendant contends the information in the report would not differ from the information that was presented in the Excel sheet. (Opp., 2; Deck Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendant asserts it previously produced the reports as a courtesy to Plaintiffs, and maintains, as it did then, that the Court’s order only applies to “records contained in the TruCase system”. (Opp., 4.) Since the information in the Excel sheet accurately reflects the substantive information in the TruCase system, as well as the form of the information, it is compliant with the Court’s order. 

 

            In support of this position, Defendant relies on Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.280(d), which states:

 

“Unless the parties otherwise agree or the court otherwise orders, the following shall apply:

 

(1) If a demand for production does not specify a form or forms for producing a type of electronically stored information, the responding party shall produce the information in the form or forms in which it is ordinarily maintained or in a form that is reasonably usable.

(2) A party need not produce the same electronically stored information in more than one form.”

 

In reply, Plaintiffs do not dispute that the raw data they possess through the Excel sheet is the same information in the reports. Plaintiffs cite Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.280(e) which states “[i]f necessary, the responding party at the reasonable expense of the demanding party shall, through detection devices, translate any data compilations included in the demand into reasonably usable form.”

 

Target has complied with the Court’s prior order. However, in light of Plaintiff’s representation that the excel spreadsheet is not “reasonably usable,” and because Target does not appear to argue that the preparation of a separate report for each of the 20 additional incidents would be burdensome or harassing, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel production of individual reports.

 

The Court declines to award sanctions because Target has complied with the Court’s order and Plaintiff’s motion contains a new discovery request, for which Target had substantial justification to refuse.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs’ motion for Order Compelling Defendant Target Corporation to Produce Court Ordered TruCase Reports is GRANTED. Target shall produce reports for the identified 20 additional incidents within 5 days.
