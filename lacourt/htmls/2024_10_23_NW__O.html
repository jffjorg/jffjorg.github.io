I.        INTRODUCTION

          This case arises out of Plaintiff Michael Malone’s (“Plaintiff” or “Cross-Defendant”) previous employment as the Chief Financial Officer (“CFO”) of Defendant SRAX, Inc., a California corporation (“Defendant” or “Cross-Complainant”). On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant, alleging breach of contract, multiple violations of Labor Code § 200, et seq., and violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq. [1] Plaintiff alleges he

 was terminated without cause on or about April 2023, and that, to date, Plaintiff has not been paid his remaining owed salary, benefits, or personally incurred business expenses. (See Complaint, ¶¶ 7-17.)         

          Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff on May 6, 2024, alleging Plaintiff failed to perform his duties as CFO and breached his confidentiality agreement, among other claims. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 21-22, 26-32.)

          Cross-Defendant now seeks to strike Cross-Complainant’s Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16.

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Violation of Labor Code §§ 201 and 202; (3) Violation of Labor Code §§ 2800 and 2802; (4) Violation of Labor Code § 204; (5) Violation of Labor Code § 226(A), and (6) Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq.

On October 9, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer and Cross-Complaint. The Court rejected the filing of the Cross-Complaint due to Defendant’s incorrect completion of the e-filing form. Defendant filed the Cross-Complaint correctly on October 25, 2023.

On November 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Answer to the Cross-Complaint but withdrew his Answer on November 13, 2023.

On May 1, 2024, this Court granted Defendant leave to file a Cross-Complaint.  

On May 6, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendant, alleging (1) Breach of Contract (Proprietary Information, Invention Assignment, and Confidentiality Agreement); (2) Breach of Contract (Employment Agreement); (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (4) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets.

On August 13, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed the instant Special Motion to Strike Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16 (“Motion”).[2]

On September 10, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed its Opposition.

On September 16, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed his Reply.   

On October 4, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed its Supplemental Opposition.

On October 11, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed his Supplemental Reply.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

“A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 (b)(1); see also Church of Scientology v. Wollersheim (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 628, 644-645 (observing the legislature enacted Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 to protect citizens exercising constitutional rights of petition and free speech from meritless lawsuits) (overruled in other part by Equilon Enters. v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 68, fn. 5).)

The trial court applies a burden shifting analysis when considering a special motion to strike brought under section 425.16. (See Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 278.) First, the defense must carry its burden of making a threshold showing that a challenged cause of action arises from “any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the [federal or state constitution] with a public issue.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 (b)(1); see also Soukup, 39 Cal.4th at 278.) Acts which are protected under section 425.16 include “any written or oral statement or writing made before a … judicial proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 (e)(1); see also Briggs v. Eden Council for Hope & Opportunity (1999) 19 Cal.4th 1106, 1116 (anti-SLAPP statute protects all petition-related activity before a governmental body, whether the statements involve a public issue or not). Second, only after the defendant has met its initial burden does the burden shift to the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the merits of the pleading or on part of the pleading at issue. (See Equilon, 29 Cal.4th at 67.)   

A plaintiff opposing a special motion to strike has the burden to “state [] and substantiate [] a legally sufficient claim.” (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88 & 93.) “Put another way, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.” (Navellier, 20 Cal.4th at 88–89 (cleaned up).) To that end, the plaintiff must present competent evidence, “that would be admissible at trial.” (HMS Capital, Inc. v. Lawyers Title Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 204, 212.) “[D]eclarations may not be based upon ‘information and belief’” and documents submitted without the proper foundation will not be considered. (Id. (citation omitted).)  The complaint, even if verified, is insufficient to carry the plaintiff's shifted burden. (See Roberts v. Los Angeles County Bar Assn. (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 604, 614; see Karnazes v. Ares (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 344, 354 (stating “pleadings do not constitute evidence”).)

IV.     ANALYSIS

A.             Cross-Defendant Meets Its Burden as to the First Prong.

“A cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike if the defendant shows that the cause of action arises from an act in furtherance of the Defendants’ constitutional right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue and the plaintiff fails to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim.” (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 883 (citations omitted).)  

“An ‘act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ is defined by statute to include ‘(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.’ … If the defendant shows that the cause of action arises from a statement described in clause (1) or (2) of section 425.16, subdivision (e), the defendant is not required to separately demonstrate that the statement was made in connection with a ‘public issue.’” (Id. (citations omitted).) 

Cross-Defendant seeks to strike Cross-Complainant’s entire Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4). (See Motion, pg. 1.) Cross-Defendant argues all four of Cross-Complainant’s causes of action concern Cross-Defendant’s “alleged sharing of ‘Proprietary and Confidential Information.’” (Motion, pg. 2.) In an attached declaration, Cross-Defendant does not deny he shared information with others, but asserts such communications were not privileged. (See Declaration of Michael Malone filed August 13, 2024 (“Malone Decl.”), ¶¶ 4-5.) Cross-Defendant declares he only communicated about Cross-Complainant’s acts he believed to be unlawful, including, but not limited to: Cross-Complainant’s misleading disclosures related to revenue and the valuation of subsidiaries; Cross-Complainant’s CEO Chris Miglino’s improper use of Cross-Complainant’s stock as collateral for a loan on a SoFi Stadium suite; and Cross-Complainant’s failure to properly disclose an increase in its security holdings. (See id.)

Cross-Defendant argues Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4) applies to his conduct, the communication of Cross-Complainant’s allegedly unlawful acts, as subsection (e)(4) is substantially broader than the other subsections, encompassing “private activity and communications that concern an issue of public interest.” (Motion, pg. 5 (citations omitted).) Such communications, in turn, concern an issue of public interest because they are specifically prohibited by Government Code § 12964.5, which prohibits an employer, as a condition of employment or continued employment, from requiring an employee to sign a nondisclosure agreement or other document “‘to the extent it has the purpose or effect of denying the employee the right to disclose information about unlawful acts in the workplace.’” (Motion, pg. 5, quoting Gov. Code § 12964.5(a)(1)(B)(i).)

In sum, Cross-Defendant argues “[t]he entire Cross-complaint is grounded on the erroneous premise that SRAX can prevent Malone from disclosing what he believes are unlawful acts. Since Gov’t Code § 12964.5 makes it clear that these are protected activities, the first prong is met.” (Motion, pg. 6.)

In opposition, Cross-Complainant focuses exclusively on Cross-Defendant’s Government Code § 12964.5 argument, stating Cross-Defendant “cannot provide any legal support for his theory that a phrase in a section of the California statutes governing workplace harassment and discrimination somehow governs Malone’s alleged ‘communications’ about his former employer which have nothing to do with harassment or discrimination.” (Opposition, pg. 4.)

In reply, Cross-Defendant engages in a lengthy discussion of statutory interpretation, comparing Gov. Code § 12964.5 to Lab. Code § 1102.5, and finally arriving at his ultimate point—that “[t]he public certainly has an interest in what the legislature has twice specifically declared is ‘public policy’ in this statute, and it is beyond reasonable debate that the public has an interest in allowing the discussion or disclosure of information about unlawful acts in the workplace.” (Reply, pg. 6.)

Finally, in supplemental opposition, Cross-Complainant argues it “denies Malone’s allegations that it participated in any illegal conduct” and that its “causes of action arise from communications by Malone to an investor about matters that are not publicly known, and which do not constitute violations of law.” (Supplemental Opposition, pg. 2.)[3] In supplemental reply, Cross-Defendant argues Cross-Complainant “doesn’t even attempt to address FilmOn….[and] merely claims, without citation to legal authority or fact, that Malone’s declaration is mostly ‘misstatements of fact,’ and not ‘violations of any laws or regulations.’ (Supp. Opp., 3:11-12.) That’s just not enough.” (Supplemental Reply, pg. 3.)

The Court agrees. As a preliminary matter, the Court does not find either Cross-Defendant or Cross-Complainant’s argument (contained in their Motion and Opposition, respectively) on whether the communications here are covered by Government Code § 12964.5 to be particularly compelling or relevant. Cross-Defendant is asking this Court to determine (1) his communications are protected under Gov. Code § 12964.5; (2) it is therefore “public policy” to protect such communications; and, as a result, (3) his communications concern an issue of public interest within the meaning of Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(e)(4). The Court declines to make such leaps in its section 425.16 analysis.

          Instead, the Court will first analyze whether Cross-Defendant has met his burden in proving all four causes of action in the Cross-Complaint are based on what he argues is protected activity—his communications. The Court finds he has not. While the Court agrees the basis for Cross-Complainant’s first, third, and fourth causes of action are Cross-Defendant’s communications, the basis for the second cause of action is both Cross-Defendant’s communications and his alleged failure to adequately perform his duties as CFO. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 40-46.) Cross-Defendant does not address the latter basis at all, much less whether this basis also constitutes protected activity. (See generally Motion.) Cross-Defendant bears “the burden of showing that each allegation supporting [Cross-Complainant’s] claim of recovery is one that rests on protected activity…to the extent any acts are unprotected, the claims based on those acts will survive.” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1012.) The Court will thus only analyze the claims that Cross-Defendant claims are protected—his communications—and will not strike or consider striking Cross-Complainant’s allegations concerning Cross-Defendant’s actions as CFO.

          The Court next addresses whether Cross-Defendant’s communications constitute protected activity. “In making its determination, the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(2).) Cross-Complainant alleges Cross-Defendant: (1) likely disclosed non-public information about Cross-Complainant to an unnamed investor; and (2) Cross-Defendant and/or the unnamed investor disclosed the non-public information to a potential customer of Cross-Complainant. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 28, 30.) Cross-Defendant doesn’t refute these allegations. Instead he provides context: the communications regarded things Cross-Defendant believes were “unlawful act[s] in the workplace.” (Malone Decl., ¶ 5.)

          Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4) has no “public forum” requirement and is substantially broader, encompassing private activity and communications, such as Cross-Defendant’s communications here with an investor and potential customer, so long as they concern an issue of public interest. (See Briggs, 19 Cal.4th at 1116.)

          The heart of this Court’s inquiry, then, is whether such communications concern an issue of public interest. “The inquiry under the catchall provision instead calls for a two-part analysis rooted in the statute’s purpose and internal logic. First, we ask what “public issue or … issue of public interest” the speech in question implicates—a question we answer by looking to the content of the speech. ([Code Civ. Proc.] § 425.16, subd. (e)(4).) Second, we ask what functional relationship exists between the speech and the public conversation about some matter of public interest. It is at the latter stage that context proves useful.” (FilmOn.com Inc. v. DoubleVerify Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 133, 149–150 (“FilmOn”).)

          “FilmOn’s first step asks what issue or issues the challenged activity may reasonably be understood to implicate. On that question, the movant’s beliefs, motivations, or characterizations may be relevant and, if objectively reasonable, will inform the analysis. But they are not themselves dispositive and, if not objectively reasonable, will not carry weight. If a reasonable inference can be drawn that the challenged activity implicates a public issue then the analysis proceeds to FilmOn's second step.” (Geiser v. Kuhns (2022) 13 Cal.5th 1238, 1254.)

          Here, the Court finds Cross-Defendant’s communications implicate an issue of public interest. Cross-Defendant declares they concern the potentially unlawful operation of “a public…financial technology company that unlocks data and insights for publicly traded companies…[allowing companies to]…track their investors’ behaviors and trends and use those insights to engage current and potential investors across marketing channels.” (Complaint, ¶ 8.) “[A] publicly traded company with many thousands of investors is of public interest because its successes or failures will affect not only individual investors, but in the case of large companies, potentially market sectors or the markets as a whole.” (Sugarman v. Benett (2021) 73 Cal.App.5th 165, 177 (citations omitted).) The Court therefore finds it is reasonable to infer Cross-Defendant’s communications concerning Cross-Complainant’s failures/unlawful acts implicate an issue of public interest.

          The Court then turns to FilmOn’s second step—what functional relationship exists between the communications and the public conversation about some matter of public interest? “[T]he inquiry of whether a statement contributes to the public debate is one a court can hardly undertake without incorporating considerations of context—including audience, speaker, and purpose.” (FilmOn, 7 Cal.5th at 151–152.) The Court finds the context surrounding Cross-Defendant’s communications furthered the discourse that makes the issue one of public interest—that is, they furthered the discourse surrounding Cross-Complainant’s allegedly unlawful acts, which would naturally be of great interest to investors and potential customers alike. The communications were to an investor and potential customer. The purpose, according to Cross-Defendant, was to communicate about Cross-Complanaint’s unlawful acts. Finally, the result was to allegedly cause Cross-Complainant “significant financial and reputational damages.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 32.)

The Court thus finds Cross-Defendant has met his burden and shown Cross-Complainant’s first, second (as discussed above), third, and fourth causes of action arose from “any act of that person in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(1).)[4]

          The burden thus shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the merits.

B.             Cross-Complainant Does Not Demonstrate a Probability of Prevailing on the Merits.

“To establish a probability of prevailing, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited. For purposes of this inquiry, the trial court considers the pleadings and evidentiary submissions of both the plaintiff and the defendant though the court does not weigh the credibility or comparative probative strength of competing evidence, it should grant the motion if, as a matter of law, the defendant’s evidence supporting the motion defeats the plaintiff’s attempt to establish evidentiary support for the claim. (Soukup, 39 Cal.4th at 291 (cleaned up, emphasis in original).) As with summary judgment motions, a court ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion must consider both the “supporting and opposing affidavits” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(2)). Gaps in plaintiff's showing of a “probability of success on the merits” may be filled by defendant’s evidence. (Salma v. Capon (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 1275, 1289.)

Cross-Complainant must therefore establish a probability of prevailing on its first two causes of action for breach of contract (but not the second cause of action insofar as it relates to Cross-Defendant’s alleged failure to adequately perform his duties as CFO, discussed above), its third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, and its fourth cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets. (See Cross-Complaint.)

In his Motion, Cross-Defendant argues the only non-public information he shared related to specific items he had reasonable cause to believe were unlawful as that term is used in Gov. Code § 12964.5. (See Motion, pg. 7.) In its Opposition, Cross-Complainant does not submit any additional evidence or make any additional argument besides its argument concerning Government Code § 12964.5. (See Opposition.) In its Supplemental Opposition, however, Cross-Complainant submits the Declaration of its CEO Chris Miglino’s (“Miglino”) as evidence, which Cross-Complainant argues proves Cross-Defendant’s communications constitute clear violations of his Employment Agreement and Proprietary Information Agreement. (See Supplemental Opposition, pg. 6; Declaration of Chris Miglino filed October 4, 2024 (“Miglino Decl.”).)

In his Supplemental Reply, Cross-Defendant argues most of Miglino’s Declaration is inadmissible. (See Supplemental Reply, pp. 4-5.) The Court disagrees and overrules Cross-Defendant’s evidentiary objections Nos. 1-7, 9, 11, 13-23 as the Court believes they go to the weight, not the admissibility of these statements. However, the Court sustains evidentiary objections Nos. 8, 10, and 12 as these portions of Miglino’s declaration are based upon information and belief and are inadmissible for purposes of proving the probable validity of a claim, such as to trials or SLAPP motions. (See Evans v. Unkow (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1490, 1498.)

Cross-Defendant further argues that, even if the Court finds Miglino’s declaration admissible, “Miglino’s declaration doesn’t factually rebut anything Malone swore to in his declaration.” (Supplemental Reply, pg. 5.)[5]

The Court will address the parties’ evidence and arguments below.

1.               Breach of Contract

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

          Cross-Complainant hasn’t met its burden in demonstrating a probability of prevailing on the merits as to its first two causes of action for breach of the Employment Agreement and the Proprietary Information Agreement. Besides the second cause of action’s allegations concerning Cross-Defendant’s failure to perform as CFO, both causes of action are based on Cross-Defendant’s disclosure of confidential and proprietary information to potential customers and investors. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 35, 43.) While the Court finds Cross-Complainant has demonstrated the existence of the agreements by attaching both to Miglino’s Declaration, the Court does not find Cross-Complainant has made a sufficient prima facie showing of facts that Cross-Defendant actually breached these agreements by disclosing confidential and/or proprietary information. (See Miglino Decl., ¶¶ 3, 7, Exs. A, B.) The only admissible evidence Cross-Complainant submits in support of its allegations that Cross-Defendant breached the agreements is the following: (1) Cross-Defendant twice-appeared at an industry event with an investor and (2) “the same investor sent a demand letter to [Cross-Complainant] which included non-public information which could only have been disclosed by” Cross-Defendant. (Miglino Decl., ¶¶ 13, 15, 17.)

The Court finds this evidence too speculative to be sufficient. Without any additional evidence as to what Cross-Defendant allegedly disclosed, the Court has no ability to compare the content of Cross-Defendant’s alleged disclosures with the prohibitions listed in the Employment Agreement and Proprietary Information Agreement and therefore determine whether he in fact breached the agreements.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s Motion is GRANTED as to Cross-Complainant’s first cause of action for breach of contract. The Court also GRANTS IN PART Cross-Defendant’s Motion as to Cross-Complainant’s second cause of action for breach of contract, as the Court will not strike Cross-Complainant’s allegations concerning Cross-Defendant’s performance as CFO, discussed above.   

2.               Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Breach of fiduciary duty requires a plaintiff to “demonstrate the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of that duty and damages.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.)

The Court reincorporates its breach of contract analysis here. Cross-Complainant has not met its burden as to its third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty because Cross-Complainant has not made a sufficient prima facie showing of facts that Cross-Defendant actually breached a fiduciary duty by disclosing confidential and/or proprietary information.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s Motion is GRANTED as to Cross-Complainant’s third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.

3.               Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

“A cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets requires a plaintiff to

show the plaintiff owned the trade secret; at the time of misappropriation, the

information was a trade secret; the defendant improperly acquired, used, or

disclosed the trade secret; the plaintiff was harmed; and the defendant’s

acquisition, use, or disclosure of the trade secret was a substantial factor in

causing the plaintiff harm.” (AMN Healthcare, Inc. v. Aya Healthcare Services,

Inc. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 923, 942 (citations omitted).)

The Court once again reincorporates its breach of contract analysis here. Cross-Complainant has not met its burden as to its fourth cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets because Cross-Complainant has not made a sufficient prima facie showing of facts that Cross-Defendant actually disclosed confidential and/or proprietary information.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s Motion is GRANTED as to Cross-Complainant’s fourth cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets.

C.      Fees Shall Be Determined by Separately Noticed Motion.

Cross-Defendant requests attorney’s fees, which he intends to file as a separate noticed motion. The Court will consider the request at that time.

V.       CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS IN PART and DENIES IN PART Cross-Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16.

The Court orders Cross-Complainant to file an amended cross-complaint in conformity with this Court’s ruling on or before November 15, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is an unlawful detainer action. Plaintiff Elshir Enterprises, LP (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendant Jechonias Williams (“Defendant”) failued to pay rent on a commercial property. (See First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).)

On June 25, 2024, the court held a bench trial after Defendant did not appear for the scheduled jury trial and ordered judgment entered for Plaintiff against Defendant for past due rent. (See Minute Order dated June 25, 2024.)

Plaintiff now seeks reasonable attorney’s fees per Code Civ. Proc. § 1717(a) [1].

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 10, 2023, Plaintiff CaliTex, LLC, filed an unlawful detainer Complaint against Defendant.

          On December 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed its FAC.

          On April 9, 2024, Defendant filed his Answer.

On June 25, 2024, the court ordered judgment entered for Plaintiff against Defendant.

          On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Deem Plaintiff Prevailing Party and Fix Attorney’s Fees at $9,625.00 (“Motion”).

          Defendant has not filed an opposition.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

“A party is entitled to an award of¿attorney¿fees¿if there is a specific authorization therefor by statute or private agreement.” (Beverly Hills Properties v. Marcolino (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d Supp. 7, 10.)  

 “With respect to attorney fees incurred to litigate a contract claim, the recovery of such fees are governed by¿Section 1717.” (Intelligent Investments Corp. v. Gonzales (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th Supp. 1, 4.) Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a) provides in, pertinent part, “[i]n any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that¿attorney's¿fees¿and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the¿prevailing¿party, then the party who is determined to be the¿party¿prevailing¿on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable¿attorney's¿fees¿in addition to other costs.” “California courts liberally construe the term ‘on a contract’ as used within¿section 1717. As long as the action involves a contract it is ‘on the contract’ within the meaning of¿Section 1717.” (Dell Merk Inc. v. Franzia (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 443, 455, (internal citations omitted).) 

“The court…shall determine who is the party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section, whether or not the suit proceeds to final judgment…the party prevailing on the contract shall be the party who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract. The court may also determine that there is no party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1717(b).)

Code of Civil Procedure § 1032(b) provides that, “[e]xcept as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled … to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” Attorneys’ fees awarded pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1717 are allowable as costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 1032 as they are attorneys’ fees authorized by contract. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(c)(5)(B); Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(a)(10)(A).) Attorneys’ fees are therefore recoverable as an element of costs.

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Plaintiff is Entitled to an Award of Reasonable Attorney’s Fees and Costs Under Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a).

Plaintiff requests a total of $9,625.00 for fees and costs incurred in connection with this action and instant Motion.

Plaintiff is the prevailing party as the court ordered judgment entered for Plaintiff against Defendant on June 25, 2024. (See Minute Order dated June 25, 2024.) Reasonable attorney’s fees are also authorized pursuant to Paragraph 31 of the lease agreement between the parties. (See Declaration of Kenny Lim filed August 26, 2024 (“Lim Decl.”), ¶ 7, Ex. 2, ¶ 31.)

Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an award of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a) as the prevailing party in this action.
