Case Number: 20STCV46551    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 34

Defendants Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, and Roger Merians’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

Background

 

On December 4, 2020, Plaintiffs Spaciot, LLC and Alessandro Paciotti (collectively, the “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Duc Pham, Vincent Pham, Verona Street Holdings, LLC, Barry Mason Enterprises, LP, Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, Roger Merians, and Daniel Hinman alleging causes of action for:

 

1.               Breach of Contract;

2.               Negligence;

3.               Negligent Misrepresentation;

4.               Fraud; and

5.               Breach of Fiduciary Duty.

 

On October 19, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On November 17, 2022, Defendants Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, and Roger Merians filed their answer to Plaintiffs’ FAC.  

 

On November 18, 2022, Defendant Daniel Hinman filed his answer to Plaintiffs’ FAC.  

 

On January 6, 2023, Defendants Duc Pham, Vincent Pham, and Verona Street Holdings, LLC filed their answer to Plaintiffs’ FAC.  

 

On January 25, 2023, by request of Plaintiffs, the Clerk’s Office dismissed without prejudice Defendant Barry Mason Enterprises, LP.

 

On June 13, 2023, Plaintiffs amended their FAC by substituting Doe 1 with Eddie Navarrette.  

 

On August 9, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).  

 

On September 12, 2023, Defendants Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, and Roger Merian filed an answer to Plaintiffs’ SAC.  

 

On October 12, 2023, the court sustained Defendant Eddie Navarrette’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ SAC with leave to amend.

 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”).

 

On November 22, 2023, Defendant Eddie Navarrette filed an answer to Plaintiffs’ TAC.  

 

On November 27, 2023, Defendants Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, and Roger Merian filed an answer to Plaintiffs’ TAC.[1]  

 

On August 29, 2024, Defendants Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, and Roger Merian (“Broker Defendants”) filed this Motion for Summary Judgment. On October 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition. On November 7, 2024, Broker Defendants filed a reply.

Legal Standard

“A party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding. The motion may be made at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (1)(a).)

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. That is because of the general principle that a party who seeks a court’s action in his favor bears the burden of persuasion thereon. There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850, citation omitted.)

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.” (Aguilar, supra, at p. 850; Smith v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1474, [applying the summary judgment standards in Aguilar to motions for summary adjudication].)

 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law. If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839, citation omitted.)

 

“The trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a fact finder to determine whose version is more likely true. Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court's evaluation of credibility.” (Binder, supra, at p. 840, citations omitted; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Dep’t of Transp. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

 

Discussion

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Pending

 

Factual Background

 

            In January 2019, Plaintiff Alessandro Paciotti (“Paciotti”),  purchased a two-story restaurant (“Blossom”) from Defendants Duc Pham and Vincent Pham (“Seller Defendants”). (Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”), Nos. 12-14.) To operate Blossom, Paciotti set up Plaintiff entity Spaciot, LLC (“Spaciot”) and leased the property at 4019 W. Sunset Blvd., Los Angeles, CA 90029 where Blossom was located. (Ibid.) The property consisted of two floors, a street and underground level, that Seller Defendants used for customer dining. (Id., No. 15.) Broker Defendants were the listing agents of Blossom and advertised it as a two-story restaurant in September 2018. (Id., No. 16.)

 

Broker Defendants were informed by Seller Defendants that the underground level was used for customer dining, and the property’s Conditional Use Permit (“CUP”) contained a diagram of the underground level referring to it as a “customer area”. (Id., Nos. 17-18.) Seller Defendants provided the CUP to Broker Defendants during the listing of Blossom, which was prepared by Seller Defendants’ consultant, Defendant Eddie Navarrette (“Navarrette”). (Id., Nos. 20-22.)

 

            In October 2018, Broker Defendants were contacted by Plaintiffs’ consultant, Defendant Daniel Hinman (“Hinman”), to purchase Blossom. (Id., Nos. 26-27.) Plaintiffs relied on Hinman to inspect the premises for ADA compliance and ensured that all permits for the restaurant were in order, including the extension permit to use the underground level for customer dining. (Id., Nos. 28-29.) Plaintiffs were also provided with the CUP. (Id., No. 30.)

 

            Prior to closing the purchase of Blossom, the parties discovered that Seller Defendants had sold alcohol in the premises for 5 years without the necessary permits due to Navarrette’s failure to acquire an alcohol license. (Id., Nos. 30-34.) As such, Broker Defendants acted as dual agents for Plaintiffs and Seller Defendants in the sale of Blossom and lease of the premises securing a lower sale price for Plaintiffs. (Id., Nos. 35-37.)

 

Before purchasing Blossom, Plaintiffs were informed by Hinman that aside from the alcohol license, all other permits were in order. (Id., No. 38.) Nevertheless, Plaintiffs discovered that the underground level could not be used to seat customers as it was not ADA compliant. (Id., No. 39.) Plaintiffs subsequently hired Broker Defendants to sell Blossom. (Id., No. 41.) Broker Defendants found a buyer around May 2020, but Plaintiffs’ refused to sell. (Id., Nos. 42-43.)

 

Merits

 

            Broker Defendants move for summary judgment, or in the alternative summary adjudication, as to Plaintiffs’ causes of action for (1) negligent misrepresentation, (2) intentional misrepresentation, and (3) breach of fiduciary duty.

 

A.              Negligent Misrepresentation

To establish a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, Plaintiff must show: (1) the misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another's reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damage. (Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal. App. 5th 1239, 1252.)

“California courts have recognized a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, i.e., a duty to communicate accurate information, in two circumstances. The first situation arises where providing false information poses a risk of and results in physical harm to person or property. The second situation arises where information is conveyed in a commercial setting for a business purpose.” (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 477.)

Broker Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ cause of action for negligent misrepresentation fails as there was sufficient information to justify a reasonable broker to conclude that the underground level of the Blossom premises could be used for customer dining as advertised. (MSJ, at pp. 12-13.) Broker Defendants contend that Seller Defendants informed Broker them that the Blossom premises consisted of two levels which were used for customer dining. (Merians Decl., ¶ 6.) Broker Defendants also relied on the CUP issued that regarded the underground level as a “customer area”. (Merians Decl., ¶¶ 7-10, Exh. 7.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs dispute that the CUP allowed for the sale and consumption of alcohol on the underground level as the conditions of the CUP were not met rendering it invalid. (Opp., at p. 3.) Plaintiffs also dispute Broker Defendants’ assertion that they did not know that the underground level could not be used a dining area. (Id., at p. 4.) In support, Plaintiffs provide a copy of an email exchange from Broker Defendant Roger Merians (“Merians”) asking Seller Defendant Duc Pham (“Duc”) the following: “Perhaps it would be interesting to ask FE Design if you sell the business do these covenants etc transfer to the new owner and just so we maybe understand a bit more as well about parking etc -  I think it may also be a function of a new renovation if that happens - but worth asking ?” (Plaintiffs’ Evidence, Exh. 1.) Duc then forwarded this email to Navarrette who responded with “covenants run with the property. The CUP is expired so it doesn't mean kaka, Best to not wake any sleeping giants with building permits anytime soon.” (Ibid. [emphasis added].) As such, Plaintiffs argue that this response indicates that at least Navarrette and Duc knew of issues with using the underground level as dining area making it reasonable to infer that Broker Defendants also knew of the issue. (Opp., at p. 4.) Additionally, Plaintiffs dispute that Broker Defendants believed that the ADA requirements were waived for the entirety of the Blossom premises presenting an email exchange between Duc and Merians where Merians indicated that it is “possible the street level will need an ADA compliant restroom.” (Id., at pp. 4-5; Plaintiffs’ Evidence, Exh. 2.)

In reply, Broker Defendants argue that Plaintiffs fail to propound any evidence showing that Broker Defendants knew that Plaintiffs could not use the underground level of Blossom as a dining area. (Reply, at p. 3.) Moreover, Broker Defendants continue to argue that they had reasonable grounds to believe that both levels of Blossom could be used for customer dining, and they did not act recklessly in any way based on the information they had. (Ibid.) Broker Defendants also provide an email showing that Duc informed Merians that the city had wave the ADA requirements for the premises due to the age and limitations of the building. (Merians Decl., ¶ 12, Exh. 8.)

The court finds that no triable of issue of fact exists as to Plaintiffs’ negligent misrepresentation claim. Plaintiffs allege that Broker Defendants falsely represented that the underground level of the Blossom premises could be used for customer dining, thus inducing Plaintiffs to purchase the restaurant and lease the premises. Broker Defendants’ evidence show that they had no information to believe that the underground level could not be used for customer dining. It is undisputed that Seller Defendants had used the underground level as a customer area for many years while operating Blossom. (UMF, No. 15.) It is also undisputed that the CUP for the premises represented the underground level as a customer area. (Merians Decl., ¶¶ 7-10, Exh. 7.) At a minimum, this evidence is sufficient to meet Broker Defendants’ moving burden. This is further supported by the declarations and discovery responses provided in Broker Defendants’ evidence. (Chen Decl., ¶ 8; Mourad Decl., ¶ 7; Merians Decl., ¶ 11; Navarette RFA Responses, Nos. 11-12.)

In turn, Plaintiffs fail to overcome their burden of raising a triable issue of material fact. None of the evidence provided by Plaintiffs allow for even an inference that Broker Defendants had any indication that the underground level could not be used as a customer space. Plaintiffs also fail to provide any evidence that contradicts the declarations provided by Broker Defendants or the undisputed facts regarding the CUP and previous use of the underground level.  

As such, Broker Defendants’ motion is granted as to Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for negligent misrepresentation.

B.              Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.)

 

As discussed above, Broker Defendants’ evidence shows that they had no knowledge that the underground level of Blossom could not be used for customer dining as all the information available to them showed otherwise. As such, Broker Defendants met their moving burden.

 

Plaintiffs also fail to show that a triable of issue of fact exists as to Plaintiffs’ intentional misrepresentation claim. Plaintiffs’ evidence fails to show that Broker Defendants had any knowledge that the underground level could not be used for customer dining to make their assertions false.

As such, Broker Defendants’ motion is granted as to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for intentional misrepresentation.

C.               Breach of a Fiduciary Duty

 

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.) 

 

Broker Defendants contend that Plaintiffs need to show that Broker Defendants failed to disclose material information regarding the Blossom premises that was known to them. (MSJ, at p. 15.) As such, Broker Defendants argue that their lack of knowledge regarding the issues with the underground level prevents Plaintiffs from showing a breach of a fiduciary duty. (Ibid.) Broker Defendants propound the same evidence as above to show they did not know of any issue with the underground level nor that they knew and failed to disclose such information to Plaintiffs.(Ibid.)

 

The court finds that Broker Defendants meet their moving burden with the evidence provided. Plaintiffs once again fail to show that a triable issue of fact exists here regarding Broker Defendants knowledge of the issues pertaining to the underground level or that Broker Defendants fail to disclose any material information to Plaintiffs.

 

As such, Broker Defendants’ motion is granted as to Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action for breach of a fiduciary duty.

 

Continuance

 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs request a continuance on the hearing of Broker Defendants’ motion arguing that Plaintiffs are awaiting responses from Seller Defendants, Duc and Vincent Pham, that may provide additional evidence to support their opposition.

 

            In reply, Broker Defendants seem to be amicable to a continuance if the court is inclined to deny their motion. The court will discuss further with the parties at the hearing.[2]

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants Atlas Real Estate Group, Ali Mourad, and Roger Merians’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.


[1]              Defendants Daniel Hinman, Duc Pham, Vincent Pham, and Verona Street Holdings, LLC have not filed their answer to Plaintiffs’ TAC.

[2]              The court notes that the discovery responses from Seller Defendants were served to Plaintiffs after filing their opposition. If after reviewing such responses Plaintiffs find evidence that supports their burden to show that a triable issue of fact exists as to any of their causes of action against Broker Defendants, Plaintiffs will be allowed to submit such evidence for the court to review. Broker Defendants will then have an opportunity to respond. However, if Plaintiffs have no other evidence to present, the court will proceed with granting Broker Defendants’ motion for the reasons discussed above.


Case Number: 24STCV19199    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 34

Defendants Gary A. Dordick Esq., Dordick Law Corporation, Terry J. Cole Esq, and Douglas D. Shaffer Esq.’s Motion to Strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint is GRANTED.

 

The court will inquire at the hearing whether leave to amend should be granted.

 

Background

 

            On August 1, 2024, Plaintiff Derrick Anderson (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Gary A. Dordick, Esq., Dordick Law Corporation, Terry J. Cole, Esq, and Douglas D. Shaffer, Esq. (“Defendants”) arising from Defendants representation of Plaintiff alleging causes of action for:

 

1.                 Negligence;

2.                 Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and

3.                 Breach of Contract.

 

On November 6, 2024, Defendants filed this Motion to Strike. No opposition or other responsive pleading has been filed.

           

Legal Standard

 

            The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.) “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.) 

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants move to strike the following portions of Plaintiff’s complaint:

 

1.               Paragraph 78, line 27: “Defendant and” and “intentionally and/or willfully”;

2.               Paragraph 79, line 1: “gross negligence, and willful misconduct, and each of them”;

3.               Paragraph 79, line 3: “pain and suffering, and emotional distress according to proof”;

4.               Paragraph 86, lines 21-22: “pain and suffering, and emotional distress according to proof”;

5.               Paragraph 91, lines 17-18: “pain and suffering, and emotional distress according to proof”;

6.               Page 26, line 23: Prayer: “For general non-economic damages according to proof at trial”

7.               Page 26, lines 24-25: Prayer: “and non-economic” and “according to proof”;

8.               Page 27, line 3: Prayer: “For general non-economic damages according to proof at trial”;

9.               Page 27, lines 4-5: Prayer: “other”, “and non-economic” and “according to proof”;

10.            Page 27, line 11: Prayer: “For general non-economic damages according to proof at trial”;

11.            Page 27, lines 12-13: Prayer: “other”, “and non-economic” and “according to proof.”

 

(Motion, at p. 2.)

 

“Pain and Suffering” and Emotional Distress Damages

 

            Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint on the basis that Plaintiff did plead sufficient facts to support a claim for “pain and suffering” and “emotional distress” damages as the Plaintiff’s alleged injuries only involve economic loss. (Id., at pp. 5-7.)

 

            Plaintiff does not oppose Defendants’ motion.

 

            General damages are damages that necessarily result from the act complained of. (Licudine v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center¿(2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 881, 891.)  General damages include damages for pain and suffering, emotional distress and other forms of detriment that are sometimes characterized as subjective or not directly quantifiable.  (Id.,¿at 892.)  General damages are generally not recoverable in legal malpractice cases where the interest of the client is economic.  (See Camenisch v. Superior Court (1996) 44 Cal.Appp.4th 1689, 1695.)  "Where the interest of the client is economic, serious emotional distress is not an inevitable consequence of the loss of money and, as noted, the precedents run strongly against recovery." (Merenda v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 1, 10, disapproved on other grounds in Ferguson v. Leiff, Cabraser, Heimann & Bernstein (2003) 30 Cal.4th 1037, 1044-45.)

 

The court finds that Plaintiff’s complaint does not adequately allege a basis for an award of general damages because the injuries alleged are all economic in nature and lack sufficient allegations of accompanying emotional distress or other non-economic losses, such as pain and suffering. As such, the court grants Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for “pain and suffering” and “emotional distress” damages.

 

“According to proof”

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff is not able to allege damages solely “according to proof” without stating an amount requested as Plaintiff’s claim is purely for economic losses under California Code of Civil Procedure section 425.10(a)(2). (Motion, at p. 8.)

 

            Plaintiff does not oppose Defendants’ motion.

 

“If the recovery of money or damages is demanded, the amount demanded shall be stated.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10(a)(2).) “Section 425.10 requires all complaints to state the amount of damages sought, except in personal injury or wrongful death cases.” (Electronic Funds Solutions v. Murphy (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1176.)

                        

The court finds that Plaintiff’s complaint does not adequately state the amount of damages sought. It is clear that Plaintiff seeks a monetary recovery which is to be stated somewhere in the complaint to comply with CCP section 425.10(a)(2). As such, the court grants Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for damages “according to proof”.

 

Gross Negligence

 

            Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s allegation of “gross negligence” in paragraph 79 of the complaint arguing that no specific facts are set forth in support of the allegation. (Motion, at p. 8.)

 

            Plaintiff does not oppose Defendants’ motion.

 

California does not recognize a distinct common law cause of action for gross negligence apart from negligence.  (Jimenez v. 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 546, 552, fn. 3; Chavez v. 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 632, 640; Eriksson v. Nunnink (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 826, 856, fn. 18 [“[i]n reality, California does not recognize a distinct cause of action for ‘gross negligence’ independent of a statutory basis”].)  Numerous cases discuss the doctrine of gross negligence; however, these cases have invariably involved a statute containing the words “gross negligence” in the text.  (Cont'l Ins. Co. v. Am. Prot. Indus. (1987) 197 Cal.App.3d 322, 329.)  Rather, as a degree of negligence, “[g]ross negligence is pleaded by alleging the traditional elements of negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.”  (Rosencrans v. Dover Images, Ltd. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1082.)  To set forth, “gross negligence” the plaintiff must also allege conduct by the defendant involving either “want of even scant care” or “an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct.”  (Jimenez, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at 555.)  Gross negligence connotes such a lack of care as may be presumed to indicate a passive and indifferent attitude toward results.  (Chavez, supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at 640.) 

 

Although gross negligence allegations may be appropriate within a cause of action for negligence, Plaintiff fails to make sufficient allegations that Defendants’ negligent conduct amount to the degree of gross negligence. (See Jimenez, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at 552 fn. 3 [referring to gross negligence as a “degree of negligence”].) As such, the court grants Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s allegation of “gross negligence” in paragraph 79.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants Gary A. Dordick Esq., Dordick Law Corporation, Terry J. Cole Esq, and Douglas D. Shaffer Esq.’s Motion to Strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint is GRANTED.

 

The court will inquire at the hearing whether leave to amend should be granted.
