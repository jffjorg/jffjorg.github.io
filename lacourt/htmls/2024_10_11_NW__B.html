DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 20STCV38285    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: B

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - NORTHWEST DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT B

 

 

KAI SMITH,

 

                        Plaintiff,

 

            vs.

 

GAME SPORTS BAR AND GRILL, LLC,

 

                        Defendant.

 

CASE NO:  20STCV38285

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO AMEND THE COMPLAINT TO ADD A CLAIM FOR FRAUD AND ADDITIONAL DEFENDANTS

 

Dept. B

8:30 a.m.

OCTOBER 11, 2024

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Kai Smith (“Plaintiff”) moves for an order granting leave to file a second amended

complaint (“SAC”) to add a cause of action for Fraud (Intentional Misrepresentation) and to add additional defendants to the operative complaint, based on alleged fraudulent declarations by Defendant Tyrei Lacy (“Defendant”) made after Plaintiff’s first amended complaint (“FAC”). and in the course of litigation (Motion, p. 2.)

            Plaintiff filed his initial Complaint on October 5, 2020, alleging four causes of action: (1) negligent hiring, retention, and supervision; (2) assault; (3) battery; and (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress against Defendants Game Sports Bar and Grill “Game Sports”) and DOES 1 to 10. (See Complaint.)

            On December 5, 2022, Plaintiff filed a FAC to add Defendants Tyrei Lacy (herein “Defendant”) and Sylvia Lacy (“Sylvia”).

            On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion.

            On September 30, 2024, Defendant filed their opposition. Plaintiff did not file a reply.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARDS

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. §473(a)(1).) 

“Trial courts are vested with the discretion to allow amendments to pleadings ‘in furtherance of justice.’ That trial courts are to liberally permit such amendments, at any stage of the proceeding, has been established policy in this state since 1901.”  (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489.) 

Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1321(a) requires that a motion to amend must: “[i]nclude a copy of the proposed . . . amended pleading . . . [and] state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be [deleted and/or added], if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the [deleted and/or additional] allegations are located.” 

CRC Rule 3.1324(b) provides, as follows: “[a] separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify: (1) [t]he effect of the amendment; (2) [w]hy the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) [w]hen the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) [t]he reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.” 

 

III. DISCUSSION

            Procedural Deficiencies

Plaintiff’s motion does not comply with CRC Rule 3.1324(a). The motion fails to include a copy of the proposed SAC.  Additionally, Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration fails to set forth the allegations proposed to be added and deleted, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number. (CRC Rule 3.1324(a).)

Plaintiff’s motion does not comply with CRC Rule 3.1324(b).  Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration does not specify the effect of the amendment nor explain why the amendments are necessary and proper.  (CRC Rule 3.1324(b).)

Plaintiff’s counsel states when the facts giving rise of the amended allegations were discovered but does not explain why the request for amendment was not made earlier.  Plaintiff’s counsel declares that, on October 12, 2023, Defendant provided two fraudulent declarations from Defendant’s employees, alleged percipient witnesses Rayshawn Billups and Dean Michels. (Diourbel Decl. ¶ 8.) Plaintiff’s counsel alleges that each declarant falsely claimed that they saw Plaintiff safely exit Game Sports Bar without any incident or harm, and that there were no reports of any altercations on the day of Plaintiff’s alleged injuries. (Ibid.) However, Plaintiff’s counsel claims that, on November 20, 2023, Defendant was shown a cellphone video of Defendant’s bouncers attacking Plaintiff. (Ibid. at ¶ 9.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s counsel declares that Defendant’s declarants provided “material, ostensibly false statements, which are refuted by the video evidence” and which are potentially felonious and perjurious pursuant to Cal. Pen. Code § 118. (Ibid. at ¶ 11.) Despite these claims, Plaintiff fails to explain why the instant motion was not filed for almost a year after the facts giving rise to the motion occurred. Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion fails to comply with CRC Rule 3.124(b).)

“A long unexcused delay may be the basis for denying permission to amend pleadings [citations], especially where the proposed amendment interjects a new issue [citations], which may require further investigation or discovery procedures [citations].” (Nelson v. Specialty Records, Inc. (1970) 11 Cal.App.3d 126, 139.) “[T]he trial court has wide discretion in determining whether to allow [proposed] amendment[s], but the appropriate exercise of that discretion requires the trial court to consider a number of factors: ‘including the conduct of the moving party and the belated presentation of the amendment. . . The law is well settled that a long deferred presentation of the proposed amendment without a showing of excuse for the delay is itself a significant factor to uphold the trial court’s denial of the amendment.’ [Citation].” (Ibid. at 613.) Here, Plaintiff knew of the facts which gave rise to the proposed amendment for nearly a year before taking action. Plaintiff does not proffer an explanation for their delay.

While the Court has a liberal policy of granting leave to amend, the Court may not do so when opposing parties would be substantially prejudiced. Prejudice exists where the amendment would result in a delay of trial, along with loss of critical evidence, added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc.  (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488; see P & D Consultants, Inc. v. City of Carlsbad (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 1332, 1345; Fisher v. Larsen (1982) 138 Cal.App.3d 627, 649, [leave to amend properly denied where P knew for over five months claims had not been properly pleaded and took no action to amend until after summary judgment granted against it.]) Here, the discovery cut-off date has passed, and Plaintiff seeks to introduce privileged declarations obtained during the course of this litigation and through efforts to get a mediation on calendar. (Masserat Decl. ¶ 2.) Pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code §§ 1119 and 1152, declarations obtained during settlement negotiations are inadmissible. Additionally, the declarations at issue are protected by the litigation privilege doctrine since they were made in the course of litigation. This doctrine protects statements even if they are “alleged to be fraudulent, perjurious, unethical, or even illegal.” (Kashian v. Harriman (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 892, 920.) Therefore, in addition to Plaintiff’s failure to comply with CRC Rule 3.124(a) and (b), granting the instant motion would substantially prejudice Defendant and the alleged statements are privileged.

IV. CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend his complaint is DENIED.

I.       BACKGROUND  

            Defendant Tyrei Lacy (“Defendant”) moves to strike portions of plaintiff Kai Smith (“Plaintiff’)’s Complaint in connection to its allegations for punitive damages (Compl., Prayer, Lines 11-12), attorney’s fees (Compl., Prayer, Lines 12-13), and any allegations of personal liability as to Defendant.

            The Complaint alleges that, on or about October 6, 2018, Plaintiff attended a birthday party at Game Sports Bar and Grill. (Compl. ¶ 6.) Later that night, as the bar was closing, Plaintiff made his way to the restroom while holding a bottle of champagne that he purchased. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges that, at this time, a bouncer confronted him, shouting that he couldn’t take the champagne bottle out of the bar. (Ibid.) Plaintiff explained that he just needed to use the restroom and began to walk away. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that another bar employee took the bottle from Plaintiff, and then two to three more bouncers assaulted Plaintiff, punching his head, face, and upper body. (Ibid.) Plaintiff claims serious injuries as a result. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

            On October 5, 2020, Plaintiff filed the Complaint against Defendants Game Sports Bar and Grill, LLC (“Game Sports”) and DOES 1 to 10 alleging four causes of action: (1) negligent hiring, retention, and supervision; (2) assault; (3) battery; and (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress. The Complaint seeks punitive damages and attorney’s fees.

            On December 5, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) adding Defendants Tyrei Lacy and Sylvia Lacy (“Sylvia”).

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARDS

            “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255; accord Cyrus v. Haveson (1976) 65 Cal. App. 3d 306, 316-17.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (Smith v. Sup. Ct. (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.)

            CCP § 3294(a), governing punitive damages, provides: “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”

            Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (CCP § 3294(c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” (Ibid., § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Ibid., § 3294(c)(3).)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Defendant’s Liability

            Defendant moves the Court to strike any and all allegations of their personal liability.

            “[P]laintiff must plead the ultimate facts which give rise to liability.” (Blegen v. Superior Court (1981) 125 Cal.App.3d 959, 963.) An employer can be liable for an employee’s tortious acts even if the acts were unauthorized, willful, malicious, or criminal, if the acts were an outgrowth of employment, inherent in the working environment, typical of or broadly incidental to the employer’s business, or reasonably foreseeable from the employee’s duties. (Yamaguchi v. Harnsmut (2003) 106 Cal. App.4th 472, 481.) However, an employer is not liable if the employee substantially deviates from their employment duties for personal purposes. If the employee’s tortious act is personal in nature, not engendered by the employment, or not an outgrowth of the employment, the employer will not be held liable. (Farmers Ins. Group v. County of Santa Clara (1995) 11 Cal.App.4th 992, 1004-1005.) The determination of whether an employee’s act falls within the scope of employment is generally a question of fact, but it can become a question of law when the facts are undisputed and no conflicting inferences are possible. (Montague v. AMN Healthcare, Inc. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1515, 1520) The burden of proving that the employee’s tortious conduct was committed within the scope of employment lies with the plaintiff. (Ibid. at 1520-1521.)

            Here, it is undisputed that Defendant is an owner of Game Sports and employer of its employees. Plaintiff alleges this fact in their FAC. (See FAC ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s FAC also alleges that Game Sports employees violently attacked and beat Plaintiff, resulting in serious injuries. (Ibid. ¶¶ 9-11.) The employees allegedly beat Plaintiff when attempting to usher him out of the premises. (Ibid. at ¶ 9.) This tortious conduct is an outgrowth of the bouncers’ employment under Defendant and, while unauthorized and malicious, was reasonably foreseeable from the bouncers’ duties. Bouncers are often hired and retained for security efforts in entertainment venues. Therefore, it is reasonably foreseeable that a bouncer who is negligently hired might provoke heated altercations with patrons, resulting in an attack like the one alleged in Plaintiff’s complaint. Therefore, Plaintiff pleads facts which, if proven at trial, may support Defendant’s liability. Defendant’s request to strike any and all allegations of their personal liability is denied.

 

Punitive Damages

            Defendant’s request to strike punitive damages is also denied. Punitive damages are properly plead when Plaintiff alleges facts showing oppression, fraud, or malice. Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (CCP § 3294(c)(1).) Plaintiff plead facts showing malicious conduct by Defendant’s employees when they violently attacked and beat Plaintiff. (FAC ¶¶ 9-11.) Additionally, Plaintiff plead facts demonstrating Lacy’s liability for this conduct. (See discussion supra.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages is well-plead and Defendant’s request to strike is denied.

Attorney’s Fees

            Plaintiff agreed to withdraw his request for attorney’s fees. Therefore, Defendant’s request to strike attorney’s fees is granted.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motion is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. Defendant’s requests to strike Defendant’s personal liability and punitive damages from Plaintiff’s complaint are denied. Defendant’s request to strike attorney’s fees is GRANTED.
