DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

MOTION TO STRIKE REFERENCES TO PUNITIVE DAMAGES IN COMPLAINT

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23CHCV02609

 

Motion filed: 10/2/23

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Magic Mountain, LLC (“Magic Mountain” or the “moving Defendant”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) 

NOTICE: ok¿¿¿ 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order granting the moving Defendant’s Motion to Strike References to Punitive Damages in Complaint.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants Magic Mountain, LLC, Jose Arriaga (“Arriaga”), and Does 1 through 50. The Complaint alleges 12 causes of action: (1) sexual battery/abuse of a minor; (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (3) sexual harassment; (4) negligent hiring, supervision & retention of an unfit employee; (5) negligent failure to warn, train or educate; (6) negligent supervision of a minor; (7) negligence; (8) retaliation in violation of the FEHA; (9) retaliation under Labor Code §§ 98.6, 1102.5; (10) sexual harassment in violation of FEHA; (11) discrimination in violation of FEHA; and (12) failure to prevent harassment and discrimination in violation of FEHA.

 

On October 2, 2023, Defendant Magic Mountain filed the instant motion to strike (the “Motion”).

 

On February 8, 2024, this case was reassigned to department F49.

 

On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition to the Motion. Subsequently, on February 15, Magic Mountain replied.

 

 

ANALYSIS

“Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b), par. (1).)

 

“Before filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).)

A.    Meet and Confer Requirement

 

The moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading to resolve the issue raised in the motion to strike. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) Moreover, the moving party must include in their motion to strike a declaration stating either (1) the means by which the meet and confer occurred and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the objections, or (2) that the party who filed the pleading failed to respond to the meet and confer request. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a), par. (3), subpar. (A) (B).)

 

However, the moving Defendant Magic Mountain has failed to include a code-compliant declaration. Furthermore, there is no presentation of facts indicating that an effort to meet and confer has been made. Consequently, the Court deems the meet and confer requirement was not been satisfied.

 

The Court directs both parties to adhere to the relevant sections and requirement for the meet and confer process outlined in Code of Civil Procedures in all future proceedings.

 

B.     Motion to Strike References to Punitive Damages

 

The moving Defendant Magic Mountain moves to strike all portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint referring punitive damages, including:

 

1.      Causes of Action, stating “oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious intent” and “Plaintiff is entitled to punitive damages, pursuant to California Civil Code section 3294, so as to punish Defendant, Magic Mountain and deter them from similar conduct in the future.” (Complaint, at 13:15-19, 14:25-15:4, 16:8-12, 17:17-21, 18:22-26.)

 

2.      Pray for Damages 2(d), 3(d), 4(d), 5(d), 6(d), stating “for punitive damages according to proof.” (Complaint, at 19-20.)

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

Specific facts must be pleaded in support of punitive damages.  (Hillard v. A.H. Robins Co. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-392.) Facts must be pled to show that a defendant “act[ed] with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff’s rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462.) Conduct that is merely negligent will not support a claim for punitive damages. (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1288.)

“An employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages . . . based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civ. Code § 3294(b).)

           

Malice is defined as either “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff,” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) “Despicable conduct is conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” (Mock v. Michigan Millers Mutual Ins. Co. (1992) 4 Cal. App. 4th 306, 331.) Fraud under California Civil Code, Section 3294(c)(3) “means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” California Civil Code, Section 3294(c)(2) defines oppression as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”

In its Motion, Magic Mountain argues that the Complaint is deficient in two key respects. First, it asserts that there are no facts demonstrating that Magic Mountain acted with oppression, fraud, or malice. (Mot., at 5.) Second, it claims that there are no specific facts alleged against any particular officers, directors, or managing agents of Magic Mountain. (Id., at 8.)

 

In her Opposition, the Plaintiff contends that the Complaint has indeed pleaded ultimate factual allegations showing that Magic Mountain acted with both malice and conscious when it terminated her employment after she reported sexual abuse and harassment by Co-Defendant Arriga, citing Roberts v. Ford Aerospace & Communications Corp. (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 793. Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that factual allegations sufficiently demonstrate ratification by a managing agent of Magic Mountain. (Opp’n., at 5-7.)

 

In Coasts v. Construction & Glen. Laborers Local No. 185 (1971) 15 Cal.App.3d 908 (Coasts), a union’s officers refused to disciple its employee after being informed that the employees assaulted a union member. The Coast Court found that the union’s failure to discharge or discipline the offending employees amounted to ratification which justified an award of punitive damages against the employer under Civil Code section 3294.

 

In Hart v. National Mortgage & Land Co., (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1420 (Hart), the plaintiff alleged his superiors were aware that other employees were harassing him and condoned it. The Court of Appeal reversed the trial court’s summary judgment, stating, specifically regarding the issue of punitive damage, that the plaintiff should have been permitted to amend his complaint to allege the ratification provision. (Id., at 1421.) The Hart court further clarified that the plaintiff could be entitled to punitive damages by showing that his supervisor's knowledge and failure to act constituted ratification imputable to the employer, or, alternative, by demonstrating that his supervisor was an “officer, director or manage agent” of the employee, and he committed acts of “oppression ... or malice.” (Id., at 1433.)

 

 Here, Plaintiff alleges that “[i]n or around August 2, 2022, Plaintiff reported the incidents of abuse to Magic Mountain. Magic Mountain referred her to the on-site deputies at the park. Since Plaintiff was a minor at the time, the deputies contacted her family and informed them of Plaintiff’s report.” (Compl., ¶ 23.) “After reporting the abuse to Magic Mountain ... Magic Mountain allowed Arriaga to continue working there and placed Plaintiff on a leave of absence.” (Id., ¶ 25.) Furthermore, “[s]ince the ‘investigation’ into Plaintiff’s complaints of workplace harassment and discrimination, Plaintiff experienced retaliation from Magic Mountain. On or around September 15, 2022, without any warning thereto, Magic Mountain’s Employee Service Office Supervisor sent Plaintiff a letter letting her know that her employment had been discontinued.” (Id., ¶¶ 26-27.)

 

 The Court finds that Plaintiff’s Complaint has pleaded sufficient factual allegation under the guidance of the Hart ruling. Plaintiff alleges that she reported Arriaga’s harassing conduct to the employer, Magic Mountain, on August 2, 2022, and subsequently, Magic Mountain terminated her employment on September 15, 2022, while Arriaga continued working there. These factual allegations raise triable issues as to whether Magic Mountain failed to act, and whether the termination decision sent by the “Employee Service Office” can be imputed to Magic Mountain.

 

Therefore, the Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Magic Mountain, LLC’s Motion to Strike References to Punitive Damages is DENIED. Defendant is to file an answer within thirty (30) days of this order.

 

Moving party to give notice of this order.
