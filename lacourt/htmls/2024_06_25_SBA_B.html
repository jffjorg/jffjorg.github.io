County of Los Angeles

LOS ANGELES WORLD AIRPORTS, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          June 25, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Ramona Murillo Gamboa

Responding Party:                  Defendants Los Angeles World Airports, City of Los Angeles, and Concesionaria Vuela Compania de Aviacion, S.A.P.I de C.V.

Motion to Tax Costs

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On July 24, 2019, plaintiff Ramona Murillo Gamboa filed a complaint against defendants Los Angeles World Airports, City of Los Angeles, SAS Services Group, Concesionaria Vuela Compania de Aviacion S.A.P.I. De C.V., and Controladora Vuela Compania de Aviacion, S.A.B. De C.V. for (1) premises liability; (2) dangerous condition of public property; (3) breach of duty of a common carrier; (4) negligence; (5) negligent hiring; and (6) negligence per se.  Plaintiff alleges that she slipped and fell causing her to sustain injuries on June 20, 2018.

On March 22, 2021, plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint. 

On March 23, 2023, the court sustained with leave to amend Buenos Travel Agency (Doe 1)’s demurrer to the FAC and denied defendant’s motion to dismiss under Barrows.

On April 25, 2023, plaintiff filed a SAC against defendants Los Angeles World Airports, City of Los Angeles, SAS Services Group, Concesionaria Vuela Compania de Aviacion S.A.P.I. De C.V., and Controladora Vuela Compania de Aviacion, S.A.B. De C.V., and Buenos Travel Agency for (1) premises liability; (2) dangerous condition of public property; (3) breach of duty of a common carrier; (4) negligence; (5) general negligence; (6) negligent hiring; and (7) negligence per se.

On May 15, 2023, the court granted defendant S.A.S. Services Group, Inc.’s application for determination of good faith settlement.

On January 29, 2024, the court granted Los Angeles World Airports and the City of Los Angeles’ motion for summary judgment.  The court also granted defendant Concesionaria Vuela Compania de Aviacion, S.A.P.I. de C.V. (“Volaris”)’s motion for summary judgment.

On April 13, 2024, judgment was entered.  Defendants were entitled to costs pursuant to a separate memorandum of costs.

On May 1, 2024, defendants filed and served a memorandum of costs

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.  This means that the prevailing party is entitled to all of his costs unless another statute provides otherwise.  Absent such statutory authority, the court has no discretion to deny costs to the prevailing party.”  Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal. App. 4th 111, 128-29 (citations and internal quotations omitted); CCP §1032(b) (“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding”).

CCP §1033.5(c) states, in relevant part:  “Any award for costs shall be subject to the following: 

            (1) Costs are allowable if incurred, whether or not paid. 

(2) Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation. 

(3) Allowable costs shall be reasonable in amount. 

(4) Items not mentioned in this section . . . may be allowed . . . in the Court’s discretion.”

“If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.”  Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal. App. 4th 761, 774 (citation omitted).   Id.  “The court’s first determination, therefore, is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item, and whether it appears proper on its face.  If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show them to be unnecessary or unreasonable.”  Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal. App. 4th 111, 131 (citations omitted).  “[I]t is not enough for the losing party to attack submitted costs by arguing that he thinks the costs were not necessary or reasonable.  Rather the losing party has the burden to present evidence and prove that the claimed costs are not recoverable.”  Seever v. Copley Press, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal. App. 4th 1550, 1557.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff requests that the Court tax defendants’ memorandum of costs.

            The memorandum of costs was filed and served on May 1, 2024, on plaintiff’s counsel by email via One Legal.  Defendants are requesting $2703.65 in filing and motion fees, $4727.56 in deposition costs, $662.15 in service of process, and $588 in interpreter fees.

            Plaintiff asserts that the prevailing party failed to timely serve the memorandum of costs because it was not served on plaintiff and that plaintiff’s counsel of record did not explicitly give permission to defense counsel that plaintiff’s counsel may be served “instead of the subject debtor” as mandated by CCP §684.020.  Plaintiff also argues that defendants are attempting “to assert damages not related to the action” and that they “have not itemized the expenses or described how the outrageous amount was arrived to, and as such, it should be denied as grossly unreasonable and is excessive.”  Plaintiff further contends that defendants “failed to itemize how the attorney’s fees were calculated.”

            In opposition, defendants argue that the memorandum of costs was filed and served timely and properly served on plaintiff’s attorney of record.  As defendants point out, the notice of entry of judgment was filed and served on April 30, 2024.  Defendants filed their memorandum of costs on May 1, 2024, well within the 15-day time period.  Defendants further contend that CCP §684.020 does not apply because it pertains to “judgment debtors” and plaintiff does not fall under the definition of “judgment debtor” as no money judgment has been entered against her.  See law revision comment under CCP §680.250.  Moreover, under Cal. Rules of Court Rule 8.25, defendants assert, a party must serve one copy of the document on the attorney for each party separately represented, which defense counsel did.  Defendants also assert that they properly itemized all allowable costs, referring to the worksheet attached to the memorandum of costs.  Further, defendants contend, they are not seeking attorney’s fees, thus, plaintiff’s argument is meritless.

            In reply, plaintiff contends that filing fees and motion fees are excessive and duplicative.  The Court notes that plaintiff did not raise this argument in the motion, and thus the Court does not consider.

            The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that the memorandum of costs was properly and timely served on plaintiffs’ counsel.  CCP §684.020 does not apply as plaintiff is not considered a “judgment debtor.”  Further, plaintiff does not identify any specific item that is either improper, excessive, or unreasonable.  Despite what plaintiff argued, defendants provided an itemization as to each cost.  Although plaintiff refers to “attorney’s fees,” defendants are not requesting attorney’s fees.  In consideration of the memorandum of costs and the itemization of each cost, the Court finds that plaintiff has not met her burden of showing that the claimed costs are not recoverable.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

ANDY GUY,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV00607

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

TIMOTHY ROTH, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          April 9, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Andy Guy

Responding Party:                  Defendants Timothy Roth, et al.

Motion for Sanctions

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion for sanctions is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On March 1, 2023, Andy Guy filed a complaint against Timothy Roth and Roth Management Group for (1) breach of contract, (2) fraud – concealment, (3) intentional misrepresentation, and (4) conversion.

On August 21, 2023, a request for dismissal was filed and the case was dismissed with prejudice.

On April 9, 2024, the court granted plaintiff’s motion to set aside dismissal.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §128.5 states in part:  “(a) A trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. . . .

(b) For purposes of this section:

(1) “Actions or tactics” include, but are not limited to, the making or opposing of motions or the filing and service of a complaint, cross-complaint, answer, or other responsive pleading. The mere filing of a complaint without service thereof on an opposing party does not constitute “actions or tactics” for purposes of this section.

(2) “Frivolous” means totally and completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.

(c) Expenses pursuant to this section shall not be imposed except on notice contained in a party’s moving or responding papers or, on the court’s own motion, after notice and opportunity to be heard. An order imposing expenses shall be in writing and shall recite in detail the action or tactic or circumstances justifying the order. . . .”

 

CCP §128.7 states, in part:  “(b) By presenting to the court, whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating, a pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person’s knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, all of the following conditions are met:

(1) It is not being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation.

(2) The claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law.

(3) The allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery.

(4) The denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief.

(c) If, after notice and a reasonable opportunity to respond, the court determines that subdivision (b) has been violated, the court may, subject to the conditions stated below, impose an appropriate sanction upon the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation. In determining what sanctions, if any, should be ordered, the court shall consider whether a party seeking sanctions has exercised due diligence.”

DISCUSSION

            This motion arises from the events surrounding the “settlement” of this case.  The communications between the counsel for the parties shows that the case counsel either had reached a settlement or were very close.  (Since the issue of whether or not a binding settlement agreement was reached is the topic of litigation, the Court does not intend to suggest any particular outcome on that question.)

            Ultimately Plaintiff Guy did not sign the proposed written agreement.  However, Defendant’s attorney, who had been provided an attorney executed Request for Dismissal form  filed the Request for Dismissal.  That resulted in the motion to set aside the dismissal.

            Plaintiff Andy Guy requests sanctions against defendants and defendants’ counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $10,617.50, pursuant to CCP §§128.5 and 128.7 claiming that the filing of the Request for Dismissal and the refusal to stipulate to the setting aside of that dismissal are worthy of sanctions.

            As noted above, the Court granted plaintiff’s motion to set aside the dismissal that had been entered on August 21, 2023.  Plaintiff had contended that his former attorney sent defendants’ counsel an executed form CIV-100 request to dismiss case with prejudice “to hold in trust and not file unless and until Guy executed the draft settlement agreement or a revised [one].”  Plaintiff had argued that he had not authorized the filing of the request for dismissal by defense counsel Einar Johnson. The Court had continued the hearing on the motion for sanctions and referred the case to mediation.

            Plaintiff argues that defendants’ counsel’s conduct was “frivolous” under CCP §128.5 and that defendants’ counsel violated his implied certification under CCP §128.7 that the request for dismissal was “not being presented primarily for an improper purpose,” was “warranted by existing law,” and had “evidentiary support” because defense counsel had filed a request to dismiss this action without authorization from plaintiff or his counsel, noting that the “clerk entered the dismissal, apparently under the impression that Guy had filed it.”  Plaintiff explains that plaintiff’s counsel had requested that defendants’ counsel stipulate and agree that the dismissal be set aside, but that defendants’ counsel had refused.  Plaintiff contends that “[n]o reasonable attorney would have surreptitiously filed a request to dismiss his adversary’s action with prejudice.” 

            In opposition, defendants request sanctions in the amount of $9633 against plaintiff, attorney Daniel Hayes, and Hayes Litigation PC, pursuant to CCP §§128.5(g) and 128.7(h).  Defendants incorporate their arguments made in opposition to the motion to set aside dismissal.  Defendants assert that they and their counsel acted consistent with valid and enforceable settlement agreements.  See Einar Johnson decl., attached to opposition to motion to vacate dismissal.  Defendants argue also that the amount requested by plaintiff is an “overinflated sum.” 

            The Court notes that it had declined to ascertain whether the settlement agreements were enforceable, and defendants have not filed a motion to enforce the settlement agreements.  In any event, as to CCP §128.5, the Court does not find that defendants’ counsel’s conduct falls under “frivolous” as such conduct does not appear to be “totally and completely without merit” based on defendants’ version of what occurred during settlement discussions and defendants’ counsel’s purported good faith belief that the case had settled.  The Court thinks that given the totality of the events as shown by the email communications between counsel regarding the settlement agreement, no sanctions are in order under this statute.

            As to CCP §128.7, an issue is whether the filing of the request for dismissal falls under “pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper.”  The Court finds that it does not, although it shall not base its decision on that question.  The Court does not find that the filing of the request for dismissal was “being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increate in the cost of litigation.”  None of the categories under CCP 128.7(b)(2)-(4) apply as they refer to “legal contentions” and “factual contentions.”  Further, the Court finds that sanctions are not warranted against plaintiff and plaintiff’s counsel as the motion was not brought for an improper purpose.

            The motion is DENIED.

            The request of defendant for sanctions is DENIED as well.

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 23TRCV03643    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Torrance Dept. B

 

MAKSIM RUSANOV,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03643

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

TESLA MOTORS, INC.,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          June 25, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Maksim Rusanov

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED and the action is STAYED.

            This Song Beverly lawsuit has a characteristic that differed from most Song Beverly actions.  This actions involves a suit against Tesla.  Unlike traditional auto manufacturers, Tesla sells its vehicles directly to the consumer-purchaser, not through a dealer.  As a result it has a direct contractual relationship with the consumer-purchaser.  It, not a dealer, has an arbitration provision in its contract with the consumer-purchaser.  Hence, the entire line of cases kicked off by Felisilda and continuing through the more recent Ford Motor Warranty Cases line of cases addressing the various theories allowing a manufacturer to obtain the benefit of the selling dealer’s arbitration provision are simply not applicable.  Plaintiff’s arguments failed to appreciate this critical distinction.

BACKGROUND

On November 1, 2023, Maksim Rusanov filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. LC for violations under Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. requests that the court compel arbitration and to stay the case.

The complaint alleges that on May 15, 2023, plaintiff purchased a 2023 Tesla Model Y.  Complaint, ¶5.  After plaintiff took possession of the vehicle and during the warranty period, the vehicle contained defects that substantially impair the use, safety, and/or value of the vehicle.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff provided defendant with sufficient opportunity to service or repair the vehicle.  Id., ¶13.  Defendant was unable and/or failed to service or repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts.  Id., ¶14.

            Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant Tesla presents evidence that on May 5, 2023, upon initially placing the order for the subject vehicle, plaintiff electronically accepted the terms and conditions of the Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”).  Defendant asserts that the MVOA contains an arbitration provision, which states, in part:  “Please carefully read this provision, which applies to any dispute between you and Tesla, Inc. and its affiliates. . . . [Y]ou agree that any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you and Tesla will not be decided by a judge or jury but instead by a single arbitrator in an arbitration administered by the [AAA] under its Consumer Arbitration Rules. . . .”  The provision contains an opt out clause and that if “you do not opt out, this agreement to arbitrate overrides any different arbitration agreement between us, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract.”  Raymond Kim decl., Exh. 1.

Defendant also contends that plaintiff took delivery of the subject vehicle on May 15, 2023, at which time plaintiff executed a Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) for the purchase of the subject vehicle on credit.  Defendant asserts that the RISC also contains an arbitration agreement.  It states in part:  “1.  Either you or we may choose to have any dispute between us decided by arbitration and not in court or by jury trial. . . . Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action. […]”  “We” and “us” are defined as the seller, Tesla Motors, Inc.  See Kim decl., Exh. 2.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the purchase agreement “eclipses” the order agreement, and thus the order agreement is not binding.  Plaintiff also contends that his claims are independent from the agreements, citing to Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324 (“Ochoa”), in that the vehicle warranty claims are disconnected from any purchase agreement.

            In reply, defendant argues that Ochoa and the other cases cited by plaintiff are irrelevant case law because defendant is the direct contracting party and does not have third-party dealerships that handle the ordering and selling of its vehicles.

The Court finds that arbitration agreements exist between plaintiff and defendant Tesla and that plaintiff did not opt-out.  Plaintiff does not dispute that he entered into both agreements.  Even if the RISC supersedes the order agreement, the RISC contains an arbitration agreement as stated above.

  The Ochoa case (which addresses whether a nonsignatory manufacturer can enforce the arbitration agreement) is not applicable to the situation at hand, as Tesla is both the manufacturer and seller of the vehicle and is a party to the agreements, and not a nonsignatory.  Cases involving car manufacturers lacking an agreement with the consumer-purchaser seeking to gain the benefit of the dealer’s arbitration contract is simply not applicable.

Scope of agreement

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims against it fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration clauses as they arise directly out of the relationship between plaintiff as purchaser/consumer and Tesla as the manufacturer/retailer/warrantor/repair facility and relate to the “purchase or condition” of the vehicle.

The Court finds that the scope of the arbitration agreements covers plaintiff’s claims against Tesla as they relate to the condition of the vehicle and arise out of the relationship.  Further, plaintiff’s citation to Ochoa is unavailing.

Enforceability

            Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the agreements are procedurally unconscionable because they are an adhesion contract drafted by defendant.  Plaintiff asserts that he merely clicked through various prompts in relation to the order agreement, indicating the lack of any substantial bargaining power.  Plaintiff also argues that the agreements are substantively unconscionable because of the requirement to exclusively and solely use AAA.  Plaintiff further contends that the arbitration agreement fails to provide any meaningful guidance as to how to access the AAA rules.  Plaintiff asserts that if the court grants the motion, then the AAA provision should be stricken.

In reply, defendant argues that the arbitration provisions are not unconscionable because plaintiff’s decision to order and purchase the subject vehicle was entirely willful and that he has not shown that he lacked bargaining power, citing to Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899.  Defendant also points out that AAA’s Consumer Arbitration Rules allow for the exchange of information between the parties, and are more efficient.

The Court finds that even if the arbitration provisions are adhesion contracts, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration clause.  Moreover, as in Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 931, “there is no reason in this case to conclude that plaintiff lacked power to bargain.  In general, nothing prevents purchasers of . . . vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that plaintiff could not bargain in this case.”  

The Court further finds little to no substantive unconscionability as the arbitration provisions do not lack mutuality.  The discovery rules apply equally to both parties, for example.  Moreover, the Court does not find a basis to strike the provision requiring AAA as arbitrators. 

The motion is thus GRANTED.

ORDER

The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

The action is STAYED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

24TRCV00649

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

THE PICERNE GROUP, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          June 25, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants The Picerne Group, Inc. and Wallcraft Drywall, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Antonio Zaragoza

Demurrer to Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the complaint and the first and second causes of action.

BACKGROUND

            On February 23, 2024, Antonio Zaragoza filed a complaint against The Picerne Group, Inc. and Wallcraft Drywall Inc. for (1) negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of employees and (2) negligence.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendants The Picerne Group, Inc. and Wallcraft Drywall, Inc. demur to the entire complaint and the first and second causes of action on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

The complaint alleges that on March 3, 2022, plaintiff was employed by Vincent Contractors, Inc., working at a construction project located at 24000 Crenshaw Blvd., Torrance.  Complaint, ¶1.  Defendant The Picerne Group, Inc. was the general contractor in charge of and responsible for the entire worksite and safety at the worksite.  Id., ¶2.  Defendant Wallcraft Drywall, Inc. was a subcontractor responsible for drywall at the worksite.  Id., ¶3.  As a result of defendant negligence, a piece of drywall was thrown or accidentally dropped off the worksite striking plaintiff in the head and knocking him off a ladder and causing him to fall on the ground from the scaffolding.  Id., ¶5.  Plaintiff sustained serious injuries while in the course of his employment, including a permanent traumatic brain injury and cervical spine injury.  Id., ¶6.

Defendants argue that the causes of action are barred by the Privette doctrine and the exclusive remedy of worker’s compensation as set forth in Labor Code §3602.  Defendants explain that Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal. 4th 689 holds that if an employee of an independent contractor hired to do work suffers a work-related injury, the hirer is generally not liable for on-the-job injuries to the independent contractor’s workers, and that an employee injured in the course and scope of performance is already entitled to recover from their employer under the state worker’s compensation system.  Defendants note that the Privette doctrine has been extended to apply to general contractors.  Defendants contend that as alleged, Picerne was the general contractor and that under Privette it would generally not be liable for plaintiff’s injuries on the understanding that plaintiff’s employer (Vincent Contractors) is responsible for his work-related injuries through the worker’s compensation system as his employer is responsible for supervising plaintiff and had a direct duty to him to comply with the standard of care in ensuring his safety.  As to Wallcraft, defendants argue that the allegations are vague as they do not specify whether Wallcraft was a hirer of plaintiff’s employer in order to determine whether Privette applies.  Defendants also argue that none of the Privette doctrine exceptions apply.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendants’ reliance on Privette is misplaced and unsupported, asserting that Privette is not a bar on plaintiff’s recovery from a third-party tortfeasor.  Rather, plaintiff argues, “it bars a plaintiff from suing his employer outside of the Workers Compensation system.”  Plaintiff asserts that he is bringing this action for a third-party’s negligence, which is permitted under Labor Code §3852.  Plaintiff further contends that even if Privette applies, defendants cannot “prove that Privette’s exceptions are inapplicable.”

In reply, defendants reiterate the Privette doctrine, which defendants assert has been broadly applied to protect general contractors from being sued by the employees of their subcontractors, and that “[t]his is the precise scenario for” The Picerne Group.  Defendants also contend that the allegations are vague as to Wallcraft and thus they cannot determine whether the Privette doctrine applies to it.

The Court rules as follows:  “The Privette doctrine holds that a hirer generally delegates to an independent contractor all responsibility for workplace safety and is not liable for injuries sustained by the contractor or its workers while on the job.”  Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021) 12 Cal.5th 29, 40.  There are, however, two exceptions to the Privette doctrine.  “The first exception . . . is usually referred to as the retained control exception.  It applies if:  (1) the hirer retains control over the manner in which the contractor performs the work; (2) the hirer actually exercises its retained control by involving itself in the work such that the contractor is not entirely free to do the work in its own manner; and (2) the hirer’s exercise of retained control affirmatively contributes to the worker’s injury. . . . The second exception . . . is usually referred to as the concealed hazard exception.  If applies if the hirer is also an owner or possessor of land, and if ‘the landowner knew, or should have known, of a latent or concealed preexisting hazardous condition on its property, the contractor did not know and could not have reasonably discovered this hazardous condition, and the landowner failed to warn the contractor about this condition.’”  Miller v. Roseville Lodge No. 1293 (2022) 83 Cal. App. 5th 825, 833-34 (citations omitted).

The Court finds that the Privette doctrine appears to bar the causes of action against The Picerne Group, which is alleged to be the general contractor, and thus the “hirer” of plaintiff’s employer, Vincent Contractors.  The allegations are insufficient to encompass any of the exceptions to Privette.  As to Wallcraft, the allegations do not show that the Privette doctrine applies as there is no allegation that Wallcraft was the hirer of plaintiff’s employer.  The Court shall not rule on the uncertainty demurrer as an amended pleading shall be filed.  The Court notes that the Complaint is exceedingly sparse in describing anything other than the identification of the parties and the bare-bones description of the event as involving a piece of drywall “thrown or accidently dropped of the WORKSITE.”  Nothing more is pleaded as to the involvement of the various actors.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the complaint and the first and second causes of action.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
