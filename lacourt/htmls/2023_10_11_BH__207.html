DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV39105    Hearing Date: October 11, 2023    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING - NO. 1

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

October 11, 2023

CASE NUMBER

21STCV39105

MOTION

Consolidate Cases or in the alternative, to Continue Trial and Discovery Cut-Off Dates

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Michael Landver

OPPOSING PARTIES

Defendants Matrix Clinical Research, Inc.; Peyman Banooni; Stan Gershovich; and Faramarz [Fred] Shaham

 

MOTION

 

Plaintiff Michael Landver (“Plaintiff” or “Landver”) moves to consolidate this case with related case, number 21STCV43580, and to continue the trial date and discovery cut-off dates to conform with the trial and discovery cut-off dates in 21STCV43580 (“-3580 related case”) which is related to 21STCV39105. 

 

Defendants Matrix Clinical Research, Inc.; Peyman Banooni; Stan Gershovich; and Faramarz [Fred] Shaham (collectively, “Defendants”) oppose the motion. 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

            Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of “its files in Cases Nos. 21STCV39105, 21STCV43580, 21SMCV01358, and 22STCV07260[.]”  Defendants object on the basis that Plaintiff did not specify which specific portions of the court files are sought to be judicially noticed.  Defendants further object on the basis that the Court cannot accept as true the contents of court files.

 

            Defendants’ objections are overruled.  Per Plaintiff’s request, and on its own motion, the Court takes judicial notice of the relevant portions of its court files in the four cases Plaintiff has identified. 

 

However, “while courts are free to take judicial notice of the existence of each document in a court file, including the truth of results reached, they may not take judicial notice of the truth of hearsay statements in decisions and court files.  Courts may not take judicial notice of allegations in affidavits, declarations and probation reports in court records because such matters are reasonably subject to dispute and therefore require formal proof.”  (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 [cleaned up].) 

 

Accordingly, the Court takes judicial notice of the relevant files in the four requested cases as court records, including the truth of results reached, but not the truth of hearsay statements or allegations contained therein.

 

Objections to Memorandum of Points and Authorities

 

Defendants take issue with various arguments raised in the memorandum of points and authorities in support of the motion.  This is not an evidentiary objection, and as such, the Court overrules it.  The Court is cognizant of the distinction between opinion, argument, and facts supported by evidence, and interprets the memorandum of points and authorities accordingly.

ANALYSIS

 

            Consolidation

 

 

Per Local Rule 3.3, subdivision (g), “Cases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department.  A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related into a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.”  (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 3.3(g)(1).)  Once the Court relates the cases, the Court may consolidate the actions and order a joint trial on matters that “involv[e] a common question of law or fact.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §1048, subd. (a).) The Court has already related these cases, and both are pending in this department.  Code of Civil Procedure, section 1048, subdivision (a) provides:

 

When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.

 

            Plaintiff moves to consolidate this case and the related -3580 case because (1) the cases involve common questions of law and fact; (2) the parties are the same in both actions; (3) the issues are essentially the same, the plaintiffs and defendants are simply in reverse roles; and (4) the relief sought and theories of liability in the related -3580 action “are identical to the counter-claim filed by defendants in the AAA arbitration[….]”  In other words, “[t]he two cases are mirror images of each other.”  (Motion at p. 14; Baranov Decl. ¶¶ 23-25.)

 

            Plaintiff argues that if the cases are not consolidated, there will be duplication of discovery and the action will be tried twice, wasting judicial resources and risking potentially inconsistent rulings.

 

            Defendants oppose the request to consolidate because (1) Plaintiff delayed in consolidating the two actions; and (2) the parties are not the same and the issues are not similar. 

 

            The Court agrees that Plaintiff delayed in moving to consolidate the two actions.  On October 4, 2022, the Court issued a Minute Order in the -3580 case, relating this case, the -3580 case, and case number 21SMCV01358 (now dismissed), and transferring them all to Department 207 in the Beverly Hills courthouse.  The Minute Order further provided, “This order is made without prejudice to the parties making a motion to consolidate in the newly assigned department.”

 

            Thus, the Minute Order relating the cases, which contemplated a forthcoming motion to consolidate, was entered approximately a year before Plaintiff filed the instant motion to consolidate.   

 

            Nonetheless, the Court finds consolidation appropriate here.  Contrary to Defendants’ argument, the Court finds that the parties are essentially the same and the issues are essentially mirrors of each other.  In this case, Michael Landver is Plaintiff, and Matrix Clinical, Fred Shaham, Peyman Banooni, and Stan Gershovich are defendants.  In the related -3580 case, Peyman Banooni, Stan Gershovich, Fred Shaham, and United Clinical Research are plaintiffs and Michael Landver and California Clinical Research are Defendants.  Moreover, the claims in the related -3580 case are the same as the counter-claims Defendants raised in the arbitration proceeding.  (Baranov Decl., Ex. A.) 

 

            Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion for consolidation.

 

            Trial and Discovery Cut-Off Dates

 

            In the alternative, Plaintiff moves to continue the trial and discovery cut-off dates to conform with the recently continued dates in the related -3580 case.

 

When the three cases were related on October 4, 2022, the Court deemed the now-dismissed -1358 case the lead case.  (October 4, 2022 Minute Order in Case Number 21STCV43580.)  The still-pending related -3580 case was filed approximately a month after this case.

 

            On September 15, 2023, the parties orally stipulated to continue trial in the related

-3580 case to March 25, 2024, and to continue the discovery cut-off dates in accordance with the new trial date.

 

            For the avoidance of doubt, in consolidating the two remaining related cases, the March 25, 2024 trial date (and attendant discovery cut-off dates) shall apply. 

 

            California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c), provides:

 

Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits.  The court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.  Circumstances that may indicate good cause include:

 

[…]

 

(6) A party’s excused inability to obtain essentially testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; or

 

(7) A significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case as a result of which the case is not ready for trial.

 

Factors the Court considers in ruling on a motion for continuance include:

 

(1)  The proximity of the trial date;

 

(2)  Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party;

 

(3)  The length of the continuance requested;

 

(4)  The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance;

 

(5)  The prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance;

 

(6)  If the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay;

 

(7)  The court's calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials;

 

(8)  Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial;

 

(9)  Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance;

 

(10)  Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and

 

(11)  Any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(d).)

 

            Plaintiff argues that a continuance is warranted because of Defendants’ discovery abuse and failure to comply with discovery orders, and the short delay will not prejudice anyone.  Defendants argue that a continuance is not warranted because Plaintiff was not diligent in pursuing discovery. 

 

The Court disagrees with Defendants’ argument.  Plaintiff served discovery on March 18, 2022, moved to compel further on June 2, 2022.  As a result of the case being transferred and stayed pending the arbitration proceeding, the motion to compel further was finally heard on April 4, 2023, and Matrix was ordered to provide answers without objections and pay sanctions.  When it failed to do so, Plaintiff moved for issue and terminating sanctions on July 27, 2023.  On August 22, the Court found that Matrix had abused the discovery process, again ordered Matrix to respond, and issued additional monetary sanctions.  Plaintiff also timely served deposition notices, but Defendants all failed to appear.  Thus, Plaintiff has been sufficiently diligent, but Defendants have failed to comply with timely discovery requests and court orders related to discovery.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiff did not try to meet and confer, set an IDC, or move to compel further responses after Defendants served their supplemental responses and production on September 8.  This argument bolsters the reasons why continuing the discovery cut-off dates is warranted; it will enable the parties to follow up regarding the September 8 supplemental discovery responses.

 

            Therefore, the Court finds that justice will be best served by conforming the trial and discovery cut-off dates in this matter to the dates that were recently re-set by stipulation of the parties in the -3580 matter.

 

Defendants’ Request for Sanctions

 

Because the Court finds Plaintiff’s motion meritorious, Plaintiff acted with substantial justification in bringing it, and the Court therefore denies Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Because this case and United Clinical Research, Inc. et al. v. Michael Landver et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2021, 21STCV43580) concern the same factual and legal issues, consolidation will permit the trier of fact to efficiently consider the overlapping issues, while avoiding duplicative litigation efforts and inconsistent rulings.

 

Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to consolidate Michael Landver et al. v. Matrix Clinical Research, Inc. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2021, No. 21STCV39105) with United Clinical Research, Inc. et al. v. Michael Landver et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2021, 21STCV43580). 

 

The Court further orders that the cases are consolidated for all purposes and Michael Landver et al. v. Matrix Clinical Research, Inc. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2021, No. 21STCV39105) is designated as the lead case per California Rules of Court, rule 3.350(b).  As such, the Court orders the following in the consolidated action:

 

·         Non-Jury Trial is set on March 25, 2024 at 10:00 A.M.

·         Final Status Conference is set for March 8, 2024 at 9:30 A.M.

·         Status Conference re Discovery, ADR and Motion Practice is set for December 19, 2023 at 8:30 A.M.  (See Minute Order of September 15, 2023 entered in 21STCV43580.)  Parties shall adhere to said Minute Order. 

·         All discovery and pre-trial motion cut off dates shall be associated with the trial date of March 25, 2024,

·         Per the discovery act, the court orders the parties to meet and confer forthwith to schedule and complete all non-expert discovery and to prepare for the completion of expert discovery to obviate the need for a continuance of the trial. 

·         Parties shall adhere to the trial preparation order issued on August 22, 2023 in 21STCV39105.

 

The Court further orders that all hearings and proceedings in United Clinical Research, Inc. et al. v. Michael Landver et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2021, 21STCV43580) are vacated.

 

Further, the Court denies Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions.

 

            Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling to all parties who have appeared in the consolidated cases and file proofs of service regarding the same.

 TENTATIVE RULING - NO. 2



 





























DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

October 11, 2023

CASE NUMBER

21STCV39105

MOTION

Terminating, Issue, and Monetary Sanctions

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Michael Landver

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Matrix Clinical Research, Inc.


 



MOTION



 



Plaintiff Michael Landver (“Plaintiff” or “Landver”) moves for terminating,
issue, and monetary sanctions for discovery abuse and willful failure to comply
with the Court’s discovery orders.  Defendant
Matrix Clinical, Inc. (“Defendant” or “Matrix”) has filed an “Objection to
Motion for Terminating, Issue, and Monetary Sanctions for Discovery Abuse and
Willful Failure to Comply with Court Orders” and seeks monetary sanctions
against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel. 



 



ANALYSIS



 



When a party misuses the
discovery process by disobeying a court order to provide discovery, the court
in its discretion may impose a terminating sanction by striking a party’s
pleading or dismissing the action of the party. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010,
subd. (g), 2023.030, subds. (d)(1) & (d)(3); 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300,
subd. (c).)



 



California discovery law
authorizes a range of penalties for a party's refusal to obey a discovery
order, including monetary sanctions, evidentiary sanctions, issue sanctions,
and terminating sanctions. A court has broad discretion in selecting the appropriate
penalty, . . . . Despite this broad discretion, the courts have long recognized
that the terminating sanction is a drastic penalty and should be used
sparingly. A trial court must be cautious when imposing a terminating sanction
because the sanction eliminates a party's fundamental right to a trial, thus
implicating due process rights. The trial court should select a sanction that
is tailored to the harm caused by the withheld discovery. Sanctions should be
appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to
protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.



 



(Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract
Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604 [cleaned up].)
Equally important, “a terminating sanction issued solely because of a failure
to pay a monetary discovery sanction is never justified.” (Newland v.
Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615.)



 



            Misuses of the Discovery Process



 



            On
April 4, 2023, the Court ordered Defendant Matrix to respond to Form
Interrogatories (“FROG”), Special Interrogatories (“SROG”), and Requests for
Production (“RFP”) without objection within thirty days, and awarded sanctions
in the amount of $4500.  (April 4, 2023
Minute Order.)  On July 20, 2023, Matrix
served supplemental discovery responses, which simply reiterated its prior
objections.  As a result, on August 22,
2023, the Court ordered Matrix to serve its discovery responses without
objection and produce all responsive documents by September 8, 2023, and
awarded an additional $2,490 in sanctions. 
(August 22, 2023 Minute Order.) 
In its order, the Court cautioned that Matrix’s “continued willful
failure” to provide “code-compliant responses to discovery” could “result in
monetary, issue, evidentiary, or terminating sanctions in the future.”



 



            On
September 8, 2023, Matrix served further supplemental responses to the FROG,
SROG, and RFP.  (Exhs. “S” through “U” to
the Baranov Decl.)  Matrix did not reassert
the same lengthy objections it had previously asserted.  However, most of Matrix’s substantive
responses appear to misconstrue the requests as seeking documents and
information as they existed in 2017, as opposed to all relevant documents and
information “from January 1, 2017 until the present” as requested.  Thus, Matrix has provided very few
substantive answers, answering only general questions about the corporation and
naming the four other defendants as Matrix’s employees and officers, etc.  With regard to the RFP, Matrix has indicated
that no documents existed in 2017.  The
only documents Matrix produced were records from the related lawsuits and
arbitration, and documents that Plaintiff had produced to Matrix in connection
with those actions.  (Baranov Decl. ¶
20.)  In response to Special
Interrogatory Number 13 and Request for Production Number 18, Matrix asserted a
new objection, that Plaintiff did not obtain an order pursuant to Code of Civil
Procedure section 3295.



 



            On
July 29, 2023, Plaintiff noticed the four individual defendants’ depositions
for August 14-17, 2023, but none of them appeared for deposition.  (Baranov Decl. ¶ 21.)  Plaintiff scheduled an IDC for September 28,
2023, which was the Court’s first available date.   Defendants filed an objection and requested to
take the IDC off calendar, since September 28, 2023 was after the discovery
cut-off date.  (Baranov Decl. ¶ 21 and
Ex. V thereto.)  With respect to the
depositions, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is
premature as Plaintiff has yet to seek to compel the depositions of the
individual defendants. 



 



            Plaintiff
also served a deposition subpoena on Matrix’s accountant, David Gashodian.  Defendants moved to quash the subpoena, and
noticed the motion for September 13, 2023. 
After Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion to quash, Defendants
took the motion to quash off calendar, apparently believing the discovery
cut-off date had already passed and the motion to quash was no longer
necessary.  (Baranov Decl. ¶ 22.)



 



The crux of the dispute about the discovery cut-off date is that the
case was originally filed in Stanley Mosk courthouse.  Trial was originally set for October 2,
2023.  In early October 2022, the case
was related with two other cases, and transferred to Department 207 of the
Beverly Hills courthouse.  Upon transfer,
the Court moved up the trial date to September 18, 2023, but did not move up
the attendant discovery cut-off deadlines. 



 



On August 7, 2023, at an ex parte hearing, the Court continued the FSC
and Trial and ordered “Discovery cut-off is based upon the current trial date
of September 18, 2023.”  (August 7, 2023
Minute Order.)  Thus, because of the
series of transfers, the stay, and the orders of three different judges
regarding trial and discovery cut-off dates, there was some confusion about the
effective discovery cut-off date.



 



Matrix “objects” to the motion
for terminating, issue, and monetary sanctions on the basis that it was filed
after September 5, 2023, which is the discovery cut-off date based upon the
trial date of September 18, 2023, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020,
subdivision  (a).  Matrix has not otherwise filed an opposition
or raised any other arguments.



 



            The
Court finds that Matrix’s September 8 discovery responses, which do not
substantively respond to the majority of the requests, on the basis of an
overly narrow interpretation, limiting the responses to January 1, 2017, which
is not supported by the language of the requests themselves, and Matrix’s
September 8 production, consisting only of documents filed (or produced by
Plaintiff) in connection with the related cases and arbitration, constitute
continuing misuses of the discovery process. Specifically, Matrix’s September 8
responses are evasive, include unmeritorious objections, and defy the Court’s
April 4 and August 22 orders.



 



            The
Court further finds that the individual defendants’ failure to appear for their
noticed depositions in August also constitutes a misuse of the discovery
process.



 



            The
Court further finds that Defendants’ withdrawal of their motion to quash the
deposition subpoena of David Gashodian after it was fully briefed and on the
eve of the hearing to be “employing a discovery method in a manner or to an
extent that causes unwarranted annoyance” as well as “undue burden and
expense.”  (Code Civ. Proc., section
2023.010, subd. (c).)  The more
appropriate course of action would be to raise the issue about the discovery
cut-off date in the briefing and/or at the hearing, and allow the Court to hear
and rule on the motion. 



 



            Terminating and Issue Sanctions



 



The Court of Appeal has noted that “terminating
sanctions are appropriate only if a party's failure to obey a court order
actually prejudiced the opposing party.”  (Moofly Productions, LLC v. Favila
(2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 1, 11.)  Moofly cites
to Morgan v. Ransom as support for this proposition.  In Morgan v. Ransom, the appellate
court held that the trial court erred because the interrogatories themselves
had not been presented to the trial court, and therefore, there had been no
analysis of whether the information sought was actually relevant to any of the
issues in the case.  (Morgan v. Ransom
(1979) 95 Cal.App.3d 664, 669-670.)  In
other words, to the extent the information sought was not relevant to the case,
the plaintiff’s failure to provide responses would not have prejudiced the
defendant.



 



Moreover, “A request for a sanction shall, in the
notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the
sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of
motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and
accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any
monetary sanction sought.”  (Code Civ.
Proc., § 2023.040; see generally Mattco Forge, Inc. v. Arthur Young &
Co. (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 1429 [appellate court addresses adequacy of
notice of monetary sanctions request].) 



 



            Here,
Plaintiff has provided copies of all relevant discovery requests and responses
served, as well as the relevant Court orders on discovery, and a declaration
describing the scope of the document production received.  Plaintiff does not argue in the Motion what
prejudice it has suffered as a result of Defendants’ continued discovery
abuses.  However, paragraph 11 of the
Baranov declaration indicates that “The discovery inquir[ing] into the
corporate structure of MATRIX, its officers, directors and shareholders, and
the clinical studies it is engaged in” are “key to LANDVER’s claims that MATRIX
stole UNITED’s business, customers and employees, and is nothing but its
successor-in-interest, surreptitiously organized by the Individual Defendants”
and “is necessary for LANDVER to prove his case against defendants and to
determine damages.”  (Baranov Decl. ¶
11.)



 



            Matrix
has responded substantively to Plaintiff’s interrogatories about Matrix’s
employees, officers, directors, and shareholders, naming only the individual
defendants.  However, Matrix has not
responded substantively to requests regarding the clinical studies it is
engaged in, or its finances. 



 



            Plaintiff
seeks an order striking Matrix’s answer to the complaint, including affirmative
damages, and finding:



 



a) That pursuant to Business and Professions Code § 17204, all
Defendants, their officers, directors, principals, assignees, successors,
agents, representatives, employees, subsidiaries, affiliates, and all persons,
corporations and other entities acting by, through, under, or on behalf of said
Defendants, or acting in concert or participation with them, be permanently
enjoined from directly or indirectly committing any violations of Business and
Professions Code § 17200 et seq., including, but not limited to, the violations
alleged in this complaint;



 



c) For an injunction enjoining Defendants from utilizing United
Clinical Research, Inc.’s (“United” or “UNITED”) trade secret information and
materials and from prohibiting them from accessing any of United’s computers
and accounts;



 



d) For a declaration that Matrix is a successor-in-interest of United
and that Plaintiff is a twenty five percent owner thereof;



 



(Notice of Motion and Motion
at p. 2.)



 



            Because Matrix has substantively responded to the
questions about its employees, officers, and directors, and because Plaintiff
has only demonstrated prejudice with respect to whether Matrix stole United
Clinical’s business and the extent of damages, Plaintiff’s sanction request
exceeds the scope of the prejudice Plaintiff has demonstrated.  Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiff’s
request for terminating and issue sanctions.



             



Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions



 



Having found Defendant has committed ongoing misuses of the discovery
process, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the
amount of $3035.00, representing 5 hours of attorney time to review Defendant’s
discovery responses, prepare the moving  and
attend the hearing on the instant motion at a rate of $595 per hour, plus the
$60 filing fee for the motion.



 



Defendants’ Request for Monetary Sanctions



 



Because the Court finds, in part, Plaintiff’s motion meritorious, Plaintiff
acted with substantial justification in bringing it.  Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’
request for monetary sanctions.



 



CONCLUSION AND ORDER



 



Having found Defendant to have committed several continuing misuses of
the discovery process, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary
sanctions.  The Court orders Defendant to
pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $3035.00 to Plaintiff by and through
counsel for Plaintiff, within 20 days of the date of this order.



 



Further, for the reasons stated above, the Court denies Plaintiff’s
request for terminating and issue sanctions. 



 



The Court denies Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions.



 



 



Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof
of service regarding the same.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

October 11, 2023

CASE NUMBER

23STCV00692

MOTIONS

Motion for an Order Discharging Stakeholder

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Beverly Hills Escrow, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Fred Behfarin

 

MOTION

 

Defendant Beverly Hills Escrow, Inc. (“BHE”) moves for an order permitting it to deposit the unrecorded grant deed and escrow funds (minus its attorneys’ fees incurred to date) with the clerk of the court and discharging it from the case.

 

Plaintiff Fred Behfarin (“Plaintiff”) opposes the motion.  BHE has replied.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 386.5 provides:

 

Where the only relief sought against one of the defendants is the payment of a stated amount of money alleged to be wrongfully withheld, such defendant may, upon affidavit that he is a mere stakeholder with no interest in the amount or any portion thereof and that conflicting demands have been made upon him for the amount by parties to the action, upon notice to such parties, apply to the court for an order discharging him from liability and dismissing him from the action on his depositing with the clerk of the court the amount in dispute and the court may, in its discretion, make such order.

 

            Code of Civil Procedure, section 386.6 provides:

 

A party to an action who follows the procedure set forth in Section 386 or 386.5 may insert in his motion, petition, complaint, or cross complaint a request for allowance of his costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred in such action. In ordering the discharge of such party, the court may, in its discretion, award such party his costs and reasonable attorney fees from the amount in dispute which has been deposited with the court. At the time of final judgment in the action the court may make such further provision for assumption of such costs and attorney fees by one or more of the adverse claimants as may appear proper.

 

            BHE is the escrow company selected to hold the escrow funds and deeds in connection with the real estate transaction between Plaintiff and Defendants DH Distribution (“DH”) and Sergey Menshikov (“Menshikov”).  (Levoy Decl. ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff, DH, and Menshikov entered into an Agreement and Escrow Instructions in connection with the transaction, which instructed BHE not to release funds held in escrow or record any deed held until it received mutual written instructions from all parties.  (Levoy Decl. ¶¶ 5-6.)  DH and Menshikov deposited with BHE a fully executed, but unrecorded grant deed, and DH and Menshikov deposited into escrow funds in the amount of $541,626.00.  (Levoy Decl. ¶¶ 7-8.)  BHE asserts it has no interest in the escrow funds, the grant deed, the subject property, or the outcome of the dispute in this litigation.  (Levoy Decl. ¶¶ 4, 12.) 

 

            BHE requests an order permitting it to deposit the escrow funds (minus its attorneys’ fees) and unrecorded grant deed with the clerk of the court, and for an order discharging it as a stakeholder.

 

            Plaintiff opposes BHE’s request on the following grounds: (1) BHE is a necessary party because if Plaintiff prevails, and BHE is ordered to record the deed, BHE will need to be a party; (2) Plaintiff signed the second set of escrow instructions under duress; and (3) the agreement indicates that DH is responsible for paying the escrow costs, so BHE’s attorney fees should not be deducted from the escrow funds. (See also Declaration of Fred Behfarin,¶¶ 5-9, Exhibits 1-2.) 

           

            With regard to the first two arguments, the parties dispute which set of escrow instructions controls.  Plaintiff contends that the second set of escrow instructions is invalid, as he allegedly signed it under duress.  As part of the lawsuit, Plaintiff seeks declaratory relief that the first set of escrow instructions controls, which would require BHE to deposit the funds with Plaintiff and record the grant deed.  (Second Amended Complaint ¶¶ 36, 95.) 

 

            In that scenario, BHE would be a necessary party to afford Plaintiff complete relief.  While the clerk of the court can accept deposits of money or property to relieve a stakeholder, it cannot step into BHE’s shoes with respect to the declaratory or injunctive relief Plaintiff seeks.

 

            Therefore, the Court denies BHE’s motion.[1]

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

BHE’s motion for an order permitting it to deposit with the clerk of the court the unrecorded grant deed and escrow funds, and discharging BHE as a party from the lawsuit is denied.

 

BHE shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service of such.
