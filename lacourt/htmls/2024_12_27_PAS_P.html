DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21BBCV00628    Hearing Date: December 27, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION – SET TWO AGAINST DEFENDANT CRISTINA MERCADO BE DEEMED ADMITTED and MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.          INTRODUCTION

On July 14, 2021, Plaintiff MIP, LLC (“Plaintiff” or “MIP”) filed an action against Defendants Monitor Polishing & Plating, Inc. (“MPP”), Miguel Mercado (“Miguel”), Cristina Mercado (“Cristina”), and Does 1 to 20 for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of guaranty, (3) open book account, (4) account stated, (5) trespass, and (6) nuisance. Plaintiff alleges that around September 2005, it entered into a written commercial lease agreement with Defendant MPP for property located at 8050 San Fernando Road, Sun Valley, CA 91352, with Defendants Miguel and Cristina Mercado as guarantors. Plaintiff alleges that MPP defaulted on rental payments due on June 08, 2020, and utilized hazardous substances on the premises in violation of the Lease; and that both Miguel and Cristina have also refused to pay the sum owed.

On November 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to deem Requests for Admissions (RFAs) - Set Two admitted against Defendant Cristina. No opposition to the motion has been filed.   

            II.        REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . ..”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).)  

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).) 

Here, Plaintiff served RFAs Set Two on Defendant Cristina Mercado on October 14, 2024, making Defendant’s responses due by November 15, 2024. (Meyers Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Defendant failed to provide timely responses, which necessitated Plaintiff filing this motion. As of the hearing date, Cristina has not served any responses to the RFAs, nor has Cristina opposed this motion. Accordingly, under CCP section 2033.280(c), the Court must deem the RFAs admitted.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to deem RFAs Set Two admitted.

Sanctions

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].) 

Here, Cristina’s failure to timely serve responses to RFAs by the November 15, 2024, deadline necessitated this motion. Plaintiff’s counsel declares that they expended one hour preparing the motion and incurred a filing fee of $60.00, for a total requested sanction of $450.00. (Meyers Decl., ¶¶ 4-5.)

Counsel’s hourly rate is $450, which the Court finds reasonable. However, the Court reduces the time spent on a motion of this nature to 0.5 hours at the $450 hourly rate, plus the $60 filing fee. Given that the motion is unopposed, the Court adjusts the sanctions accordingly.

The Court imposes monetary sanctions in the amount of $285.00 against Defendant Cristina Mercado, payable to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of this order.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in part awarding $285.00.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to deem the RFAs Set Two admitted, and awards Plaintiff $285.00 in monetary sanctions to be paid within 20 days of this order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT ROSEMEAD DEMURRER TO THE COMPLAINT WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND and ORDER RENDERING DEFENDANT STIMPERT & FORD LLP ANTI-SLAPP MOTION AS MOOT

I.          INTRODUCTION

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff Jose Edwardo Ornelas Gonzalez (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Rosemead Boulevard Properties, LLC (“Rosemead” or “Defendant”) and Stimpert & Ford, LLP (“Stimpert”), alleging damages for violation of the California Identity Theft Act (Civ. Code, § 1798.92 et seq.).

On May 2, 2024, Defendant Stimpert filed a special motion to strike pursuant to California’s anti-SLAPP statute (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16). On May 14, 2024, Defendant Rosemead filed a demurrer, arguing that Plaintiff’s claims are barred under Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30, the compulsory cross-complaint rule. On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the demurrer, and on December 19, 2024, Defendant Rosemead filed a reply.

The Court now considers both Defendant Rosemead’s demurrer and Defendant Stimpert’s anti-SLAPP motion.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, on April 23, 2024, Defendant’s counsel met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the demurrer. Despite their efforts, the parties were unable to resolve the issues. (Traver Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) Accordingly, the Court finds that the meet-and-confer requirement has been satisfied.

Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of the following documents filed with the Los Angeles Superior Court in Rosemead Boulevard Properties, LLC v. Jose Edwardo Ornelas Gonzales (Case No. 23STCV01945):

  1. Exhibit A: The Complaint filed on January 30, 2023.
  2. Exhibit B: The Answer filed on March 10, 2023.
  3. Exhibit C: The Request for Dismissal filed on November 13, 2023.
  4. Exhibit D: The Register of Actions.

Judicial notice of court records is proper under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d). As such, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice for Exhibits A, B, C and D.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

When a defendant has failed to allege a related cause of action in a cross-complaint in a prior action, the bar of the compulsory cross-complaint rule can be raised by demurrer in the subsequent action. See, e.g., AL Holding Co. v. O'Brien & Hicks, Inc. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 1310 (Pursuant to CCP § 426.30, defendant had demurred on the ground that the cause of action was barred because the plaintiff never raised it in a compulsory cross-complaint in a prior action instituted by defendant.).

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claims are barred under Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30 because Plaintiff failed to file a compulsory cross-complaint in the underlying action, Rosemead Boulevard Properties, LLC v. Jose Eduardo Ornelas Gonzales (Case No. 23STCV01945). Specifically, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s identity theft claims are inextricably linked to the original collection lawsuit, which sought to recover unpaid rent arising from the same lease transaction that Plaintiff now disputes. Defendant further asserts that under the compulsory cross-complaint rule, Plaintiff was required to assert any related claims in the prior action to avoid piecemeal litigation. (Demurrer, pp. 4–6.)

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff cannot maintain an action under Civil Code section 1798.93 because Defendant is not a “claimant” as defined by section 1798.92, subdivision (a). Defendant asserts that it dismissed the underlying collection lawsuit without prejudice on November 13, 2023, before Plaintiff filed the instant action. Accordingly, Defendant argues that it no longer has a present claim for money or property and thus does not qualify as a "claimant." (Demurrer, pp. 6-7)

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that his claims are not barred by the compulsory cross-complaint rule because the California Identity Theft Act ("CITA") requires written notice of identity theft under Civil Code section 1798.93, subdivision (c), and allows the claimant 30 days to investigate before a victim of identity theft can file an action. Plaintiff argues that his statutory notice, served on May 17, 2023, was sent after he filed his Answer in the prior action and that his claims did not accrue until Defendant failed to conduct the required investigation. Plaintiff further asserts that section 1798.93, subdivision (a), uses the permissive term “may” when referring to filing a cross-complaint, which Plaintiff interprets as optional rather than mandatory. (Opposition, pp. 4–7.)

Plaintiff also disputes Defendant’s contention that it is not a “claimant” under CITA. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s dismissal of the underlying lawsuit without prejudice does not extinguish its status as a claimant, as Defendant retains the right to refile the dismissed lawsuit (Opposition, p.7)

In reply, Defendant contends that Plaintiff misinterprets the timing requirements under CITA. Defendant asserts that the 30-day waiting period in Civil Code section 1798.93, subdivisions (c)(5) and (6), applies only to claims for damages and not to cross-complaints asserting identity theft. Defendant further argues that Plaintiff’s statutory notice timing does not relieve Plaintiff of the obligation to file a cross-complaint for claims arising from the same transaction or occurrence as the original collection lawsuit. Defendant cites Flickinger v. Swedlow Engineering Co. (1955) 45 Cal.2d 388, 393, to emphasize the prohibition against piecemeal litigation. (Reply, pp. 3–5.)

The central issue in this demurrer is whether Plaintiff’s claims under Civil Code section 1798.93 are barred by Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30, which mandates that a party assert any related cause of action in a cross-complaint in the original action or be precluded from bringing it in a subsequent lawsuit.

Under CCP section 426.30, a “related cause of action” is defined in CCP section 426.10, subdivision (c), as one that arises from the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the plaintiff’s claims. Courts interpret “transaction” broadly to encourage the resolution of all interrelated claims in a single action to avoid piecemeal litigation and conflicting judgments. (Ranchers Bank v. Pressman (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 612, 620; Manning v. Wymer (1969) 273 Cal.App.2d 519, 525.)

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s identity theft claims arise directly from the lease transaction and allegations of unpaid rent at the heart of Defendant’s prior collection lawsuit. Defendant further asserts that Plaintiff’s failure to file a cross-complaint in the original action precludes him from asserting these claims now, citing the compulsory cross-complaint rule under CCP section 426.30. Defendant also contends that Plaintiff’s argument about the permissive language in Civil Code section 1798.93 is misplaced, as the timing of Plaintiff’s statutory notice does not exempt him from the requirement to assert related claims in the original action.

Plaintiff, who represented himself pro per in the prior action, argues that Civil Code section 1798.93 permits, but does not require, filing a cross-complaint. Plaintiff asserts that his claims under the California Identity Theft Act accrued only after Defendant failed to investigate his statutory notice, which was sent after he filed his Answer. 

The Court acknowledges the challenges faced by litigants representing themselves. However, the rules governing compulsory cross-complaints apply equally to all parties, including those acting in pro per. The purpose of CCP section 426.30 is to ensure that all claims arising from the same transaction are resolved within a single action, thereby promoting judicial efficiency, and avoiding duplicative lawsuits.

In this case, Plaintiff’s identity theft claims under Civil Code section 1798.93 are logically interrelated with Defendant’s prior collection lawsuit. The facts giving rise to Plaintiff’s claims were known at the time of the original action, even if Plaintiff was unaware of his obligation to file a cross-complaint. Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff was required to assert these claims as a cross-complaint in the prior action. Filing them in a separate action is procedurally improper.

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend.

Motion to Strike – Anti-SLAPP

California’s anti-SLAPP statute states: “A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(1), italics added.) The purpose of the statute is to “protect defendants from meritless lawsuits that might chill the exercise of their rights to speak and petition on matters of public concern.” (Wilson v. Cable News Network, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 871, 883–884.)

This special motion to strike is made on the pleadings and affidavits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(2).) If the moving party prevails, he or she is entitled to recover attorney’s fees and costs. (Id., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).) But if the plaintiff prevails on the motion, the court shall award the plaintiff’s reasonable attorney’s fees and costs (subject to certain exceptions not relevant here). (Ibid.)

In light of the Court’s ruling sustaining Defendant Rosemead’s demurrer without leave to amend, the anti-SLAPP motion filed by Defendant Stimpert & Ford, LLP is rendered MOOT.

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to the Complaint in its entirety, without leave to amend.

Defendant Stimpert & Ford, LLP's anti-SLAPP motion is rendered MOOT.

Defendant Rosemead is to file a proposed judgment consistent with this ruling within 30 days.
