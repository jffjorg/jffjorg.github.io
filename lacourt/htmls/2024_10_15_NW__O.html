ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case arises out of a contract between Plaintiff National Oak Distributors, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) and J & S Auto Body Supply, Inc. (“Defendant”). Plaintiff alleges it and Defendant entered into an agreement “whereby Plaintiff would provide automotive related goods and for which Defendants promised to pay Plaintiff” but that Defendants have not paid the amount due under the agreement. (See Complaint, pg. 3, ¶ BC-2.)

Plaintiff now moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to its second cause of action for Common Counts.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed its Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract; and (2) Common Counts.

On October 24, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication (“Motion”).

Defendant did not file an opposition.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (See Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing such motions, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) Thus, summary judgment or summary adjudication is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c)[1]; Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.) 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate or establish an essential element. (See Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must be denied where the moving party’s evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition or where the opposition is weak. (See Leyva v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475; Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384 & 387.) When a plaintiff is the moving party, the plaintiff has a higher burden: it must “present evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not.” (See Aguilar, 25 Cal.4th at 845 (emphasis added).) “The burden of the moving party is to persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find.” (PP Mortgage, Ltd. v. Bizar (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 773, 776 (emphasis in original) (cleaned up).)

Once the moving party has met its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material fact exists to a cause of action or a defense thereto. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(o)(2).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Request for Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice of its Complaint and Defendant’s Answer.   

B.             Undisputed Material Facts

Between February 27, 1995, to November 1, 2022, Plaintiff provided automotive related goods to Defendant. (See Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) No. 3.) Despite Plaintiff’s demands for payment, Defendant refused to pay for all goods received and currently owes Plaintiff $96,527.54. (See UMF No. 4.) In its responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions (“RFAs”), Defendant admitted Plaintiff issued it $96,527.54 in credit; that within the last four years, it utilized the credit provided to it; and that within the last four years it failed to pay Plaintiff the $96,527.54 it owes. (See UMF Nos. 7-9.)  

C.             Plaintiff is Entitled to Summary Adjudication on Plaintiff’s Common Count Claim.

Plaintiff “respectfully requests that the Court grant summary judgment in favor of Plaintiff on Plaintiff’s Cause of Action for Common Counts. In the alternative, Plaintiff respectfully requests that Plaintiff’s Common Count Cause of Action against Defendant be summarily adjudicated.” (Motion, pg. 1.) Plaintiff also requests summary adjudication as to Defendant’s general denial in its Answer. (See id. at 6-7.)

As an initial matter, Plaintiff in its Complaint asserts breach of contract and common counts; however, Plaintiff’s only moves for summary judgement or summary adjudication on its common counts claim. (See id. at 1.) Because Plaintiff is not attempting to dispose of all its claims, Plaintiff is only requesting summary adjudication.[2] The Court also notes Plaintiff’s request for summary adjudication of Defendant’s general denial in its Answer is procedurally improper. “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(f)(1).) A general denial in an answer is not a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty and is therefore not the proper subject of a motion for summary adjudication.  

The Court now turns its attention to the merits of Plaintiff’s Motion. “A common count is not a specific cause of action, . . . rather, it is a simplified form of pleading normally used to aver the existence of various forms of monetary indebtedness, including that arising from an alleged duty to make restitution under an assumpsit theory.” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th379, 394 (internal citations omitted).) “The only essential allegations of a common count are (1) the statement of indebtedness in a certain sum, (2) the consideration, i.e., goods sold, work done, etc., and (3) nonpayment.” (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460 (internal quotations and citations omitted).)

Plaintiff has submitted evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not and has thus met its burden on summary adjudication. Plaintiff has submitted its statement of account which shows Defendant owes $96,527.54. (See Declaration of Alexander Hettena filed July 12, 2024 (“Hettena Decl.”), ¶ 2, Ex. A.) Plaintiff has also submitted its discovery requests and Defendant’s responses, whereby Defendant admits: (1) it is a corporation operating by the name J & S AUTO BODY SUPPLY, INC.; (2) that on February 27, 1995, it signed "Business Credit Application”; (3) that on February 27, 1995, it entered into a written contract with Plaintiff per the "Business Credit Application"; (4) that the attached marked Exhibit "A" is a true and correct copy of the "Business Credit Application"; (5) that per the terms of the "Business Credit Application" it manifested your assent to the term "'Buyer agrees to pay interest at the rate of 1 1/2% per month on the unpaid balance after 30 days, and seller costs of collection including reasonable attorney's fees"; (6) that the attached marked Exhibit "B" is a true and correct copy of the Statement of Account dated November 1, 2022, reflecting an outstanding balance of $96,527.54; (7) that it was issued $96,527.54 in credit provided by Plaintiff; (8) that within the last four years, it utilized the credit provided by Plaintiff; (9) that within the last four years, it failed to pay Plaintiff $96,527.54 due; (1) that it breached the terms of the "Business Credit Application" when it failed to pay Plaintiff $96,527.54; and (11) that it owes Plaintiff $96,527.54 for the credit provided by Plaintiff. (See Hettena Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. C, RFAs; ¶ 5, Ex. D, Defendant’s Reponses to RFAs.)

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff has met its burden on summary adjudication. Therefore, the burden shifts to Defendant to create a triable issue of material fact. Defendant did not submit an opposition. Therefore, Defendant has not met its burden.  

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to its claim for common counts.

ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTIONS TO COMPEL AUTONATION AND JAGUAR’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case arises out of Plaintiff Andi Yaco’s (“Plaintiff”) purchase and financing of a 2018 Range Rover (“Vehicle”) from Defendant Terry York Motor Cars Ltd dba Jaguar Land Rover Woodland Hills (“Jaguar”). (See First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶ 11.) Plaintiff alleges Jaguar and Defendants TD Bank, National Association [erroneously sued as TD Auto Finance], Amir Mansour, Erik Vander Meulen, and AutoNation (collectively, “Defendants”) demanded he obtain financing through Defendants despite Plaintiff obtaining financing on his own; as a result, Plaintiff alleges is being billed twice for the same loan. (See FAC, ¶¶ 14-18.)

On August 15, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s request for a continuance of the hearing on AutoNation’s Motion for Summary Judgment to allow Plaintiff to conduct additional discovery.

Plaintiff now seeks to compel Jaguar and AutoNation’s further responses to his requests for production of documents, set one (“RFPS”).

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Complaint, alleging (1) Intentional Misrepresentation; (2) Negligent Misrepresentation; (3) Violation of Consumers Legal Remedies Act – Injunctive Relief; and (4) Unfair Competition (Bus. & Prof. Code Sections 17200).

On October 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed a FAC, alleging (1) Intentional Misrepresentation; (2) Negligent Misrepresentation; (3) Violation of Consumers Legal Remedies Act – Injunctive Relief; and (4) Unfair Competition (Bus. & Prof. Code Sections 17200).

On January 8, 2024, Defendant TD Bank, National Association filed its Answer.

On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff served his FAC on AutoNation.

On March 14, 2024, Defendant Amir Mansour filed his Answer.

On May 8, 2024, Jaguar filed its Answer.

On August 15, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s request for a continuance of the hearing on AutoNation’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel AutoNation’s Further Responses to his RFPs (“AutoNation Motion”).

On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Jaguar’s Further Responses to his RFPs (“Jaguar Motion”).

On October 3, 2024, AutoNation filed its Opposition.

On October 3, 2024, Jaguar filed its Opposition.

On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Replies.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

In responding to a request for production of documents, a party must provide one of the following: (1) a legally adequate statement of compliance; (2) a statement of inability to comply; or (3) an objection to the particular demand. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210(a).)[1] If any objections are made, they must: (1) “[i]dentify with particularity any document … falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made”; and (2) “[s]et forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground for, the objection.”¿(Id. at § 2031.240(b).) A statement that the responding party is unable to comply with a particular demand “shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand”; “shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party”;  and “shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Id. at § 2031.230.) 

A party who believes a responding party has served (1) an incomplete statement of compliance, (2) an inadequate, incomplete, or evasive statement of compliance, or (3) an unmeritorious objection can move for an order compelling further responses but must “set forth specific acts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (See id. at §§ 2031.310(a)-(b)(1).

///

IV.          MEET AND CONFER

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b)(1), and 2033.290(b)(1).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Id. at § 2016.040.) However, a discovery motion should not be denied automatically based upon the reason that the moving parties failed to meet and confer in good faith. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434.)

Plaintiff provides a meet and confer declaration attesting to the parties meet and confer efforts, which consists of an exchange of meet and confer letters. (See, e.g., Jaguar Motion Declaration of Michael Debenon filed September 6, 2024 (“Debenon Decl.”), ¶¶ 10-14, Exs. D-H.)

The Court finds Plaintiff has fulfilled his meet and confer requirements and thus turns its attention to the merits of the motions.

V.             ANALYSIS

There are two discovery motions presently before this Court. The Court will address AutoNation and Jaguar’s Motions together.

A.             The Court Denies Plaintiff’s Motions.

1.               Plaintiff’s Motions Are Timely.

Notice of a motion to compel further responses must be given within 45 days of service of verified responses or supplemental responses, or on or before any specific later date to which the parties have agreed in writing. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.230(c), 2030.300(c), 2033.290(c).) When responses are served by mail, that time is extended by five days. (See id. at 1013(a).) If such notice is not given timely, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (See id.)¿¿ ¿¿

Here, AutoNation and Jaguar served supplemental responses on August 22, 2024, and Plaintiff filed the instant motions on September 6, 2024, 15 days later and well within the time to serve notice of a motion to compel further responses. (See, e.g., Debenon Decl., ¶¶ 11-12, Ex. E; Jaguar Motion.) Therefore, the motions are timely.

2.               RFPs

Plaintiff moves to compel AutoNation and Jaguar’s further responses to his RFPs Nos. 9-14 and Nos. 3-7, 9-14, respectively. (See AutoNation’s Separate Statement (“SS AutoNation”); Jaguar’s Separate Statement (“SS Jaguar”).)

Plaintiff’s AutoNation RFPs request all communications between AutoNation and any customer, vendor, director, officer, employee, and independent contractor of Jaguar. (See SS AutoNation RFPs Nos. 9-14.)

Plaintiff’s Jaguar RFPs request all advertising materials, employee handbooks, training materials, and letterhead or business cards provided to Jaguar by AutoNation (RFPs Nos. 3-4, 6, 9) and all employee handbooks and training materials provided by Jaguar to its own directors, officer, or employees (RFPs Nos. 5, 7). (See SS Jaguar RFPs Nos. 3-7, 9.) Like Plaintiff’s AutoNation RFPs, Plaintiff’s Jaguar RFPs also request all communications between AutoNation and any customer, vendor, director, officer, and employee of Jaguar. (See id. at RFPs Nos. 10-14.)

To each, AutoNation and Jaguar responded by objecting that each request is overly broad, irrelevant, and/or seeks trade secrets. (See, e.g., SS AutoNation RFP No. 9; SS Jaguar RFP No. 3.) After their objections, AutoNation and Jaguar also provided a brief synopsis of AutoNation and Jaguar’s relationship to each other and general corporate structure. (See id.)

Plaintiff is not entitled to further responses from AutoNation or Jaguar. As an initial matter, the Court overrules AutoNation’s and Jaguar’s objections on the grounds that the requests seek irrelevant information or information protected by trade secrets. However, the Court sustains, AutoNation’s and Jaguar’s objections that each request is overbroad. (See, e.g., SS AutoNation RFP No. 9.) Plaintiff made no attempt to reasonably tailor his requests, nor does Plaintiff’s argument in support of his motions provide any additional information about what he is seeking such that the Court could limit his requests to a narrower universe of documents. (See id.) The Court declines to tailor Plaintiff’s requests without such guidance and thus finds it appropriate to sustain AutoNation and Jaguar’s objection as to Plaintiff’s overly broad requests.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s AutoNation Motion and Jaguar Motion.

VI.          CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel AutoNation’s Further Responses to his RFPs and his Motion to Compel Jaguar’s Further Responses to his RFPs.

Finally, the Court appreciates that counsel were apparently so professional throughout their interactions on the instant Motions that Plaintiff’s counsel felt it appropriate to forego seeking sanctions. That this sort of professionalism is the exception rather than the rule is a sad reflection on the state of our profession but the Court is hopeful others will follow counsels’ examples.
