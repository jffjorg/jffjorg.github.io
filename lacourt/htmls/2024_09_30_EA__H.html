DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20PSCV00884    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: H

City of El Monte v. Chen, et al., Case No. 20PSCV00884

ORDER ON DEFAULT JUDGMENT APPLICATION

Plaintiff City of El Monte’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Background

Plaintiff City of El Monte (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows: 

Defendants Zhao Gu Chen (“Gu Chen”), Ping Chen (“P. Chen”) and James Wong (“Wong”) (collectively, “Defendants”) own, inhabit, and/or operate the property located at 4491 Shasta Lane in El Monte (the “subject property”). On December 20, 2016, members of the El Monte Police Department (“Police”) executed a search warrant at the subject property. While executing the warrant, the Police observed that the house had been converted into marijuana cultivation facilities. The Police observed several industrial lights, electrical modifications, and air filtration systems commonly used for large-scale indoor marijuana cultivation. The Police seized numerous items which had been used to cultivate, manufacture, store, and/or sell marijuana at the subject property in violation of state and municipal law. 

On December 22, 2020, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Gu Chen, P. Chen, Wong, and Does 1-100 for:

1.       Violation of Unfair Competition Law (Business & Professions Code §§ 17200 et seq.); and

2.       Violation of MAUCRSA (Business & Professions Code §§ 26000 et seq.). 

On June 28, 2021, the Clerk entered default against Gu Chen.

On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a “Notice of Settlement as Between Plaintiff City of El Monte and Defendants Ping Chen and James Wong.” 

On June 21, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed P. Chen and Wong, with prejudice. 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment. 

 

Discussion 

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defects are noted:

1.       Plaintiff seeks, inter alia, to permanently restrain and enjoin Gu Chen “from unlawfully storing, keeping, cultivating, or giving away marijuana or any other controlled substances on the Property or anywhere else in the City.” (See Proposed Judgment, ¶ 1). The above requested injunctive relief does not match the requested injunctive relief sought in Plaintiff’s complaint. (See Complaint, 7:19-22 and 8:9-12 [“That Defendants Gu Chen, Chen, Wong, Does 1 through 100, and any agents, officers, employees, and anyone acting on their behalf be perpetually enjoined from transferring, conveying, or encumbering any portion of the Property for consideration without first obtaining the Court’s prior approval”].)

2.       Plaintiff is directed to brief the issue of the statute of limitations.

3.       Plaintiff’s Judicial Council Form CIV-100 identifies the “[d]emand of complaint” as $2,877,420.00. This amount, however, is not set forth anywhere in Plaintiff’s complaint.

Plaintiff, in Paragraph 2 of his Prayer for Relief on the first cause of action, seeks civil penalties of $2,235,000.00. (See Complaint, 7:15-18 [“That Defendants each individually be assessed a civil penalty of two thousand five hundred dollars ($2,500.00) per day, per violation for each unlawful practice, for a total at least two million, two hundred and thirty-five thousand dollars ($2,235,000.00) per Defendant, pursuant to Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq.”].) 

Plaintiff seeks an additional $6,510.00 in Paragraph 7 of his Prayer for Relief on the second cause of action. (Id., 8:4-8 [“That Defendants each individually be assessed a civil penalty of up to three times the amount of the license fee for each unlawful violation, for a total of at least six thousand five hundred and ten dollars ($6,510.00) pursuant to Business and Professions Code section 26000 et seq. The appropriate license application fee for the square footage of the Property is $2,170.00. (See “Specialty Indoor/Type 1A, CA Code of Regulations, Ch. 3, Division 8, Ch. 1, § 8101(f).)”].)

These amounts together equal $2,241,510.00. No other monetary figures are set forth in the complaint. “The relief granted to the plaintiff, if there is no answer, cannot exceed that demanded in the complaint, in the statement required by Section 425.11, or in the statement provided by for Section 425.115. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 580, subd. (a).) “[I]n all default judgments the demand sets a ceiling on recovery.” (Greenup v. Rodman (1986) 42 Cal.3d 822, 825.) Plaintiff now appears to seek $2,877,420.00 in penalties under Business and Professions Code section 26038(a) when it only requested $6,510.00 in such penalties in its complaint.

4.       Plaintiff has not completed the “Declaration of nonmilitary status” on Judicial Council Form CIV-100.

5.       Plaintiff seeks $1,737.00 in “[l]aw [e]nforcement [c]osts.” Plaintiff is directed to provide the Court with a further explanation of these costs. 

6.       Paragraph 24 of Plaintiff’s factual summary and Paragraph 24 of Detective Jacob Burse’s declaration both reference an individual by the name of “Pham.” It is unclear to the Court whether or not these references are typographical errors. Plaintiff is directed to provide clarification in this regard.

Accordingly, the Court denies the default judgment application without prejudice. Plaintiff is directed to submit a revised default judgment packet that addresses the aforementioned issues.

Case Number: 22PSCV01000    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: H

Aknine Group, et al. v. Wang, et al., Case No. 22PSCV01000

 

ORDER ON MOTIONS FOR RECONSIDERATION

1.       Plaintiff Aknine Group Inc.’s Motion for Reconsideration Re: August 1, 2024 Ruling on Plaintiffs’ Accounting of the Funds Obtained from the Bank Accounts Subject to the Preliminary Injunction is DENIED. 

2.       Plaintiff Key Ecommerce Inc.’s Motion for Reconsideration Re: August 1, 2024 Ruling on Plaintiffs’ Accounting of the Funds Obtained from the Bank Accounts Subject to the Preliminary Injunction is DENIED.

3.       Plaintiff Yizhong Chen’s Motion for Reconsideration Re: August 1, 2024 Ruling on Plaintiffs’ Accounting of the Funds Obtained from the Bank Accounts Subject to the Preliminary Injunction is DENIED.

 

I.        Background

Plaintiffs Aknine Group Inc. (“Aknine”), Key Ecommerce Inc. (“KEI”), and Yizhong Chen (“Chen”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege that their former employee, Zhongliang Wang (“Wang”), embezzled at least $4.5 million from Plaintiffs’ various business bank accounts between August 26, 2022 and September 9, 2022 and transferred the funds to various bank accounts solely controlled and managed by him, including those opened in the names of AZ Express Group Inc. (“AZ Express”), KB Int’l Inc. (“KB Int’l”), Luckie Trading LLC (“Luckie Trading”), and EZ Supply Group LLC (“EZ Supply”) (collectively, “Defendants”). 

On September 12, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action against Wang, AZ Express, KB Int’l, Luckie Trading, EZ Supply, and Does 1-50 for (1) breach of contract, (2) conversion, (3) breach of fiduciary duty, (4) theft and misappropriation under California Penal Code § 496(c); and (5) unjust enrichment. 

On May 2, 2023, pursuant to the parties’ stipulation, the Court (the Honorable Thomas Falls) issued an order granting Plaintiff’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction. The Court ordered Defendants to return to Plaintiffs all proceeds, monies, funding, cash, and savings obtained by Defendants. The Court ordered Plaintiffs to maintain and provide a detailed accounting of the receipt and usage of any funding returned to Defendants and ordered Plaintiffs to use the funds in the normal course of business. 

On May 7, 2024, the Court (the Honorable Bryant Yang) granted Defendant Wang’s Motion to Vacate and Set Aside the May 2, 2023 Order because the motion was not opposed and because Plaintiffs had failed to comply with the Court’s order to provide a detailed accounting of the funds. The Court ordered Plaintiffs to transfer the funds back into a trust account. 

On June 21, 2024, Wang filed a Second Amended Cross-Complaint asserting causes of action against Aknine, KEI, Ainien, Inc. (“Ainien”), Chen and Roes 1-50 for (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) breach of fiduciary duty, (4) fraud, (5) promissory estoppel, (6) negligent misrepresentation, (7) accounting, (8) failure to pay minimum and overtime wages, (9) failure to provide accurate wage statement, (10) failure to provide rest and meal breaks, (11) failure to keep accurate records, and (12) failure to comply with Labor Code § 226 demand. 

On July 31, and August 1, 2024, Judge Yang held an evidentiary hearing to obtain an accounting of the funds and to determine how the funds were used by Plaintiffs. Following the hearing, the Court found that Plaintiffs had not established by a preponderance of the evidence that the funds transferred by Defendants to Plaintiffs were used in the normal course of business, as required by the May 2, 2023 Order. Accordingly, on August 1, 2024, the Court ordered Plaintiffs to post bond in the amount of $2,956,675.49 or transfer said amount into the trust account by September 30, 2024. 

A Case Management Conference and Order to Show Cause Re: Compliance of the August 1, 2024 Order of Posting Bond or Transferring Funds Into Trust Account are set for October 10, 2024.

Aknin, KEI, and Chen have each moved for reconsideration of Judge Yang’s August 1, 2024 ruling regarding Plaintiffs’ accounting of the money obtained from the bank accounts subject to the preliminary injunction; in the alternative, they request that the Court order a new evidentiary hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 657(4). For the reasons stated herein, the motions are denied.

 

II.      Legal Standard

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)

“A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on ex parte motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (b).) 

“The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 213.)

“The verdict may be vacated and any other decision may be modified or vacated, in whole or in part, and a new or further trial granted on all or part of the issues, on the application of the party aggrieved, for any of the following causes, materially affecting the substantial rights of such party: . . . 4. Newly discovered evidence, material for the party making the application, which he could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered and produced at the trial.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 657(4).)

 

III.     Discussion 

Aknine, KEI and Chen (together, “Plaintiffs”) each move the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, for reconsideration of its August 1, 2024 ruling regarding Plaintiffs’ accounting of the money obtained from the bank accounts subject to the preliminary injunction; in the alternative, they request that the Court order a new evidentiary hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 657(4). 

The Court will address the three motions together, as they appear to be identical in all respects. 

A.       Jurisdiction 

The Court determines that it has jurisdiction to consider a motion for reconsideration of the evidentiary hearing, notwithstanding the fact that the August 1, 2024 ruling was made by Judge Yang. Plaintiffs’ motions are predicated on Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (b), which does not require that the same judge hear a renewal motion. (See Deauville Restaurant, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 843, 849-851). 

Moreover, even if the motions were deemed motions for reconsideration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a), Judge Yang is “unavailable” following his acceptance of the peremptory challenge filed by Cross-Defendant Ne Miracle Inc. on August 8, 2024. (See Geddes v. Superior Court (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 417, 425-426 [“Where a judge has been disqualified, the newly-assigned judge may review the ruling of the disqualified judge because the disqualified judge, having no authority to rule, is ‘unavailable.’ Any comity concern that ordinarily would preclude the second judgment from reviewing the first judge’s ruling would not prevent the court from making rulings necessary to resolve the matter.”].) 

Accordingly, the Court will address the merits of the motions. 

B.       Merits 

The Court has familiarized itself with the instant dispute. By way of background, Judge Yang granted Wang’s Motion to Vacate and Set Aside Order Granting Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preliminary Injunction and the Order re: Defendants’ Ex Parte Application Modifying the Temporary Restraining Order and Writ of Attachment to Prohibit Plaintiffs from Using or Moving Funds Obtained by Plaintiffs and Transferring All Funds Received by Plaintiffs into a Trust Account on March 7, 2024. 

On March 11, 2024, Judge Yang issued an Order to Show Cause Re: Why the Court’s March 7, 2024 Order Regarding the Trust Account Should Not Be Modified (“OSC”) for March 28, 2024. 

On March 28, 2024, the hearing on the OSC was held; at that time, in light of Plaintiffs’ counsel’s representation that funds had already been used in accordance with Judge Falls’ May 2, 2023 order granting Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preliminary Injunction, Judge Yang ordered Plaintiffs to file an accounting of all funds used or transferred pursuant to the previously entered Stipulated Order Granting Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preliminary Injunction, stayed the March 7, 2024 order, and scheduled an evidentiary hearing for June 27, 2024. 

On June 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an “Accounting of the Funds Obtained from the Bank Accounts Subject to the Preliminary Injunction,” with a supporting declaration from Plaintiff Chen; Wang filed a response thereto on June 26, 2024. 

The matter proceeded to an evidentiary hearing on June 27, 2024, but the hearing had to be continued to July 31, 2024, as Plaintiffs sought to introduce exhibits that defense counsel had not reviewed. The Court continued the hearing to July 31, 2024 and ordered the parties to exchange all exhibits by no later than July 10, 2024 and to file their exhibits lists with the Court by July 24, 2024. 

On July 24, 2024, the parties filed their exhibit lists. On July 31, 2024, Judge Yang held the evidentiary hearing, orally pronounced his tentative findings and orders, and continued the hearing to August 1, 2024. On August 1, 2024, the Court issued its final order and ruling regarding the evidentiary hearing, finding that Plaintiffs had not established by a preponderance of the evidence that the funds transferred by Defendants back to Plaintiffs were used in the normal course of business, as required by Judge Falls’ May 2, 2023 order.

Plaintiffs first contend that Exhibits 9-12 and “JD Logistics USA’s additional information” and the issue of hardship constitute “new or different facts, circumstances or law that was not known or could not have been presented to the Court at the time of the original hearing.” (Motion, 2:7-10.) Plaintiffs claim that “WANG raised the fake sale argument to rebut Plaintiffs’ accounting report and Plaintiffs’ prima facie case presented in the hearing” and that Judge Yang sustained Wang’s counsel’s objections to Plaintiffs’ attempt to introduce Exhibits 9-12 into evidence even after Plaintiffs’ counsel explained that “she did not know that Defendant WANG was planning to use the fake sale argument until the time she received Defendant WANG’s exhibits.” (Id., 4:27-5:1 and 5:3-10.)

Exhibits 9-12 do not constitute new or different facts, circumstances, or law. The foregoing, in fact, reflects that Plaintiffs’ counsel sought to introduce Exhibits 9-12 into evidence at the time of the hearing but was precluded from doing do by Judge Yang’s rulings sustaining Wang’s counsel’s objections. The Court declines to “second guess” Judge Yang’s evidentiary rulings.[FN1]

Further, the issue of hardship and “JD Logistics USA’s additional information” do not constitute new or different facts, circumstances, or law. There is no reason that Plaintiffs could not, with reasonable diligence, have obtained the information regarding JD Logistics USA identified in Paragraph 9 of Helen Quan’s declaration before the evidentiary hearing. Wang, moreover, has provided the Court with excerpts from the August 1, 2024 hearing transcript, which reveal that Plaintiffs did present information regarding JD Logistics at the hearing, but that Judge Yang found Plaintiffs’ argument “speculative” and unsupported by evidence or testimony. (York Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A, 3:17-4:5.) These excerpts also reflect that Plaintiffs did argue the issue of hardship. (Id., 10:26-12:26.)

Next, Plaintiffs take issue with the evidence presented at the hearing and the hearing procedure, claiming, inter alia, that there was no substantial evidence that Wang had refuted Plaintiffs’ prima facie case and that Plaintiffs were denied their due process right to a fair hearing. (Motion, 3:13-15.) Plaintiffs’ dissatisfaction with Judge Yang’s ruling does not constitute “new or different facts, circumstances or law.” The Court will not reweigh the evidence and “second guess” Judge Yang’s decision.

The Court declines to consider Plaintiffs’ equipment (projector) failure argument, raised for the first time in reply. “Points raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument.” (American Drug Stores, Inc. v. Stroh (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1446, 1453.)

Finally, the Court declines Plaintiffs’ request to consider the instant motions as a motion for new trial. Plaintiffs filed a separate motion for new trial, which was denied by the Court on September 19, 2024.

The motions are denied.


[FN1] As an aside, the Court disagrees that Plaintiffs were unaware until the time they received Wang’s exhibits that Wang was going to assert a “fake sale” argument. As Wang points out, Wang’s Second Amended Cros Complaint (SACC) filed June 21, 2024 (i.e., approximately three weeks before the parties were required to exchange exhibits for the July 31, 2024 evidentiary hearing) alleged there were “fake sales” and money being moved around. (See SACC, ¶¶ 19-23.) Additionally, the Court notes that “Defendants’ Response to Plaintiffs’ Accounting of the Funds Obtained from the Bank Accounts Subject to the Preliminary Injunction” filed June 26, 2024 also raised the “fake sales” argument.

Case Number: 23PSCV01989    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: H

Sam v. The Board of Trustees of the California State University on Behalf of the California State Polytechnic Pomona, Case No. 23PSCV01989

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

Defendant Board of Trustees of the California State University’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED. The Court will hear from Plaintiff’s counsel as to whether leave to amend is requested and as to which cause(s) of action, and if so, will require an offer of proof.


I.        Background 

Plaintiff Isabel Sam (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

Plaintiff has been employed by Board of Trustees of the California State University (erroneously sued as “The Board of Trustees of the California State University on Behalf of the California State Polytechnic Pomona”) (“Defendant”) as a Communication Analyst since March 25, 1999. Plaintiff has been diagnosed with anxiety, thyroid disease, GERD, head, hand, and full body tremors, and other medical conditions. Since March 2019, a lead staff member at Plaintiff’s work has intimidated, stalked, and harassed Plaintiff and mimicked Plaintiff’s head and body tremors. Plaintiff requested the use of an unoccupied private office so that her tremors would not occur in plain view of the other employees and to shield her from the aforementioned lead staff member. Plaintiff also requested that she be allowed to continue to work remotely, as she did during the pandemic. Plaintiff’s requests were denied. Defendant’s Office of Compliance and Equity conducted an investigation of Plaintiff’s complaints regarding the lead staff member, which first resulted in a “no contact” directive issued to the lead staff member and later, after Plaintiff complained that lead staff member’s conduct was continuing, in a “mutual no contact directive.” Plaintiff has, inter alia, been subjected to disparate treatment regarding time off and has been denied a promotion. 

On April 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC” or the “Operative Complaint”), asserting causes of action against Defendant and Does 1-25 for: (1) discrimination based on medical condition under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”) (Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12900 et seq.); (2) harassment due to medical condition under FEHA (Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12900 et seq.); (3) retaliation for complaining of discrimination and harassment in violation of FEHA (Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12900 et seq.); (4) hostile work environment in violation of FEHA (Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12900 et seq.); (5) retaliation in violation of Labor Code § 6310; (6) failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation in violation of FEHA (Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12900 et seq.); and (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress. 

A Case Management Conference is set for September 30, 2024. Pending before the Court is Defendant’s demurrer to all seven causes of action in the First Amended Complaint. 

II.      Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

III.     Discussion

Defendant demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10, subdivision (e), to the first through seventh causes of action in the Operative Complaint, on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action.

A.       Citation to Judicial Council of California Civil Jury Instructions (“CACI”)

As an initial matter, much of Defendant’s demurrer relies on the Judicial Council of California Civil Jury Instructions (“CACI”) as its authority, which Plaintiff asserts is improper. Indeed, “jury instructions, whether published or not, are not themselves the law, and are not authority to establish legal propositions or precedent. They should not be cited as authority for legal principles.” (Evans v. Hood Corp. (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 1022, 1049, quoting People v. Morales (2001) 25 Cal.4th 34, 48, fn. 7 [“Morales”]).

Defendant, in turn, argues that “a defendant can rely on CACI and BAJI instructions in support of a demurrer when the complaint fails to contain allegations supporting the required elements.” (Carr & Schwing, 1 Cal. Affirmative Def. (2d ed. 2023) § 10:13), that CACI instructions “when they are accurate. . . restate the law” (Morales, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 48, fn. 7), and that CACI instructions are encouraged for use and are regularly used for trial (see Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.1050). 

The Court agrees that Plaintiff has not cited to any legal authority demonstrating inaccuracies in the CACI instructions referenced in Defendant’s demurrer and that Plaintiff has submitted a substantive opposition, undermining any claimed prejudice. Under the circumstances presented, the Court declines to summarily overrule Defendant’s demurrer on this basis.

B.       Request for Judicial Notice 

Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of certain California State University and California Department of General Services records. A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court grants Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice in full.

 

C.       First, Second and Fourth Causes of Action (i.e., for Discrimination Based on Medical Condition and Disability, Harassment, and Hostile Work Environment, Respectively) 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff has failed to allege facts sufficient to establish a qualifying medical condition or disability with respect to the first, second, and fourth causes of action. The Court agrees. 

FEHA (Gov. Code § 12900 et seq.) prohibits employment discrimination on the basis of “physical disability, mental disability [and] medical condition.” (Gov. Code § 12940, subd. (a).)

Under FEHA, a physical disability includes “(1) [h]aving any physiological disease, disorder, condition, cosmetic disfigurement, or anatomical loss that . . . both . . . (A) [a]ffects one or more of the following body systems: neurological, immunological, musculoskeletal, special sense organs, respiratory, including speech organs, cardiovascular, reproductive, digestive, genitourinary, hemic and lymphatic, skin, and endocrine” and “(B)[l]imits a major life activity” or (2) “[a]ny other health impairment. . . that requires special education or related services.” (Gov. Code § 12926, subd. (m)(1) and (2)). A mental disability includes “(1) [h]aving any mental or psychological disorder or condition, such as intellectual disability, organic brain syndrome, emotional or mental illness, or specific learning disabilities, that limits a major life activity” or “(2) [a]ny other mental or psychological disorder. . . that requires special education or related services.” (Gov. Code § 12926, subd. (j)(1) and (2).) A medical condition means “either of the following: (1) [a]ny health impairment related to or associated with a diagnosis of cancer or a record or history of cancer [or] (2) [g]enetic characteristics . . . [such as a] scientifically or medically identifiable gene or chromosome . . that is known to be a cause of a disease or disorder in a person or that person’s offspring.” (Gov. Code § 12926, subd. (i)(1) and (2).) 

The term “major life activity” is “broadly construed” and includes “physical, mental, and social activities and working.” (Gov. Code § 12926, subds. (j)(1)(C) and(m)(1)(B)(iii).) “[L]imits” means “it makes the achievement of the major life activity difficult” (Gov. Code § 12926, subds. (j)(1)(B) and(m)(1)(B)(i)). 

Plaintiff alleges that she “has been diagnosed with Anxiety, Thyroid Disease, Gerd, head, hand, full body tremors and other medical conditions, which . . . when triggered at work result in her disability.” (FAC, ¶ 11.)  Plaintiff alleges that she “suffers from anxiety due to open glares and gazes when others notice the pronounced tremors of her head and body.” (Id., ¶ 15.) 

Plaintiff’s alleged “disability” of not wanting to be “stare[d], gaze[d] and glare[d]” at does not limit a major life activity. “FEHA does not guarantee employees a stress-free working environment.” (Arteaga v. Brinks, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 327, 344 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) Plaintiff fails to allege that any of the disabilities and/or medical conditions cited above themselves limit a major life activity.

Defendant’s demurrer to the first, second, and fourth causes of action is sustained on this basis.[FN1] 

 

D.      Third and Fifth Causes of Action (i.e., for Retaliation in Violation of FEHA, and Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code Section 6310, Respectively) 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s third and fifth causes of action fail because Plaintiff has failed to allege causation. The Court agrees. Plaintiff must allege causation in both a FEHA claim for retaliation and a claim for retaliation in violation of Labor Code section 6310. (Flait v. North American Watch Corp. (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 467, 476; Muller v. Automobile Club of So. California (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 431, 451, disapproved of on other grounds by Colmenares v. Braemar Country Club Inc. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1019, 1031, fn. 6.) 

Plaintiff alleges that she complained of harassment and filed a complaint of discrimination with the Department of Fair Employment and Housing and the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. (FAC, ¶¶ 22 and 26.) Plaintiff further alleges that “[t]he Parties engaged in an unsuccessful Mediation on or about September of 2022[],]” and that “[t]hereafter, Defendants engaged in a course of retaliation against [her][,]” which included not allowing her requested job reclassification or 100% remote work, creating additional job duties for her, having her edit her “alternate work schedule” form, not providing her with access to an empty counseling room during ADA sessions, and reclassifying her to a different assignment. (Id., ¶ 28.) Plaintiff fails to allege facts connecting the purported adverse employment actions to her complaints. 

Defendant next asserts that Plaintiff has alleged insufficient facts indicating that she made a whistleblower health or safety complaint with respect to her fifth cause of action. The Court agrees. Labor Code section 6310 provides, in relevant part, that no employee may be discriminated against because they “[m]ade any oral or written complaint to the division, other governmental agencies having statutory responsibility for or assisting the division with reference to employee safety or health, their employer, or their representative.” Plaintiff’s allegations are based on her individual experience as Defendant’s employee and do not concern unsafe or unhealthy working conditions as a whole. “The voicing of a fear about one’s safety in the workplace does not necessarily constitute a complaint about unsafe working conditions under Labor Code section 6310.” (Muller, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at 452.) 

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action fails for non-compliance with claims presentation requirements. The Court agrees. “Under the Claims Act, no suit for ‘money or damages’ may be brought against a public entity until a written claim has been presented to the entity and the claim either has been acted upon or is deemed to have been rejected.” (Canova v. Trustees of Imperial Irrigation Dist. Employee Pension Plan (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1493.) The “failure to allege facts demonstrating or excusing compliance with the claim presentation requirement subjects a claim against a public entity to a demurrer for failure to state a cause of action.” (State of California v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1239.) While Plaintiff correctly notes that “[a]ctions under FEHA are not subject to the Tort Claims Act’s claim presentation requirements” (Opp., 7:24-25, citing Garcia v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 701, 711), she overlooks the fact that her fifth (and seventh) causes of action are not FEHA causes of action. 

The Court thus sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the third and fifth causes of action.


E.       Sixth Cause of Action (i.e., for Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment, and Retaliation)         

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action fails because Plaintiff’s discrimination, harassment, and retaliation claims fail. The Court agrees. There can be no liability under Government Code section 12940(k) where there is no viable cause of action for discrimination, harassment, and retaliation. (Trujillo v. North County Transit Dist. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 280, 289 [“The trial court correctly interpreted the verdict as not including an essential foundational predicate of harassment or discrimination, as required by the statutory scheme to support a finding of violation of Government Code section 12940, subdivision (i).”].)

Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained on this basis.

F.       Seventh Cause of Action (i.e., for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress)

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action fails because public entities are not liable for common law torts. The Court agrees. “Under the Government Claims Act (Gov. Code, § 810 et seq.), there is no common law tort liability for public entities; instead, such liability must be based on statute (Gov. Code § 815, subd. (a) [‘Except as otherwise provided by statute: A public entity is not liable for an injury, whether such injury arises out of an act or omission of the public entity . . .’].” (Guzman v. County of Monterey (2009) 46 Cal.4th 887, 897; Cochran v. Herzog Engraving Co. (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 405, 409 [“in the absence of some constitutional requirement, public entities may be liable only if a statute declares them to be liable”] and Miklosy v. Regents of University of California (2008) 44 Cal.4th 876, 899 [“our own decisions concern that section 815 abolishes common law tort liability for public entities”].) Plaintiff has not identified a statute authorizing an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action against Plaintiff.

Plaintiff asserts that there is an “exception. . . noted in Leon v. County of Riverside (2023) 14 Cal.5th 910, 923 . . .,” but fails to explain what this exception is that would create liability for Defendant. (Opp., 8:11-12.) Further, while Plaintiff argues that “a public entity may be vicariously liable for its employee’s actions,” Plaintiff has not alleged vicarious liability. (Opp. 8:15-16.)

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action fails for non-compliance with claims presentation requirements. The Court agrees, for the reasons set forth above.

Defendant next asserts that workers’ compensation is Plaintiff’s exclusive remedy for injuries arising out of and in the course of her employment. The Court agrees. Labor Code section 3600 establishes an employee’s right to workers’ compensation “for any injury sustained. . . arising out of and in the course of the employment. . .” Section 3602 provides what, where section 3600 applies, “the right to recover compensation is. . . the sole and exclusive remedy of the employee. . .” Plaintiff, in turn, asserts that “claims for intentional infliction of emotional distress in the employment context may be asserted where the actionable conduct also forms the basis for a FEHA violation.” (Light v. Department of Parks & Recreation (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 75, 97.) While true, Plaintiff overlooks the fact that she has not adequately pled a FEHA violation.

Defendant also asserts that Plaintiff has failed to adequately plead the elements of this cause of action. The Court agrees. “The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (i) outrageous conduct by defendant, (ii) an intention by defendant to cause, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress, (iii) severe emotional distress, and (iv) an actual and proximate causal link between the tortious conduct and the emotional distress.” (Nally v. Grace Community Church (1988) 47 Cal.3d 278, 300.) “Severe emotional distress means emotional distress of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable man in a civilized society should be expected to ensure it.” (Kiseskey v. Carpenters’ Trust for So. California (1983) 144 Cal.App.3d 222, 231 [quotations and citation omitted].)

Personnel management decisions are not considered “outrageous.” (See Janken v. GM Hughes Electronics (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 55, 80 [“Managing personnel is not outrageous conduct beyond the bounds of human decency, but rather conduct essential to the welfare and prosperity of society. A simple pleading of personnel management activity is insufficient to support a claim of intentional infliction of emotional distress, even if improper motivation is alleged. If personnel management decisions are improperly motivated, the remedy is a suit against the employer for discrimination”].) Plaintiff has not pled “outrageous” conduct by Defendant, that Defendant intended to cause (or recklessly disregarded the probability of causing) emotional distress, and that she suffered from “severe” emotional distress. 

The demurrer to the seventh cause of action is sustained on this basis. 

IV.     Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, the Court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the first through seventh causes of action. At the hearing, the Court will hear from Plaintiff’s counsel as to whether leave to amend is requested and as to which cause(s) of action, and if so, will require an offer of proof.

[FN1] The Court declines to consider Defendant’s additional arguments made as to Plaintiff’s first and fourth causes of action at this juncture as unnecessary.
