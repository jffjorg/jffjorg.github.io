DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV44845    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: W

CARLOS ARITA vs 7-ELEVEN INC

 

defendants’ demurrer to the fourth amended complaint with motion to strike

 

Date of Hearing:        December 12, 2024                     Trial Date:     None set

Department:              W                                                   Case No.:      21STCV44845

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Suresh Kumar Luthra, Saroj Luthra and S and R Luthra Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Carlos Arita

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is personal injury action that occurred on September 11, 2020, at a 7-Eleven franchise store located in Van Nuys, CA (the “Subject Incident”). Plaintiff alleges he was “beat up” by Defendants while in the 7-Eleven to purchase some items.

 

Plaintiff Carlos Josue Arita filed the original Complaint on December 9, 2021 and filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on December 20, 2021, against defendants 7-Eleven Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 100. On February 17, 2023, Plaintiff added S and R Luthra Inc. (dba 7-Eleven as Doe 1, Suresh Kumar Luthra as Doe 2, and Saroj Luthra as Doe 3. Plaintiff dismissed 7-Eleven, Inc. on March 21, 2023.

 

The instant action is related to 22STCV28081 (Carlos Arita v. Saros Luthra) and 22STCV28062 (Carlos Arita v. Suresh Kumar Luthra).

 

On May 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint. The SAC alleges negligence and premises liability. Plaintiff added back 7-Eleven, Inc. on December 18, 2023. On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint after seeking leave to amend. This court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for leave to file a Fourth Amended Complaint and on September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a 4AC alleging assault, battery, negligence, false imprisonment, and intentional infliction of emotional distress.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the Fourth Amended Complaint is OVERRULED. Defendants’ Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Fourth Amended Complaint is DENIED.  

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff request this court take judicial notice of (1) Defendant’s Notice of Motion and Motion To Strike Portions to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint filed with the Los Angeles Superior Court on June 18, 2024, and heard on September 17, 2024 (Exh. 1); (2) meet and confer e-mail of July 1, 2024, from Plaintiff’s counsel, Macauley Ekpenisi, Esq, on Amended Complaint after consolidation of case (Exh. 2); (3) Plaintiff’s Counsel, Macauley Ekpenisi, Esq, follow up on meet and confer of July 1, 2024 (Exh. 3); and (4) Defendant's Counsel’s responses of July 17, 2024, to the e-mail meet and confer of July 1, 2024 (Exh. 4).

 

The court GRANTS the request for judicial notice as to Exhibits 1.

 

Plaintiff also objects to Defendant’s request for judicial notice. Specifically, the deposition transcript (Exh. B) and police report (Exh. A). The court sustains the objections.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits objections to the demurrer on the grounds it is improper and based on an inadmissible the police report. As noted above, the court has sustained the objection to the police report. The court overrules the objection to the demurrer.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Suresh Kumar Luthra, Saroj Luthra, and S and R Lutrha Inc. demur to each cause of action of the Fourth Amended Complaint (“4AC”) on the grounds each cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to amount to a cause of action against Defendants and the claims are uncertain, ambiguous and unintelligible. Defendants also move to strike portions of the 4AC including punitive damages allegations.

 

Defendants demur to the 4AC on the ground the 4AC makes material omissions that contradict earlier allegations including that the alleged incident was unprovoked including omitting the fact Plaintiff was drunk at the scene. Defendants first argue the 4AC is vague, ambiguous and uncertain but fails to point out anywhere in the complaint that is uncertain other than that the fourth cause of action fails to state which of the Defendants is the target of this count. However, demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and should only be sustained where the complaint is so uncertain that the demurring defendant cannot reasonably respond. (See, e.g., Khoury v. Maly's of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) From the allegations of the fourth cause of action, it is clear the claim for false arrest/imprisonment is against both Defendants Suresh Kumar Luthra and Saroj Luthra.

 

Next, Defendants demur to the first and second causes of action for assault and battery on the grounds the prior allegations admitted that Plaintiff was intoxicated and that he never paid for the items and as a result, Defendants were trying to address a loss prevention issue. As a result, Plaintiff cannot make a claim for assault or battery because Plaintiff cannot allege Defendants acted with the intent to harm or offend Plaintiff.

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. Although Defendants state otherwise, Defendants are turning this demurrer into a contested evidentiary hearing. The “admissions” made in the earlier versions of the verified complaints are not so inconsistent that the amendment would be considered a sham pleading. Plaintiff’s “removal” of the police report and admission to being intoxicated would not necessarily establish failure to allege facts sufficient to constitute a battery or assault claim. Even if the allegations of intoxication and not paying for items were included, it would be for a jury to decide whether Defendants acted with reasonable force to protect their property/concern for loss prevention.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the first and second causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Next, Defendants demur to the third cause of action for negligence on the grounds Defendants owed no duty because as noted under prior allegations, he was intoxicated and not acting as an ordinary customer. Defendants contend as a result, they believed he was trying to steal merchandise. Regardless of whether Plaintiff includes the allegations of intoxication, the court believes the allegations sufficient to withstand a demurrer. Nowhere in any of the versions of the complaint does it stand that Plaintiff was the one who created the danger and therefore, Defendants did not owe Plaintiff a duty. These type of arguments are better suited for summary judgment or trial.

 

Lastly, Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for false imprisonment on similar grounds. Defendants contend the facts that have been alleged in earlier pleadings reveal that Defendants availed themselves of the merchant’s privilege. Defendants claim not only was Plaintiff intoxicated when he entered the store, but he acted suspiciously, lurking around the store, and causing Defendants to feel concerned about his intent. Moreover, the police report and Plaintiff's own deposition transcript shows Plaintiff was intoxicated at the time he entered the business. Again, these arguments are better suited for summary judgment or trial.   As noted by Defendants, the standard is that the detention itself must be carried out for a reasonable time and reasonable manner. Based on the allegations, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the elements for false imprisonment.

 

The court notes Defendants do not make any arguments regarding Plaintiff’s claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to the 4AC is OVERRULED.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint for punitive damages on the grounds Plaintiff has failed to plead facts to support a claim for punitive damages and by pleading the facts that only serve Plaintiff, the 4AC fails to satisfy the heightened pleading obligation to show entitlement to punitive damages under a clear and convincing standard.

 

The allegations supporting a request for punitive damages must be alleged with specificity; conclusory allegations without sufficient facts are not enough.  (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-1042. The court concludes plaintiff has alleged facts to support a claim for punitive damages.  He alleges two intentional torts – assault/battery and false imprisonment.   He claims that these actions were done with malicious and a conscious disregard for his health and safety.  These allegations are sufficient. 


Case Number: 23VECV02460    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: W

BEBAWY YAKOUB vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.

 

defendant meredith joan kurz, trustee of the meredith joan kurz living trust utd dated 06/03/2009’s motion for summary judgment

 

Date of Hearing:        December 12, 2024                          Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV02460

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Meredith Joan Kurz, Trustee of The Meredith Joan Kurz Living Trust UTD Dated 06/03/2009

Responding Party:     No Opposition  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of a tri and fall on a public sidewalk. On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff Bebawy Yakoub filed a complaint against City of Los Angeles, County of Los Angeles, State of California, California Department of Transportation and Meredith Joan Kurz, Trustee of The Meredith Joan Kurz Living Trust UTD Dated 06/03/2009 (“Kurz”) for negligence and premises liability.

 

On November 15, 2023, Defendant Kurz filed a cross-complaint against County of Los Angeles for indemnity and contribution. Defendant Kurz dismissed her cross-complaint on February 2, 2024.

 

On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Defendant County of Los Angeles. Plaintiff dismissed Defendant State of California and California Department of Transportation on January 8, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Meredith Joan Kurz, Trustee of The Meredith Joan Kurz Living Trust UTD Dated 06/03/2009’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Kurz moves this court for summary judgment on the grounds Defendant Kurz did not create the defect in the public sidewalk that allegedly caused Plaintiff’s fall and Defendant Kurz did not own, maintain, or control the public sidewalk adjacent to their residential property and had no duty to repair any defect existing on the sidewalk.

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (CCP § 437c(a).) “[I]f all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law,” the moving party will be entitled to summary judgment.  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. (CCP § 437c(f)(2).)    

 

The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if the party does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; accord CCP § 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 0159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also CCP § 437c(c).)

 

Defendant Kurz argues that the causes of action for negligence and premises liability have no merit because Defendant Kurz did not own, maintain or control the public sidewalk where Plaintiff fell or create the alleged dangerous condition. Therefore, Defendant did not owe a duty to Plaintiff.

 

“In order to establish liability on a negligence theory, a plaintiff must prove duty, breach, causation and damages.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.) “The elements of a negligence and a premises liability claim are the same.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1133, 1158.) “The owner of property, in so far as an invitee is concerned, is not an insurer of safety but must use reasonable care to keep his premises in a reasonably safe condition and give warning of latent or concealed perils.” (Blodgett v. B.H. Dyas Co. (1935) 4 Cal.2d 511, 512.)

 

Here, Defendant has presented the following undisputed material facts: This action arises out of a trip and fall that occurred on June 18, 2022, on a public sidewalk, located in front of 7801 Louise Avenue, Northridge, CA 91325 (the “property”). (UMF 1.) Defendant owns the property. (UMF 4.) The roots from a tree in the parkway fronting the neighbors’ property at 7801 Louise Ave, Northridge, CA 91325 caused the vertical uplift of the sidewalk where Plaintiff allegedly tripped and fell. (UMF 2.) Defendant did not have any plants in the vicinity of the uplifted sidewalk capable of displacing the sidewalk. (UMF 3.) Moreover, the location of the incident lies within the right-of-way of the City of Los Angeles. (UMF 5.) Defendant did not install, build, or construct the sidewalk or parkway adjacent to the property and Defendant did not plant the trees on the parkway. (UMF 6, 7.) Defendant also did not control, maintain, or repair any part of the sidewalk, parkway or tress in the parkway. (UMF 8.)

 

Defendant has met their burden in showing that they did not breach a duty to Plaintiff. Defendant has presented evidence that Defendant did not have any plants in the vicinity that would have uplifted the sidewalk as Plaintiff complains thereof and that where the alleged incident occurred lies within the right-of-way of the City of Los Angeles and not Defendant’s property. (See Green Decl.; See Mekhaiel Decl.) As such, no duty of care exists.

 

Given that the motion is unopposed, Plaintiff has not met their burden in showing a triable issue of a material fact. Thus, as a matter of law, the court finds that Defendant did not owe a duty and Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligence and premises liability cannot be established. (CCP §437c(o).)   

 

Accordingly, Defendant Meredith Joan Kurz, Trustee of The Meredith Joan Kurz Living Trust UTD Dated 06/03/2009’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 24VECV02482    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: W

ANDREA OCAMPOS vs LAW OFFICE OF MICHAEL POOLE

 

defendant law office of michael poole’s motion pursuant to code of civil procedure section 128.7

 

Date of Hearing:        December 12, 2024                          Trial Date:       None set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV02482

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Law Office of Michael Poole

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Andrea Ocampos

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff Andrea Ocampos filed a complaint against Defendant Law Office of Michael Poole PAC for several Labor Code violations, violation of Business and Professions Code 17200, and FEHA violations.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Law Office of Michael Poole’s Motion Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 is DENIED.

 

discusion

 

Defendant Law Office of Michael Poole move this court, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, to impose monetary and non-monetary sanctions, including striking the complaint and dismissing the action as well as reasonable attorney fees, on the ground that Plaintiff and her attorneys presented pleadings for an improper purpose.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, “[a] party seeking sanctions must follow a two-step procedure. First, the moving party must serve on the offending party a motion for sanctions.” (Malovec v. Hamrell (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 434, 440.) Specifically, Section 128.7 provides for a 21-day period during which a party may avoid sanctions by withdrawing the offending pleading or other document. After expiration of the safe harbor period, a court may impose sanctions if it concludes a pleading was filed for an improper purpose, frivolous, or was indisputably without merit, either legally or factually. (CCP §128.7(b).)

 

Plaintiff takes no issue with Defendant’s compliance of the Safe Harbor provision. Defendant served Plaintiff the motion on September 26, 2024 and the motion was not filed with the court until November 4, 2024, 39 days later. Accordingly, the court finds the motion complies with the safe harbor provision.

 

Next, “[u]nder section 128.7, a court may impose sanctions if it concludes a pleading was filed for an improper purpose or was indisputably without merit, either legally or factually. [Citation.]” (Bucur v. Ahmad (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 175, 189.) “A claim is factually frivolous if it is ‘not well grounded in fact’ and is legally frivolous if it is ‘not warranted by existing law or a good faith argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law.’ [Citation.] In either case, to obtain sanctions, the moving party must show the party's conduct in asserting the claim was objectively unreasonable. [Citation.] A claim is objectively unreasonable if ‘any reasonable attorney would agree that [it] is totally and completely without merit.’ [Citations.]” (Id.) No showing of bad faith is required. (In re Marriage of Reese & Guy (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1214, 1221.) 

 

Defendant argues the pleadings against it were brought for an improper purpose because Defendant has presented to Plaintiff’s counsel evidence that they did not employ five or more employees and that she was paid her regular pay along with bonuses prior to her termination. Moreover, Defendant contends Plaintiff failed to allege that they provided Defendant with any notice with respect to Plaintiff’s migraines.

 

The court disagrees. “‘A bad faith action or tactic is considered ‘frivolous’ if it is ‘totally and completely without merit’ or instituted ‘for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.’’”  (In re Marriage of Sahafzadeh-Taeb & Taeb (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 124, 135 (quoting Levy v. Blum (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 625, 635).)  “‘Whether an action is frivolous is governed by an objective standard:  any reasonable attorney would agree it is totally and completely without merit.’”  (Id. (quoting Levy, supra, 92 Cal.App.4th at 635).) 

 

Here, any reasonable attorney would find the pleadings nonfrivolous under circumstances. Plaintiff has alleged she notified Defendant of her migraines and was thereafter shortly terminated. She also alleged she worked in excess of eight hours and regularly worked in excess for 40 hours per week. Even if FEHA does not apply, Plaintiff has brought other causes of action. Accordingly, nothing before the court indicates the complaint was filed for an improper purpose or was indisputably without merit. Defendant is attempting to argue the merits of the case.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 is DENIED.

 

The court notes Plaintiff seeks their own 128.7 sanctions against Defendant on the grounds the 128.7 motion was brought for the purpose of harassing and intimidating Plaintiff. Plaintiff seeks $7,500.00 in sanctions.

 

A motion for sanctions under section 128.7 that is brought primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation, is itself subject to a motion for sanctions. (CCP § 128.7(h).)

 

As noted by Plaintiff, this motion was brought at an early stage of litigation even prior to discovery being conducted. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,000.00.
