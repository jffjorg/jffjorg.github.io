DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 20STCV18798    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Lauren Drablier’s Motion for Appointment of Referee

            Plaintiff Lauren Drablier moves to appoint a referee to sell real property at 8203 Zitola Terrace, Los Angeles, California 90012.  Code of Civil Procedure section 873.010(a) provides that in a partition action, “[t]he court shall appoint a referee to divide or sell the property as ordered by the court.”  For partition by sale, the referee “shall sell the property in the manner and following the procedures provided in this chapter.”  (CCP § 873.510.)

Defendant John Thomas McGuire opposes this motion by arguing plaintiff is responsible for the delay in selling the property.  Regardless of whether either party bears more responsibility for the delay, the court finds it appropriate to appoint a referee to sell the property. 

Disposition

Plaintiff Lauren Drablier’s motion for appointment of referee is granted.  Within 15 days, plaintiff shall file and serve a written proposal of three candidates to serve as referee.  For each candidate, the proposal shall state (1) the name, address, telephone number, and email address of the candidate; (2) the candidate’s hourly rates for services rendered in this case, and (3) a brief description of the candidate’s credentials.  Within seven days of plaintiff’s proposal, defendant shall notify the court and plaintiff in writing of his selection of one of the three candidates. 

The court hereby sets an order to show cause re: appointment of referee for January 23, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

The court has prepared a tentative order appointing the referee that it has provided to the parties.    At the January 23, 2024, hearing, the parties may raise any concern they have about the specific terms and conditions of the tentative order.

Case Number: 23STCV13642    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 52

Defendants Hilla Group LLC, Hella-Effective LLC, Viewpoint Estates LLC, and 9270 Flicker LLC’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint

Demurrer

Defendants Hilla Group LLC, Hella-Effective LLC, Viewpoint Estates LLC, and 9270 Flicker LLC demur to all 10 causes of action alleged by plaintiff ARTES Design Build’s first amended complaint. 

Business & Professions Code § 7031

            Defendants argue plaintiff’s first through eighth causes of action fail because plaintiff seeks to recover compensation for work as an unlicensed contractor under the Contractors State License Law.  “[N]o person engaged in the business or acting in the capacity of a contractor, may bring or maintain any action … for the collection of compensation for the performance of any act or contract where a license is required by this chapter without alleging that they were a duly licensed contractor at all times during the performance of that act or contract.”  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 7031(a).)

            It is not clear from the face of the complaint that the work for which plaintiff seeks compensation required a contractor’s license.  A demurrer should be overruled when “a ‘reasonable interpretation of the agreement[s] between the parties’ is that at least some of the work that [plaintiff] performed did not require a license.”  (Phoenix Mechanical Pipeline, Inc. v. Space Exploration Technologies Corp. (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 842, 852.)  The Contractors State License Law generally requires licensing of a “contractor” as defined under Business & Professions Code section 7026: “ ‘Contractor’ … is synonymous with ‘builder’ and …a contractor is any person who undertakes to or offers to undertake to …  or does himself or herself or by or through others, construct, alter, repair, add to, subtract from, improve, move, wreck or demolish any building, highway, road, parking facility, railroad, excavation or other structure, project, development or improvement.”

            Defendants’ demurrer makes only conclusory arguments on this issue without citing the statutory definition of “contractor” or any other authority.  Defendants rely on the first amended complaint’s allegations that plaintiff was hired for “construction” (¶ 71) and on the scope of work in the contract attached to the first amended complaint.  (Ex. A, p. 8.)  The first amended complaint, however, also alleges the parties’ contract required plaintiff “to provide project management, inspection, design, pre-construction, and consulting services at various projects.”  (¶ 16.)  The scope of work, meanwhile, provides that plaintiff would perform “Construction Plan Coordination” and “Project Management” that amount to acting as an intermediary between defendants and professionals such as architects and engineers hired “until plans are submitted to Los Angeles Department of Building and Safety.”  (Ex. A, p. 8.) 

At this stage in the proceedings, one can reasonably interpret the parties’ contract as not undertaking to construct or build any structure or project, but only facilitating planning and design of the project before construction.  The court therefore cannot sustain defendants’ demurrer based on Business and Professions Code section 7031.

1st to 4th Causes of Action: Breach of Contract and Common Counts

Defendants also demur to these causes of action on the grounds that plaintiff makes only conclusory alter ego allegations.  Regardless of any alter ego allegations, plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for these causes of action against each defendant.  The contract attached to the first amended complaint provides, “THIS AGREEMENT is made and entered into by and between ARTES Design Build .. and Hilla Group LLC, its affiliates and subsidiaries, and on behalf of the following wholly owned and controlled companies of Hilla Group LLC, namely Hella-Effective LLC, Viewpoint Estates LLC, 9270 Flicker LLC, jointly and severally.”  (FAC, Ex. A, p. 1.)  Alter ego liability is therefore not necessary. 

5th to 8th Causes of Action: Foreclosure of Mechanics Liens

Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts for these causes of action.  Civil Code section 8400 provides: “A person that provides work authorized for a work of improvement, including, but not limited to, the following persons, has a [mechanics] lien right under this chapter: (a) Direct contractor.  (b) Subcontractor.  (c) Material supplier.  (d) Equipment lessor.  (e) Laborer.  (f) Design professional.”  The first amended complaint does not allege plaintiff is any of those. 

Plaintiff’s opposition argues only that the statute should be liberally construed and that it performed “various services and contributed to multiple Projects.”  (Opp., p. 4, citing FAC, ¶ 16.)  The first amended complaint alleges plaintiff “provide[d] project management, inspection, design, pre-construction, and consulting services at various projects.”  (FAC, ¶ 16.)  Plaintiff does not articulate how those services fit any category enumerated in Civil Code section 8400.

9th Cause of Action: Fraud

            Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  The first amended complaint asserts a claim for promissory fraud.  “ ‘Promissory fraud’ is a subspecies of the action for fraud and deceit.  A promise to do something necessarily implies the intention to perform; hence, where a promise is made without such intention, there is an implied misrepresentation of fact that may be actionable fraud.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)  “[F]raud must be pled specifically.”  (Id. at p. 645.)  “ ‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’ ”  (Ibid.)  “A plaintiff’s burden in asserting a fraud claim against a corporate employer is even greater.  In such a case, the plaintiff must ‘allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.’ ”  (Ibid.) 

            Plaintiff does not specifically allege the required facts.  The first amended complaint alleges, “The aforementioned promises were made by DEFENDANTS in January, 2023, as it relates to the scoped work, and in the ensuing months for change order work, and the promises were made orally and in writing by DEFENDANTS directly to Plaintiff.”  (¶ 73.)  But plaintiff is a corporation (¶ 1), and the demurring defendants are limited liability companies.  Plaintiff does not allege the names of any individuals who spoke on defendants’ behalf or their authority to speak.  Nor does plaintiff specify the name of any individual to whom defendants made the alleged misrepresentations.    

10th Cause of Action: Unfair Competition

Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  The Unfair Competition Law “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.”  (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)  As discussed above, plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts for a fraud cause of action.  Plaintiff’s 10th cause of action itself makes only conclusory allegations based on the paragraphs above.  Plaintiff thus alleges no more than that defendants did not pay or its services provided under a contract.  “A breach of contract may form the predicate for Section 17200 claims, provided it also constitutes conduct that is unlawful, or unfair, or fraudulent.”  (Puentes v. Wells Fargo Home Mortgage, Inc. (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 638, 645, internal quotes and alterations omitted [finding nothing unfair or fraudulent].) 

Plaintiff has alleged a mere breach of contract.  Plaintiff alleges no conduct that is unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent under Business and Professions Code section 17200.

Plaintiff also does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action because it does not allege a basis for any remedy permitted under the Unfair Competition Law.  Stating a cause of action for unfair competition requires “a viable claim for” one of the “remedies available” under it.  (Madrid v. Perot Systems Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 440, 467.)  “[R]estitution is the only monetary remedy authorized in a private action brought under the unfair competition law.”  (Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 614.)  “The word ‘restitution’ means the return of money or other property obtained through an improper means to the person from whom the property was taken.”  (Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 614.)  “ ‘The object of restitution is to restore the status quo by returning to the plaintiff funds in which he or she has an ownership interest.’ ”  (Ibid.)

Plaintiff alleges the purported unfair competition caused plaintiff to “suffer[] damages.”  (FAC, ¶ 82.)  For this cause of action, plaintiff prays for “Actual and/or consequential damages” and “treble damages.”  (Id., p. 19.)  Plaintiff does not allege facts establishing a viable claim for restitution or injunction or any other remedy permitted under the Unfair Competition Law.

Motion to Strike

Defendants Hilla Group LLC, Hella-Effective LLC, Viewpoint Estates LLC, and 9270 Flicker LLC move to strike three portions of plaintiff’s first amended complaint.  Courts may strike a “demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint.”  (CCP § 431.10(b)(3).)    

Interest

Defendants move to strike the following portion of the first amended complaint: “18% per annum as provided for in the Agreement, plus a ‘charge’ on progress payments not timely made at 2% per month from the date the payment should have been made until the date it was made, pursuant to Business and Professions Code § 7108.5; and its attorneys’ fees herein, plus a ‘charge’ on the retention under the Projects at 2% per month since the date of completion of the work of improvement, pursuant to Civil Code §§ 8812 and 8818.”  (FAC, ¶ 18.) 

Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to recover 18% annual interest.  Article XV, section 1 of the California Constitution generally prohibits interest rates over 10%.  Plaintiff’s opposition argues only that the constitutional provision on usury does not apply because the parties’ contract provides for 18%.  That portion of the agreement is void.  The California Constitution’s provision on usury expressly limits the interest rate for which “it shall be competent for the parties” to agree.  (Cal. Const., art. XV, § 1.)  Plaintiff does not allege facts establishing any exception to the general usury provision.

Plaintiff also does not allege sufficient facts for 2% monthly charges under the cited statutes.  Business and Professions Code section 7108.5 applies to money “[a] prime contractor or subcontractor” owes “to any subcontractor.”  (Id., subd. (a).)  Civil Code section 8818, meanwhile, provides for “a penalty of 2 percent per month” (id., subd. (a)) on the amount “withheld by an owner from a direct contractor or by a direct contractor from a subcontractor.”  (Civ. Code, § 8810.)  Plaintiff does not allege either party is a contractor. 

Damages for Unfair Competition

Defendants move to strike portions of the first amended complaint regarding “damages” and “treble damages” for the 10th cause of action for unfair competition.  “Plaintiffs may not receive damages, much less treble damages” under the Unfair Competition Law.  (Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 179.)

Disposition

Defendants Hilla Group LLC, Hella-Effective LLC, Viewpoint Estates LLC, and 9270 Flicker LLC’s demurrers to plaintiff ARTES Design Build’s first through fourth causes of action are overruled.  Defendants’ demurrers to plaintiff’s fifth through 10th causes of action are sustained with 20 days’ leave to amend.

Defendants Hilla Group LLC, Hella-Effective LLC, Viewpoint Estates LLC, and 9270 Flicker LLC’s motion to strike portions of the first amended complaint is granted with 20 days’ leave to amend.

The court hereby strikes the following portions of the first amended complaint with 20 days’ leave to amend:

(1) “18% per annum as provided for in the Agreement, plus a ‘charge’ on progress payments not timely made at 2% per month from the date the payment should have been made until the date it was made, pursuant to Business and Professions Code § 7108.5; and its attorneys’ fees herein, plus a ‘charge’ on the retention under the Projects at 2% per month since the date of completion of the work of improvement, pursuant to Civil Code §§ 8812 and 8818.”  (FAC, ¶ 18, p. 5, lines 13-18.)

(2) “Plaintiff has suffered damages in an amount to be proven at trial.”  (FAC, ¶ 82, p. 15, lines 17-18.)

(3) “1.  Actual and/or consequential damages in an amount not yet ascertained, but in excess of the minimum jurisdictional amount of this Court.  2.  For treble damages.”  (FAC, prayer, p. 19, lines 4-6.)
