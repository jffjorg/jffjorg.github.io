DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV00829    Hearing Date: November 21, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

ATLANTIC SPECIALTY INSURANCE COMPANY,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22TRCV00829

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

BENNIE NORMAN ADAMS, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 21, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Atlantic Specialty Insurance Co.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Amend Plaintiff’s Name on Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED with prejudice.

BACKGROUND

            On September 15, 2022, plaintiff Atlantic Specialty Insurance Company a/s/o One Beacon An Intact Company filed a complaint against Bennie Norman Adams, aka Bennie Adams and Jared Evan Hill for subrogation.  Plaintiff alleges that defendant Hill lost control of defendant Adams’ vehicle, which caused it and the insured vehicle to be totaled.

            On January 26, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s motion to correct the filing date of the complaint to September 2, 2022.

            On July 11, 2023, the court denied without prejudice plaintiff’s proposed amendment to complaint, stating noticed motion and points and authorities required and to explain substantial difference in the names.

            On July 31, 2023, at the CMC, plaintiff was instructed to file further declarations or file a noticed motion to amend/change plaintiff’s name.

            On November 2, 2023, the Court denied without prejudice plaintiff’s motion for an amendment to complaint correcting plaintiff’s name.

            On January 9, 2024, plaintiff attempted to file a FAC, which was denied on August 28, 2024, because the Court had not given leave.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are:  (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 486, 490.  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Atlantic Specialty Insurance Company a/s/o One Beacon An Intact Company requests leave to amend plaintiff’s name to OBI National Insurance Company a/s/o Ben Larbi and Company LLC, pursuant to CCP §576.

            The Court notes that previously on November 2, 2023, more than a year ago, the Court had denied plaintiff’s motion to correct plaintiff’s name.  In that motion, plaintiff had cited to CCP §116.50 and CCP §473, neither of which were applicable.   

            This time around, plaintiff cites to CCP §576, which states:  “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”  Plaintiff has not cited any case authority where this section has been used to allow plaintiff to amend to change to an entirely different plaintiff.

            In the herein motion, which is the same as the one previously filed, plaintiff asserts that it has come to the attention of plaintiff’s counsel that plaintiff should be reflected as “OBI National Insurance Company a/s/o Ben Larbi and Company LLC” because the currently named plaintiff Atlantic Specialty Insurance Company is a subrogee of One Beacon An Intact Company (OBI National Insurance Company).  “OBI” is an acronym for One Beacon An Intact Company.  OBI National Insurance Company is the underwriting company, “so this is the correct party to be listed as Plaintiff.”  The subrogor is the insured, Ben Larbi and Company LLC.  OBI National Insurance Company issued an insurance policy to Ben Larbi and Company LLC.  Pursuant to the terms of the policy, plaintiff was obligated to indemnify the insured Ben Larbi and Company for damages incurred to the insured’s vehicle.  See counsel Alexander Hettena decl.; Terrill Willis decl. (executed in September 2023).

            Plaintiff has not cited to any applicable authority whereby plaintiff can substitute in or correct or amend the complaint to a different plaintiff, under these circumstances.

Thus, the motion is DENIED with prejudice.

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED with prejudice.

Plaintiff is to give notice of the ruling.

BACKGROUND

            On April 9, 2024, Monica Mansour and Hane Zarzour filed a complaint against Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC and House of Imports, Inc. for (1) violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of express warranty, (2) violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of implied warranty, and (3) negligent repair.  Plaintiffs allege that on April 28, 2023, they leased a 2023 Mercedes-Benz EQS-Class.  In July 2023, plaintiffs presented the vehicle for repairs and reported the A/C system malfunctioning and the “Battery Malfunction” light flashing on the dashboard.  In September 2023, plaintiffs presented the vehicle and reported the “Driver Assistance Operative,” “Tire Pressure Monitoring System,” and “Battery Malfunction” warnings illuminating on the dashboard.  In December 2023, plaintiffs presented the vehicle and reported the High Voltage battery depleting rapidly and the battery monitor displayed significantly varied estimates when charging the vehicle.  In February 2024, plaintiffs presented the vehicle for repeated concerns of the A/C system malfunctioning and difficulty charging the vehicle.  The following error messages illuminated on the dashboard:  “Towing Not Permitted,” “Active Emergency Stop Assist Inoperative,” “Battery Malfunction,” and “Active Steering Assist Inoperative.” 

            On September 18, 2024, defendant Mercedes filed motions to compel further responses to written discovery.

            On September 30, 2024, plaintiffs filed a motion to compel the deposition of Mercedes’ PMK and a motion to compel further responses to request for production.

            On October 3, 2024, defendant filed a motion to compel the deposition of plaintiff Monica Mansour.

            On October 30, 2024, defendant took its six motions to compel further responses off calendar.

            On November 1, 2024, an IDC was held.  The Court ordered that the depositions of plaintiffs take place on dates certain, and that defense counsel try to obtain a date for defendant’s PMK to take place prior to April 2025.  Counsel was ordered to meet and confer and to file a joint report as to the discovery requests that remain in dispute. 

            On November 7, 2024, counsel filed a joint status report.

DISCUSSION and RULING

            The joint status report filed on November 7, 2024 identified eight requests still at issue in plaintiff’s request for production of documents:  Nos. 3, 7, 9, 11, 13, 21, 30, and 31.

The Court rules as follows:

            No. 3 - pre-delivery inspection reports

No. 7 - pre and post litigation Xentry Diagnostic Trouble Code Reports generated from subject vehicle

No. 9 - unredacted Xentry Tip Case No. 245497135 opened on subject vehicle

No. 21 - Mercedes-Benz’s training materials for calculating repurchase

As to No. 3, defendant states that it is still in the process of performing a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and to the extent responsive documents are identified, defendant will comply by producing all non-privileged documents in its possession, custody, or control.  As to Nos. 7 and 9, defendant states that it will comply with requests by producing all non-privileged documents (after the completion of plaintiffs’ depositions).  As to No. 21, defendant states that it has already produced non-privileged documents.

Plaintiffs’ position is that defendant’s responses contain the “same deficient partial statements of compliance which fail to identify with particularity which document/s it is withholding” and that defendant’s responses “yield the same undefined universe as to what documents are being produced, and which documents are being withheld on the basis of an objection.”

Defendant made objections based on privilege.

The Court orders that defendant serve a privilege log.

 

            No. 11 - summary report of amounts paid for warranty repairs

Defendant states that “it is not clear” what documents that the request seeks, but that to the extent it understands what documents are responsive, it has fully complied by producing all non-privileged documents in its possession, custody, or control.

The Court orders that defendant serve a privilege log.

 

            No. 13 - Salesforce documents encompassing communications between Mercedes-Benz and/or any third parties responsible for operating Mercedes-Benz’s customer hotline, or which Mercedes-Benz has legal “control” over

Defendant states that it has conducted a diligent search and has not identified any responsive documents.

The Court finds that defendant’s response is sufficient.

 

            Nos. 30-31 – federally mandated “Customer Complaint” and “Field Service Action” Excel or XML data submissions that Mercedes-Benz uploaded onto its “NHTSA Early Warning Reporting Manufacturer Account,” which encompass the same powertrain and electrical defects in the subject vehicle as those found in vehicles of the same year, make, and model. 

Defendant states that any responsive documents are publicly and/or equally available to plaintiff, it is not aware of any recalls with regard to the subject vehicle, and it is not entirely clear as to what documents these requests seek.

The joint status report does not provide plaintiffs’ position as to Nos. 30 and 31.  In any event, whether the documents are equally available to plaintiffs is not a valid ground for failing to produce responsive documents.

The Court orders that defendant serve responsive documents to Nos. 30 and 31.
