DEPARTMENT F43 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22CHCV00676    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F-43

Date: 6-3-24

Case # 22CHCV00676, Vershinin v. Cohen & Cross-Action

Trial Date: 2-10-25

 

SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTY:                Cross-Defendants Coldwell Banker Commercial Realty and William Anthony Gilmore

RESPONDING PARTY:       Cross-Complainant Jane Cohen

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication

 

RULING: Summary judgment is granted.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Sergey Vershinin sued defendant Jane Cohen on August 23, 2022, for (1) breach of contract, (2) intentional misrepresentation, (3) negligent misrepresentation, and (4) violation of the Civil and Public Resources Codes. Vershinin alleges he purchased a commercial property (Property) from Cohen in November 2020. Before the parties executed their purchase agreement (Agreement), Vershinin informed Cohen that he intended to use the Property to build a fuel station, truck stop, and convenience store. Cohen did not disclose to Vershinin that the Property is located on or near the San Andreas Fault, rendering it largely unfit for this purpose.

 

On January 20, 2023, Cohen cross-complained against cross-defendants Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Company (Coldwell) and William Anthony Gilmore, the moving parties here, alleging causes of action for (1) negligence and (2) breach of fiduciary duty. Cohen alleges Coldwell and Gilmore represented both her and Vershinin as their joint broker during the sale of the Property. The parties’ escrow company, Chicago Title, prepared a “Natural Hazards Report” that included information about the fault lines on the Property. Cross-Defendants ensured that Chicago Title provided the Natural Hazards Report to Vershinin. But they did not ensure Vershinin read or understood it. Cohen contends they were duty-bound to do so, and if they had this litigation might have been avoided.

 

Cross-Defendants filed their motion for summary judgment on December 12, 2023. Cohen filed her opposition on May 21, 2024. Cross-Defendants filed their reply on May 29, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Evidentiary Matters

 

Cross-Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice: Cross-Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of three filings in this action. The Court grants the requests, although they are unnecessary.

 

Cross-Complainant’s Evidentiary Objections:

 

Cross-Complainant has directed her objections to the facts within Cross-Defendants’ Separate Statement rather than the evidence. This is procedurally improper. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1354 [format of written objections].) The Court makes best efforts to apply her objections as intended to the relevant evidence.

 

Objections Nos. 1-3 are moot. The Court regards Cohen’s Exhibit B as a true and correct copy of the same agreement. It is identical in all relevant respects to the exhibit she has objected to, and its admission does not undermine Cross-Complainants’ Separate Statement.

 

Objection No. 4 is overruled.

 

Objections Nos. 5-9 to Gilmore’s Exhibit 4 are overruled. Cross-Complainant cites no authority for the proposition that an email with attachments is incomplete, for purposes of Evidence Code section 1402, unless its proponent also proffers the attachments. Moreover, Cross-Complainant’s objection is not based on proffered file attachments, but hyperlinks to files hosted elsewhere. Her reasoning implies that any document that contains hyperlinks must be excluded as incomplete unless it appends all material linked to. This is unreasonable and illogical. Perhaps most importantly, Cohen does not challenge the authenticity of the email, or contend that she could not access the documents, or otherwise deny they were sent or received.

 

Objection No. 10 is overruled.

 

Objection No. 11 is overruled. Cohen does not demonstrate how or why the Counter-Offer should have been appended to the Property Purchase Agreement, such that the latter should be excluded.

 

Objections Nos. 12-13 are overruled.

 

Objections Nos. 14-15 are sustained, except to the extent that paragraph 13 authenticates the relevant exhibit.

(Ballesteros Decl., ¶ 2.) Cross-Defendants have not provided the requests responded to or a verification from Vershinin, so the efforts to authenticate it are inadmissible hearsay.

Procedural Matters

 

Cross-Defendants gave timely notice of their motion, and it is set more than thirty (30) days before the trial. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(2).)

 

Cross-Complainant filed and served her opposition thirteen (13) calendar days before the hearing, which is one day late. (Id., subd. (b)(2).) The Court exercises its discretion to consider the opposition regardless of the minor lapse.

 

Cross-Defendants’ reply was timely filed.

 

Discussion

 

Cross-Defendants move for summary judgment, or summary adjudication of each cause of action, on the grounds that (1) Cohen can establish none of the elements of negligence, and (2) although Cross-Defendants owed Cohen a fiduciary duty, she cannot establish they breached it.

 

Cohen argues a triable issue exists with regard to each relevant element.

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)” (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) “On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467; see also CCP § 437c(c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.” (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

Cross-Defendants first move for summary adjudication on Cohen’s first cause of action for negligence. The elements of negligence are (1) duty, (2) breach, (3) causation, and (4) injury. (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.)

 

Cross-Defendants argue they owed Cohen a contractual duty and a fiduciary duty, but no other duty, and for the same reasons they did not breach any fiduciary duty, they breached no ordinary duty of care even if they owed one.

 

Cohen does not oppose the portion of Cross-Defendants’ motion directed to her first cause of action. Her opposition pivots from facts and legal standard directly to her argument that “Coldwell and Gilmore violated their fiduciary duties”. (Opp., 3:11, emphasis added.) The remainder of her opposition refers to fiduciary duties repeatedly, and never to any ordinary duty of care – which comports with the law cited by Cross-Defendants.

 

Cohen having waived opposition, the motion is granted as to her first cause of action.

 

As to the second cause of action: the elements of breach of fiduciary duty are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)

 

The issues in the cross-complaint must frame Cohen’s motion for summary judgment. (See Scolinos v. Kolts, supra, 37 Cal. App. 4th at pp. 640-641.) In her cross-complaint, Cohen alleges only:

 

“The terms of [Cohen and Vershinin’s] AGREEMENT were that certain disclosures were made by COHEN to VERSHININ concerning environmental hazards such as earthquake fault lines. [¶] Escrow prepared an environmental hazard report and provided it to COHEN and GILMORE which indicated fault lines on the SUBJECT PROPERTY. [¶] GILMORE had a duty to provide the environmental hazard report to VERSHININ but failed to do so.

...

[Cross-Defendants’] fiduciary duty required [them] to, among other things, act with the

utmost care, integrity, honesty and loyalty in dealing with COHEN. [¶] COHEN is informed and believes, and upon such information and belief alleges, that COLDWELL and GILMORE, and each of them, breached their duties to COHEN by failing to act with the utmost care, integrity, honesty and loyalty in dealing with COHEN.”

 

(Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 11-13, 25-26.)

 

Cross-Defendants concede they owed Cohen (and Vershinin) fiduciary duties as the parties’ dual agent.

 

As to the only breach alleged in the cross-complaint: it is not reasonably disputed that Cross-Defendants did ensure delivery of the Natural Hazards Report to Vershinin. Cross-Defendants carry their burden with Gilmore’s testimony authenticating two “Document Delivery Emails,” one to recipients including him and Vershinin, another to him and Cohen, containing links to an Escrow Acceptance Letter and the crucial Natural Hazards Report. (See Gilmore Decl., Exhs. 14-15.) This alone carries Cross-Defendants’ initial burden to negate Cohen’s claim.

 

Cohen does not deny any of this evidence, so she fails to raise a triable issue. Although she disclaims knowledge of the fault line issue until Vershinin raised it during escrow, she does not argue that Vershinin did not receive the Report, or indeed that she did not receive it. For reasons discussed below, Gilmore only needed to ensure it was sent. Cohen raises no triable issue suggesting he did not.

 

Confining Cross-Defendants’ motion to the issues raised in the cross-complaint, this finding by itself entitles them to judgment. Cohen only alleged Gilmore failed to send the Report. Cross-Defendants refuted this allegation and Cohen raised no issue in response.

 

In the moving and opposition papers, the parties seem to have shifted to an argument not implicated in the cross-complaint: that Cross-Defendants were obligated to ensure Vershinin received and understood the Natural Hazards Report. The Court discusses this argument as well, although it is unnecessary because the cross-complaint has already been defeated on its own terms.

 

Cross-Defendants argue that, particularly in a commercial real estate transaction, a broker has no fiduciary duty to specially inspect and discover material facts for disclosure to a buyer. (Smith v. Rickard (1988) 205 Cal.App.3d 1354, 1360.) A broker also cannot be ascribed constructive knowledge in a commercial transaction; actual knowledge of undisclosed defects is required. (Assilzadeh v. California Fed. Bank (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 399, 410 (Assilzadeh).) Even if a defect is known, a broker need only disclose the simple fact, not elaborate on its effect or ramifications. (Sweat v. Hollister (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 603, 609.)

 

Cohen argues “one email to a 3rd party without follow-up does not fulfill [a] broker’s fiduciary duties” (Opp., 5:-23-24 [formatting omitted, edited for typos].) She argues Gilmore, as her fiduciary, was at least obligated to ensure that Vershinin had received and reviewed the report, or even to review it with him. Cross-Defendants do not suggest Gilmore did anything other than attach the report to an email.

 

Cohen cites no case law in the portion of her opposition devoted to this issue. She advances only the conclusion that Gilmore was neither diligent nor faithful. (Cohen criticizes Gilmore for not specifically stating in his declaration that he satisfied his fiduciary duty. This is not a defect; such testimony would be an unnecessary and inadmissible conclusion.)

 

Assilzadeh, supra, and later cases citing it carry Cross-Defendants’ motion.

 

In Assilzadeh, a seller’s and a seller’s agent’s duties of disclosure were satisfied where it informed the buyer of a commercial property that the property had been involved in construction defect litigation. (Assilzadeh, supra, at pp. 411-412.) The Court of Appeal found that, even where the seller and agent knew that the specifics of the litigation likely affected the property’s value, it did not have to relay this opinion to the buyer. As here, delivering the information satisfied its obligation; the buyer was obligated to follow up on the information it was given. (Ibid.)

 

Later cases citing Assilzadeh clarified that a broker has a duty to learn and disclose facts material to a property’s desirability, but the broker does not need to investigate or further verify the information as long as it discloses to the principal that it has not done so. (See Horiike v. Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Co. (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1024, 1040-1041 (Horiike), citing Assilzadeh, supra, at pp. 414-416 [“a dual agent representing the seller and the buyer [has a] duty to learn and disclose facts”, not to explain or interpret them] and Salahutdin v. Valley of California, Inc. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 555, 562-563 [broker need only disclose that facts are unverified].)

 

It is not reasonably disputed that Vershinin and Cohen received the Natural Hazards Reportr from Chicago Title, or that it revealed the fault line problems with the Property. (See Gilmore Decl., Exhs. 14-15.) Gilmore was not obligated to do an extensive investigation into how the report affected Vershinin’s intended use of the Property. (The Court also fails to see how Gilmore was obligated to inform Cohen that the results of a report might affect Vershinin’s intended use.)

 

Cohen also argues Gilmore neglected to inform either she or Vershinin that the other was in default on the parties’ sale agreement when transaction deadlines passed, and as a result neither of them prepared necessary payment demands (Cohen) or demands to terminate the transaction (Vershinin). Cohen argues that if Gilmore had been more diligent, the parties might have avoided this dispute.

 

None of these claims are raised in the cross-complaint. And regardless, Gilmore’s exhibits amply show he stayed in contact with both parties throughout the fault-line dispute. (See Gilmore Decl., Exhs. 13-20.)

 

Finally, Cross-Defendants raise a parenthetical argument that Cohen indemnified them in their listing agreement. This is irrelevant to the motion and not discussed in the opposition.

 

Cross-Defendants are entitled to judgment on Cohen’s cross-complaint as a matter of law.

 

The motion is granted in its entirety.

DEMURRER

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Leslie Gudiel

RESPONDING PARTY: N/A - Unopposed

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Moving party requests that the Court sustain the demurrer.

 

RULING:

SUSTAINED

 

 

 

        I.            BACKGROUND

            On November 17, 2023 Roger Velasco and Marisa Cisneros (Plaintiffs) filed a Complaint against Leslie Gudiel (Defendant). The Complaint appears to be grounded in a breach of contract claim and alleges $60,000.00 in damages and $15,000.00 in attorney’s fees. (See generally, the Complaint.) The motion before the Court now is Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint. No opposition has been filed.

 

     II.            DISCUSSION

 

Meet and Confer

            “Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a).) Defendant provides the Declaration of Luis Duenas (Duenas Decl.) which states that the parties spoke on January 26, 2024 but could not reach an agreement. Nonetheless, the requirements of Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a) have been satisfied. The Court now turns to the demurrer.

 

Legal Standard

            “[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Analysis

            Defendant contends the demurrer should be sustained because (1) the form Complaint was improperly filled out and (2) the Complaint lacks sufficient facts to sustain a cause of action for breach of contract. The Court agrees, sustains the demurrer, and grants Plaintiffs 20 days leave to amend.

 

            As outlined in the demurrer, Plaintiffs failed to properly complete the Complaint form. For example, in paragraph 4(a)(5) on page 1, the box adjacent to the word, "other (specify)" is checked, but there is nothing inserted to the right of that designation. Another example is paragraph 4(b)(1) on page 2, Plaintiffs check the box adjacent to the box "Doe Defendants", but neither the caption of the Complaint, nor the remainder of that paragraph identifies any Doe Defendants. Finally, in paragraph 8 on page 2, Plaintiffs allege what appears to be a cause of action for breach of contract and claim that it concerns an “illegal apartment unit”, however no details are provided.

 

            The lack of detail bolsters Defendant’s second point which is the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action. To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

 

            There is no detail in the Complaint as to whether the contract was written, oral, or implied. The contract is not attached, repeated verbatim, nor are the legal effects of the contract plead. Therefore, the demurrer is sustained.

 

Leave to Amend

            Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. As there is reasonable possibility of successful amendment, the Court shall grant leave to amend.

 

  III.            CONCLUSION

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint is SUSTAINED. Plaintiffs are granted 20 days leave to amend.

 

Moving party to give notice.
