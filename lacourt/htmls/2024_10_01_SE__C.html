Case Number: 21NWCV00400    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

The Windmill Co. Inc. vs Fayek Sedrak, et al.

Case No.: 21NWCV00400

Hearing Date: October 1, 2024 at 9:30 a.m.

 

ADD-ON

Tentative Ruling

Defendants Fayez Sedrak and O&C Hillside Resources Management Company’s Motion for Reconsideration is GRANTED in part as set forth below. 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

On June 21, 2021, Plaintiff The Windmill Co., Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed suit against Fayez Sedrak and O&C Hillside Resources Management Company (collectively “Defendants”) alleging Breach of Contract, Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, and Declaratory Relief. Plaintiff alleges that on January 3, 2020, Plaintiff entered into a Sale of Business Agreement with Defendants to purchase the business known as ARCO AM PM located at 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638 (“Subject Property”) for $1,590,0000.00.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to perform on the agreement and refused to sell the business to Plaintiff resulting in lost profits.

On May 16, 2023, Plaintiff issued a Subpoena for the Deposition of Third-Party Accountant John Soldat.  Soldat is the third-party tax preparer for Defendants and provides no other services other than tax return preparation.  The subpoena requested the following:

“a. Any and all DOCUMENTS relating to Profit & Loss statements (“P&Ls”) for each year (cash basis and accrual), audited (if applicable), including but not limited to bank records, credit card statements, and receipts that support the P&Ls; Any and all DOCUMENTS pertaining to journal entries regarding any and all bookkeeping record pertinent to O&C Hillside ARCO AMPM located at 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638 from 01/01/2015 to present date.

“b. Any and all DOCUMENTS YOU submitted to Tesoro relating to sale of the ARCO AMPM located at 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638 from 01/03/2020 to present date.

“c. Any and all DOCUMENTS YOU submitted to ABC relating to sale of ARCO AMPM located at 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638, from 01/03/2020 to present date.

“d. Any and all email or phone call records (including but not limited to call logs) from Fayez Sedrak or any person on behalf of O&C Hillside to YOU, pertaining to request for tax returns, profit and loss statements, and journal entries related to bookkeeping, relating to sale of ARCO AMPM located at 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638, from 01/03/2020 to present date. TAX RETURNS ARE NOT DEMANDED TO BE PRODUCED.”

Soldat did not have responsive documents for Items “b” and “c.” He produced a Privilege Log and then a Supplemental Privilege Log for Items “a” and “d.”  Soldat’s Privilege Log claimed four (4) privileges: Tax Preparer Privilege; Tax Return Privilege; Financial Information Privilege with no court Order Under Civil Code section 3295; Invasion of Privacy.

Soldat’s deposition began on May 22, 2023, but it was suspended pending resolution of this discovery dispute.

On January 3, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel resumption of Soldat’s deposition testimony and production of documents.  The Court ordered Soldat to appear for resumption of deposition within 30 days or within such time as agreed upon by the parties.  The Court also imposed sanctions in the amount of $2,785.00, inclusive of fees, against Defendants and Counsel, jointly and severally, payable within 30 days.

On January 16, 2024, Defendants timely filed the instant motion for reconsideration on the grounds that nearly all the documents referenced in Soldat’s privilege log are irrelevant and violate the privacy rights of third parties. 

Legal Standard

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc. §1008(a):¿ 

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code of Civ. Proc., §1008(a).)¿ 

A court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon “new or different facts, circumstances or law.”¿ (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal. App. 4th 1494, 1499.)  There is a strict requirement of diligence, meaning the moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence, different facts, or law earlier.¿ (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 674, 690.)¿ A motion for reconsideration is properly denied when it is based on evidence that could have been presented in connection with the original motion.¿ (Morris v. AGFA Corp. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1460;¿Hennigan v. White (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 395, 406.) ¿

Discussion

Defendants state that after the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion on January 3, 2024, Soldat disclosed the documents to Counsel for Defendants. (Benge Decl., ¶9.) Upon review, Counsel for Defendants discovered that nearly all the documents are irrelevant and violate the privacy rights of third parties. (Ibid.)

Soldat explains, “When I responded to the Subpoena I mistakenly identified every communication to and from me and Fayez Sedrak because of the language of the Subpoena. I mistakenly included all communications as listed in my computer to and from me with the entire Sedrak Family and businesses who are clients and who have nothing to do with this lawsuit, O&C Hillside Resources Management Co., or the sale of the ARCO Gas Station located at 13550 S. Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638.” (Soldat Decl., ¶1.) “My designation of these documents on my Privilege Log was an error and the product of misunderstanding what the Subpoena requested.” (Id., ¶5.)

Defendants argue that Soldat’s misunderstanding of the scope of Plaintiff’s subpoena arose from the language in paragraph (a) of the subpoena which states: “a. Any and all DOCUMENTS relating to Profit & Loss statements (“P&Ls”) for each year (cash basis and accrual), audited (if applicable), including but not limited to bank records, credit card statements, and receipts that support the P&Ls; Any and all DOCUMENTS pertaining to journal entries regarding any and all bookkeeping record pertinent to O&C Hillside ARCO AMPM located at 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638 from 01/01/2015 to present date.” According to Defendants, the semicolon after “P&Ls” indicated to Soldat that he was to produce profit and loss statements regardless of which company the profit and loss statements pertained to.  Thus, Soldat produced communications between himself and the entire Sedrak Family, even though they have nothing to do with this lawsuit. (Soldat Decl., ¶1.)

Defendants’ motion for reconsideration is GRANTED in part.  The Court determines that the Soldat declaration is new evidence that Defendants could not reasonably have produced in connection with the original motion.  The Court grants Defendants’ motion for reconsideration to dispel any perceived ambiguity in Plaintiff’s subpoena.  Towards that end, the Court expressly limits Paragraph (a) of the subpoena to documents relating to O&C Hillside’s profit and loss statements, and the basis for those profit and loss statements, from January 1, 2025 to the present.  Any confidential third-party information contained within said documents shall be redacted.  No tax returns, specific entries in the tax returns, or records that are an “integral part” of the tax returns shall be disclosed. (Brown v. Superior Court (1977) 71 Cal.App.3d 141, 143-44.) Defendants’ other objections and claims of privilege are overruled.   Soldat is ordered to appear for resumption of deposition with 30 days or within such time as agreed upon by the parties.  In all other respects, the Court’s January 3, 2024 ruling shall remain unchanged. 

The Court declines Defendants’ request to review Soldat’s document production in camera.  Copies of the document production provided to the Court by Defendants are to be returned to Defendants. 

 

 

 

 


Case Number: 21NWCV00540    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

MENDOZA vs. INTERINSURANCE EXCHANGE

CASE NO.: 21NWCV00540

Hearing Date: October 1, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

 

#3

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff Xiomara Verela Mendoza’s Motion to Set Aside the Court’s Order deeming matters admitted is GRANTED in part as set forth below. 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

Plaintiff Xiomara Verela Mendoza (“Plaintiff”) alleges that her 2009 Mercedes Benz S550 was stolen from her driveway on or about October 5, 2020.  When the vehicle was recovered a week later, it had been vandalized.  Plaintiff alleges that her insurer, Defendant Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club, Inc. (“Defendant”), has improperly refused to repair or pay her the value of the vehicle pursuant to her automobile insurance policy.  In a Complaint filed on August 25, 2021, Plaintiff brings causes of action for: 1) breach of insurance contract, 2) bad faith breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and 3) Declaratory Relief. 

On February 16, 2023, Defendant served Form Interrogatories (sets one and two), Special Interrogatories (set one), Requests for Admission (set one), and Requests for Production of Documents (set one).  Defendant moved to compel responses after Plaintiff failed to serve responses by the due date. 

A hearing was held on October 11, 2023.  In its ruling, the Court stated, “it is not clear if Plaintiff served responses [to Defendant’s Request for Admissions] prior to the date of this hearing … The Court has not received a copy of Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Requests for Admission, if any such response was served. Therefore, Defendant’s request to Deem Matters Admitted is GRANTED, however, Plaintiff may by the end of this week file with the Court a declaration with her responses if any such response was served prior to the date of this hearing. This Order will become final on Friday October 13, 2023, should no declaration be filed.” (10/11/23 Minute Order.) The Court then took the matter under submission.  Plaintiff did not file a declaration by the end of the week, so the Court’s ruling became final on October 13, 2023. 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to set aside the Court’s October 13, 2023 order deeming matters admitted.  Plaintiff argues the motion should be granted on the basis of excusable neglect. (CCP § 473, subd. (b).)

Legal Standard

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (CCP § 473, subdivision (b).)

The purpose of mandatory relief is to alleviate the hardship on parties who lose their day in court due to an inexcusable failure to act by their attorneys. (Rodriguez v. Brill (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 715, 723.) “Section 473 is often applied liberally where the party in default moves promptly to seek relief, and the party opposing the motion will not suffer prejudice if relief is granted. [Citations.] In such situations ‘very slight evidence will be required to justify a court in setting aside the default.’ [Citations.] [¶] Moreover, because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.” (Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 681, 695.)

Discussion

Here, the Court’s order deeming matters admitted became final on October 13, 2023.  Plaintiff filed the instant motion to set aside the order on April 2, 2024, which is within the six-month time period specified in CCP § 473.  Hence, the motion is timely. 

Plaintiff asserts that responses to Defendant’s Request for Admissions were served on October 5, 2023, six days before the October 11, 2023 hearing. (Motion, Ex. A.) Counsel for Plaintiff, Vincent Davis, states that “a staff attorney” attended the October 11, 2023 hearing on Defendant’s motion to compel discovery responses. (Davis Decl., ¶3.) On October 13, 2023, Davis received an email containing a copy of the Court’s order. “At the time I received the email, I was deeply involved in pursuing another matter, and was unable to review the email on receipt. At the end of the day, the receipt of the Order had slipped my mind and I did not review or see the order that day.” (Id., ¶5.) On Monday, October 16, 2023, I reviewed all my emails pending and it was then I discovered that it was too late to supply copies of Plaintiff's responses to Defendant's Requests for Admission, which we had served on to Defendant's counsel's office on October 5, 2023. (Id., ¶6.) “My failure to review that email was the consequence of my inadvertent neglect in not reviewing the pertinent email on receipt.” (Id., ¶7.) 

Defendant argues, among other things, that Plaintiff’s counsel admits to receiving notice of the Court’s ruling on October 13, 2023, but counsel waited nearly six months before filing the instant motion.  Defendant also argues it will be prejudiced if the Court grants the requested relief because Defendant can no longer prevail on its motion for judgment on the pleadings set for October 10, 2024. 

Because the motion is timely, and it remains unclear whether Plaintiff served responses before the hearing date, the Court finds excusable neglect under CCP § 473.  However, it is undisputed that Plaintiff has not responded to Defendant’s Request for Admissions No. 4 (“Admit that the EXCHANGE is not guilty of malice, oppression or fraud under Civil Code, section 3294.”) Thus, Plaintiff’s constructive admission to that request shall stand.   

Accordingly, the motion to set aside the Court’s October 13, 2023 order deeming matters admitted is GRANTED in part as set forth above.  Plaintiff to serve responses to Defendant’s Request for Admissions (Set One) Nos. 1-3, without objections, within 10 days.

Case Number: 22NWCV01596    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

A.F., et al. v. Casarez et. al.

Case No.: 22NWCV01596

Hearing Date: October 1, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

 

#4

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiffs A.F.’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part as set forth below.

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

Plaintiff A.F., a minor, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, FRANCISCO A. MIGUEL ESTEBAN (“Plaintiff”) alleges multiple statutory and regulatory violations committed by Defendant County of Los Angeles and its employees in the investigation and response to repeated incidents of physical abuse committed by his foster parents, Defendants Gabriela Casarez (“Casarez”) and Charlie Lopez Cruz (“Cruz”).  Plaintiff was 4 years old at the time. 

On August 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting causes of action for (1) Negligence; (2) Negligence; (3) Breach of Mandatory Duties/Negligence Per Se; (4) Breach of Mandatory Duties; (5) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (6) Declaratory Relief against the County Defendants.

On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff served Request for Production, Set One, on Defendant. The requests at issue are Requests Nos. 68-123. Request Nos. 68-98 seek various policies and procedures applicable to the County’s Department of Children and Family Services (“DCFS”) and its social workers. Request Nos. 99-122 seek documents contained within the County social workers’ confidential employment files.  Request No. 123 seeks a privilege log in the event Defendant withholds any documents or communications based on a claim of privilege, work product, or otherwise. 

Legal Standard

“(a) The party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling has been directed shall respond separately to each item or category of item by any of the following: (1) A statement that the party will comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling by the date set for the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (c) of Section 2031.030 and any related activities; (2) A representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of a particular item or category of item; (3) An objection to the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.” (CCP § 2031.210.)

“A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (CCP § 2031.230.)

“(a) On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (CCP § 2031.310.)

Discussion

Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) was served upon Defendant on February 9, 2024. (Innabi Decl., ¶13.) Defendant served its initial responses on May 24, 2024. (Id., ¶14.) Defendant served supplemental responses on July 10, 2024. (Id., ¶18.) The instant motion was filed on July 24, 2024.

Request for Production of Documents Nos. 68-98

Request for Production of Documents (“RFP”) Nos. 68-98 seek various policies and procedures applicable to the County’s Department of Children and Family Services (“DCFS”) and its social workers.

In Opposition, Defendant argues that it has fully complied with these requests because it has directed Plaintiff to websites which contain the materials. (Weiss Decl., ¶9.) Plaintiff does not dispute this assertion in Reply.  Thus, the Court determines that Defendant’s response to RFP Nos. 68-98 substantially complies with the Code.   

The motion to compel further response to RFP Nos. 68-98 is DENIED.

Request for Production of Documents Nos. 99-122

RFP Nos. 99-122 seek documents contained within the County social workers’ confidential employment files. 

Defendant has agreed to provide responsive documents to these requests subject to a protective order. (Weiss Decl., ¶15.) On July 17, 2024, Defendant’s counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a draft protective order. (Id., ¶16.) However, Plaintiff is only willing to sign the protective order when Defendant discloses all the documents at issue. (Reply, p. 4:14-16.) Plaintiff’s position is not well taken.  The Court determines that Defendant’s response to RFP Nos. 99-122 complies with the Code.   

The motion to compel further response to RFP Nos. 99-122 is DENIED.  Defendant shall disclose responsive documents after Plaintiff executes a stipulated protective order.

Request for Production of Documents No. 123

Request No. 123 seeks a privilege log in the event Defendant withholds any documents or communications based on a claim of privilege, work product, or otherwise. 

Defendant argues that this request seeks documents and information which can only be obtained from the juvenile dependency court pursuant to WIC § 827. Plaintiff argues that the documents requested in RFP Nos. 68-98 (policies and procedures) and RFP Nos. 99-122 (social workers’ confidential employment files) are not documents that can be obtained from a juvenile case file pursuant to WIC § 827.  Plaintiff is correct. 

“For purposes of this section, a “juvenile case file” means a petition filed in a juvenile court proceeding, reports of the probation officer, and all other records, including any writing as defined in Section 250 of the Evidence Code, or electronically stored information relating to the minor, that is filed in that case or made available to the probation officer in making the probation officer’s report, or to the judge, referee, or other hearing officer, and thereafter retained by the probation officer, judge, referee, or other hearing officer.) (WIC § 827, subd. (e).)

Social worker employment files are not listed as records contained within a “juvenile case file.”  Thus, they cannot be obtained by way of a Section 827 petition. 

The motion to compel further response to RFP No. 123 is GRANTED.  Defendant to provide further response within 45 days, absent an agreement by the parties to a later date. 

 


Case Number: 23NWCV01008    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

KENNETH CRAWFORD, et al. vs ADVANCE GROUP 69-89 ETAT

Case No.: 23NWCV01008

Hearing Date: October 1, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

 

#2

Tentative Ruling

Defendant Advance Group 06-89’s unopposed Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Set Aside Entry of Default is GRANTED.  Defendant to answer within 10 days. 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

In a Complaint filed on April 3, 2023, Plaintiffs KENNETH CRAWFORD and JAMOD RAMBO (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendant ADVANCED GROUP 06-89 d/b/a NORTHWIND APARTMENT (“Defendant”) for “harassment, fictitious filing documents, discrimination fraud, and defamation.” (Complaint, ¶1.)

Defendant moves to quash service of summons and set aside entry of default on the grounds that the summons and complaint were not properly served upon Defendant’s registered agent for service.  The Court has granted relief for Defendant on the same grounds before. 

Default was first entered against Defendant on May 8, 2023.  On October 17, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to quash service of summons and set aside default due to improper service.  Plaintiffs served the summons at 16227 Eucalyptus Avenue #38, Bellflower, CA 90706, but the business address for Defendant’s registered agent for service of process, Richard J. Julian, is 15320 Barranca Parkway, Suite 100, Irvine, CA 92618. (10/17/23 Minute Order (incorrectly noting that service was purportedly made at “16627 Eucalyptus Avenue”).)

On November 11, 2023, Plaintiffs filed an Amendment to Complaint naming “ADVANCED GROUP 69-89 ETAT” as the correct entity instead of Northwind Apartment. 

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs effectuated substitute service upon “LAURA KEEFER APARTMENT MANAGER” at 16227 Eucalyptus Avenue, #38, Bellflower, CA 90706.  (Amended Proof of Service filed May 20, 2024.)

On May 20, 2024, default was entered. 

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiffs filed proof of service by mail upon Defendant at 16227 Eucalyptus Avenue, #38, Bellflower, CA 90706. 

The instant motion was filed on July 5, 2024.

Legal Standard

“A defendant . . . may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:  (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a).)  “[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.]” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.)  “[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper” but only if it “complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.”  (Id. at pp. 1441-1442.)  When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.”  (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.)  “A court lacks jurisdiction over a party if there has not been proper service of process.”  (Ruttenberg v. Ruttenberg (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 801, 808.) 

Discussion

Defendant argues service of summons was once again improper because the summons should have been served upon Defendant’s registered agent for service of process, Richard J. Julian, at 15320 Barranca Parkway, Suite 100, Irvine, CA 92618. (Chang Decl., ¶6, Ex. 4.) Instead, Plaintiffs effectuated substitute service upon “LAURA KEEFER APARTMENT MANAGER” at 16227 Eucalyptus Avenue, #38, Bellflower, CA 90706. 

Plaintiffs have not opposed the motion.  The Court finds that Plaintiffs have not satisfied their burden of demonstrating that service was properly effectuated upon Defendant. 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to quash service of summons is GRANTED.

CCP § 473(d) provides in pertinent part: “The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.(d).)

For the reasons stated above, entry of default is SET ASIDE AND VACATED.  Defendant to answer within 10 days.

Case Number: 23NWCV04114    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

MARIA EUGENIA TELLEZ vs MERCEDES-BENZ USA, LLC, et al.

Case No.: 23NWCV04114

Hearing Date: October 1, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

 

#ADD-ON

Tentative Ruling

Defendant Jones Ontario Acquisition, LLC dba Mercedes-Benz of Ontario’s unopposed Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

This is a lemon law action. In the operative Complaint filed on December 18, 2023, Plaintiff Maria Eugenia Tellez (“Plaintiff”) sues Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC and Defendant Mercedes-Benz of Ontario (“Ontario”) for the following: (1) Violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(d); (2) Violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(b); (3) Violation of Civil Code § 1793(a)(3); (4) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; and (5) Negligent Repair.  

As relevant here, the only cause of action against Ontario is the fifth cause of action for Negligent Repair.  In that regard, Plaintiff alleges that she “delivered the Subject Vehicle to Defendant Ontario for substantial repair on at least one occasion.” (Complaint ¶ 56.) Plaintiff pleads that “Defendant ONTARIO breached its duty to Plaintiff to use ordinary care and skill by failing to properly store, prepare and repair the Subject Vehicle in accordance with industry standards.” (Complaint ¶ 58.) Plaintiff further pleads “Defendant ONTARIO's negligent breach of its duties owed to Plaintiff was a proximate cause of Plaintiff's damages.” (Complaint ¶ 59.)

Ontario moves for Summary Judgment on the grounds that the fifth cause of action for negligent repair is barred under the economic loss rule. 

No opposition filed as of September 26, 2024. 

Legal Standard

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”¿(Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843 (Aguilar); Code Civ. Proc., § 437c (c).)¿ A plaintiff reaches its burden on summary judgment by showing prima facie evidence for each element of its cause of action.¿ Code Civ. Proc, § 437c (p); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (Scalf).¿ The burden will then shift to the defendant to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact for at least one element of the cause of action at issue.¿(Ibid.) 

 

Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment or summary adjudication and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿(Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿However, if all inferences reasonably deducible from the submitted evidence are uncontradicted by other inferences and there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the moving party is entitled to summary adjudication as a matter of law.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c (c); Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

Discussion

“Economic loss consists of damages for inadequate value, costs of repair and replacement of the defective product or consequent loss of profits – without any claim of personal injury or damages to other property.” (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 988.) “[W]here a purchaser’s expectations in a sale are frustrated because the product he bought is not working properly, his [or her] remedy is said to be in contract alone, for he [or she] has suffered only economic losses.” (Ibid.)

Ontario argues that the fifth cause of action for negligent repair is barred under the economic loss rule because Plaintiff does not allege personal injury or property damage. 

In support of the motion, Ontario presents evidence of the following:  

·        On March 26, Plaintiff Maria Eugenia Tellez entered into a warranty contract with defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (“MBUSA”) regarding a 2022 Mercedes-Benz GLA vehicle (the “vehicle”), which was manufactured or distributed by MBUSA. (SS # 1.)

·        Defects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves within the warranty period. (SS # 2.)

·        MBUSA and its representatives were unable to repair the vehicle to conform to the express warranty after a reasonable number of opportunities. (SS # 3.)

·        Plaintiff did not suffer any personal injuries as a result of MBO’s alleged negligent repairs. (SS # 6.)

·        Plaintiff did not suffer any property damages as a result of MBO’s alleged negligent repairs. (SS # 7.)

Based thereon, the Court finds that Ontario has made a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists.  The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact.  As the motion is unopposed, Plaintiff fails to meet her burden of proof.   

Accordingly, Ontario’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Case Number: 24NWCV01845    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

RAFAEL ARROYO vs FIRESTONE PLAZA, LLC, et al.

Case No.: 24NWCV01845

Hearing Date: October 1, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

 

#5

Tentative Ruling

Defendant Firestone Plaza, LLC’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.

Defendant’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

Plaintiff Rafael Arroyo (“Plaintiff”) has a spinal injury that significantly impairs or limits his ability to walk. He uses a wheelchair for mobility. (Complaint, ¶20.) On December 27, 2023, Plaintiff visited Bobo’s Burgers located at 2709 Firestone Blvd., South Gate, CA 90280 and encountered barriers to access. (Id., ¶¶5, 22.) Plaintiff sues Defendant Firestone Plaza, LLC (owner of the land) and B Four Corporation (owner of the restaurant) for damages and injunctive relief based upon violations of Americans with Disabilities Act and Unruh Civil Rights Act.

Defendant Firestone Plaza, LLC (“Defendant”) demurs on the ground that the second cause of action, Violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act, fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant.  Defendant also moves to strike language in the Complaint pertaining to injunctive relief. 

Judicial Notice

Defendant’s request for judicial notice of the list of cases commenced by plaintiff in Los Angeles Superior Court, pursuant to Evidence Code §452(d). Judicial Notice is GRANTED.

Discussion

Demurrer

As a general matter, a party may respond to a pleading against it by demurrer on the basis of any single or combination of eight enumerated grounds, including that “the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿ 

“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)¿ 

Civil Code Section 51, subdivision (b), states in pertinent part, “All persons within the jurisdiction of this state are free and equal, and no matter what their sex, race, color, religion, ancestry, national origin, disability, medical condition, genetic information, marital status, sexual orientation, citizenship, primary language, or immigration status are entitled to the full and equal accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, or services in all business establishments of every kind whatsoever.” (Civ. Code § 51, subd. (b); see also 42 U.S.C.A. § 12182, subd. (a).) 

 

“(a) An allegation of a construction-related accessibility claim in a complaint, as defined in subdivision (a) of Section 55.52 of the Civil Code, shall state facts sufficient to allow a reasonable person to identify the basis of the violation or violations supporting the claim, including all of the following: (1) A plain language explanation of the specific access barrier or barriers the individual encountered, or by which the individual alleges he or she was deterred, with sufficient information about the location of the alleged barrier to enable a reasonable person to identify the access barrier […] (2) The way in which the barrier denied the individual full and equal use or access, or in which it deterred the individual, on each particular occasion […] (3) The date or dates of each particular occasion on which the claimant encountered the specific access barrier, or on which he or she was deterred […] (4) (A) Except in complaints that allege physical injury or damage to property, a complaint filed by or on behalf of a high-frequency litigant shall also state all of the following: 

(i) Whether the complaint is filed by, or on behalf of, a high-frequency litigant. 

(ii) In the case of a high-frequency litigant who is a plaintiff, the number of complaints alleging a construction-related accessibility claim that the high-frequency litigant has filed during the 12 months prior to filing the complaint. 

(iii) In the case of a high-frequency litigant who is a plaintiff, the reason the individual was in the geographic area of the defendant’s business. 

(iv) In the case of a high-frequency litigant who is a plaintiff, the reason why the individual desired to access the defendant’s business, including the specific commercial, business, personal, social, leisure, recreational, or other purpose. 

(B) As used in this section “high-frequency litigant” has the same meaning as set forth in subdivision (b) of Section 425.55.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.50, subd. (a).) 

 

Here, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Complaint is too vague in that it does not adequately specify what barriers were encountered or when Plaintiff encounter the barrier.  The Complaint alleges, among other things, that Plaintiff has a spinal injury that significantly impairs or limits his ability to walk. (Complaint, ¶20.) On December 27, 2023, Plaintiff went to the establishment and discovered that the tables were non-compliant because they utilize a fixed-in-place booth-style design that inhibits the ability to fully pull under the table while using a wheelchair. (Id., ¶37.)

The Court determines that the Complaint sufficiently explains the specific access barriers Plaintiff encountered, the location of the alleged barrier, the way in which the barrier denied Plaintiff full and equal use or access, and the date or dates of each particular occasion on which the claimant encountered the specific access barrier.

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff, a high-frequency litigant, fails to comply with the requirements for high-frequency litigants by failing to identify the reason why he was in the geographic area and why he desired access to the business.  The Complaint alleges Plaintiff was in the geographic area because he lives there. (Id., ¶50.) Plaintiff was interested in the particular business because he wanted to eat there. (Id., ¶51.) The Court finds that the Complaint states sufficient facts to support a cause of action for Violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act under Civil Code Section 51.   

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED. 

Motion to Strike

Upon motion, or at any time in its own discretion, a court may grant a motion to strike any of the following material: (1) irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) all or any part of any pleading not drawn in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. §436.) These provisions also authorize “the striking of a pleading due to improprieties in its form or in the procedures pursuant to which it was filed.” (Ferraro v. Camarlinghi, (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 528, emphasis in original.)

Defendant moves to strike language in the Complaint pertaining to injunctive relief: page 4, ¶17 and page 11, ¶¶74-77.  Defendant argues that under Civil Code § 3368, preventative injunctive relief may only prohibit a party from doing that which ought not to be done (i.e., a prohibitory injunction).  A plaintiff is not entitled to injunctive relief compelling Defendant to take action to modify property.  As Plaintiff points out, however, the Complaint does not seek injunctive relief under state law; rather, the Complaint seeks injunctive relief under the ADA (See Prayer, ¶74 (“For permanent injunctive relief, pursuant to and appropriate to comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act …”).) Injunctive relief is available for violations of the ADA. (42 U.S.C. §12188(a)(2).)  Thus, the state law authority cited by Defendant is inapplicable. 

The motion to strike is DENIED.

Case Number: 24NWCV02923    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: C

Cooper vs. Optum Services, Inc., et al., Case No. 24NWCV02923

 

This is an employment case. It is also a continuance. Plaintiff moves ex parte for a temporary restraining order and order to show cause re preliminary injunction to prevent the arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims.

 

“[T]rial courts should evaluate two interrelated factors when deciding whether to issue [a restraining order]. The first is the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial.¿The second is the interim harm that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the [restraining order] were denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the [order] were issued."¿(Church of Christ in Hollywood v. Super. Ct. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1244, 1251.)¿“The trial court’s determination must be guided by a 'mix' of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support [a restraining order] . . ..¿ Of course, ‘[t]he scope of available preliminary relief is necessarily limited by the scope of the relief likely to be obtained at trial on the merits.’¿ . . .¿ A trial court may not grant a [restraining order], regardless of the balance of interim harm, unless there is some possibility that the plaintiff would ultimately prevail on the merits of the claim.”¿(Id. at pp. 1251-1252.)¿
