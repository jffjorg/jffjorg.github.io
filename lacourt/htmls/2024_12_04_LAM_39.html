DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV21002    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: 39

  TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

December 4, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV21002

MOTION

Special Motion to Strike

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Rolling Hills Covenant Church, Clyde League, Sam Tabari, Craig Knickerbocker, Sam Evans, and John Thill

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Daniel A. Burgoyne

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Daniel A. Burgoyne (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Rolling Hills Covenant Church (“Rolling Hills”), Clyde LaGue, Sam Tabari, Craig Knickerbocker, Sam Evans, and John Thrill (collectively, “Defendants”), alleging Defendants wrongfully terminated his employment as pastor at Rolling Hills.  Defendants filed this special motion to strike Plaintiff’s complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

In ruling on a defendant's special motion to strike, the trial court uses a two-step process.  First, the defendant must show the act or acts of which the plaintiff complains were taken in furtherance of the defendant's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitutions in connection with a public issue.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(1).)  If the defendant carries that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(3).) 

In the instant case, Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action are based on claims Defendants posted statements “online for the entire world to see.”  (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶¶ 408, 429.)  Claims based on “any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest” are subject to a special motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e)(3).) “The Internet is a classic public forum . . . .” (Chaker v. Mateo (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1138, 1146.) As Plaintiff’s claims are based on Defendants’ online posts, Plaintiff’s claims are based on Defendants’ statements in a public forum. However, Defendants have not shown that Defendants’ statements were in connection with a public issue.

 

“At a sufficiently high level of generalization, any conduct can appear rationally related to a broader issue of public importance.  What a court scrutinizing the nature of speech in the anti-SLAPP context must focus on is the speech at hand, rather than the prospects that such speech may conceivably have indirect consequences for an issue of public concern.” (Rand Resources, LLC v. City of Carson (2019) 6 Cal.5th 610, 625.)  Conceivably, the issue of improper conduct by clergy or the termination of members of the clergy could be a matter of public concern.  However, no evidence before the Court suggests the issue of Plaintiff’s conduct is a matter of public concern.  Defendant advances evidence Plaintiff maintains a website and has 1,500 followers on Facebook.  (See Declaration of Robert A. Hufnagel, Exhibits E, F.)  That Plaintiff maintains a modest internet following does not make Plaintiff a public figure such that his termination is a matter of public concern.  Accordingly, Defendants fail to show Defendants’ online statements fall within the special motion to strike statute.

Plaintiff’s other claims are based on his termination from Rolling Hills.  Claims based on “conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest” are subject to a special motion to strike.  (Civ. Proc. Code § 425.16, subd. (e)(4).)  Defendant contends the First Amendment bars Plaintiff’s claims.  Generally, nonprofit religious associations or corporations are not employers for purposes of the Fair Employment and Housing Act.  (Gov. Code, § 12926, subd. (d); Cal. Code Regs., tit. 2, § 11008, subd. (d)(5); Henry v. Red Hill Evangelical Lutheran Church of Tustin (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1049.)  This doctrine is called the ministerial exception. 

“The ministerial exception protects religious organizations from the normally attendant adverse consequences of employment discrimination.  The exception is constitutionally compelled and arises out of the establishment and free exercise clauses of the First Amendment.  [¶]  The ministerial exception doctrine is based on the notion a church's appointment of its clergy, along with such closely related issues as clerical salaries, assignments, working conditions and termination of employment, is an inherently religious function because clergy are such an integral part of a church's functioning as a religious institution.  Therefore, secular courts will not attempt to right wrongs related to the hiring, firing, discipline or administration of clergy.  Implicit in this statement of the rule is the acknowledgment that such wrongs may exist, that they may be severe, and that the administration of the church itself may be inadequate to provide a remedy.  The preservation of the free exercise of religion is deemed so important a principle as to overshadow the inequities which may result from its liberal application.  In our society, jealous as it is of separation of church and state, one who enters the clergy forfeits the protection of the civil authorities in terms of job rights.”  (Henry v. Red Hill Evangelical Lutheran Church of Tustin (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1053-1054, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

However, the Court need not reach the issue of whether the ministerial exception bars Plaintiff’s claims, as Defendants fail to show Plaintiff’s termination was in connection with a public issue.  The Court notes Defendant argues for the first time in its reply brief Plaintiff’s termination received news coverage.  Defendant’s purported evidence of this fact does not show any such news coverage.  (See Hufnagel Decl., Exhibit F.)  In any event, the Court cannot consider this argument because Plaintiff has not had an opportunity to respond to it.  (In re Marriage of Hoffmeister (1984) 161 Cal.App.3d 1163, 1171.)  As such, the special motion to strike is denied.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

CASE NUMBER:

MOTION: 

Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Rosa Hernandez

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendants Henry Shahery, an individual and as trustee of the H&N Shahery Revocable Trust, Niloufar Shahery, Shason Inc., and HSH Management Group, Inc.

 

MOTIONS

            Plaintiff Rosa Hernandez (“Plaintiffs”) moves to compel further responses from Defendants Henry Shahery, an individual and as trustee of the H&N Shahery Revocable Trust, Niloufar Shahery, Shason Inc., and HSH Management Group, Inc. (“Defendants”) to Special Interrogatories, set two, No. 8 (“SROG”).  Defendants oppose the motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300.) 

Plaintiff’s SROG, which are the subject of Plaintiff’s motions, require Defendants to identify documents and individuals with knowledge pertaining to Defendants’ financial condition.  Defendants served objections to the SROG.  As Plaintiff timely filed these motions to compel further responses, Defendants have the burden to justify their objections.  (Fairmont Insurance Company. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  In opposing the motions, Defendants argue the SROG improperly seek information about their financial conditions.    

Civil Code section 3295 (section 3295), subdivision (c) provides, in pertinent part, “[n]o pretrial discovery by the plaintiff shall be permitted with respect to evidence of [the defendant’s profits or financial condition] unless the court enters an order permitting such discovery. . . . However, the plaintiff may subpoena documents or witnesses to be available at the trial for the purpose of establishing the profits or financial condition . . . and the defendant may be required to identify documents in the defendant’s possession which are relevant and admissible for that purpose and the witnesses employed by or related to the defendant who would be most competent to testify to those facts. . . . the court may at any time enter an order permitting the discovery otherwise prohibited . . . if the court finds, on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented, that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294.”  (Cal. Civ. Code, § 3295, subd. (c) (emphasis added).)

Plaintiff has pleaded she is entitled to punitive and exemplary damages.  However, under section 3295, subdivision (c), before this Court may order the pretrial discovery Plaintiff seeks, the Court must find Plaintiff “has established that there is a substantial probability” Plaintiff will prevail on her punitive damages claim.  Based on the record before this Court, Plaintiff has not satisfied that requirement at this stage in the litigation.  Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for pretrial discovery compelling Defendants to further respond to the subject SROG is unsupported.  Section 3295, subdivision (c) certainly permits Plaintiff to subpoena documents and witnesses “to be available at the trial”; but Plaintiff’s request through her motions is for Defendants to provide specific information pertaining to their financial condition now, rather than such information being available at trial.[1] 

Despite Plaintiff’s conjecture seeking profit and financial condition information after she has established Defendants’ liability may be too late for purposes of being awarded punitive damages, such supposition is not a sufficient basis under the statute for the Court to order Defendants to identify documents in their possession at this time versus having such documents available at the trial of this action.  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to the SROG are denied.

Under the circumstances, the Court declines to impose sanctions because such an award would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to the SROG are denied. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


[1]Moreover, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1987, subdivisions (b) and (c), a party can serve on another party a notice to appear in lieu of a subpoena at least 20 days before the time required for attendance at trial that includes a request for that party to bring books, documents, and other information to the trial.  Therefore, section 3295, subdivision (c) and Code of Civil Procedure section 1987 provide appropriate mechanisms for a party to obtain the information necessary to establish a defendant’s profits and financial condition to support a claim for punitive damages.

  Defendant’s notice of motion indicates Defendant filed a memorandum of points and authorities and submitted declarations from Jaime Ortega and Mainak D’Attaray in support of its motion.  However, Defendant did not file these documents with the Court.  The Court cannot grant a motion without a memorandum of points and authorities, as such a motion is incomplete.  (Cal.
