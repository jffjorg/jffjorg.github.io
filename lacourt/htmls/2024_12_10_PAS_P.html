DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22BBCV00516    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING HCMC’S MOTION TO COMPEL NON-PARTY TRU STAFFING PARTNERS, INC. TO COMPLY WITH HCMC’S SUBPOENA FOR BUSINESS RECORDS and DENYING MONETARY SANCTIONS, and GRANTING MONETARY SANCTIONS TO TRU STAFFING PARTNERS, INC.

I.          INTRODUCTION

This is a breach of contract action initiated on July 15, 2022, by Plaintiff HCMC Legal Inc. ("HCMC" or “Plaintiff”) against Defendants Brian Hartstein (“Hartstein”) and Epiq Systems Inc., Epiq Systems Solutions Inc., and Epiq Systems Acquisition Inc. (“Epiq”) (collectively, “Defendants”) HCMC alleges that Hartstein breached his employment agreement and fiduciary duties by resigning without notice and subsequently joining Epiq, a direct competitor. HCMC further contends that Hartstein solicited HCMC’s clients and former employees, thereby causing significant harm to its business operations.

On November 7, 2024, HCMC filed a motion to compel non-party Tru Staffing Partners, Inc. (“Tru Staffing”) to comply with a subpoena to produce business records and requested monetary sanctions. Tru Staffing, a staffing and recruiting agency, is alleged to have facilitated Hartstein’s transition to Epiq, including assisting with negotiations related to Hartstein’s employment and interactions concerning HCMC’s clients. On November 25, 2024, Tru Staffing filed its opposition to the motion, and on December 3, 2024, HCMC filed its reply.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Any party may obtain discovery by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.010.)¿Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, “the court, upon motion reasonably made [by a party] . . . may make an order . . . directing compliance with [a subpoena] upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare . . .” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1(a).)¿Likewise, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480 subdivision (a), “[i]f a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.480(a).)¿If a nonparty deponent, properly served with a deposition subpoena, fails to attend a deposition, the court may impose¿sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1987.2, 2020.240, 2025.440(b), 2025.480(j).)

III.      DISCUSSION

HCMC brings this motion to compel compliance with a subpoena served on non-party Tru Staffing and seeks monetary sanctions. Tru Staffing opposes the motion, asserting that it is procedurally flawed due to untimeliness and fails to comply with statutory requirements. In addition, Tru Staffing requests monetary sanctions and terminating sanctions against HCMC and its counsel for alleged misuse of the discovery process. 

Procedural issues

Tru Staffing contends that HCMC’s motion to compel compliance with the subpoena is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480 subdivision (b), which sets a mandatory 60-day deadline for such motions.

Tru Staffing argues that the record was completed on March 1, 2024, when it served its objections to HCMC’s subpoena. Citing Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 136, Tru Staffing asserts that the 60-day statutory deadline began on this date because objections complete the record of the deposition for document requests. Accordingly, Tru Staffing maintains the motion to compel was due by May 2, 2024, inclusive of the two-court-day extension for electronic service under Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (a)(4). HCMC’s motion, filed on November 7, 2024—189 days after the deadline—was therefore untimely. (Opposition, pp.8-9)

HCMC, however, argues that the motion is timely because of the discovery stay issued by the court on April 28, 2024, which HCMC asserts tolled all discovery-related deadlines, including the 60-day period to file motions to compel. (Motion, p.8) HCMC further asserts that it reasonably relied on the understanding that the stay applied to its subpoena motion while it prioritized resolving discovery disputes with Defendants. (Reply, pp. 4-6)

The Court finds that HCMC motion to compel compliance with the subpoena is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480, subdivision (b), which imposes a mandatory 60-day deadline for filing such motions. Tru Staffing served its objections to HCMC’s subpoena on March 1, 2024, Accordingly, the statutory deadline to file a motion to compel compliance was May 2, 2024, inclusive of the two-court-day extension for electronic service under Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (a)(4). HCMC’s motion, filed on November 7, 2024, was 189 days past the statutory deadline.

The Court rejects the argument that the discovery stay issued on April 28, 2024, tolled all discovery-related deadlines, including those applicable to non-parties like Tru Staffing. The scope of the stay was expressly limited to specific, pending motions involving the parties, as outlined in Attachment 4B to the order appointing a discovery referee. None of these motions pertained to the subpoena directed to Tru Staffing. (Lubrano Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. G) Additionally, the stay order explicitly stated that “the parties have agreed to stay the deadlines for the aforementioned discovery motions,” indicating that the stay applied only to the motions specifically enumerated in the order. Tru Staffing, as a non-party, was not referenced in the order. Moreover, since the parties did not stipulate to any tolling or extension of HCMC’s mandatory deadline, the motion is untimely.

Furthermore, HCMC’s motion was not pending at the time the discovery stay was issued. The April 28, 2024, order was limited to then-existing motions and did not extend to motions filed subsequently. Without clear authority or language applying the stay to third-party disputes, HCMC’s reliance on the stay to justify its delay is misplaced. 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES HCMC’s motion for untimeliness.

Monetary Sanctions

HCMC request for monetary sanctions:

HCMC seeks monetary sanctions of $8,625.00, Given that the motion to compel is untimely, the Court DENIES HCMC’s request for discovery sanctions.

 

Tru Staffing request for monetary sanctions:

Tru Staffing seeks $36,459.50 in monetary sanctions against HCMC and its counsel pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.020 and 2023.030. Tru Staffing argues that HCMC failed to comply with its statutory obligation to meet and confer in good faith before filing its motion, constituting a misuse of the discovery process. (Opposition, p. 12)

According to the declaration of Joelle A. Berle, Tru Staffing’s lead attorney, the fees incurred include at least 22.8 hours spent by Ms. Berle preparing the opposition and supporting documents at an hourly rate of $840, and 21.5 hours by senior associate Alexis Cruz at a rate of $805. Additionally, Ms. Berle anticipates spending three hours preparing for, traveling to and from, and attending the hearing, amounting to an additional $2,520. In total, these efforts result in $36,459.50 in fees for opposing the motion. (Berle Decl., ¶ 14)

Tru Staffing also seeks an additional $21,757.50 in monetary sanctions for fees associated with reviewing and analyzing the subpoena, assessing the claims and scope of the document requests, meeting and conferring with HCMC’s counsel, and conducting related legal research. These fees reflect 20.8 hours by Ms. Berle at $840 per hour, 6.3 hours by junior associate Sally Kim at $545 per hour, and 0.8 hours by senior partner Ted Jacobs at $1,065 per hour. Tru Staffing argues that these fees were necessary due to HCMC’s overbroad and improper subpoena. (Berle Decl., ¶ 15; Opposition, p. 14)

Tru Staffing contends that HCMC’s subpoena improperly sought documents that were already produced by Defendants, including 12 of the 13 document requests targeting communications between Tru Staffing and either Defendant Epiq or Defendant Hartstein. These unredacted records were produced by Defendant Hartstein on October 8, 2024, pursuant to the Discovery Referee’s orders. Tru Staffing further argues that the subpoena was vague, ambiguous, overbroad, and sought protected information outside the permissible scope of discovery. (Lubrano Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A; Berle Decl., ¶¶ 6-7)

Tru Staffing characterizes the subpoena as a fishing expedition for documents already in HCMC’s possession, causing unwarranted annoyance, oppression, and undue burden on Tru Staffing as a non-party. It asserts that HCMC’s conduct violates Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010 and 2023.030, which prohibit discovery misuse and authorize monetary sanctions. (Opposition, pp. 12, 14)

The Court finds that HCMC failed to meet and confer in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2016.040 and 2025.480, subdivision (b). While HCMC asserts that it believed discovery deadlines were stayed, this misunderstanding does not excuse its obligation to engage in meaningful discussions with Tru Staffing to resolve the issues informally. The obligation to meet and confer was particularly critical given the alleged ambiguity surrounding the discovery stay, which did not explicitly include non-parties like Tru Staffing. Evidence presented demonstrates that HCMC’s communication with Tru Staffing was minimal and failed to meet the statutory requirement for good faith efforts.

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.020, monetary sanctions are mandatory for failure to comply with the meet-and-confer requirement. However, the Court exercises discretion in determining the reasonableness of the amount sought. While Tru Staffing requests $36,459.50 and an additional $21,757.50 in attorneys’ fees, the Court finds this amount excessive given the scope and complexity of the motion. The Court reduces the sanctions award to $20,000, reflecting reasonable expenses incurred in opposing the motion.

 

Terminating Sanctions

The Court notes that terminating sanctions are a severe remedy and should only be imposed in circumstances where a party’s noncompliance is willful, persistent, and without reasonable justification, and where lesser sanctions would be ineffective in ensuring compliance. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246; Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.) 

Here, while Tru Staffing raises concerns about HCMC’s discovery conduct, the record does not demonstrate the level of willful or persistent misconduct necessary to justify terminating sanctions. Lesser sanctions, such as monetary penalties, remain sufficient to address the issues raised and to ensure compliance with discovery obligations.

 Accordingly, the Court DENIES terminating sanctions.

 

            IV. CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel and motion for sanctions.  The Court DENIES Tru Staffing’s request for terminating sanctions. The Court GRANTS Tru Staffing’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $20,000, payable by Plaintiff within 30 days of this order.

The Court declines to issue a ruling regarding the second subpoena, as the matter is not before the Court at this time.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS and GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS AS TO DEFENDANT EPIQ EDISCOVERY SOLUTIONS, INC.

I.        INTRODUCTION

This is a breach of contract action initiated on July 15, 2022, by Plaintiff HCMC Legal Inc. ("Hire Counsel" or “Plaintiff”) against Defendants Brian Hartstein (“Hartstein”) and Epiq Systems Inc., Epiq Systems Solutions Inc., and Epiq Systems Acquisition Inc. (collectively, “Epiq” or “Defendant”). Plaintiff alleges that Hartstein breached his employment agreement and fiduciary duties by resigning without notice and subsequently joining Epiq, a direct competitor. Plaintiff further contends that Hartstein solicited Hire Counsel’s clients and former employees, thereby causing significant harm to its business operations.

On October 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for sanctions. Defendants filed their opposition on November 7, 2024, and Plaintiff submitted a reply on November 13, 2024.

II.       LEGAL STANDARD

California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 permits courts to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. Section 2023.010 provides a non-exhaustive list of conduct that is considered misuse of the discovery process, including as relevant here, failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Cal. Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).) 

Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 subdivision (d) gives courts the authority to issue a terminating sanction by one of four following orders: (1) an order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in misuse of the discovery process; (2) an order staying further proceedings until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) an order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; or (4) an order rendering a default judgment against that party. 

In determining whether to impose terminating sanctions, trial courts must consider the totality of the circumstances, including whether the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of informal and formal attempts to obtain the discovery. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App,4th 1225, 1246.) 

The trial court has broad discretion in selecting discovery sanctions, subject to reversal only for abuse. (Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1293; Miranda v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 913, 928–929.)¿ The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery and, in choosing a sanction, should “attempt [] to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.” (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 36.) 

The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.¿ “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange, 231 Cal.App.3d at p. 487.) ¿If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted; continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.¿ (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal. App. 4th 967, 992.) 

 

III.     DISCUSSION

Plaintiff brings the instant motion seeking terminating sanctions and monetary sanctions of $15,000, alleging Defendant has engaged in a prolonged pattern of discovery abuse. Plaintiff contends that over the past year, Defendant has repeatedly failed to comply with its discovery obligations and has violated the Court’s September 4, 2024, order. Despite being given numerous opportunities to comply, Defendant has consistently delayed production, providing only partial responses that Plaintiff deems insufficient and incomplete. Plaintiff asserts that Defendant has failed to produce code-compliant responses required by statute and court order and remains in violation of the Court’s order. To date, Defendant has not provided the mandated supplemental responses, failed to produce required documents, and has not corrected deficiencies in its privilege log. Defendant has made no substantive effort to rectify these violations, disregarding the Court’s directives and obstructing Plaintiff’s ability to conduct thorough discovery.

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s misconduct, which includes the failure to comply with discovery orders and incomplete document production, has prejudiced Plaintiff’s ability to timely and effectively prosecute its claims. With trial only three months away, Plaintiff contends that punitive measures are necessary to address Defendant’s interference with Plaintiff’s discovery rights. Plaintiff further requests that in addition to terminating sanctions, the Court should impose a monetary sanction of $4,000 per day for every day Defendant has remained in violation of the Court’s order since September 24, 2024, amounting to $124,000 as of the date of this motion and accruing daily. Additionally, Plaintiff seeks reimbursement for the hundreds of hours expended on meet-and-confer efforts, retaining a discovery referee, and filing discovery motions, requesting that Defendant and its counsel be jointly and severally ordered to pay no less than $15,000 in fees and costs associated with compelling compliance. Defendant has offered no adequate justification for these ongoing violations despite having over a year to provide substantive, code-compliant responses (Lubrano Decl., ¶¶ 25, 27).

Defendant, in opposition, argues that terminating sanctions are unwarranted, asserting that it has made significant efforts to comply with its discovery obligations and that recent delays were due to circumstances beyond its control. Defendant specifically attributes these delays to the family emergency of its in-house counsel, Matthew Cates, who manages this litigation. Defendant explains that on October 9, 2024, during Plaintiff’s ex parte application for sanctions, the Court was informed of Mr. Cates’ father’s terminal illness, which led Mr. Cates to take family leave. Subsequently, after Mr. Cates’ father passed away, he took bereavement leave to handle funeral arrangements and assist his mother’s transition. Although another in-house attorney was assigned to oversee the production in Mr. Cates’ absence, that attorney was not fully acquainted with the case before Mr. Cates’ return. Defendant asserts that since Mr. Cates resumed his duties, he has prioritized finalizing the supplemental production, and Epiq is diligently working to complete its document production before the hearing on this motion.

Defendant further contends that it has provided hundreds of documents responsive to Plaintiff’s requests, including a privilege log, and is actively conducting additional searches for any remaining documents, including text message records from employees’ devices. Defendant maintains that it is operating in good faith to comply with the Court’s September 4, 2024, order and that its actions do not constitute willful misconduct or an abuse of the discovery process as Plaintiff alleges.

Moreover, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s complaints of deficiencies in Epiq’s supplemental responses were raised for the first time in this motion, without any prior meet-and-confer process, which Defendant asserts violates standard procedural requirements. Defendant claims that Plaintiff’s failure to confer about these alleged deficiencies before filing the motion is premature and improper, as it deprived Defendant of the opportunity to address and potentially resolve these issues without court intervention.

Regarding Plaintiff’s request for daily monetary sanctions, Defendant asserts that this is excessive and unnecessary, particularly given that the parties have agreed to a continuance of the trial date from February 2025 to May 2025, pending review of a written stipulation. Defendant argues that the additional time provided by the trial continuance will allow Plaintiff ample opportunity to review forthcoming documents and engage in a meet-and-confer process to address any lingering discovery issues.

The Court finds that, while Defendant’s document production was delayed, the circumstances surrounding in-house counsel’s family emergency provide a reasonable explanation for the recent delays, particularly given that the September 24, 2024, deadline was less than two months ago. Given the agreed-upon continuance of the trial date, any prejudice to Plaintiff’s trial preparation is mitigated by the additional time. Furthermore, the Court notes that terminating sanctions are a severe remedy, only imposed when a party's noncompliance is found to be willful, persistent, and without reasonable justification, and when lesser sanctions would be ineffective in ensuring compliance (Lang v. Hochman, 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246; Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez, 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390). Here, Defendant has demonstrated a commitment to compliance following Mr. Cates’ return, and the delays do not rise to the level of willful misconduct or a pattern of discovery abuse.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that terminating sanctions are not warranted under these circumstances. Plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions is therefore DENIED.

Monetary sanctions

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated significant and ongoing prejudice caused by Defendant’s failure to comply with its discovery obligations. Defendant has exhibited a persistent pattern of failing to respond adequately to discovery requests, some of which preceded Mr. Cates’ unfortunate family circumstances, necessitating extensive efforts by Plaintiff to compel compliance. Specifically, Plaintiff granted Defendant five extensions between December 2023 and February 2024 (Lubrano Decl., ¶¶ 4–8), with Defendant repeatedly promising that substantive responses were forthcoming. Plaintiff engaged in multiple meet-and-confer efforts (Lubrano Decl., ¶¶ 7, 9), exchanged numerous emails (Lubrano Decl., ¶¶ 8, 22), and ultimately required the appointment of a discovery referee to resolve the disputes (Lubrano Decl., ¶¶ 15–16). Even after the Court adopted the referee’s recommendations on September 4, 2024, ordering Defendant to provide code-compliant responses within 20 days, Defendant failed to fully comply, producing only partial responses without Bates-stamped documents or a proper privilege log (Lubrano Decl., ¶¶ 21, 26).

While the Court acknowledges that recent circumstances involving Defendant’s in-house counsel reasonably explain some delays in responding to the September 4, 2024, order, Plaintiff has suffered significant prejudice in pursuing these responses. The costs incurred by Plaintiff, including meet-and-confer efforts, motion practice, and participation in proceedings with the discovery referee, were necessitated solely by Defendant’s ongoing noncompliance. As such, the Court awards Plaintiff $15,000 in monetary sanctions to compensate for the prejudice caused by Defendant’s lack of discovery compliance.

           Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.

           IV. CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions. The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $15,000, payable by Defendant Epiq Ediscovery Solutions, Inc within 30 days of this order.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

JUDGE OF THE SUPERIOR COURT

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL to MAY 20, 2025


I.        INTRODUCTION

This is a breach of contract action initiated on July 15, 2022, by Plaintiff HCMC Legal Inc. ("HCMC" or “Plaintiff”) against Defendants Brian Hartstein (“Hartstein”) and Epiq Systems Inc., Epiq Systems Solutions Inc., and Epiq Systems Acquisition Inc. (“Epiq”) (collectively, “Defendants”) HCMC alleges that Hartstein breached his employment agreement and fiduciary duties by resigning without notice and subsequently joining Epiq, a direct competitor. HCMC further contends that Hartstein solicited HCMC’s clients and former employees, thereby causing significant harm to its business operations.

On November 14, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to continue the trial. On November 25, 2024, HCMC filed an opposition to the motion. On December 3, 2024, Defendants filed a reply. Trial is currently set for February 25, 2025.

II.       LEGAL STANDARD

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subdivision (c) states that although disfavored, the trial date may be continued for “good cause,” which includes (without limitation): (1) unavailability of trial counsel or witnesses due to “death, illness, or other excusable circumstances”; (2) the addition of a new party depriving the new party (or other parties) from conducting discovery and preparing for trial; (3) “excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts”; or (4) “[a] significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case” preventing it from being ready for trial. (Id., Rule 3.1332(c).)¿

Other relevant considerations may include: “(1) The proximity of the trial date; [¶] (2) Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party; [¶] (3) The length of the continuance requested; [¶] (4) The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance; [¶] (5) The prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; [¶] (6) If the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay; [¶] (7)¿The court's calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials; [¶] (8) Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial; [¶] (9) Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; [¶] (10) Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and [¶] (11) Any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.” (Id., Rule 3.1332(d).)¿

Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 allows a court to grant leave to complete discovery proceedings. In doing so, a court shall consider matters relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to: (1) the necessity of the discovery, (2) the diligence in seeking the discovery or discovery motion, (3) the likelihood of interference with the trial calendar or prejudice to a party, and (4) the length of time that has elapsed between previous trial dates. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2024.050.)

 

III.     DISCUSSION

Defendants move to continue the trial, arguing that HCMC agreed to continue the trial, later reneged on the agreement, and engaged in discovery abuses. Defendants contend they reasonably relied on HCMC’s initial agreement, which caused them prejudice when HCMC failed to follow through.

Defendants assert that HCMC’s counsel initially agreed to discuss continuing the trial to May 12, 2025. Based on this understanding, Defendants refrained from filing motions for summary judgment or adjudication before the cut-off date. However, when HCMC later reneged on the tentative agreement, Defendants argue that they were unfairly prejudiced as a result (Motion, pp. 7–8).

Defendants further claim that HCMC delayed producing its Person Most Knowledgeable (PMK) for deposition, despite being served with a deposition notice on September 19, 2024. HCMC initially refused to provide dates and ultimately offered deposition dates in January 2025, just days before the discovery cut-off for the February trial. Defendants argue that this delay leaves them with insufficient time to address deposition-related issues through court intervention. (Motion, pp. 9–12, 14).

In opposition, HCMC argues that Defendants' arguments lack merit and are a pretext for delay. First, HCMC asserts that adequate discovery time remains because the deposition dates for January 2025 fall within the discovery cut-off period. HCMC contends that Defendants' demand for earlier dates is unreasonable, particularly given the logistical challenges of scheduling the PMK, who is in Chicago and New York. HCMC further argues that Defendants’ insistence on conducting the deposition in person in Los Angeles exacerbates these challenges (Opposition, p. 5).

Next, HCMC asserts that any discussions about a potential trial continuance were contingent on client approval, which was never granted. HCMC contends that Defendants’ reliance on a tentative agreement was misplaced and does not constitute good cause for a continuance (Opposition, p. 3). Additionally, HCMC highlights that Defendants previously assured the Court that no trial continuance would be necessary when seeking leave to file a cross-complaint in October 2024. HCMC argues that this change in position undermines Defendants’ credibility and suggests a strategy of delay rather than a legitimate need for additional time (Opposition, pp. 3–5).

HCMC also contends that it will be equally prejudiced under the current trial schedule, which requires it to complete Hartstein’s deposition within a constrained timeframe. HCMC notes that Hartstein has limited availability during the first weeks of December 2024, further complicating its ability to conduct necessary discovery. HCMC concludes that the current schedule allows sufficient time for both parties to complete their discovery obligations and adequately prepare for trial without the need for a continuance (Opposition, pp. 7-8)

The Court finds that good cause exists to continue the trial date based on Defendants’ reasonable reliance on the trial continuance discussions. The record demonstrates that HCMC’s counsel engaged in discussions regarding a potential continuance, and Defendants even drafted a stipulation to memorialize the proposed continuance. Defendants’ reliance on these discussions was not entirely unjustified. Accordingly, the Court finds that good cause exists to grant the motion.

Furthermore, the Court acknowledges the logistical challenges raised by HCMC, including the scheduling of its PMK deposition and its planned deposition of Hartstein in December. The holiday season often results in delays, and the PMK’s out-of-state location adds to the complexity of scheduling. Under these circumstances, the Court is inclined to grant the motion to ensure both parties have adequate time to complete their discovery obligations.

Finally, the requested continuance is relatively brief and does not appear to unduly prejudice HCMC. The additional time will allow all parties to complete necessary discovery and adequately prepare for trial, thereby ensuring fairness.

In balancing the interests of the parties, the Court concludes that the interests of justice favor granting the motion to continue the trial.

           IV. CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion to continue trial. The trial is continued to May 20, 2025.[1] All motion and discovery cutoff dates shall be based on the new trial date.  There will be no further trial continuances absent extraordinary circumstances.

Defendants are to give notice.
