DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV14102    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

May 20

October 1, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV14102

MOTION: 

Motion for Attorney Fees

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Madeleine Kristin Varela

OPPOSING PARTIES:

Defendants Ford Motor Company and Autonation Ford Valencia

 

MOTIONS

            Plaintiff Madeleine Kristin Varela (“Plaintiff”) settled her claims against Defendants Ford Motor Company and Autonation Ford Valencia (“Defendants”).  Plaintiff seeks attorney fees and costs in the amount of $29,958.10.  Defendants oppose the motion.

ANALYSIS

The prevailing party must seek an award of attorney fees through a noticed motion and bears the burden of proof to justify the amount sought.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5)(A).) 

In the instant case, Plaintiff contends her attorneys billed $22,525 in attorney fees.  This lodestar figure is based on billing the hourly rate of $425 per hour.  “The reasonable market value of the attorney's services is the measure of a reasonable hourly rate.  This standard applies regardless of whether the attorneys claiming fees charge nothing for their services, charge at below-market or discounted rates, represent the client on a straight contingent fee basis, or are in-house counsel.”  (Chacon v. Litke (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1234, 1260, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  Based on the Court’s experience in reviewing requests for attorney fees in similar litigation and the evidence before the Court, the Court concludes $425 per hour is a reasonable rate for attorneys engaged in similar litigation in the Los Angeles area.  (See Stratton v. Beck (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 483, 496.) 

As Defendants point out, and Plaintiff counsel’s billing entries reflect, counsel billed 11 hours in this case (from October 20 to the first seven entries on December 4, 2023) before he began drafting the complaint in this matter.  Until Plaintiff executed the retainer agreement, Plaintiff was not counsel’s client.  Certainly, some of that 11 hours could be attributed to office overhead expenses, which are not recoverable as attorney fees.  Despite counsel’s remarks concerning his extensive experience in the area of law at the heart of this matter, the Court will award Plaintiff 5.5 hours for her counsel’s work in gearing up for the case up to the filing of the complaint, or $2,337.50.

Defendants advance evidence that, on January 16, 2024, Defendants offered to repurchase the vehicle and pay Plaintiff $9,500 in attorney fees.  Plaintiff accepted the repurchase offer, but indicated Plaintiff would file a fee motion.  (See Declaration of Matthew M. Proudfoot, ¶¶ 8,9, Exhibits E, F.)  From the time Plaintiff retained counsel to January 16, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel billed $10,795.  It was not reasonable for counsel to incur an additional $7,055 in attorney fees after the parties resolved the substance of Plaintiff’s claim. The Court finds the amount of these additional fees are not reasonable and decreases them by one half, or $3,527.50.  Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees an awards her $16,660 ($2,337.50 plus $10,795.00 plus $3,527.50). 

The Court declines to award a positive multiplier.  The Court finds that the hourly rate of Plaintiff’s attorney reflects the complexity of the case, which was not novel, and did not preclude other employment. 

Plaintiff also seeks costs in the amount of $675.60.  If Plaintiff seeks an award of costs, Plaintiff must do so by filing a memorandum of costs.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a).) 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

Plaintiff Ricky Montes

  

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Rick Montes (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Defendant”) for disability discrimination, contending Defendant did not accommodate his disability.  Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative first amended complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action therein.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

In its order of February 20, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the second and third causes of action and the sixth cause of action to the extent it relied on a theory of harassment, and the Court granted Plaintiff’s request to dismiss the eighth cause of action.  The Court continued the motion with respect to the remaining causes of action and ordered the parties to file supplemental briefing, which the Court now considers.  (See February 20, 2024 Minute Order.)

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

 EVIDENCE

            Defendant’s objections to Plaintiff’s evidence are overruled.  The objections go to the weight, not the admissibility, of Plaintiff’s evidence.

DISCUSSION

First, Fourth, and Fifth Causes of Action for Disability Discrimination      

Plaintiff asserts causes of action for discrimination under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA).  To state a claim for discrimination under FEHA, the employee must demonstrate “(1) the employee's membership in a classification protected by the statute; (2) discriminatory animus on the part of the employer toward members of that classification; (3) an action by the employer adverse to the employee's interests; (4) a causal link between the discriminatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damage to the employee; and (6) a causal link between the adverse action and the damage.”  (Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  “A defending employer seeking summary judgment in a discrimination case may meet its burden by showing that one or more of these prima facie elements is lacking, or that the adverse employment action was based on legitimate nondiscriminatory factors.  [¶]  If the employer has met its burden by showing a legitimate reason for its conduct, the employee must demonstrate a triable issue by producing substantial evidence that the employer's stated reasons were untrue or pretextual, or that the employer acted with a discriminatory animus, such that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude that the employer engaged in intentional discrimination or other unlawful action.”  (Cucuzza v. City of Santa Clara (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1038, internal citation omitted.)  An employer is likewise not liable for discrimination “if the employer proves it would have fired the employee anyway for lawful reasons.”  (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 234.)

Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is for failure to provide reasonable accommodations. As Plaintiff’s employer, Defendant had to reasonably accommodate any of Plaintiff’s known disabilities to enable Plaintiff to perform the essential functions of Plaintiff’s job, unless doing so would produce undue hardship to Defendant’s operations. (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (m); 2 Cal. Code Regs., § 11068, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is for failure to engage in a good faith interactive process. An employer must engage in a “timely, good faith interactive process . . . in response to a request for reasonable accommodation by an employee or applicant with a known physical or mental disability or known medical condition.” (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (n); see 2 Cal. Code Regs, tit. 2, § 11069, subd. (a).)

Defendant asserts Plaintiff indicated at his deposition he never informed Defendant when he could return to work.  (Declaration of Jennifer A. Goldberg, Exhibit A, pp. 50, 139.)  Plaintiff also testified his supervisor, Nicole Barker (“Barker”), told him on the day Plaintiff first sought treatment for his injury that his work was unacceptable.  (Goldberg Decl., Exhibit A, p. 261.) 

Defendant also advances Barker’s deposition testimony, wherein she testified when Plaintiff initially requested time off, Baker denied the request because she did not know Plaintiff was experiencing medical issues (Goldberg Decl., Exhibit B, pp. 15-17); after Plaintiff went on leave for his medical issues, Plaintiff never requested any accommodations that could enable him to return to work (id., Exhibit B, p. 73);  if Plaintiff could not sit, stand, or drive for more than four to six hours per day, he could not meet the essential functions of his job (id., Exhibit B, p. 168); and Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment because Plaintiff would not provide Defendant with any accommodation request beyond an indefinite request for leave (id., Exhibit B, p. 186.) 

Defendant also supports its contentions with deposition testimony from Baker’s supervisor, John Hubert (“Hubert”), who testified Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment because Plaintiff was not meeting job expectations, he did not return to work, and he did not indicate when he could return to work.  (Goldberg Decl., Exhibit C, pp. 71-72.)  Hubert further testified Plaintiff could not perform the essential functions of his job with the limitations he had.  (Id., Exhibit C, p. 109.) 

Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing Defendant did not discriminate against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s disability and that Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment for a legitimate reason.  Because Defendant was aware of Plaintiff’s disability, Defendant had the obligation to propose accommodations for it, even if Plaintiff did not request any accommodations.  (See Prilliman v. United Air Lines, Inc. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 935, 949-950.)  Defendant fails to advance any evidence to show it proposed accommodations to Plaintiff prior to terminating his employment.  While Defendant argues it was Defendant’s policy to receive proposed accommodations from its employees, Defendant cites no authority for the proposition that its policies altered its obligations under law. 

Furthermore, in opposition, Plaintiff advances evidence that, as of June 23, 2021, an orthopedic surgeon evaluated Plaintiff and stated he could return to work with restrictions only on heavy lifting, pushing, and pulling, as well as forward bending, stooping, twisting, squatting, or turning.  (Plaintiff’s Compendium of Evidence (Compendium), Exhibit 9, Exhibit 96, pp. 48-49, Exhibit 104, p. 49.)  While Defendant argues in reply Defendant was unaware of this evaluation when it terminated Plaintiff’s employment on that date, the evaluation is, nonetheless, relevant.  Had Defendant proposed accommodations to Plaintiff to facilitate Plaintiff’s return to work, Defendant may have been aware of this evaluation prior to Plaintiff’s termination. 

Plaintiff also provided evidence Defendant encouraged employees to work remotely during the COVID-19 pandemic and, therefore, remote work could have been an accommodation afforded to Plaintiff, given his disability.  (Compendium, Exhibits 11-13.)  Plaintiff also offers communications which reflect Defendant’s position Plaintiff faked his injury because of a poor performance review.  (Compendium, Exhibits 15-16.)  Even if Defendant had satisfied its burden of showing Plaintiff could not prevail on his claims for disability discrimination, Defendant’s failure to provide Plaintiff with reasonable accommodations for his disability and failure to engage in a good faith interactive process, have raised triable issues of material fact requiring this Court to deny Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

Sixth Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Discrimination

Plaintiff’s claim for failure to prevent discrimination is derivative of his general claims for discrimination. Where triable issues of material fact exist as to discrimination that preclude summary adjudication, those issues likewise preclude summary adjudication on claims for failure to prevent discrimination.  (Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 288.)  As set forth above, triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Defendant discriminated against Plaintiff.

Seventh Cause of Action: Retaliation

To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must establish “by a preponderance of the evidence, that retaliation for an employee's protected activities was a contributing factor in a contested employment action. . . . Once the plaintiff has made the required showing, the burden shifts to the employer to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.” (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 718.)

As set forth above, Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing Defendant terminated Plaintiff for a legitimate reason and did not terminate Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s disability.  As such, triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s disability. 

Ninth Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy

California law provides “a tort remedy when an employer's discharge of an employee contravenes the dictates of public policy.” (Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 167, 177.)  As set forth above, Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing it terminated Plaintiff for a legitimate reason and not because of his disability.  As such, Defendant fails to satisfy its burden of demonstrating Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy.

Punitive Damages

Defendant also moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages.  As Plaintiff advances evidence Defendant “concoct[ed] a reason for termination” after terminating Plaintiff based on his medical condition, Plaintiff has raised triable issues as to her claim for punitive damages.  (Colucci v. T-Mobile USA, Inc. (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 442, 455.) 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the remaining issues is denied.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

            Defendant AYS Hospitality, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to deem admitted matters specified in Requests for Admissions, set one (“RPD”), which Defendant served on Plaintiff Pedro de la Cruz Ramirez (“Plaintiff”). And his counsel

ANALYSIS

When a party fails to respond to requests for admissions, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  The Court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted the matters specified in the requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) 

Defendant served the RFA on Plaintiff by mail and electronically on February 16, 2024.  As of the filing date of this motion, Plaintiff has not responded to the RFA.  Accordingly, the motion is granted.  Plaintiff is deemed to have admitted the matters specified in the RFA. 

Defendant seeks sanctions in connection with the motion.  The Court finds Plaintiff’s failure to respond to the RFA an abuse of the discovery process and awards sanctions. The Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff in the amount of $840, which represents three hours for Defendant to prepare the motion and for its counsel attend the hearing at $260 per hour, plus the motion’s filing fee.  Plaintiff is to pay sanctions in the amount of $840 to Defendant, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

CASE NUMBER

MOTION

Motions to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Deep Green Housing and Community Development, Inc. and Central Village Apartments, L.P.

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

MOTION

            Plaintiffs Shelbie Sanders, Shaina Sanders Shepard, Ronald Eugene Scott, and Regina Louise Hill (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Deep Green Housing and Community Development, Inc. (“Deep Green”) and Central Village Apartments, L.P. (“Central Village,” collectively, “Defendants”) for habitability violations.  Plaintiffs took Defendants’ defaults, and the Court entered default judgment against Defendants.  Defendants move to set aside the default and default judgment.

            Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition to the motion.

ANALYSIS

To the extent Defendants seek relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), the motion is untimely with respect to the entry of default. The Court entered default against Defendants on November 16, 2023, and default judgment against Defendants on January 16, 2024.  Defendants did not file this motion until July 11, 2024.  The Court, therefore, cannot set aside the default, as Defendants filed this motion more than six months after entry of default.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Pulte Homes Corp. v. Williams Mechanical, Inc. (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 267, 273.) However, Defendant’s motion for relief from default judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is timely.  The Court, therefore, considers the motion as a motion to set aside the default judgment only. 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), a court may “relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

Defendants rely on the declaration of Adrian Imonti (“Imonti”), who is the Deep Green’s Executive Director.  (Declaration of Adrian Imonti, ¶ 1.)  Deep Green is the managing general partner of Central Village.  (Ibid.)   Imonti states he received the complaint in October 2023, contacted Deep Green’s insurance broker, David Sheinin (“Sheinin”), and asked Sheinin to tender the defense to Deep Green’s insurer, Atlantic Casualty.  (Id., ¶¶ 4,6.)  Imonti, therefore ,believed Atlantic Casualty was handling the defense of this action.  (Id., ¶¶ 7, 11.)   Imonti received no other correspondence regarding this case until he received a copy of the abstract of judgment in May 2024.  (Id., ¶¶ 9, 12.) 

David Sheinin corroborated Imonti’s statements in a declaration he provided.  Sheinin added Atlantic Casualty had declined coverage of the matter, sent a declination letter to Deep Green, but the letter was returned to Atlantic Casualty having been deemed undeliverable, and Atlantic Casualty took no further steps to inform Deep Green of the declination.  (Declaration of David Sheinin, ¶ 4.)  Defendants then filed this motion to set aside the default and default judgment on July 11, 2024.

Defendants have provided evidence Plaintiffs obtained a default judgment against Defendants due to Imonti’s mistake.  Therefore, the motion to set aside the default judgment is granted.  (See Rogalski v. Nabers Cadillac (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 816, 821 [defendant’s excusable neglect was grounds for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) when defendant’s liability insurer failed to notify defendant it had denied coverage].)  Defendants, however, remain in default. 

This order is without prejudice to Defendants’ ability to bring a motion to vacate the default and default judgment on grounds other than Code of Civil Procedure, section 473, subdivision (b).

Defendants are to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
