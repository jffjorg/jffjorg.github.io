Case Number: 23VECV03837    Hearing Date: April 26, 2024    Dept: W

PATRICE KAUFMAN VS ANTHONY JOHNSON, ET AL.

 

MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS OF SUMMONS AND COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        April 26, 2024                                    Trial Date:       None set   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03837

 

Moving Party:            Specially Appearing Defendant JJC Construction, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Patrice Kaufman  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 1, 2023, Plaintiff Patrice Kaufman filed a complaint against Defendants Anthony Johnson and JJC Construction, Inc. for 1) Negligence (Breach of Implied Duty of Care); 2) Breach of Contract; 3) Recission of Contract; and 4) Statutory Abandonment (B&P Code §§ 7107 & 7113). Plaintiff hired a contractor to help with new floors and walls, paint, new kitchen (cabinets, countertops, appliances, water heater), and to install two toilets after Plaintiff’s pipes burst. Plaintiff alleges the contractor performed defective work that will cost in excess of $114,000 to correct and complete.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendant JJC Construction, Inc.’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint is DENIED.

DISCUSSION

 

Specially Appearing Defendant JJC Construction, Inc. moves the court for an order quashing service of summons and complaint to avoid an irresponsible request for default. JJC Construction also seeks relief based on mistake, error, surprise, inadvertence, and/or excusable neglect.

 

JJC Construction argues they were never properly served the summons and complaint. Specifically, JJC Construction contends service occurred upon an unknown and unauthorized California Secretary of State, Designee, and not defendant's known and authorized agent for service of process, Anthony Johnson, as plaintiff was previously advised, but refused to recognize. Said Designee is not known nor an employee, nor in charge, nor an officer, nor authorized to accept service for defendant JJC.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Contractor’s claims are completely without merit. Plaintiff contends they made numerous attempts to serve Contractor’s agent, Johnson. However, the addresses provided were either false or outdated. Being such, Plaintiff has clearly demonstrated reasonable diligence to serve Contractor’s designated agent, Johnson, and this court, in fact, already found that Plaintiff demonstrated Defendant could not be served despite reasonable diligence and granted the Application to serve Defendant through the Secretary of State.

 

The court finds service proper. As with domestic corporations, an out-of-state corporation may properly be served by service of process on the California Secretary of State pursuant to a court order. (See Corp. Code, § 2111(b).) As such, Plaintiff was permitted to personally serve the California Secretary of State pursuant to this court’s January 2, 2024 Order.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to quash service of summons and complaint is DENIED.

ALLAN MARTIA, et al. vs S.B.S. TRUST DEED NETWORK (SBS), et al.

 

PLAINTIFF ALLAN MARTIA'S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        April 26, 2024                                    Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV05137

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Allan Martia  

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 16, 2023, Plaintiff Allan Martia and Knolton, Inc. filed a complaint against Defendants S.B.S. Trust Deed Network (SBS), Bryan Russolesi, Daymon Harris, Barbara Chiling, and Timothy Glasserand for (1) Violation of CC 2924.11 (d); (2) Breach of Contract; (3) Unfair Business Practices in Violation of Business and Professions Code §§ 17200 et seq.; (4) Tortuous Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (5) Interference with Contract; (6) Quiet Title. (7) Declaratory Relief; (8) Temporary and Permanent Injunction; (9) Fraud; (10) Wrongful foreclosure (threatening to wrongfully foreclose); and (11) Cancellation of Instrument.

 

On January 22, 2024, Bryan Russolesi filed Cross-Complaint against Allan Martia, Knolton, Inc., and Reel West, Inc. for (1) Fraud; (2) Judicial Foreclosure; (3) Breach of Contract; and (4) Declaratory Relief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff Allan Martia move for an order granting leave to file a First Amended Complaint and to add a new party, Reel West, Inc., on the grounds Plaintiff was not consulted in drafting the complaint and the new claims/allegations are appropriate.

 

Motions for leave to amend the pleadings are directed to the sound discretion of the court. “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading . . . .” (CCP § 473(a)(1); see CCP § 576.) Policy favors liberally granting leave to amend so that all disputed matters between the parties may be resolved. (See Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) Absent prejudice to the adverse party, the court may permit amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial.” (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761 [internal quotes omitted].) Where leave is sought to add entirely new claims, the court may grant leave to amend if the new claims are based on the same general set of facts, and the amendment will not prejudice the opposing party. (Austin v. Massachusetts Bonding & Ins. Co. (1961) 56 Cal.2d 596, 600-602; Glaser v. Meyers (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 770, 777 [holding trial court did not abuse its discretion in permitting amendment of complaint, which originally alleged constructive eviction, to allege retaliatory eviction where the new claim was based on the same general set of facts].)

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See CRC, rule 3.1324(a), (b).)

 

Plaintiff seeks to i) replace the complaint in its entirety as to Mr. Martia’s causes of action and prayers for relief; ii) delete the eleven causes of action; iii) add one new cause of action for quiet title and two new causes of action for cancellation of instrument; iv) remove all existing Defendants; v) name Knolton, Inc. as a defendant as their interests are adverse to Plaintiff’s; vi) name new defendant, Reel West, Inc.; vii) eliminate all legal prayers for relief; and viii) add equitable prayers for relief: quiet title, cancellation of two instruments; and attorney fees and costs. Plaintiff contends he was not consulted during the drafting of the complaint nor was he ever asked to review it for factual accuracy or to verify the truth of the allegations as is required for complaints to quiet title.

 

The court finds Plaintiff has fulfilled the requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a) and (b). Plaintiff submits the declaration of Counsel David Fleck who provides the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Fleck Decl. ¶¶3-5.) Plaintiff also attaches a copy of the proposed amendment. (Fleck Decl. Exh. A.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 24VECV00461    Hearing Date: April 26, 2024    Dept: W

NANCY SHAWISH vs HOMEGOODS, INC.

 

DEMURRER to plaintiff’s complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        April 26, 2024                         Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        24VECV00461

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Homegoods, Inc.  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Nancy Shawish  

Meet and Confer:      Yes (Thompson Decl. ¶¶2-5.)  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 1, 2024, Plaintiff Nancy Shawish filed a complaint against Defendant Homegoods, Inc. for assault, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. Plaintiff alleges while on the premises of Homegoods, Inc., located at Reseda Boulevard in Tarzana, California, DOE employee of Defendant was following Plaintiff around the store, making her feel uncomfortable to shop. When Plaintiff wanted to leave the store, she noticed the DOE employee was touching himself, fully erected. Plaintiff further alleges she went to the front desk to complain about the incident. The DOE employee then approached the Plaintiff, and Plaintiff pulled a knife to scare him away.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Homegoods, Inc.’s Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED in Part and SUSTAINED, IN PART, WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Homegoods, Inc. demurs to the complaint of Plaintiff Nancy Shawish on the grounds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant and moreover, is uncertain, ambiguous and unintelligible as Plaintiff has not stated any facts or allegations.

 

Vicarious Liability

 

Defendant Homegoods, Inc. demurs to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiff has failed to allege that the acts by Doe Defendant were committed within the course and scope of his employment for  which HomeGoods, Inc. would be vicariously liable.

 

An employer may be vicariously liable for an employee’s tort, even if it was malicious, willful, or criminal if the employee’s acts was an “outgrowth” of his employment, “inherent in the working environment,” “typical of or broadly incidental to” the employer’s business, or, in a general way, foreseeable from his duties.  (Yamaguchi v. Harnsmut (2003) 106 Cal. App. 4th 472, 482.  See also Flores v. Autozone West, Inc. (2008) 161 Cal. App. 4th 373, 379; Lisa M. v. Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital (1995) 12 Cal. 4th 291, 300-301 [assaults that were not committed to further the employer’s interests have been considered outgrowths of employment if they originated in a work-related dispute].) 

 

“The rule of respondeat superior is familiar and simply stated: an employer is vicariously liable for the torts of its employees committed within the scope of the employment.  (Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons, Inc. (1986) 41 Cal. 3d 962, 967.)  Equally well established, if somewhat surprising on first encounter, is the principle that an employee’s willful, malicious and even criminal torts may fall within the scope of his or her employment for purposes of respondeat superior, even though the employer has not authorized the employee to commit crimes or intentional torts.”  (Lisa M., supra, 12 Cal. 4th at 296-97.)  “While the employee . . . need not have intended to further the employer’s interests, the employer will not be held liable for an assault or other intentional tort that did not have a causal nexus to the employee’s work.”  (Id. at 297.)  “The nexus required for respondeat superior liability—that the tort be engendered by or arise from the work—is to be distinguished from ‘but for’ causation.  That the employment brought tortfeasor and victim together in time and place is not enough. . . . [T]he incident leading to injury must be an ‘outgrowth’ of the employment; the risk of tortious injury must be ‘inherent in the working environment’ or ‘typical of or broadly incidental to the enterprise [the employer] has undertaken.’”  (Id. at 298 [citations omitted].)  “California courts have also asked whether the tort was, in a general way, foreseeable from the employee's duties.   Respondeat superior liability should apply only to the types of injuries that ‘as a practical matter are sure to occur in the conduct of the employer’s enterprise.’  The employment, in other words, must be such as predictably to create the risk employees will commit intentional torts of the type for which liability is sought.”  (Id. at 299.) 

 

The determination of whether an employee was acting within the scope of their employment is a question of fact to be decided by a jury.  However, when the facts are undisputed, it becomes a question of law.  (Ibid.) 

 

Defendant argues the rule of respondeat superior does not apply to HomeGoods given the complaint fails to allege any causal nexus between Doe Defendant’s alleged act of “touching himself, fully erected” and his job duties or HomeGoods’s business enterprise. Moreover, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts showing a meaningful difference between Doe Defendant’s alleged conduct and the case law which holds that an employee’s sexual misconduct does not occur within the scope of employment. Rather, the allegations made in Plaintiff’s complaint fall within the line of cases finding that an employee’s sexual tort is fairly attributable only to the employee’s own “propinquity and lust,” not to work-related conditions or events. (See, e.g., Lisa M., supra, 12 Cal.4th at pp. 301-303.) Lastly, Defendant contends Plaintiff is required to allege facts which establish that Doe Defendant’s act of assaulting Plaintiff was foreseeable in light of HomeGoods’ enterprise.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues contrary to Defendant’s assertions, Plaintiff has alleged that the acts by Doe Defendant were committed within the course and scope of his employment. Moreover, the complaint alleges that HomeGoods had knowledge of and ratified Doe Defendant’s misconduct, which legally renders HomeGoods vicariously liable for Doe Defendant’s acts.

 

For the purposes of a demurrer, the court finds the conduct complained thereof occurred in the scope of employment or, as alleged, was ratified by the employer which was aware of this employee engaging in unusual conduct towards customers on the floor. 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the entire complaint on respondeat superior grounds is overruled. 

 

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds Plaintiff’s complaint is completely void of any facts to support a claim of intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

The elements for a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) an actual and proximate causal link between the tortious conduct and the emotional distress.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.)   

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff only makes the conclusory allegations that HomeGoods conduct was “extreme and outrageous.” Moreover, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts that HomeGoods’ conduct, rather than Doe Defendant’s conduct, was extreme and outrageous or that HomeGoods acted intentionally or recklessly.

 

Plaintiff does not address this contention in their opposition. Upon review of the complaint, Plaintiff alleges Defendant HomeGoods conduct was extreme and outrageous when it acted without regard to Plaintiff’s rights and safety and with the intent to cause severe and permanent emotional distress. These allegations are too conclusory. They do not allege specifically what conduct of HomeGoods was extreme and outrageous.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress on the grounds Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action.

 

California courts have repeatedly recognized that negligent infliction of emotional distress is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence such that the traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply.  (See, e.g., Spates v. Dameron Hospital Association (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 213.)  Thus, to plead negligent infliction of emotional distress, negligence must be established. “The tort is negligence, a cause of action in which a duty to the plaintiff is an essential element.”  (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 984.)   A legal duty may be imposed by law, be assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship.  (Id. at 985.)  There is no duty to avoid negligently causing emotional distress to another, “unless the defendant has assumed a duty to plaintiff in which the emotional condition of the plaintiff is an object, recovery is available only if the emotional distress arises out of the defendant's breach of some other legal duty and the emotional distress is proximately caused by that breach of duty.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff has pled negligence twice in her complaint. Specifically, Plaintiff’s cause of action for NIED merely contains the conclusory statement that HomeGoods’ conduct was negligent and resulted in severe emotional distress. (Compl. ¶¶20-21.) However, Plaintiff does not identify the negligent conduct, does not allege any facts identifying the resulting emotional injuries, and does not allege the existence of any special preexisting relationship between herself and HomeGoods.

 

The court agrees that plaintiff has sufficiently alleged that she was a customer in the store, that defendant had a general duty to ensure the safe treatment in the store of its customers, that it was aware of the Doe Defendant’s improper conduct toward customers and that it failed to control him such that he did not engage in such conduct toward plaintiff.  Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is overruled.
