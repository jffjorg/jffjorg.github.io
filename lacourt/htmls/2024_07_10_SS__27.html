DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV11485    Hearing Date: July 10, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES AND REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION; REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 7/10/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV11485 KELLY DUANGRUDEESWAT vs LONG BEACH TRANSIT, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Defendant Long Beach Transit

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES AND REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE DENIED.

 

On March 31, 2023, Plaintiff served Special Interrogatories Set one and Requests for Production Set two. Plaintiff gave Defendant Long Beach Transit several extensions to provide initial responses, with the final extension set for June 15, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that the responses were not provided until October 4, 2023, and that the responses were replete with objections. Plaintiff argues that because the responses were untimely, objections have been waived and seeks to compel further responses without objections. Defendant filed an opposition arguing that timely responses were provided on June 15, 2023, and therefore objections have not been waived.

In its opposition, Defendant attached a copy of its further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories Set One and Requests for Production Set Two, served on May 29, 2024. Defendant’s further responses to the requests at issue render the present motion moot. Should Plaintiff take issue with Defendant’s further responses, the parties are required to meet and confer and attend an Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) before filing a motion to compel further responses.

The only issue remaining is whether Defendant served its responses late and thereby waived its objections. The parties do not dispute that Plaintiff gave Defendant an extension to provide initial responses by June 15, 2023. Defendant provided proof of service showing that the initial discovery responses were mailed on June 15, 2023. Plaintiff argues that all other discovery responses were served by email, except this particular response, indicating foul play. However, Defendant can choose any method of service permitted by statute and service by mail is one of the permitted methods.

Plaintiff’s counsel also declares that she did not receive any mail containing the discovery responses. However, in the case of service by mail, service is complete at the time of deposit (CCP § 1013(a)). Defendant has provided proof of service showing that the discovery responses were deposited with the United States Postal Service in a sealed envelope with postage fully prepaid; at that time, then, service was deemed complete.

Plaintiff then argues that the verifications and responses were created in October 2023 and not June 2023, when Defendant alleges to have sent out the responses by mail. Plaintiff points to the metadata of the PDF provided to Plaintiff, showing that the PDF was created in October 2023, and argues it defies logic that the original responses were not already saved as a PDF in the computer system before service to Plaintiff in June 2023. However, this scenario is possible – for instance, Defendant could have printed out the responses to be mailed from a Word document. In fact, Defendant provided metadata of a Word document of the verifications showing it was created on June 5, 2023, consistent with email exchanges between Defendant and Defendant’s counsel regarding reviewing discovery responses and signing verification to the discovery at issue around June 5, 2023 (Def’s Ex. A-C). The evidence submitted, is insufficient to find that Defendant falsified the proof of service indicating the discovery responses were mailed on June 15, 2023.

Plaintiff’s final argument is that because Defendant’s verification did not contain the exact date, it is defective under CCP § 2015.5, and unverified responses are tantamount to no responses at all (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632). Defendant in its reply concedes that it made a clerical mistake in the verifications, where the verifications were signed on June 5, 2023, but mistakenly stated they were made in May 2023. Defendant’s verification substantially complies with the requirements set forth under CCP § 2015.5, as it contained the location of execution, the declarant’s signature, and the declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California.

Furthermore, when a discovery response served within the statutory deadline includes both objections and unverified fact-specific answers, verification is not necessary for the objections to be deemed timely. There is no requirement for objections to be verified and objections must be considered timely notwithstanding the lack of verification. (Food 4 Less Supermarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 651, 657.) Only the fact-specific responses must be verified. The only scenario where a lack of verification leads to both an untimely response and a waiver of objections is if the initial, unverified response served within the timeline does not contain any objections. (Id.) The court examined the discovery responses at issue and finds that the majority of these responses consist of only objections, as pointed out by Plaintiff in the moving papers. Therefore, Defendant has not waived its objections even if the verification was defective.

As for sanctions, the Court finds that there was a genuine dispute over whether the initial responses were timely served and whether objections had been waived and, thus, both parties acted with substantial justification. Therefore, the Court denies the requests for sanctions.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT¿AND SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 7/10/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV40190 BATOOL SALEKI vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES 

Moving Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED

 

Background

 

On March 21, 2021, Plaintiff allegedly tripped and fell over a 1-inch sidewalk displacement located on a public sidewalk on Beverly Glen Blvd in the City of Los Angeles. Plaintiff sued the City for premises liability and general negligence.  The City now moves for summary judgment because (1) a public entity can only be sued as authorized by statute, not under a general negligence theory, and (2) the defect at issue was trivial, consisting of a 1-inch displacement and no aggravating factors.

 

Legal Standard

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

  

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.)

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. [Citation.] Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.  If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true. [Citation.] Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

Judicial Notice and Evidentiary Ruling 

The Court overrules Plaintiff’s Objection No 2. The Parties did not make any other objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)

General Negligence

 

A public entity in California is not liable for any injury, whether it arises out of act or omission of the public entity or a public employee or any other person, except as provided by statute. (Branch v. State of California Ex Rel. Dept. of Transportation (1984)159 Cal. App. 3d 340, 343; Cal Gov. Code § 815(a).) The sole statutory basis for imposing premise liability on public entities as property owners is Government Code section 835. (See Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1131- 32.) In the opposition, Plaintiff acknowledges that “first cause of action should be dismissed as the City cannot be held liable for general negligence.” (Opposition at pg. 6.) Thus, Defendant’s summary judgment against Plaintiff’s general negligence cause of action against the City is GRANTED.

Premises Liability

 

The Trivial Defect Doctrine

 

“Property owners are required to maintain land in their possession and control in a reasonably safe condition and to use due care to eliminate dangerous conditions on their property.  But a property owner is not liable for damages caused by a minor, trivial, or insignificant defect on its property.  The so-called ‘trivial defect doctrine’ recognizes that persons who maintain walkways, whether public or private, are not required to maintain them in an absolutely perfect condition.  The duty of care imposed on a property owner, even one with actual notice, does not require the repair of minor defects.”  (Fajardo v. Dailey (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 221, 226 [cleaned up].)  

 

“In limited circumstances a court may determine a walkway defect is trivial as a matter of law.  Where reasonable minds can reach only one conclusion—that there was no substantial risk of injury—the issue is a question of law, properly resolved by way of summary judgment.  But where sufficient evidence has been presented so that reasonable minds may differ as to whether the defect is dangerous, summary judgment is inappropriate.”  (Id. at p. 226.)   

   

California Courts have developed two substantially similar tests to determine whether a sidewalk defect is trivial, i.e., not dangerous, as a matter of law.  In Stathoulis v. City of Montebello (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 559, 567-68, the Court of Appeal set out a two part test. “First, the court reviews evidence regarding the type and size of the defect.  If that preliminary analysis reveals a trivial defect, the court considers evidence of any additional factors such as the weather, lighting and visibility conditions at the time of the accident, the existence of debris or obstructions, and plaintiff’s knowledge of the area.  If these additional factors do not indicate the defect was sufficiently dangerous to a reasonably careful person, the court should deem the defect trivial as a matter of law ....” (See also Nunez v. City of Redondo Beach (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 749, 758 [adopting two-step framework]; Huckey v. City of Temecula (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1105 [same].)

 

More recently, in Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102, 110, the Court of Appeal read Government Code section 830.2 to require a more “holistic” approach, stating, “[a]lthough we agree with the premise that the size of the defect is the primary determinant of triviality, as discussed below, we modify the prevailing two-step framework into a holistic, multi-factor analysis.”  The Court will follow Stack’s holistic approach, although the result would be the same under either test.

 

1.  Size of the Defect

 

“We begin with the most important factor: the defect’s size.” (Stack, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 110 [cleaned up].)  “Sidewalk elevations ranging from three-quarters of an inch to one and one-half inches have generally been held trivial as a matter of law.” (Huckey, supra, 37 Cal.App.5th at p. 1107; see Stathoulis, supra, 164 Cal.App.4th at p. 568 [“Several decisions have found height differentials of up to one and one-half inches trivial as a matter of law.”])  A more “accurate encapsulation” of the law, according to Stack, is that “when the size of the depression begins to stretch beyond one inch the courts have been reluctant to find that the defect is not dangerous as a matter of law, i.e., that it is minor or trivial.”  (Stack, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 112.)  “That said, there is no firmly fixed arbitrary measurement in inches below which a defect is trivial as a matter of law and above which it becomes a question of fact whether or not the defect is dangerous.  This is because a court should not rely solely upon the size of the defect ... although the defect’s size ‘may be one of the most relevant factors’ to the court’s decision.”  (Id. at pp. 112–113 [cleaned up].)

 

2.  Aggravating Factors

 

The Court next considers additional factors. “Beyond size, additional factors courts typically consider in assessing a sidewalk condition’s triviality as a matter of law are: the nature and quality of the defect (including whether it has jagged breaks or cracks); whether anything was obstructing or concealing the defect (for instance, an object, debris, or other substance); the lighting and weather conditions at the time of the incident; whether the defect has caused other accidents; and plaintiff's familiarity with the area.” (Stack, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 115.)

 

Defendant’s Burden

Defendant has met its initial burden. The displacement in question was measured at 1 inch in height (Luevano Decl., ¶¶ 7-8). The incident occurred on March 21, 2021, around 6:30 p.m. Plaintiff fell when she was taking a walk with her daughter on Beverly Glen Blvd near her daughter's apartment. At the time of the incident, it was light outside. The weather was sunny. (Plaintiff’s Deposition, 14:7-11; 59:6-8; 62:10-15). Plaintiff had previously walked on the sidewalk at issue approximately 10 to 50 times. It is located just a few steps from her daughter's apartment (Plaintiff’s Deposition, 14:12-15:2; 26:24-27:2). Plaintiff is not aware of anybody else who had tripped and fallen at the same location where she fell (Plaintiff’s Deposition, 64:19-21). A search of City records showed that no claims for personal injury with an incident date range of March 21, 2011, through March 21, 2021, had been presented to the City of Los Angeles due to any dangerous condition located at or near 2265 S. Beverly Glen Blvd, other than the claim presented by Plaintiff Batool Saleki. (Brian Sam Decl. ¶6.)

Defendant has met its burden in showing that the displacement was 1 inch in height without the presence of any significant aggravating factors.

Triable issue of fact

Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact. The parties do not dispute that the displacement is 1 inch in height, Plaintiff has previously walked on the same street between 10-50 times (UMF 7), and the weather at the time of the incident was sunny and without rain (UMF 6).

Plaintiff attempted to argue that the area was shaded and covered with debris, but the Court's examination of the photographs taken near the time of the incident showed clear visibility and minimal debris. Any debris present was on the grass or edge of the sidewalk, not near the displacement, and did not obscure visibility of the displacement (Exhibit C, at pp. 33-34, and Exhibits 4, 5, 6, and 9).

Plaintiff introduced evidence of a prior incident at the location, but the incident was relating to a sign and not any trip and fall (F. Gary Gsell Decl., ¶ 16). Additionally, Plaintiff did not present any evidence showing prior trip and fall incidents at the location in question.

Plaintiff's counsel argued that Plaintiff was unlikely to notice the displacement because Beverly Glen Blvd is a major street with high-speed vehicles, people riding sccoters, rising crime rates, homelessness, and her age made her a susceptible target for crimes. In Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102, 115, the Court outlined the aggravating factors it considers in assessing the triviality of a defect and it does not include factors such as rising crime rates or homelessness.  (The Court recognizes that homelessness could play a role if an unhoused person or encampment obstructed the sidewalk, but there is no evidence of that here.)  Even if those factors were considered, they are simply speculative here – no evidence was presented to support the argument that they played a role in Plaintiff’s trip and fall.

After examining all the evidence, the Court finds the displacement in question to be trivial as a matter of law. First, the 1-inch displacement is below the threshold where the Court would hesitate to find it trivial. (Stack, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 112). Courts routinely found height differentials of up to one and one-half inches trivial as a matter of law. (See, e.g., Barrett v. City of Claremont (1953) 41 Cal.2d 70, 74 [in the absence of aggravating conditions, differential of less than half an inch deemed trivial]; Caloroso v. Hathaway (2004) 122 Cal.App.4th 922 927 [elevation difference of under half an inch]; Fielder v. City of Glendale (1977) 71 Cal.App.3d 719, 724, fn. 4 [same]; Nicholson v. City of Los Angeles (1936) 5 Cal.2d 361, 367 [one and one-half inch elevation difference]; Whiting v. City of National City (1937) 9 Cal.2d 163, 165-166 [ 69 P.2d 990] [elevation difference of a maximum of three-fourths of an inch].)  

Second, there are no significant aggravating factors present. Plaintiff presented no evidence of any prior fall at the location in question. Plaintiff is knowledgeable of the area and has walked the sidewalk at issue many times. There are no jagged edges or potholes. The photographs show good lighting, sunny, clear weather, and minimal debris, not covering the displacement itself.

Therefore, the Court grants summary judgment.

DEMURRER

Hearing Date: 6/18/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV08491 DORA JIMENEZ, et al. vs ROSE CREMATION SERVICES, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant LOS ANGELES COMMUNITY HOSPITAL

Responding Party: Macera

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: Defendant Hospital’s demurrer as to Macera’s causes of action for equitable indemnity and equitable contribution is sustained. The demurrer to the cause of action for declaratory relief is overruled.

 

Background

 

On March 9, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a Complaint against three Defendants involved in the handling of the remains of Pedro Reyes (“decedent” or “Reyes”) after his death: ALTA Los Angeles Community Hospital (“Hospital"), Rose Cremation Services ("Rose"), and Macera Crematory (“Macera”). On May 6, 2024, Macera filed its first amended cross-complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant Hospital for equitable indemnity, equitable contribution, and declaratory relief. Defendant Hospital now demurs to Macera’s FAC.

 

Judicial Notice

 

Generally, the Court may not take judicial notice of discovery responses. (See TSMC North America v. Semiconductor Mfg. Intern. Corp. (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 581, 594.) However, the Court may take judicial notice of discovery responses “to the extent ‘they contain statements of the [party] or his agent which are inconsistent with the allegations of the pleading before the court.” (Bounds v. Superior Court (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 468, 477 (quoting Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 605).) “But in doing so, ‘[t]he hearing on demurrer may not be turned into a contested evidentiary hearing through the guise of having the court take judicial notice of affidavits, declarations, depositions, and other such material which was filed on behalf of the adverse party and which purports to contradict the allegations and contentions of the plaintiff.’” (Id.) 

 

Plaintiff seeks compensatory damages against Defendants, including Macera and Hospital, in the complaint. However, Plaintiffs' discovery responses do not allege any special damages, only non-economic damages in terms of emotional distress. There exists an inconsistency between Plaintiff’s discovery responses and the complaint. Thus, the Court takes judicial notice of the discovery responses. Further, Defendant Macera did not object to Hospital’s request for judicial notice.

 

Government Code 8659 & Executive Order Immunity 

 

Defendant argues that it should be granted immunity because California State actors have expressly or implicitly requested that Defendant render services during the pandemic.   

 

Government Code 8659(a) provides:  

 

“Any physician or surgeon (whether licensed in this state or any other state), hospital, pharmacist, respiratory care practitioner, nurse, or dentist who renders services during any state of war emergency, a state of emergency, or a local emergency at the express or implied request of any responsible state or local official or agency shall have no liability for any injury sustained by any person by reason of those services, regardless of how or under what circumstances or by what cause those injuries are sustained; provided, however, that the immunity herein granted shall not apply in the event of a willful act or omission.” 

 

The order does not confer unfettered immunity on Defendant. The immunity provided applies to those acting at the express or implied request of any responsible state or local official or agency. As such, Defendant must show that a state actor expressly or implicitly requested that Defendant render services above and beyond the services Defendant was already providing before the State of Emergency declaration. The service in question is not providing medical assistance to the patient with COVID-19, as the decedent had already passed when the alleged negligence occurred. Plaintiff is also not alleging medical malpractice relating to hospital treatment of Decedent’s Covid-19. Temporary storage of the decedent’s body before it is sent out does not constitute services above and beyond what Defendant was already rendering before the State of Emergency declaration.

Equitable Immunity and Equitable Contribution.

“The right to indemnity flows from payment of a joint legal obligation on another’s behalf.” (Expressions at Rancho Niguel Association v. Ahmanson Developments, Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1135, 1139.) “The elements of a cause of action for indemnity are (1) a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is contractually or equitably responsible.” (Id. (emphasis in original).) Equitable indemnity is premised on a joint legal obligation to another for damages, whether between joint tortfeasors or to a third party. (See Prince v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1151, 1158; Greystone Homes, Inc. v. Midtec, Inc. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1194, 1218.)  

 

Equitable indemnity applies “only among defendants who are jointly and severally liable to the plaintiff.” (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc. v. Forcum/Mackey Construction, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852.) Cal. Civ. Pro. § 875 holds, in pertinent part, that: (a) Where a money judgment has been rendered jointly against two or more defendants in a tort action there shall be a right of contribution among them.

 

Under California law, specifically Civil Code § 1431.2 (part of Proposition 51), non-economic damages are apportioned according to each defendant's proportionate share of fault, meaning that each defendant is only responsible for the percentage of non-economic damages that corresponds to their level of fault. Because non-economic damages are several and not joint, there is no joint legal obligation among defendants for these damages. This means that one defendant cannot seek indemnification or contribution from another defendant for non-economic damages, as each defendant is only responsible for their own share of these damages

Defendant Hospital points to Plaintiffs’ discovery responses that the court has taken judicial notice of to support its argument. Plaintiffs’ responses to Defendant Macera’s Form Interrogatories – General, Set One, Nos. 6.4, 6.6, 6.7, 8.1, 9.1, 10.3 show that Plaintiffs are not seeking medical expenses, loss of earnings, burial costs, loss of use of property, loss of employment and loss of business, employment opportunities or any type of special damages. Plaintiffs’ Form Interrogatories – General, Set One, Nos. 6.1 and 6.3  show that Plaintiffs are only seeking non-special damages like emotional distress in this action.

Macera argues that in cases where damages are awarded through a special verdict, the verdict often does not specify economic and non-economic damages, but merely awards an undifferentiated lump sum. (C.B. v. City of Sonora (2014) 769 F.3d 1005, 1032.) When this occurs, the lump sum is treated as non-economic damages, and a party waives any right to any offset under joint and several liability. (Id.) As a result, there would be no indemnity under a special verdict. Based on the nature of damages sought by Plaintiffs, Defendants are only responsible for their own share of the damages. Therefore, Hospital’s demurrer against Macera's cause of action for equitable indemnity and contribution is sustained without leave to amend. Should Plaintiffs' stance on economic damages change, the Court will consider Macera’s motion to amend.

Conclusion

Defendant Hospital’s demurrer as to Macera’s cause of action for equitable indemnity and equitable contribution is sustained. The demurrer to the cause of action for declaratory relief is overruled.

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT JUDGMENT

Hearing Date: 7/10/24 

CASE NO./NAME: BC653633 XIAO HUA WAN VS BIN ZHANG, ET AL.

Moving Party: Defendant Bin Zhang

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT JUDGMENT

IS GRANTED 

 

Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure Section 473.5 provides for setting aside a default "when service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action." (Section 473.5, subd. (a).

 

A party who has not received such notice "may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action." (Ibid.)

 

The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. (Ibid.)

 

(b) A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.

 

(c) Upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action. 

 

Discussion

On March 7, 2017, Plaintiff filed the present case alleging that Defendant Bin Zhang attacked him in Oklahoma while both were employed by Defendant De Ozark. On November 13, 2018, Plaintiff purportedly served Defendant Zhang via substitute service on "John Doe" at 15356 Nelson Ave, City of Industry, CA 91744, an address associated with Defendant De Ozark. On August 30, 2022, the Court entered default judgment against Defendants. Defendant Zhang now moves the court for relief, claiming no association with De Ozark and stating he learned of the case only in late March 2024, upon receiving an Abstract of Judgment against his property.

Timeliness of Motion

Motions under CCP 473.5 must be filed either within two years of entry of default judgment or within 180 days after service of written notice of default or default judgment Plaintiff’s attorney contends that the 180-day deadline applies. However, upon examining the docket and Plaintiff’s exhibits, the Court could not locate a written notice of default judgment served on Defendant. Therefore, the applicable deadline is two years after entry of default judgment. Since the default was entered in August 2022 and the motion was filed on June 4, 2024, the present motion is timely.

Defendant's Lack of Actual Notice

Defendant declares that he did not receive actual notice of the lawsuit until recently when he received the Abstract of Judgment registered with the Office of the Clerk of the County Los Angeles in March 2024. Plaintiff provided no evidence to counter Defendant’s declaration. The evidence he did submit – his declaration stating that he personally served a copy of the judgment in March 2024 – leads to the same analysis as with Defendant receiving notice through the abstract of judgment in March 2024, shortly before Defendant filed the present motion.

Defendant's Association with De Ozark

Defendant declares that he is not associated with De Ozark company in any way. He asserts that he has his own trucking company, Bin's Trucking, and provided a Secretary of State printout of his company that does not show the 15356 Nelson Ave, City of Industry, CA 91744 address. The only evidence provided by Plaintiff that Defendant was associated with De Ozark is his own declaration stating, "At the time we were independent contractor team truck drivers working for Defendant DE OZARK GROUP" (Plf’s Declaration ¶ 2). Plaintiff’s declaration failed to show the duration and frequency for which Defendant worked for De Ozark.  Without more information, Plaintiff has not shown that an address associated with De Ozark is Defendant’s “usual” place of business as required for substitute service under the statute.

Furthermore, Plaintiff has not shown if 15356 Nelson Ave, City of Industry, CA 91744 is even a place of business for Defendant De Ozark or only where its agent for service of process resides. The proof of service shows that Ozark’s agent for service was served at this address. Additionally, documents of Defendant, such as his driver’s license and Secretary of State printout for his company, do not bear the Nelson Ave address. Plaintiff did not provide any documentary evidence associating Defendant with the Nelson Ave address.

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to set aside.  The Court excuses Plaintiff's counsel’s mistake in not attaching the answer to the motion and orders Plaintiff to file an answer within 20 days of today.
