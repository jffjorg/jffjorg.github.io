Case Number: 21STCV18722    Hearing Date: November 6, 2023    Dept: 71

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CARROLL H. CASWELL, JR., et al., 

 

         vs.

 

WWC HOLDINGS, LLC, et al.

 Case No.:  21STCV18722

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  November 6, 2023

 

Cross-Defendants Raymond Wang’s, Victor Wang’s, Sherry Wang’s, Cyrus Investment, LLC’s, Optimal Property, LLC’s, Cub Capital, LLC’s, and SRS Infrastructure, LLC’s demurrer to Cross-Complainant WWC Corp.’s fourth amended cross-complaint is:

(1) overruled as to the 1st cause of action as to Victor Wang only; 2nd cause of action; 3rd cause of action as to Victor Wang only; 4th cause of action; 5th cause of action; 6th cause of action; and 7th cause of action; and

(2) sustained without leave to amend as to the 1st cause of action as to Cyrus Investment, LLC, Optimal Property, LLC, Cub Capital, LLC, and SRS Infrastructure, LLC; 3rd cause of action as to Cyrus Investment, LLC, Optimal Property, LLC, Cub Capital, LLC, and SRS Infrastructure, LLC; and 8th cause of action.

 

Cross-Defendants Raymond Wang (“Raymond”), Victor Wang (“Victor”), Sherry Wang (“Sherry”), Cyrus Investment, LLC, (“CIL”), Optimal Property, LLC (“OPL”), Cub Capital, LLC, (“CCL”) and SRS Infrastructure, LLC (“SRS,”) (collectively “Cross-Defendants”) demur to each cause of action in Cross-Complainant WWC Corp.’s (“WWC”) fourth amended cross-complaint (“4AXC”).  (Notice of Demurrer, pg. 1; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f); §581(f).)[1]

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

Cross-Defendants’ 10/11/23 request for judicial notice of Exhibits A-J is denied, as the Court does not need to take judicial notice of documents filed in the instant case.

Cross-Complainant’s 10/23/23 request for judicial notice of Exhibits A-D is denied, as the Court does not need to take judicial notice of documents filed in the instant case.

 

Background

WWC filed its initial verified cross-complaint (“CC”) against the Wang Defendants on June 1, 2021. Cross-Complainant lodged a first amended cross-complaint (“FAXC”) April 1, 2022, but it was not filed. WWC filed a Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SAXC”) on April 25, 2022. The Court sustained a demurrer to the SAXC on November 17, 2022. WWC then filed its Third Amended Cross-Complaint (“TAXC”) on December 19, 2022. 

On July 11, 2023, this Court ruled on Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the TAXC as the following: (1) overruling the 1st cause of action as to Raymond; 2nd cause of action; 3rd cause of action as to Raymond; 5th as to Raymond and Victor; 6th cause of action; 7th cause of action; and 9th cause of action as to Raymond and Victor; (2) sustaining with leave to amend the 1st cause of action as to the remaining cross-defendants; 3rd cause of action as to the remaining cross-defendants; 5th cause of action as to the remaining cross-defendants; 8th cause of action; and 9th cause of action as to the remaining cross- defendants; and (3) sustaining without leave to amend as to the 4th and 10th causes of action.  (7/11/23 Ruling.)

On September 8, 2023, Cross-Complainant filed the operative 4AXC alleging eight causes of action: (1) intentional concealment of material facts and constructive fraud (Civil Code §1573) [against Raymond, Victor, CIL, SRS, CCL, and OPL]; (2) conversion [against Raymond and Victor]; (3) breach of fiduciary duty [against Raymond, Victor, CIL, SRS, CCL, and OPL]; (4) intentional interference with prospective economic relations [against Raymond and Victor]; (5) declaratory relief [against Raymond, Victor, and SRS]; (6) accounting [against Cross-Defendants]; and (8) voidable transfer [against Raymond, Victor, CIL, CCL, and OPL].

On October 11, 2023, Cross-Defendants filed the instant demurrer.  On October 23, 2023, Cross-Complainant filed its opposition.  On October 30, 2023, Cross-Defendants filed their reply.

 

Summary of Demurrer

Cross-Defendants demur to the entire 4AXC for failure to amend the pleading within the time allowed by this Court’s prior demurrer ruling.  Cross-Defendants demur to Cross-Complainant’s 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th, 6th, 7th, and 8th causes of action on the basis they fail state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action.  (Demurrer, pgs. 2-4; C.C.P. §430.10(e).)  Cross-Defendants demur to the 8th cause of action on the basis the cause of action is uncertain and ambiguous.  (Demurrer, pg. 4; C.C.P. §430.10(f).) 

 

          Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a).)  A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Cross-Defendants’ counsel’s declaration states he met and conferred with Cross-Complainant’s counsel on August 23 and 28, 2023, and September 7 and 12, 2023, addressing deficiencies in the 4AXC and parties could not resolve the issues in this motion outside of court.  (Decl. of Jazayeri ¶¶2, 4, Exh. A.)  However, Cross-Defendants’ counsel does not declare that he met and conferred either in person or by telephone.  Nevertheless, Defendants’ counsel’s declaration is sufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Timeliness

Dismissal of an action pursuant to C.C.P. §581(f) for failure to amend a complaint within the time allowed by the court is required unless the plaintiff establishes an excuse for the failure to amend.  (Dumm v. Pacific Valves (1956) 146 Cal.App.2d 792, 795; Daniels v. Daniels (1955) 136 Cal.App.2d 224, 228.) 

Here, Cross-Defendants were given notice of the Court’s demurrer ruling granting them 20 days leave to amend on July 18, 2023, via email, making their 4AXC due on August 9, 2023.  Cross-Complainants’ 4AXC was electronically received on August 10, 2023, at 9:11 a.m.  However, the 4AXC was not filed until September 8, 2023.  The Court declines to dismiss the 4AXC pursuant to C.C.P. §581(f).  Cross-Complainant sufficiently establishes an excuse for the failure to amend, namely that the corrected caption page for the 4AXC was cause for the delayed acceptance of the document by the Court.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer on the basis of untimeliness is overruled.

 

Failure to State a Claim

Concealment of Material Facts (1st COA) [against Raymond, Victor, CIL, SRS, CCL, and OPL]

“[T]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.”  (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.)

“A cause of action for concealment must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations will not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  This requirement of particularity necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means any fraudulent representations were tendered.  (Hamilton v. Greenwich Investors XXVI, LLC (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1602, 1614.)

In Casey v. United States Bank National Association, the Court of Appeal held that liability may be imposed on one who aids and abets the commission of an intentional tort if the person (1) knows the other’s conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to act, or (2) gives substantial assistance to the other in accomplishing a tortious result and the person’s own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.  (Casey v. United States Bank National Association (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 1138, 1144.)

Cross-Complainant fails to allege CCL, CIL, or OPL either (1) knew of Raymond’s conduct constituting a breach of duty and gave substantial assistance or encouragement to Raymond to act, or (2) gave substantial assistance to Raymond in accomplishing a tortious result and their own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.  (See Casey, 127 Cal.App.4th at pg. 1144.)  Cross-Complainant’s allegation in ¶38 that “[Raymond] breached his fiduciary duties to Cross-Complainants when he, among other things, requested greater access to and control over WWC’s finances and bank accounts and the governing documents of the LLCs while concealing the material fact that he intended, through the substantial assistance of other co-conspirators and/or aiders and abettors described herein, to exploit, and did in fact exploit his position of access and control through WWC over the LLCs and their respective bank accounts, by illicitly transferring the monetary and real property assets of WWC and/or the joint ventures to which it was a party with homeowners to himself, to his family (including [Victor]), and/or to entities which he owned and/or controlled (including CIL, SRS, CCL, and OPL),” is insufficient to demonstrate they gave substantial assistance or encouragement to Raymond to act.  (See 4AXC ¶38.)

Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges Victor knew Raymond’s conduct constituted a breach of duty and Victor gave substantial assistance or encouragement to Raymond to act by alleging in ¶27(f), “on or about March 1, 2021, at [Raymond’s] direction, [Victor] executed Grant Deeds under his own name and signature purporting to transfer, without receipt of reasonably equivalent consideration, the real properties described in Paragraphs 23 and 24, held in trust by the LLCs for the benefit of WWC’s real estate renovation and resale business, to SRS which was formed the following day (March 2, 2021) and not to further the completion of the WWC Incomplete Projects. Upon information and belief, [Victor] knew at the time he executed these Grants Deeds on March 1, 2021, to the real properties described in Paragraphs 23 and 24, that neither he nor [Raymond] had the authority to convey the real properties, and that doing so was in violation of the rights, duties, and obligations [Raymond] owed to the LLCs and WWC. With [Victor’s] unauthorized transfer of the real properties held by the LLCs to SRS, the LLCs were effectively rendered insolvent as they each lacked an asset or other thing of value to pay their debts.”  (FAXC ¶27(f).)  Such allegations are sufficient to allege Victor aided and abetted Raymond.  However, Cross-Complainant’s allegations in ¶27(f) explicitly state the abovementioned actions occurred the day before SRS was formed, and therefore fails to allege SRS had knowledge of Raymond’s actions because it did not exist at the time.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 1st cause of action for concealment of material facts is overruled as to Victor and sustained without leave to amend as to SRS, CCL, CIL, and OPL.

 

          Conversion (2nd COA)

Cross-Defendants’ notice of demurrer states it demurs to the 2nd cause of action for failure to state a claim but presents no argument.  (See Demurrer, pg. 3.)

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 2nd cause of action for conversion is overruled.

 

Breach of Fiduciary Duty (3rd COA) [against Raymond, Victor, CIL, SRS, CCL, and OPL]

A cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty requires the following elements: (1) the existence of a fiduciary relationship, (2) its breach, and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach.  (Knox v. Dean (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 417, 432-433.)

For the same reasons stated in the Court’s reasoning on the 1st cause of action, Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges Victor aided and abetted Raymond in his alleged breach of fiduciary duty, but fails to allege CIL, SRS, CCL, and OPL gave substantial assistance or encouragement to Raymond.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 3rd cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty is overruled as to Victor and sustained without leave to amend as to SRS, CCL, CIL, and OPL.

 

          Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations (4th COA)

“Intentional interference with prospective economic advantage has five elements: (1) the existence, between the plaintiff and some third party, of an economic relationship that contains the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentionally wrongful acts designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm proximately caused by the defendant’s action.”  (Roy Allan Slurry Seal, Inc. v. American Asphalt South, Inc. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 505, 512.)

Cross-Defendants’ argument that Cross-Complainant’s failure to plead a contract claim dooms all of its claims is unavailing.  The 4th cause of action is a tort claim that does not require the existence of a contract claim.  A demurrer takes all facts alleged to be true.  (Aubry, 2 Cal.4th at pg. 967.)  At this juncture, Cross-Defendants’ argument that ¶17’s allegation of an “‘oral and/or implied agreement’ between Raymond and Clark/WWC resulted in: (1) Raymond becoming a shareholder, director and officer of WWC, (2) WWC owning the LLCs, which in turn purchased various real properties that were held in trust for WWC, and (3) Raymond making secured loans to WWC and the LLCs, which WWC would pay back with interest,” is taken to be true.  A demurrer is not the proper mechanism to challenge such allegations, rather, summary judgment is.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 4th cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic relations is overruled.

 

Declaratory Relief (5th COA)

A cause of action for declaratory relief requires the following elements: (1) a person interested under a written instrument or a contract; or (2) a declaration of his or her rights or duties; (a) with respect to another; or (b) in respect to, in, over or upon property; and (3) an actual controversy.  (C.C.P. §1060.)

Cross-Complainant alleges a judicial declaration is required to determine the parties’ respective rights, duties, and obligations under WWC.  (4AXC ¶73.)  Cross-Complainant alleges despite Raymond’s role in WWC as a director and/or officer, the parties dispute the actions taken by Raymond, including the taking of corporate funds owned by WWC and the actions taken by Raymond, and his affiliates, as to the LLCs.  (4AXC ¶73.)

Cross-Complainant alleges a judicial declaration is required to determine who were the proper owners and managers of the LLCs, whether the managers of record for those LLCs owed a concurrent fiduciary duty to WWC in connection with the overarching real property renovation and sale business financed by Raymond and conducted by WWC, and limitations upon the scope of appropriate discretion and authority to be exercised on behalf of those LLCs in light the purpose of forming and managing the LLCs as holding companies for properties for renovation and resale activities by WWC.  (4AXC ¶74.)  Cross-Complainant alleges a judicial declaration is necessary and appropriate at this time under the circumstances in order for the parties to ascertain their respective rights, duties, and obligations with respect to the LLCs, as these issues are crucial to the determination of whether the Cross-Defendants’ actions of diverting properties to SRS was a proper exercise of Raymond’s and Victor’s duties owed to Cross-Complainants.  (4AXC ¶74.)

Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges a cause of action for declaratory relief.  Cross-Defendants’ argument that Cross-Complainant’s failure to allege a written contract claim is of no consequence, as Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges it seeks a declaration of its rights or duties for a live controversy.  Further, the Court overruled Cross-Defendants’ previous demurrer to this cause of action.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 5th cause of action for declaratory relief is overruled.

 

Accounting (6th COA)

Accounting is an independent cause of action in equity.  (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 180.)  A cause of action seeking an accounting may be maintained when (1) a relationship exists between a plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and (2) some balance is due to the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.  (Id. at pg. 179.)  No fiduciary relationship is required.  (Id.)  

Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges a cause of action for an accounting.  Further, the Court overruled Cross-Defendants’ previous demurrer to this cause of action.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 6th cause of action for accounting is overruled.

 

Slander of Title (7th COA) [against Raymond and Victor]

A cause of action for slander of title requires the following elements: (1) defendant made a statement or performed a specific act that cast doubts about plaintiff’s ownership of the property; (2) the statement was made to a person other than plaintiff/or some other form of publication; (3) the statement was untrue and plaintiff did in fact own the property; (4) defendant knew that/acted with reckless disregard of the truth or falsity as to whether plaintiff owned the property; (5) defendant knew or should have recognized that someone else might act in reliance on the statement/e.g., deed, causing plaintiff financial loss; (6) plaintiff did in fact suffer immediate and direct financial harm because someone else acted in reliance on the statement/e.g., deed/ or by incurring legal expenses necessary to remove the doubt cast by the statement/e.g., deed and to clear title; and (7) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  (CACI 1730.)

Cross-Complainant sufficiently alleges this cause of action.  Further, the Court overruled Cross-Defendants’ previous demurrer to this cause of action.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 7th cause of action for slander of title is overruled.

 

Voidable Transfer (Civ. Code §3439 et seq.) (8th COA)

Cross-Complainant added a new 8th cause of action for voidable transfer for which it was not granted leave from the Court to assert.

Cross-Defendant concedes in its opposition that Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the 8th cause of action should be granted.  (Opposition, pg. 7.)

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant’s 8th cause of action for voidable transfer is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Cross-Complainant WWC Corp.’s fourth amended cross-complaint is:

(1) overruled as to the 1st cause of action as to Victor only; 2nd cause of action; 3rd cause of action as to Victor only; 4th cause of action; 5th cause of action; 6th cause of action; and 7th cause of action; and

(2) sustained without leave to amend as to the 1st cause of action as to CIL, OPL, CCL, and SRS; 3rd cause of action as to CIL, OPL, CCL, and SRS; and 8th cause of action.
