DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV40539    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: 73

9/12/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

CHUNG v. PALUX OPTICS CORP, et al. (21STCV40539)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff: Charles Ray (Ray & Seyb LLP)

Counsel for Defendants: Pro Per

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION (filed 1/30/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 3, 2021, Plaintiff Edward Chung filed this employment action against Defendant Billy Lee, aka Billy K. Lee, doing business as Palux Optics Corp.

 

On May 31, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:  

1.      Breach of Contract

2.      Failure to Pay Earned Wages

3.      Failure to Timely Pay Wages at Time of Termination

4.      Wrongful Termination and Retaliation in Violation of Public Policy

The FAC alleges that Plaintiff Edward Chung (hereinafter “Plaintiff”) while working for Defendant Billy Lee, aka Billy K. Lee, doing business as Palux Optics Corp. (hereinafter collectively referred to as “Defendant”) was denied payment of wages and suffered retaliation culminating in his wrongful termination in violation of public policy. Due to the Defendant’s conduct, Plaintiff experienced loss of wages, emotional distress and other damages.

On January 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication of the FAC’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action.

No opposition was filed. 

II. ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment or adjudication of the FAC’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action.

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(f) provides that a defendant may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action or one or more claims for damages if it is contended that the cause or claim has no merit. Motions for summary adjudication are procedurally identical to motions for summary judgment. (Serri v. Santa Clara University (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 859.)

 

“A party may move for summary adjudication of issues, either by itself or as an alternative to summary judgment. If it appears that the proof supports the granting of the motion for summary adjudication as to some but not all the issues involved in the action, or that one or more of the issues raised by a claim is admitted, or that one or more of the issues raised by a defense is conceded, the court shall, by order, specify that those issues are without substantial controversy. Moreover, upon a motion for summary adjudication, the court shall, by written order or oral order recorded verbatim, specify those issues raised by the motion for summary adjudication as to which there exists a material, triable controversy, and shall specifically refer to the evidence which establishes a triable issue of fact regarding each of those issues ....”  (Cal. Code. Civ. Proc. § 437c(f)).   

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment/summary adjudication bears the initial burden of showing that “there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Plaintiff's¿initial¿burden¿of proof in moving for summary judgment, however, d[oes] not include disproving any affirmative defenses asserted by defendants.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co.¿(2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564, citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853 (“Aguilar”) [“summary judgment law in this state no longer requires a¿plaintiff¿moving for summary judgment to disprove any defense asserted by the defendant as well as prove each element of his own cause of action.... All that the¿plaintiff¿need do is to ‘prove[ ] each element of the cause of action’ ”])  Once Plaintiff meets its initial burden of proving each element of the cause of action, “the burden shifts to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action¿or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

 

The California Supreme Court has recently confirmed that the purpose of the 1992 and 1993 amendments to the summary judgment statute was “‘to liberalize the granting of [summary judgment] motions.’” (Perry v. Bakewell Hawthorne, LLC (2017) 2 Cal.5th 536, 542.)  It is no longer called a “disfavored” remedy.  “Summary judgment is now seen as a ‘particularly suitable means to test the sufficiency’ of the plaintiff’s or defendant’s case.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.) 

 

B.      First, Second and Third Causes of Action: Breach of Contract, Failure to Pay Earned Wages, and Failure to Timely Pay Wages at Time of Termination

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication of the first, second and third causes of action.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

Pursuant to Cal. Lab. Code § 201, when an employee is discharged, the employee's earned and unpaid wages become due and payable immediately. Further, Cal. Lab. Code § 202 requires employers to compensate their employees their final paycheck immediately upon termination.

 

Cal. Lab. Code § 203 (a) provides: “If an employer willfully fails to pay, without abatement or reduction, in accordance with Sections 201, 201.3, 201.5, 202, and 205.5, any wages of an employee who is discharged or who quits, the wages of the employee shall continue as a penalty from the due date thereof at the same rate until paid or until an action therefor is commenced; but the wages shall not continue for more than 30 days.”

 

The FAC alleges that Plaintiff entered into an Employment Agreement with Defendant on or about January 9, 2019, in which Plaintiff was to work as a general manager for Defendant, and in return for his services, Defendant was to compensate Plaintiff $60,000.00 annually payable monthly on the last day of each month. (FAC, ¶¶ 10-12.) The FAC further alleges that despite Plaintiff’s performance, Defendants breached the Employment Agreement by failing to compensate Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶¶ 22-23.) Plaintiff alleges he never received payment from Defendant during his employment and Plaintiff was terminated in or about March 2019. (FAC, ¶¶ 23-24.) The FAC alleges that as a result, Plaintiff has suffered damages and loss of earnings. (FAC, ¶¶ 24-26.)

 

Plaintiff argues that there exist no disputed issues of material fact regarding any of the elements of the breach of the Employment Agreement and Defendant’s failure to compensate Plaintiff. In support, Plaintiff submits his declaration and the Employment Agreement. (Chung Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 3.) The executed Employment Agreement states, in relevant part:

 

“As compensation for the services provided by Edward Eunjay Chung under this Contract, Palux Optics Corp. will pay Edward Eunjay Chung an annual salary of $60,000.00 payable monthly on the last day of each month and subject to applicable federal, state, and local withholding.”

 

(Chung Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 3, p. 1.) Plaintiff states that he never received compensation from Defendant during his employment and that he has suffered loss of earnings. (Chung Decl., ¶¶ 6-11.)

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff also attaches purported text messages between him and Defendant Lee as evidence. However, the messages appear to be in Korean and Plaintiff fails to provide a certified translation. Evidence Code §753(a) provides that “[w]hen the written characters in a writing offered in evidence are incapable of being deciphered or understood directly, a translator who can decipher the characters or understand the language shall be sworn to decipher or translate the writing.” Specifically with respect to motions, California Rule of Court 3.1110(g) requires that for any filing to the court, “[e]xhibits written in a foreign language must be accompanied by an English translation, certified under oath by a qualified interpreter.” Thus, the Court disregards this evidence.

 

Nevertheless, the Court finds that Plaintiff has met his burden. Plaintiff provided a copy of the Employment Agreement showing the existence of a contract between the parties and Plaintiff sufficiently provided testimony attesting to Defendant’s failure to pay Plaintiff’s wages. The burden shifts to Defendant. Yet, Defendant did not file an opposition and did not provide evidence of a disputed material issue of fact. 

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s first, second and third causes of action.

 

C.      Fourth Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination and Retaliation in Violation of Public Policy

 

Next, Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication of the fourth cause of action.

 

“The elements of a claim for wrongful discharge in violation of public policy are (1) an employer-employee relationship, (2) the employer terminated the plaintiff’s employment, (3) the termination was substantially motivated by a violation of public policy, and (4) the discharge caused the plaintiff harm.  It is well established that a termination premised on an employee’s refusal to violate either a statute or an administrative regulation may support a claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy.”  (Nosal-Tabor v. Sharp Chula Vista Medical Center (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1224, 1234, cleaned up.) Pursuant to Cal. Lab. Code § 98.6, it is unlawful to terminate an employee in retaliation for claiming unpaid wages, reporting another wage and hour violation, or filing a lawsuit to claim backpay.

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff has sufficiently established an employer-employee relationship by providing the executed Employment Agreement and Plaintiff’s testimony that he was employed by Defendant. Plaintiff states in his declaration that he raised the issue of not receiving any compensation for his services to Defendant, and Defendant subsequently terminated him in or around March 2019. (Chung Decl., ¶¶ 6-8.) Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently met his burden as to the fourth cause of action. The burden shifts to Defendant. However, Defendant did not file an opposition and did not provide evidence of a disputed material issue of fact. 

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action. In sum, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

AVERY v. BELANGER, et al. (22STCV18602)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Jason Stone (Stone & Sallus, LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  R. Steven Derryberry (Derryberry & Associates LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT ONLY ESCROW, INC. (filed 08/05/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. is GRANTED. The truth of the matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. in the reduced amount of $1,515.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 7, 2022, Plaintiff Veronica Avery filed the instant action against Defendants Catherine Belanger (“Catherine”), Benjamin Belanger, Kannella, Inc. dba Re/Max All-Pro (Re/Max), and Does 1-10.

 

On October 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Elder Financial Abuse

2.      Breach of Fiduciary Duty

3.      Fraud

4.      Violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200

5.      Declaratory Relief and Injunctive Relief

The FAC alleges that Plaintiff is an elder with dementia and cognitive decline, and that Plaintiff suffered damages when Defendants took her home against her will and forged her signature to complete the sale of her home to themselves at a discounted price.

 

On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc., arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves the Court for an Order that the truth of each matter specified in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One to Defendant, served on June 7, 2024, be deemed admitted and established for all purposes in this action

·       Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendant and/or its counsel in the amount of $2,000.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

 When a party fails to serve a timely response to a request for admission, the party propounding the request for admission may move for an order to deem the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.280, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction… on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff served Request for Admissions, Set One on Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. on June 7, 2024. (Carpinelli Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Defendant’s response was due on or before July 9, 2024, however, no response has been served. (Ibid.)  Therefore, because a response has not been served, the motion is granted. The truth of the matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions for counsel’s work on the instant motion. Plaintiff requests a total of $2,000.00, representing 2 hours spent on the motion and 2 hours to draft a Reply and appear at the hearing, at an hourly rate of $485, plus $60 for the filing fee. (Id. ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Defendant has failed to respond. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court reduces the total award by $485.00 which represents one hour of Plaintiff counsel’s hourly rate. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the total amount of $1,515.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted is GRANTED. The truth of the matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant in the reduced amount of $1,515.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order.

KIMI KIM v. JACOB WOOHER (23STCV26561)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Kimi Kim (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant: Kevin Hermansen (Law Office of Daniel J. Bramzon & Associates, P.C.)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE/VACATE JUDGMENT, THE AMOUNT OF ATTORNE FEES (filed on 08/01/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Judgment is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 31, 2023, Plaintiff Kimi Kim filed this lawsuit against Defendant Jacob Woocher alleging causes of action for:

C/A 1: Fraud Intentional Misrepresentation 

C/A 2: Conversion Theft 

C/A 3: Legal Malpractice

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant lied, committed perjury and submitted falsified documents in his representation of an unlawful detainer action. The unlawful detainer action was filed by Plaintiff and Defendant provided legal representation to the defendants in that underlying action.

 

On December 20, 2023, Defendant filed a Special Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s Complaint pursuant to C.C.P Section 425.16 (Anti-Slapp).

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a request for dismissal of the entire action without prejudice.

 

On March 21, 2024, the Court granted the Motion for Attorneys Fees filed by Defendant. The Court stated in its Minute Order: “The court finds that Woocher would have prevailed on his anti-SLAPP motion as to the fraud cause of action. Woocher is therefore entitled to attorneys’ fees pursuant to section 415.16, subdivision (c).” (Court’s 03/21/24 Minute Order, p. 1.)

 

On April 11, 2024, the Court entered Judgment for Defendant against Plaintiff in the total amount of $5,565.00. 

 

On April 2, 2024, Defendant (now Judgment Creditor) filed an Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Plaintiff (now Judgment Debtor). 

 

On June 25, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s Motion to Modify Attorney Fees.

 

On August 13, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the Judgment Creditor's Application for Order for Appearance and Examination of the Plaintiff/Judgment Debtor. Upon Plaintiff’s failure to appear as previously ordered, a bench warrant was ordered and held until September 19, 2024, and bail was set in the amount of $5,000.00. The hearing was continued to September 19, 2024.

 

On August 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Judgment the Amount of Attorney Fees. Plaintiff seeks to set aside/vacate the amount of attorney fees granted to Defendant on March 21, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff seeks an order vacating or setting aside the order granting attorneys fees to Defendant on March 21, 2024. However, the Court finds the instant motion is an improper motion for reconsideration.  (CCP § 1008.)   

 

The moving party must present new facts, circumstances, or law to meet its burden on a motion for reconsideration.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); see also Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.)  The party seeking reconsideration of an order shall state by affidavit what application was made before, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts or circumstances are claimed to be shown.  (Code Civ. Proc, § 1008.)  Further, “…the party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.”  (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457.)  The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered and some valid reason for not offering it earlier.  (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.)  A disagreement with a ruling is not a new fact that will support the granting of a motion for reconsideration.  (Id.) 

 

Here, the Court has already heard Defendant’s motion for attorneys fees. Plaintiff filed an opposition on March 19, 2024. After the motion was heard and argued, the Court granted Defendant’s motion on March 21, 2024.

 

In addition, on April 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Modification of the Amount of Attorneys Fees again requesting the Court to modify the amount of attorneys fees granted to Defendant. The Court denied that motion on June 25, 2024.

 

In the instant motion, Plaintiff does not present any new laws, facts, or circumstances that would justify reconsidering the Court's previous holding. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Judgment is DENIED.

 

III. CONCLUSION

 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Judgment is DENIED.
