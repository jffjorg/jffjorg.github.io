DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20SMCV00819    Hearing Date: June 24, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Yang v. Venice Business Partners Inc., et al.

CASE NO.:                20SMCV00819

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Further Deposition

HEARING DATE:   6/24/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

            In the absence of contrary court order, a civil litigant’s right to discovery is broad. “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action . . . if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010; see Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301.)

 

            “If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production." (CCP § 2025.480 (a).) “If the court determines that the answer or production sought is subject to discovery, it shall order that the answer be given or the production be made on the resumption of the deposition.” (CCP § 2025.480(i).) Failure to obey an order may be considered contempt of court and could subject a party to the action to issue, evidentiary, or terminating sanctions as well as monetary sanctions. (CCP § 2025.480(k).)

 

            The only proper basis to instruct a deponent to not answer a question is an objection based upon a privilege or manifestly irrelevant questions or questions designed only to harass. (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1015 n. 3; see also LASC Local Rules, Rule 3.26 Appendix 3.A(e)(9) [“Counsel shall not direct a deponent to refuse to answer questions unless they seek privileged information or are manifestly irrelevant or calculated to harass.”].)

 

            Further, a motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections. (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion for order compelling further responses “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) Absent a claim of privilege or attorney work product, the moving party meets its burden of showing good cause by a fact-specific showing of relevance. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) If the moving party has shown good cause for the RPDs, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Ibid.) 

 

 

Analysis

 

Defendant Venice Business Partners, Inc. moves to compel Plaintiff Tom Yang to: (1) produce at a resumed deposition of Yang all documents responsive to requests for production numbers 1-3, 5-14, and 16-26 contained; (2) re-appear for resumption of the deposition of Yang to answer questions, without objection except for objections on the basis of privilege, regarding said responsive documents; or (3) alternatively, if Yang is not in possession, custody or control of the responsive documents, serve on Defendant, without any objection, verified written responses to each request pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.230-2031.285. Defendant also seeks sanctions in the amount of $9,605.00 against Yang and his counsel of record, Steven M. Garber and Steven M. Garber & Associates, jointly and severally.

 

Request Nos. 1-3

 

Requests Nos. 1-3 pertain to all documents and communications between Yang and Levine regarding acquisition of any vehicle outside of the Subject Vehicle, which is a 2018 Mercedes G650 Landaulet VIN# WDCYC7EF2JX286162. The Court concurs that these requests are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Simply put, any communications or documents pertaining to the parties’ acquisition of vehicles other than the Subject Vehicle are not relevant to the dispute concerning the Subject Vehicle. Defendant does not explain how such transactions would have any bearing on the issues presented in this action. The Court is therefore not inclined to grant the motion on these requests.

 

Request No. 5

 

This Request seeks documents evidencing or referring to Yang’s filing of an undertaking of $900,000 pursuant to the Court’s September 25, 2020, Order for Writ of Possession. Yang objects on the grounds of relevance and privacy.

 

If there is a serious invasion of a constitutional right to privacy, the party seeking the evidence must establish that the information sought is not only essential and directly relevant, but also that this information could not be discovered through less intrusive means. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) If there is no serious invasion to a privacy right, the court must simply balance the right of privacy against the interest in having just litigation. (Id.; Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 371.) Personal financial information comes within the zone of privacy protected by the California Constitution, Article 1, Section 1. (Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, 655-656; see Schnabel v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 712-713 [finding a right to privacy in confidential customer information in whatever form it takes, including tax returns, checks, statements, or other account information].) The state's privacy provision “extends to one's confidential financial affairs as well as to the details of one's personal life” (Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Superior Court¿(2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 368.)

 

The Court concurs that this request is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Yang’s filing of an undertaking has no bearing on the substantive legal or factual issues presented in this action. Instead, Defendant seeks private and irrelevant financial condition evidence. Since the sought documents are not essential or directly relevant to the issues in this action, Yang’s privacy and relevancy objections would be sustained.

 

The Court is therefore not inclined to grant the motion on this request.

 

Request nos. 6-14 and 16-24

 

            Each of the cited requests seek documents evidencing certain factual allegations in the SAC that Levine made certain representations to Plaintiff, or efforts that Yang took to investigate the truth of such representations. Yang asserts that these requests are unauthorized contention requests for production, which are appropriate for interrogatories but not a request for production at deposition. (Rifkind v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 1255, 1259.)

 

The rule discussed in Rifkind would not apply to the requests at issue. Rifkin specifically discussed questions that ask a deponent during deposition to “state all facts, list all witnesses and identify all documents that support or pertain to a particular contention in that party’s pleadings.” (Rifkind, supra, 22 Cal.App.4th at p. 1259, emphasis added.) The Rifkind court reasoned that a lay witness could not be reasonably expected to answer legal contentions during a deposition, as this would require a lay person to substantively analyze and apply the laws to the facts on the fly. (Id. at 1263.) The Rifkin court emphasized that parties may ask deponents about the basis for, or information about, factual conclusions or assertions in the complaint, as distinguished from basis for legal conclusion:

 

We emphasize at the outset what we are not discussing: questions at a deposition asking the person deposed about the basis for, or information about, a factual conclusion or assertion, as distinguished from the basis for a legal conclusion. Thus, if a deponent says that a certain event happened at a particular time or place, it is quite proper to ask the person, at deposition, how he or she became aware of it, his or her knowledge about it, and for similar information of a factual nature.

 

(Id., at 1259, emphasis added.)

 

The questions posed here do not seek information on Plaintiff’s legal conclusions. Instead, they each ask Plaintiff for factual information regarding the alleged misrepresentations. For example, no. 6 requests evidence of Plaintiff’s allegation at paragraph 46, that Levine represented that “VBP, for several years, was, and will continue to be, a valid and ongoing business concern.” Request no. 24 seeks documents evidencing or referring to “any efforts [Yang] took between January 1, 2019, and March 1, 2020, to investigate the truth of the representations that the Second Amended Complaint alleges LEVINE made to YANG in October of 2018.”  

 

            Plaintiff posed no further objections to the notice. As Plaintiff’s sole objection is without merit, further production is required.

 

Request nos. 25-26

 

Requests nos. 25-26 seek documents produced to Yang by Defendants’ banks – Bank of America and JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A., pursuant to subpoenas issued by Yang.

 

Yang does not explain the legal basis of his objections. Yang apparently concedes that such requests are not improper, as Yang “has already agreed to ‘turn over copies of the bank records’”. (Garber Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 2.) Yang does not state that he has produced such documents or has been questioned on such documents at deposition. Therefore, the Court would grant the motion as to these requests.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to request nos. 6-14 and 16-26. Plaintiff is ordered to attend a resumed deposition within 10 days, produce any responsive documents at that deposition, and answer questions related to the documents without objection except for objections based on privilege. The parties are ordered to meet and confer on a mutually agreeable time and date for the resumed deposition. If Yang is not in possession, custody or control of categories of responsive documents, Plaintiff must serve on Defendant verified written responses to each request that Yang lacks the ability to comply with the requests at least 5 days prior to the resumed deposition. (See CCP §§ 2031.230-2031.285.)

 

SANCTIONS

 

Defendant seeks sanctions in the amount of $9,605.00 against Yang and his counsel of record, Steven M. Garber and Steven M. Garber & Associates, jointly and severally. The Court must sanction any party that unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response, “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (CCP, § 2025.480(j).) Plaintiff does not demonstrate that he acted with substantial justification in opposing the motion as to nos. 6-14, 16-26. Therefore, sanctions are mandatory. That said, the motion was only partially successful. Further, the hours claimed on the motion appear excessive. Considering the totality of the record, the Court finds that a reasonable sanction in this instance would be $3,860.00.

 

Accordingly, the request for sanctions is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $3,860.00, inclusive of costs, against Plaintiff Yang and his counsel of record, Steven M. Garber and Steven M. Garber & Associates, jointly and severally. Sanctions are to be paid within 30 days.
