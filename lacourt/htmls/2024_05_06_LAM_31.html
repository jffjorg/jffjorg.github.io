DEPARTMENT 31 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling by emailing Dept. 31 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. The email address is smcdept31@lacourt.org. Please do not call the court to submit on the tentative. Please do not submit to the tentative ruling on behalf of the opposing party. Please do not e-mail the Court if you plan to appear and argue.

In deciding whether to submit on the tentative ruling or attend the hearing and present oral argument, please keep the following in mind:

The tentative rulings authored by this court reflect that the court has read and considered all pleadings and evidence timely submitted to the court in connection with the motion, opposition, and reply (if any). Because the pleadings were filed, they are part of the public record.

Oral argument is not an opportunity to simply regurgitate that which a party set forth in its pleadings. Nor, is oral argument an opportunity to "make a record" when there is no court reporter present and the statements and arguments of counsel are already part of the record because they were set forth in the pleadings. Finally, simply because a party or attorney disagrees with the court's analysis and ruling or is not satisfied with it does not necessarily warrant oral argument when no new arguments will be articulated.

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify all other parties email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. If all parties to the motion submit, this tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date and it will be memorialized in a minute order. This tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such document will be considered by the Court.

**Tentative rulings on Motions for Summary Judgment will only be available for review in the courtroom on the day of the hearing.


Case Number: 21STCV16180    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 31

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Kerry Bensinger, Department 31

 

 

HEARING DATE:     May 6, 2024                           TRIAL DATE:  January 27, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Carlos Chavez v. Schlumberger Technology Corporation, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV16180

 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT,

OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Schlumberger Technology Corporation and Timothy Ramey

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Carlos Chavez

 

 

I.          FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

Carlos Chavez (“Chavez”) was employed by Schlumberger Technology Corporation (“Schlumberger”).  In the early 2000s, Chavez was diagnosed with diabetes.  Years later, as a result of that condition, Chavez developed ulcers and gangrene in his right foot.  Chavez informed Schlumberger of his condition and thereafter went on CFRA leave from October 2018 to May 2019.  Upon his return to work in or about May 2019, Chavez was allowed to work in an office.  His job title and duties changed but his salary remained the same.  Chavez complained to Human Resources about the change in his job position, which was eventually restored.  As alleged by Chavez, his complaint upset Chavez’s supervisor, Timothy Ramey (“Ramey”), who responded by mocking Chavez during shift change team meetings. 

 

In March 2020, Chavez developed an ulcer on his left foot.  In April/May 2020, Chavez informed Ramey that his doctor directed him to take time off.  Ramey replied, “you can’t take a doctor’s notice because if you do, you’re going to get terminated” or words to that effect.  

 

In June 2020, Chavez was laid off.  Chavez was told the purported reason for his layoff was cost-saving measures related to COVID-19.  Chavez argues the purported reason for his termination was contrived, and the real reason he was terminated was because of his physical disabilities, his need to take medical leave due to his physical disabilities, and his need for future leave(s) of absence due to his physical disabilities.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

            When reviewing a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿ (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)¿ A motion for summary judgment must be granted “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  

 

            “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿ (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (Scalf).)¿ A defendant seeking summary judgment “bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (Aguilar).)  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿ A moving defendant need not conclusively negate an element of plaintiff’s cause of action.¿ (Aguilar, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿ 

 

            To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿ (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿ It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿ (Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891 (Gaggero).)¿ The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿ (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)¿ 

 

            “Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿A plaintiff opposing summary judgment defeats the motion by showing one or more triable issues of material fact exist as to the challenged element. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 849.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)¿ 

 

            The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿ (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037 (Yanowitz); Aguilar, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.)¿ “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿ While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿ [Citation.]¿ Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿ “Put another way, have defendants conclusively negated a necessary element of the [plaintiff’s] case or demonstrated that under no hypothesis is there a material issue of fact that requires the process of trial?”  (Jeld-Wen, Inc. v. Superior Court (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 853, 860, internal citation omitted.) Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿ [Citation.]¿ Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility.¿ [Citation.]” ¿(Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel.¿Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 (Weiss) [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)¿¿

            Analysis          

 

The Complaint alleges nine causes of action.  Defendants seek summary judgment, or alternatively, summary adjudication of each cause of action.  The Court addresses them in turn.

 

1.      First Cause of Action for Interference with Right to Medical Leave Under the CFRA (Gov. Code § 12945.2(t))

 

“A [California Family Rights Act (“CFRA”)] interference claim consists of the following elements: (1) the employee’s entitlement to CFRA leave rights; and (2) the employer’s interference with or denial of those rights.”  (Choochagi v. Barracuda Networks, Inc. (2020) 60 Cal.App.5th 444, 454; Bachelder v. America West Airlines, Inc. (9th Cir. 2001) 259 F.3d 1112, 1125 [“The regulation promulgated by the Department of Labor, 29 C.F.R. 825.220(c) plainly prohibits the use of FMLA-protected leave as a negative factor in an employment decision. In order to prevail on her claim, . . . [plaintiff] need only prove by a preponderance of the evidence that her taking of FMLA-protected leave constituted a negative factor in the decision to terminate her. She can prove this claim, as one might any ordinary statutory claim, by using either direct or circumstantial evidence, or both.”].)  An interference claim under the CFRA does not invoke the burden shifting analysis of the McDonnell Douglas test.  (Faust v. California Portland Cement Co. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 864, 879 (Faust).)

 

Chavez’s interference claim is based upon Ramey’s “implied threat” that if Chavez took  “any more disability time off, you will be laid off because Schlumberger is laying off people.”   (Complaint, ¶¶ 15-16.)  As a result, Chavez did not submit his request for leave and continued to work against his doctor’s recommendation.    

 

Defendants argue the interference claim fails because Chavez was granted all leaves of absences he requested.  In support, Defendants submit evidence to show (1) Chavez took medical leave for eight months beginning in October 2018 (Defendants’ Undisputed Material Facts (UMF) 1.5) and (2) Chavez’s doctor issued notes excusing him from work from May 19, 2020 to June 30, 2020 (Roberts Decl., Ex. 6).  Chavez ultimately did not submit a request for medical leave in April 2020.  Defendants meet their initial burden.

 

The burden shifts.  Chavez does not dispute that he took a leave of absence from October 2018 to May 2019.  Chavez contends that although his doctor issued notes excusing Chavez from work due to disability, Chavez did not actually take time off work for fear he would be terminated.  (Plaintiff’s Appendix of Evidence, Chavez Depo., Ex. B, pp. 51:25-65:8, 113:13-16, 157:4-160:24, 181:12-182:16.)  Chavez demonstrates that Schlumberger (via Ramey’s implied threat) interfered with Chavez’s request for medical leave by dissuading Chavez from submitting his doctor’s note and requesting leave.  Chavez meets his burden to show there are triable issues regarding his interference claim.

 

In reply, Defendants pivot and submit two new arguments.  First, they argue Chavez exhausted his entitlement to CFRA leave within a 12-month period.  Second, Defendants contend there is no evidence to show Plaintiff’s foot infection qualified him for CFRA leave. 

 

Each argument fails.  First, Defendants’ “exhaustion of CFRA leave” argument is undeveloped.  Government Code section 12945.2 provides, in relevant part, “It shall be an unlawful employment practice for any employer … to refuse to grant a request by any employee with more than 12 months of service with the employer, and who has at least 1,250 hours of service with the employer during the previous 12-month period or who meets the requirements of subdivision (r), to take up to a total of 12 workweeks in any 12-month period for family care and medical leave.  (Gov. Code, § 12945.2, subd. (a).)  Leave provided for pursuant to this section may be taken in one or more periods. The 12-month period during which 12 workweeks of leave may be taken under this section shall run concurrently with the 12-month period under the FMLA, and shall commence the date leave taken under the FMLA commences.  (Gov. Code § 12945.2, subd. (o).)  California Code of Regulations section 11090 provides, in part, “If the leave is common to both CFRA and FMLA, this 12-month period will run concurrently with the 12-month period under FMLA. An employer may choose any of the methods (the calendar year, any fixed 12-month “leave year,” the 12-month period measured forward from the date any employee's first CFRA leave begins, or a “rolling” 12-month period measured backward from the date an employee uses any CFRA leave) allowed in the FMLA regulations that became effective March 8, 2013, 29 C.F.R. Part 825, section 825.200, subdivision (b), for determining the 12-month period in which the 12 weeks of CFRA leave entitlement occurs.  (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 2, § 11090, subd. (b).) 

 

Here, Defendants do not submit any evidence to show which method it employs in calculating CFRA or FMLA leave.  Indeed, if Defendants calculate leave according to the calendar year or a fixed 12-month “leave year”, Chavez, if he had ultimately taken leave in April 2020, would not have exceeded his CFRA leave allowance.

 

Second, Defendants argument that Plaintiff’s foot infection does not qualify for CFRA leave fails because Defendants do not present any evidence in support of that proposition.  Merely pointing out Plaintiff’s lack of evidence is insufficient.  To meet the burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” ¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854, italics in original.)¿ It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿ (Gaggero, supra, 108 Cal.App.4th at p. 891.)¿ The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿ (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)¿ 

 

            The motion for summary adjudication of the First Cause of Action is DENIED.

           

2.      Second Cause of Action for Retaliation for Taking Medical Leave Under the CFRA (Gov. Code § 12945.2(t))

 

Discussion of the Second Cause of Action is complicated by the parties disagreement over the basis for Chavez’s retaliation claim.  Defendants argue the claim is based on Ramey’s mockery of Plaintiff’s job title change and Plaintiff’s termination for taking CFRA medical leave.  (Def. Motion at pgs 7-9.)  Plaintiff contends the claim is based on Chavez’s alleged demotion following his return from CFRA medical leave.  (Plaintiff’s Opp. at pgs 8-9.)

 

The court turns to the Complaint. “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues: the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings. [Citation.]”  (FPI Dev., Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 380.)  Evidence offered on an¿unpleaded¿claim, theory, or defense is irrelevant because it is outside the scope of the pleadings.”¿¿(California Bank & Trust v. Lawlor¿(2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 625, 637, fn. 3.)  In Laabs v. City of Victorville (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1256 (Laabs), the appellate court stated that “… new factual issues presented in opposition to a motion for summary judgment should be considered if the controlling pleading construed broadly, encompasses them.  In making this determination, courts look to whether new factual issues present different theories of recovery or rest on a fundamentally different factual basis.” 

Here, a review of the Complaint demonstrates that the Second Cause of Action is predicated on Chavez’s termination for taking medical leave due to his physical disabilities, and due to his exercise of his rights under the CFRA. (Complaint, ¶ 44.)  Having “delimited” the scope of the issues, the court revisits the parties’ arguments.

 

The CFRA “is intended to give employees an opportunity to take leave from work for certain personal or family medical reasons without jeopardizing job security.” (Soria v. Univision Radio Los Angeles, Inc. (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 570, 600 (Soria) (cleaned up); accord, Moore v. Regents of University of California (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 216, 233 (Moore).)  The CFRA provides that a qualified employee of an employer with 50 or more employees may take up to 12 weeks of family care and medical leave in any 12-month period.  (Gov. Code, § 12945.2, subd. (a).)  The act prohibits an employer from taking any adverse employment action against an individual because of his or her exercise of the right to family care and medical leave.  (Id., subd. (l)(1); Bareno v. San Diego Community College Dist. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 546, 560; Soria, at pp. 600-601.) 

 

            CFRA retaliation claims are subject to the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792 (McDonnell Douglas) burden-shifting analysis. (Bareno v. San Diego Community College Dist. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 546, 560 (Bareno) (cleaned up).)  In order to establish a prima facie case for retaliation as required by the first prong of the McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting analysis, an employee must show: (1) he or she engaged in a ‘protected activity[ ]’ [such as taking leave for a CFRA-protected purpose,] (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer's action.  If the employee successfully establishes these elements and thereby shows a prima facie case exists, the burden shifts to the employer to provide evidence that there was a legitimate, nonretaliatory reason for the adverse employment action.  If the employer satisfies this prong by producing evidence demonstrating the existence of a legitimate reason for the adverse employment action, the presumption of retaliation drops out of the picture, [citation], and the burden shifts back to the employee to provide substantial responsive evidence’ that the employer's proffered reasons were untrue or pretextual.”  (Bareno, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th 546, 560 (cleaned up).)

 

In the context of a motion for summary judgment, “an employer may satisfy its initial burden of proving a cause of action has no merit by showing either that one or more elements of the prima facie case ‘is lacking, or that the adverse employment action was based on legitimate nondiscriminatory factors.’ [Citations.]” (Husman v. Toyota Motor Credit Corp. (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 1168, 1181-1182.) If the employer shows it had a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the action, the plaintiff “‘may establish pretext “either directly by persuading the court that a discriminatory reason more likely motivated the employer or indirectly by showing that the employer’s proffered explanation is unworthy of credence.”’” (Morgan v. Regents of University of California (2000) 88 Cal.App.4th 52, 68-69.) “In responding to the employer’s showing of a legitimate reason for the complained-of action, the plaintiff cannot ‘simply show the employer’s decision was wrong, mistaken, or unwise. Rather, the employee must demonstrate such weaknesses, implausibilities, inconsistencies, incoherencies, or contradictions in the employer’s proffered legitimate reasons for its action that a reasonable factfinder could rationally find them ‘unworthy of credence,’ and hence infer that the employer did not act for the [asserted] non-discriminatory reasons.” (McRae v. Department of Corrections & Rehabilitation (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 377, 389-390 (cleaned up).) “[A]n employer is entitled to summary judgment if, considering the employer’s innocent explanation for its actions, the evidence as a whole is insufficient to permit a rational inference that the employer’s actual motive was discriminatory.” (Guz, supra, 24 Cal.4th at p. 361.) 

 

Prima Facie Case        

 

To establish a prima facie case of retaliation, a plaintiff must show that: (1) plaintiff engaged in protected activity; (2) the employer subjected the plaintiff to an adverse employment action; and (3) the protected activity and the employer’s action were causally connected. (Yanowitz, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 1042.)   

 

Focusing on whether Chavez was terminated for exercising CFRA rights, there are two time periods at issue: 2018-2019 (when Chavez took a seven-month medical leave), and April/May 2020 (when Chavez told Ramey about needing to take time off and possibly needing surgery).  Viewed independently, there is no casual connection between the 2018-2019 CFRA leave and the 2020, termination.  Viewed cumulatively, however, the court reaches a different conclusion.[2]

 

Added to the 2018-2019 leave are following additional April -June 2020 facts:

·         in April 2020, Ramey told Chavez not to submit his request for medical leave because if he did, he would be terminated.  As a result, Chavez did not submit a particular request for leave;

·         In May 2020, Chavez did submit other requests for medical leave, which were granted;

·         Ramey informed Reed that Chavez might require additional medical leave;

·         Ramey told Li that he would not consider Chavez for separation in a RIF and that he believed that Chavez eventually would recover to the point where he could take over his previous duties;

·         On June 19, 2020, Chavez tells Reed he has to go home because he is in so much pain.  Chavez also asks that if he could start working half-days from home. Reed replied, “yeah, we’ll see.”;

·         June 23, 2020, Chavez is terminated.

 

Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff told Ramey of the possible need to take medical leave.  Ramey, in turn, told Reed in May of 2020.  Reed played a role in deciding to terminate Plaintiff’s employment in June of 2020.  (See Declaration of Brandon Reed, ¶¶ 4-5.) 

 

Under CFRA, “[t]erminating an employee when it anticipates an otherwise eligible employee will be asking for a CFRA-qualifying leave in the future” constitutes an interference, restraint, or denial of the exercise of rights provided by CFRA.  (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 2 § 11094, subd. (a)(3).)  “CFRA's prohibition against “interference” prohibits an employer from discriminating or retaliating against an employee or prospective employee for having exercised or attempted to exercise CFRA rights[.]”  (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 2 § 11094, subd. (b).)

 

                With respect to notice, “California Code of Regulations, title 2, section 11091, subdivision (a)(1) describes the notice requirements of a reasonable request for CFRA leave in relevant part as follows: “[A]n employee shall provide at least verbal notice sufficient to make the employer aware that the employee needs CFRA leave, and the anticipated timing and duration of the leave. The employee need not expressly assert rights under CFRA or FMLA, or even mention CFRA or FMLA, to meet the notice requirement; however, the employee must state the reason the leave is needed, such as, for example, the expected birth of a child or for medical treatment. The mere mention of ‘vacation,’ other paid time off, or resignation does not render the notice insufficient, provided the underlying reason for the request is CFRA-qualifying, and the employee communicates that reason to the employer. The employer should inquire further of the employee if necessary to determine whether the employee is requesting CFRA leave and to obtain necessary information concerning the leave (i.e., commencement date, expected duration, and other permissible information).” (Italics added.) Thus, an employer bears a burden, under CFRA, to inquire further if an employee presents the employer with a CFRA-qualifying reason for requesting leave.”  (Moore v. Regents of University of California (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 216, 249 (Moore).)

 

Based upon the foregoing, Plaintiff presents a prima facie case that (1) he engaged in a protected activity; (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer's action.  The burden shifts to the Defendants.  Defendants challenged Plaintiff’s prima facie case by attacking the first element – engaging in protected activity.  Chavez both engaged in protected activity (2018-2019; May 2020); raised the issue with Ramey who told him not to submit his request for fear of being terminated; and requested leave on June 19, 2020.    

 

Defendants Legitimate, Nondiscriminatory Reason

 

Defendants argue the reduction in force precipitated by COVID was the reason for Plaintiff’s termination.  The burden shifts to Plaintiff.

 

Plaintiff’s Response. 

 

Given the close proximity between Plaintiff’s termination and Reed becoming aware of Plaintiff’s possible need for additional medical leave, the court finds there are triable issues of material fact regarding pretext.  As the appellate court stated in Moore, “the timing of the events leading up to Moore’s termination could suggest that something other than simple restructuring was at play.”  (Moore, supra, at p. 860.)  Indeed, “[p]retext may … be inferred from the timing of the company’s termination decision, by the identity of the person making the decision, and by the terminated employee’s job performance before termination.” (Flait v. North American Watch Corp. (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 467, 479.)  Plaintiff provided statements from Ramey that suggested Chavez should not be considered as part of the RIF.  And Ramey told Plaintiff that if he took leave (which he did in May), he would be terminated (which he was in June).  No doubt, Reed perceived Chavez as having ongoing disability issues and was aware Chavez might require additional medical leave.[3]     

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the Second Cause of Action is DENIED.

                                                                                       

3.      Third Cause of Action for Retaliation for Taking Medical Leave (Gov. Code § 12940(h))

 

The Third Cause of Action is based on Plaintiff’s termination for taking disability leave from October 2018 to May 2019 and between February 2020 and May 2020.  (Complaint, ¶ 48.) 

 

FEHA retaliation claims are subject to the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792 (McDonnell Douglas) burden-shifting analysis.  (Yanowitz v. L’oreal USA, Inc (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042.)  As with the Second Cause of Action, the Court begins by considering whether Plaintiff states a prima facie case for retaliation.

 

To establish a prima facie case of retaliation, a plaintiff must show that: (1) plaintiff engaged in protected activity; (2) the employer subjected the plaintiff to an adverse employment action; and (3) the protected activity and the employer’s action were causally connected. (Yanowitz, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 1042.)   

 

A confluence of factors complicates the ruling on Defendants’ challenge to the Third Cause of Action.  First, Defendants appear to be under the impression that Ramey’s mockery of Chavez forms the principal basis for the retaliation claim.  The Complaint, however, indicates otherwise.  The Complaint states the FEHA retaliation claim is predicated on Defendant’s termination of Plaintiff for engaging in protected activity under the FEHA for taking leave for his physical disability in 2019 and between February and May 2020.  (Complaint ¶ 48.)

 

            As discussed above, Plaintiff states a prima facie case.  Defendants challenge Plaintiff’s prima facie case under the FEHA. Notably, the FEHA includes the following language: (2) For an employer or other entity covered by this part to, in addition to the employee protections provided pursuant to subdivision (h), retaliate or otherwise discriminate against a person for requesting accommodation under this subdivision, regardless of whether the request was granted.” (§ 12940, subd. (m).)(emphasis added.)  Plaintiff’s challenge fails. 

            For the same reasons stated above, Plaintiff raises triable issues of fact with respect to Defendant’s pretext.           

The motion for summary adjudication of the Third Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

4.      Fourth Cause of Action for Discrimination (Gov. Code § 12940(a))

 

Plaintiff alleges that Schlumberger discriminated against him because of his physical disability.  (Complaint, ¶ 52.) 

 

            FEHA discrimination claims are subject to the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792 (McDonnell Douglas) burden-shifting analysis.  (Sandell v. Taylor-Listug, Inc. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 297)

 

                In the context of disability discrimination, the plaintiff initially has the burden to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. The plaintiff can meet this burden by presenting evidence that demonstrates, even circumstantially or by inference, that he or she (1) suffered from a disability, or was regarded as suffering from a disability; (2) could perform the essential duties of the job with or without reasonable accommodations, and (3) was subjected to an adverse employment action because of the disability or perceived disability. To establish a prima facie case, a plaintiff must show actions taken by the employer from which one can infer, if such actions remain unexplained, that it is more likely than not that such actions were based on a [prohibited] discriminatory criterion.... The prima facie burden is light; the evidence necessary to sustain the burden is minimal. (Sandell v. Taylor-Listug, Inc. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 297, 310 (cleaned up).)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not establish a prima facie case of disability discrimination.  Specifically, Plaintiff does not establish that he could perform the essential duties of his job.  Chavez testified that after he returned from his 2018/2019 medical leave, he “couldn’t do the job that I used to do.  I couldn’t go to the shop anymore.  Build, supervise at – at the same time … Or I could supervise, but I couldn’t build equipment, test, and go on the floor like I used to do before, before my disability.”  (Chavez Depo., at p. 126:13-26.)  Plaintiff argues he was well-qualified for the position and cites the same testimony for the proposition he could still supervise.  However, Chavez’s admissions that he was not capable of building and testing equipment and “go[ing] on the floor like [he] used to do before [his] disability” stand unrebutted.  Indeed, Plaintiff states in his Separate Statement, “Ramey testified that Chavez had an extensive skill set and that Chavez would have been his pick to continue leading the shop over Castellanos if he was physically capable of doing so, and that his skillset would be very valuable to retain, but only if he was physically able to perform all those skills, which was not the case when Ramey resigned.”  (PAF 57, emphasis added.)  

 

While the Defendant may be correct that Chavez could not perform the functions of his job at that time, Defendant fails to address the contention that Plaintiff could do so with a reasonable accommodation.  A reasonable accommodation includes time off work.  (Moore, supra, 248 Cal. App.4th at p. 243.)  And in this case, time off work to have surgery.  Ramey specifically told Li that he believed Chavez eventually would recover to the point where he could take over his previous duties.  Plaintiff establishes a prima facie case. 

 

As discussed above with respect to the Second Cause of Action, Plaintiff raises triable issues of material fact.  

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the Fourth Cause of Action is DENIED.     

 

5.      Fifth Cause of Action for Failure to Accommodate (Gov. Code § 12940(m))

 

            “Two principles underlie a cause of action for failure to provide a reasonable accommodation.  First, the employee must request an accommodation.  Second, the parties must engage in an interactive process regarding the requested accommodation and, if the process fails, responsibility for the failure rests with the party who failed to participate in good faith.  While a claim of failure to accommodate is independent of a cause of action for failure to engage in an interactive dialogue, each necessarily implicates the other.” (Moore v. Regents of University of California (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 216, 242.)

 

            “A term of leave from work can be a reasonable accommodation under FEHA, and, therefore, a request for leave can be considered to be a request for accommodation under FEHA.”  (Moore v. Regents of University of California (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 216, 243.)(internal citations omitted.)  

 

            Defendant argues the Fifth Cause of Action fails because Defendant considered Plaintiff’s accommodation requests and accommodated him.  In support, Defendants point to Chavez’s testimony where he admits that Defendants permitted Plaintiff to work from home from time-to-time in 2020.  (Chavez Depo., p. 58:17-59.)  The burden shifts.

 

            Plaintiff points to contrary evidence.  Chavez told Ramey that he needed to take medical leave and Ramey told him not to submit the request or he would be terminated.  Being told by a supervisor not to submit a request for accommodation cannot be used by the employer as a basis to argue the request for accommodation was not made.  Similarly, Chavez requested time off in June and Reed, told him, “we’ll see.”  Shortly thereafter, Chavez  was terminated.  Instead of accommodating Plaintiff, he was terminated.  The court finds Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of material fact whether Defendants failed to reasonably accommodate Plaintiff. 

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the Fifth Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

6.      Sixth Cause of Action for Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process (Gov. Code § 12940(n))

 

FEHA requires employers to engage in a good faith interactive process to determine effective reasonable accommodations, if any, “in response to a request for reasonable accommodation by an employee . . . with a known physical or mental disability . . . .”  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (n); Raine v. City of Burbank (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1222.)  To establish a claim for failure to engage in the interactive process, a plaintiff must show: (1) defendant was an employer; (2) plaintiff was defendant’s employee; (3) plaintiff was disabled; (4) plaintiff requested reasonable accommodation; (5) plaintiff was willing to participate in a timely good faith interactive process with plaintiff to determine whether a reasonable accommodation could be made; (6) defendant failed to participate in this process; (7) plaintiff was harmed; and (8) defendant’s failure to engage in a good-faith interactive process was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  (CACI No. 2546.)¿

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the Sixth Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

7.      Seventh Cause of Action for Harassment (Gov. Code § 12940(j))

 

The Seventh Cause of Action is asserted against Schlumberger and Ramey.  Chavez’s harassment claim is based on Ramey repeatedly mocking Chavez in front of subordinates during team meetings, Ramey telling Chavez that if he takes any more disability time, Schlumberger would lay Chavez off, and Ramey telling Chavez that he should get a scooter so he could do his work faster.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 12, 15, 17.)  Chavez alleges that the foregoing constitutes harassment on account of his physical disability and race.  (Complaint, ¶ 66.)

 

The FEHA states: “It is an unlawful employment practice. . . .[f]or an employer, labor organization, employment agency, apprenticeship training program or any training program leading to employment, any other person, because of race, religious creed, color, national origin, ancestry, physical disability, mental disability, medical condition, genetic information, marital status, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, age, sexual orientation, or military veteran status, to harass an employee, an applicant, an unpaid intern or volunteer, or person providing services pursuant to a contract.” (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(1).)  “To constitute harassment, the conduct must be so objectively severe or pervasive as to create a hostile or abusive working environment.  Factors to consider in this context include the frequency of the conduct, its severity, whether it is physically threatening or humiliating, and whether it unreasonably interferes with the employee's work performance. (Doe v. Dep’t of Corr. & Rehab. (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 721, 736.)  Harassment must be of a repeated, routine, or generalized nature when the harassing conduct is not severe.  (See Lyle v. Warner Bros. Television Productions (2006) 38 Cal.4th 264, 283.) Harassment that is occasional, isolated or sporadic is insufficient. (Id.) 

 

Defendants argue this claim fails because Ramey’s mocking did not concern Chavez’s disability, the comments were not severe or pervasive, and did not interfere with Chavez’s work performance.  Here, it is undisputed Ramey teased Chavez about mispronouncing customer names, wearing “girl’s glasses” and/or drinking “girl tea” on over 15 occasions.  (PAF 66.)  On their face, the comments are not related to Chavez’s disability or race.  Moreover, there is no evidence these comments interfered with Chavez’s work performance.  As to Ramey’s “implied threat” that Chavez would get fired if he took any more time off and Ramey’s scooter comment, Defendants argue, generally, that these comments are isolated and not severe or pervasive enough to constitute disability harassment.  Defendants meet their initial burden.

 

The burden shifts.  Chavez argues that the “girl’s glasses” and “girly tea” comments are actionable harassment under the FEHA because he believed they were precipitated by Chavez’s complaint to Human Resources regarding his alleged demotion.  Chavez further argues that fifteen instances is severe and pervasive.  The court disagrees.  First, as framed by the Complaint, Ramey’s comments, and not Chavez’s complaint to Human Resources about his alleged demotion, forms the basis for the Seventh Cause of Action.  (Complaint, ¶ 66.)  Plaintiff cannot assert an unpleaded theory of liability to oppose a motion for summary judgment.  (California Bank & Trust, supra, 222 Cal.App.4th at p. 637, fn. 3.)  The same applies to Ramey’s teasing of Chavez’s mispronunciation of customer names.  Second, the comments (girl’s glasses, girl tea, mispronunciation, complaint to Human Resources) are not actionable because they are not tied to Chavez’s physical disability or race. 

 

In essence, this claim boils down to Ramey’s comments about using a scooter and warning Plaintiff not to take more time off.  Of the two comments, only Ramey’s scooter reference can be considered harassment.  This is because Ramey’s warning not to take more time off is simply that—a warning.  The warning may have had the effect of prompting Plaintiff not to take off work, but on its face, there is nothing in the remark that can be objectively construed as abusing Plaintiff for being disabled, or physically threatening or humiliating.

 

This leaves the scooter reference.  Plaintiff, however, does not demonstrate how this one comment is severe enough to constitute harassment.  Harassment must be of a repeated, routine, or generalized nature when the harassing conduct is not severe.  (See Lyle, supra, 38 Cal.4th at p. 283.)  Harassment that is occasional, isolated or sporadic is insufficient. (Id.)  The FEHA is not a civility code.  (Id. at p. 295.)  The scooter reference is not abusive.  And one comment alone does not rise to the level of pervasive.  Further, Plaintiff does not show how the scooter reference altered the terms of his employment.  At most, Ramey’s scooter reference is isolated conduct.  This is insufficient to sustain a claim for harassment.

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the Seventh Cause of Action is GRANTED.

 

8.      Eighth Cause of Action for Failure to Prevent Harassment, Discrimination and Retaliation (Gov. Code § 12940(k))

 

A cause of action for failure to prevent discrimination or retaliation requires the following elements: (1) plaintiff was an employee of defendant; (2) plaintiff was subjected to discrimination/retaliation in the course of employment; (3) defendant failed to take all reasonable steps to prevent the discrimination/retaliation; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s failure to take all reasonable steps to prevent discrimination/retaliation was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  (CACI No. 2527; Jumaane v. City of Los Angeles (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 1390, 1410.)¿ “The employer’s duty to prevent discrimination and retaliation is affirmative and mandatory.”  (Northrop Grumman Corp. v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 1021, 1035.)   

 

            This cause of action is intertwined with Plaintiff’s retaliation claims.  Given the court’s ruling regarding those claims (Second, and Third Causes of Action), the court finds there are triable issues regarding Plaintiff’s claim for failure to prevent retaliation.

 

            The motion for summary adjudication of the Eighth Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

9.      Ninth Cause of Action for Wrongful Discharge in Violation of Public Policy

 

A cause of action for wrongful termination in violation of public policy requires the following elements: (1) plaintiff was employed by defendant; (2) defendant discharged plaintiff; (3) violation of public policy was a substantial motivating reason for plaintiff’s discharge; and (4) the discharge caused plaintiff’s harm.  (CACI No. 2430.)¿ “As a matter of law, only an employer can be liable for the tort of wrongful discharge in violation of public policy.” ¿(Khajavi v. Feather River Anesthesia Med. Grp. (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 32, 53.) ¿  

 

The parties summarily address the Ninth Cause of Action.  Defendants argue that because this claim is based entirely on violations of the CFRA and FEHA, Chavez’s wrongful discharge claim should likewise fail.  Because the court has found there are triable issues regarding Plaintiff’s CFRA and FEHA claims, summary adjudication of the wrongful discharge claim is not proper.

             

            The motion for summary adjudication of the Ninth Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

V.        CONCLUSION

 

            The motion for summary judgment is denied.

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the First, Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, Eighth, and Ninth Causes of Action is DENIED.

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the Seventh Causes of Action is GRANTED.

CASE:                         Steve K. Lopez v. Mai Thai

 

CASE NO.:                      23STCV14971

 

 

MOTION FOR INTERLOCUTORY JUDGMENT OF PARTITION ON THE PLEADINGS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Steve K. Lopez

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant Mai Thai

 

 

I.          BACKGROUND

 

This is a partition action concerning the real property located at 1114, 1114 ½, and 1116 S. Kern Ave., Los Angeles, CA 90022 (the “Subject Property”).  On June 27, 2022, Plaintiff Steve K. Lopez (“Plaintiff” or “Lopez”) filed a Verified Complaint for Partition By Sale of Real Property against Defendant Mai Thai (“Defendant” or “Thai”)[1].  Plaintiff alleges that he and Defendant are joint tenants with equal 50% ownership interest in the Subject Property.  The grant deed evidencing the parties’ equal ownership of Subject Property as joint tenants is attached as Exhibit B to the Verified Complaint.

 

On February 1, 2024, Defendant filed her Answer.  The Answer is not verified.  Defendant admits that she and Plaintiff each hold an undivided 50% interest in the Subject Property.

 

On November 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion for interlocutory judgment of partition and for appointment of a referee.  On March 15, 2024, the court denied the motion.

 

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Recording of Pendency of Action Pursuant to CCP § 405.20 (Lis Pendens) concerning the Subject Property.

 

Plaintiff now moves for an interlocutory judgment of partition on the pleadings and for an order appointing a referee.  Defendant filed an opposition.  Plaintiff replied.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.¿ [Citations.]”¿ (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057 1064.)¿ The court must assume the truth of all properly pleaded material facts and allegations, but not contentions or conclusions of fact or law.¿ (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Wise v. Pacific Gas and Elec. Co. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 725, 738.)¿ “A judgment on the pleadings in favor of the defendant is appropriate when the complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(3)(B)(ii).)”¿ (Kapsimallis v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 667, 672.)¿ A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be brought by a plaintiff on the grounds that “the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(3)(A).)  “Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.¿[Citation.]”¿ (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.)¿ “The common law ground for a motion for judgment on the pleadings is identical to the statutory ground[.]”  (Korchemny v. Piterman (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 1032, 1055.)  Allegations are to be liberally construed.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)¿¿¿ 

 

If the motion for judgment on the pleadings is granted, it may be granted with or without leave to amend.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (h)(1).)¿ “Where a demurrer is sustained or a motion for judgment on the pleadings is granted as to the original complaint, denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion if the pleading does not show on its face that it is incapable of amendment.”¿ (Virginia G. v. ABC Unified School Dist.¿(1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1852 (emphasis added).)¿ 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

 

A.    Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of the following documents:

(1)   Plaintiff’s Verified Complaint;

(2)   Defendant’s Answer;

(3)   Grand Deed transferring the Subject Property to “Steve K. Lopez, a Single Man,” recorded on May 18, 2012 in the Official Records of Los Angeles County as Instrument Number 20120748102;

(4)   Grant Deed transferring the Property to “Steve K Lopez, a Single Man and Mai Phuong Thai, a Single Woman as Joint Tenants,” recorded on June 5, 2012, in the Official Records of Los Angeles County as Instrument Number 20120833519; and

(5)   Deed of Trust in favor of Paramount Residential Mortgage Group, Inc., recorded against the Subject Property on May 18, 2012, in the Official Records of Los Angeles County as Instrument Number 20120748103.

The unopposed requests are GRANTED.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (d)(1), (h).)

 

B.     Analysis

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be brought by a plaintiff on the grounds that “the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(3)(A).)  “When the complaint is verified, the answer shall be verified.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 446, subd. (a).)

 

Plaintiff seeks a judgment on the issue of his right to partition of the Subject Property and for the appointment of a referee.

 

A co-owner has an absolute right to partition unless barred by a valid waiver. (Orien v. Lutz (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 957, 962; Code Civ. Proc., § 872.210; Code Civ. Proc., § 710, subd. (b); see also Lazzarevich v. Lazzarevich (1952) 39 Cal.2d 48, 50 [co-owner includes joint tenants].)  The absolute right to partition may be waived by contract, either express or implied. (See Orien, supra, 16 Cal.App.5th at p. 963; see also LEG Investment v. Boxler (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 484, 493.) “If the court finds that the plaintiff is entitled to partition, it shall make an interlocutory judgment that determines the interests of the parties in the property and orders the partition of the property and, unless it is to be later determined, the manner of partition.” (Code Civ. Proc., §872.720)  As such, to establish a right to partition, a plaintiff must allege that he has never waived his right to partition, that the parties are joint tenants with equal ownership interests, and the manner of partition, unless it is to be determined later.  Each element is alleged.

 

Waiver

 

The first requirement is that the “court finds that the plaintiff is entitled to partition unless barred by a valid waiver.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.720, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges he “has never waived the right to partition in writing or in any other manner.”  (Verified Complaint, ¶ 20.)  In Defendant’s unverified Answer, Defendant alleges that the “Complaint . . . is barred by reason of Plaintiff’s actions, representations, conduct and/or omissions, and Plaintiff has waived each and every alleged cause of action, remedy, and/or other rights against Defendant.”  (Answer, Third Affirmative Defense.)  However, because Defendant’s Third Affirmative Defense is not verified, Defendant’s Answer is insufficient to state a defense.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 446, subd. (a).)  Accordingly, the first element is met.  Defendant does not present any argument to the contrary.

 

Ownership Interests

 

The second factor is “the statute’s plain requirement that the parties’ ownership interests be determined before or when the manner of partition is decided.”  (Summers v. Superior Court (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 138, 143.)

 

Here, Plaintiff establishes by Grant Deed that the Subject Property was granted evenly between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (See Verified Complaint, ¶ 3, Ex. B.)  In her Answer, Defendant admits to the same.  She alleges, “Defendant does not deny she holds an interest in the subject property located  1114,  1114 1/2, and 1116 S. Kern Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90022 (the “Property”), as provided in Paragraph 2 of the Complaint, as joint tenants with Plaintiff and Defendant each holding an undivided 50% interest in the Property.”  (Answer, General Denial.)  The second element is satisfied.  Defendant does not raise any relevant argument to the contrary.[2]     

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff demonstrates agreement that the parties have equal ownership interests in the Subject Property.  It is further uncontroverted that Plaintiff has not waived his right to partition.  The court therefore finds that Plaintiff is entitled to partition. 

 

            Manner of Partition & Referee

 

Plaintiff alleges that “[p]artition by sale is more equitable than division in kind for the Property. The Property is a multi-family residence with a single assessor’s parcel number with no surplus land, making division in kind in the Property unfeasible as the co-owners would receive an interest valued at a price materially less than the share of proceeds the co-owners would receive through the sale of the Property as a whole, thereby resulting in loss to the co-owners.”  (Verified Complaint, ¶ 22.)  Defendant alleges that the “Complaint … is barred because Partition of sale of the property would be unfair under the circumstances.”  (Answer, Eleventh Affirmative Defense.)  Given this disagreement, the manner of partition must be determined at a later time.

 

Given the disagreement in the manner of partition, appointment of a referee is necessary.  “The referee or any party may, on noticed motion, petition the court for instructions concerning the referee’s duties.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 873.070.)  Additionally, the referee has a broad range of authority over the Subject Property as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 873.060, which states, “[t]he referee may perform any acts necessary to exercise the authority conferred by this title or by order of the court.”

 

Here, Plaintiff has nominated Matthew Taylor, an attorney, to serve as the referee.  Absent objections to Mr. Taylor’s candidacy, the court grants the request to appoint Matthew Taylor as the referee. The court orders Mr. Taylor to determine the manner of partition.  If Mr. Taylor decides that the tenants must vacate the Subject Property, the parties must return to the court to specifically request such authority.  The court adopts the Plaintiff’s remaining proposed terms.  (See Mot., p. 11.)

 

III.         CONCLUSION

                                                                                                                                

            The motion for interlocutory judgment of partition on the pleadings is GRANTED.  Interlocutory judgment of partition on the Subject Property is entered. 

 

            Plaintiff’s request for appointment of a referee is GRANTED.  Matthew Taylor is appointed as referee.           

 

            The court sets a Status Conference re: Manner of Partition for July 3, 2024 at 9:00 a.m.  Mr. Taylor is to submit his report regarding the manner of partition to the court no later than 5 court days prior to the status conference.
