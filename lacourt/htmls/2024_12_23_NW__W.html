DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV01130    Hearing Date: December 23, 2024    Dept: W

Kurtz v. un, et al.

 

MOTION to COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

 

Date of Hearing:          December 23, 2024                Trial Date:       February 10, 2025

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         19VECV01130

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiffs Gary Kurtz, Esq. and Law Office of Gary Kurtz, Inc.

Responding Party:       Defendant Ruth Soto

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 6, 2019, Plaintiffs Gary Kurtz, Esq. (“Kurtz”) and Law Office of Gary Kurtz, Inc. f/k/a as Law Office of Gary Kurtz, a PLC (“Law Office”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendant ABBA Bail Bonds, Inc. (“ABBA”), Jane Un (“Un”), Ruth Soto (“Soto”), International Capital Alliance, Inc. (“ICA”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and DOES 1 to 25, inclusive for: two counts of Breach of Written Contract; two counts of Common Count – Account Stated; and two counts of Quantum Meruit.

 

The Complaint alleges Plaintiffs entered into a written contract with Defendants to perform legal services pursuant to a hybrid billing arrangement at a billing rate, which was amended to increase over time. (Compl., ¶¶10, 14, 17, 21, 26, 29.) Defendants breached the written contract by failing to make payments as required by the engagement agreement. (Id. at ¶¶12-13.)

 

On May 23, 2022, this Court vacated default judgment entered against Defendant Soto.

 

On October 20, 2022, this Court entered default judgment against Defendants ABBA, Un, and ICA jointly and severally for $176,708.07.

 

On September 23, 2024, Defendant Soto filed an Answer to Complaint.

 

On November 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents. On December 10, 2024, Defendant Soto filed an Opposition. On December 13, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs Gary Kurtz, Esq. and Law Office of Gary Kurtz, Inc. Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Ruth Soto is ordered to provided verified, objection-free responses and responsive documents to Special Interrogatories Nos. 1-3 and Request for Production of Documents Nos. 1-3 within 30 days of this order.

 

Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $2,560.00 and payable by Defendant Soto and her counsel of record within 30 days of this order.

 

Defendant Soto’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Parties may move for a further response to interrogatories and requests for production of documents where an answer to the requests are evasive or incomplete or where an objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (a); 2031.310, subd. (a).)

 

Notice of the motions must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c); 2031.310, subd. (c).)

 

Finally, Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3)).¿¿

 

PRELIMINARY ISSUES

 

As the preliminary matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff improperly combined its motion to compel further special interrogatories and request for production of documents. Multiple motions cannot be combined into a single filing.¿¿(See¿Govt. Code¿§ 70617(a)(4) (setting forth the required filing fee for each motion, application, or any other paper or request requiring a hearing);¿see¿also¿Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011)¿(“Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.”) Similarly, improperly combines the requests into one separate statement. Plaintiff is advised to comply with the filing requirements moving forward.

 

Likewise, Defendant Soto contends she was not served with the instant motion until two days before her opposition was due. (Chopuryan Decl., ¶¶2-4.) Contrarily, Plaintiffs have submitted evidence suggesting Defendant Soto was electronically served with the motion via an automatic eFiling service Plaintiffs use. (Kurtz Reply Decl., ¶12.)

 

Nonetheless, the Court will exercise its discretion and rule on the merits below.

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

A motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(b).)

 

Here, Plaintiff Kurtz attests to meet and confer efforts with Defendant Soto prior to the filing of this instant motion. (Kurtz Decl., ¶8, Ex. 6.) Plaintiff Kurtz further avers that Defendant Soto agreed to provide supplemental responses and failed to follow through. (Id. at ¶9.)

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs have fulfilled the meet and confer requirement.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs Gary Kurtz, Esq. (“Kurtz”) and Law Office of Gary Kurtz, Inc. f/k/a as Law Office of Gary Kurtz, a PLC (“Law Office”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) move to compel further Defendant Ruth Soto’s (“Soto”) responses to Special Interrogatories (“SROG”) Nos. 1-3 and Request for Production (“RFP”) Nos. 1-3 propounded on August 30, 2024. (Kurtz Decl., ¶¶2-5, Exs. 1-4.)

 

SROG NO. 1: To the best of your recollection state or approximate the unpaid balance of your loan, originally obtained from Argent Mortgage Company LLC but subsequently obtained by Wells Fargo Bank that was the subject of the action entitled Ruth Soto v. Wells Fargo Ban, et al, Riverside Superior Court, Case No. RIC 1503961 (“THE WELLS FARGO LOAN”) as of the last date you made a payment on said loan.

 

SROG NO. 2: To the best of your recollection state or approximate the unpaid balance of THE WELLS FARGO LOAN as of the date closest to August 19, 2016 that you recall.

 

SROG NO. 3: To the best of your recollection state or approximate the amount of the loan that was forgiven as the settlement of the action entitled Ruth Soto v. Wells Fargo Ban, et al, Riverside Superior Court, Case No. RIC 1503961.

 

RFP NO. 1: Any and all DOCUMENTS relating to THE WELLS FARGO LOAN.

 

RFP NO. 2: Any and all DOCUMENTS relating to the unpaid balance of THE WELLS FARGO LOAN, as of the date closest to August 19, 2016.

 

RFP NO. 3: Any and all DOCUMENTS relating to the amount of THE WELLS FARGO LOAN. That was forgiven as the settlement of the action entitled Ruth Soto v. Wells Fargo Ban, et al, Riverside Superior Court, Case No. RIC 1503961.

 

Here, Plaintiffs seek information and responsive documents regarding the amount of the unpaid balance on Defendant Soto’s loan that was subject to the action entitled Ruth Soto v. Wells Fargo Ban, et al., Riverside Superior Court, Case No. RIC 1503961 (“Underlying Action”). In response to these discovery requests, Defendant Soto objected on the basis of irrelevancy, attorney work-product privilege, and argues the information is equally available to Plaintiffs.

 

 

Relevancy

 

Specifically, Defendant Soto argues these requests are not relevant to the subject matter at issue in this case and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence because Plaintiffs unjustifiably withdrew as legal counsel in the Underlying Action. In reply, Plaintiffs assert there was good cause to withdraw as counsel for Defendant Soto in the Underlying Action.

 

“No California court has addressed the question of the right of an attorney on a contingent fee contract to assert a lien upon amounts recovered by a former client when the attorney discharges the client and is unable to point to some action of the client which provided the attorney with justifiable cause for abandonment of the case. As a general rule, other jurisdictions have allowed recovery for services already rendered only when the attorney has justifiable cause for withdrawing from the case. They have denied any compensation when the attorney has voluntarily abandoned the case without good cause. [Citation].”(Hensel v. Cohen (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 563, 567.)

 

Plaintiffs contend Defendant Soto agreed to a hybrid fee agreement in the Underlying Action and failed to keep with the hourly payment obligation. Defendant Soto does not contest this argument in her opposition. Thus, Plaintiffs had good cause to withdraw from the Underlying Action as legal counsel for Defendant Soto. Furthermore, the parties do not dispute payment for said legal representation in the Underlying Action is at issue in this present lawsuit. As such, information and responsive documents pertaining to the unpaid balance on Defendant Soto’s loan that was the subject of the Underlying Action, wherein Plaintiffs allegedly executed a settlement agreement on Defendant Soto’s behalf on a contingency basis are relevant to the case at bar.

 

Attorney Work-Product Privilege

 

Likewise, Defendant Soto contends the information and documents requested are protected by attorney work-product privilege because Plaintiffs were no longer her counsel of record when the Underlying Action was settled in 2019. On the other hand, Plaintiffs assert in their reply brief that they were able to settle a claim that resulted in Defendant Soto being excused from paying off a majority of her first deed of trust prior to the withdrawal. To the extent this statement is true, documents related to that particular settled claim would not constitute the attorney work-product of subsequent counsel in the Underlying Action.

 

Equally Available

 

Finally, Defendant Soto contends Plaintiffs possessed copies of the relevant records concerning the Wells Fargo Loan, which he was obligated to maintain as her counsel of record from January 2016 through November 2017. Defendant Soto also contends she does not have the documents Plaintiffs are requesting. However, Plaintiffs contend they returned all file materials when the representation relationship ended and the amount of forgiveness is only calculable with the documents that were returned to Defendant Soto.

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 2030.220 states:

 

“(a) Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.

 

(b) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.

 

(c) If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”

 

The Court notes that both parties assert the documents responsive to the RFPs are not in either of their possession, custody, or control. If this is Defendant Soto’s contention, her responses must reflect that. As to the interrogatories, Plaintiffs ask whether Defendant Soto has knowledge and recollection of the information sought, which is not something that would reasonably be equally available to Plaintiffs.

 

Therefore, further responses to the SROGs and RFPs are warranted.

 

Request for Sanctions

 

Where the court grants a motion to compel further responses, sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (d); 2031.310, subd. (h).)

 

Here, Plaintiffs seek $3,260.00 in monetary sanctions at an hourly rate of $500.00/hr plus $60.00 filing fees.  The court finds that defendant’s objections were substantially justified, although ultimately overruled, and that sanctions are inappropriate in this case.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs Gary Kurtz, Esq. and Law Office of Gary Kurtz, Inc. Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Ruth Soto is ordered to provided verified, objection-free responses and responsive documents to Special Interrogatories Nos. 1-3 and Request for Production of Documents Nos. 1-3 within 30 days of this order.

 

The parties’ cross requests for sanctions are denied.

Motion for leave to file Second Amended Complaint

 

Date of Hearing:          12-23-2024                              Trial Date:       TBD

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         24VECV00095

 

Moving Party:             DAROLD M SHIRWO (Plaintiff)

Responding Party:       none, unopposed.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff Darold M. Shirwo (Plaintiff) filed suit against the County of Los Angeles (County) and Emily J. Cole (Cole; collectively Defendants) arising out of a fee dispute. Plaintiff is a criminal defense attorney as well as a bar panel attorney for Los Angeles. Plaintiff alleged Cole improperly reduced a fee payment for work done in violation of policies and procedures established by the County to fulfil fee payment request pursuant to Penal Code section 987.2.

 

Plaintiff amended the complaint on March 4, 2024. Plaintiff now seeks permission to file a second amended complaint.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The motion for leave to file a second amended complaint is DENIED without prejudice.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The Court may, in the furtherance of justice, and upon any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 473, 576.)  There is “a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.” (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296.) Thus, the court generally grants requests for leave to amend unless the party seeking to amend has been dilatory in bringing the proposed amendment before the Court, and the delay in seeking leave to amend will cause prejudice to the opposing party. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.) 

 

The Rules of Court require that a motion to amend the pleading before trial must

 

(1)   Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading. . .

(2)   State what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where by page, paragraph, and line, the deleted allegations are located; and

(3)   State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

 

(Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1324, subd. (a).) Additionally, the moving party must include a supporting declaration stating the facts of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Procedural Deficiencies

 

Here, Plaintiff has not complied with the requirements of the Rules of Court. Plaintiff has not attached the proposed amended pleading. Plaintiff has not stated what allegations are being added or deleted. Plaintiff has included a declaration from counsel, but the declaration lacks adequate detail regarding the amendment. Counsel does not state the facts of the amendment, why it is proper, when the facts were discovered and why the amendment was not made earlier. The only context for amendment which Counsel gives is his statement that while having ongoing discussions with Defendants’ counsel “it appears that the first amended Complaint needs to be amended to conform to the facts that will be presented during the anticipate litigation herein.” (Declaration of Darold M. Shirow, ¶3.) This is facially deficient.

 

The motion is denied without prejudice.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion for leave to file a second amended complaint is denied without prejudice on procedural grounds. Plaintiff has not complied with Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324.


Case Number: 24VECV03167    Hearing Date: December 23, 2024    Dept: W

ALI SALEHPOUR v. RISING ROADS RECOVERY SERVICES FOR WOMEN, LLC, et al.

 

DEMURRER to plaintiffS’ First Amended COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        December 23, 2024              Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        24VECV03167

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Kimiko Miller

Responding Parties: Plaintiffs Ali and Suzanne Salehpour, individually and as personal representatives of The Estate of Amelia Salehpour, Deceased

Meet and Confer:      Demurrer, Bridwell Decl. ¶¶ 3, 4.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This wrongful death action arises from alleged negligence in the care and treatment of Amelia Salehpour, now deceased (“Decedent”).

 

The operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges the following. Decedent had struggled for years with (among other things) mental health issues and cognitive disability, which caused her to self-medicate and abuse drugs. In May 2023, Decedent was admitted to Defendant Rising Roads Recovery Services for Women, LLC (“Rising Roads”), a women’s only in-patient substance abuse program in Costa Mesa, California. Rising Roads then discharged her to a subcontractor facility, Defendant Saddleback Recovery. Five days after entering Saddleback Recovery, Decedent was allowed to discharge herself despite being a danger to herself and gravely disabled. Due to the defendants’ negligence, Decedent was (among other things) trafficked from the licensed treatment facility, and within 72 hours, beaten, raped, and killed over 60 miles away at a Los Angeles residence.

 

On July 2, 2024, Ali Salehpour filed this lawsuit.

 

On October 21, 2024, Ali Salehpour and Suzanne Salehpour (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), individually and as the personal representatives of Decedent’s estate, filed the FAC against 19 named defendants and Does 1 through 100, asserting the following causes of action:

 

1.      Professional Negligence;

2.      Negligence Per Se;

3.      Negligence;

4.      Violation of Mandatory Duty - Gov. Code § 815.6;

5.      Negligence;

6.      Negligent Hiring, Training, and Supervision;

7.      Continuation of Decedent’s Causes Of Action (Survival Action);

8.      Wrongful Death;

9.      Intentional Misrepresentation/Concealment; and

10.  Intentional Misrepresentation/Concealment.

 

Named defendant Kimiko Miller (“Miller”) now demurs the second (2nd) cause of action for negligence per se and the sixth (6th) cause of action for negligent hiring, training, and supervision, arguing that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Plaintiffs oppose the demurrer. Miller has filed a reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Kimiko Miller’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED and SUSTAINED IN PART as follows.

 

The demurrer to the second cause of action for negligence per se is OVERRULED.

 

The demurrer to the sixth cause of action for negligent hiring, training, and supervision is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

 

Plaintiffs are ordered to file and serve their second amended complaint within 30 days of this ruling.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Second Cause of Action for Negligence Per Se

 

Miller argues that the second cause of action for negligence per se fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because according to case law, “negligence per se” is an evidentiary doctrine and not an independent cause of action. Thus, the defendant argues, the Court should sustain his demurrer to that claim without leave to amend because Plaintiffs already has a separate negligence or malpractice claim (i.e., the first cause of action for professional negligence and third cause of action for negligence) against her.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that California courts have long stated that a plaintiff can assert separate, alternative, and even inconsistent legal theories of recovery in the same action, and that Miller concedes negligence per se is an alternate means of establishing negligence liability. Plaintiffs also argue it was efficient to assert a separate cause of action for negligence per se given the number of defendants and factual allegations at issue in this case.

 

In reply, Miller reiterates that it is clear from California case law that negligence per se is a burden of proof and not a separate cause of action.

 

The FAC asserts three negligence-related claims: the first cause of action for professional negligence, second cause of action for negligence per se, and third cause of action for (ordinary) negligence.

 

As Miller argues, “‘the doctrine of negligence per se is not a separate cause of action, but creates an evidentiary presumption that affects the standard of care in a cause of action for negligence.’ [Citation.]” (Turner v. Seterus, Inc. (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 516, 534 (“Turner”).)

 

Further, the California Supreme Court has held that there is no substantive distinction between professional negligence and ordinary negligence claims. (See Assurance Co. of America v. Haven (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 78, 81, fn. 3 [“The caption of the ACA complaint states it is for general negligence and professional negligence. The text of the complaint sets forth one cause of action for general negligence. A recent decision from the California Supreme Court, Flowers v. Torrance Memorial Hospital Medical Center, et al. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 992, 35 Cal.Rptr.2d 685, 884 P.2d 142 [“Flowers”], concludes there is no substantive distinction between a cause of action for professional negligence and one for ordinary negligence”]; see also Flowers, supra, 8 Cal.4th at pp. 996–997 [“The Court of Appeal majority erroneously premised their result on a perceived conceptual distinction between ‘ordinary’ and ‘professional’ negligence, which in their view differentiates separate and independent theories of liability even when based on the same facts asserted by the same plaintiff. While this distinction may be relevant and necessary for purposes of statutory construction and application (cf. Central Pathology Service Medical Clinic, Inc. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 181, 10 Cal.Rptr.2d 208, 832 P.2d 924 [construing ‘professional negligence’ as used in Code Civ. Proc., § 425.13] ), it is misplaced in resolving a motion for summary judgment in which the question is whether the moving party has demonstrated or negated negligence as a matter of law. In the latter context, the nature of the alleged breach of duty affects only the determination of the appropriate standard of care, which otherwise remains constant irrespective of the terminology used to characterize it”].)

 

However, the California Supreme Court has also stated that “[a]lternative theories of common law negligence and statutory liability may be pleaded in a single count [citation] or in separate counts [citation]; or the statutory basis of liability need not be pleaded at alll [sic], as the trial court is required to take judicial notice of acts of the Legislature (Evid. Code, s 451, subd. (a)).” (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 413 [emphasis added].)

 

Where plaintiffs have asserted separate theories of negligence liability as separate causes of action, California courts have treated those theories as one cause of action.

 

For example, in Turner, the California Court of Appeal (while reviewing a trial court’s order sustaining a demurrer) “treat[ed] [the] plaintiffs’ fourth and fifth causes of action [for negligence and negligence per se, respectively] as alleging a single cause of action for negligence.” (Turner, supra, 27 Cal.App.5th at p. 534.)

 

In this case, rather than requiring the Plaintiffs to amend the FAC to combine the different theories of negligence, the Court will proceed with the understanding that the Plaintiffs are asserting alternative theories of negligence.

 

Therefore, Miller’s demurrer to the second cause of action for negligence per se is OVERRULED.

 

Sixth Cause of Action for Negligent Hiring, Training, and Supervision


Miller demurs the sixth cause of action, arguing that the FAC failed to plead (1) Miller hired the employees named in the FAC, (2) Miller knew or should have known that the employees were unfit or incompetent, and the unfitness and incompetence created a particular risk to others, and (3) Miller’s negligence in supervising those employees was a substantial factor in causing the alleged harm.

 

“California case law recognizes the theory that an employer can be liable to a third person for negligently hiring, supervising, or retaining an unfit employee. [Citation.]” (Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1054.)

 

“In determining whether plaintiffs have stated sufficient facts to support their claim for negligent hiring, supervision, and retention of [an employee], we ask whether plaintiffs have alleged facts demonstrating that ‘“supervisory or administrative personnel who allegedly knew, or should have known,”’ or had reason to know ‘of the foreseeable risk [of a specific harm to third parties] by [the] employee and nevertheless hired, retained, and/or inadequately supervised that employee.’ [Citation.]” (Roe v. Hesperia Unified School District (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 13, 28–29; see also CACI No. 426 [stating that a plaintiff must prove (1) that the employer hired the employee at issue, (2) the employee was/became unfit/incompetent to perform the work for which they were hired; (3) the employer knew or should have know the employee was/became unfit/incompetent and the unfitness/incompetence created a particular risk to others; (4) the employee’s unfitness/incompetence/other particular risk harmed the plaintiff, and (5) the employer’s negligence in hiring/supervising/retaining the employee was a substantial factor in causing the plaintiff’s harm].)

 

The Court agrees with Miller that the sixth cause of action fails to state a claim against her because it fails to allege that Miller was the “employer” of any employee who was allegedly incompetent, unfit, or posed a particular risk to the Decedent.

 

In their opposition, Plaintiffs argue the FAC has pleaded an employment relationship by pleading that “Miller was the Clinical Director of Rising Roads and supervised certain employees at Rising Roads, including Llysa Lott, an Associate Marriage and Family Therapist.  FAC, ¶¶ 25, 155-56.” (Opposition, p. 13:22-24.)

 

However, California courts have held a manager or supervisor generally cannot be held liable for negligently supervising an employee, absent a special protective relationship between the supervisor and the plaintiff. (See C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 869-879 [finding school personnel owe duty of care to students based on the “special relationship” between school district and the district's pupils]; de Villers v. County of San Diego (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 238, 249 [finding no special protective relationship and thus no liability under section 815.2].)

 

Instead, “C.A. explained that ‘a school district and its employees have a special relationship with the district’s pupils, a relationship arising from the mandatory character of school attendance and the comprehensive control over students exercised by school personnel, “analogous in many ways to the relationship between parents and their children.” [Citations.]’ (C.A., supra, 53 Cal.4th at p. 869, 138 Cal.Rptr.3d 1, 270 P.3d 699.) [That] special relationship imposes ‘obligations beyond what each person generally owes others under Civil Code section 1714,’ including ‘the duty to use reasonable measures to protect students from foreseeable injury at the hands of third parties acting negligently or intentionally.’ [Citation.]” (Thomas v. Regents of University of California (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 587, 624 (“Thomas”).)

 

Here, Plaintiffs have not argued or alleged that Miller had any special relationship with Decedent such that Miller can be held directly liable for the alleged negligent hiring, training, or supervision of Rising Roads employees that allegedly harmed Decedent even if Miller was not those employees’ employer.

 

Further, even if the FAC has alleged that Miller had a special relationship with the Decedent, that does not automatically mean that Miller had the kind of duty that Plaintiffs seek to impose.

 

The Court would have to analyze the factors articulated in one California Supreme Court case, Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 (“Rowland”), to determine the existence and scope of Miller’s duty. (See Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 218-219 (“Brown”) [noting that cases that found a special relationship existed went on to analyze the factors in Rowland to determine the existence and scope of duty]; Thomas, supra, 97 Cal.App.5th at p. 625 [noting that C.A. and another case “analyzed the Rowland factors in determining the scope of the duty arising from the school-student special relationship ...”].)

 

The Rowland factors are “‘[1] the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, [2] the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, [3] the closeness of the connection between the defendant’s conduct and the injury suffered, [4] the moral blame attached to the defendant’s conduct, [5] the policy of preventing future harm, the extent of the burden to the defendant and consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for breach, and [6] the availability, cost, and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.” (Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 217.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs have neither discussed the Rowland factors in their opposition, nor cited any law that shows the FAC has stated facts sufficient to constitute a negligent hiring, training, or supervision claim against Miller.

 

For those reasons, Miller’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action for negligent hiring, training, and supervision is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.


Case Number: 24VECV04071    Hearing Date: December 23, 2024    Dept: W

barrera v. caledron, et al.

 

MOTION to STRIKE (NOT ANTI-SLAPP)

 

Date of Hearing:          December 23, 2024                Trial Date:       None set

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         24VECV04071

 

Moving Party:             Defendants Krista Castro and Pawparazzi Dogs, LLC

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Emeterio Marroquin Barrera

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff Emeterio Marroquin Barrera (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Sherrie Calderon (“Calderon”), Lovinjeet Singh (“Singh”), Lily Thomas (“Thomas”), Krista Castro (“Castro”), Giselles Legacy (“Legacy”), Pawparazzi Dogs LLC (“PDL”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and DOES 1 to 5o, inclusive for: (1) Strict Liability; (2) Assault and Battery; (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (4) Negligence.

 

The Complaint alleges on August 26, 2023, at approximately 2:30 p.m., Plaintiff was attacked by an unleashed, uncollared Pitbull dog named Koda, which resulted in severe and permanent injuries. (Compl., ¶1.) Plaintiff further alleges Defendants Calderon and Legacy were the owners of Koda, Defendant Singh the dog handler or custodian responsible for Koda, Defendant Thomas the coordinator and transporter responsible for Koda, and Defendant Castro the owner of Pawparazzi, a boarding facility responsible for Koda at the time of the incident. (Id. at ¶¶2-5.)

 

On October 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amendment to Complaint identifying BAW Foundation Capones Coalition, Inc. as Doe 1.

 

On November 18, 2024, Defendants Castro and Pawparazzi (“Moving Parties”) filed the instant Motion to Strike without Demurrer. On December 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition. On December 16, 2024, Moving Parties filed a Reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Krista Castro and Pawparazzi Dogs, LLC’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

Defendants must file an Answer within 20 days of this order.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)¿¿

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)¿

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

Prior to filing a motion to strike, moving party is required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video-conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.)¿¿¿

 

Here, Moving Parties’ counsel attests to attempts to meet and confer prior to the filing of the instant motion. (McIntyre Decl., ¶2, Ex. A.) However, Moving Parties’ counsel avers Plaintiff’s counsel never responded to the meet and confer letter or returned her phone call. (Id. at ¶73-5, Ex. B.) As such, the parties did not meet and confer per the statutory requirement before the filing of this present motion.

 

Nonetheless, the Court will exercise its discretion and rule on the merits below.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Krista Castro and Pawparazzi Dogs LLC (hereinafter, “Moving Parties”) move to strike paragraphs 4 (lines 13-17), 34 (line 9), 37 (lines 3-9), 46 (lines 4-11), 56 (lines 26 through 5), and the prayer for relief at lines 15-18, which make reference to punitive damages and exemplary damages.

 

Moving Parties argue the Complaint does not plead facts sufficient enough to support a request for punitive damages.

 

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the¿defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042. The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. Id. A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged, if proven, would not support a finding that the defendant acted with malice, fraud or oppression. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California (2010) 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63.¿) However, “Subdivision (b) thus authorizes the imposition of punitive damages on an employer in three situations: (1) when an employee was guilty of oppression, fraud or malice, and the employer with advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others, (2) when an employee was guilty of oppression, fraud or malice, and the employer authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct, or (3) when the employer was itself guilty of the oppression, fraud or malice.” (Weeks v. Baker & McKenzie (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1151.)¿ 

 

“Malice” is defined in section 3294(c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.¿¿“Fraud” is defined in section 3294(c)(3) as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”¿¿ 

 

To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87. Rather, a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he [or she] knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.” (Id. at 90). Further, the allegations must be sufficient for a reasonable jury to conclude that Defendant’s conduct was “despicable” defined as “base, vile or contemptible.” College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725.¿ 

 

The Complaint alleges on August 26, 2023, at approximately 2:30 p.m., “Plaintiff was lawfully exercising at Warner Park, a public park, located in the County of Los Angeles, State of California. Plaintiff was leaving the park and walking to his car when he was suddenly attacked by an unleashed, uncollared Pit bull dog named “Koda”. The unprovoked attack continued for several minutes during which time “Koda” aggressively and savagely bit Plaintiff multiple times, locked onto his wrist and ultimately knocked Plaintiff to the ground where the dog continued to attack attempting to reach Plaintiff’s neck area and go for in for the kill. Plaintiff fought off the dog for several minutes until finally a bystander jumped in to try and stop the attack and then he was attacked.” (Compl., ¶¶1, 15.)

 

The Complaint further alleges Defendant Castro is the owner of Defendant Pawparazzi, a boarding facility responsible for Koda at the time of the incident. (Id. at ¶5.)

 

The Complaint also alleges “Defendants CALDERON, THOMAS, CASTRO, GISELLES LEGACY and PAWPARAZZI DOGS LLC, knew that on August 15, 2022, “Koda” was surrendered to a shelter because his family was afraid of him. When the family brought “Koda” to the shelter it was documented by the shelter that “Koda” was barking, snarling, and trying to bite through the bars of the crate he was in. Defendants CALDERON, THOMAS, CASTRO, GISELLES LEGACY and PAWPARAZZI DOGS LLC knew that “Koda” was displaying “resource guarding” behavior prior to entrusting “Koda” to Defendant SINGH, to take the dog on a field trip to Warner Park on the date of the incident.” (Id. at ¶18.)

 

The Complaint also alleges “On or about April 8, 2023, prior to the attack on Plaintiff, “Koda’s” previous owners Joseline Moran and Marley Real attempted to get “Koda” off a victim of a previous attack and when they did “Koda” bit his owner on the right upper leg. As a result, the previous owner surrendered “Koda” to a shelter. On or about July 18, 2023, “Koda” was placed on the Red Alert List.” (Id. at ¶19.)

 

The Complaint further alleges “On July 21, 2023, Defendants CALDERON, THOMAS and GISELLES LEGACY rescued “Koda” from LA Animal Services through the New Hope program. Defendants CALDERON, THOMAS, CASTRO, GISELLES LEGACY and

PAWPARAZZI DOGS LLC, were made aware that “Koda” had a previous bite history, prior attacks on persons and despite being made aware of the violent past, they assumed responsibility for “Koda”. Defendants CASTRO and PAWPARAZZI DOGS LLC., knew that “Koda” was displaying “resource guarding” behavior prior to the attack on Plaintiff. Defendant SHERRIE CALDERON stated to investigator Anderson that all paperwork from the shelter “Koda” was rescued from are supposed to be handover to the boarding facility, Defendants CASTRO and PAWPARAZZI DOGS LLC.” (Id. at ¶20.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s Complaint states specific facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. Here, the Complaint alleges that Moving Parties were aware of Koda’s violent propensities including his previous bite and attack history. Furthermore, the Complaint alleges that Moving Parties assumed responsibility for Koda despite having this knowledge. Moreover, the Complaint alleges that Defendant Singh who was supposed to be watching Koda on the day of the incident is an agent of the Moving Parties and allowed Koda to be unleashed and uncollared in conscious disregard to Plaintiff’s rights. (See Compl., ¶11.) As such, a reasonable jury could infer that Defendant Singh was within the scope of her employment with Moving Parties when the attack occurred.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants Krista Castro and Pawparazzi Dogs, LLC’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

Defendants must file an Answer within 20 days of this order.


Case Number: 24VECV05879    Hearing Date: December 23, 2024    Dept: W

MOET FAWAZ V. SELECT PORTFOLIO SERVICING, INC., ET AL.

MOTION FOR PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

Date of Hearing:         December 23, 2024                Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         24VECV05879

Complaint Filed:          November 26, 2024

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Moet Fawaz   

Responding Party:       Defendant Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc.   

           

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Moet Fawaz alleges that in late 2023, he sought a loan modification because his income reduced sharply due to the economic effects of COVID-19. He claims that Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. delayed his application, contradicted themselves, and did not provide substantive responses, depriving him of a meaningful opportunity to resolve the loan arrears.

 

On November 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Complaint for:

 

1.      Statutory Entitlement to Injunction

2.      Violation of Civil Code section 2923.55

3.      Violation of Civil Code section 2923.6

4.      Violation of Civil Code section 2924

 

On November 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Ex Parte Application for the issuance of a TRO/Preliminary Injunction. The court granted the TRO and set the hearing for preliminary injunction for December 23, 2024.  

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction is DENIED.

 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of the following: (1) Notice of Default, recorded on December 26, 2023, Instrument No. 20230905482; (2) Notice of Trustee’s Sale, recorded on May 29, 2024, Instrument No. 20240349248.

 

The Court grants the requests. A court may take judicial notice of the fact of a document's recordation, the date the document was recorded and executed, the parties to the transaction reflected in a recorded document, and the document's legally operative language, assuming there is no genuine dispute regarding the document's authenticity. (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 256, 265.) From this, the court may deduce and rely upon the legal effect of the recorded document, when that effect is clear from its face. (Ibid.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Regarding preliminary injunctions, both irreparable harm and the likelihood of prevailing on the merits must be established. (Millennium Rock Mortg., Inc. v. T.D. Service Co. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 804, 812.) “The ultimate questions on a motion for a preliminary injunction are (1) whether the plaintiff is 'likely to suffer greater injury from a denial of the injunction than the defendants are likely to suffer from its grant,’ and (2) whether there is ‘a reasonable probability that the plaintiffs will prevail on the merits’” (Huong Que, Inc. v. Luu (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 400, 408. Procedurally, an application for a preliminary injunction, must be based upon sufficient evidence.  (Code Civ. Proc., §527(a); Bank of America v. Williams (1948) 89 Cal.App.2d 21, 29.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show all elements necessary to support the issuance of a preliminary injunction. (O'Connell v. Super. Ct. (Valenzuela) (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1481.)

Likelihood of Success on the Merits

Plaintiff contends that Defendant did not contact him to determine an option to avoid foreclosure in violation of Civil Code section 2923.5. However, in his declaration, he states that he spoke with an Elizabeth, who encouraged him to pursue an in-house loan modification. (Decl. Fawaz, ¶ 6.) He further states that he submitted documents to Defendant. (Decl. Fawaz, ¶¶ 7, 8.)

Plaintiff also contends that he submitted a completed loan modification before September 18, 2024, Defendant denied it on September 18, 2024, Plaintiff then appealed, and Defendant recorded the Notice of Trustee Sale without issuing an appeal decision. Plaintiff contends that this violates Civil Code section 2923.6. However, the relevant sections of Civil Code section 2923.6 apply when a borrower like plaintiff submits a completed loan modification before a notice of default or notice of sale is recorded. Here, the notice of default was recorded on December 26, 2023 and the notice of sale occurred on May 29, 2024. (RJN, No. 1 and 2.)

Plaintiff does not make a separate argument regarding Civil Code section 2924.17, which is what Plaintiff alleges Defendant violated as part of the fourth cause of action.

Plaintiff has not demonstrated Defendant violated the Civil Code section 2923.55, 2923.6, and 2924.17.

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff has failed to meet its burden of providing sufficient evidence in establishing a likelihood of prevailing on the merits

Irreparable Harm

Plaintiff contends they will suffer irreparable harm if their home is lost to foreclosure. Plaintiff fails to proffer any arguments or admissible evidence to support its contentions. These averments, without anything more, are insufficient in establishing irreparable harm.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff has failed to meet its burden of providing sufficient evidence in establishing a likelihood of prevailing on the merits and the suffering irreparable harm.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion is DENIED.
