DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 1806544    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: 26

8/19/2024

Dept. 26

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

CHEN, et al. v. GILMORE, et al. (18STCV06544)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Miranda Chen (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Michael Long (Long & Associates), Todd Stevenson (Stevenson Law Office), Daniel Friedlander (Law Office of Daniel Friedlander)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO PRESENT ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE & REQUEST FOR RELIEF (filed 04/09/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Present Additional Evidence & Request for Relief is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 29, 2018, Plaintiffs Miranda Chen aka Jien Chen (“Chen”) and Rolling Roles, LLC (jointly “Plaintiffs”) filed the instant action fraud action against Defendants Christine Gilmore, Red Velvet Entertainment, Inc., Blood Pageant, LLC, and Anthony J. Sands.  On September 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed the operative second amended complaint against Christina Gilmore aka Chris Gilmore aka Christina Ferra Gilmore dba Red Velvet Entertainment, Red Velvet Entertainment, Inc., a California Corporation, Red Velvet Entertainment, Inc. a Wyoming Corporation, Blood Pageant, LLC, Robert Burton, Anthony Sands, and Mark Askey.  The second amended complaint asserted three causes of action for (1) Fraud and Deceit, (2) Negligent Misrepresentation, and (3) Constructive Trust.   

 

On May 3, 2019, Defendants Christine Gilmore, Red Velvet Entertainment, Inc., a California Corporation, and Blood Pageant, LLC (collectively “Cross-Complainants”) filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiffs, Otis Cooper, Cindy Villareal, and Tony Boldi.  On October 14, 2022, Cross-Complainants filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint against Plaintiffs and Otis Cooper.  The Second Amended Cross-Complaint asserted four causes of action for (1) Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations, (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage, (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage, and (4) Breach of Contract. 

 

On June 6, 2023, pursuant to the jury verdict, judgment was entered in favor of Defendants on both the second amended complaint and the second amended cross-complaint. 

 

On August 9, 2023, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ motions for a judgment notwithstanding the verdict and for a new trial.

 

On April 9, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion for Leave to Present Additional Evidence & Request for Relief, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves to introduce new evidence pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 473

·       Plaintiff seeks to introduce an email from Defendant Red Velvet Entertainment dated April 9, 2020, that show discriminatory remarks and bias towards her because she is referenced in a derogatory manner. The evidence showcases Defendants’ prejudiced motivations.

Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·        Plaintiffs’ motion is untimely.

o   A motion to set aside judgment “shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” C.C.P. §473(b)

o   Notice of entry of judgment was served and filed on June 16, 2023. Chen’s motion was filed on April 9, 2024.

·       The email Plaintiffs attempt to introduce as new evidence is not new. The email was already presented at trial.

·       Plaintiffs cite to C.C.P. §473(b); however, Plaintiffs do not demonstrate any grounds to permit a new trial or to warrant the Court to set aside a jury verdict and judgment entered pursuant to said verdict

·       The motion improperly seeks reconsideration or new trial. Seeking to relitigate the merits of the underlying case is barred by collateral estoppel and res judicata.

Plaintiffs did not file a reply.

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

Section 473, subdivision (b) states in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.... Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Italics added.) 

 

Section 473, subdivision (b), provides for two distinct types of relief.  “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,” the court has discretion to allow relief from a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” a party or his or her attorney. Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,” the court shall vacate any “resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  The range of attorney conduct for which relief can be granted in the mandatory provision is broader than that in the discretionary provision, and includes inexcusable neglect.  But the range of adverse litigation results from which relief can be granted is narrower. Mandatory relief only extends to vacating a default which will result in the entry of a default judgment, a default judgment, or an entered dismissal.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615–616.)  ¿¿ 

 

To gain the statutory benefits, a party or legal representative must seek such relief “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); see Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980 (Rappleyea).  If relief is sought after the six-month period, a trial court may still vacate a default on equitable grounds even if statutory relief is unavailable.¿ (Olivera v. Grace (1942) 19 Cal.2d 570, 575-76.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

Plaintiffs seek to introduce newly discovered evidence under Code of Civil Procedure § 473. Plaintiffs argue that the evidence they seek to introduce is an email that is critical to the case and shows discrimination and bias against Plaintiff.

 

However, Plaintiffs motion fails to establish any basis for relief. While Plaintiffs cite to Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b), Plaintiffs have not shown any mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect that would allow them relief from judgment. Plaintiffs argue that the email is newly discovered evidence. However, Plaintiffs do not explain how the email was newly discovered. Moreover, in Defendants’ opposition, they assert that the email is not newly discovered evidence and was presented at trial. Plaintiffs did not file a reply and failed to respond to any of Defendants’ arguments.

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiff is entitled to relief under Code of Civil Procedure § 473. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Present Additional Evidence & Request for Relief is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Present Additional Evidence & Request for Relief is DENIED.

Daniel Figueroa v. Mohini, LLC (22STCV11779)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Kaveh Elihu and Melad Haddad from Employee Justice Legal Group     

 

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Lyne Richardson and Daniel Valenti from Ogletree, Deakins, Nash, Smoak & Stewart, P.C. 

 

Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment (filed on 03/08/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

Background

 

Factual Background

            Daniel Figueroa (Plaintiff) filed suit for wrongful termination after he was terminated in October of 2020, by his former employer Mohin, LLC (Defendant) after being hired six months earlier on June 14, 2020. Plaintiff alleges that during his time working with Defendant he was living with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS). (Complaint, ¶16.) Additionally, Plaintiff injured his hand in a non-work-related accident on October 25, 2020. (Complaint, ¶ 18.) Plaintiff’s Complaint states that he requested accommodations due to his disabilities. (Complaint, ¶ 19.) On October 29, four days after injuring his hand, Plaintiff arrived for his scheduled shift. (Complaint, ¶ 20.) When his supervisor noticed the injury, Plaintiff was instructed to take time off of work. (Id.) When Plaintiff contacted his supervisor on December 1, 2020 and notified him he was able to resume work, his supervisor allegedly responded, “We do not have any availability for you,” “I have to let you go.” “…you are always going to the bathroom, and I cannot deal with it.”(Complaint, ¶21.) Plaintiff then filed suit. 

 

Procedural Background

            Plaintiff filed a Complaint on April 7, 2022 containing seven causes of action against his former employer Mohini, LLC (Defendant). The causes of action are as follows:

 

1.     Discrimination in Violation of Gov. Code §§12940 Et Seq.;

2.     Retaliation in Violation of Gov. Code §§12940 Et Seq.;

3.     Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Retaliation in Violation Of Gov. Code §12940(K);

4.     Failure To Provide Reasonable Accommodations In Violation Of Gov. Code §§12940 et seq.;

5.     Failure to Engage in a Good Faith Interactive Process in Violation Of Gov. Code §§12940 et seq.;

6.     Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy; and

7.     Declaratory Judgment.

            The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment (the Motion). Plaintiff opposes the Motion; no reply was filed. 

 

Evidentiary Objections

            Plaintiff submitted several evidentiary objections with their opposition, primarily to the Declaration of Daniel E. Valenti. All objections are overruled.

    

            Likewise, Defendant submitted several evidentiary objections with their reply, primarily to the Declaration of Melad Haddad (Haddad Decl.). All objections are overruled.    

 

Discussion

 

Legal Standard

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

            “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. 

To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Analysis

            In their moving papers, Defendant contends that Plaintiff cannot establish a prima facie case of disability discrimination because Plaintiff was not disabled when he was terminated. Plaintiff proffers no evidence that either IBS or the hand injury suffered was a disability under the Fair Employment & Housing Act. Therefore, absent such evidence no cause of action based on Plaintiff’s disability can stand. Accordingly, the Motion is granted. 

  

            Defendant negates Plaintiff’s claim of a disability

            Defendant’s initial burden is to present facts that negate an essential element of Plaintiff’s claim. Plaintiff’s claim is based on discrimination and wrongful termination because of an actual or perceived disability.

            FEHA makes unlawful any discrimination based upon several broad and inclusive protected categories. “It is an unlawful employment practice, unless based upon a bona fide occupational qualification…(a) for an employer, because of the...physical disability [or]…medical condition … of any person, … to discriminate against the person in compensation or in the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment.”

(Gov. Code §12940(a).)

            In order to plead a violation of FEHA based on disability, a plaintiff must allege the following: (1) that plaintiff suffered from a disability, (2) plaintiff could perform the essential duties of the job with or without reasonable accommodations, and (3) plaintiff was subjected to an adverse employment action because of the disability. (Glynn v. Superior Court (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 47, 53.)

            In their moving papers, Defendant attempts to show there was no disability nor discrimination based on a disability. Defendant proffers the deposition of Plaintiff to demonstrate that Plaintiff, not only did not have a disability when terminated, but did not have a disability at all by his own admission.

Q: When you applied with Karim, did you notify him of any medical issues or disabilities that you have?

A: No.

(Evidence ISO Defendant’s MSJ, Exh. 2, pg. 29, lines 19-22.)

            Moreover, when Plaintiff informed his supervisor Karim of the need to use the restroom multiple times a day because of IBS, Plaintiff said that Karim responded “Okay, its’ fine.” (Id. pg. 32, line 6.) Plaintiff also never applied for disability benefits. (Haddad Decl., Exh. 6, pg. 9, line 17.)

            The main contentions of the parties surround the events that preceded Plaintiff’s termination. On October 25, 2020, Plaintiff injured his hand in a non-work-related incident which required immediate medical attention. Plaintiff testified in his deposition that he did not remember if he called his supervisor after the injury. (Evidence ISO Defendant’s MSJ, Exh. 2, pg. 89, lines 18-22.) Plaintiff was terminated the same month. When Ketan Sharma (Sharma), the owner of Defendant, was asked why, he stated that Plaintiff was fired because he abandoned his position after being a “no-call, no-show” for three days. (Haddad Decl., Exh. 1, pg. 149, lines 15-25.) Moreover, Sharma who made the final decision to terminate Plaintiff (Haddad Decl., Exh. 1, pg. 149, lines 8-14), was not aware of Plaintiff possessing any physical disability during employment. (Haddad Decl., Exh. 1, pg. 156, lines 17-24.)

            In their opposition papers, Plaintiff contends that Plaintiff’s IBS forced him to use the restroom multiple times, relieving him of his work duties, and that the laceration in his hand limited his work duties, thereby constituting a disability. However, Plaintiff’s arguments are unpersuasive, as little evidence is offered to counter Defendant’s evidence. There is no evidence that Plaintiff experienced discrimination based on his having IBS. At no point does Plaintiff contend that his supervisor or any other coworker reprimanded or discouraged him from using the restroom. Plaintiff also contends that whether Plaintiff abandoned his job is disputed. However, even by Plaintiff’s own admission, he does not recollect whether he informed Defendant about the hand injury after his termination, does not know if he was scheduled after his injury, and does not know if he complied with his schedule. (Evidence ISO Defendant’s MSJ, Exh. 2, pg. 126 and 129.)

             Without substantial evidence to dispute whether (1) Plaintiff had a disability and (2) whether Plaintiff was discriminated against and terminated for said disability, Plaintiff’s claims fail. Accordingly, the Motion is granted.

           

Conclusion and ORDER

            Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Christina Development Corporation v. Steven Raymond Short (22STCV28564) 

 

Counsel for Short:  Steve Short, in Pro Per 

Counsel for Christina Development Corporation:  Louis Miller (Miller Barondess, LLP) 

 

PLAINTIFF SHORT’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES – GENERAL, SET TWO (filed on 12/12/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery is MOOT.

 

The parties’ requests for sanctions are DENIED.   

 

I. BACKGROUND

On September 1, 2022, Plaintiff Christina Development Corporation (“CDC”) filed the complaint against Defendant Steve Raymond Short (“Short”) and Does 1 through 10. The complaint alleges (1) tortious interference with contractual relations; (2) defamation; (3) civil harassment; (4) conversion; and (5) breach of contract.¿¿ 

On January 18, 2023, Plaintiff Steve Short filed the case entitled Steve Short v. Christina Development Corporation; Christina Properties Limited, et al. Los Angeles Superior Court, Case No. 23VECV00203 for Labor Code violations against CDC, Christina Properties Limited, G&A Partners, LTD, David Allen Whitehead, Lawrence Norman Taylor, Adam Jacob Rosenkranz, Vincent Chan, Connor Stephen Wilks, JW Associates, LLC, Empire View Homes, LLC, and Crescent Hill Lofts, LLC.¿¿ 

On March 10, 2023, CDC filed a notice of related cases with the case entitled Steve Short v. Christina Development Corporation; Christina Properties Limited, et al. Los Angeles Superior Court, Case No. 23VECV00203. On March 29, 2023, the Court found the two cases related and set an Order to Show Cause as to why the cases should not be consolidated. (Minute Order 3/29/23.) On April 20, 2023, the Court consolidated the two actions for all purposes. (Minute Order 4/20/23.)¿¿ 

On December 12, 2023, Plaintiff Short filed the Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories – General, Set Two, arguing:

·       Short served Form Interrogatories – General, Set Two on Defendants Vincent Chan and David Whitehead. However, Chan and Whitehead failed to serve responses.

·       Short also seeks $500 in monetary sanctions against each Defendant.

 

On August 6, 2024, CDC Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendants’ responses to Plaintiff’s form interrogatories were originally due on October 20, 2023. However, Plaintiff granted to Defendants a couple of extensions which the parties had documented in writing. Defendants were concerned with divulging personal information in their discovery responses. The attorney handling this matter left Defendants’ counsel’s firm and this matter fell through the cracks. The new attorney handling this matter served Form Interrogatory responses on February 20, 2024. This was 5 months before the parties even had an operative Complaint. (Gill Declaration, ¶ 6, Exhibit C.)

·       Plaintiff’s instant motion cannot be granted because responses were served. If Plaintiff did not like the responses he received, he should have met and conferred and filed a motion to compel further responses.

·       Plaintiff’s request for permissive sanctions should be denied.

·       Defense counsel has been forced to prepare a full substantive opposition to Plaintiff’s motion, even though this motion should have been withdrawn or limited to Plaintiff’s sanctions request. Defendants therefore request sanctions under CCP Sections 2023.10(h), 2023.030, and 2030.290 in the amount of $2,550.

 

Plaintiff Short filed a reply maintaining that he is entitled to sanctions.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)  

 

The Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(c) provides in relevant part that,   

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under [Section 2023.010] against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).)

B.     Discussion

 

                                                    i.     Motion to Compel Responses

 

Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories – General, Set Two on Defendants Vincent Chan and David Allen Whitehead on September 18, 2023. (Short Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Responses were due on or before October 20, 2023.

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff had granted Defendants an open extension to provide form interrogatory responses, so that the parties could continue to meet and confer about sensitive discovery and disclosure issues. (Williams Declaration, ¶¶ 3-4.) However, Defendants’ prior counsel states he could not locate any documentation memorializing this extension and that after he transitioned to another law firm, this matter fell through the cracks. (Ibid.) Defendants’ counsel declares that verified responses were eventually served on February 20, 2024. (Gill Declaration, ¶ 6, Exhibit C.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s motion to compel is moot as Defendants’ counsel provided the requested responses. When respondents serve untimely discovery responses after parties have filed motions to compel initial responses, courts have broad discretion as to the ruling and may hold that the motion is moot. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., v. Klugman (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 409.) 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery is MOOT.

 

                                                   ii.     Sanctions

Plaintiff requests $500.00 in sanctions against each Defendant based on hours it took to research and prepare his motion. (Short Decl., ¶ 5.)  

In opposition, Defendants dispute this request for sanctions, arguing that Plaintiff “(a) had granted to Defendants an open extension to respond, (b) had served form interrogatories many months before there was an operative Complaint, (c) had no real reason to serve those discovery requests and did not raise them in opposing Defendants’ demurrers, and (d) had insisted upon Defendants’ disclosure of personal identifying information despite his criminal background.” (Oppos., p. 7.) Further, Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is procedurally deficient since his declaration does not provide a basis for calculating an award. Defendants assert that sanctions should be awarded against Plaintiff in the amount of $2,550 for Defendants’ work on its opposition.

Here, the Court will not award either parties’ request for sanctions. As discussed above, Plaintiff’s motion is moot. Defendants have already provided Plaintiff with responses to Plaintiff’s discovery request. The Court will not exercise its discretion to award sanctions.

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery is MOOT.

 

The parties’ requests for sanctions are DENIED.
