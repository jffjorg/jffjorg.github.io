DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCP00075    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Corson, et al., v. Widjaja, et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCP00075

MOTION:                  Motion for Leave to File a Cross-Complaint

HEARING DATE:   6/14/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

Leave of court is required to file a compulsory cross-complaint when it is not filed before or at the same time as the answer. (CCP § 428.50(a), (c).) Where a party seeks leave to file a compulsory cross-complaint, whether due to oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause, leave must be granted anytime during the course of litigation so long as the defendant acted in good faith. (CCP § 426.50.) Leave should be liberally granted to avoid forfeiture of causes of action. (Id.)

 

“Factors such as oversight, inadvertence, neglect, mistake or other cause, are insufficient grounds to deny the motion unless accompanied by bad faith.” (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank¿(1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 99.) “[The] principle of liberality requires that a strong showing of bad faith be made in order to support a denial of the right to file a cross-complaint under this section.” (Foot's Transfer & Storage Co. v. Superior Court (1980) 114 Cal.App.3d 897, 902.) To establish bad faith, the opposing party must show “actual or constructive fraud, or a design to mislead or deceive another, or a neglect or refusal to fulfill some duty or some contractual obligation, not prompted by an honest mistake ..., but by some interested or sinister motive[,] ... not simply bad judgment or negligence, but rather ... the conscious doing of a wrong because of dishonest purpose or moral obliquity; ... [bad faith] contemplates a state of mind affirmatively operating with furtive design or ill will.” (Id. at 100.) While the good faith requirement gives courts a “modicum of discretion,” the law strongly favors granting leave. (Sidney v. Superior Court (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 710, 718.) 

 

Analysis

 

Respondent Wesley Widjaja moves for an order granting leave to file a cross-complaint against Respondent/Cross-Defendant Ananthan Thangavel. Widjaja seeks liability against Thangavel for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.

 

On April 23, 2024, in conjunction with his answer to the operative pleading, Widjaja attempted to file a cross-complaint asserting causes of action for implied and equitable indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief. (Maatta Decl.) The court filing system rejected the cross-complaint. The court clerk informed Respondent’s counsel that he must seek leave to file a cross-complaint. Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50(a) states that “a party shall file a cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint.” “Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial.” (Id., §428.50(b).)

 

In light of these statutes, Widjaja timely presented the cross-complaint for filing with the answer to the operative pleading. To the extent that leave is required, the court grants leave to file the proposed cross-complaint. (Id., § 428.50(c).)

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.  Widjaja shall file the cross complaint by June 18, 2024.

 


Case Number: 23SMCV00234    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Uzun v. City of Santa Monica, et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCV00234

MOTION:                  Motion for New Trial

HEARING DATE:   6/14/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

“A motion for new trial is a creature of statute . . ..” (Neal v. Montgomery Elevator Co.¿(1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1194, 1198.) A movant must satisfy Code of Civil Procedure sections 657 and 659. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 657, a motion for new trial may be granted if there is any:

 

[¶] 1. Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury, or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial… [¶] 6. Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law. [¶] 7. Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application.  

 

When ruling on an application for a new trial, the court sits as an independent trier of fact. (Lane v. Hughes Aircraft Co. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 405, 412.) The court, therefore, has broad discretion to order new trials, limited only by the obligation to state its reasons for granting a new trial and the existence of substantial evidence in the record to support those reasons. (Ibid.) In assessing the need for a new trial, the court must rely on its view of the overall record, taking into account such factors, among others, as the nature and seriousness of the alleged misconduct, the general atmosphere, including the judge’s control, of the trial, the likelihood of prejudicing the jury, and the efficacy of objection or admonition under all the circumstances. (Dominguez v. Pantalone (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 201, 211.)

 

The party intending to move for a new trial must file with the clerk and serve upon each adverse party a notice of his intention to move for a new trial, designating the grounds upon which the motion will be made and whether the same will be made upon affidavits or the minutes of the court or both, either (1) “after a decision is rendered and before the entry of judgment”; (2) “within 15 days of the date of mailing notice of entry of judgment by the clerk of the court . . . , or service upon him or her by any party of written notice of entry of judgment, or within 180 days after the entry of judgment, whichever is earliest”; or (3) if another party files the first motion for new trial, “each other party shall have 15 days after the service of that notice upon him or her to file and serve a notice of intention to move for a new trial.” (CCP § 659.) These time limits are jurisdictional and cannot extended or waived by stipulation nor court order. (Marriage of Herr (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1469 [while trial judge characterized order as one “granting reconsideration,” ruling effectively granted new trial and was untimely].)

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff Guven Uzun moves for a new trial. Plaintiff argues that the court erred and abused its discretion by: 1) not excluding the reason for the police response to plaintiff’s home and for his arrest (domestic violence); 2) granting defendants’ motions in limine numbers 1 and 2, thereby creating the impression that plaintiff was guilty of committing an act of domestic violence that sent the victim to the emergency room for medical treatment; 3) excluding the deposition testimony of Sergeant Chun, wherein he stated that he heard the “clicking” sound of the handcuffs tightening when plaintiff sat back in the rear seat of the police vehicle; and 4) precluding Dr. Rosabel Young from testifying entirely. (CCP § 657(1), (6), (7).)

 

Nature-of-the-Call Evidence

 

In his motion, Plaintiff argues that it was irrelevant why the police came to Plaintiff’s home with respect to their alleged negligence in handcuffing Plaintiff without double-locking the handcuffs.

 

            “In order to prove facts sufficient to support a finding of negligence, a plaintiff must show that the defendant had a duty to use due care, that he breached that duty, and that the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Hayes v. Cnty. of San Diego, (2013) 57 Cal. 4th 622, 629.) In the use-of-force context, “[t]he reasonableness of an officer’s conduct is determined in light of the totality of circumstances.” (Id.; See Graham v. Connor (1989) 490 U.S. 386, 396 [listing “Graham factors”]; see also Pen. Code § 835a(a)(4); CACI 440.)

 

Here, the nature of the call was directly relevant to the reasonableness of the officers’ decision to use force. Plaintiff alleged that Officers Valenzuela and King were negligent when they handcuffed him without double-locking his handcuff. To determine whether this use of force was reasonable in such circumstances, the jury had to consider any facts pertinent to the officer’s decision to detain, handcuff and/or arrest Plaintiff. As Plaintiff observes, the jury had to consider whether the officers’ negligent failure to double-lock or deliberate decision not to double-lock was reasonable. This analysis would necessarily include examining the domestic violence report, as this shows the level of seriousness of the crime at issue and whether Plaintiff may or may not have posed a threat at the time – either to the officers, or other family members. While Plaintiff disagrees that the officers were ever in danger, that fact was for the jury to assess.

 

Accordingly, the court finds no error in allowing the evidence of why the officers were at Plaintiff’s home into the record.

 

Defendants’ MIL Nos. 1 & 2

 

The Court did not err by granting Defendants’ motions in limine nos. 1 and 2. As to MIL no. 1, the post-hoc outcome of Plaintiff’s criminal case had no bearing on the officers’ alleged negligence at the time of arrest. Penal Code section 851.91 does not suggest that the Court cannot exclude such information. In fact, the sealed nature of the action, if anything, reinforces the outcome of the case’s irrelevance. Plaintiff also contends that there has “clearly” been unlawful access to and disclosure of plaintiff’s sealed court records, in violation of Penal Code section 851.92. Even if there were unlawful access, Plaintiff does not explain how or why this fact would affect the propriety of the evidentiary rulings at issue or the ultimate verdict.

 

As to MIL no. 2, Plaintiff presents no reason why the court should have allowed information not known to the arresting officers. Plaintiff does not explain why testimony from Plaintiff’s children would have been relevant to the officers’ negligence in handcuffing Plaintiff or any other issue in the case. Plaintiff admits that the children’s testimony could not have influenced the officer’s decisions regarding the arrest, as they were not interviewed by the officers.  

 

Dr. Young’s Testimony

 

Finally, the Court did not err in excluding Dr. Young’s testimony under Evidence Code section 402. (See Sargon Enters., Inc. v. Univ. of S. Cal., (2012) 55 Cal. 4th 747, 771–72.) Dr. Young could not, with any degree of medical certainty, apportion the causation between the torsion that Plaintiff suffered at the door and the compression that Plaintiff purportedly suffered when the handcuffs tightened in the car. (3/7/24 Trial Transcript, at 69:6–71:20.) For example, Dr. Young “still believe[d] both are a component” of plaintiff’s injury. (Id., at 70:3–7.) Since Dr. Young was not able to apportion causation to the alleged negligent acts, her opinion of the cause of Plaintiff’s injury was speculative.

Moreover, Plaintiff proffered Dr. Young’s testimony in support of medical causation. The jury did not reach the question of causation, as the jury found that Defendants were not negligent. Therefore, Dr. Young’s testimony would not have impacted the ultimate outcome of the trial.

Sergeant Chun’s Deposition


            Plaintiff fails to demonstrate that the Court erred in excluding Sergeant Chun’s deposition testimony. Plaintiff sought to present evidence that Officer Chun heard the “clicking” sound of the handcuffs tightening when plaintiff sat back in the rear seat of the police vehicle. Plaintiff failed to show that the testimony was admissible at the time of trial.

 

Plaintiff claims that Sergeant Chun’s testimony was admissible under Evidence Code sections 1220 and 1224. The hearsay exception for a party admission would not apply to this testimony because Sergeant Chun was no longer a party. (Evid. Code §1220.) Evidence Code section 1224 does not apply because the action was not based in whole or in part upon the liability, obligation, or duty of Sergeant Chun. Plaintiff sought liability against the City on the individual liability of Officers Valenzuela and King, and not on any liability, obligation, or duty of Sergeant Chun.

 

Moreover, the deposition testimony was only relevant, if at all, to the issue of causation. Again, the jury never reached the issue of causation. Plaintiff cannot therefore claim any prejudice from this ruling.

 

            Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.


Case Number: 23SMCV00471    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Esposito v. Gross

CASE NO.:                23SMCV00471

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Independent Medical Examination

HEARING DATE:   6/14/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) section 2032.220 states in any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff, if both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive; and (2) The examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.

 

CCP section 2032.250 provides that, when a plaintiff fails to respond to a demand, or refuses to submit to the physical examination, the defendant may move for an order compelling a response to the demand and compelling compliance with the request for an exam.

 

Analysis

 

Defendant Heather Rose Gross moves to compel Plaintiff Jorie Esposito to submit to a physical examination conducted by Steven Nagelberg, M.D., an orthopedic surgeon. Defendant demonstrates proper service of a demand for physical examination of plaintiff. (Bandhold Decl., Ex. D.) The examination was initially noticed for January 23, 2024. On January 3, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel notified defense counsel that Plaintiff was not available for the January 23, 2024 examination and requested that the examination be continued to early March 2024. (Bandhold Decl., ¶6, Ex. E.) Defense counsel later proposed several dates in March 2024 for rescheduling plaintiff’s examination, but plaintiff’s counsel failed to respond. (Bandhold Decl., ¶¶7-9, Exs. F-G.) Defendant therefore unilaterally served a notice of continuance of the examination for March 14, 2024. (Id., Ex. H.) Plaintiff again objected to the exam on the grounds that plaintiff would be unavailable. (Ex. I.) Plaintiff indicated that she was going to be in California in March, but unexpectedly delayed her return to California “due to personal family reasons.” (Id.) Following meet and confer efforts, the parties agreed to an examination set for April 11, 2024. (Id., Ex. J.) Defendant served plaintiff with a notice of second continuance of the demanded examination, which set the examination for April 11, 2024. (Id., Ex. K.)

 

On March 7, 2024, plaintiff served an objection to the April 11, 2024 examination, which contested several terms and conditions of the examination but “agree[d] to allow a defense medical examination to go forward on April 11, 2024 . . ..” (Id., Ex. L.) On March 11, 2024, the parties discussed the conditions during meet and confer efforts. (Id., Ex. M.) On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel requested that the medical examination be taken off calendar to accommodate their newly filed motion to be relieved, and that Plaintiff would not be appearing at the exam. (Id., ¶¶ 15-16, Ex. N.) Defense counsel refused to take the exam off calendar. (Id.) Plaintiff failed to appear at the noticed exam. (Id. ¶ 17.)

 

Plaintiff presents no substantive reason why she failed to appear for her noticed and agreed-upon examination. Plaintiff suggests that she could not make it to Los Angeles due to unspecified family matters. The Court does not find this to be a sufficient or recognized excuse for failing to attend a noticed examination. Even so, Plaintiff fails to substantiate her purported inability to attend the exam. Plaintiff claims that a court order is unnecessary, because she will submit to an exam without one. In fact, Plaintiff’s course of conduct suggests the opposite—that she will continue to refuse to attend the examination unless ordered.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to attend the examination as noticed in the motion. To summarize, the examination will be conducted by Steven Nagelberg, M.D., on July 24, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., at 10800 Paramount Blvd. Suite 204A Downey, CA 90241.

 

Sanctions are mandatory, unless the Court finds that plaintiff acted with substantial justification or other circumstances which make the imposition of sanctions unjust. The record shows that Plaintiff willfully failed to appear at her noticed and agreed-upon examination. Therefore, sanctions are mandatory against Plaintiff. Accordingly, sanctions are GRANTED in the noticed amount of $1,100.54 against Plaintiff only. Plaintiff is to pay sanctions to defense counsel within 20 days.


Case Number: 24SMCP00198    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           SMC Specialty Finance v. Redbox Entertainment LLC

CASE NO.:                24SMCP00198

MOTION:                  Motion to Confirm Arbitration Award

HEARING DATE:   6/14/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Procedure

 

A petition to confirm an award shall be served and filed not later than four years after the date of service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner. (CCP §1288.)  No petition may be served and filed until at least 10 days after service of the signed copy of the award upon petitioner. (CCP §1288.4.) 

 

If the agreement sets forth a method of service for the petition, that method holds. (CCP §1290.4(a).) If the arbitration agreement does not set forth a method, service shall be made in the manner provided by law for service of summons in an action if the party has not appeared. (CCP § 1290.4(b).) If the party upon whom the petition is to be made has appeared, service can be made by noticed motion. (CCP § 1290.4(c).) Service shall be made in the manner provided in section 1010 et seq. if the person on whom service is to be made has previously been served in accordance with section 1290.4(b). (Id.)

 

Merits

 

Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court for confirmation of the award. (CCP, § 1285.) Upon service and filing of a petition to confirm arbitration award, the court shall confirm the award as made, unless it corrects or vacates the award, or dismisses the proceeding. (Id., § 1286.) The contents of a petition to confirm an arbitration award shall set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate, the names of the arbitrators, and shall set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4.) Where the petition is served but no response is served and filed, the allegations in the petition are deemed admitted.  (CCP § 1290; Taheri Law Group, A.P.C. v. Sorokurs (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 956, 962.)   

 

Analysis

 

Petitioner SMC Specialty Finance, LLC (“Petitioner”) seeks to confirm the arbitrator’s Final Award entered on March 29, 2024, in Petitioner’s favor and against Respondent, Redbox Entertainment, LLC. The award is attached and verified to the petition. Respondent does not oppose the petition. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

Judgment will be entered in Petitioner’s favor, and against Respondent, in accordance with the Final Award. Petitioner is ordered to submit a proposed judgment for signature.  In sum, the proposed judgment should reflect that Respondent will pay Petitioner the sum of $1,043,316.10, plus prejudgment interest on the entire award amount at 10% per annum from March 30, 2024, consisting of the following: (1) $550,000 for the unpaid MG Payment due and owing from Respondent; (2) $392,071.43 in accrued interest on the $550,000 MG Payment through March 29, 2024, calculated at the default interest rate of 1% per week in accordance with Paragraph 12.5 of the Agreement; (3) $72,250.00 for Petitioner’s reasonable outside attorney’s fees; and (4) $28,994.76 in costs. (Mekhael Decl., Exh. 2, pp. 9-10.)  In addition, Respondent will produce the underlying license agreements by which Respondent purported to license the streaming rights to the various streaming platforms, within thirty days of the Final Award.

 

Petitioner to give notice.
