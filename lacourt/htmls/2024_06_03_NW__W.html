DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV01803    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: W

Osvaldo Martignoni, et al., v. Arash Majlessi, et al.,

 

Motion for leave to file amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        June 3, 2024                          Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        19VECV01803

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni

Responding Party:     Defendant Bank of Hope 

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a contractual fraud action. On December 19, 2019 Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action. Plaintiff filed a Fourth Amended Complaint on February 9, 2022 against Defendants Arash Majlessi, Majlessi Corporation, Gloria Center LLC, Kamran Hekmat, United Escrow Co., John C. Lee, and Ian Bhak for (1) rescission, (2) fraud, (3) breach of contract, (4) rescission, (5) breach of duty and gross negligence, (6) breach of duty and gross negligence, (7) fraud, and (8) declaratory relief.  

 

On November 18, 2022, the court sustained, without leave to amend, Defendants Kamran Hekmat and Gloria Center LLC’s (“Hekmat Defendants”) demurrer to the fourth and seventh causes of action and dismissed the Hekmat Defendants with prejudice.

 

On December 7, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their operative Fifth Amended Complaint against Arash Majlessi, Majlessi Corporation, United Escrow Co. Harry Hwang, John C. Lee and Ian Bhak.

 

On March 9, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Arash Majlessi, Majlessi Corporation, Harry Hwang, and Ian Bhak. On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Gloria Center, LLC. Plaintiff named Bank of Hope as Doe 1.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni’s Motion for Leave to file Amended Complaint is DENIED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs Osvaldo Martignoni and Claudia Martignoni move the court for an order allowing Plaintiffs to file an amended complaint to add a cause of action for negligence against Bank of Hope.

 

Motions for leave to amend the pleadings are directed to the sound discretion of the court. “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading . . . .” (CCP § 473(a)(1); see CCP § 576.) Policy favors liberally granting leave to amend so that all disputed matters between the parties may be resolved. (See Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) Absent prejudice to the adverse party, the court may permit amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial.” (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761 [internal quotes omitted].) Where leave is sought to add entirely new claims, the court may grant leave to amend if the new claims are based on the same general set of facts, and the amendment will not prejudice the opposing party. (Austin v. Massachusetts Bonding & Ins. Co. (1961) 56 Cal.2d 596, 600-602; Glaser v. Meyers (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 770, 777 [holding trial court did not abuse its discretion in permitting amendment of complaint, which originally alleged constructive eviction, to allege retaliatory eviction where the new claim was based on the same general set of facts].)

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See CRC, rule 3.1324(a), (b).)

 

On May 1, 2024, the instant matter came before the court. The court continued the hearing due to Plaintiff’s failure to submit a declaration in support of the motion or a copy of the proposed amendment. On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration in support of the motion for leave to amend along with a copy of the sixth amended complaint.

 

Defendant Bank of Hope (“BOH”) opposes the motion on the grounds Plaintiffs have engaged in unexcused delay in seeking to add BOH as a party and statute of limitations concerns. Defendant BOH contends Plaintiffs have litigated this case since December of 2019, fully aware of their loan from BOH. Plaintiff Claudia Martignoni purports to have communicated directly with BOH prior to July of 2018 regarding the transaction, so no concealment of facts could justify a delay in seeking to pursue a claim against BOH. Moreover, the statute of limitations for Plaintiffs to assert a negligence claim against BOH ran as of at least July 18, 2021. Plaintiffs seeks to avoid the application of the statute of limitations first by naming BOH as a DOE defendant in December of 2023, and now by the proposed Sixth Amended Complaint.

 

Plaintiffs have been dilatory in bringing the proposed amendment.  BOH has demonstrated that the delay in seeking leave to amend has prejudiced BOH, as it is now being brought into a case stemming from events which occurred in 2018 and 2019.  (See Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿ Plaintiff has given no explanation as to why it did not bring the claim earlier, since all of the facts underlying the new claims were known to plaintiffs by 2019: namely, that BOH had approved the loan after making representations that the purchase was sound.   Any negligence claim would have to have been brought within two years of plaintiff learning that the purchase was not sound.  Without determining precisely when that occurred, it certainly was prior to December 2021.   The relation back doctrine does not save this amendment because it only will “relate back” new causes of action to the date on which a new defendant was named.  While the relation back doctrine has some elasticity, Plaintiff cannot stretch the doctrine back in time to bring a claim against a newly named defendant based on the initial filing date against unrelated defendants. 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend is DENIED.


Case Number: 19VECV01817    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: W

FARZIN MALY, ET AL. V. BRETT C HARRISON, ET AL.

 

MOTION OF DEFENDANTS XQB MANAGEMENT, INC., TRYSTON HARRISON, AND DAVID THOMAS LLLOYD FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ATLERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION OF THE ISSUES

 

Date of Hearing:        June 3, 2024                                      Trial Date:       September 16, 2024  

Department:              W                                                        Case No:         19VECV01817

 

Moving Party:            Defendants XQB Management, Inc., Tryston Harrison and David Thomas Lloyd

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Farzin Maly and Bahareh Mohammadzadeh Moghaddam

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On December 20, 2019, Plaintiffs Farzin Maly and Bahareh Mohammadzadeh Moghaddam filed a complaint against Defendants Brett C. Harrison aka Brett Christian Harrison; BCH Enterprises, LLC; Tryston Harrison; Re/Max Olson & Associates; David Thomas Lloyd; Center Street Lending Fund IV Spe, LLC; Bagnard Company, LLC; CT Lien Solutions; Mario Simone; The Turoci Firm, Inc.; BM Stewardship Inv., LLC; Prominence Capital Partners, LLC, and Charles Ockwell.

 

On May 24, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a third amended complaint alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Specific Performance; (3) Fraud; (4) Conversion; (5) Violation of Penal Code section 496; (6) Quiet Title; (7) Breach of Fiduciary Duties; and (8) Negligence. Plaintiff also named XQB Management, Inc. and Twisted River Capital, LLC as additional defendants.

 

On April 30, 2021, XQB Management, Inc., Tryston Harrison, and David Thomas Lloyd filed a cross-complaint against BCH Enterprises, LLC and Brett C. Harrison asserting causes of action for indemnification, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants XQB Management, Inc., Tryston Harrison and David Thomas Lloyd’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication of the Issues is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In support of their motion for summary judgment, Defendants XQB Management, Inc., Tryston Harrison and David Thomas Lloyd request this court take judicial notice of several documents including statements of information filed with the California Secretary of State, Articles of Organizations and certificate of amendment to Articles of Organizations filed with the California Secretary of State, and grant deeds. (See Exhs. 17 – 26.)

 

The court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of the license records of David Thomas Lloyd, Brett Christian Harrison, and Tryston Alexander Harrison. (Exh. SS) The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

In reply, Defendants XQB Management, Inc., Tryston Harrison and David Thomas Lloyd submit objections to the evidence of Plaintiffs Maly and Moghadam.

 

The court overrules objections nos. 1 – 7

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants XQB Management, Inc., Tryston Harrison and David Thomas Lloyd (“Re/Max Defendants”) move the court, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, for an order granting summary judgment over the entire action as it pertains to these ReMax Defendants, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of issues on the third, fourth, fifth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.  At the very end of their opposition, plaintiffs request a continuance so Plaintiffs’ new counsel can depose Defendant Lloyd regarding various communications. However, they do not provide the request in their declaration as required. (CCP §473c(h).)  As a result, the court denies this non-code compliant request.  

 

Plaintiffs’ Knowledge of the Liens

 

Re/Max Defendants first move for summary judgment on the grounds Plaintiffs’ actual knowledge of the liens on the property prevents Plaintiffs from alleging the Re/Max Defendants are liable to them.

 

Re/Max Defendants argue on March 18, 2019, Plaintiffs received and signed a document (hereinafter the “Acknowledgment”) acknowledging that they had received the preliminary title report. (UMF 15.) The Acknowledgment was a one-page letter from “Jodi Hunter/escrow officer” and addressed to Plaintiff Maly, which read “The undersigned hereby acknowledges receipt of the following: Preliminary title report….” (UMF 18.) Within the title report, on pages 6 and 7, the preliminary title report indicated that there were three Deeds of Trust recorded on the Property totaling $2,412,795.00. (UMF ¶ 25.) As a result, Re/Max Defendants argue estoppel prevents Plaintiffs from now claiming that they did not receive the Preliminary Report, because their signature on the Acknowledgment is valid confirmation of the kind regularly upheld by the courts. Moreover, there are no circumstances that could indicate that the Acknowledgment could have been fraudulent as it went through the parties’ escrow agent and Plaintiffs themselves had the ability to read the Acknowledgment and Preliminary Report, and to obtain expert assistance with it if they had asked.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue their knowledge of the Title Report does not defeat Re/Max Defendants’ duty. Plaintiffs cite to Holmes v. Summer (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1510 to support this contention. In Holmes, citing Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Ass'n., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, held “even when a buyer is on constructive notice of matters of record, that does not necessarily mean a cause of action in tort arising out of failure to disclose will not lie…. The fact that the purchasers were on constructive notice of the recorded affordable housing restrictions did not preclude an action for damages against the seller arising out of an alleged breach of the duty to disclose.” (Holmes v. Summer (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1521.) Plaintiffs further dispute that they had actual knowledge of the title report and instead claim they only signed the acknowledgement of the title report because of Lloyd’s insistence, creating a factual dispute. Specifically, Plaintiffs contend at no time up to and including March 18, 2019 was the preliminary title report provided to Plaintiffs; only the approval form was provided. (Maly Decl. ¶9.)

 

The court finds Holmes does not support the facts of this case. Holmes did not stand for the proposition that when a buyer has constructive notice regarding matters of public record, the seller’s broker still has a duty to inform Plaintiffs of the liens and/or encumbrances preventing the purchase. Rather, the court in Holmes confirmed that the duty to disclose exists only when a buyer cannot reasonably ascertain material defects on its own: “ ‘where the seller knows of facts materially affecting the value or desirability of the property which are known or accessible only to him and also knows that such facts are not known to, or within the reach of the diligent attention and observation of the buyer, the seller is under a duty to disclose them to the buyer.’ “ (Holmes, supra, 188 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1518–1519, quoting Lingsch v. Savage (1963) 213 Cal.App.2d 729, 735–736.)

 

However, the court finds a triable issue of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs were actually given the preliminary title report. Plaintiff Maly, under penalty of perjury, states “at no time up to and including March 18, 2019 was the preliminary title report provided to buyers; only the approval form was provided.” (Maly Decl. ¶9.)

 

Re/Max Defendants’ Knowledge of the Liens

 

Re/Max Defendants next argue they had no knowledge of the seller’s financial condition or intention to perform on the purchase agreement and even if they did, they were prohibited from disclosing it. Re/Max Defendants contend there is no evidence that the Re/Max Defendants handled the Preliminary Report or even saw it and in any event, the Re/Max Defendants would have no need to see the Preliminary Report because the Purchase Agreement explicitly stated that Re/Max “[s]hall not be responsible for inspecting public records or permits concerning the title” to the Property. (Purchase Agreement at ¶ 18.B (vi); UMF ¶ 10.) Again, even if they did, California law prohibited the Re/Max Defendants from disclosing any “confidential information” regarding the “Seller’s financial position, motivations, bargaining position, or other personal information….” (Dual Agency Disclosure, Ex. 2, quoting Civ. Code § 2079.16.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the Re/Max Defendants do owe a duty to Plaintiffs, which includes the duty to learn of and disclose all material affecting the value or desirability of the property, both as the salesperson and the employing broker. (Civ. Code §2079.16.) Moreover, despite the Re/Max Defendants’ contention 2079.16 prohibits them from disclosing “confidential information”, the prohibition does not include when the information materially affects the value or desirability of that property.  

 

Civil Code section 2079.16 provides that brokers are not obligated to reveal confidential information unrelated to their Civil Code section 2079.16 affirmative duties, which includes explicitly a “duty to disclose all facts known to the agent materially affecting the value or desirability of the property that are not known to, or within the diligent attention and observation of, the parties.” (Civ. Code §2079.16(c).) While the court agrees that the confidentiality provisions of section 2079.16 would not have prevented the brokers from disclosing such information as it would have certainly materially affected the value or desirability of the property, Plaintiffs have not demonstrated the Re/Max Defendants’ actual knowledge of the liens and/or encumbrances. Plaintiffs present evidence that Lloyd signed the email as part of Harrison Luxury Group and was part of Brett’s entities. None of this establishes Re/Max Defendants were aware of what was on the preliminary title report. As a result, even if Plaintiffs were not provided the preliminary title report, they have not presented evidence Re/Max Defendants breached the duty to disclose. The expert declaration of Gregory M. Libby discusses the duty to investigate but Civil Code section 2079.3 explicitly provides the scope of inspection does not include public records or permits concerning title or use of the property.

 

Fraud

 

Re/Max Defendants move for summary judgment of the third cause of action for fraud on the grounds the Re/Max Defendants did not make any promises in the purchase agreement, there was no misrepresentation of the “ability” of the seller to perform, and Re/Max Defendants did not conceal any information from Plaintiffs.

 

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.)

 

Re/Max Defendants point to the Purchase Agreement, noting there is no term in which any of the Re/Max Defendants promised that the Seller would convey title to the property. In fact, Re/Max Defendants expressly disavowed any duty to somehow require the Seller’s performance, and Plaintiffs agreed. (COE, Exh. 3, Re/Max Addendum, ¶32.) As such, there was no false promise to Plaintiffs that the Re/Max Defendants would convey and deliver to Plaintiffs’ title to the property pursuant to the terms set forth in the Purchase Agreement. Similarly, the Re/Max Defendants never made any kind of representation of the “ability” of the Seller to convey title. Re/Max again points to the Purchase Agreement noting there is no such statement there or any other document. Lastly, Re/Max Defendants argue there is no evidence they hid from Plaintiffs Defendants’ financial situation or that Defendants lacked the capability of actually conveying title to the subject property. Re/Max Defendants point to the Dual Agency Disclosure which posits, “In representing both Seller and Buyer, a dual agent may not, without the express permission of the respective party, disclose to the other party confidential information, 'including, but not limited to, facts relating to either the Buyer's or Seller's financial position, motivations, bargaining position, or other personal information that may impact price, including the Seller's willingness to accept a price less than the listing price or the Buyer's willingness to pay a price greater than the price offered.” (COE, Exh. 2.) Moreover, Re/Max Defendants did not have a duty to review the preliminary report.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs only refer to their complaint. Plaintiffs have not presented any evidence Re/Max Defendants falsely promised to Plaintiffs that Defendants would convey and deliver to Plaintiffs title to the property, fraudulently misrepresented to Plaintiffs the ability of Defendants to actually convey title to the property, or hid and obscured from Plaintiffs the fact that senior liens on the property were in default and each of them lacked the capability of actually conveying title to the property.

 

Accordingly, the motion for summary adjudication as to the third cause of action for fraud is GRANTED.

 

Conversion

 

Re/Max Defendants move for summary judgment of the fourth cause of action for conversion on the grounds the release of the money from escrow was expressly authorized, in writing, by the Plaintiffs alone to the Seller.

 

“ ‘…The basic elements of the tort [of conversion] are (1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) the defendant's disposition of the property in a manner that is inconsistent with the plaintiff's property rights; and (3) resulting damages. [Citation.]’ (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119….)” (Regent All. Ltd. v. Rabizadeh (2014) 231 Cal. App. 4th 1177, 1181.) 

 

Re/Max Defendants contend the core of Plaintiffs’ conversion claim is that Plaintiffs’ money “should not have been released” from escrow because Defendants “knew or had reason to know that [Seller] could not transfer title to Plaintiffs.” However, the Re/Max Defendants neither received the money described by Plaintiffs in the TAC nor aided or abetted in such receipt by others. (UMF 44-45.) Moreover, the release of the money from escrow was expressly authorized, in writing, by the Plaintiffs alone to the Seller. (UMF 46.) Moreover, the Re/Max Defendants never actually exercised any kind of control over the indicated money. (UMF ¶ 48.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the Brokers need not have obtained the funds. Instead, the Brokers acted for Seller in obtaining the transfers and told Plaintiffs to do the transfers. Further, they aided and abetted Defendants in Defendants taking of the funds.

 

The court agrees Plaintiffs has not presented evidence over direct disposition over the property. “Generally, ‘California has adopted the common law rule for subjecting a defendant to liability for aiding and abetting a tort. “Liability may . . . be imposed on one who aids and abets the commission of an intentional tort if the person (a) knows the other’s conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act or (b) gives substantial assistance to the other in accomplishing a tortious result and the person’s own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.” [Citations.].’”  (Das v. Bank of America, N.A. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 727, 744.) However, Plaintiffs have not presented evidence Re/Max Defendants aided and abetted Defendants in coordinating, authorizing, and requesting each of the monetary releases from escrow of monies that should not have been released.

 

Accordingly, Re/Max Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth cause of action for conversion is GRANTED.

 

Violation of Penal Code section 496

 

Re/Max Defendants move for summary adjudication of the fifth cause of action for violation of Penal Code section 496 on the grounds the funds were not stolen.

 

“[T]he elements required to show a violation of section 496(a) are ... that (i) property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft, (ii) the defendant knew the property was so stolen or obtained, and (iii) the defendant received or had possession of the stolen property.  (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.) Section 496 liability may lie relating to “property that has been ’stolen’ or ‘that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion.’  ([Pen. Code] § 496(a), italics added.)” (Ibid.) A criminal conviction is not a requirement – but theft or extortion, as defined in the Penal Code, must be pleaded or proven. (See Bell v. Feibush (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1048.) Any person who has been injured by a violation of subdivision (a) or (b) may bring an action for three times the amount of actual damages, if any, sustained by the plaintiff, costs of suit, and reasonable attorney's fees. (Pen. Code §496(c).)

 

Re/Max Defendants argue given that the funds were not stolen when they were in escrow’s possession, the transfer of the funds do not fall under the gambit of section 496 when they were released by escrow to the seller. Additionally, the text of the escrow amendments themselves make it clear that the funds were to be released to the seller and not the Re/Max Defendants.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue treble damages under section 496(c) applies to business disputes where plaintiff alleges theft, or where a defendant aids in concealing or withholding funds from plaintiff. (Siry Investment, L.P. v. Farkhondehpour (2022) 13 Cal.5th 333.) As a result, Plaintiffs contend they will show that the Brokers aided in withholding funds from Plaintiffs.

 

As noted above, Plaintiffs have not presented evidence Re/Max Defendants had possession of the funds released or aided and abetted the defendants. Accordingly, Re/Max Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth cause of action for conversion is GRANTED.

 

Breach of Fiduciary Duty / Negligence

 

Re/Max Defendants move for summary adjudication of the seventh and eighth causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty and negligence on the grounds Re/Max Defendants did not owe Plaintiffs a duty, as a fiduciary or otherwise, to do any of the things alleged in the TAC.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are: (1) existence of a fiduciary duty; (2) breach of the fiduciary duty; and (3) damage proximately caused by the breach.” (Gutierrez v. Girardi (2011) 194 Cal. App. 4th 925, 932.) To plead a cause of action for negligence, one must allege (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.)

 

Re/Max Defendants argue as a matter of law, the Re/Max Defendants did not owe Plaintiffs a duty to (1) inspect the Preliminary Report; (2) advise Plaintiffs on what, if anything, to do in response to the Preliminary Report; or (3) give legal advice to Plaintiffs, because the parties had already expressly agreed otherwise in the Purchase Agreement. Because the parties have expressly agreed that the Re/Max Defendants do not have the duty to review title reports or other public documents, nor to guarantee the Seller’s performance under the Purchase Agreement, they cannot be liable for breach of fiduciary duty or negligence.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the brokers, as dual agents, owe a fiduciary duty to Buyer-Plaintiffs, which is the same obligation of undivided service and loyalty, owed by trustees to their beneficiaries. The duty includes the duty to learn of and disclose all material affecting the value or desirability of the property, both as the salesperson and the employing broker. As noted above, Plaintiffs have not presented a duty owed to investigate or that Defendants breached their duty to disclose material facts.

 

Accordingly, Re/Max Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the seventh and eighth causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty and negligence is GRANTED.

 

Conspiracy / Aiding and Abetting

 

Re/Max Defendants move for summary adjudication of Plaintiffs’ allegations of “conspiracy” and “aiding and abetting”.

 

“The elements of a civil conspiracy are the formation and operation of the conspiracy and damage resulting to plaintiff from an act done in furtherance of the common design.” (Stueve Bros. Farms, LLC v. Berger Kahn (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 303, 323.) “‘Liability may . . . be imposed on one who aids and abets the commission of an intentional tort if the person (a) knows the other’s conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act or (b) gives substantial assistance to the other in accomplishing a tortious result and the person’s own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.’”  (Fiol v. Doellstedt (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1318, 1325-26 (quoting Saunders v. Superior Court (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 832, 846; see also Schulz v. Neovi Data Corp. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 86, 93.) 

 

Re/Max Defendants argue there is no evidence that the Seller and the Re/Max Defendants conspired to commit any of the breach of contract or the tortious acts alleged in the TAC nor is there evidence of any intentional participation with knowledge of the object to be attained. The court agrees. There is no underlying tort nor are there facts of Re/Max Defendants knowingly and willingly entered into a common scheme.

 

Alter Ego

 

Re/Max Defendants lastly seek summary judgment on the grounds there is no evidence of alter ego. This argument is moot. Moreover, Plaintiffs have not presented evidence of alter ego establishing a triable issue of material fact.

 

 


Case Number: 22VECV02435    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: W

MARCO LOPEZ v. KEVIN JON GALLO, et al.

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DISCOVERY

 

Date of Hearing:        June 3, 2024                                                 Trial Date:       None set  

Department:              W                                                                    Case No:         22VECV02435

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Kevin Jon Gallo    

Responding Party:     None

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

This is a premises liability case. On December 18, 2022, Plaintiff Marco Lopez filed a complaint against Defendants Kevin Jon Gallo, and Mauricio Antonio Siguenza asserting causes of action for premises liability and negligence. Plaintiff alleges he was injured when he fell from the roof of Defendants’ premises while performing contractor’s services.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

I.                    Defendant Kevin Gallo’s Motion to Deem Matter Specified in Request for Admissions, Set One Admitted as to Plaintiff Marco Lopez is GRANTED.

II.                  Defendant Kevin Gallo’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Marco Lopez’s Responses to Request for Document Production is GRANTED.

III.                Defendant Kevin Gallo’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Marco Lopez’s Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Kevin Gallo moves this court for an order deeming the truth of all matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One served on Plaintiff Marco Lopez. Defendant Gallo also seeks monetary sanctions against Plaintiff for costs incurred as a result of bringing each motion.

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may also move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).)

 

On February 8, 2024, Defendant propounded discovery requests on Plaintiff including request for admissions, request for production of documents, and form interrogatories. (Slack Decl. ¶4, Exh. A.) On March 1, 2024, Defendant sent a letter to Plaintiff requesting discovery responses, without objection. (Slack Decl. ¶5, Exh. B.) To date, no discovery responses have been received nor has Plaintiff attempted to reach out to Defense Counsel. (Slack Decl. ¶6.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff has failed to timely respond to Defendant’s properly served discovery request. Defendant’s unopposed motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One and deem admitted Requests for Admissions, Set One are GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide, objectionless responses within 20 days.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendant seeks $230 per motion for approximately 1 hour per motion plus $60 filing fee for each motion, totaling $690 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the amount of $230 per motion ($690 total). (See CCP §2023.010.) Sanctions to be paid within 30 days.
