DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV05238    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 107

SUPERior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Northwest District

DEPARTMENT 107

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

  Vagan Kirakosyan, et al.

 

         v.

 

13961 Oxnard, LLC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  23VECV05238

 

 TENTATIVE RE Demurrer 

 without Motion to Strike

 

 

[Tentative Ruling]:            Sustain and grant Plaintiffs leave to amend.

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

This is a landlord-tenant dispute concerning 13961 Oxnard St. Unit 9, Van Nuys, California (the “Subject Property”). (Complaint ¶ 18.) On November 27, 2023, Plaintiffs Vagan Kirakosyan, Marine Shadoyan, Ani Kirakosyan, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Marine Shadoyan, and Irina Kirakosyan, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Marine Shadoyan (“Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants 13961 Oxnard, LLC, Michael Zehavi, R & E Management, Inc., and Does 1 to 100, alleging causes of action for: (1) property damage; (2) general negligence; (3) violation of Cal. Civ. Code section 1940.2; (4) violation of Cal. Civ. Code section 1942.5; (5) violation of Cal. Civ. Code section 1954, subdivision (a)(2); (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (7) negligent infliction of emotional distress; (8) breach of implied covenant of quiet enjoyment; (9) breach of implied covenant of habitability; (10) constructive eviction; (11) violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code section 17200, et seq.; and (12) conversion.

On September 30, 2024, Defendants 13961 Oxnard, LLC, R & E Management, Inc. and Michael Zehavi (“Defendants”) demurred to the complaint. No opposition has been filed.

II.             PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿430.40(a).)

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

 

On April 17, 2024, Defendants 13961 Oxnard, LLC and R & E Management, Inc. filed a declaration of demurring or moving party in support of automatic extension. Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a)(2) states:

 

If the parties are not able to meet and confer at least 5 days before the date the responsive pleading is due, the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension of time within which to file a responsive pleading, by filing and serving, on or before the date on which a demurrer would be due, a declaration stating under penalty of perjury that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made and explaining the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer. The 30-day extension shall commence from the date the responsive pleading was previously due, and the demurring party shall not be subject to default during the period of the extension. Any further extensions shall be obtained by court order upon a showing of good cause.

 

Plaintiffs filed no proof of service for Defendant Michael Zehavi. Defendants R & E Management, Inc. and 13691 Oxnard, LLC were served by substituted service on March 7, 2024, and the documents were mailed that same day. Defendants filed the demurrer over five months later, on September 30, 2024.  Accordingly, the demurrer was untimely under section 430.40. But this court will exercise its discretion and consider it. (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 749 [“[T]he trial court ha[s] discretion to consider [a] defendant’s untimely demurrer.”])

 

The demurrer was timely filed under section 1005.

 

  1. Meet and Confer

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)¿

 

Defendants’ counsel submits a declaration stating counsel met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel. (Abramson Declaration ¶ 3.)

 

This court finds that Defendants satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

A party may demur to a complaint because it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)¿ A party may also demur because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  

¿ 

Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)¿ 

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

Defendants argue each of Plaintiffs’ twelve causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and the causes of action are ambiguous and uncertain. (Demurrer pp. 3-6.)

 

  1. Property Damage

 

Based on the complaint, it is unclear to this court how the elements of Plaintiffs’ property damage cause of action differ from a negligence cause of action. Accordingly, this court will evaluate the property damage cause of action as a negligence cause of action.

 

The elements of a negligence cause of action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917-918.)

 

Plaintiffs allege they “suffered loss of use of property, general damage, and property damage” and “Defendants’ conduct directly and proximately caused economic and non-economic damages to Plaintiffs” but Plaintiffs do not specifically allege that Defendants owed Plaintiffs a duty to use due care or breached said duty. (Complaint ¶¶ 50, 53.)

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. General Negligence

 

The elements of a negligence cause of action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo, supra, 12 Cal.4th at pp. 917-918.)

 

Plaintiffs allege they “suffered property damage, physical injury, and emotional distress” and “Defendants’ conduct directly and proximately caused economic and non-economic damages to Plaintiffs” but Plaintiffs do not specifically allege that Defendants owed Plaintiffs a duty to use due care or breached said duty. (Complaint ¶¶ 50, 56.)

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Violation of Cal. Civ. Code section 1940.2

 

Civil Code section 1940.2 states:

 

(a)   It is unlawful for a landlord to do any of the following for the purpose of influencing a tenant to vacate a dwelling:

 

(1) Engage in conduct that violates subdivision (a) of Section 484 of the Penal Code.

(2) Engage in conduct that violates Section 518 of the Penal Code.

(3) Use, or threaten to use, force, willful threats, or menacing conduct constituting a course of conduct that interferes with the tenant's quiet enjoyment of the premises in violation of Section 1927 that would create an apprehension of harm in a reasonable person. Nothing in this paragraph requires a tenant to be actually or constructively evicted in order to obtain relief.

(4) Commit a significant and intentional violation of Section 1954.

(5) Threaten to disclose information regarding or relating to the immigration or citizenship status of a tenant, occupant, or other person known to the landlord to be associated with a tenant or occupant. This paragraph does not require a tenant to be actually or constructively evicted in order to obtain relief.

 

Subdivision (c) states: “An oral or written warning notice, given in good faith, regarding conduct by a tenant, occupant, or guest that violates, may violate, or violated the applicable rental agreement, rules, regulations, lease, or laws, is not a violation of this section. An oral or written explanation of the rental agreement, rules, regulations, lease, or laws given in the normal course of business is not a violation of this section.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1940.2(c).)

 

Plaintiffs allege:

 

Defendants, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, R&A, and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive, engaged in a course of conduct meant to interfere with the Plaintiffs’ quiet enjoyment of their home of 10 years through conduct, including but not limited to: refusing to inspect, repair, maintain, or prevent plumbing pipes from leaking and bursting; hiring unlicensed day laborers to remove personal property from the kitchen and bathroom and protect them from exposure to mold and asbestos; delaying repairs to prevent a timely return of Plaintiffs’ family into their home; threatening to evict the Plaintiffs, issuing a frivolous, baseless, and retaliatory Notice to Vacate; refusing to offer Plaintiffs’ family to reside in one of Defendants’ vacant properties while repairs to the Subject Property were made, and; causing to be posted a defamatory and pretextual Notice of Belief of Abandonment in which a public allegation of failure to pay rent was made.

 

(Complaint ¶ 60.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs allege that Defendants 13961 Oxnard, LLC and R & A issued a frivolous, baseless, and retaliatory notice to vacate meant to interfere with Plaintiffs’ quiet enjoyment of the home. However, Plaintiffs do not specifically allege that Defendants 13961 Oxnard, LLC and R & A issued the notice to vacate with the intent to influence Plaintiffs to vacate the dwelling.

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Violation of Cal. Civ. Code section 1942.5.

 

Civil Code section 1942.5, subdivision (a) states:

 

If the lessor retaliates against the lessee because of the exercise by the lessee of the lessee's rights under this chapter or because of the lessee's complaint to an appropriate agency as to tenantability of a dwelling, and if the lessee of a dwelling is not in default as to the payment of rent, the lessor may not recover possession of a dwelling in any action or proceeding, cause the lessee to quit involuntarily, increase the rent, or decrease any services within 180 days of any of the following:

 

(1) After the date upon which the lessee, in good faith, has given notice pursuant to Section 1942, has provided notice of a suspected bed bug infestation, or has made an oral complaint to the lessor regarding tenantability.

(2) After the date upon which the lessee, in good faith, has filed a written complaint, or an oral complaint which is registered or otherwise recorded in writing, with an appropriate agency, of which the lessor has notice, for the purpose of obtaining correction of a condition relating to tenantability.

(3) After the date of an inspection or issuance of a citation, resulting from a complaint described in paragraph (2) of which the lessor did not have notice.

(4) After the filing of appropriate documents commencing a judicial or arbitration proceeding involving the issue of tenantability.

(5) After entry of judgment or the signing of an arbitration award, if any, when in the judicial proceeding or arbitration the issue of tenantability is determined adversely to the lessor.

 

Plaintiffs allege that the Subject Property had various issues including mold. (Complaint ¶¶ 24, 26-27.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege there was an issue relating to tenantability.

 

Plaintiffs also allege Zehavi asked Plaintiffs to move out of the Subject Property “because of a work order on the premises, and its uninhabitable condition” and Plaintiffs moved out on December 15, 2021. (Complaint ¶¶ 32-33.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege that Defendants “placed a notice on the Plaintiffs’ unit door a ‘notice of eviction notice of eviction for not paying rent and abandonment without giving Plaintiffs verification of any remediation work performed on the premises, or a reasonable opportunity to Plaintiffs to inspect the property.” (Complaint ¶ 43.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege they quit the Subject Property involuntarily.

 

However, section 1942.5 prohibits a lessor from causing the lessee to quit involuntarily, increase the rent, or decrease services within 180 days of the lessee’s actions listed in section 1942.5, subdivision (a). Plaintiffs have not alleged when they informed Defendants of the issues with the Subject Property. Also, Plaintiffs have not clearly alleged a timeline showing Defendants took retaliatory action within 180 days of Plaintiffs’ taking one of the actions listed in section 1942.5, subdivision (a).

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Violation of Cal. Civ. Code section 1954, subdivision (a)(2)

 

Civil Code section 1954, subdivision (a) states:

A landlord may enter the dwelling unit only in the following cases: (1) In case of emergency. (2) To make necessary or agreed repairs, decorations, alterations or improvements, supply necessary or agreed services, or exhibit the dwelling unit to prospective or actual purchasers, mortgagees, tenants, workers, or contractors or to make an inspection pursuant to subdivision (f) of Section 1950.5. (3) When the tenant has abandoned or surrendered the premises. (4) Pursuant to court order. (5) For the purposes set forth in Chapter 2.5 (commencing with Section 1954.201). [or] (6) To comply with the provisions of Article 2.2 (commencing with Section 17973) of Chapter 5 of Part 1.5 of Division 13 of the Health and Safety Code.

Although Plaintiffs allege the day laborers were not providing “necessary or agreed repairs [or] necessary or agreed services” section 1954, subdivision (a) provides that there are five other circumstances when a landlord may enter a dwelling unit. Plaintiffs have not alleged Defendants’ entering the unit violated the remaining provisions in section 1954, subdivision (a). Plaintiffs allege the day laborers were hired to fix the Subject Property’s mold problems. This court is not persuaded by Plaintiffs’ allegation that, because the day laborers performed their work poorly, the day laborers were not present in the unit to make “necessary or agreed repairs[.]” However, because Plaintiffs do not allege that the landlord was also violating the other sections in subdivision (a), it is not necessary to reach this issue.

 Thus, Plaintiffs’ allegation that the landlord violated Civil Code section 1954, subdivision (a)(2) is insufficient.

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

¿“The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) the defendant engages in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffers extreme or severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant's extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's extreme or severe emotional distress.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 671.)¿ To be outrageous, the conduct must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.” (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.) 

 

Plaintiffs allege that “Even though ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A knew that Vagan was often coming to the Subject Property to protect their family’s belongings, ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A purposefully and maliciously concealed from the Plaintiffs that the Subject Property was still positive for asbestos and mold. ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A acted with malice aforethought and with a conscious disregard for the health, safety, and rights of the Plaintiffs.” (Complaint ¶ 73.) Purposefully concealing the presence of mold and asbestos could be interpreted as extreme and outrageous conduct. Accordingly, Plaintiffs have alleged the first element of intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

Plaintiffs also allege they “suffer[d] humiliation, mental anguish, stress, anxiety, sadness, extreme emotional and physical distress.” (Complaint ¶ 70.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege they suffered extreme or severe emotional distress.

 

Finally, Plaintiffs allege that “Plaintiffs have also suffered extreme emotional distress as a result of the DEFENDANTS’ actions and inactions.” (Complaint ¶ 51.)

 

However, although Plaintiffs allege Defendants acted “with a conscious disregard for the health, safety, and rights of the Plaintiff” and that Defendants’ conduct caused Plaintiffs extreme emotional distress, Plaintiffs have not alleged that Defendants engaged in the extreme and outrageous conduct “with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress.” (So v. Shin, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 671.)¿

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

The elements of a negligent infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) legal duty to use care; (2) breach of such legal duty; (3) damage or injury; and (4) cause of the resulting damage or injury. (Huggins v. Longs Drug Stores California, Inc. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 148, 151.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that they suffered “humiliation, mental anguish, stress, anxiety, sadness, extreme emotional and physical distress” and “Plaintiffs have also suffered extreme emotional distress as a result of the DEFENDANTS’ actions and inactions”, but Plaintiffs have not alleged Defendants owed them a legal duty to use care or breached said duty. (Complaint ¶¶ 51, 70.)

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Breach of the Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

 

The elements of a breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment cause of action are: (1) A lease agreement between plaintiff and defendant; (2) absence of language contrary to the implied covenant that tenant shall have quiet enjoyment and possession; (3) act or omission of the landlord, or anyone claiming under the landlord, which substantially interferes with a tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for purposes contemplated; and (4) an applicable remedy. (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 588-591.) The plaintiff must also allege either: (a) the tenant remains in possession and was damaged; or (b) tenant surrendered possession and is relieved of obligation to pay rent. (Id.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that “At all times herein mentioned, Plaintiffs were the residents in lawful possession of the rental property located at 13961 Oxnard St. Unit 9, Van Nuys, California, pursuant to a written Lease-Rental Agreement.” (Complaint ¶ 10.) Plaintiffs also allege that “DEFENDANTS ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, R&E, and DOES 1-100 are, and at all times herein mentioned were the owners of records, managers, lessors, and agents responsible for maintaining and operating that certain real property located at 13961 Oxnard St. Unit 9, Van Nuys, California.” (Complaint ¶ 9.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege a lease agreement between the Plaintiffs and Defendants.

 

Plaintiffs allege that “R&A employees would not, on a regular basis, come into the Subject Property to inspect the interior of the Subject Property. Even though R&A would log the necessary repairs to be done of the Subject Property among other things, R&A would not order repairs or make the repairs be performed by a licensed contractor” and “Defendants also prohibited the Plaintiffs from using the Subject Property after the Marine’s report to R&A.” (Complaint ¶¶ 79-80.) And “As a result of these actions, in addition to the above-referenced actions and inactions committed by Defendants ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A, there was substantial interference with the Plaintiffs’ use and enjoyment of the Subject Property.” (Complaint ¶ 81.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege an act or omission by the landlord that substantially interfered with Plaintiffs’ right to use and enjoy the premises.

 

Plaintiffs have not alleged the absence of language contrary to the implied covenant that Plaintiffs shall have quiet enjoyment and possession. (See Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates, supra, 125 Cal.App.4th at pp. 588-591 [“In the absence of language to the contrary, every lease contains an implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, whereby the landlord impliedly covenants that the tenant shall have quiet enjoyment and possession of the premises.”]) Plaintiffs have also not attached the lease to the complaint as an exhibit.

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Breach of the Implied Covenant of Habitability

 

The elements of a breach of the implied warranty of habitability claim are: (1) the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises' habitability; (2) notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant's discovery of the condition; (3) the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency; and (4) resulting damages.  (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1297.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that “On November 28, 2021, Marine and Vagan noticed the paint on the bathroom walls of his rental property bubbling. Shortly after, Plaintiffs found a “bouquet” of mushrooms growing on the bathroom wall near the sink cabinet. The walls of the same bathroom were wet again. Vagan notified R&A about this.” (Complaint ¶ 26.) Plaintiffs also allege that “ZEHAVI sent a group of people (the ‘day laborers’) to look into this” and “[t]he interior of the bathroom walls was covered with a thick layer of dark-colored mold.” (Complaint ¶ 27.) Plaintiffs allege that “DEFENDANTS failed to perform their duties owed to the Plaintiffs to remediate mold infestations and address the issue of asbestos within the Subject Property.” (Complaint ¶ 42.)  Accordingly, Plaintiffs allege the existence of a material defective condition affecting the Subject Property’s habitability and, based on the fact Defendant Zehavi sent persons to repair the issue, one can infer that Defendants had notice.

 

Plaintiffs also allege that “Notwithstanding the notification and actual knowledge of the existence of the defective conditions set forth in this Complaint, Defendants continued to demand payment (indeed, full payment) of rent from Plaintiffs” and “Plaintiffs seek compensatory damages, punitive damages, special damages, and attorneys’ fees and costs.” (Complaint ¶¶ 45, 85.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege resulting damages.

 

Plaintiffs have not clearly alleged a timeline showing Defendants were given a reasonable time to correct the deficiencies. Plaintiffs also have not specifically alleged that Defendants were given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency.

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Constructive Eviction

 

The elements of a constructive eviction cause of action are: (1) the landlord’s acts or omissions or disturbance or interference with the tenant’s possession; and (2) causing either a substantial portion of the premises to be unfit for the purposes for which it was leased, or causing the tenant to be deprived of the beneficial enjoyment or use of the premises for a substantial time. (Groh v. Kover’s Bull Pen, Inc. (1963) 221 Cal.App.2d 611, 639.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that “The day laborers installed commercial drying fans to dry the interiors of the kitchen walls without proper insolation of the kitchen from other rooms. As such, mold-infested air was being circulated in the air of the Subject Property” and “The Defendants’ acts and refusal to act caused a permanent interference with the Plaintiffs’ possession of the Subject Property.” (Complaint ¶¶ 29, 87.)

 

Plaintiffs also allege that “From December 15, 2021, the Defendants have deprived the Plaintiffs of the beneficial enjoyment and use of the Subject Property.” (Complaint ¶ 88.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs sufficiently allege a constructive eviction cause of action.

 

  1. Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code section 17200

 

“An unlawful business practice under [Business and Professions Code] section 17200 is ‘an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1361.) “Virtually any law – federal, state or local – can serve as a predicate for an action under Business and Professions Code section 17200.” (Id.)   To have standing a party must “(1) establish a loss or deprivation of money or property sufficient to qualify as injury in fact, i.e., economic injury, and (2) show that that economic injury was the result of, i.e., caused by, the unfair business practice or false advertising that is the gravamen of the claim.” (Lagrisola v. North American Financial Corp. (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 1178, 1187.)  “A plaintiff alleging unfair business practices under these statutes must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that “Defendants ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A failed to comply with Cal. Health & Safety Code §25249.6 and its related 27 C.C.R. § 25601 and §25605.2 notice requirements, when ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A failed to inform the Plaintiffs that the Subject Property tested ‘hot’ for asbestos, mold, and possibly lead paint. Defendants ZEHAVI, 13961 OXNARD, LLC, and R&A continue to endanger their current tenants by failing to post clear and reasonable warnings of the same chemicals.” (Complaint ¶ 96.) Plaintiffs also allege that “On March 21, 2022, Vagan received a ‘Limited Asbestos & Lead Survey’ report from ‘Clear Air Environmental, LLC’ providing that Asbestos was found in the samples taken from the interior walls of the Subject Property” and “Defendants were notified about the existence of Asbestos on the premises of the Subject Property and failed to take any measures to fix the hazard.” (Complaint ¶¶ 40-41.)  Thus, Plaintiffs allege Defendants engaged in an unlawful business practice.

 

Plaintiffs also allege that “Plaintiffs have been personally aggrieved by Defendants’ unfair and unlawful business acts and practices alleged herein by the loss of money or property.” (Complaint ¶ 102.)

 

However, an unfair business practices cause of action requires a plaintiff to “state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.App.4th at p. 619.) Plaintiffs’ allegations here are insufficient to meet this standard. For example, Plaintiffs have not alleged when Defendants were notified about the existence of asbestos, mold, and lead paint. And it is unclear how Plaintiffs’ alleged damages are connected to Defendants’ failure to comply with the cited statutes.

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Conversion

 

The elements of a conversion cause of action are: “(1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 108.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that “Defendants, and each of them, wrongfully took and destroyed Plaintiffs’ money or property, without permission or authorization, for their own personal and wrongful use. Defendants, and each of them, were direct beneficiaries of the conversion as they obtained financial benefits including but not limited to, the payment of personal and business debts and liabilities.” (Complaint ¶ 106.)  Plaintiffs also allege that “As a result of the Legal Conversion by Defendants, Plaintiffs have suffered damages, including but not limited to, the amount of money converted, as well as the time and money expended to recover said wrongfully converted fund, damaged properties, including but not limited to, attorneys’ fees and costs.” (Complaint ¶ 107.) Accordingly, Plaintiffs allege an ownership or right to possession of property and damages.

 

However, the only factual allegation that can reasonably be tied to the legal conclusion that Defendants took Plaintiffs’ property is the allegation that “Many of Plaintiff’s personal property went missing during the presence of the day laborers on the Subject Property.” (Complaint ¶ 31.) The fact property went missing does not suggest that property went missing because of “defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights.” (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 108.) Also, this allegation suggests the day laborers took Plaintiffs’ property, not Defendants. Accordingly, Plaintiffs have alleged no facts showing Defendants’ conversion by wrongful act or disposition of property rights.

 

Thus, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

  1. Ambiguous and Uncertain

The complaint sets forth factual allegations in paragraphs 18 through 51. The causes of actions are in paragraphs 52 through 108.

For most of the causes of action, it is not clear to the court what factual allegations Plaintiffs allege satisfy the elements of each cause of action. Accordingly, the complaint is not well drafted in this sense. But this court does not find that it rises to the level of unintelligible. The complaint is sufficiently clear to put Defendants on notice of the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiffs’ causes of action. This court overrules the demurrer on the grounds the complaint is uncertain.

V.              LEAVE TO AMEND

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, ‘leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.’” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.)¿

This court finds there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiffs can cure the defects in the complaint. Accordingly, Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend.

VI.           CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer and grants Plaintiffs leave to amend
