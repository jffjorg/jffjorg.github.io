Case Number: 23GDCV02700    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE DEFENDANT RUIZ'S MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND COMPLAINT


I.        INTRODUCTION

           Plaintiffs Jane Doe 3, Jane Doe 4, and Jane Doe K.C. 1 (collectively, Plaintiffs) allege that Defendants Gloria de Los Angeles Trevino Ruiz aka Gloria Trevi, Sergio Gustavo Andrade Sanchez aka Sergio Andrade, and Maria Raquenel Portillo Jimenez aka Mary Boquitas and Mary Raquenel engaged in sexual abuse, sexual battery, assault, molestation, and abuse against Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs further allege that Defendants, along with Defendants Gloria de Los Angeles Ruiz Arredondo Rodriguez aka Gloria Ruiz Arredondo (Ruiz) and TelevisaUnivision, Inc., acted as individuals and through entities, including Conexiones Americanas Corporation and Magical Image Entertainment, Inc., to perpetuate and cover up the abuse.

 

          Plaintiffs filed the initial complaint on December 29, 2023, and filed a first amended complaint on April 16, 2024.

 

          Ruiz filed this motion to quash service of summons on July 29, 2024, along with a declaration.

 

          Plaintiffs filed their opposition to the motion on September 16, 2024. Ruiz filed a reply to the opposition on September 30, 2024.

 

          Ruiz’s motion to quash service of summons is GRANTED.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

          Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10, subdivision (a)(1) provides that a defendant, on or before the last day to plead, may file a motion to quash service of the summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction.

 

          California’s “long-arm” statute provides for jurisdiction “on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.” (Code Civ. Proc. §410.10.)

 

          The possible bases for jurisdiction in this state are:

          (1) Physical presence in the forum when served, aka “gotcha” jurisdiction (Burnham v. Superior Court (1990) 495 U.S. 604, 612.);

          (2) Domicile in the forum state (Milliken v. Meyer (1940) 311 U.S. 457, 462.);

          (3) General appearance in the action (Code Civ. Proc. §410.50(a).);

          (4) Contractual consent/forum selection clause (National Equipment Rental, Ltd. v. Szukhent (1964) 375 U.S. 311, 315-316.); or,

          (5) Minimum contacts. (International Shoe Co. v. State of Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316; Burger King v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475.)

 

The extent to which a California court can exercise personal jurisdiction over a defendant depends on the nature and quality of defendant’s “contacts” with the state.¿ U.S. Supreme Court decisions recognize two types of jurisdiction: (1) “general” and (2) “specific.” (See¿Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Sup.Ct. of Calif., San Francisco County¿(2017) 582 U.S. 255, 262.)¿

 

General jurisdiction requires¿“substantial . . . continuous and¿systematic” contacts¿with California, i.e., the defendant’s contacts with the forum are so wide-ranging that they take the place of a physical presence¿in the state.  (Vons Companies Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 16 Cal.4th 434, 452.)  Specific jurisdiction exists where contacts are insufficient for¿general jurisdiction, but the contact is related to the cause of action presented. (Epic Communications, Inc. v. Richwave Technology, Inc.¿(2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 314, 327.) There must be some connection between the forum and the underlying cause of action, “principally, [an] activity or an occurrence that takes place in the forum State.” Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.A. v. Brown (2011) 564 U.S. 915, 919. “When there is no such connection, specific jurisdiction is lacking regardless of the extent of a defendant's unconnected activities in the State.” (Bristol-Myers, 582 U.S. at p. 264.) A court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant only if: “(1) the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits; (2) the controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant’s contacts with the forum, and (3) the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice.” (Snowney v. Harrah’s Entertainment, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1054, 1062.)

 

Although the defendant¿is the moving party on a motion to quash service of process, the burden of proof is on the plaintiff in opposing the motion: “[W]hen jurisdiction is challenged by a nonresident defendant, the burden of proof is upon the plaintiff to demonstrate that ‘minimum contacts’ exist between defendant and the forum state to justify imposition of personal jurisdiction.” (Mihlon v. Sup.Ct. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 703, 710.)  The burden is on the plaintiff to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that all jurisdictional criteria are met. (Ziller Electronics Lab GmbH v. Sup.Ct.¿(1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1232.)  If plaintiff meets this burden, “it becomes the defendant’s burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.” (Buchanan v. Soto¿(2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 1353, 1362;¿Swenberg v. Dmarcian, Inc.¿(2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 280, 291.)

 

A plaintiff attempting to assert jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant may request time to conduct discovery of jurisdictional facts necessary to sustain its burden of proof. (Safieddine v. MBC FZ, LLC (2024) 103 Cal. App.5th 1086, 1108.) To prevail on a motion for a continuance for jurisdictional discovery, the plaintiff has the burden to demonstrate that discovery is likely to lead to production of evidence of facts establishing jurisdiction. (Ibid.) A court’s denial of a plaintiff’s request is not abuse of discretion if the plaintiff failed to show that further discovery was likely to lead to the production of evidence establishing jurisdiction. (Ibid.)

           

IV.     ANALYSIS

          General Jurisdiction

The court lacks general jurisdiction over Ruiz.  She has no contacts with California that can be characterized as systematic or continuous enough to conclude she is “at home” in California.  At all relevant times, from the 1980’s through to and including the present, Ruiz has been a resident of either Mexico or Texas.  (Ruiz Decl., ¶ 4.)  She has never worked or lived in California and has never owned any property or assets in California.  (Ruiz Decl., ¶¶ 4-7.)

 

          Specific Jurisdiction

The court also lacks specific jurisdiction over Ruiz. Plaintiffs have asserted that Ruiz has contacts with California, having traveled there for recording sessions and business activities related to music production ventures, and Ruiz does not contest this. (Opp. Mot. Quash, p. 7.) This satisfies the first prong of specific jurisdiction, showing that Ruiz has purposefully availed herself of forum benefits. However, Plaintiffs have not demonstrated how Ruiz’s contacts with California relate to or give rise to their cause of action against her. All allegations in the FAC involving Ruiz concern conduct that occurred in Texas or Mexico, not in California. (FAC, ¶¶ 318-331.) Plaintiffs’ causes of action arise out of sexually abusive conduct and the ensuing cover-up; they do not arise out of Ruiz’s music production ventures, although the sexually abusive conduct occurred during the process of record and movie production. Plaintiffs therefore have failed to satisfy the second prong of specific jurisdiction, having failed to show that the controversy arises out of or is related to Ruiz’s contacts with California.

Given this finding, the Court need not analyze the third requirement for specific jurisdiction, i.e. whether the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice.  However, it is worthwhile to note that this Court does not find that exercising personal jurisdiction over Ruiz would comport with fair play and substantial justice.  Defendant Ruiz’s contacts with California have been so minimal as to be almost nonexistent.  According to Plaintiff, Ruiz visited Los Angeles twice over thirty years ago, in 1989 and 1990.  Requiring Ruiz to submit to the authority of a California court would not comport with fair play and substantial justice.  

Jurisdictional Discovery

Plaintiffs request, in the alternative to denial of Ruiz’s motion to quash service of summons, a continuance to conduct jurisdictional discovery. The court denies this request.

 

Plaintiffs anticipate that jurisdictional discovery will permit them to obtain further information concerning:

 

“(1) Additional evidence of Ruiz’s trips to California connected to Plaintiffs’ claims.

(2) Business documents related to Excis, including contracts with record companies, publishing companies, rights to royalties related Trevi’s albums recorded in California, payments made to Excis and Ruiz.

(3) Defendants Ruiz’s involvement, interest in, and/or business relations with the California-based promotion company, MIE;

(4) Defendant Ruiz’s involvement, interest in, and/or business relations with CAC;  and,

(5) The relationships between Ruiz, Excis, and Televisa, including contracts that were entered into between the parties, and particularly as it pertains to the production of Trevi’s albums which were recorded in California, and the distribution in California of Trevi-related calendars and magazines distributed by Televisa’s subsidiary, Intermix.”

(Opp. Mot. Quash, pp. 14 – 15.)

 

Out of these bases for jurisdictional discovery, all but the first concern solely the first prong of specific jurisdiction analysis and therefore are not reasonably likely to lead to the production of evidence of facts establishing jurisdiction. The first basis may concern the second prong of specific jurisdiction analysis; however, Plaintiffs have not provided facts that would justify a reasonable belief that additional relevant jurisdictional evidence exists. Plaintiffs assert that jurisdictional discovery may lead to additional evidence of Ruiz’s contacts with California, which would be insufficient to establish personal jurisdiction over Ruiz.

Plaintiffs have therefore failed to show that jurisdictional discovery is reasonably likely to lead to the production of evidence that would establish jurisdiction over Ruiz.

 

V.       CONCLUSION

           

          Ruiz’s motion to quash service of summons is GRANTED.

I.        INTRODUCTION

           Plaintiffs Jane Doe 3, Jane Doe 4, and Jane Doe K.C. 1 (collectively, Plaintiffs) allege that Defendants Gloria de Los Angeles Trevino Ruiz aka Gloria Trevi, Sergio Gustavo Andrade Sanchez aka Sergio Andrade, and Maria Raquenel Portillo Jimenez aka Mary Boquitas and Mary Raquenel engaged in sexual abuse, sexual battery, assault, molestation, and abuse against Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs further allege that Defendants, along with Defendants Gloria de Los Angeles Ruiz Arredondo Rodriguez aka Gloria Ruiz Arredondo and TelevisaUnivision, Inc. (TUI), acted as individuals and through entities, including Conexiones Americanas Corporation and Magical Image Entertainment, Inc., to perpetuate and cover up the abuse.

 

TUI was formed in early 2022, well after the allegations in the complaint took place, as a result of a transaction between Televisa and Univision. Plaintiffs allege that TUI is liable as a successor to Televisa.

 

          Plaintiffs filed the initial complaint on December 29, 2023, and filed a first amended complaint on April 16, 2024.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

A defendant may be substituted for a Doe defendant named in the original complaint if the plaintiff did not know the name or identity of the defendant at the time of filing. (Code. Civ. Proc., § 474.) In such a case the complaint is considered to have been brought against the defendant at the time that the complaint was originally filed rather than at the time when the defendant is named. (Ibid.) Failure to substitute a defendant for a Doe defendant does not necessarily bar application of the relation back doctrine, as a curable procedural defect. (Woo v. Sup.Ct. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 169, 177.)

         

Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10, subdivision (a)(1) provides that a defendant, on or before the last day to plead, may file a motion to quash service of the summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction.

 

          California’s “long-arm” statute provides for jurisdiction “on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.” (Code Civ. Proc. §410.10.)

 

          The possible bases for jurisdiction in this state are:

          (1) Physical presence in the forum when served, aka “gotcha” jurisdiction (Burnham v. Superior Court (1990) 495 U.S. 604, 612.);

          (2) Domicile in the forum state (Milliken v. Meyer (1940) 311 U.S. 457, 462.);

          (3) General appearance in the action (Code Civ. Proc. §410.50(a).);

          (4) Contractual consent/forum selection clause (National Equipment Rental, Ltd. v. Szukhent (1964) 375 U.S. 311, 315-316.); or,

          (5) Minimum contacts. (International Shoe Co. v. State of Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316; Burger King v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475.)

(Epic Communications, Inc. v. Richwave Technology, Inc.¿(2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 314, 327.) Brown (2011) 564 U.S. 915, 919. “When there is no such connection, specific jurisdiction is lacking regardless of the extent of a defendant's unconnected activities in the State.” (Bristol-Myers, 582 U.S. at p. 264.) Harrah’s Entertainment, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1054, 1062.)

 

Where a plaintiff seeks to find purposeful availment based on agency theory, the proper jurisdictional question is “whether the defendant has purposefully directed its activities at the forum state by causing a separate person or entity to engage in forum contacts.” (Anglo Irish Bank Corp., PLC v. Sup.Ct. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 969, 983.)

 

Although the defendant¿is the moving party on a motion to quash service of process, the burden of proof is on the plaintiff in opposing the motion: “[W]hen jurisdiction is challenged by a nonresident defendant, the burden of proof is upon the plaintiff to demonstrate that ‘minimum contacts’ exist between defendant and the forum state to justify imposition of personal jurisdiction.” (Mihlon v. (Ibid.) A court’s denial of a plaintiff’s request is not abuse of discretion if the plaintiff failed to show that further discovery was likely to lead to the production of evidence establishing jurisdiction. (Ibid.)

 

When a corporation purchases all or substantially all of the assets of another corporation, the purchaser generally does not assume the seller’s liabilities unless “(1) there is an express or implied agreement of assumption, (2) the transaction amounts to a consolidation or merger of the two corporations, (3) the purchasing corporation is a mere continuation of the seller, or (4) the transfer of assets to the purchaser is for the fraudulent purpose of escaping liability for the seller's debts.” (Ray v. Alad Corp. (1977) 19 Cal.3d 22, 28.)

           

III.     ANALYSIS

          Relation Back

The court finds the complaint was timely brought against TUI. Plaintiffs failed to properly substitute TUI as a Doe Defendant; however, the court finds that this is a curable procedural defect and holds that noncompliance with the procedural requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 474 does not foreclose consideration of the relation-back doctrine. Here, Plaintiffs, upon adding TUI, altered the named Doe Defendants from “Does 8 through 50” to “Does 9 through 50,” clearly indicating an intent to substitute TUI as a Doe Defendant.

 

Plaintiffs were ignorant that TUI had knowledge of Andrade’s sexual abuse and only learned it after filing the original complaint. (Menzies Decl., ¶¶ 2-6.) Plaintiffs were also ignorant that TUI is a successor to Televisa and only learned it after filing the original complaint. (Menzies Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.)

 

Plaintiffs have therefore satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 474. The complaint was timely brought against TUI.

 

          Jurisdiction

                    General Jurisdiction

The Court lacks general jurisdiction over TUI.  It has no contacts with the State that can be characterized as systematic or continuous sufficient to conclude it is “at home” in California. TUI was incorporated in Delaware and maintains addresses in Florida and new Jersey, and all its corporate executives are based in Florida.  (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 4, 7.) It has no employees or presence in California. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.) Plaintiffs allege that TUI is liable for Televisa’s conduct as its successor. The Court also lacks general jurisdiction over Televisa, which was incorporated in Mexico and has or had headquarters in Mexico. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 12.)

 

                    Specific Jurisdiction

The Court also lacks specific jurisdiction over TUI. TUI was formed in 2022 and therefore its contacts with California could not have directly given rise to or are related to Plaintiffs’ cause of action. Plaintiffs have asserted that TUI, through Televisa as its predecessor, had contacts with California because Andrade traveled to California with Plaintiffs in order to record music, fulfilling contractual obligations to Televisa. (Opp. Mot. Quash, pp. 6-7.) This is insufficient to establish that Televisa purposefully availed itself of the benefits of being in California or created a substantial connection with California because Plaintiffs have not alleged or shown facts demonstrating that Televisa requested, directed, or otherwise caused Andrade to travel to California for any purpose. However, Plaintiffs have also asserted that TUI, through Televisa as its predecessor, had contacts with California because it was involved in organizing and promoting contracts for the Trevi musical group under its contract with Trevi, including at least one concert in California. (FAC, ¶¶ 342-346.) This is sufficient to satisfy the first prong of specific jurisdiction, showing that TUI has purposefully availed itself of forum benefits.

 

However, Plaintiffs have not demonstrated how Televisa’s contacts with California give rise to Plaintiffs’ claims. Plaintiffs have not alleged that Televisa engaged in any cover-up activities or otherwise engaged in conduct underlying Plaintiffs’ cause of action while in California. Plaintiffs’ causes of action arise out of sexually abusive conduct and the ensuing cover-up; they do not arise out of Televisa’s music production ventures, although the sexually abusive conduct occurred during the process of record and movie production. Plaintiffs therefore have failed to satisfy the second prong of specific jurisdiction, having failed to show that the controversy arises out of or is related to Televisa’s contacts with California.

 

Because the second requirement for specific jurisdiction is not met, the Court will not go on to consider the third requirement. There is no basis to exercise jurisdiction over TUI as a successor to Televisa.

 

Successor Liability

The only information the Court has about the relationship between Televisa and TUI is that there is some overlap in management between them. This is insufficient to establish that TUI has inherited Televisa’s liabilities as a successor. The Court does not have enough information to clearly determine whether TUI has inherited Televisa’s liabilities as a successor. However, because the Court does not have personal jurisdiction over either TUI or Televisa, this question need not be addressed.

 

          Jurisdictional Discovery

Plaintiffs request, in the alternative to denial of the motion to quash service of summons, a continuance to conduct jurisdictional discovery. The court grants this request.

Plaintiffs anticipate that jurisdictional discovery will permit them to obtain further information concerning:

 

“(1) The details of the purchase agreement between Televisa and Univision, including a breakdown of the assets sold and retained by Televisa, the extent of liability Univision agreed to assume, and the value of the Televisa assets acquired as compared to the amount paid by Univision;

(2) The nature and duration of Sergio Andrade’s employment and/or business relations with Televisa;

(3) The relationship between Gloria Trevi and Televisa, including contracts that were entered into between the parties, and particularly as it pertains to the production of Trevi’s albums which were recorded in California.”

(Opp. Mot. Quash, pp. 11-12.)

 

These bases for jurisdictional discovery are reasonably likely to lead to the production of evidence that would establish jurisdiction over TUI. Plaintiffs have clearly and specifically described the discovery that they seek to conduct. These bases for jurisdictional discovery are likely to lead to the production of relevant information concerning whether Televisa’s conduct in California relates to Plaintiffs’ cause of action as well as to whether TUI inherited Televisa’s liabilities.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION

          TUI’s motion to quash service of summons will be CONTINUED to a date convenient to the Court and parties for the purpose of conducting jurisdictional discovery.
