Case Number: 21STCV17425    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

DAVID CROCKETT, et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

21STCV17425

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

PK3C, LLC,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 26, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Kae Soo Kim, Yong Min Kim, Yong Son Kim, and Yong Mi Kim

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Quash Service of Summons

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED in part.  The proofs of service as to defendants Kae Soo Kim, Yong Min Kim, and Yong Son Kim are quashed.  The motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE as to defendant Yong Mi Kim.

BACKGROUND

            On May 7, 2021, David Crockett and Tamicka Oliver filed a complaint against PK3C, LLC for (1) breach of implied warranty of habitability, (2) negligent maintenance of premises, (3) maintenance of nuisance, (4) IIED, (5) NIED, (6) failure to disclose latent defects, (7) negligent misrepresentation, (8) breach of contract, and (9) loss of consortium.  Plaintiffs allege that on March 1, 2016, plaintiffs entered into a written Residential Lease Agreement with defendants.  At the time plaintiffs took possession, the premises were uninhabitable and unfit for human occupation in that the toilet and bathtub routinely backed up and water leaked from the ceiling light fixture.  From the date plaintiffs moved into the premises, they became increasingly aware of numerous defects and dangerous conditions, including an incident occurring in January 2019 when the ceiling caved in about the bathtub/shower area and another incident on November 21, 2020, when the bathroom ceiling caved in about the toilet plaintiff was sitting and striking him in the head, neck, and upper body thereby causing him serious bodily injury.  They repeatedly notified the manager of the defects and dangerous conditions, but defendants failed and refused to make the necessary and proper repairs.

            On July 14, 2023, the Court denied without prejudice plaintiffs’ motion to file a First Amended Complaint.

            On September 27, 2023, the Court granted plaintiffs’ motion to file a FAC.

            On October 3, 2023, plaintiffs filed a FAC adding Yong Min Kim, Kae Soo Kim, Yong Mi Kim, Yong Son Kim, and Puk Ja Kim as defendants and alter ego allegations.

            On February 21, 2024, plaintiffs filed a SAC for (1) breach of implied warranty of habitability, (2) maintenance of nuisance, (3) IIED, (4) NIED, (5) negligent misrepresentation, (6) breach of contract, and (7) loss of consortium.

            On August 17, 2024, plaintiffs filed an amendment designating Kang Won Lee as Doe 10.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §418.10 states:  “(a) A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:

(1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.

(2) To stay or dismiss the action on the ground of inconvenient forum.

(3) To dismiss the action pursuant to the applicable provisions of Chapter 1.5 (commencing with Section 583.110) of Title 8.”  This section provides the exclusive procedure for challenging personal jurisdiction at the outset.  Roy v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal. App. 4th 337, 342.  Although defendant is the moving party, the burden of proof is on plaintiff to defeat the motion by establishing that jurisdictional grounds exist.  Mihlon v. Superior Court (1985) 169 Cal. App. 3d 703, 710.

Under Evidence Code § 647, “[t]he return of a process server registered pursuant to Chapter 16 (commencing with Section 22350) of Division 8 of the Business and Professions Code upon process or notice establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return.”  Under Evidence Code § 604, “[t]he effect of a presumption affecting the burden of producing evidence is to require the trier of fact to assume the existence of the presumed fact unless and until evidence is introduced which would support a finding of its nonexistence, in which case the trier of fact shall determine the existence or nonexistence of the presumed fact from the evidence and without regard to the presumption.  Nothing in this section shall be construed to prevent the drawing of any inference that may be appropriate.”

DISCUSSION

            Defendants Kae Soo Kim, Yong Min Kim, Yong Son Kim, and Yong Mi Kim request an order quashing the service of summons on the ground for lack of proper service.

            Kae Soo Kim (identified as “father”)

            The proof of service filed on April 17, 2024 indicates that defendant was personally served by a registered process server at 2432 El Moreno Street, La Crescenta, on March 19, 2024.  He is described as in their 30s, Asian female, 135 lbs., 5’4” height.

            Kae Soo Kim states in his declaration that he is an 89-year-old Asian man, approximately 6’ tall and weighs 165 lbs.  He states that he is a resident of Germany and lists his address in Duesseldorf and that he has lived in Germany for approximately 55 years.  He was not in California in March 2024, the La Crescenta house is not his place of residence or work, and he was not personally served at the La Crescenta address.

            Plaintiffs did not file an opposition.  They did file an amended proof of service on November 5, 2024 indicating that Kae Soo Kim was substitute served on March 19, 2024, via “Jane Doe,” with the same description as stated on the April 17, 2024 proof of service.

            The Court finds that defendant has met his burden that he was not properly served.  Plaintiffs have not met their burden of providing sufficient evidence to show that defendant was properly served via substitute service at their dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address. 

 

            Puk Ja Kim (identified as “mother”)

The proof of service filed on April 17, 2024 indicates that defendant was substitute served by a registered process server at 2432 El Moreno Street, La Crescenta, on March 19, 2024 via Kae Soo Kim, 30s, Asian female, 135 lbs., 5’4”.

Defendants state that Puk Ja Kim is deceased, having passed away in June 2017.  See Kae Soo Kim decl.  Further, the description of Kae Soo Kim is incorrect as he is an 89-year old male.

            Plaintiffs did not file an opposition.  They did file an amended proof of service on November 5, 2024 indicating that Puk Ja Kim was substitute served on March 19, 2024, via “Jane Doe,” with the same description as stated on the April 17, 2024 proof of service.

            Defendants have met their burden that Puk Ja Kim was not properly served.  The evidence indicates that she is deceased.  An action cannot be brought against her individually.  See CCP §377.40.

 

            Yong Min Kim (identified as son)

            The proof of service filed on April 17, 2024 indicates that defendant was personally served by a registered process server at 2432 El Moreno Street, La Crescenta, on March 22, 2024.

The proof of service filed on May 8, 2024 indicates that defendant was substitute served at 2432 El Moreno Street, La Crescenta on April 28, 2024 via Pok Ja Kim [which the Court notes is purportedly deceased], described as 52, Asian female, 160 lbs., 5’6” height.

Yong Min Kim states in his declaration that he is a current resident of Spain and that his address is in Santa Cruz de Tenerife.  He states that he has lived and worked in Spain for approximately four years and that he was not in California in March 2024, and has not been to California since the start of the Covid-19 pandemic.  He states that the La Crescenta address is not his place of residence or work and that he was not at the house on March 22, 2024 and was not personally served.

Plaintiffs did not file an opposition.  They did file an amended proof of service on November 5, 2024 indicating that Yong Min Kim was substitute served on March 19, 2024, via “Jane Doe,” 30s, Asian female, 135 lbs., 5’4”.

The Court finds that defendant has met his burden that he was not properly served.  Plaintiffs have not met their burden of providing sufficient evidence to show that defendant was properly served via substitute service at their dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address. 

 

            Yong Son Kim (identified as “daughter”)

            The proof of service filed on April 17, 2024 indicates that defendant was personally served by a registered process server at 2432 El Moreno Street, La Crescenta, on March 22, 2024.

            Yong Son Kim states in her declaration that she is a current resident of Orange County and her current address is on Lilly Avenue in Los Alamitos, and that the La Crescenta address is not her place of residence or work or mailing address.  She also states that she was not at the La Crescenta address on March 22, 2024 and was not personally served.

            The Court finds that defendant has rebutted the presumption that they were properly served.  Plaintiffs have not met their burden of providing sufficient evidence to show that defendant was properly served via substitute service at their dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address. 

 

            Yong Mi Kim (identified as “daughter”)

The proof of service filed on April 17, 2024 indicates that defendant was substitute served by a registered process server at 2432 El Moreno Street, La Crescenta, on March 19, 2024, via Kae Soo Kim, described as in their 30s, Asian female, 135 lbs., 5’4” height.

Defendant argues that “Kae Soo Kim’s” description is incorrect as he is an 89-year old male.

Plaintiffs did not file an opposition.  They did file an amended proof of service indicating that Yong Mi Kim was substitute served on March 19, 2024, via “Jane Doe,” with the same description as stated on the April 17, 2024 proof of service.

There is no declaration from defendant.  Also, it is unclear to the Court as to whether Yong Mi Kim resides at the La Crescenta address.  The amended proof of service indicates that she was substitute served by a “Jane Doe,” thus, defendant’s argument that the first proof of service is defective because it gives a wrong description of Kae Soo Kim is of little import. 

Thus, the Court finds that defendant Yong Mi Kim has not met her burden of rebutting the presumption that she was properly served.

Thus, the motion is GRANTED as to Kae Soo Kim, Yong Min Kim, and Yong Son Kim.   The motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE as to defendant Yong Mi Kim.

 

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED as to defendants Kae Soo Kim, Yong Min Kim, and Yong Son Kim.  The proofs of service are QUASHED.   The motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE as to defendant Yong Mi Kim.

Moving defendants are ordered to give notice.

The Court considered the moving papers.

RULING

At the hearing, plaintiff’s counsel is to provide further information as to the reasons why she seeks to be relieved.  See also other issues noted below.

BACKGROUND

On May 3, 2024, Juan Hernandez filed a complaint against Monica Jenkins for motor vehicle negligence based on an incident that occurred on May 9, 2022 at the Sepulveda Blvd. exit off the 105 Freeway.

On November 19, 2024, defendant filed an answer.

LEGAL STANDARD

The court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.  See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal. App. 2d 398.

CRC Rule 3.1362 (Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel) requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)).

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff’s counsel Mindy S. Bish, Esq./Bish & Cutting APC seeks to be relieved as counsel.

            Counsel states in her declaration that “[f]rom the time this firm was retained by Plaintiff, I have faithfully and to the best of my ability represented Plaintiff in this action.  The attorney-client relationship has been undermined by differences between my firm and the Plaintiff,

 and I am unable to fulfill my duties as an officer of the Court as a representative of Plaintiff in this case in light of these differences and resultant deterioration of the attorney-client relationship.  I decline to be more specific so as to not prejudice Plaintiff’s case, but am willing to offer further details at the time of hearing on this matter if necessary.”

            The Court finds it necessary for plaintiff’s counsel to provide further details at the hearing.

            Further, the Court notes, on October 25, 2024, the same date as the filing of the motion to be relieved, plaintiff’s counsel filed a notice of change of firm name that effective September 30, 2024, Bish & Cutting APC will change its name to Bish Law, APC and that the attorney of record for plaintiff has changed, effective immediately to Mindy S. Bish at Bish Law, APC.  The Court is unclear as to why plaintiff’s counsel is seeking to be relieved as “Mindy S. Bish, Esq./Bish & Cutting APC” if Bish Law, APC is the new name, and the name changed prior to the filing of the motion to be relieved.

Also, plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration incorrectly marked para. 6.b. rather than 6.a. as the trial in this action has not yet been set.

Moving Parties:                      Defendant City of Lomita

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Christy Andrews

Motion for an Award of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs Pursuant to CCP §1038

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion for attorney’s fees and costs is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On January 4, 2023, plaintiff Christy Andrews filed a complaint against City of Lomita for (1) premises liability under Gov. Code §§830 and 835 and (2) negligence of a public entity under Gov. Code §815.2.  Plaintiff alleges that on January 19, 2021, plaintiff was walking down the side of the road when she was struck by a tree which fell onto plaintiff at or near Walnut Street and 241st Street, Lomita, which caused plaintiff to sustain serious physical injuries.

On January 18, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC against City of Lomita, City of Torrance (as Doe 1), and West Coast Arborists (as Doe 2) for (1) premises liability under Gov. Code §§830 and 835, (2) negligence of a public entity under Gov. Code §815.2, (3) negligence, and (4) premises liability.

On March 1, 2023, City of Torrance filed a cross-complaint against City of Lomita.

On March 14, 2023, West Coast Arborists filed a cross-complaint against Roes.

On February 20, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to West Coast Arborists.

On July 1, 2024, City of Lomita filed a motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, for summary adjudication.

On July 8, 2024, City of Torrance filed a motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, for summary adjudication.

On October 3, 2024, a hearing was held on the motions for summary judgment.  The Court took the matters under submission.

On October 17, 2024, the Court issued its ruling, adopting the tentative rulings heard on October 3.  The Court denied Torrance’s motion for summary judgment, granted summary adjudication as to the second, third, and fourth causes of action, and denied summary adjudication as to the first cause of action.  The Court granted Lomita’s motion for summary judgment.

On October 28, 2024, Torrance dismissed its cross-complaint against Lomita.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §1032(b) states:  “Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.”

CCP §1033.5(a)(10)(B)-(C) provides that attorney’s fees are recoverable as costs when authorized by “statute” or “law.”

CCP §1038 provides:  “(a) In any civil proceeding under the Government Claims Act (Division 3.6 (commencing with Section 810) of Title 1 of the Government Code) or for express or implied indemnity or for contribution in any civil action, the court, upon motion of the defendant or cross-defendant, shall, at the time of the granting of any summary judgment, motion for directed verdict, motion for judgment under Section 631.8, or any nonsuit dismissing the moving party other than the plaintiff, petitioner, cross-complainant, or intervenor, or at a later time set forth by rule of the Judicial Council adopted under Section 1034, determine whether or not the plaintiff, petitioner, cross-complainant, or intervenor brought the proceeding with reasonable cause and in the good faith belief that there was a justifiable controversy under the facts and law which warranted the filing of the complaint, petition, cross-complaint, or complaint or answer in intervention.  If the court should determine that the proceeding was not brought in good faith and with reasonable cause, an additional issue shall be decided as to the defense costs reasonably and necessarily incurred by the party or parties opposing the proceeding, and the court shall render judgment in favor of that party in the amount of all reasonable and necessary defense costs, in addition to those costs normally awarded to the prevailing party.  An award of defense costs under this section shall not be made except on notice contained in a party’s papers and an opportunity to be heard.

(b) ‘Defense costs,’ as used in this section, shall include reasonable attorney's fees, expert witness fees, the expense of services of experts, advisers, and consultants in defense of the proceeding, and where reasonably and necessarily incurred in defending the proceeding.

(c) This section shall be applicable only on motion made before the discharge of the jury or entry of judgment . . . .

(d) This section shall only apply if the defendant . . . has made a motion for summary judgment, judgment under Section 631.8, directed verdict, or nonsuit and the motion is granted.”

“Section 1038 encompasses both the initial filing of an action and its continued maintenance if done without good faith and reasonable cause.”  Suarez v. City of Corona (2014) 229Cal. App. 4th 325, 332 (citation omitted).

“We hold that section 1038 defense costs, which include attorney fees, may not be imposed on a plaintiff’s attorney even if counsel lacks reasonable cause and good faith in filing or maintaining a tort action against a public entity.”  Settle v State (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 215, 219.

DISCUSSION

Defendant City of Lomita requests attorney’s fees in the amount of $109,984.50 (487.20 hours) pursuant to CCP §1038.

“Section 1038 ‘provides public entities (which, since 1983, have been constitutionally proscribed from filing malicious prosecution actions) and other specified defendants with a way to recover the costs of defending against unmeritorious and frivolous litigation.’”  Suarez, supra, at 332 (citation omitted).

Defendant was the prevailing party on its motion for summary judgment and it is a public entity. 

Defendant argues that plaintiff maintained her lawsuit against Lomita “with full knowledge that she had no valid claim against Lomita.”   Defendant asserts that on February 9, 2023, defense counsel sent a letter to plaintiff’s counsel stating that “maintenance of the case against City of Lomita lacks good faith and reasonable cause” because “the City of Lomita did not own or control the tree in the alleged incident” and asserting that it would pursue recovery of attorneys’ fees and costs under CCP §1038.  In this letter, City of Lomita represented that the tree was on the City of Torrance property and that it “belonged to the City of Torrance.”  Defense counsel sent another letter to plaintiff’s counsel on August 29, 2023, stating that it did not have control of the tree and that it did not have notice.  In the August letter, defendant no longer represents that the tree was on Torrance property.  In support of the motion, defendant reiterates its arguments made in the motion for summary judgment, including that the tree was included within the City of Torrance’s tree inventory and that Torrance serviced and maintained the subject tree for at least five years prior to the incident.  Lomita contends that it was not until after the accident occurred that it determined that the subject tree was within the jurisdictional boundary of Lomita.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that Lomita cannot meet its burden of showing that plaintiff’s claims were frivolous or were otherwise brought and maintained without good faith and reasonable cause.  Plaintiff asserts that “Lomita’s arguments ignore or gloss over substantive factual questions and ambiguities with respect to Lomita’s legal relationship to the site of Plaintiff’s accident and the subject.”  Plaintiff contends that there was no evidence indicating that Lomita had ever in any way affirmatively forfeited, ceded, or abandoned its responsibility for the care and maintenance of the subject tree and points out that Lomita removed the tree the day immediately after the occurrence of the incident.  Plaintiff also argues that Lomita’s own party witness purportedly testified that the tree was visibly “not structurally sound,” “thereby raising the question of whether Lomita employees knew or should have known about the tree’s dangerous condition.”  Plaintiff asserts that she maintained the action against Lomita in good faith and with reasonable cause.  Moreover, plaintiff argues, Lomita’s request for fees is unreasonable and unsupported.

In reply, defendant reiterates its arguments.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that plaintiff did not lack good faith and reasonable cause in filing or maintaining the complaint against City of Lomita.  There were ambiguities as plaintiff stated.  The motion for summary judgment and opposition were extensively briefed, and the Court took the matter under submission in light of the issues, including as to ownership, possession, and control.  While it would have been preferred that the issues of ownership and control over the tree were clearly understood by all the parties, even the city defendants were unclear regarding the tree.  That confusion inured to the benefit on no one.  But it can not be concluded that Plaintiff’s pursuit of this case was so lacking in merit that the Court should award attorney fees under the statute cited by the City of Lomita.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

ORDER

The motion is DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion for Summary Judgment

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            On July 9, 2024, plaintiff Adam Garasad filed an unlawful detainer complaint against defendant Linda Veerkamp based on CCP §1161a.

            On August 15, 2024, defendant (self-represented) filed an answer.

            On September 18, 2024, defendant filed a substitution of attorney and is now represented by counsel.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  “A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.  Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto. The defendant or cross-defendant shall not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  Id.  “’An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.’”  Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal. App. 4th 1001, 1014 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Adam Garasad requests summary judgment for unlawful detainer in favor of plaintiff and against all defendants on the ground that there is no triable issue of fact and as a matter of law, plaintiff prevails.

The unlawful detainer complaint alleges that defendant resides in and is the former owner of the real property located at 18320 Regina Avenue, Torrance.  Complaint, ¶2.  Plaintiff is the current owner and entitled to possession of the premises/subject property.  Id., ¶4.  On May 9, 2024, plaintiff acquired title to the subject property at a duly noticed trustee’s sale, and such title as perfected by the recordation of a Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale on May 17, 2024, citing to Exhibit 1 for true and correct copy of TDUS.  Id., ¶5.  On June 3, 2024, a Grant Deed was recorded transferring title to Adam Garasad, who originally acquired title under his nickname, Dimo Garasad, citing to Exhibit 2 for true and correct copy of grant deed.  Id., ¶6.  Pursuant to CCP §1161a, on July 2, 2024, plaintiff caused to be served upon defendants a three/thirty/ninety day written notice requiring defendants to quit and deliver possession of the subject property to plaintiff after expiration of the notice to vacate, citing to exhibit 3 for true and correct copy of notice and proof of service.  Id., ¶9.  No defendant is entitled to more than three days’ notice to quit occupation of the property because defendant, the former homeowner and a party to the foreclosed note and deed of trust, still occupies the property.  Id., ¶10.  More than three days have elapsed and defendants have failed and continue to fail to deliver possession.  Id., ¶11.

“In any of the following cases, a person who holds over and continues in possession of . . . real property after a three-day written notice to quit the property has been served upon the person, or if there is a subtenant in actual occupation of the premises, also upon such subtenant, as prescribed in Section 1162, may be removed therefrom as prescribed in this chapter: . . . (3) Where the property has been sold in accordance with Section 2924 of the Civil Code, under a power of sale contained in a deed of trust executed by such person, or a person under whom such person claims, and the title under the sale has been duly perfected.”  CCP §1161a(b)(3).

Under CCP §1161a(b)(3), three conditions must be satisfied as prerequisites to filing an unlawful detainer:  (1) property that has been sold in accordance with Civil Code §2924; (2) pursuant to a power of sale contained in a deed of trust executed by the holdover possessor, or a person under who such person claims; and (3) the title under the sale has been duly perfected.  See Dr. Leevil, LLC v. Westlake Health Care Center (2018) 6 Cal.5th 474, 479.  Duly perfected title requires that title be recorded, and title must be perfected prior to service of a 3-day notice under CCP §1161a(b)(3).  Id.

Plaintiff presents evidence of a proper trustee’s sale pursuant to a power of sale and the rebuttable presumption of a properly noticed sale under Civil Code §2924(c); (2) duly perfected title by delivery and recordation; and (3) service of a 3-day notice to quit after a proper trustee’s sale under Civil Code §2924 and due perfection of title.  Plaintiff filed the complaint more than three days after service of the notice.  See Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“PUMF”) 1-12; Request for Judicial Notice (Exhs. 1-5); Katherine Meleski decl. and Exh. 1 (notice to quit); Adam Garasad decl., Hector Flores (Exhs. 1, 2 – notice to quit and proof of service).

There is no opposition.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that plaintiff has met his burden of showing that there is no defense to the cause of action for unlawful detainer because he has proved each element of the cause of action under Civil Code §1161a.

There is no opposition.  Thus, defendant has not met her burden of presenting sufficient evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact.

The motion is GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to submit a form of Judgment.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
