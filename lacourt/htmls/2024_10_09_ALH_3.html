[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S MOTIONS TO COMPEL DEPOSITIONS OF DEFENDANTS YUN XIANG TSENG AND KRISTINE A. NALDJIAN

 

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

October 9, 2024

 

On January 18, 2022, Fan Peng (“Peng”) filed this action against Yun Xiang Tseng (“Tseng”), Zhao Cesarone, Anna Grace, Kristine A. Naldjian (“Naldjian”), Dao House, LLC, and WDA, LLC for breach of contract, intentional and negligent misrepresentation, false advertising, conversion, unjust enrichment, intentional infliction of emotional distress, “purchase money resulting trust”, and “alter ego.” Peng filed an amended complaint (“FAC”) on January 31, 2022, which added Childwise International Corporation as a defendant. On March 30, 2022, Tseng filed a cross-complaint for breach of contract against Peng. Then, Peng filed the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on July 19, 2022, which added Zhou Sail Electronic Co., Ltd. (“Sail Electronic”) as a plaintiff.

On May 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed these two motions for orders compelling Tseng and Naldjian to appear for their depositions. Plaintiff also request sanctions against Tseng and Naldjian in the amount of $3,067.26 each.

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party or party-affiliated deponent to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production . . . of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff’s moving papers attach nearly 80 pages of email correspondence between Plaintiff’s counsel and counsel for Tseng and Naldjian from March 2023 to March 2024, evidencing attempts to schedule their depositions. The emails also show that before filing these motions, Plaintiff’s counsel inquired about Tseng and Naldjian’s availability in April 2024, but did not receive any reply. (Motion, Ex. O.)

However, the Court notes that Plaintiff did not include any deposition notice (either the initial or any amended notices) for Tseng or Naldjian. Therefore, the threshold requirement that a deposition notice was actually served has not been established. The Court also notes that Plaintiff’s counsel provides no explanation for why the requested sanctions are reasonable. Accordingly, the motions are denied without prejudice.

VI.    CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motions are DENIED without prejudice.

Moving party to give notice. 

 

Dated this 9th day of October 2024

 

 

 

 

William A. Crowfoot

Judge of the Superior Court

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA FOR EMPLOYMENT RECORDS

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

October 9, 2024

On June 27, 2023, plaintiff Jocelyn Lopez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against her former employer, defendant Arcadia Living, LLC (“Defendant”). Plaintiff alleges that she was employed as an Office Manager for Defendant and began working for Defendant on or about July 16, 2016. (Compl., ¶¶ 6-7.) Plaintiff alleges that she was a non-exempt employee and that Defendant failed to: (1) pay overtime compensation, (2) pay minimum wage, (3) provide meal periods, (4) provide rest breaks, and (5) issue accurate and itemized wage statements. Plaintiff also asserts a claim for violation of Labor Code section 2802 et seq. and Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq. 

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for an order quashing Defendant’s subpoenas to Plaintiff’s current employer, Cedars Sinai Health Ventures (“Cedars Sinai”). Alternatively, Plaintiff seeks an order limiting the documents to be produced. The documents sought by Defendant from Cedars Sinai include the following:

1)   Any and all resumes PLAINTIFF submitted at any time from 2018 to the present.

2)   Any and all job applications PLAINTIFF submitted at any time from 2018 to the present.

3)   Any and all receipts for employee handbooks or manuals regarding terms of employment PLAINTIFF signed at any time from 2018 to the present.

4)   Any and all employee handbooks or manuals regarding terms of employment YOU provided to PLAINTIFF from 2018 to the present. 

Plaintiff objects to the production of these documents on the grounds that the documents are irrelevant and production of her employment records from Cedars Sinai, her current employer, would violate her right to privacy. (Motion, p. 5.)

The right of privacy protects an individual’s “reasonable expectation of privacy against a serious invasion.” (Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Sup.Ct. (2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 370.) The protection is qualified, not absolute, and the Court must balance the right of privacy against the need for discovery. The burden is on the party asserting a privacy interest to “establish its extent and seriousness of the prospective invasion.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557.)  Once a privacy interest is established, the party seeking discovery of information within the “zone of privacy” must show that the information is essential to determining the truth of the disputed matters and not available from other sources or through less intrusive means. (Britt v. Sup.Ct. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 859-862.)

Here, Plaintiff argues that her employment records are private and that her resume, job applications, and acknowledgement of receipts for employee handles would violate not only her right to privacy but the privacy of third parties. Plaintiff argues that since she does not seek lost wages or damages extending beyond her employment with Defendant, any information about subsequent employment is irrelevant.

Personnel documents and information, communicated to an employer in confidence are covered by the employee’s constitutional right of privacy. Board of Trustees v. Superior Court (1981) 119 Cal. App. 3d 516, 524-26; see also Harding Lawson Assocs. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 7, 10 [holding that personnel files are protected by the right of privacy unless the requesting party shows a compelling need for the particular documents and that the information cannot reasonably be obtained through depositions or from nonconfidential sources].)

In opposition, Defendant argues that the documents sought are relevant because it asserts as an affirmative defense that Plaintiff was an administrative employee exempt from overtime and minimum wage laws. (Opp., p. 4.)  Defendant argues that Plaintiff “customarily and regularly exercised discretion and independent judgment, which is a required element to prove exempt administrative employee status under state law.” (Opp., p. 4.) Therefore, Defendant claims that Plaintiff’s resumes and job applications to prospective employers, which outline her prior job responsibilities, duties, and title with Defendant, are relevant to the claim in dispute and serve as declarations against interest. Defendant also argues, without providing any authority, that Plaintiff does not have a privacy right to information voluntarily disclosed in anticipation of potential employment, and that resumes, job applications, employee handbooks, and manuals are not private documents with respect to which Plaintiff would have a reasonable expectation of privacy. (Opp., p. 5.)

The Labor Code authorizes the Industrial Welfare Commission (IWC) to establish exemptions for employees who perform certain duties and who “earn[ ] a monthly salary equivalent to no less than two times the state minimum wage for full-time employment.” (Lab. Code, § 515, subd. (a).) In turn, IWC wage order No. 4-2001 (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 8, § 11040; Wage Order 4), which governs “persons employed in professional, technical, clerical, mechanical, and similar occupations,” states an employee is exempt under the administrative exemption if that employee (1) is primarily engaged in exempt duties and (2) earns “a monthly salary equivalent to no less than two (2) times the state minimum wage for full-time employment.” (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 8, § 11040, subd. 1(A)(2)(g).) The assertion of an exemption is an affirmative defense and the employer bears the burden of proving its existence. (Semprini v. Wedbush Securities, Inc. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 246, 251.)

Although Defendant argues that records sought are relevant to one element of its affirmative defense, Defendant does not establish an evidentiary foundation for an exemption because there is no evidence that Plaintiff was salaried or paid the equivalent of, or more than, 2 times the state minimum wage for full-time employment. In her moving papers, Plaintiff includes a declaration in which she states that she was most recently paid on an hourly basis of $26. (Motion, Lopez Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant’s supplemental response to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatory No. 9 also states: “In January 18, 2021, the hourly rate was increased from $20 to $23; the hourly rate was increased to $25.5 thereafter; In July 1, 2022, the hourly rate was increased from $25.5 to $26, until June 15, 2024.” (Reply, Kreiman Decl., Ex. E.)

Since Plaintiff was paid on an hourly basis, indicating that her earnings are subject to the number of hours worked, she was not paid a salary. Plaintiff also asserts that, based on Defendant’s discovery responses, Defendant admits that she was not paid the requisite amount. Therefore, Defendant fails to show that the documents sought are relevant.  Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the motion to quash the subpoena directed to Cedars Sinai. The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $5,850 against Defendant and counsel of record. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is not properly noticed and there is no explanation for how that amount constitutes her reasonable attorney’s fees. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.)
