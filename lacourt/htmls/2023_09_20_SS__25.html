Case Number: 20STLC05524    Hearing Date: September 20, 2023    Dept: 25

State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company v. Guadalupe Garcia

20stlc05524


ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On July 1, 2020, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Guadalupe Garcia (“Garcia”), for subrogation, stemming from an automobile collision between Defendant, on the one hand, and an individual insured by Plaintiff’s insurance policy, on the other hand.  (Compl.)  Plaintiff compensated the insured for claimed damages in the amount of $21,815.33 and filed the instant claim against Defendant for allegedly causing the damages.  (Ibid. pp. 3-4.)  Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint on October 5, 2020.

 

On June 22, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for Settlement and Entry of Judgment and Order (“Stipulation”), signed by Plaintiff and its representatives and Defendant and her attorney, to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $14,500.  (6-22-21 Stipulation.)  On July 15, 2021, the Court dismissed the entire case without prejudice pursuant to the Stipulation.  (7-15-21 Order.)

 

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement Agreement, and Enter Judgment (“Motion”), as well as a Memorandum of Costs.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

CCP § 664.6, provides a summary procedure that enables judges to enforce a settlement agreement by entering a judgment pursuant to the terms of the parties’ settlement.  In particular, the statute provides:

 

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.  If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following:

 

(1) The party.

(2) An attorney who represents the party.

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf.

 

CCP § 664.6(a)-(b) (emphasis added).

 

III.            Discussion

 

A. Retention of Jurisdiction

 

“‘[V]oluntary dismissal of an action or special proceeding terminates the court’s jurisdiction over the matter.’  (Conservatorship of Martha P. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 857, 867) [12 Cal.Rptr.3d 142.) ‘If requested by the parties,’ however, ‘the [trial] court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce [a] settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.’ (§ 664.6, italics added.)”  (Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 917.)  “‘Because of its summary nature, strict compliance with the requirements of section 664.6 is prerequisite to invoking the power of the court to impose a settlement agreement.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Sully-Miller Contracting Co. v. Gledson/Cashman Construction, Inc. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 30, 37).)

 

“A request for the trial court to retain jurisdiction under section 664.6 ‘must conform to the same three requirements which the Legislature and the courts have deemed necessary for section 664.6 enforcement of the settlement itself: the request must be made (1) during the pendency of the case, not after the case has been dismissed in its entirety, (2) by the parties themselves, and (3) either in a writing signed by the parties or orally before the court.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 440).)  “The ‘request must be express, not implied from other language, and it must be clear and unambiguous.’” (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen, supra, 97 Cal.App.4th at 440).)

 

Here, the parties signed a Stipulation for Settlement and Entry of Judgment and Order (“Stipulation”) containing the parties’ agreement for the Court to retain jurisdiction pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 664.6 to enforce the terms of the stipulation and enter judgment in the event of default.  (6-22-21 Stip. ¶ 15.)  Prior to the dismissal of this action, the Stipulation was signed by the parties and their representatives and submitted to the Court.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)  On July 15, 2021, the Court dismissed the entire case pursuant to the Stipulation and expressly stated that it “shall retain Jurisdiction to enforce the Stipulation for Settlement and Entry of Judgment pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.”  (7-15-21 Order.)

 

The Court finds that the Stipulation complies with § 664.6 requirements and the Court has retained jurisdiction to enter judgment pursuant to the parties’ Stipulation in this action.

 

B. Entry of Judgment

 

The Stipulation Agreement filed by Plaintiff on June 22, 2021, provides that Plaintiff and Defendant agreed to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of 14,500.00, without any interest.  (6-22-21 Stip. ¶ 5B.)  Pursuant to the Stipulation, Defendant’s insurance carrier, Infinity Insurance, would pay the sum of $10,000.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.A.I.)  Defendant would pay the remaining balance of $4,500 in monthly payments of $50.00 beginning on July 1, 2021, and continuing on the first day of each month, until the balance is paid in full.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.A.II.)  All parties signed the Stipulation.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)

 

The Stipulation also provides that Defendant will have a 10-day grace period to make payments.  (Ibid. at ¶ 10.)  If Defendant fails to make payments, Plaintiff will provide written notice of default to Defendant.  (Ibid.)  If Defendant does not cure the default within ten (10) days of receiving notice, “Plaintiff may immediately cause Judgment to be entered pursuant Paragraph 4, in the amount of $21,815.33, plus costs of suit, interest from from [sic] 3/27/2019, and attorney’s fees.”  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 8, 10.)

 

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion alleging that Defendant breached the Stipulation and seeking judgment in the amount of $13,609.67 for the settlement amount, interest accrued at 7% per annum, attorney’s fees and costs, minus payments from Defendant and Defendant’s insurance carrier.  (Mot. pp. 1-2.)

 

Around June 21, 2021, Defendant’s insurance carrier, Infinity Insurance Company, made a payment of $10,000 to Plaintiff.  (Mahfouz II Decl. ¶ 7.)  As of the date of the Motion, Defendant has only made payments in the amount of $650.  (Ibid. at ¶ 9.)  Plaintiff’s counsel sent past due notices to Defendant on March 29 and June 5, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 9, Ex. B.)  As of the date of this Motion, Defendant has not cured the default and there is an outstanding balance remaining.  (Ibid. at ¶ 10.)  Plaintiff seeks to have the dismissal set aside and judgment entered in the amount of $13,609.67 as follows: $21,815.33 (principal amount), plus $873.62 (interest accrued at a legal rate of 7% per annum since default date of December 1, 2022), plus $526.27 (costs), plus $1,044.45 (attorney’s fees), minus $10,650.00 (payments made by Defendant and her insurance carrier).  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 11-12.)

 

Plaintiff states that it is entitled to prejudgment interest at a rate of seven (7) percent per annum, as set forth in the Stipulation.  (Mem. p. 5; Stip. ¶ 4.)  Plaintiff calculates interest from December 1, 2022, date of default, to June 28, 2023, the date the instant Motion was prepared.  (Mem. p. 5.)  Plaintiff accurately calculates interest in the amount of $873.62.

 

Plaintiff calculates attorney’s fees based on the Los Angeles County Superior Court Local Rule 3.214, which provides that a recovery between $10,000.01 to $50,000 warrants $690 in attorney’s fees, plus 3% of the excess over $10,000, amounting to a total of $1,044.45.  (Ibid. at p. 6; Mahfouz II Decl. ¶ 12.)  Furthermore, Plaintiff has submitted a Memorandum of Costs showing that it requests $391.27 for filing the initial Complaint, $75 for service of process, and $60.00 for filing the instant Motion, for a total of $526.27 in costs.  (Ibid.; Mem. of Costs.)

 

The Court finds the Stipulation to be valid and enforceable under Code of Civil Procedure § 664.6.  Plaintiff provides evidence that Defendant stopped making payments and did not respond to Plaintiff’s written notice of default.  Thus, a valid and signed stipulation agreement was breached and the Court retained jurisdiction to enter judgment upon breach.  Plaintiff has also provided authority to show that it is entitled to the settlement amount, less any payments made, costs incurred in enforcing the Stipulation, interest, and attorney’s fees.  (Mem. pp. 4-6.)  The Court finds Plaintiff’s request as to attorney’s fees and costs to be reasonable.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment is GRANTED.  Dismissal entered on July 15, 2021, is vacated, and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $13,609.67 as follows: $11,165.33 (principal amount), $873.62 (interest), $526.27 (costs), $1044.45 (attorney’s fees).

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment is GRANTED.  Dismissal entered on July 15, 2021, is vacated, and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $13,609.67 as follows: $11,165.33 (principal amount), $873.62 (interest), $526.27 (costs), $1044.45 (attorney’s fees).

Case Number: 21STLC08740    Hearing Date: September 20, 2023    Dept: 25

State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company v. Eddie Nieves

21stlc08740

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On December 10, 2021, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Eddie Nieves aka Edward Nieves (“Defendant”) for subrogation.  The action arose out of an alleged automobile accident on December 24, 2020, between Defendant, on the one hand, and an individual insured by Plaintiff’s insurance policy, on the other hand.  (Compl. ¶ 6.)  Plaintiff compensated insured in the amount of $12,389.79 and filed the instant action to recover damages from Defendant.  (Compl. pp. 2-3.)  On April 25, 2022, Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint.

On October 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted and Request for Sanctions.  The Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion and request for sanctions in the amount of $460.  (11-21-22 Minute Order.)

 

On February 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.  On May 18, 2023, Defendant filed a late Opposition, two court days before the hearing, which was not received by the Court in time to be reviewed and considered.  On May 22, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion and on June 5, 2023, judgment was entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $12,389.79 (principal amount), $1,256.64 (interest), and $365.00 (costs.), for a total judgment of $14,011.43.  (5-22-23 Minute Order; 6-5-23 Judgment.)  On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed proof of serving the Notice of Judgment on Defendant.  (6-27-23 Notice of Judgment.)

 

On June 29, 2023, Defendant filed A Motion to Vacate Judgment and for Leave to Defend.  On July 24, 2023, the Court granted the Motion and vacated all judgments entered against Defendant.  (7-24-23 Minute Order.)

 

Defendant also filed the instant Motion to be Relieved from Deemed Admissions (“Motion”).  No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.300 provides, in pertinent part:

 

“(a) A party may withdraw or amend an admission made in response to a request for admission only on leave of court granted after notice to all parties.

 

(b) The court may permit withdrawal or amendment of an admission only if it determines that the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.300(a)-(b).)

 

(c) The court may impose conditions on the granting of the motion that are just, including, but not limited to, the following:

 

(1) An order that the party who obtained the admission be permitted to pursue additional discovery related to the matter involved in the withdrawn or amended admission.

 

(2) An order that the costs of any additional discovery be borne in whole or in part by the party withdrawing or amending the admission.

 

“A party seeking discretionary relief on the ground of attorney error must demonstrate that the error was excusable, since the attorney’s negligence is imputed to the client. [Citation.]”  (Huh v. Wang (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 1406, 1419.)  “Excusable neglect is that neglect which might have been the act of a reasonably prudent person under the same circumstances.  [Citation.]” (Ibid.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Defendant moves to be relieved from the Court order deeming Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions (Set One) admitted on November 21, 2022, on the grounds of attorney’s “inadvertence, mistake or reasonable neglect.”  (Mot. p. 2.)

 

Defendant has submitted counsel Jon E. Hellesen’s declaration in support of the Motion.  Hellesen explains that the former attorney representing Defendant has left the firm, along with several other attorneys.  (Hellesen Decl. ¶¶  2-3.)  Hellesen joined the firm approximately two months prior and was recently assigned to handle Defendant’s case.  (Ibid.)  After Hellesen was assigned to the case, he immediately contacted Defendant and obtained a signed verification of Defendant’s responses to the Requests for Admission.  (Ibid. at ¶ 3.)  He asserts that the failure to obtain and provide signed verifications was due to excusable neglect because the former attorney left the firm abruptly, and verifications could not be obtained until Hellesen was assigned to the case and established contact with Defendant.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  He attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s attorney and immediately sent the responses with verifications on May 18, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  Counsel was unable to oppose Plaintiff’s Motions as the verification was not served at the time.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)  Counsel has submitted a copy of Defendant’s unverified responses, the executed verification, and proof of service of the verification.  (Ibid.at ¶¶ 7-9.)

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

The Court finds that Defendant has presented sufficient evidence that failure to respond to the Requests for Admission was the result of his attorney’s excusable neglect.  The Court also finds that Plaintiff will not be substantially prejudiced if Defendant is relieved from the deemed admissions and permitted to respond to the discovery request.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s Motion to Be Relieved from Deemed Admissions.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons: The Motion to Be Relieved from Deemed Admissions, filed by Defendant Eddie Nieves, is GRANTED.  The Court’s November 21, 2022, Order granting Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted, is hereby VACATED.

Case Number: 23STCP01692    Hearing Date: September 20, 2023    Dept: 25

All Premium Contractors, Inc. v. Kenneth Caldera

23stcp01692

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On November 7, 2022, arbitrator Stephen L. Backus, Esq., from the American Arbitration Association, issued a Final Award of Arbitrator in favor of Petitioner All Premium Contractors, Inc. (“Petitioner”) and against Respondent Kenneth Caldera (“Respondent”) in the sum of $22,249.70.  (Pet. ¶¶ 6-8, pp. 16-19.)

 

On May 17, 2023, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (“Petition”) as to Respondent Caldera.  Petitioner filed proof that Respondent was personally served with the Petition and supporting documents on May 30, 2023.  (6-7-23 Proof of Service.)

 

On June 20, 2023, Respondent filed a General Denial.  On June 28, 2023, Respondent filed a Demand for Arbitration.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Once arbitration is concluded, “any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.”  (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.).  Any of the parties may file a petition with the court, which must then “confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.”  (Code of Civil Proc. §§ 1285, 1286; EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc. (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.)  “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.”  (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.)  “Neither the trial court, nor the appellate court, may ‘review the merits of the dispute, the sufficiency of the evidence, or the arbitrator’s reasoning, nor may we correct or review an award because of an arbitrator’s legal or factual error, even if it appears on the award’s face.”  (EHM Productions, supra, at p. 1063-64.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure, § 1285.4 states: “A petition under this chapter shall:

 

(a)   Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement.

(b)   Set forth the names of the arbitrators.

(c)   Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.

 

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 1283.6 provides that: “The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”  (Emphasis added.)  In addition, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by filing and serving a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served.  (Code Civ. Proc.  §§ 1288, 1288.4.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 1290.4, the statute governing proper service of this Petition states, in pertinent part:

 

“(a) A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.

 

(b) If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision: ¶ (1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.

 

Moreover, the Court shall vacate the award if any of the following conditions are proven:

 

(1)   The award was procured by corruption, fraud or other undue means.

 

(2)   There was corruption in any of the arbitrators.

 

(3)   The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced by misconduct of a neutral arbitrator.

 

(4)   The arbitrators exceeded their powers and the award cannot be corrected without affecting the merits of the decision upon the controversy submitted.

 

(5)   The rights of the parties were substantially prejudiced by the refusal of the arbitrators to postpone the hearing upon sufficient cause being shown therefor or by the refusal of the arbitrators to hear evidence material to the controversy or by other conduct of the arbitrators contrary to the provisions of this title.

 

(6)   An arbitrator making the award…(B) was subject to disqualification upon grounds specified in Section 1281.91 but failed upon receipt of timely demand to disqualify himself or herself as required by that provision. . . .”

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.2(a).)

 

Only where both (1) the arbitrator abused his or her discretion and (2) there was resulting prejudice, can a trial court properly vacate an arbitration award.  (SWAB Financial, LLC v. E*Trade Securities, LLC (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1198.)

 

A party may seek an order vacating or correcting an award in a standalone petition “not later than 100 days after date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.”  (Darby v. Sisyphian, LLC (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 1100, 1110, citing to Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 1285, 1288, 1288.2.)  Alternatively, a party may seek an order vacating or correcting an award in its response to a prior-filed petition to confirm that award, as provided in § 1285.2.  (Ibid.)  In this case, the deadline for filing that response is 10 days from the date the responding party is served with the petition to confirm, unless the deadline is extended by the parties by an agreement in writing or by the Court for good cause.  (Ibid. at 1110-1111, citing to Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.6.)  In Darby, the Court ruled that if a petition to confirm arbitration award is filed less than 90 days after the award is served, the request to vacate the award “must be filed and served within 10 days of service of the petition to confirm.”  (Ibid. at 1110-1111.)  However, if the petition to confirm arbitration award is filed more than 90 days after an award is served, the request to vacate the award must be filed within 100 days of service of the award.  (Ibid. at 1110-1111.)  While the 10-day deadline is subject to extension by the parties or the Court, the 100-day deadline is “otherwise immovable, as the statute setting that deadline brooks no exceptions.”  (Ibid. at 1111.)  The Court concluded that if a procedurally proper petition to confirm arbitration award is filed and there is no timely filing seeking to vacate or correct the award, the “trial court ‘shall’ confirm the award.”  (Ibid. at 1113.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Petitioner asserts that on July 26, 2018, Petitioner and Respondent entered into a Home Improvement Agreement containing an arbitration provision governing disputes between the parties arising out of the Agreement.  (Pet. ¶ 4.)  A copy of the agreement is filed as Attachment 4(b) and indicates that any controversy or claims would “be settled by binding arbitration in accordance with the construction industry arbitration rules of the American Arbitration Association.”  (Pet. pp. 4-15.)  On November 7, 2022, arbitrator Stephen L. Backus, Esq., from the American Arbitration Association, issued a Final Award of Arbitrator in favor of Petitioner and against Respondent in the sum of $22,249.70.  (Pet. ¶¶ 6-8, pp. 16-19.)  A copy of the Award is filed as Attachment 8(c).  (Pet. pp. 16-19.)

 

Petitioner filed proof that Respondent was personally served with the Petition and supporting documents on May 30, 2023.  (6-7-23 Proof of Service.)

 

The Court finds the following deficiencies in the Petition.  First, the Petition lists the Stanley Mosk Courthouse address instead of the Spring Street Courthouse address.  (Pet. p. 1.)  However, the Notice of Hearing, issued by the Court and served on Respondent, lists the Spring Street Courthouse, thus, Respondent has been provided adequate notice.  Second, Petitioner has not filed any proof that the neutral arbitrator served the Final Award on the parties.

 

In response to the Petition, Respondent has filed a General Denial and Demand for Arbitration.  Respondent argues that Petitioner has violated the Home Improvement Agreement by filing a complaint instead of submitting the dispute to arbitration.  (Demand for Arbitration p. 1.)  The Court finds that that there is sufficient evidence presented by Petitioner that it has submitted the dispute to arbitration to the American Arbitration Association, Construction Arbitration Tribunal.

 

However, the Court continues the hearing on the Petition and orders Petitioner to file proof that the Petition was served on the parties by the neutral arbitrator in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure § 1283.6.

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

The hearing on the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award, filed by All Premium Contractors, Inc., is CONTINUED to October 30, 2023 at 10:00 a.m., in Department 25 at Spring Street Courthouse.  Petitioner is ordered to file supplemental papers as discussed herein at least sixteen (16) court days before the scheduled hearing.  Failure to do so may result in the Petition being placed off calendar or denied.

 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23STLC00488    Hearing Date: September 20, 2023    Dept: 25

1409 Manhattan Apts, LLC v. RidgeGate Escrow, Inc.

23stlc00488

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On January 23, 2023, Plaintiff 1409 Manhattan Apt, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant RidgeGate Escrow, Inc. for (1) breach of contract, (2) negligence, and (3) fraud.

 

On April 7, 2023, Defendant filed a Demurrer with Motion to Strike.  Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on April 18, 2023.  Thus, Defendant took the Demurrer with Motion to Strike off calendar.

Defendant filed an Answer on May 22, 2023.

 

On the same day, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Strike Punitive Damages from First Amended Complaint (“Motion”).  Plaintiff filed an Opposition on August 18, 2023, and Defendant filed a Reply on August 24, 2023.

 

On August 28, 2023, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion to September 20, 2023.  (8-28-23 Minute Order.)

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

According to Code of Civil Procedure § 436:

 

The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:

 

(a) Strikeout any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.

(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.

 

However, motions to strike in limited jurisdiction courts may only challenge pleadings on the basis that “the damages or relief sought are not supported by the allegations of the complaint.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 92(d).)  The Code of Civil Procedure also authorizes the Court to act on its own initiative to strike matters, empowering the Court to enter orders striking matter “at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) ¿ Furthermore, § 435.5 requires that “[b]efore filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5(a).)

 

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.”  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  A request for punitive damages may be made pursuant to Civil Code §¿3294(a) which provides that “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”

 

Under the statute, malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others” and oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(c)(1), (c)(2).)  Although not defined by the statute, despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.  College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)  Also, “[u]nder the statute, malice does not require actual intent to harm…Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences…. [Citation.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)

 

Furthermore, parties cannot recover attorney’s fees unless expressly authorized by a statute or contract.  (Hom v. Petrou (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 459, 464.)

 

When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.”  (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)  Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue.  (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

III.            Discussion:

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

On May 15, 2023, defense counsel left a voicemail message for Plaintiff’s counsel and followed-up with an email in order to meet and confer regarding the Motion to Strike.  (Davis Decl. ¶ 3.)  The following day, Plaintiff’s counsel responded, stating that she disagreed and that all of the elements were adequately alleged.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  Defense counsel states that based on these communications and prior discussions, the attorneys were unable to reach an agreement.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds that defense counsel’s declaration is sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement.

 

B.     Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike portions of the Complaint seeking punitive damages on the basis that the allegations do not support this form of relief.  (Mot. p. 2.)

 

1.     Item 1: Page 6, Paragraph FIR-7: “The aforementioned acts of Defendants are willful, wanton, malicious, intentional, oppressive, and despicable, and were done in willful and conscious disregard of the rights of Plaintiff, and were done by or with the express knowledge, consent, and ratification of managerial agents and employees of Defendants, and thereby justify punitive and exemplary damages to be proven at trial.”

 

2.     Item 2: Page 7, Exemplary Damages Attachment, Ex-1, Ex-2: “The aforementioned acts of Defendants are willful, wanton, malicious, intentional, oppressive, and despicable, and were done in willful and conscious disregard of the rights of Plaintiff, and were done by or with the express knowledge, consent, and ratification of managerial agents and employees of Defendants, and thereby justify punitive and exemplary damages to be proven at trial. Specifically Defendant’s agent Nicole Nichols asked for Plaintiffs forwarding address knowing she would not use it as requested;” and Ex-3.

 

(Ibid.)

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff has “pleaded insufficient facts supporting circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice to justify its request for punitive damages in these circumstances.”  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  According to the First Amended Complaint, the parties entered into an agreement whereby Plaintiff was to buy an apartment complex with Defendant serving as the escrow company.  (Ibid.; See FAC.)  Plaintiff alleges that, according to the agreement, Defendant agreed to set up mail forwarding so that mail addressed to the Property would be forwarded to Plaintiff’s business address.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff has not attached the agreement.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant did not set up mail forwarding, thus, property tax bills were sent to the property and Plaintiff was unaware of these bills.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff was late in paying the bills and accrued about $8,500 in delinquent fees for overdue tax payments.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff sets forth causes of action for breach of contract, negligence, and fraud, and also seeks punitive damages for RidgeGate’s failure to change Plaintiff’s mailing address.  (Ibid.)

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that Defendant’s conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious.  (Ibid.)  The FAC presents conclusory facts and “buzzwords” that do not provide any specific facts demonstrating such conduct.  (Ibid. at pp. 5-7.)  Furthermore, the breach of an escrow holder’s fiduciary duty or negligence do not support an award of punitive damages.  (Ibid. at p. 8.)  Defendant’s duty was in processing the escrow and the only allegation presented in the FAC is regarding Defendant’s failure to change the mailing address.  (Ibid.)  This conduct cannot “rise to the level of ‘despicable’ or ‘willful’ conduct constituting ‘malice’ that might render RidgeGate liable for punitive damages.”  (Ibid.)

 

            In Opposition, Plaintiff argues that “Plaintiff has adequately plead facts supporting circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice sufficient to justify exemplary damages in this case.”  (Oppos. p. 2.)  Plaintiff argues that its cause of action for fraud pleads very specific facts regarding Defendant’s conduct.  (Ibid.)  The FAC states:

On June 16, 2021, Defendant's agent, Nicole Nichols, a branch manager at RIDGEGATE ESCROW emailed Plaintiffs agent, Shaun Setareh, requesting Plaintiffs forwarding address as part of its promise to set up mail forwarding at Property after the completed sale. However, Ms. Nichols had no intention of performing on this promise as she never updated the address despite Plaintiff responding within minutes of the request with the proper address: 9665 Wilshire Blvd. Ste. 430, Beverly Hills, CA 90212.

 

(FAC at p. 6.)  Based on these allegations, “it is reasonable to ‘infer that defendants acted in callous disregard of plaintiffs’ rights, knowing that their conduct was substantially certain to vex, annoy, and injure plaintiffs.’”  (Oppos. p. 2.)  Plaintiff adds that Defendant promised to set up mail forwarding in the written agreement and later, also requested the forwarding address, which Plaintiff provided in minutes.  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  However, “Defendant’s repeated and willful failure to perform its promise warrants the inference that the promisor did not intend to perform as promised.”  (Ibid.)  Defendant’s conduct injured Plaintiff, resulting in the accrual of late penalties.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff argues that the FAC “contains a plethora of facts that are sufficient to show Defendant’s actions and/or omissions were ‘conceived in a spirit of mischief or with criminal indifference towards the obligations owed to others’ meriting punitive damages.”  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  Defendant acted with criminal indifference toward Plaintiff’s tax obligations and willfully misrepresented, on at least two occasions, that it would set up mail forwarding.  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  As an experienced escrow company, Defendant should have known about its obligations.  (Ibid.)  Punitive damages will “serve to deter the Defendant from committing similar misdeeds.”  (Ibid. at p. 5.)

 

            In its Reply, Defendant reiterates that the law does not favor punitive damages and Courts should grant them with caution.  (Reply p. 2.)  Defendant argues that “Plaintiff is mistaken and mischaracterized simple conduct with radical characterizations” to show that Defendant’s failure to set up mail forwarding rises to the level of conduct appropriate for punitive damages.  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  Defendant adds that an escrow holder’s breach of duty cannot alone, without malice, fraud, or oppression, rise to the level of conduct that would merit an award of punitive damages.  (Ibid. at pp. 4-5.)  Defendant repeats that Plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory and use “buzz-words” but do not allege any specific facts regarding Defendant's conduct.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)  Defendant also states that there was no escrow instruction or promise to set up mail forwarding, but even if there was, failure to set up mail forwarding “is not fraud or even the type of conduct that punitive damages would be appropriate.”  (Ibid.)

 

Having reviewed the parties’ arguments, the Court makes the following findings.

 

First, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant’s failure to set up mail forwarding was a breach of Defendant’s promise and the parties’ agreement.  The Court notes that punitive damages cannot be awarded in an action for the breach of an obligation arising out of a contract.  (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)

 

Second, Plaintiff alleges a cause of action for fraud and argues that its request for punitive damages arises from this cause of action.  The Court does not find that Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to show that it is entitled to punitive damages.  The allegations made in its fraud cause of action do not contain any specific facts that demonstrate that any of Defendant’s conduct could possibly demonstrate oppression, fraud, or malice.  Moreover, Plaintiff’s allegations in support of its request for punitive damages are conclusory and merely recite the requirements for a request for punitive damages.

 

The Court also finds that Plaintiff has previously had an opportunity to amend the Complaint.  In its Opposition, Plaintiff has not shown that another amendment could cure the defect in the pleadings.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.

 

V.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant RidgeGate Escrow, Inc.’s Motion to Strike without leave to amend.

 

Moving party to give notice.

Case Number: 23STLC04002    Hearing Date: September 20, 2023    Dept: 25

Mishel Melamed v. Law Offices of Ramin R. Younessi

23stlc04002

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On June 23, 2023, Plaintiff Mishel Melamed (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Law Offices of Ramin R. Younessi (“Defendant”) for breach of contract.

 

On August 21, 2023, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint (“Demurrer”).  Defendant filed a Notice of Non-Opposition on September 13, 2023.  No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is a pleading that may be used to test the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in the complaint.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10.)  There are two types of demurrers – general demurrers and special demurrers.  (See McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 77.)

 

General demurrers can be used to attack pleadings for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e); McKenney, 167 Cal.App.4th at 77.)  Such demurrers can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable; evidence or extrinsic matters are not considered.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 430.30, 430.70; Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the Court admits “all material facts properly pleaded” and “matters which may be judicially noticed,” but does not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].”  (Blank, 39 Cal.3d at 318.)  It gives these facts “a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (Ibid.). At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him.  (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.)  The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint.  (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.)  "If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence."  (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.)

 

Special demurrers can be used to attack the pleadings on grounds that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible, or in a contract case, for failure to allege whether a contract is oral or written.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  However, special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction civil actions and any grounds for special demurrers must be raised as affirmative defenses in the answer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 92(c).)

 

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).)  The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(2).)  Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.”  (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)  Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue.  (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227 ("Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.").)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.  (Dem. p. 2.)  Defendant also demurs to first cause of action on the grounds that it is “uncertain, vague, and ambiguous, which prevents Defendant from reasonably responding.”  (Ibid.)

 

The Court notes that special demurrers, on the basis that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible, are not allowed in limited jurisdiction actions.  Accordingly, the Court will rule on the Demurrer on the basis that the first cause of action fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

A.     Meet and Confer

 

Defendant’s counsel states that he has “made multiple attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff in advance of filing this demurrer.”  (Geshgian Decl. ¶ 4.)  On August 16, 2023, counsel left a voicemail and sent an email to Plaintiff in an attempt to meet and confer.  (Ibid. ¶ 5, Ex. B.)  On August 18, 2023, counsel left another voicemail and sent another email to Plaintiff.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. C.)  Plaintiff did not respond to any of defense counsel’s communications, thus, necessitating the instant Demurrer.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant has presented sufficient evidence that it has attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff.  Thus, the requirement to meet and confer has been satisfied.

 

B.     Demurrer

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.  (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)  “A written contract may be pleaded either by its terms – set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference – or by its legal effect. [Citations.]  In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms.  This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’ [Citation.]”  (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.)

 

The Complaint states the following:

 

Dear Mr. Mrs. Judge.

 

I Mishel Melamed was injured at work I whent saw Mr. Ramin Younessi for worker’s compensation to get help and also to represent me during my case his worker never care any thing about my case he told me you need to see a Dr. and he will decide about your case after see the Dr. only 45 Day Limit the Dr. send in the report again my paper work got Lost in Ramin Younessi’s office I have proof the Dr did sent in the paper work (2) after seeing he do not care about me I told him I do not want to be with his Law firm right away I hired a different attoerny it took Almost 10 month’s to see a state Dr. I believe he oweis me for 10 month’s of my expense which comes to $10,000 – thank you.

 

(Compl.)

 

            Defendant argues that “[t]he complaint is completely devoid of allegations regarding the facts that give rise to any breach of contract by the Defendant” including any allegations regarding the existence of a contract, breach of contract, and causation.  (Dem. pp. 4-5.)  Plaintiff himself states that he has hired a new attorney to represent him in the worker’s compensation case, thus, “any contract that Plaintiff had at the time of any alleged breach was with his new attorneys.”  (Ibid. at p. 5.)  Furthermore, there are no allegations regarding the terms of any agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant or how the $10,000 in damages was caused by this breach.  (Ibid. at pp. 5-6.)

 

            The Court finds that the allegations stated in the Complaint do not contain sufficient facts for any element of a cause of action for breach of contract.  Plaintiff has not alleged the existence of an agreement between the parties, how this agreement was breached, and how it resulted in damages of $10,000.

 

Given that Plaintiff has not had any opportunities to amend the Complaint, the Demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract is sustained with leave to amend.

 

 

 

 

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reason,

 

The Demurrer, filed by Defendant Law Offices of Ramin R. Younessi, is SUSTAINED with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
