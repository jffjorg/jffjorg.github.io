DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV38741    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for New Trial

Moving Party: Defendant Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority 
Responding Party: Plaintiff Juan Andres Espinoza Torres 
Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND

This is a personal injury case arising from Plaintiff’s motorcycle collision with a pickup truck driven by a LACMTA employee. The jury returned a verdict in favor of Plaintiff and awarded $750,000 solely for past and future noneconomic damages. At trial, there was evidence admitted about Plaintiff's medical expenses, but Plaintiff waived economic damages before resting his case. At trial, Defendant intended to present body camera video of the accident scene interview of Jacqueline Newell, but the Court sustained Plaintiff’s objection to playing the video to the jury.

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues the verdict is contrary to the weight of the evidence and indicates juror passion and prejudice — reflected by excessive damages awards and a grossly disproportionate 100% fault allocation against Defendant. (Motion for New Trial, p. 13.) Defendant also asserts Plaintiff's attorney engaged in misconduct by examining witnesses and offering into evidence medical expenses only to waive special damages prior to resting his case. (Id. at 16.) Defendant further argues the Court abused its discretion by sustaining an objection to admitting into evidence the body camera video of witness Jacqueline Newall. (Id. at 17.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues that evidence of Plaintiff’s past injuries and pain and suffering justified the jury’s general damages verdict whereas evidence of the reasonable medical certainty of future arthritis and surgery justified the jury’s verdict of future general damages. Plaintiff further denies any misconduct, and argues that the Court’s exclusion of witness video was not an abuse of discretion. (Opposition, p. 8.)

REPLY

Defendant maintains none of Plaintiff’s injuries support the verdict awarded by the jury, that Plaintiff’s attorney’s trial tactic was designed to prejudice the jury against it, and that Plaintiff’s comparative negligence should have been part of the verdict. (Reply, p. 4.)

ANALYSIS

A motion for new trial is a creature of statute; . . .” (Neal v. Montgomery Elevator Co.¿(1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1194, 1198.) A movant must satisfy Code of Civil Procedure sections 657 and 659. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 657, a motion for new trial may be granted if there is any:¿
[¶] 1. Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury, or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial. [¶] 2. Misconduct of the jury; and whenever any one or more of the jurors have been induced to assent to any general or special verdict, or to a finding on any question submitted to them by the court, by a resort to the determination of chance, such misconduct may be proved by the affidavit of any one of the jurors. [¶] 3. Accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against. [¶] … [¶] 5. Excessive or inadequate damages. [¶] 6. Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law. [¶] 7. Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application.¿¿¿
(Code Civ. Proc., section 657.)¿¿¿

Granting a new trial is not appropriate in this case, as Defendant fails to show an excessive verdict, that liability was improperly determined, or an error by the Court committed error.

Damages

With respect to the damages award, Seffert v. Los Angeles Transit Lines (1961) 45 Cal.2d 498, is instructive here, as the Supreme Court notes that “[t]he amount to be awarded is ‘a matter on which there legitimately may be a wide difference of opinion.’” (Seffert at 508.) Moreover, the Seffert emphasizes that verdicts should not be disturbed “unless it is so grossly disproportionate to any reasonable limit of compensation warranted by the facts that it shocks the court’s sense of justice and raises a presumption that it was the result of passion and prejudice.” (Id.) 

Here, there was credible testimony by Plaintiff and his wife regarding Plaintiff’s past physical pain, mental suffering, physical impairment, inconvenience, and emotional distress.  Plaintiff underwent a surgery and rehabilitation and there was credible testimony that Plaintiff was likely to have a lifetime of physical limitations and would need for future surgical intervention.  Accordingly, there was credible evidence supporting a finding that Plaintiff would suffer future physical pain, mental suffering, physical impairment, inconvenience, and emotional distress.  While the jury’s damages award might be described as generous, the Court does not find that the award is grossly disproportionate to the evidence, nor does it shock the Court’s sense of justice or raise a presumption that the award was a result of passion or prejudice.  Accordingly, the Court finds that there is no basis under Seffert to disturb the jury’s verdict.  

Comparative Fault

Similarly, there was credible evidence to support the finding that Plaintiff was not comparatively at fault.  While Plaintiff testified that he was aware of the dangers of riding a motorcycle in another vehicle’s blind spot, the fact that Plaintiff was briefly in what was described as “the kill zone” alone does not necessarily require a finding of comparative fault.  Defense expert Edward Milich testified regarding the protective gear that he believed was required to be reasonably careful when riding a motorcycle.  However, on cross-examination, he admitted that many riders do not ride with the gear he recommended.  The jury was free to use their common experience and reject Mr. Milich’s opinion regarding the standard of care for motorcycle riders.  

Attorney Misconduct

Here, Plaintiff withdrew his claims for past and future medical expenses after Dr. Babak Samimi testified about Plaintiff’s past medical treatment and his opinions about Plaintiff’s future medical needs.  As noted in the Opposition, parties are free to pursue or dismiss liability and damages theories at any point in the case.  As such, the fact that Plaintiff dismissed his economic damages claims is not, in and of itself, evidence of misconduct.  In addition, because the Court was unexpectedly shut down due to a ransomware attack, Plaintiff’s counsel informed the Court that Plaintiff’s medical billing expert was not available to testify as planned.  While alternative arrangements might have been made, in light of the unexpected scheduling issues, there is evidence to support that Plaintiff’s withdrawal of his economic damages claim was not nefarious.  Further, assuming arguendo that there was any potential irregularity in the proceedings, the Court specifically instructed the jury not to consider any medical expenses in calculating their damages award.  Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that there was no attorney misconduct and that any irregularity in the proceedings was addressed by an appropriate jury instruction.

Exclusion of the Newell Video

The Court additionally finds it did not abuse its discretion by sustaining Plaintiff’s objection and withholding the Newell video interview from entering evidence. Courts have discretion to exclude evidence if it risks confusing or misleading a jury.  California Evidence Code Section 352 empowers courts to exclude evidence if its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will create a substantial danger of undue prejudice, confusing the issues, or misleading the jury (People v. Anderson (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 851.) The balancing of evidence under this statute is undertaken on a sliding scale, where the more substantial the probative value of the evidence, the greater the danger of one of the excluding factors must be to warrant exclusion (People v. Castaneda-Prado (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 1260.). The trial court's discretion in this matter is broad and is reviewed for abuse of discretion (Meeks v. Autozone, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 855.) 

The Court here exercised its discretion to exclude the video because it entailed an unjustifiably high risk of confusing the jury. Specifically, the witness failed to provide clear or perceptible statements of fact for the jury to assess and weigh the credibility of. For instance, she fails to state clearly where she was when she allegedly witnessed the incident, how clearly she saw the incident, where each of the vehicles was prior to the collision, or where the collision occurred.  Further, the witness repeatedly offered her opinions about ultimate fault and the dangers of riding motorcycles.  Finally, the Court found that simply editing out the offending statements was not practical.  The Court thus reasoned her statements were so vague that they lacked probative value, posed a significant risk of confusing the jury and exercised its discretion to exclude the video on that basis. (Exh. E to Plaintiff’s Compendium.) 

RULING

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for a new trial is DENIED.

Demurrer

Moving Party: Defendant Geocomp Consulting, Inc.
Responding Party: Plaintiff Domingo David Tambriz Cuc
Tentative Ruling: Overruled  

BACKGROUND 

This is a personal injury case arising from a May 19, 2020 incident in which Plaintiff alleges that, while riding a bicycle on a public street, a hole in the pavement caused him to fall. (Complaint at ¶ 7, Exhibit “A.”) Plaintiff’s complaint was filed on December 4, 2020, and the amended complaint was filed on May 2, 2024. (Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibits “A” and “B”.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues the complaint was not served with a certificate of merit as required by CCP § 411.35, and even if Plaintiff were to now file and serve a certificate, it would be untimely. (Demurrer, p. 4.)

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff argues the Court must override Defendant’s demurrer because Plaintiff is not required to submit a certificate of merit because this is not a professional negligence action. (Opposition, p. 5.) Plaintiff asserts his claim is for general negligence against Defendant arising from an alleged dangerous condition at the location of the subject accident that Defendant shares responsibility for maintaining — and that Plaintiff’s allegations do not implicate Defendant’s duty of reasonable care exclusive to its status as a licensed engineer nor that Defendant did not meet industry standards of the engineering.

REPLY

Defendant argues that Plaintiff alleges that Defendant’s negligence arises from services pertaining to its vocation and relating to Defendant’s responsibility monitor and maintain the area of the subject incident. (Reply, p. 2.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Judicial Notice 

As an initial matter, the Court takes judicial notice of the following:
• Defendant holds a professional engineering license issued pursuant to Chapter 7 of Division 3 of the Business and Professions Code by the California Board for Professional Engineers, Land Surveyors, and Geologists, License Number 53436, under the name of W. Allen Marr.
• Plaintiff’s Complaint. (“Exhibit “A”.)
• Plaintiff’s Amendment to Complaint (“Exhibit “B”.)
(Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice, p. 2.)

II. Demurrer

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer for sufficiency attacks a pleading on the ground that it does not contain facts sufficient to state a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10 (e); Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  Parties may also demur to causes of action individually.  In either case, the procedure is also called a ‘demurrer for sufficiency,’ and it tests the pleading by itself, alone and disconnected from extraneous matters.  (Hahn at p. 747.) Failing to serve a complaint with a certificate of merit in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure § 411.35 can support a demurrer. According to § 430.10(h), a party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer if no certificate was filed as required by Section 411.35 (West's Ann.Cal.C.C.P. § 430.10.) Section 411.35 (g) explicitly states that the failure to file a certificate in accordance with this section shall be grounds for a demurrer (West's Ann.Cal.C.C.P. § 411.35.) 

Here, Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege professional negligence. Rather, Plaintiff’s complaint alleges that Defendant engaged in ordinary negligence occurred while “acting within the course and scope of their duties”. (Complaint at ¶ 8.) While this broad language could potentially include allegations of professional negligence, it does not necessarily mean that it does.  Similarly, while Defendant Geocomp Consulting does have a professional license, that does not mean that all work it performs is necessarily under the scope of that license.  As such, a fair reading of Plaintiff’s complaint does not compel a finding that this is an action for professional negligence.  While beyond the scope of the Court’s analysis on a demurrer, this finding is consistent with the testimony in the April 26, 2024 deposition of Mr. Waguespack, who testified the allegedly dangerous condition was created by Defendant Geocomp Consulting when it monitoring well and filled the resulting hole.  This work would normally be characterized as ordinary construction work subject to regular negligence rather than a professional negligence standard. The Court thus finds Plaintiff’s complaint alleges a cause of action for ordinary negligence against Defendant and that Plaintiff is not required to file a certificate of merit. 

To the extent that a more fulsome factual record establishes that Plaintiff's allegations do indeed fall under the scope of professional negligence, this ruling is without prejudice to Defendant seeking future relief.   

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

Motion for Summary Judgement

Moving Party: Defendant City of San Fernando
Responding Party: Plaintiff Josefina Martinez
Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND 

This is a trip and fall case in which Plaintiff tripped over a sidewalk uplift owned by Defendant and alleges a dangerous condition of public property. Plaintiff’s government claim alleges that the uneven/raised pavement on the sidewalk created a dangerous condition, that Defendant manages and maintains the area where Plaintiff was injured, that Defendant had actual or constructive knowledge of the condition and failed to take measures to warn or remedy the condition to protect pedestrians. (Defendant’s Appendix of Evidence in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 9.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues it is entitled to summary judgment because the alleged dangerous condition constitutes a trivial defect. Moreover, Defendant argues Plaintiff is precluded from arguing a dangerous condition existed based on poor lighting conditions because Plaintiff failed to make any such allegations in her government claim or complaint. (Reply p. 4.) Defendant alternatively argues it had no actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition, and that the condition was open and obvious and could have been avoided by Plaintiff. (Motion for Summary Judgement p. 8.)

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff argues that evidence shows a triable issue on whether the condition was dangerous or trivial. Plaintiff also argues Defendant had actual and constructive notice of the dangerous condition, and that the condition was not open, obvious, or avoidable.

REPLY

Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to meet the burden of showing any triable issues of material fact and “improperly attempts to dispute her own admissions, or fails to set forth any evidence which actually creates a factual dispute.” (Reply p. 4.) 

ANALYSIS 

I. Evidentiary Objections 

Defendant submits a total of 11 objections to the Declarations of Plaintiff Josefina Martinez, Fernando Gomez, as well as Philip Rosescu and accompanying exhibits.

Sustained: No. 5 (not qualified.)
Overruled: Nos. 1-4, 6-11.

II. Summary Judgement

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.  

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998 (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Civil Code § 1714(a)).) To impose liability on a property owner for a dangerous condition, the owner must have either actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition or have been able to discover the condition by the exercise of ordinary care. (See Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at 1206.) An owner is charged with constructive knowledge of the existence of a dangerous condition if the condition was present for a sufficient period. (See id.) “Whether a dangerous condition has existed long enough for a reasonably prudent person to have discovered it is a question of fact for the jury.” (Id. at 1207.)  To establish premises liability, plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached a duty of care owed to the plaintiff that proximately caused injury and damages. “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  

A. Trivial Defect 

Defendant here fails to meet its initial burden of making a prima facie showing that the condition constituted a trivial defect and not a dangerous condition. “The existence of a dangerous condition is ordinarily a question of fact—resolved here by the jury's express finding that the [sidewalk] was a dangerous condition of [public] property—but it can be decided as a matter of law if reasonable minds can come to only one conclusion.” (Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102, 110.) While Defendant provides several cases  in which courts have found analogous height differences in sidewalks constitute trivial defects, California does not use a tape measure test and considers the totality of the circumstances. (Lechler v. City of San Francisco (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 523.) Thus, Defendant must make a prima facie showing that reasonable minds—considering the totality of the circumstances—could only arrive at one conclusion. 

Here, the Court finds that Defendant’s evidence in Plaintiff’s admissions that nothing blocked her view of the sidewalk uplift, that the weather conditions were clear, and that she walked by the same location three times per week, is not sufficient to show that the condition was open and obvious. (Motion for Summary Judgement, p. 11.) While Defendant provides evidence weighing in favor of a trivial defect, the picture of the condition provides ambiguity as to whether the condition is trivial. Because reasonable minds may consider the picture and disagree as to whether it is a dangerous condition, the Court concludes Defendant here has not made a prima facie showing that the condition is not dangerous.

Nevertheless, Plaintiff provides admissible evidence showing a triable issue exists on whether the condition is dangerous. Namely, Plaintiff provides an expert declaration of Mr. Rosescu, stating that “any vertical displacement in excess of 0.55” can cause a trip to the average pedestrian, and that the height differential in this case was high enough to cause a trip but low enough that it would not be obvious. (Decl. Rosescu ¶ 9-10.) Because Plaintiff provides admissible evidence that the subject lift was measured between 11/16 and 1.5 inches, Plaintiff provides evidence sufficient to potentially find the condition was dangerous and not trivial. 

B. Notice 

Defendant here does meet its burden of making a prima facie showing that it lacked notice of the condition. Specifically, Defendant provides admissible evidence that no reported incidents have occurred in that area within the past ten years, supporting the inference that Defendant did not have notice of the uplifted sidewalk in this case. (Declaration of Public Works Operations Manager Rodrigo Mora.) The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to establish a triable issue exists that Defendant had notice.

Plaintiff has also met her burden to show a triable issue exists on the issue of notice. Constructive notice can be established if the condition existed long enough that it may be inferred a reasonable inspection would have ascertained its existence. (Gentekos v. City and County of San Francisco (1958) 163 Cal.App.2d 691); (see also, (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200) (acknowledging that notice of a dangerous condition may be inferred from a failure to inspect within a reasonable period of time.) Here, Mr. Rosescu’s declaration raises a triable issues of fact over whether Defendant’s inspection system was inadequate and whether it would have discovered the dangerous condition and fixed it prior to the incident if it had employed a reasonable inspection system. (Decl. Rosescu ¶ 6-12). Mr. Rosescu’s declaration provides an evidentiary basis suggesting that the hazard “has existed for multiple years prior to the incident and should have been spotted, reported, and repaired by Defendant employees.” (Id. ¶ 12.) The Court therefore finds Plaintiff met her burden of showing a triable issue exists as to whether Defendant had constructive notice of the defect. 

C. Open and Obvious 

Defendant fails to satisfy its burden of making a prima facie showing that the condition was open and obvious. A city can make a prima facie showing that a condition was open and obvious based on the plaintiff's admissions that the weather was clear and that she had previously walked in the area. The relevant case law supports the notion that a plaintiff's familiarity with the area and the visibility of the defect are significant factors in determining whether a condition is open and obvious. For instance, in Stack v. City of Lemoore, the court noted that the defect in the sidewalk was open and obvious where its existence was common knowledge in the community and known to plaintiff herself, who tripped over the defect in the daytime while walking toward the exposed side of the rise without anything obstructing her vision. (Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102.). The court’s finding suggests that a city may make a prima facie showing that a defect is open and obvious where the plaintiff had prior knowledge and where there are no obstructions to visibility. (Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102.) 

Here, Defendant’s evidence of Plaintiff’s admissions that nothing blocked her view of the sidewalk uplift, that the weather conditions were clear, and that she walked by the same location three times per week, is not sufficient to show that the condition was open and obvious. (Motion for Summary Judgement, p. 11.) Defendant’s evidence, while directly supporting that Plaintiff’s view was not obstructed, fails to make a direct prima facie showing of Plaintiff’s prior awareness of the dangerous condition. Rather, Defendant’s evidence merely suggests the possibility that Plaintiff, by walking in the area, could have had prior awareness of the condition. As a result, reasonable minds may disagree on the open and obvious nature of the alleged dangerous condition.

Even assuming Defendant met its burden to make a prima facie showing, Plaintiff nevertheless shows a triable issue exists. Plaintiff does so through admissible evidence in Plaintiff’s expert declaration, wherein Mr. Rosescu asserts that the uplift could be difficult to perceive for typical pedestrians. (Rosescu Decl., ¶ 10.) Even disregarding Plaintiff’s evidence that the area was dark, the Rosescu declaration nevertheless establishes a sufficient evidentiary basis to raise a triable issue of fact as to whether the defect was open and obvious. (Id.)

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for summary judgement is DENIED.

ANALYSIS

The court declines to take judicial notice of these items because they are not material to the disposition of this motion. St. Croix v. Superior Court (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 434, 447. 

B. Motion to Strike

A court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter. Under CCP § 435, “[a]ny party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” Under CCP § 436(a), “[t]he court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper . . . [s]trike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” Under CCP § 436(b), the court may “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” 

A motion to strike is also properly directed to unauthorized claims for damages, meaning damages which are not allowable as a matter of law. See, e.g. Commodore Home Systems, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (1982) 32 Cal.3d 211, 214 (motion to strike lies against request for punitive damages when the claim sued upon would not support an award of punitive damages as a matter of law). A complaint requesting punitive damages must include “specific factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for punitive damages.” Today's IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193. Punitive damages may be stricken where the facts alleged do not rise to the level of “malice, fraud or oppression” required to support a punitive damages award. See, e.g. Turman v. Turning Point of Central Calif., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.

Civil Code § 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in noncontract cases “where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice…” “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights. “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. Civ. Code § 3294. A conscious disregard for the safety of others may constitute malice. G. D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 28 (“Searle”).

“When nondeliberate injury is charged, allegations that the defendant's conduct was wrongful, willful, wanton, reckless or unlawful do not support a claim for exemplary damages; such allegations do not charge malice.” Id. at 29. “The central spirit of the exemplary damage statute, the demand for evil motive, is violated by an award founded upon recklessness alone.” Id. at 32. “Conscious disregard of safety as an appropriate description of the Animus malus which may justify an exemplary damage award when nondeliberate injury is alleged.” Id. “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–896 (“Taylor”); see, e.g., Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89-90 (“Dawes”) (merely pleading that a defendant was intoxicated at the time of the accident was insufficient to show a conscious disregard for safety of others); cf. Sumpter v. Matteson (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 928, 936 (factual findings that defendant was driving under the influence and knowing the light was red as the defendant sped toward it justified the award of punitive damages.)

“There are, however, few situations in which claims for punitive damages are predicated on ... conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others and in which no intentional torts are alleged.” Central Pathology Service Medical Clinic, Inc. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 181, 191. “‘(M)alice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs' interests. The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.” College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725. Despicable conduct is conduct “so vile, base miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down on by ordinary decent people.” Scott v. Phoenix Schools, Inc. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 702, 715 (internal quotations omitted). Despicable conduct has been described as eliciting the same type of outrage frequently associated with the commission of crimes. Id.

Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard” of others, even understood in the light most favorable to Plaintiff’s case, fail to rise to the level of “despicable and willful” conduct. This is insufficient due to the language of Civ. Code § 3294, as amended in 1987. The court in Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, explained:

The definition of malice has not always included the requirement of willful and despicable conduct. Prior to 1980, section 3294 did not define malice. It was construed to mean malice in fact, which could be proven directly or by implication (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894 (Taylor); 6 Witkin, Summary of Cal. Law (9th ed. 1988) Torts, § 1335, p. 793) and could be established by conduct that was done only with “a conscious disregard of the safety of others....” (Taylor, supra, at 895)  Relying on the reasoning in G.D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, the Taylor court recognized that recklessness alone is insufficient to sustain an award of punitive damages because “ ‘[t]he central spirit of the exemplary damage statute, the demand for evil motive, is violated by an award founded upon recklessness alone.’”  Id.  The court concluded that “[i]n order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Id. at 895–896) Applying that test, the Supreme Court directed the trial court to reinstate a claim for punitive damages where it was alleged the defendant was operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated, under circumstances which disclosed a conscious disregard of the probable dangerous consequences.

In 1980, the Legislature amended section 3294 by adding the definition of malice stated in Taylor.  (Stats.1980, ch. 1242, § 1, pp. 4217–4218; College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713) The definition was amended in 1987. As amended, malice, based upon a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights, requires proof that the defendant's conduct is “despicable” and “willful.” (Stats.1987, ch. 1498, § 5.) The statute's reference to “despicable conduct” represents “a new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.” (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at 725)  Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210–1211.

In order to allege despicable conduct, Plaintiff must allege conduct which elicits the same type of outrage as the commission of crimes. Scott v. Phoenix Schools, Inc. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 702, 715. Even before the amendment of Civil Code § 3294, courts have elucidated that “(t)he central spirit of the exemplary damage statute, the demand for evil motive, is violated by an award founded upon recklessness alone.” G. D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 32.

The facts alleged by Plaintiff here clearly allege that Defendant was careless, even reckless. However, recklessness is insufficient to support a punitive damages claim.  Plaintiff must be able to allege ultimate facts that evidence “despicable” conduct “with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” Civ. Code § 3294. Even where actions were actually criminal, courts have found that the pleading must contain additional facts sufficient to show they meet the requirements of the punitive damages statute. See Taylor, supra, 24 Cal.3d at 893 (punitive damages was adequately plead where defendant repeatedly drove under the influence, had convictions for drunk driving, and had previously caused accidents while intoxicated); see also Dawes, supra, 111 Cal.App.3d at 90 (punitive damages was adequately plead where defendant drove while intoxicated, speeding into a crowded intersection at 65 mph in a 35 mph zone, and attempted to mislead officers as to whether or not he was driving).

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED with leave to amend.
