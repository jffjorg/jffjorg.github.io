DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV00328    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: P

Motion for Summary Judgment

Moving Party: Defendant Ronald Peak

Responding Party: None

RULING

Having considered the moving papers, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On April 29, 2022, Plaintiff Bennett Georgen (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Ronald Peak (“Defendant”) and all other persons claiming any interest in the Property, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Written note; (2) Quiet Title; and (3) Declaratory Relief.

On December 19, 2022, Defendant filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff.

On September 24, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication. As of December 6, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On May 30, 2000, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a Purchase Agreement for the purchase and sale of a property located at 404 S. Juanita Ave., Redondo Beach, CA 90277 (the “Property”). (UMF No. 1.) The Purchase Agreement required Defendant to make monthly installment payments. (Complaint, ¶ 10.)

On June 1, 2021, Defendant failed to make the required monthly payment. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) As of November 2023, Defendant owed Plaintiff $1,177,885.00. (UMF No. 2.)

On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff obtained a $1,169,000.00 loan secured by a Deed of Trust recorded against the Property. (UMF No. 3.)

On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a Residential Purchase Agreement for Defendant to purchase the Property for $1,285,000.00. (UMF No. 4.) As a condition to close escrow on August 7, 2024, Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the pending case against Defendant. (UMF No. 5.)

Escrow closed on August 14, 2024, and Plaintiff was paid in full. (UMF No. 6.) A deed transferring the Property to Defendant was recorded. (UMF No. 6.)

Defendant has asked Plaintiff to dismiss the case, pursuant to their agreement, but Plaintiff has refused. (UMF No. 7.)

LEGAL STANDARD

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) A moving defendant need not conclusively negate an element of plaintiff’s cause of action. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.) It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence. (Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Argument

Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the case in full as a condition of the sale of property to Defendant. Plaintiff has been paid in full, and thus the case should have been dismissed.

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant seeks judicial notice of two documents, a Deed of Trust recorded on February 5, 2024 and a Grant Deed transferring the Property from Plaintiff to Defendant. Both documents are recorded with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office.

The court may take notice of the official acts of any legislative, executive, or judicial body in any state, pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (c).

Accordingly, Defendant’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

Merits of the Motion

Defendant argues that there is no dispute of genuine material fact as to any cause of action because Defendant has paid Plaintiff in full for the purchase of the Property. Further, Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the instant case as a condition of escrow, which closed on August 14, 2023.

In support, Defendant offers his own declaration, the declaration of Nickie Bento (“Bento”), a Deed of Trust, and a Grant Deed. Bento declares that he was the escrow officer in the transaction whereby Plaintiff sold the Property to Defendant. (Bento Decl., ¶ 2.) As part of escrow, Plaintiff was required to submit a letter stating that he would dismiss the lawsuit against Defendant upon the close of the transaction. (Bento Decl., Exh. 3.)

Defendant declares that he paid the agreed upon purchase price in full on August 14, 2024, and the deed transferring the Property form Plaintiff to Defendant was recorded on the same day. (Peak Decl., ¶ 6; RJN, Exh. B.) Defendant also provides the Final Closing Statement to support his contention that Plaintiff was paid in full. (Peak Decl., Exh. 4.)

Defense counsel also provides Plaintiff’s deposition responses as Exhs. 5-6 of her declaration. Such documents show that Defendant, as of November 2023, owed Plaintiff $1,177,885.00. (Kendall Decl., Exh. 6.) On August 14, 2024, Defendant paid $1,285,000.00 to cover the balance and obtain title to the Property. (Peak Decl., Exh. 2.)

Thus, Defendant has met his burden of showing that there is no triable issue of material fact, as he has paid Plaintiff in full, Plaintiff transferred the Property to Defendant, and Plaintiff was obligated to dismiss the case as a condition of escrow. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to show that there is a triable issue of material fact.

As Plaintiff has not filed an opposition, he does not meet his burden. In addition, the court exercises its discretion under Code Civ. Proc. section 437c, subd. (b)(3) to grant Defendant’s motion for Plaintiff’s failure to oppose it.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS the motion for summary judgment.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 23TRCV00947    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: P

Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration

Moving Parties: Defendants South Park Doggie, Inc.; Amir Nayebdadash; and Danielle

Nayebdadash

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Lauren and Armando Hogan

RULING

The court considered the moving papers and opposition. Plaintiffs’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On March 29, 2023, Plaintiffs Lauren Hogan (“Lauren”) and Armando Hogan (“Armando”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint. On September 27, 2023, Defendant South Park Doggie, Inc. (“SPD”) filed a demurrer to the complaint.

On June 7, 2024, Defendants Amir Nayebdadash and Danielle Nayebdadash (collectively, “Nayebdadash Defendants”) filed a demurrer to the complaint and motion to strike.

On July 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an amended complaint.

On September 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) against SPD; Nayebdadash Defendants; Doggieworld Enterprises; Aimee Varguez; Elizabeth Varguez; and DOES 2-20, inclusive, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Negligence; (3) Trespass to Chattel; (4) Conversion; and (5) Strict Liability – Dangerous Animal.

On September 24, 2024, Nayebdadash Defendants and SPD filed a demurrer and motion to strike. On October 11, 2024, Defendant SPD filed a notice of non-opposition to the demurrer. The court sustained SPD’s demurrer and granted Nayebdadash Defendants’ motion to strike without leave to amend.

On November 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion. On November 25, 2024, Nayebdadash Defendants and SPD filed oppositions to the motion.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs allege that Moving Defendants are the owners of two vicious dogs, which they left in the control of SPD. (SAC, ¶ 5.) SPD placed Plaintiffs’ dog, Skye, in the same room as Moving Defendants’ dogs without supervision. (SAC, ¶ 13.)

On August 9, 2022, Moving Defendants’ dogs attacked and killed Skye. (SAC, ¶ 14.) Plaintiffs allege that Moving Defendants knew of their dogs’ propensity for violence and trained them to be vicious and unruly. (SAC, ¶¶ 55-56.)

LEGAL STANDARD

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., §1008, subd. (a).)

A court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon “new or different facts, circumstances or law.” (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal. App. 4th 1494, 1499.) There is a strict requirement of diligence, meaning the moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence, different facts, or law earlier.  (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 674, 690.)  A motion for reconsideration is properly denied when it is based on evidence that could have been presented in connection with the original motion. (Morris v. AGFA Corp. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1460; Hennigan v. White (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 395, 406.)  

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Argument

Plaintiffs make this motion on the grounds that the court abused its discretion in sustaining the demurrer and motion to strike without leave to amend. Further, Plaintiffs have new facts showing that SPD was aware of the dogs’ vicious propensities. Plaintiffs also argue that the court misapplied the legal standard under Civ. Code section 3340.

Opposing Party’s Argument

Nayebdadash Defendants argue that a “misinterpretation” of law is not a proper basis for a motion for reconsideration. They also argue that Plaintiffs have not presented new facts pertaining to them because they are not parties to the agreement.

SPD contends that the Plaintiffs offer no evidence of the alleged temperament tests SPD conducted, nor did Plaintiffs’ counsel explain why these facts were not discovered earlier. Further, SPD was dismissed from the case, and the court no longer has jurisdiction.

Merits of the Motion

A. New Law

An argument that the court misinterpreted California law and therefore the moving party is relying on different law “is utterly specious. What [the moving party] essentially argues is that section 1008 does not apply when the litigant disagrees with the trial court’s ruling.” (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.) Section 1008 is “the clear legislative intent to restrict motions to reconsider to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or authority that was not previously considered by it.” (Ibid.) “The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-13.)

Here, Plaintiffs ask the court to reconsider its ruling because it misapplied Civ. Code section 3340. Plaintiffs argue that the statute specifically provides for exemplary damages based on gross negligence, which Plaintiffs adequately pleaded.

Plaintiffs offer no other case law or statute to support their motion. Rather, Plaintiffs attempt to relitigate the sufficiency of their pleading. The court previously assessed Plaintiffs’ pleading and found that Plaintiffs continued to rely on mere conclusions without stating supporting facts and that Plaintiffs did not assert that any other facts were available. In so doing, the court considered Section 3340.

Accordingly, the court finds that Plaintiffs have not offered any new law which would warrant reconsideration.

B. New Facts

When relying on new facts, the moving party must provide a “satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Shiffer v. CBS Corp. (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 246, 255.)

Plaintiffs introduce Exhibit 1, a “Service Agreement” to support their contention that there are new facts showing that SPD had actual knowledge of the Basenjis’ temperament. Plaintiffs claim that the Service Agreement requires all dogs to undergo a temperament test prior to admission to SPD’s facilities. The Service Agreement contains no such provision. At best, it requires dog owners to complete a “guest profile” for their pets and warns pet owners that their pets may not pose a danger to other guests or SPD staff. There is simply no mention of a temperament assessment.

Further, Plaintiffs’ counsel does not explain why, in the exercise of reasonable diligence, he could not have produced this document sooner.

Accordingly, the court finds that Plaintiffs have not offered new facts that would warrant reconsideration.

C. Judgment has been entered as to South Park Doggie

While the court has denied Plaintiffs’ motion to reconsider on its merits, the court also finds that it is without power to reconsider its ruling as to SPD. That entity was dismissed by the order dated October 22, 2024. Once the dismissal was entered, the court is without jurisdiction to reconsider the demurrer. (Passavanti v. Williams (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1602, 1606.)

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion is DENIED in its entirety.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.
