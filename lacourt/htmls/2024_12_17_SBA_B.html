Hearing Date:                          December 17, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff SMBD Investments, LP

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Fix Attorneys’ Fees

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The Court awards $11,093.19 in attorney’s fees in favor of plaintiff and against defendants.

BACKGROUND

On May 30, 2023, plaintiff SMBD Investments, LP filed a complaint against Coco’s Restaurants, LLC, Shari’s Management Corporation, and Fri-M, LLC for breach of lease and account stated.

On August 7, 2023, defendants filed an answer.

On June 18, 2024, the Court granted defense counsel’s motions to be relieved as counsel.

On July 11, 2024, plaintiff filed a notice of settlement.

On August 15, 2024, plaintiff filed an amendment designating Shari’s CP, LLC as Doe 1.

On August 21, 2024, judgment pursuant to stipulation was entered.

On October 22, 2024, plaintiffs filed a memorandum of costs for $3,906.81.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §1032(b) states:  “Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.”

CCP §1033.5(a)(10)(B)-(C) provides that attorney’s fees are recoverable as costs when authorized by “statute” or “law.”

Civil Code §1717 states:  “(a) In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce the provisions of that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the prevailing party, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees.”

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff SMBD Investments, LP requests attorney’s fees in the amount of $11,093.19, if the Court grants plaintiff’s memorandum of costs in the amount of $3,906.81, so that the total costs and fees not exceed $15,000.

The Court entered judgment pursuant to stipulation on August 21, 2024 in the amount of $151,105.80 plus attorney’s fees.  It orders that attorneys’ fees may be sought pursuant to paragraph 6 of the Stipulation and Rule of Court 3.1702.  Paragraph 6 states, “If Plaintiff elects to have a money judgment entered in this action, the judgment shall be in the amount of: 1) $151,108.50, less any payments previously received by plaintiff from Defendants; and 2) reasonable attorney’s fees and costs incurred by plaintiff in this matter, not to exceed $15,000.00.”

Plaintiff argues that it is the prevailing party and that it is entitled to attorney’s fees and costs not to exceed $15,000, pursuant to the stipulation and judgment.  Plaintiff further contends that the amount requested is reasonable.  See Richard P. Keavney decl., and Exhibit 2 showing statement of account totaling $11,325 ($300/hr. x 27.75 hrs.).

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that as stated in the judgment, plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees.  The Court awards $11,093.19 in attorney’s fees.

 

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.

The Court awards $11,093.19 in attorney’s fees in favor of plaintiff and against defendants.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

BACKGROUND

On February 2, 2024, plaintiff Ryan Sierra filed a complaint against Hector Toledo and Tricom Networks, Inc. for negligence.  Plaintiff alleges that on February 2, 2022, he was riding his bicycle, westbound on El Segundo Blvd. at or near its intersection with Yukon Avenue, in Hawthorne when defendants’ vehicle collided with plaintiff.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.

Request for Production of Documents

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of “good cause” is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487.

Request for Admissions

Pursuant to CCP § 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).  “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions.  Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.”  Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶ 8:1370, citing CCP § 2033.280(b).  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  CCP § 2033.280(c).

DISCUSSION

Defendants Hector Toledo and Tricom Networks, Inc. request that the Court compel plaintiff Ryan Sierra to respond without objections to defendants’ initial form and special interrogatories and demand for production of documents and to deem admitted the requests for admissions.

Defendants assert that they served the discovery requests on plaintiff on May 13, 2024.  Responses were due by June 14, 2024.  On June 17, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel requesting responses.  Plaintiff’s counsel requested an extension to July 8, which defense counsel granted.  Having not received any responses, on July 22, August 2, August 14, and September 3, defense counsel followed up with plaintiff’s counsel regarding plaintiff’s outstanding discovery responses, to no avail.  On October 25, defense counsel informed plaintiff’s counsel that if plaintiff’s discovery responses were not received by October 31, defendants would file motions to compel responses and request sanctions.  On November 5, defense counsel called plaintiff’s counsel, who stated that plaintiff’s responses would be served on November 6.  Still having received no discovery responses, on November 11, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel “in a final attempt to avoid having to file any motions,” requesting responses by November 12.  On November 12, plaintiff’s counsel responded that responses would be provided on November 13.  As of the filing date of the motions (November 18), defense counsel had not received responses.

There is no opposition.

The Court finds that defendants properly served discovery requests and plaintiff failed to respond.

The motions are GRANTED.

                        Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Defendants request sanctions in the amount of $2040 ($200/hr.) against plaintiff and plaintiff’s attorney of record, Michael V. Jehdian, Esq. of the Law Offices of Michael V. Jehdian, APC in total for the four motions.  The Court finds that $1040 ($200/hr. x 4 hrs., $240 filing fees) is reasonable and warranted against plaintiff and his attorney of record, Michael V. Jehdian, Esq., in total for the four motions.
