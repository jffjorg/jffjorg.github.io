DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV15295    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: T

MOTION FOR ORDER GRANTING RELIEF FROM POTENTIAL WAIVER OF TRIAL BY JURY

Moving Party: Plaintiff Rafael Moreno
Responding Party: N/A

Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

This is a personal injury case arising from a car accident. Plaintiff alleges he was injured on April 30, 2019, when defendant driver, Ryan M. Thomas, failed to stop in time for traffic on the highway and caused a chain-reaction collision, impacting the rear-end of Plaintiff’s vehicle. 

Shortly prior to the filing of this Motion, Plaintiff’s counsel mistakenly believed both a demand for a jury trial and the payment of jury fees had been timely completed after the initial complaint was filed on April 22, 2021; Plaintiff’s counsel became aware in late July that Plaintiff inadvertently failed to timely demand a jury trial and to timely post jury fees in this case. The Complaint was filed by Plaintiff’s co-counsel, who preceded the Notice of Association of Counsel filed by Plaintiff’s counsel at Dordick Law on July 13, 2022. Plaintiff paid his jury fees and served his Notice of Posting Jury Fees for the above-entitled matter on October 16, 2023. Plaintiff and Defendants have made a jury demand, and both Plaintiff and Defendants fulfilled their jury fee obligations. Trial is scheduled for December 2, 2024.

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Plaintiff argues the Court should grant his motion because he has always sought to have this matter heard before a jury and fulfilled the technical and core objective of the statute. Plaintiff also argues he would suffer prejudice if the motion is not granted, whereas Defendants would not suffer any prejudice if the motion is granted.

OPPOSITION

No opposition has been filed.

ANALYSIS

CCP section 631 provides that “[a]t least one party demanding a jury on each side of a civil case shall pay a nonrefundable fee of one hundred fifty dollars ($150), unless the fee has been paid by another party on the same side of the case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 631(b).) The fee must be paid before the date scheduled for the initial case management conference in the action. (Id., § 631(c).) If no case management conference is scheduled in a civil action, the fee shall be due no later than 365 calendar days after the filing of the initial complaint. (Id., § 631(c)(2).) A party waives jury trial by failing to timely pay the fee, unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee. (Id., § 631(f)(5).) 

“The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.” (Id., § 631(g).) “A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when [] relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice¿to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver.”¿ (Tesoro del Valle Master Homeowners Assn. v. Griffin¿(2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 619, 638.) Policy favors the court using its discretion to proceed with a jury trial. (Bishop v. Anderson¿(1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 821, 823.)¿ 
“Where the right to jury is threatened, the crucial focus is whether any prejudice will be suffered by any party or the court if¿a motion for relief from waiver is granted. [Citations.] A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when ‘…relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver. [Citations.]’” (Wharton v. Superior Court¿(1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 100, 104.) 

Here, while Plaintiff inadvertently failed to timely demand a jury trial and to timely post jury fees, Plaintiff – and indeed all other parties to this case – have consistently reflected a shared understanding that a jury trial is to be expected, supported in their payments of jury fees and jury demands. Given that Defendants have not filed an opposition, the Court has no reason to conclude that they would prejudiced by granting Plaintiff’s motion. 

RULING

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for order granting relief from potential jury waiver is GRANTED.

Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND 

This is a premises liability negligence case. Plaintiff alleges she was injured on February 23, 2021 at the Los Angeles International Airport (LAX) by a malfunctioning or ill-maintained elevator. Plaintiff filed her complaint on February 16, 2022 using a Judicially Approved Form complaint, which includes two causes of actions, the first for General Negligence second for Premises Liability. The City of Los Angeles and Does 1 to 50 were the original defendants, but on February 5, 2024, Defendant was substituted for Doe 2.

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s complaint is devoid of factual allegations regarding how the elevator caused Plaintiff’s alleged injury and further fails to allege facts that would establish Defendant’s culpability. 

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues the complaint alleges sufficient facts to put Defendant on notice of the issues and does not negate Defendant’s control over the elevator; however, if the Court grants Defendant’s motion, Plaintiff argues it should do so with leave to amend.


REPLY

No reply has been filed.

ANALYSIS 

I. Judicial Notice 

The Court takes notice of Plaintiff’s complaint filed on February 16, 2022 in Breeanna Daoud v. Los Angeles World Airports (“LAWA”), et al., case number 22STCV05878. The Court additionally takes notice of Plaintiff’s amendment to the complaint filed on February 5, 2024.

II. Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings

It is well established in California that either prior to trial, but after the time to answer or demur has passed, or at the trial, the plaintiff or the defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings and that the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense.  (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791; See also Sofias v. Bank of America (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586 [The non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings can be made at any time, even during trial, since the grounds for a general demurrer are never waived.], see also Code Civ. Proc. §438(f).)   

A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. (See, e.g., Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 1998) §§ 7:275, 7:322; Lance Camper Manufacturing Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.) Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Id.; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.)  Both a demurrer and a motion for judgment on the pleadings accept as true all material factual allegations of the challenged pleading, unless contrary to law or to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.  (Mechanical Contractors Assn. v. Greater Bay Area Assn. (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 672, 677; Edwards v. Centex Real Estate Corp, (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 15, 27.) 

A defendant may bring a motion for judgment on the pleadings if the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time to demur to the complaint has expired.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(f).)  
“Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a).) A declaration regarding meet and confer efforts must be filed with the motion for judgment on the pleadings. (See id., § 439(a)(3).)  However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to grant or deny the motion for judgment on the pleadings. (See id., § 439(a)(4).)  The Court notes that Defendant has met the meet and confer requirement, and evaluates Defendant’s motion on the merits.

Here, Plaintiff’s complaint alleges Defendant “owed a duty of care to Plaintiff” and “owned, leased, rented, occupied, possessed, designed, constructed, developed, landscaped, operated, inspected, repaired, maintained, modified, managed, controlled, and/or supervised, the subject premises, and permitted or created the dangerous condition.” (Declaration of Jade S. Koller in Support of Motion for Judgment on The Pleadings, p. 7.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant “knew of the dangerous condition” and failed to address it, and moreover “breached the above mentioned duty of care,” through the negligent control over premises, causing Plaintiff’s injury. The Court therefore finds Plaintiff’s pleadings are sufficient to make out a cognizable negligence action. 

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion is DENIED.
