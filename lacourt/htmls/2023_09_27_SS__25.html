Case Number: 20STLC04034    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 25

Aspire General Insurance Company. v. Julian Evelio Cabreza Juarez 

20STLC04034

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On May 12, 2020, Plaintiff Aspire General Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Julian Evelio Cabreza Juarez (“Defendant”) seeking damages of $21,264.06.

 

On November 3, 2020, the Court granted Plaintiff’s default judgment against Defendant in the amount of $22,252.16.

On March 1, 2021, the case settled and the parties entered into a settlement agreement pursuant to a Stipulation for Entry of Judgment (“Stipulation”). On January 9, 2023, the Court dismissed the matter without prejudice but retaining jurisdiction pursuant to CCP Section 664.6.

 

On June 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation to Vacate Default Judgment, requesting the Court to vacate the dismissal entered on January 9, 2023 and enforce the terms of the settlement agreement and enter judgment against Defendant in the amount of $18,200.48. On that same day, the Court vacated the default judgment and scheduled an Order to Show Cause Re: Defendant’s Filing of An Answer for October 13, 2022. Defendant filed its Answer on October 12, 2022.

 

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate the Dismissal, Enforce Settlement Agreement, and Enter Judgment (the “Motion”) on July 10 2023. No Opposition is filed.

 

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

            Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6: 

 
(a)¿If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside¿of¿the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following: 

 

(1) The party. 

(2) An attorney who represents the party. 

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf.” 

 

III.            Discussion

 

The Stipulation filed by Plaintiff was signed by Defedant on March 1, 2021 and by Plaintiff’s authorized representative, by Plaintiff’s counsel on March 1, 2021. (03/01/2021 Stipulation.) It provides that the matter would be settled for $21,264.06, $5,865.57 of which would be paid on or before March 20, 2021 and payments of $100.00 per month would be due on or before the 20th of each month from April 20, 2021 to September 20, 2023. (Id.) Failure to make a timely payment may cause Plaintiff to file this Stipulation along with a Declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel, reciting such default in payment and setting forth the full amount then owing plus an additional costs incurred by Plaintiff. (Id.) Default is deemed to occur if a payment is not received within 7 days of its due date. (Id.) In the event of default, Plaintiff is under no obligation to notify Defendant of its intent to file default. (Id.)

Case Number: 21STLC01026    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 25

NY Properties, LLC, v. Justine Emma Harari, et al.

21stlc01026

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On February 5, 2021 Plaintiff NY Properties, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Justine Emma Harari (“Defendant Harari”), John Howard McFadden (“Defendant McFadden”) (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 10, seeking damages of $19,200.00.

 

On February 28, 2023, counsel represented to the Court that a settlement has been reached and a stipulated settlement would be filed shortly. (02/28/23 Minute Order.) On March 22, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel represented that additional time was needed to complete the settlement. (03/22/2023 Minute Order.) On May 24, 2023, counsel indicated that the settlement was still pending as Defendant Harari was deceased. (05/24/23 Minute Order.)  No Stipulation has been filed with the Court.

 

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement on July 24, 2023. Defendant filed an Opposition to the Motion to Enforce Settlement on August 28, 2023. Plaintiff filed a Reply to the Opposition to the Motion to Enforce Settlement on September 1, 2023.

 

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6: 

 
(a)¿If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside¿of¿the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following: 

 

(1) The party. 

(2) An attorney who represents the party. 

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf.”   

 

III.            Discussion

 

Plaintiff seeks a court order entering judgment against Defendant McFadden pursuant to CCP section 664.6.

 

This matter has not been dismissed and is still pending. As such, the Court has jurisdiction to consider the motion.

 

Plaintiff has provided a copy of the settlement agreement between the parties. (Motion, Ex. 1.) The settlement agreement provides that Defendants would pay $14,000.00, of which one thousand dollars was to be paid on March 1, 2023 and the remaining monthly payments of one thousand dollars for thirteen additional months. (Motion at p. 3, Ex. 1). To date, Defendants have not signed the agreement. (Ex. 1.) Plaintiff asserts that Defendant Harari has died and Defendant McFadden remains liable for the debt pursuant to the written lease between the parties. Plaintiff argues that Defendants performed their initial payment under the terms of the agreement and appeared by telephone in court and agreed to the settlement.

 

                        In opposition, Defendant McFadden asserts that he did not sign the Settlement Agreement and was not a party to the settlement agreement and the alleged settlement agreement was not enforceable. Furthermore, Defendant Harari died prior to signing the settlement agreement.

 

            Plaintiff presents evidence that Defendant Harari made the first payment of $1,000.00 to Plaintiff under the settlement agreement (Reply, Fine Decl. ¶6, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff also asserts evidence that Defendant Harari appeared in person and Defendant McFadden appeared by telephone and orally stipulated to the settlement agreement before the court. (Ex. 1, 5.)

 

            The Supreme Court has held that “[i]f parties to pending litigation stipulate in writing… or orally on the record before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” (In re Marriage of Assemi (1994) 7 Cal.4th 896, 900.) Under the Assemi two-part test, oral stipulations are considered before the court if: “1) the court officer was empowered to act with an adjudicatory function, and 2) the court officer did, in fact, act in that capacity.” (Murphy v. Padilla (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 707, 712) (citing Assemi, 7 Cal.4th at p. 909.)

 

Here, Counsel represented to the court that a settlement has been reached and a stipulated settlement was to be filed shortly before the court. (02/28/23 Minute Order.) Although Defendant Harari made the first payment on March 2, 2023, Defendant McFadden did not make a payment to Plaintiff. (Reply, Ex. 1.) Furthermore, at the March 22, 2023 hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel needed additional time to complete the settlement between the parties. (03/22/2023 Minute Order.) Furthermore, Plaintiff’s counsel indicated again to the court that the settlement was still pending since Defendant Harari was deceased. (05/24/23 Minute Order.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s argument that Defendants orally stipulated to the settlement agreement before the court is mischaracterized. In Assemi, the parties informed Judge Meyers that they affirmatively agreed with the terms of the settlement agreement. (Assemi, 7 Cal.4th at p. 902.) In this case, the parties did not inform Judge Chilton that they affirmatively agreed to the terms of the settlement. The parties needed additional time to finalize settlement because the stipulated settlement was not signed by any of the Defendants. (Fine Decl., Ex. 1). The Court cannot enforce this settlement, as it is not signed by all parties to the settlement. Therefore, the Court concludes this settlement is not enforceable, as it was not signed by both parties.

 

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement under California Code of Civil Procedure 664.6 is DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 21STLC07451    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 25

Ramirez v. Espinoza

21stlc07451

BACKGROUND 

 

            On October 13, 2021, Plaintiff Ruben Sandoval Ramirez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Jose Luis Espinoza and Myriam Espinoza (“Defendants”) for a negligence cause of action related to a motor vehicle accident. 

 

            On October 13, 2021, the court set a date of April 12, 2023, at 8:30am for a non-Jury Trial. 

           

On April 12, 2023, Plaintiff failed to show up to the hearing.

 

On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to set aside/vacate dismissal.

 

Grounds for Motion 

 

Plaintiff moves to vacate the dismissal under CCP § 473(b) on the grounds that the action was dismissed as a result of its attorney’s mistake and inadvertence. 

 

LEGAL STANDARD 

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).)  

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment or a dismissal, but that discretion can be exercised only if the defendant establishes a proper ground for relief, by the proper procedure and within the set time limits. Pursuant to CCP section 473(b), a motion to set aside/vacate cannot be brought more than 6 months after the entry of default and must be made within a “reasonable time.” 

 

CCP section 473(b) provides that when an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of dismissal and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, the court shall set aside a dismissal entered against the attorney’s client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) “The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney's affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties.” 

 

Where an “attorney¿affidavit of fault” is filed, there is no requirement that the¿attorney's¿mistake, inadvertence, or neglect be excusable. Relief¿must¿be granted even where the default or dismissal resulted from¿inexcusable¿neglect by defendant's¿attorney. (Robert E. Weil, et al., California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial ¶ 5:495 (2018).) 

 

DISCUSSION 

 

Plaintiff moves to vacate the dismissal under CCP § 473(b) on the grounds that the action was dismissed as a result of its attorney’s mistake and inadvertence.  Plaintiff’s counsel admits that the failure to appear at the April 12, 2023, was a mistake that was” [his] fault and only [his] fault as [he] is in charge of all files.” (Declaration of James M. Hodges, ¶ 7.) Counsel attests that his only staff member of over 40 years had been hospitalized for multiple medical issues involving Lymphoma, liver problems, hemoglobin problems, and COVID-19, which resulted in her passing on August 11, 2022.  (Id., ¶ 6.)  Counsel states that the grief from his staff member’s passing, the illness of his wife, and problems receiving mail at his prior office all lead to the mis-calendaring of the Trial Date. (Id., ¶¶ 4,5,6,7.)  Counsel declares this statement to be true under the penalty of perjury in the State of California.  (Id., at pg. 7.)  Further, as required by statute, the motion was within the 6 months of the order.  Therefore, Counsel attests that the order was due to his personal mistake and has filed an affidavit of fault in support of his mistake.

 

 

CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff submitted an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to vacate the dismissal under CCP § 473(b) is GRANTED.

I.                Background

On June 13, 2023, Plaintiff served his first set of requests for admissions, request for production, and form interrogatories. Responses to those requests were due on or before July 17, 2023. (CCP § 2031.010 et seq.)

 

On July 21, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel advising that responses were overdue.  (Delman Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 2).

 

On August 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed three motions on each Defendant individually: (1) Motions to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanctions; (2) Motions to Compel Responses to Request to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanctions; and (3) Motions to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted.  Defendant is requesting a combined total of $7,710 in sanctions.

III.           Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Motion to Compel Responses To Form Interrogatories–General. 

 

Plaintiff has filed two separate discovery motions on each Defendant individually: (1) Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Set One; (2) Motion to Compel Responses to Request to Form Interrogatories, Set One. (collectively “Motions” or “Discovery Motions.”)  Upon review of the separate motions submitted, the documents for the two motions to compel responses are identical in substance.  All of the documents contain the same factual allegations and legal support.

Plaintiff has also filed a motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted on each Defendant individually. Upon review of the separate motions submitted, the documents for the two motions are identical in substance.  Each contains the same factual allegations and legal support.

 

Each motion request monetary sanctions for a total sanction amount of: $2,570.

 

Plaintiff alleges that as of the date the Motions were filed, no discovery responses had been provided to any of the requests.  

 

Legal Standards

 

A.     Motions to Compel Responses. 

 

A party may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).) 

 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.060, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.070, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.070, subd. (a) - (b).) 

 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)  

 

The party who propounded the discovery request may bring a motion to compel and the court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for production of documents or interrogatories, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).) 

 

B.    Deem Requests for Admissions Admitted 

 

Where there has been no timely response to requests for admissions, a “requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with section 2023.010).”  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem requests for admission admitted “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).) 

 

C.    Sanctions. 

 

Misuse of the discovery process constituting conduct subject to sanctions include “failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery” and “failing to confer in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d) and (i).) The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).) All discovery motions require a meet and confer declaration stating facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)  

 

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with a motion to deem matters specified in a request for admissions as true and motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c), 2033.280(c).) 

 

 

V.                Discussion 

A.    Motions to Compel Responses.

 

Plaintiff timely served all the discovery requests on Defendants Brown and Johnson Lee on June 13, 2023.  (Delman Decl. for Motions to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Ex. 1; Delman Decl. for Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Ex. 1).

 

Defendants had 30 days to respond but did not do so, nor they did not request any extension.  (Id., ¶ 4). Plaintiff’s attorney of record declares that as of August 11, 2023, Defendants have not answered any of the discovery requests or conferred with Plaintiffs about the status of the responses.  (Id., ¶ 4.)  

 

Because Defendants have failed to respond to the discovery requests, each Defendant is compelled to respond to the request for production of documents and form interrogatories, without objection, including objections based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300 subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)  For interrogatories, the failure to respond also waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230.

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s Motions are GRANTED.

 

B.    Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted.

 

Plaintiff moves to deem its Request for Admissions admitted as to each Defendant.  

 

Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming its Requests for Admissions admitted. Plaintiff supports its claims with a declaration from Counsel. As noted above, Plaintiff propounded RFAs, on Defendant on June 13, 2023. (Delman Decl. for Motion for Requests for Admission to be Deemed Admitted, ¶ 2; Exhibit 1.) However, verified responses were never served on Plaintiff.  (Id., ¶ 3.)

 

Because Defendants have not sent verified responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED. The genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in Defendant’s motion are admitted.   

 

C.    Sanctions

 

Additionally, failure to respond to discovery requests is misuse of the discovery process subject to sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.) The court is required to award monetary sanctions against Defendant unless Defendant can show they “acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).) The monetary sanction shall be in the amount of “the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.) Counsel for Defendant requests two separate awards of sanctions.

 

1.  For the requests for production:

 

$1,285 for: (1) one hour researching and drafting this motion; (2) the additional one hour reviewing Defendant’s opposition to this motion and drafting a reply; and (3) an additional hour of preparing for and attending the hearing on this motion all at a rate of $525.00 per hour.   Plaintiff also requests reimbursement of the filing fee of $60. (Declaration of Dana Delman re Request for Production ¶ 5.)

 

2.  For the Motion to Compel Responses to Request to Form Interrogatories, Set One:

 

$1,285 for: (1) one hour researching and drafting this motion; (2) the additional one hour reviewing Defendant’s opposition to this motion and drafting a reply; and (3) an additional hour of preparing for and attending the hearing on this motion all at a rate of $525.00 per hour.   Plaintiff also requests reimbursement of the filing fee of $60. (Declaration of Dana Delman re Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories ¶ 5.)

 

3.   For the Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted

 

$1,285 for: (1) one hour researching and drafting this motion; (2) the additional one hour reviewing Defendant’s opposition to this motion and drafting a reply; and (3) an additional hour of preparing for and attending the hearing on this motion all at a rate of $525.00 per hour.   Plaintiff also requests reimbursement of the filing fee of $60. (Declaration of Dana Delman re Requests for Admission be Deemed Admitted ¶ 5.)

 

Utilizing a lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances including Defendant’s lack of replies and the similarity of the motions to each other, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the pending motions against Defendant is $2,100, reflecting 4 hours incurred at $525 per hour (three hours for drafting all the motions and and a hour for attending the hearing on the three motions.) Plus reimburse of the filing fee of $360 (six motions at $60.00 filing fee each.)

 

              V.  Conclusion 

 

Plaintiff’s Motions are GRANTED.  Defendants are ordered to serve verified responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production, Form Interrogatories-General, all without objection, within 20 days.  

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to deem Request for Admissions admitted as to Defendant Brown and Defendant Johnson.

 

The Court GRANTS sanctions in the reduced total amount of $2,100.

Case Number: 23STLC03804    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 25

Hallett v. VCA Inc., et al.


23stlc03804

ANALYSIS:                             

 

I.                Background

 

Plaintiff Douglas L. Hallett (“Plaintiff”) filed this Complaint against Defendants VCA, Inc. (“VCA”), Marie Kerl, DVM (“Kerl”), and Michael Kwan, DVM (“Kwan”) (collectively, “Defendants”). The action arises out the loss of Plaintiff’s pet dog, Rosie, which occurred on October 6, 2022. (Compl., ¶ 1.)

 

Defendants filed the instant Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint (the “Motion”) on August 07, 2022. The Motion seeks to strike the allegations supporting the claim for exemplary damages under Civil Code § 3340 as well as the request for judicial referral of the individual defendants to the California Veterinary Medical Board for disciplinary action. (See Notice of Motion at pg. 2.) Plaintiff opposes the Motion.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

California law authorizes a party’s motion to strike matter from an opposing party’s pleading if it is irrelevant, false, or improper. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435; 436, subd. (a).) Motions may also target pleadings or parts of pleadings that are not filed or drawn in conformity with applicable laws, rules, or orders. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (b).)

 

Motions to strike in limited jurisdiction courts, however, may only challenge pleadings on the basis that “the damages or relief sought are not supported by the allegations of the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 92, subd. (d).)

 

 “Before filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Defendant’s Motion is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure § 435.5 (Mot., Burdine Decl., ¶¶ 4-5.)

 

A. Plaintiff’s Allegations

 

Plaintiff alleges the following: (1) that Plaintiff adopted a Boxer dog, Roise, on March 8, 2020 from the Pasadena Humane Society (Compl. ¶ 1); (2) that, prior to the adoption, Roise had been spayed on February 27, 2020 (Ibid.); (3) that, on or before July 12, 2022, Rosie experienced bloody discharge from her vulva, which is alleged to have been a symptom of a urinary tract infection and/or ovarian remnant syndrome (“ORS”) (Id. at ¶ 10); (4) ORS is further alleged that to be “a potentially perilous condition stemming from ovarian tissue inadvertently left int eh body when a dog is spayed” (Ibid.); (5) that, on July 12, 2023, Plaintiff communicated his concerns with VCA’s Foothill Veterinary Hospital branch of Rosie’s condition and the possibility that she had ORS (Id. at ¶¶ 10, 15); (6) that the Defendants failed to return Plaintiff’s phone call for three days and applied a “mistaken diagnosis protocol establish and maintained by VCA and Kerl to deny Rose care she desperately needed” (Id. at ¶¶ 11, 16); (7) that Rosie’s discharge had ended by the time Kwan returned Plaintiff’s call, and when the phone call was returned, Kwan informed Plaintiff that they could not diagnose Rosie until she discharged again. (Id. at ¶¶ 11, 18.); (8) by September 5, 2022, Rosie exhibited a second manifestation of ORS, and on September 11, 2022, she was taken to an emergency veterinarian where Rosie was diagnosed with ORS (Id. at ¶¶ 11, 21-22); (9) by then, Rosie’s condition degenerated into pyometra, which is a potentially fatal infection, and because of VCA’s lack of availability, Plaintiff elected to have the emergency veterinarian conduct the necessary surgery (Id. at ¶ 12); (10) that the blood work done relating to this surgery showed that Rosie had developed a low red blood cell count and eventually anemia due to the pyometra (Ibid.); (11) despite best efforts, Rosie succumbed to a seizure resulting from her anemia and died on October 6, 2022 (Id. at ¶¶ 12, 26).

 

It is further alleged that VCA, under Kerl’s leadership, maintains on its website that a urinalysis can be performed without the presence of discharge and that signs of ORS can include a spayed dog going into heat as well as “swelling of the vulva and blood-tinged vaginal discharge.” (Id. at ¶¶ 13-14, Exh. A.) Plaintiff alleges that VCA’s policy on diagnosing ORS falsely states that the dog must be in heat. (Id. at ¶¶ 19-20.)

 

B. Merits

 

                                     i.                    Exemplary Damages per Civil Code § 3340

 

Defendants first seek to strike Plaintiff’s request for exemplary damages under Civil Code § 3340 as well as portions of the following paragraphs that support this request: (1) Para. 32 (“gross negligence” found on pg. 14, lns: 7-8); (2) Para. 37 (“gross violations of the standard of car” found on pg. 16, ln. 4); and (3) Para., (“carelessly” and “recklessly” found on pg. 17, ln. 17). (Mot., pg. 2.)

 

In addition to the reasonable costs of care occasioned by the [defendant’s conduct], a plaintiff may seek exemplary damages where the wrongful conduct perpetrated against the pet was committed willfully or by gross negligence. (Civ. Code, § 3340.) In order to establish a claim for gross negligence, the complaint must allege that the defendant’s conduct was extreme. (Eastburn v. Regional Fire Protection Authority (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1185–1186.) In other words, this alleged conduct must rise to the level of “either a ‘ “ ‘want of even scant care’ ” ’ or ‘ “ ‘an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct.’ ” ’ [Citations.]” (City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal.4th 747, 754) Thus, Gross negligence “ ‘connotes such a lack of care as may be presumed to indicate a passive and indifferent attitude toward results....’ ” (Eriksson v. Nunnink (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 826, 857.)

 

            Here, Defendants contend that the Complaint fails to allege that they acted intentionally or that their conduct was an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of care. (Mot. at pg. 4.) In this regard, Defendants assert that the advice to wait until Rosie exhibited discharge again did not amount to gross negligence, even if another veterinarian would have acted differently under the circumstances. (Ibid., relying on Williamson v. Prida (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 1417, 1426.)

 

However, Defendants’ reliance on Williamson is misplaced. In that case, the Court of Appeal was examining whether the lower court erred in not granting the defendants’ motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict on the basis that the evidence did not support a finding that they had acted below the standard of care, and ultimately, the Court of Appeal agreed with the defendants’ contention that they did not act below the standard of care. (Williamson, supra, 75 Cal.App.4th at 1419.) There, one of the theories of negligence hinged on the assertion that the usage of Oxytetracycline shots on a horse to treat an infection was unnecessary. (Id. at 1425.) Plaintiff relied solely on the testimony of a veterinarian who testified that he disagreed with the use of that drug under the circumstances, but as the Court of Appeal noted, this veterinarian did not treat race-track horses. Competing testimony had provided from race-track veterinarians that the treatment was appropriate. (Id. at 1425-1426.) Thus, it was concluded that “[t]his disagreement does not establish the standard of care, or a violation thereof.” (Id. at 1426.)

 

In this case, Plaintiff does not allege that Defendants chose to treat Rosie in a different manner. Rather, it is alleged that Defendants elected to not treat Rosie at all based on a false notion that she must be in heat in order to rule out whether she had ORS. (Compl. ¶¶ 10-11,18-20) It is further alleged that, after Rosie’s passing, Plaintiff presented to Defendants literature establishing their misconduct, but the misinformation regarding treatment for ORS remains on VCA’s website. (Id. at ¶¶ 38, 42.) Effectively, it has been alleged that Defendants’ failure to treat Rosie and refusal to take accountability was an extreme departure of the standard of conduct when medical literature existed prior to Rosie’s passing evidencing ORS could be diagnosed when a dog was not in heat. Because these allegations show an “passive and indifferent attitude toward results”, the Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficient alleged his claim for exemplary damages under Civil Code § 3340.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ request to strike Plaintiff’s exemplary damages under Civil Code § 3340 and the supporting allegations is DENIED.

 

                                             ii.          Request for Referral to the California Veterinary Medical Board

 

Next, Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s request for referral of Kerl and Kwan to the California Veterinary Medical Board because there is no mechanism for court referral and Kerl did not treat Rosie. (Mot. at pp. 4-5.)  However, the lack of a formal referral mechanism does not render the request irrelevant, false, or improper pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 436, subdivision (a). The purpose of the California Veterinary Medical Board is to investigate and prosecute veterinarian misconduct, and this misconduct includes negligence, incompetence, and cruelty to animals. (Bus. & Prof. Code § 4875.1, subd. (a).)  Moreover, the Court has the “[p]ower to create a remedy for a wrong in the absence of a statutory provision. (See 3 Cal. Proc. (6th), Actions, § 3 et seq.)” (2 Witkin, Cal. Proc. 6th (2023) Courts § 224.) Therefore, the requested referral is not improper. Additionally, the fact that Kerl did not treat is not dispositive at this stage because it alleged that Kerl maintains false treatment information on the VCA’s website, which could constitute negligence that lead to Rosie’s death.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ request to strike Plaintiff’s request for referral is DENIED.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants VCA, Inc., Marie Kerl, and Michael Kwan’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is DENIED in its entirety.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
