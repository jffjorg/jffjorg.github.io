DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation.

BACKGROUND

            On January 23, 2023, Plaintiffs Thris Van Taylor, a Law Corporation and Thris Van Taylor, an individual (“Plaintiffs”) filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Katie L. Little, Optima Escrow, Inc., Marty Rangel, Mumtaz Thakor (“Defendants”) and DOES 1 to 100, inclusive for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Third Party Contract; (3) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Negligence; (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (6) Fraud and Deceit; (7) Intentional Misrepresentation; and (8) Conversion. The FAC alleges Defendants conspired to not pay Plaintiffs money owed under various oral and written contracts regarding obtaining judgment for and, the sale and remodeling of the real property located at 700 Cory Drive, Inglewood, CA 90302 (“Cory Property”). (FAC ¶¶1, 4-16, 18-22.)

            On February 17, 2023, Defendants Optima Escrow, Inc., Marty Rangel, and Mumtaz Thakor filed a Demurrer to the FAC. The Court sustained Defendants Optima Escrow, Inc., Marty Rangel, and Mumtaz Thakor demurrer to the second through eighth causes of action without leave to amend on March 17, 2023.

            On April 10, 2023, Defendant Katie L. Little filed her Answer.

            On April 14, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Notice of Appeal on the Order Sustaining the Demurrer to the FAC.

            On October 19, 2023, Defendant Little filed a Motion for Stay of Proceedings Pending Appeal. The Court denied the motion for stay on November 14, 2023.  

            On August 8, 2024, Defendant Little filed the instant Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings. On September 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an Opposition. On October 4, 2024, Defendant Little filed a Reply.

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            The standard for ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings is essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer, that is, under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.¿(Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-322, citing Schabarum v. California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216.)¿Matters which are subject to mandatory judicial notice may be treated as part of the complaint and may be considered without notice to the parties. Matters which are subject to permissive judicial notice must be specified in the notice of motion, the supporting points and authorities, or as the court otherwise permits. (Id.)¿The motion may not be supported by extrinsic evidence. (Barker v. Hull (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 221, 236.)¿¿ 

When the moving party is a defendant, he or she must demonstrate that “The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint” or “The complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(c)(1)(B)(i)-(ii).)¿¿¿ ¿¿

 

Application to Facts

            Defendant Katie L. Little (hereinafter “Defendant Little”) moves for an order granting judgment on the pleadings as to the second through eighth causes of action. The motion is made on the grounds that the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute the subject causes of action against Defendant Little because they are based upon conspiracy to interfere with or deprive Plaintiffs of a claimed property right acquired via an alleged lien, which never existed.

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that there is a current pending appeal on the March 17, 2023 Order Sustaining the Demurrer to the FAC as to the second through eighth causes of action. However, the March 17, 2023 Order was sustained without leave to amend as to Defendants As such, Defendant Little was not affected by the order or current appeal.

Charging Lien

“When an attorney wishes to secure payment of hourly legal fees and costs of litigation by obtaining a charging lien against a client’s future recovery, under rule 3–300 the attorney must ‘explain the transaction fully, to offer fair and reasonable terms, to provide a copy of the agreement, to give the client an opportunity to seek independent legal advice, and to secure the client’s written consent.’ ” (Shopoff & Cavallo LLP v. Hyon (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1489, 1522; see also Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, rule 1.8.1 (formerly cited as rule 3–300).) “If the attorney fails to comply with the rule, the “lien may not be enforced ” in a proceeding to enforce a charging lien.” (Id.)

            In Paragraph 3 of the FAC, Plaintiffs allege: “At all times herein mentioned, Plaintiffs, including but not limited to, May 4, 2015, November 2, 2016, February 5, 2019, had a written and oral retainer agreements with LITTLE, for the work being done on LITTLE's behalf, that ranged from $250.00 per hour to $350.00 per hour, to $450.00 per hour. Each and every retainer agreement granted LAW CORPORATION a lien on any recovery as security for payment of Attorney’s fee and disbursement. A balance of $274,277.90 is overdue.” (FAC ¶3, Exs. 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 3.1, 3.2.)

            In Paragraph 23 of the FAC, Plaintiffs alleges: “At all times herein mentioned, PLAINTIFFS have performed all the terms of each agreement with LITTLE, LITTLE has breached each agreement including the oral agreement for the remodeling loan even though LITTLE and other DOES 1-100 have received PLAINTIFFS proceeds money of$27 4,456.60 from Optima Escrow No. 20502MR for the sale of the CORY PROPERTY without payment to PLAINTIFFS.” (FAC ¶23, Exs. 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 2.1, 2.2, 2.3, 2.4, 3.1, 3.2, 4.1.)

            In Paragraph 186 of the FAC, Plaintiffs allege: “On May 4, 2015, November 2, 2016 and February 5, 2017, PLAINTIFFS and LITTLE  entered into contractual agreements to pursue various actions against Elmer Clarke, Case No. BC84795, the just mentioned agreements, all the contracts granted PLAINTIFFS a lien on any recovery to secure payments of any indebtedness.” (FAC ¶186.)

            Although Plaintiffs attempt to differentiate the lien alleged in the FAC from a “charging lien,” by definition “a charging lien grants the attorney considerable authority to detain all or part of the client's recovery whenever a dispute arises over the lien’s existence or its scope.” (Fletcher v. Davis (2004) 33 Cal.4th 61, 69.) As set forth in the FAC, Plaintiffs allege the retainer agreements they entered into with Defendant Little are subject to a lien on any recovery as security for payment of attorney’s fees and disbursement. Thus, the lien alleged in the FAC constitutes a “charging lien.” The FAC is devoid of any facts alleging that Defendant Little was provided an explanation of the retainer agreements including the charging liens, provided a copy of the agreements, or given an opportunity to seek independent legal advice as to the charging liens. Plaintiffs also fails to contest that Defendant Little was not informed of the nature of the charging lien, potential consequences, and/or given an opportunity to seek independent legal advice prior to entering into the alleged agreements. As such, taking the allegations as true one could not reasonably infer the charging liens are valid and enforceable against Defendant Little. Likewise, the subject causes of action and Plaintiffs right to recovery are premised on the charging lien included in the retainer agreement. Additionally, the purported admission by Defendant Little in her initial Answer is irrelevant to the present motion as she has since filed a First Amended Answer upon being granted leave from the Court.

            Therefore, the FAC fails to state sufficient facts to support the second through eighth causes of action against Defendant Little.

 

CONCLUSION

            The Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings is GRANTED without leave to amend. Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions in the amount of $6,500.00 is DENIED.

Moving Party:                         Defendant Haresh Jogani

Ruling:                                    The Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Ireli Astorga Nevarez, Francisca Nevarez Sicairos, Juventino Garcia Gallegos, Sandra Garcia, Sofia Garcia Astorga, and Octavio Garcia Astorga (“Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Haresh Jogani (“Defendant”); J.K. Residential Services, Inc.; 7520 S. Broadway, LLC; and Does 1 to 100. The Complaint alleged five causes of action: (1) violation of Civil Code section 1942.4; (2) breach of the warranty of habitability; (3) private nuisance; (4) Business & Professions Code § 17200 et seq.; and (5) negligence. On June 18, 2024, Plaintiffs served discovery on Defendant. On July 2, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion for Dismissal, or in the Alternative, for Stay Based on Forum Non Conveniens.

            On July 10, 2024, Defendant filed an ex parte Application for an Order to Stay Discovery, which the court granted, allowing Defendant to stay discovery responses until September 11, 2024. On July 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an ex parte application to continue Defendant’s motion to dismiss until after they had a fair opportunity to conduct discovery on movant. On July 17, 2024, the court granted the continuance and ordered that Defendant’s deposition be taken within thirty days following the ruling. On July 30, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion for Reconsideration of the July 17, 2024 Order.

Defendant now moves for a protective order precluding his deposition.

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025. 420(a).)  

The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420(b).)  

            “[W]hen a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president or other official at the highest level of corporate management, and that official moves for a protective order to prohibit the deposition, the trial court should first determine whether the plaintiff has shown good cause that the official has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information. If not, as will presumably often be the case in the instance of a large national or international corporation, the trial court should issue the protective order and first require the plaintiff to obtain the necessary discovery through less-intrusive methods.” (Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court¿(1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1289 (Liberty Mutual).) “Should these avenues be exhausted, and the plaintiff make a colorable showing of good cause that the high-level official possesses necessary information to the case, the trial court may then lift the protective order and allow the deposition to proceed.” (Ibid.)  

Therefore, “it amounts to an abuse of discretion to withhold a protective order when a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president, or corporate officer at the apex of the corporate hierarchy, absent a reasonable indication of the officer's personal knowledge of the case and absent exhaustion of less intrusive discovery methods.” (Id. at p. 1287.) 

Meet and Confer

A motion for a protective order must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (a).) The attached Declaration of Zena M. Kalioundji, attorney of record for Defendant, does not specifically identify any meet and confer efforts prior to filing this motion. Therefore, the requirement that parties meet and confer has not been met.

Application to Facts

1.     Protective Order

The court finds that Plaintiffs have shown good cause to depose Defendant in order to establish personal jurisdiction. However, the court finds that Plaintiffs have not shown good cause to establish Defendant has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information. Nevertheless, Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order is denied, due to a failure to meet and confer prior.

Defendant argues he is a director of J.K. Properties, Inc., and does not have everyday knowledge of what occurs at the property or a witness to any of the events that occurred at the property during the alleged habitability issues. (Motion for Protective Order, p. 6; Declaration of Ritesh Desai, ¶¶ 2, 4.) Defendant relies on Preciado v. Freightliner Custom Chassis Corp. (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 964 for the proposition that Plaintiff must provide affidavits and other competent evidence to show facts that justify the exercise of jurisdiction. (Reply to Opposition, p. 9.) Defendant further argues that Plaintiffs have not shown what information they believe will be obtained from Defendant, or the basis for the belief that he would have relevant knowledge or information. (Motion for Protective Order, p. 6.)

However. Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s reliance on Liberty Mutual’s apex doctrine is improper because Plaintiffs are seeking to depose him to assess whether the court has personal jurisdiction over him, not to seek information from a director of other entity Defendants in the underlying action. (Opposition to Motion, pp. 6–7.) Plaintiffs argue that there is a corresponding right to discovery with the burden to prove Defendant’s sufficient minimum contacts. (Opposition to Motion, p. 7.) Plaintiffs identify their areas of inquiry to include information about Defendant’s business enterprises in Los Angeles County, the status of interests he holds, and his business dealings in Los Angeles County over the last several years. (Opposition to Motion, p. 8.)

In this case, Plaintiffs have demonstrated that discovery is likely to lead to the production of evidence of facts establishing jurisdiction, which is required under Preciado v. Freightliner Custom Chassis Corp. (Preciado v. Freightliner Custom Chassis Corp. (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th at 973.) Plaintiffs’ allegations of Defendants’ business contacts and possible use of California courts demonstrate a likelihood that discovery will yield information relevant to determining personal jurisdiction. Furthermore, the court has previously granted a continuance on Defendant’s motion to dismiss and ordered his deposition to be taken. Plaintiffs have previously shown good cause to depose Defendant and establish the court’s personal jurisdiction over him. The court finds that Plaintiffs have shown good cause to depose Defendant on issues necessary to establish personal jurisdiction.

However, the court finds that Plaintiffs have not shown good cause, that Defendant has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information, to depose him as to the issues in the case beyond personal jurisdiction.  

Plaintiffs argue that the apex doctrine does not apply to Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order beyond the issue of personal jurisdiction because Defendant is being sued in his personal capacity as the owner of the property at issue in this case, not just as director of entity Defendant J.K. Properties. (Opposition to Motion, p. 8.) Plaintiffs’ allegations seek to pierce the corporate veils of the entity Defendants and hold Defendant personally liable for Plaintiffs’ damages. (Opposition to Motion, p. 8.)

When a corporate officer sued in their personal capacity, the primary considerations from the apex doctrine are still relevant, as the rationale for the apex doctrine seeks to prevent high-level officials from being deposed when they are unlikely to have unique information pertaining to the case. (Liberty Mutual, 10 Cal.App.4th at 1287.) In their Complaint, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant personally failed to structure, operate, and manage Defendants 7520 S. Broadway, LLC; J.K. Properties, Inc.; and J.K. Residential Services. (Complaint, ¶ 10.) Plaintiffs contend that Defendant committed the unlawful acts underlying their five causes of action, or had advance knowledge of the unfitness of agents, employees, or representative who committed the unlawful acts. (Complaint, ¶ 17.) These allegations do not identify specific discoverable material that weighs toward Defendant’s personal involvement in the management and operation of the premises that underlie Plaintiffs’ causes of action.

The court finds that Plaintiff has not shown good cause that Defendant has unique personal knowledge, and orders Plaintiffs to seek discovery on matters beyond the issue of personal jurisdiction via less intrusive discovery methods, for example, by written examination or interrogatories. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subds. (6),(7).)

Finally, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant has refused to respond to less intrusive discovery, like written discovery requests, and a recent ruling by another department in this court noted that Defendant “willfully refused and failed to comply with several prior orders compelling him to produce evidence.” (Opposition to Motion, p. 10.)  Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ propounded written discovery went much further than information related to minimum contacts—Defendant argues that only 7 out of 104 Special Interrogatories, 3 of 35 Requests for Production, 12 of 105 Requests for Documents, and none of the Form Interrogatories relate to Defendant’s minimum contacts. (Reply to Opposition, pp. 3–6.) Defendant argues that the information sought in Plaintiffs’ written deposition make it clear that Plaintiffs seek to depose Defendant as a percipient witness to their claims, not just to establish the court’s personal jurisdiction. (Reply to Opposition, p. 7.) Based on the court’s findings above, Plaintiffs cannot depose Defendant as witness, and can instead seek this information via Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production, Requests for Documents, and Form Interrogatories, like the ones they have already propounded.

Based on the forgoing, the court DENIES Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order due to a failure to meet and confer prior to the motion. Defendant is ordered to attend deposition regarding the issue of personal jurisdiction.

2.     Sanctions

In their Opposition to Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order, Plaintiffs seek sanctions of $1,600.00, because Defendant’s Motion did not include a meet and confer declaration and was “made in bad faith.” (Opposition to Motion, pp. 12–13.) In his Reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions does not meet the procedural requirements of California Code of Civil Procedure, section 2023.040, because sanctions are not included in the Opposition’s caption or notice. (Reply to Opposition, p. 2.)

            However, Defendant failed to meet and confer prior to the filing of this motion. The court must impose a monetary sanction on a party or attorney who fails to meet and confer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.020.) The court accordingly grants sanctions of $700.00 to Plaintiffs.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order is DENIED. Plaintiffs may depose Defendant as to the issue of personal jurisdiction. Defendant to pay sanctions of $700.00.

BACKGROUND 

On
June 6, 2024, Plaintiff Yuyuan Gao (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against
Defendants Payward, Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1–100, inclusive, alleging
Defendant profited from Plaintiff’s transactions made on Defendant’s
cryptocurrency platform. Plaintiff alleged fifteen causes of action, including
for fraudulent misrepresentation, breach of contract, and violations of the
Consumers Legal Remedies Act and the Financial Information Privacy Act.

On
July 12, 2024, Plaintiff served discovery requests on Defendant.

On
September 5, 2024, Plaintiff moved for an order deeming his Requests for
Admissions, Set One, as admitted and an order to compel responses to Special
Interrogatories, Set One. The court denied both motions. Plaintiff also filed
the instant motion at that time.

On
September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging
six causes of action: (1) negligence, (2) breach of contract, (3) breach of
implied warranty of fitness for particular purpose, (4) breach of implied
covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (5) unfair business practices, and (6)
unjust enrichment.

Plaintiff
moves for an order compelling Defendant to provide complete responses to
Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

DISCUSSION 

Applicable Law 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by
inspecting, copying, testing, or sampling documents, tangible things, land or
other property, and electronically stored information in the possession,
custody, or control of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., §
2031.010, subd. (a).) Where a party fails to timely respond to demand for
inspection, copying, testing or sampling “[t]he party to whom the demand for
inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to
the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work
product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) Code Civ. Proc. §
2031.300(b) provides that “[t]he party making the demand may move for an order
compelling response to the demand.”

 Code of Civil Procedure
section 2023.010, subdivision (d) provides that a misuse of the discovery
process is “[f]ailing to respond to or to submit to an authorized method of
discovery.”  Code of Civil Procedure, section 2023.010, subdivision (h)
states that a misuse of the discovery process includes “[m]aking or opposing,
unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or
limit discovery.”   “The court may impose a monetary sanction against
a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney
advising that conduct” under Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).  A court has
discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone
Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th
771, 791.)

Application to Facts 

Plaintiff
argues that Defendant’s responses to Request
for Production of Documents, Set One,
are insufficient because Defendant failed to verify the responses. (Motion to
Compel, p. 4.)

Defendant
indicates that it served verifications on September 10, 2024. (Declaration of
Yemi Fasoro; Proof of Service of Verification.) Because verified responses have
been provided, the Motion to Compel Responses to Plaintiff’s Demand for
Production and Inspection of Documents, Set One, is denied.

Defendant
argues that Plaintiff should be subject to sanctions due to a failure to meet
and confer prior to filing this motion. However, “[u]nlike a motion to compel further
responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45-day time limit,
and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or
that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare
Consulting, Inc. v. Pacifici Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th
390, 404.) Because the motion is denied, and based on the forgoing, the court
declines to impose sanctions.

CONCLUSION 

Plaintiff’s
Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One is
DENIED.

BACKGROUND 



On
June 6, 2024, Plaintiff Yuyuan Gao (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants
Payward, Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1–100, inclusive, alleging Defendant
profited from Plaintiff’s transactions made on Defendant’s cryptocurrency
platform. Plaintiff alleged fifteen causes of action, including for fraudulent
misrepresentation, breach of contract, and violations of the Consumers Legal
Remedies Act and the Financial Information Privacy Act.



On
July 12, 2024, Plaintiff served discovery requests on Defendant.



On
September 5, 2024, Plaintiff moved for an order deeming his Requests for
Admissions, Set One, as admitted and an order to compel responses to Special
Interrogatories, Set One. The court denied both motions. Plaintiff also filed
the instant motion at that time.



On
September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging
six causes of action: (1) negligence, (2) breach of contract, (3) breach of
implied warranty of fitness for particular purpose, (4) breach of implied
covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (5) unfair business practices, and (6)
unjust enrichment.



Plaintiff
moves for an order compelling Defendant to provide complete and verified
responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories-General, Set One.



DISCUSSION 



Applicable Law 



“Within 30 days after service of
interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall
serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on
motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response,
or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for
response.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If the party to whom
interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, “[t]he party to
whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option
to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the
interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work
product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) “The party propounding
interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the
interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).)   



Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010,
subdivision (d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is “[f]ailing
to respond to or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  Code of
Civil Procedure, section 2023.010, subdivision (h) states that a misuse of the
discovery process includes “[m]aking or opposing, unsuccessfully and without
substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery.”  
“The court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the
misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct” under
Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).  A court has discretion to fix the amount
of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v.
Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)



Application to Facts 



Plaintiff
argues that Defendant’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, are
insufficient because Defendant failed to verify the responses. (Motion to
Compel, p. 3.) Plaintiff argues that because Defendant failed to verify the
responses, they are untimely, and Defendant waives its right to object to the
responses.



Defendant
indicates that it served verifications on September 10, 2024. (Declaration of
Yemi Fasoro; Proof of Service of Verification.) Furthermore, the omission of a
verification does not waive timely served objections to a discovery request. (Food
4 Less Supermarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 651,
657–658.) Because verified responses have been provided, the Motion to Compel
Responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, is denied.



Defendant
argues that Plaintiff should be subject to sanctions due to a failure to meet
and confer prior to filing this motion. However, “[u]nlike a motion to compel further
responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45-day time limit,
and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or
that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare
Consulting, Inc. v. Pacifici Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th
390, 404.) Because the motion is denied, and based on the forgoing, the court
declines to impose sanctions.



CONCLUSION 



Plaintiff’s
Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is DENIED.
