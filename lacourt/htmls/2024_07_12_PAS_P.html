DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00735    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF YERVAND VARDUMYAN’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUESTS FOR FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE; GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

                          I.          INTRODUCTION

            This personal injury case arises from a dog bite incident. Plaintiff Yervand Vardumyan (“Plaintiff”) alleges that Defendant Celeste C. Esguerra (“Defendant”) owns the dog that attacked Plaintiff and his dog. Plaintiff sues Defendant both in her individual capacity and her capacity as trustee of her trust. The complaint asserts two causes of action: (1) strict liability and (2) negligence per se. 

On October 6, 2023, Plaintiff propounded his “First Set of Requests for Production” on Defendant, including Form Interrogatories, Set One.

Before the Court is a Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses as to Form Interrogatory Nos. 16.2, 16.3, 16.4, and 16.5 filed by Plaintiff on February 28, 2024. Plaintiff also requested sanctions of $1,000 be imposed against Defendant and/ or her counsel. Defendant filed an opposition on June 13, 2024. Plaintiff filed a reply on June 27, 2024.

            The hearing was initially reserved for April 19, 2024. The hearing was continued to July 5, 2024, and continued again to July 12, 2024. (Min. Order, June 20, 2024.) Originally, three separate hearing reservations were made on motions to compel further discovery responses for July 12, 2024. (Reservation Nos.: 890767701495, 489705389241, and 733061673421.) On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel took two of three reserved motions off the calendar. (See Reservation No.: 489705389241 and 733061673421.) The instant motion remains on the calendar to which Plaintiff filed a reply to on June 27, 2024. (See Reservation No: 890767701495.) As such, the Court is unaware and uninformed of any subsequent further meet and confer efforts that may have occurred after the June 25, 2024 hearing cancellations by Plaintiff’s counsel.

Having reviewed the moving, opposition, and reply papers, the Court makes the following rulings:

           

                        II.          ANALYSIS

A.    Procedural Matters

45-Day Rule

This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP §1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).  

 

Meet-and-Confer Requirement 

The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2). 

 

Separate Statement

 Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3).   

 

On October 6, 2023, Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories, Set One; Request for Production, Set One, and Request for Admission, Set One on Defendant. Plaintiff moves the Court issue an order compelling Defendant to produce responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 16.2, 16.3, 16.4, and 16.5. The deadline to respond was November 21, 2023. On November 6, 2023, Defendant served responses to the Form Interrogatories, Set One. On January 12, 2024, Defendant served further responses, which include responses to the Interrogatories at issue. On November 14, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel delivered a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel informing Plaintiff that the objections as to all interrogatories except 15.1 were waived pursuant to CCP §2030.290 because they were not timely (Burunsuzyan Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 3, p. 2.) On January 12, 2024, Defendant served further responses, which include responses to the Interrogatories at issue. Plaintiff argues the supplemental responses are untimely and waived. (Id., ¶ 5, Ex. 4.) On February 1, Plaintiff’s counsel delivered another meet and confer letter asking for responses by February 9, 2024. Defendant’s counsel delivered a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff on February 9, 2024.

 The motion is not timely as it was filed on February 28, 2024, forty-seven days after service of Defendant’s supplemental responses. The parties exchanged meet and confer correspondence following receipt of Defendant’s supplement responses. The meet and confer requirement is fulfilled. The motion is accompanied by a separate statement that contains some procedural defects.

Therefore, all procedural requirements are not satisfied. However, the Court will consider the motion on its merits.

 

B.    Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories – General

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, a court may order a party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the court finds that any of the following apply:

1.     An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

2.     An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

3.     An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. 

Under Civil Procedure Code 2030.22(a) and (b), a responding party’s answer to interrogatories must be “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits. If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.” It is improper for an answer to only respond to a portion of the information sought, particularly when an interrogatory is specific and explicit. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783.) 

Plaintiff moves the Court issue an order compelling Defendant to produce responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 16.2, 16.3, 16.4, and 16.5.

Defendant’s opposes the motion on Plaintiff’s alleged failure to “specifically meet and confer over Interrogatory Nos. 16.2, [16.3], 16.4 and 16.5.” (Babadjanian Decl., ¶ 12.) Defendant also argues that their responses are proper because she does “not have personal knowledge sufficient to know whether the incident giving rise to this action caused any, some, and/or all of plaintiff’s claimed injuries because I am not a medical or biomechanical expert and cannot answer or explain the question(s) of medical causation presented by this interrogatory”. (Opp’n., p. 10.)

Plaintiff motion to compel further responses to its Form Interrogatories 16.2, 16.3, 16.4 and 16.5 is premised on the argument that Defendant’s responses of a “lack of personal knowledge” are meritless and evasive. (Reply, p. 5.) Since Plaintiff has filed this motion to compel further responses, Defendant has the burden to justify each of its objections. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220 - 221.) 

 

a)     Form Interrogatory No. 16.2 asks Defendant whether Plaintiff was injured in the incident. Specifically, it states:

 

“Do you contend that plaintiff was not injured in the INCIDENT? If so: (a) all facts upon which you base your contention; (b) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of the facts; and (c) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your contention and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing...” (Plaintiff’s Mot., Exh. 1, p. 6.) 

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 16.2:

 

 “(a) Responding party lacks sufficient information to respond, as discovery into plaintiff’s medical history remains outstanding. [Response to FROG No. 6.2]

 

b)     Form Interrogatory No. 16.3 asks Defendant about the extent of Plaintiff’s injuries from the incident. Specifically, it states:

 

“Do you contend that the injuries or the extent of the injuries claimed by plaintiff as disclosed in discovery proceedings thus far in this case were not caused by the INCIDENT? If so, for each injury… (a) identify it; (b) state all facts upon which you base your contention; (c) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of the facts; and (d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your contention and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing” (Plaintiff’s Mot., Exh. 1, p. 7.) 

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 16.3:

 

 “All of plaintiff’s injuries at this time as discovery and case evaluation continues.” [Response to FROG No. 6.3]” (Opp’n., p. 10.)

 

c)     Form Interrogatory No. 16.4 asks Defendant to confirm if any of the healthcare services furnished were not due to Plaintiff’s injuries arising from the incident. Specifically, it states:

 

“Do you contend that any of the services furnished by any HEALTH CARE PROVIDER claimed by plaintiff in discovery proceedings thus far in this case were not due to the INCIDENT? If so? If so, for each injury… (a) identify each service; (b) state all facts upon which you base your contention; (c) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of the facts; and (d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your contention and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.” (Plaintiff’s Mot., Exh. 1, p. 7.) 

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 16.4:

 

“a) All services furnished by Healthcare Provider. [Response to FROG No. 6.4.]” (Opp’n., p. 10.)

 

d)     Interrogatory No. 16.5 asks Defendant to confirm whether any costs of furnished healthcare services were necessary or unreasonable. Specifically, it states:

 

“Do you contend that any of the costs of services furnished by any HEALTH CARE PROVIDER claimed as damages by plaintiff in discovery proceedings thus far in this case were not necessary or unreasonable? If so: (a) identify each service; (b) state all facts upon which you base your contention; (c) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of the facts; and (d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your contention and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing” (Plaintiff’s Mot., Exh. 1, p. 7.) 

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 16.4:

(a) Costs of services for all services furnished by any Healthcare Provider.” [Response to FROG No. 6.5.] (Opp’n. p. 10.)

As to subsection (b) of each of the Interrogatories at issue, Defendant responds:

“(b) . . . To the extent defendant can respond, I do not have personal knowledge sufficient to know whether the incident giving rise to this action caused any, some, and/or all of plaintiff’s claimed injuries because I am not a medical or biomechanical expert and cannot answer or explain the question(s) of medical causation presented by this interrogatory. I am not required to inquire of other natural persons or organizations because the information is at least equally – and in fact more – available to the propounding party than to responding party. Indeed, ‘Contention interrogatories cannot be used to require a layperson to provide answers to scientific matters on which expert testimony will be required at trial,’ and as such, a layperson’s ‘I don’t know’ responses to such interrogatories are not admissions that he or she will be unable at trial to provide expert testimony on causation.’ (Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial (Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 8:986.5, citing Bockrath v. Aldrich Chem. Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 84.)” “(c) Responding party” and “(d) Not applicable.” [See P’s Motion – Exh. 4, at 13:20-17:28.] (Opp’n. p. 10.)

            The Court agrees with Plaintiff’s reasoning for requesting additional responses to the interrogatories at issue.

 

“The facts sought, those presently relied upon by plaintiffs to prove their case, are discoverable no matter how they came into the attorney’s possession.” (Southern Pacific Co. v. Superior Court (1969) 3 Cal.App.3d 195, 199 [83 Cal.Rptr. 231.) Further, “[v]erification of the answers is in effect a declaration that the party has disclosed all information which is available to him. . . . [A]n answer which supplies only a portion of the information sought is wholly insufficient. Likewise, a party may not provide deftly worded conclusionary answers designed to evade a series of explicit questions.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 782-783 [149 Cal.Rptr. 499].) Plaintiff has clearly not disclosed “all information which is available to him.” Finally, it is not appropriate to incorporate another pleading by reference: “[I]t is not proper to answer by stating, . . . ‘See my pleading’.” (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 784.) The party raising objections has the burden of demonstrating that the objections apply. See Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255, citing Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-21 (if a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on responding party to justify any objection). In failing to file a timely Opposition, Plaintiff as the party raising the objections has failed to justify the objection

 

            Specifically, the Court finds that Defendant’s lack of direct facts in its supplemental responses to the Interrogatory Nos. 16.2, 16.3, 16.4, and 16.5 are delaying discovery by failing to address the key questions in determining the outcome of the case. The Court agrees that Plaintiff is entitled to supplemental responses 45 days after receipt of these provided responses. Thus, Defendant must comply in providing code compliant responses to the foregoing requests without further delay or prejudice to Plaintiff. Failure to do so will result in the Court awarding sanctions on its own motion.

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion compelling Defendant to produce the request form interrogatory responses.

 

C.    Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 is a general statute authorizing the Court to impose discovery sanctions for “misuse of the discovery process,” which includes (without limitation) a variety of conduct such as: making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery; making an evasive response to discovery; and unsuccessfully and without substantial justification making or opposing a motion to compel or limit discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.)

If sanctions are sought, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.040 requires that the notice specify the identity of the person against whom sanctions are sought and the type of sanction requested, that the motion be supported in the points and authorities, and the facts be set forth in a declaration supporting the amount of any monetary sanction.  

Plaintiff requests the court to impose a monetary sanction of $1,000.00 on Defendant and/or their counsel for Defendant’s alleged failure to prove the requested documents. (Motion, p. 5.) Plaintiff’s Counsel, Kristine Azatyan, Esq., a California-licensed attorney, and associate of the Law Offices of Maro Burusuzyan states that $1,000.00 were incurred in preparing this motion based on her hourly billing rate of $250.00 as applied to 4 hours to prepare this motion. (Decl. of Azatyan, ¶ 1-2.).

In opposition, Defendant argues that attorney fees should not be awarded because the Plaintiff has not cited the correct authority. (Opp’n., p. 5.) In their reply, Plaintiff’s counsel argues that an award for sanctions remains reasonable because “Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is 5 times less than the costs expanded in bringing this motion”. (Reply, p. 5.)

Plaintiff was justified in bringing the motions. Defendant failed to successfully oppose the motions and sanctions are therefore mandatory. Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $1,000.00. Each Defendant and Defendant’s counsel are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $1,000.00 to Plaintiff within 30 days of today’s date.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant is ordered to provide code compliant responses to form interrogatories 16.2, 16.3, 16.4 and 16.5 within 30 days of this order.

Moving party to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENAS TO MEDICAL PROVIDERS; DENYING REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

I. INTRODUCTION

On June
15, 2023, Plaintiff Christopher Mills (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against
Defendants Xin Sun and Lei Song (jointly “Defendants”) asserting motor vehicle
negligence and negligence per se causes of action. Plaintiff alleges that he suffered
serious injuries on December 16, 2021, when Defendant Sun, driving Defendant
Song’s vehicle, pulled out of a driveway and negligently hit Plaintiff, who was
a pedestrian.

From
January 16, 2024 to January 25, 2024, Defendants served several subpoenas for the
production of business records on Plaintiff’s health care providers including:
(1) Subhash Dhand, Inc., a general practitioner, offering substance abuse and
mental health services, (2) Joel Miller, M.D., physiatrist, (3) John Sovec,
therapist and counselor, and (4) Aurora Charter Oak Hospital, a behavioral
healthcare facility (together the “medical providers”).  The subpoenas requested: 

“Records…within
the last 10 years from the date of this subpoena, regarding [Plaintiff]….to
include but not limited to examinations, medical history, diagnosis, treatment,
testing, radiology, prognosis, prescriptions, and billing[.]”

(Declaration
of LaShon Harris, “Harris Decl.,” ¶¶ 6-8, Exhs A-E.)

On
February 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel delivered a meet and confer letter to
Defendant’s counsel requesting that Defendant withdraw the subpoenas as their
scope posed an invasion of Plaintiff’s right to privacy. On February 15, 2024, Plaintiff
filed the instant motion to quash the subpoenas. On April 15, 2024, Defendant
filed an opposition. To date, no reply has been filed. The hearing is scheduled
for July 12, 2024. A jury trial is set for April 29, 2025.

                       
II.         
LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civ. Proc. §1987.1:

“(a)
If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books,
documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court,
or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the
court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b),
or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity
to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or
directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall
declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other
order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or
oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy
of the person.”

Subd.
(b)(1) permits a party to seek relief.

Code
Civ. Proc. §1987.2 provides for an award of sanctions in the court’s
discretion. “[I]n making an order pursuant to motion made under. . . Section
1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable
expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable
attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith
or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of
the subpoena was oppressive.”

            Code
Civ. Proc. §1985.3: 

“(g)¿Any
consumer whose personal records are sought by a subpoena duces tecum and who is
a party to the civil action in which this subpoena duces tecum is served may,
prior to the date for production, bring a motion under Section 1987.1 to quash
or modify the subpoena duces tecum. Notice of the bringing of that motion shall
be given to the witness and deposition officer at least five days prior to
production. The failure to provide notice to the deposition officer shall not
invalidate the motion to quash or modify the subpoena duces tecum but may be
raised by the deposition officer as an affirmative defense in any action for
liability for improper release of records.

Any
other consumer or nonparty whose personal records are sought by a subpoena
duces tecum may, prior to the date of production, serve on the subpoenaing
party, the witness, and the deposition officer, a written objection that cites
the specific grounds on which production of the personal records should be
prohibited.

No
witness or deposition officer shall be required to produce personal records
after receipt of notice that the motion has been brought by a consumer, or
after receipt of a written objection from a nonparty consumer, except upon
order of the court in which the action is pending or by agreement of the
parties, witnesses, and consumers affected.” (CCP § 1985.3(g).)

                    
III.         
ANALYSIS

a.    
Motion to Quash as to Medical
Providers

Relevance

Plaintiff
argues the subpoenas served on the medical providers request documents that are
not relevant because they have no direct correlation to the injuries from the
accident and would violate Plaintiff’s right to medical privacy.

“‘[F]or
discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a
party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement’
and ‘[a]dmissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is
discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.’  These rules are applied liberally in favor of
discovery . . . and (contrary to popular belief) fishing expeditions are
permissible in some cases.”  (Cruz v.
Superior Court (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 646, 653, citations omitted.)  The Court must “balance the public need
against the weight of the privacy right,” and only serious invasions of privacy
will bar discovery.  (Crab Addison,
Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 958, 966.)  There is not an egregious invasion of privacy
every time there is a request for private information, and courts must “place
the burden on the party asserting a privacy interest to establish its extent
and seriousness of the prospective invasion.” 
(Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557.)

Plaintiff
argues that substance abuse treatment records and mental health treatment records
deserve robust protection and Defendants’ broad requests are not relevant to
the instant action. (Motion, p. 9.) Specifically, Plaintiff argues that
documents related to substance abuse treatment should be protected under physician-patient
and psychotherapist-patient privileges because there is no indication that
Plaintiff was suspected of being under the influence of a substance during the incident.
(Motion p. 9; Harris Decl., ¶ 13.) Plaintiff relies on the January 11, 2024,
deposition of Beth Fournier, the responding paramedic who “unequivocally
confirmed that Plaintiff had not been suspected as being under the influence of
alcohol or drugs at the time of the incident.” (Id.)

In
opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has placed his medical condition,
including emotional injuries, at issue in this action, making the request for
mental health records relevant. (See e.g., Britt v.
Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 849 [plaintiffs waive the
physician-patient and psychotherapist-patient privileges to all information
concerning medical conditions which they place at issue in a lawsuit].) Defendants
argue that they are entitled to the records from medical providers to properly evaluate
Plaintiff’s injuries for the calculation of damages, which includes evaluating the
nature of extent of any pre-existing medical and physiological conditions.
(Opp’n., pp. 8-9.)

Plaintiff
urges that any substance abuse treatment records are not relevant, as he was
not suspected of being under the influence of substance or alcohol by the first
responders to the accident. (Harris Decl. ¶ 13.) The Court agrees.  Had there been evidence of Plaintiff being
under the influence of drugs or alcohol at the time of the accident, the Court
would view this request differently. 
However, given the current state of the evidence, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s
substance treatment records are not relevant. 

Regarding
Plaintiff’s injuries, Plaintiff points out that none of the listed medical
providers treated Plaintiff for any of the injuries he allegedly suffered in
this case.  Plaintiff also argues that the
disclosure of these documents would invade Plaintiff’s right to privacy.  In a personal injury case, it is reasonable
for a Defendant to seek Plaintiff’s medical records relating to past injuries
that are the same or substantially similar to the injuries allegedly caused by
Defendant.  In this case, Defendants have
not made this showing.  Defendants have
not demonstrated the relevance of any information contained in Plaintiff’s past
medical records.  

Defendant
is entitled only to records related to Plaintiff’s injuries that are at issue
in this action, not records spanning a decade before the 2021 accident. Any
inquiry into them must be narrowly tailored, to limit impingement on privacy. (Wood
vs. Superior Court (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 1138, 1149.) Specifically, the
party seeking the subpoena must “identify the particular records that contain
pertinent information and, unless it is obvious to a lay person, show why that
information must be obtained to resolve the investigation.” (Id. at
1150.) The requests for production of medical records are not narrowly
tailored, as they simply seeks all records involving Plaintiff. Therefore, the motion to quash is granted.

b.    
Sanctions

Cal.
Civ. Proc. Code § 1987.2(a) provides: Except as specified in subdivision (c),
in making an order pursuant to motion made under subdivision (c) of Section
1987 or under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount
of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including
reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed
in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the
requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.

            The Court declines to award monetary
sanctions to either side, as the Court does not find the motion was made nor
opposed in bad faith. Both parties raised reasoned arguments as to privilege,
privacy, and scope of the deposition subpoena at issue.

                    
IV.         
CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The
motion to quash is GRANTED.

 Plaintiff
to give notice.

The hearing is scheduled for July 12, 2024. A jury trial is set for April 29, 2025.

In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”

Subd. (b)(1) permits a party to seek relief.

Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”

                     III.          ANALYSIS

a.     Motion to Quash as to Arcadia Police Department

Relevance

“‘[F]or discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement’ and ‘[a]dmissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.’  These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery . . . and (contrary to popular belief) fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases.”  (Cruz v. Superior Court (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 646, 653, citations omitted.)  The Court must “balance the public need against the weight of the privacy right,” and only serious invasions of privacy will bar discovery.  (Crab Addison, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 958, 966.)  There is not an egregious invasion of privacy every time there is a request for private information, and courts must “place the burden on the party asserting a privacy interest to establish its extent and seriousness of the prospective invasion.”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557.)

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ request for Plaintiff’s arrest records is too broad and not relevant to the instant action. (Motion, p. 6) Specifically, Plaintiff argues that documents related to an arrest where no conviction resulted have a constitutionally recognized privacy right. (Central Valley Cap. 7th Step Foundation v. Younger (1979) 95 Cal.App. 3d 212; see also Denari v. Superior Court (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1488, 1498 [release of arrest records or dissemination of information about arrest implicates the right to privacy of the arrestees, which is protected by the California Constitution; Motion p. 7.)

In opposition, Defendants argue that they are entitled to Plaintiff’s previous arrest records because they contain evidence of a pattern of incidents of Plaintiff’s harassment and altercations with Asian and Hispanic members of his community and injuries sustained by Plaintiff to the date of the subject incident. “The requested records would determine the motivation for bringing this action against defendants, herein who are Asian as well as the claims in this action.”

            The Court finds that this argument conflates two disparate sets of incidents: 1) past incidents involving acts of alleged aggressive or racist behavior; and 2) past incidents which resulted in injuries to Plaintiff.  As to the first set, the documents sought have no relevance to the allegations in the current matter and violate Plaintiff’s right to privacy under the California Constitution.  Police reports and associated documents relating to allegations of past conduct by Plaintiff involving racist or aggressive behavior would arguably be discoverable in a case involving allegations of battery and self-defense.  However, in a vehicle versus pedestrian accident case involving allegations of negligence, they are not.  As to all category 1 documents, the Court grants the motion to quash. 

            However, past incidents which may have resulted in injury to Plaintiff, if such incidents preceded the accident in the present case and are recent in time, are discoverable.  The May 8, 2021 incident, described in Defendants’ opposition, may have resulted in physical injury to Plaintiff similar to the injuries alleged in the current case.  This incident occurred approximately seven months before the accident involving Defendants.  The Court finds that any documents relating to this incident are relevant and Defendants’ right to such documents overcomes Plaintiff’s privacy objection.  The Court denies the motion to quash as to these documents.

b.     Sanctions

Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 1987.2(a) provides: Except as specified in subdivision (c), in making an order pursuant to motion made under subdivision (c) of Section 1987 or under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.

            The Court declines to award monetary sanctions to either side, as the Court does not find the motion was made nor opposed in bad faith. Both parties raised reasoned arguments as to privilege, privacy, and scope of the subpoena at issue.

                     IV.          CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Accordingly, the motion to quash is GRANTED.
