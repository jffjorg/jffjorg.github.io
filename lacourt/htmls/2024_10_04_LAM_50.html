DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV41848    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

LA LIVE THEATRE, LLC,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

  20STCV41848

Hearing Date:

October 4, 2024

Hearing Time:

2:00 p.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANT COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES’ DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF LA LIVE THEATRE, LLC’S FIRST AMENDED VERIFIED COMPLAINT;

 

DEFENDANT COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES’ MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF LA LIVE THEATRE’S FIRST AMENDED VERIFIED COMPLAINT

 

           

Background

On November 2, 2020, Plaintiff LA Live Theatre, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a “Verified Complaint for Refund of Property Taxes Paid” (the “Complaint”) in this action against Defendant County of Los Angeles (“Defendant”). On June 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Verified Complaint for Refund of Property Taxes Paid (“FAC”). The FAC alleges one cause of action for recovery of taxes paid after denial of claims for refund. 

            The FAC alleges, inter alia, that “Plaintiff owns and operates a performing arts theater venue, formerly known as the Nokia Theatre and presently called the Microsoft Theatre, located within the LA Live Entertainment Complex at Figueroa Street and Olympic Boulevard in downtown Los Angeles. The theater’s building improvements were assessed for property tax purposes following completion of construction on or about October 12, 2007. Plaintiff administratively challenged that new construction assessment before the Los Angeles County Assessment Appeals Board No. 1 (the ‘Appeals Board’ or ‘Board’). This action is an

appeal from the administrative decision by the Appeals Board on the base year value for the newly-constructed theater building improvements as of October 12, 2007.” (FAC, ¶ 1.)

            Defendant now demurs to the first (and sole) cause of action of the FAC. Defendant also moves to strike portions of the FAC. Plaintiff opposes both.

Request for Judicial Notice

The Court grants Defendant’s request for judicial notice. The Court notes that it takes judicial notice only of the fact of the existence of Exhibits 1-6 and the identified portions of the Administrative Record in this case. The Court notes that “[a]lthough the existence of a document may be judicially noticeable, the truth of statements contained in the document and its proper interpretation are not subject to judicial notice if those matters are reasonably disputable. StorMedia stated: In ruling on a demurrer, a court may consider facts of which it has taken judicial notice. This includes the existence of a document. When judicial notice is taken of a document, however, the truthfulness and proper interpretation of the document are disputable.” (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113 [internal quotations and citations omitted, emphasis in original].)

Demurrer

A.    Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

B.     Uncertainty

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (¿¿Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f)¿¿.) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams ¿v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2¿¿.) ¿¿

First, Defendant argues that the FAC is uncertain. As noted by Defendant, the FAC alleges that “[t]he Board’s Findings are deficient as a matter of law and violate Section 1611.5, Rule 324(e), and case law, including Next Century and Farr…” (FAC, ¶ 37.) Defendant asserts that the FAC is uncertain because it “does not identify a single specific finding Plaintiff alleges is insufficient and instead simply makes general allegations of deficiency…” (Demurrer at p. 8:3-4.) In addition, Defendant cites to Roe v. Hesperia Unified School Dist. (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 13, 23, where the Court of Appeal noted that “to state a claim against a public entity, every fact material to the existence of its statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity.” (Internal quotations omitted.) Defendant asserts that it is “left to guess at what findings Plaintiff will claim are deficient. This is not consistent with the standard required of pleading statutory liability against a public entity.” (Demurrer at p. 9:13-15.)

However, in Roe v. Hesperia Unified School Dist., supra, 85 Cal.App.5th at page 23, cited by Defendant, the Court of Appeal also noted that “[t]o survive a demurrer, however, even against a governmental entity…the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (Internal quotations omitted.) In addition, as Defendant acknowledges, the FAC alleges as follows:

 

“The Board’s Findings are deficient as a matter of law and violate Section 1611.5, Rule 324(e), and case law, including Next Century and Farr, because, among other reasons: the Findings do not fairly disclose the Board’s determination of all material points raised in the Applications and at the hearing; the Findings do not set forth findings, or sufficient findings, to bridge the analytic gap between the raw evidence and the Board’s ultimate decision or order; the Findings randomly leap from evidence to conclusion; the Findings do not draw or include all legally relevant sub-conclusions supportive of the Board’s ultimate decision; the Findings fail to enable a reviewing court to trace and examine, or adequately trace and examine, the Board’s mode of analysis; the Findings do not address, or sufficiently address, specifically the Board’s reasoning for accepting or rejecting each issue raised by the parties; the Findings do not address, or sufficiently address, specifically the Board’s reasoning for accepting or rejecting each valuation of the Subject Property set forth by the parties; the Findings do not address, or sufficiently address, specifically the Board’s reasoning for accepting or rejecting the evidence set forth by the parties; the Findings do not tell the parties, and reviewing courts, why the Board rejects evidence in other than conclusory terms; the Findings do not provide sufficient explanation or analysis for the Board’s ultimate decision; and the Findings force reviewing courts into unguided and resource-consuming explorations and/or to grope through the record to determine whether some combination of credible evidentiary items which supported some line of factual and legal conclusions supported the Board’s ultimate decision.” (FAC, ¶ 37, emphasis omitted.)

In addition, Plaintiff notes that “[a] demurrer for uncertainty does not lie where the ambiguous facts alleged are presumptively within the knowledge of the demurring party.” (Schessler v. Keck (1954) 125 Cal.App.2d 827, 836.) Plaintiff asserts that here, “Plaintiff’s particular reasons for claiming the Findings are deficient not only are presumptively, but are actually, within Defendant’s knowledge.” (Opp’n at p. 7:18-19.) As noted by Plaintiff, Defendant’s demurrer acknowledges that “[i]n Plaintiff LA Live Theatre, LLC’s Supplemental Closing Brief…three alleged deficiencies in the Findings are identified…” (See Demurrer at p. 5:5-20.)

In light of the foregoing, the Court does not find that Defendant has demonstrated that the FAC is uncertain.

Defendant also argues that “[t]he First Amended Complaint does not state facts sufficient to establish a cause of action under the standards applicable to findings of fact issued by Assessment Appeals Boards.” (Demurrer at p. 9:24-25.) Defendant cites to Revenue and Taxation Code section 1611.5, which provides in part that “[w]ritten findings of fact of the county board shall be made if requested in writing by a party up to or at the commencement of the hearing…The written findings of fact shall fairly disclose the board’s determination of all material points raised by the party in his or her petition and at the hearing, including a statement of the method or methods of valuation used in appraising the property.” Defendant also cites to Topanga Assn. for a Scenic Community v. County of Los Angeles (1974) 11 Cal.3d 506, 514, where the California Supreme Court discusses “Code of Civil Procedure section 1094.5, the state’s administrative mandamus provision which structures the procedure for judicial review of adjudicatory decisions rendered by administrative agencies.” The Topanga Court concluded that “implicit in section 1094.5 is a requirement that the agency which renders the challenged decision must set forth findings to bridge the analytic gap between the raw evidence and ultimate decision or order.” (Id. at p. 515.)

Defendant argues that here, “[t]he Findings meet these standards…The Findings are 45 pages long, containing detailed analysis of the facts of the case and the contentions of the parties, concluding with the AAB’s Findings and Decision synthesizing and analyzing the facts and contentions of the parties. In performing this thorough evaluation, the AAB determined that the income approaches to value presented by the parties were all unreliable and not based on market rent, the most reliable method of valuing the property was the cost approach presented by Defendant, and there was no evidence supporting the need to make any deduction from the cost approach valuation to account for obsolescence.” (Demurrer at p. 11:10-16.) Defendant asserts that “the findings on the unreliability of the 2005 pro forma are not deficient,” that “the findings of the AAB on the lack of comparability of the Greek Theatre and Shrine Auditorium are clear, unequivocal and supported by the record,” and that “there is no deficiency in the findings on external obsolescence.” (Demurrer at pp. 13:9; 14:11-12; 15:5.) In the opposition, Plaintiff counters that “the Board’s Findings are legally deficient for numerous reasons…” (Opp’n at p. 9:19.)

As an initial matter, the Court finds that these are factual arguments that are not appropriate on demurrer. The Court notes that “¿[o]n a demurrer a court’s function¿is limited to testing the legal sufficiency of the complaint. A demurrer is simply not the appropriate procedure for determining the truth of disputed facts.¿” (¿Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage¿(1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374 [internal quotations and citation omitted]¿.) Defendant asserts that “[t]he three findings that Plaintiff has identified in its Supplemental Closing Brief at 23-24 as being deficient meet the standards applicable to findings by administrative agencies as can be determined by review of the record in this case judicial notice of relevant portions of which has been requested.” (Demurrer at pp. 12:26-13:1.) However, “[t]he hearing on demurrer may not be turned into a contested evidentiary hearing through the guise of having the court take judicial notice of documents whose truthfulness or proper interpretation are disputable.” (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 114.) “Taking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning.” (Id. at p. 113.)

Lastly, the first cause of action does not solely concern Plaintiff’s allegation that “[t]he Findings made by the Board are…deficient…” (FAC, ¶ 47.) The first cause of action also alleges, inter alia, that “the Appeals Board over-assessed the Subject Property as of October 12, 2007,” and that “[t]he property taxes for which Plaintiff claims a refund are based on an assessment and taxes that were erroneously and/or illegally assessed, levied and/or collected.” (FAC, ¶¶ 39-40.) In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that “even if the Demurrer were sustained (it should not be), that would only foreclose Plaintiff’s deficient Findings claim, but could not result in dismissal of the entire action, because the Court must still decide the primary issue in this case, whether the Board erred in its decision on the assessed values of the subject theater.” (Opp’n at p. 5:22-25.) As Defendant acknowledges, ¿¿“[a] demurrer does not lie to a portion of a cause of action.” (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682; see also Kong ¿v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redevelopment Agency (2002) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1047 [“a demurrer cannot rightfully be sustained to part of a cause of action or to a particular type of damage or remedy.¿¿”].) Here, Defendant appears to demur only to a portion of the first cause of action and acknowledges that “[i]t may be argued that the present demurrer is attacking just a portion of Plaintiff’s single cause of action.” (Demurrer at p. 7, fn. 3.)

 In light of the foregoing, the Court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action of the FAC.

            Motion to Strike

A court may strike any “¿irrelevant, false, or improper matter¿inserted in any pleading¿” or any part of a pleading “¿not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿” (¿¿Code Civ. Proc., § 436¿¿.) “¿The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 437¿.)¿¿ 

Defendant moves to strike “[t]he entirety of paragraphs 33-37,” “[t]he entirety of paragraphs 46-48,” “[t]he remaining allegations of the First Cause of Action in Paragraphs 38-45,” and “[t]he entirety of the Prayer for Relief, including paragraphs 3-4.” (Notice of Motion at p. 2:7-16.) The Court notes that the notice of motion does not appear to specify what “remaining allegations of the First Cause of Action” Defendant is referring to. It appears that Defendant seeks to strike all of the paragraphs contained within the first cause of action.

In the motion Defendant states that “[t]he grounds for striking the matter described above in the Notice of Motion to Strike and Section III.A. of this memorandum is that the First Amended Complaint is not drawn in conformity with California law since it is uncertain, fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, fails to identify any findings that it claims are deficient and thereby fails to plead with particularity the existence of statutory liability against a public entity as required as described in Section III of the Demurrer Memorandum. Moreover, the findings Plaintiff claims are deficient in its briefing comply in every respect with the standards required of findings by an administrative agency as described in detail in Sections III.B. and III.C. of the Demurrer Memorandum.” (Mot. at p. 11:3-10.) As discussed in further detail above, the Court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action of the FAC. Thus, the Court does not find that Defendant has demonstrated grounds to strike the allegations identified in Defendant’s notice of motion.

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Defendant’s motion to strike.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action of the FAC is overruled. Defendant’s motion to strike is denied.

BRIEFING ON THE ISSUES OF THE JUDGMENT AS A CONTRACT AND THE STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS

 

 

Background

Plaintiffs C&M Investment Group, Ltd. and Karlin Holdings Limited Partnership (jointly, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action on June 23, 2021 against Defendant Harvey Champlin (“Defendant”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) intentional interference with contractual relations and (2) intentional interference with prospective economic benefit.

On September 26, 2024, a Final Status Conference was held in this matter. The Court’s September 26, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Briefing schedule as to the issues of the judgment as a contract and the statute of limitations is as follows: Defendant’s opening brief is due September 26, 2024, Opposition is due by 4:00 p.m. on October 1, 2024, and the reply is due at noon on October 2, 2024. The hearing on these issues will be on October 3, 2024 at 1:30 p.m. in Department 50.”

 

 

Discussion

A.    Applicable Statute of Limitations

In his supplemental brief, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ two causes of action for intentional interference with contractual relations and intentional interference with prospective economic benefit are subject to the two-year limitations period set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 339, subdivision (1).

Code of Civil Procedure section 339, subdivision (1) sets forth a two-year statute of limitations for “[a]n action upon a contract, obligation or liability not founded upon an instrument of writing, except as provided in Section 2725 of the Commercial Code or subdivision 2 of Section 337 of this code; or an action founded upon a contract, obligation or liability, evidenced by a certificate, or abstract or guaranty of title of real property, or by a policy of title insurance; provided, that the cause of action upon a contract, obligation or liability evidenced by a certificate, or abstract or guaranty of title of real property or policy of title insurance shall not be deemed to have accrued until the discovery of the loss or damage suffered by the aggrieved party thereunder.”

Defendant cites to Knoell v. Petrovich (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 164, 168, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he third amended complaint alleges that Attorney Petrovich published defamatory letters on December 5, 1994, and January 26, 1995, and slandered appellant at a February 16, 1995, office conference. The action was filed November 14, 1997, more than two years later. The trial court ruled that the defamation causes of action were barred by the one-year statute of limitations (Code Civ. Proc., § 340, subd. (3)), and the causes of action for interference with contractual relations and interference with prospective business advantage were barred by the two-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 339, subd. 1; Tu-Vu Drive-In Corp. v. Davies (1967) 66 Cal. 2d 435, 437 [58 Cal. Rptr. 105, 426 P.2d 505].) It did not err.” 

In their opposition to Defendant’s supplemental brief, Plaintiffs argue that “the applicable limitations period is three years because the gravamen of Plaintiffs’ claims is fraud.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 2:7-8.) Plaintiffs cite to Romano v. Wilbur Ellis & Co. (1947) 82 Cal.App.2d 670, 671, where the “Plaintiff sued in tort arising from the alleged representations of defendants which induced a third party to repudiate its contract with plaintiff.”[1] The Romano Court found that “[t]his is not an action for breach of the contract between appellant and Pesquera. The latter is not a party to the action. The suit is for damages for fraud. The accepted rule of the cases hereinabove cited is that the gist of this type of action is fraud.” (Id. at p. 673.) The Romano Court further noted that “[t]he complaint sets forth that these false representations were relied on by Pesquera, that they were uttered with the intent to mislead Pesquera to the prejudice of plaintiff and that they were made for the purpose, and accomplished the result, of gaining an advantage to the person in fault. This is fraud under section 1573 of the Civil Code. As an action based on fraud it comes within the three-year limitation of subdivision 4, section 338, Code of Civil Procedure. Since the complaint was filed within three years of the act complained of, it is not barred by the statute.” (Id. at p. 674 [internal quotations omitted].)

In his supplemental brief, Defendant asserts that the limitations period set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 338 does not apply. Defendant asserts that “the ‘gravamen’ of the Complaint is Champlin’s purported interference with Plaintiffs’ efforts to enforce the 2012 Default Judgment against Powers. Any ‘fraud’ referenced in the Complaint relates to the allegedly ‘large-scale fraud perpetrated by [Powers] over the course of more than six years that led to [the 2012 Default Judgment].’ (Complaint at ¶ 1). The key allegations about Champlin focus on his actions in setting up and managing financial structures for Powers’ benefit (Id., ¶¶ 14-17) and his role in managing accounts and trusts, not making deceptive statements or misrepresentations. (Id., ¶¶ 18-24).” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 3:9-15.)

As discussed, the Romano Court noted that “the gist of the action is the fraudulent inducement leading to the breach of the contract. The complaint sets forth that these false representations were relied on by Pesquera, that they were uttered with the intent to mislead Pesquera to the prejudice of plaintiff and that they were made for the purpose, and accomplished the result, of gaining an advantage to the person in fault.” (Romano v. Wilbur Ellis & Co., supra, 82 Cal.App.2d at p. 674 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) Here, Plaintiffs do not allege that Defendant made false representations that were relied upon by a third party, which were made with the intent to mislead the third party to the prejudice of Plaintiffs. Rather, as Defendant notes, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant “systemically aided Powers in hiding Powers’ assets from collection.” (Compl., ¶ 14.) Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s a result of his activity, Champlin deprived Plaintiffs of the ability to collect at least $12 million in assets from Powers and otherwise frustrated and burdened Plaintiffs’ collection efforts, to the injury of Plaintiffs.” (Compl., ¶ 16.)

Plaintiffs assert that “Champlin committed actual and constructive fraud by telling material lies to Plaintiffs while under a duty (oath) to disclose the truth and with the intent and/or effect of misleading Plaintiffs to their detriment.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 3:11-13.) However, Plaintiffs do not appear to cite to any allegations of the Complaint stating that Defendants told “material lies to Plaintiffs.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 3:11.) Rather, Plaintiffs allege that “Champlin also misrepresented the nature and extent of his activities for Powers in sworn testimony.” (Compl., ¶ 15.)

Plaintiffs also cite to SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Central Pacific Bank (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 859, 864, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” Plaintiffs assert that “Powers had such a relationship with Plaintiffs by virtue of the Powers Judgment and owed them a duty not to hide assets. Powers violating that duty by using Champlin to move money and funnel him cash while concealing that the money was his is a classic constructive fraud and fraudulent concealment. And Champlin aided and abetted and conspired with Powers, and Plaintiffs’ allegations therefore show he is thus equally responsible for this fraud.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 4:9-14.)

But the Complaint here does not appear to allege that Defendant (as opposed to Powers) had any duty to disclose a material fact to Plaintiffs, or that Defendant “intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff[s]…” (SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Central Pacific Bank, supra, 207 Cal.App.4th at p. 864.) Plaintiffs also do not appear to allege that they were “unaware of the fact and would not have acted as [they] did if [they] had known of the concealed or suppressed fact.” (Ibid.) Further, the Complaint does not appear to specifically allege that Defendant “aided and abetted and conspired with Powers…” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 4:12-13.) As noted by Defendant, Plaintiffs appear to “conflate Powers’ alleged fraudulent conduct with Champlin’s actions…” (Defendant’s Reply at pp. 3:28-4:1.)

As to the argument concerning constructive fraud, Defendant notes that “[c]onstructive fraud arises on a breach of duty by one in a confidential or fiduciary relationship to another which induces justifiable reliance by the latter to his prejudice.” (Odorizzi v. Bloomfield Sch. Dist. (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 123, 129.) Here, Plaintiffs do not appear to allege any confidential or fiduciary relationship between Plaintiffs and Defendant.

Plaintiffs also assert that “Champlin committed wire fraud.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 4:1.) However, the Complaint does not appear to allege that Defendant committed wire fraud. In addition, Plaintiffs cite to non-binding federal authority to support this argument. Plaintiffs also rely on Abselet v. Levene Neale Bender Yoo and Brill L.L.P. (C.D.Cal. Aug. 10, 2017, No. CV 16-6263-JFW (JEMx)) 2017 U.S.Dist.LEXIS 222730, a non-binding federal case.

Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that Plaintiffs have demonstrated that the three-year statute of limitations set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 338 applies here. The Court finds that Defendant has shown that the two-year statute of limitations set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 339, subdivision (1) applies.

B.     Judgment as a Contract

Next, Defendant argues that “default judgments are not contracts for purposes of intentional interference with contractual relations.” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 4:10-11.) In the first cause of action of the Complaint, Plaintiffs allege, inter alia, that “[o]n January 17, 2012, this Court entered judgment against Powers and in Plaintiffs’ favor in the amount of $36,680,676.46 in C&M Investment Group Ltd. & Karlin Holdings Limited Partnership v. Philip Richard Powers & Neil Campbell, Case No. BC378888.” (Compl., ¶ 19.) Plaintiffs allege that “Champlin engaged in the above conduct with the intent and result of frustrating Plaintiffs’ collection efforts.” (Compl., ¶ 21.) As discussed, Plaintiffs allege that “Champlin systemically aided Powers in hiding Powers’ assets from collection.” (Compl., ¶ 14.)

“Tortious interference with contractual relations requires (1) the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of that contract; (3) the defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.” (Ixchel Pharma, LLC v. Biogen, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 1130, 1141 [internal quotations omitted].) Defendant asserts that here, “four of the five elements expressly require or are predicated upon the existence of a valid, performing contract between the plaintiff and a third party. In this case, there was never such a contract between Plaintiffs and Powers, nor did Powers ever agree to pay Plaintiffs the amount of the 2012 Default Judgment.” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 4:22-5:1.)

In the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that “the Powers Judgment is a contract for Plaintiffs’ claims.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 6:7.) Plaintiffs cite to Grotheer v. Meyer Rosenberg, Inc. (1936) 11 Cal.App.2d 268, 269-270, where “Plaintiffs [sought]…to hold the defendant Meyer Rosenberg, Inc., liable for the payment of a money judgment previously obtained by plaintiffs against the defendant Meyer Rosenberg, individually, upon the ground that said corporation is the alter ego of Meyer Rosenberg, individually. At the time of the commencement of the action they secured a writ of attachment which they caused to be levied on a bank account of $ 624.33 standing in the name of said corporation. Thereafter the corporation moved to dissolve the attachment, and pursuant to said motion an order was entered vacating, setting aside and recalling the writ of attachment and directing the sheriff to release all property levied upon.” The plaintiffs appealed the order. (Id. at p. 270.) The Grotheer Court noted that “[i]t is true that the present case is based on a judgment for the direct payment of money, whereas the causes of action stated in the two cases above cited were based upon original contractual obligations for the direct payment of money; but the distinction mentioned does not alter the situation, because it is well settled that an action based on a judgment is an action based on a contract, and this is so irrespective of the nature of the transaction on which the judgment is founded, and even though the judgment be rendered upon an unassignable cause of action for tort, it being held in this regard that after the recovery of the judgment, whether it is for a tort or upon contract, the judgment becomes a debt which the judgment debtor is obligated to pay and the law implies a contract on his part to pay it.” (Id. at pp. 272-273 [internal citations omitted].) Plaintiffs also cite to Becker v. Becker (1950) 36 Cal.2d 324, 326, where the California Supreme Court noted that “[t]his court has said recently that [a]n interlocutory judgment of divorce is, therefore, so far as it determines the rights of the parties, a contract between them…” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.)

In his brief, Defendant argues that “Grotheer’s equation of judgments and contracts is limited to specific procedural contexts, primarily related to attachment statutes.” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 5:14-15.) Defendant cites to 1A Cal. Jur. 3d Actions § 19, which provides, inter alia, that “[f]or some procedural purposes, such as the interpretation and application of an attachment statute referring to actions arising on contract, a judgment is treated as a contract, and an action on a judgment is a contract action, since the law implies a contract by the judgment debtor to pay the judgment.” (Citing Grotheer v. Meyer Rosenberg, Inc., supra, 11 Cal.App.2d 268.)

Defendant also cites to 6 Witkin, Cal. Proc. 6th Prov Rem § 62 (2024), which provides that “[a] judgment is, for some purposes, regarded as contractual or quasi-contractual; and an action on a judgment is on ‘implied contract’ within the meaning of the attachment statute, even though the judgment was rendered on a tort claim. (Grotheer v. Meyer Rosenberg (1936) 11 C.A.2d 268, 272, 53 P.2d 996; Minor v. Minor (1959) 175 C.A.2d 277, 279, 345 P.2d 954 [action to recover defaulted installments on divorce decree]; see 3 Cal. Proc. (6th), Actions, § 151.).”

In their brief, Plaintiffs counter that “[b]oth secondary sources that Champlin cites are unavailing as both recognize that a judgment is, for some purposes, regarded as contractual or quasi-contractual…and point to the attachment statute as merely one example of such a purpose.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 7:20-23, emphasis omitted.) The Court agrees. As set forth above, the Grotheer Court found that “after the recovery of the judgment, whether it is for a tort or upon contract, the judgment becomes a debt which the judgment debtor is obligated to pay and the law implies a contract on his part to pay it.” (Grotheer v. Meyer Rosenberg, Inc., supra, 11 Cal.App.2d at p. 273.)

Defendant also argues that “[t]he California Legislature has enacted comprehensive statutory schemes governing judgment enforcement and fraudulent transfers, including the Enforcement of Judgments Law (EJL), Code of Civil Procedure §§ 680.010-724.260, and the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (UVTA), Civil Code §§ 3439-3439.14. These statutes already provide robust and efficient remedies for judgment creditors…” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 6:23-26.) But the Court does not see how this forecloses Plaintiff’s cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations.

In light of the foregoing, the Court agrees with Plaintiffs that “the Powers Judgment is a contract for purposes of Plaintiffs’ [first cause of action].” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 2:5.)

Lastly, Defendant argues that “[t]he plaintiffs’ cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic benefit (IIPEB) fails as a matter of law because the 2012 Default Judgment does not constitute a valid business expectancy.” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 7:12-14.) Defendant asserts that “the 2012 Default Judgment is not a valid business expectancy because it did not arise from ordinary commercial dealings.” (Defendant’s Brief at p. 8:4-5.) But as noted by Plaintiffs, this “third issue [is] not reflected in the Court’s order directing supplemental briefing.” (Plaintiffs’ Brief at p. 1:22-23.) As set forth above, the Court’s September 26, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Briefing schedule as to the issues of the judgment as a contract and the statute of limitations is as follows:…” Defendant does not indicate that he obtained leave to address this issue. Thus, the Court declines to consider it.

Conclusion 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendant has shown that the two-year statute of limitations set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 339, subdivision (1) applies to Plaintiffs’ causes of action. The Court also finds that Plaintiffs have shown that the alleged “Powers Judgment” is a contract for purposes of Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this Order.
