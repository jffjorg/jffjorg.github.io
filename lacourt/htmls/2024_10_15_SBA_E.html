DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV44852    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

CANDACE A RAVANA,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

20STCV44852

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Sustained

 

 

ROIC CALIFORNIA, LLC, a Delaware limited liability company; TJX INCENTIVE SALES INC. a Virginia corporation dba Marshalls; and DOES 1 through 5, inclusive,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             October 15, 2024

 

Moving Parties:          Defendant ROIC California, LLC

 

Responding Party:      None

 

HEARING: Demurrer to the Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.

 

RULING          The Demurrer is sustained with leave to amend within 20 days of the Court's ruling.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On November 23, 2020, Plaintiff filed the complaint alleging causes of action for premises liability, premises liability-negligence, and premises liability-willful failure to warn against Defendants ROIC California, LLC and TJX Incentive Sales Inc. arising from a fall outside on Defendant's property.

 

            On July 25, 2024, Defendant ROIC California, LLC filed the instant demurrer to the Complaint.

           

            No opposition has been filed.

           

            No reply has been filed.

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Defendant demurrers to all three causes of action. The Complaint reads, in pertinent part, “The Terraces at South Bay which is a mall at 28901 S Western Avenue Rancho Palos Verdes California 90275, apn 7550-019-018 (28821 S Western Avenue Rancho Palos Verdes California 90275) in the parking lot directly adjacent to the Marshalls, (the "Property"), Marshalls is a retail store leasing space at The Terraces of South Bay including the parking lot which invited Plaintiff to shop at its retail store, when Plaintiff exited the store after shopping, she tripped and fell and was injured as a result of inadequate lighting at night and improper placement of a parking stop in front of the Marshalls.” (Complaint p. 4.)

 

Meet and Confer 

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) 

 

            Between July 10, 2024, and July 24, 2024, Defendant made multiple attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff's counsel for record but was unable to because of Plaintiff’s conduct. (Kreisman Decl. ¶ 6-10.) The Court finds this is sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement.

 

 

Cause of Action 1 – Premises Liability

 

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.  (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp¿(2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)  Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property¿to¿avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.  (Annocki¿v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC¿(2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  The existence and scope of duty are legal questions for the court.  (Id. at p. 36.)  If there is a condition that poses a danger to customers and others on the premises, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition reasonably safe for their use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.”  (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc.¿(1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff's complaint fails to meet the necessary elements required to establish a claim for premises liability. Specifically, the Complaint does not provide specific facts demonstrating that Defendant owned, leased, occupied, or controlled the property where the incident occurred. Instead, it relies on a generic statement without any supporting details, merely checking a box on the form complaint to allege that Defendant was responsible. Additionally, the Complaint does not describe how Defendant was negligent in maintaining the property, nor does it present facts indicating that there was a dangerous condition creating an unreasonable risk of harm or that Defendant had actual or constructive knowledge of such a condition. The location of the incident is also ambiguous, leaving Defendant uncertain as to where the alleged trip and fall occurred. Without clear facts establishing that Defendant owned or controlled the area in question or was negligent in its maintenance, Defendant contends that the Complaint is uncertain and insufficient to support a claim for premises liability. As a result, Defendant requests that the court sustain its demurrer and dismiss the premises liability cause of action.

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

            A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

            Here, Plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to show that Defendant owned, leased, or controlled the property. Instead, there is the mere conclusion that “The defendants who negligently owned, maintained, managed and operated the described premises were… ROIG California LLC.” (Complaint p. 4.) Therefore, because Plaintiff has failed to allege facts that show Defendant owned or controlled the premises, and thus, no duty existed, the Demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to cause of action one. (C.A, supra, 53 Cal.4th 861, 872; (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp¿(2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)

           

Cause of Action 2 – Premises Liability-Negligence

 

            The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.  (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp¿(2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) 

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff's complaint fails to meet the basic elements required for a negligence or premises liability claim, which are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159.) Defendant points out that the Complaint does not provide specific facts demonstrating that Defendant owned, maintained, or operated the property. Additionally, it fails to establish that Defendant had prior knowledge of any alleged dangerous condition or describe how the premises were hazardous or how that hazard caused Plaintiff's injuries. The Complaint does not specify the nature of the injuries or explain what "special relationship" existed between Plaintiff and Defendant that would create a duty of care. Furthermore, it fails to allege that any actions or omissions by Defendant were a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm. Defendant also highlights that the Complaint is filled with legal conclusions but lacks actual facts to support any cause of action, and after almost six years since the incident, Plaintiff still has not met the basic requirements for pleading. As a result, Defendant argues that its demurrer to the premises liability claim based on negligence must be sustained.

 

            Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

For the reasons discussed above, the Court sustains the demurrer to the second cause of action with leave to amend.

 

 

           

 

 Cause of Action 3 - Premises Liability-Willful Failure to Warn

 

If there is a condition that poses a danger to customers and others on the premises, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition reasonably safe for their use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.”  (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc.¿(1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)

           

Defendant argues that for a premises liability claim based on a willful failure to warn, three key elements must be alleged with specificity: (1) the defendant had actual or constructive knowledge of a dangerous condition, (2) the defendant knew that injury was probable (not just possible) as a result of that danger, and (3) the defendant consciously failed to act to avoid the peril. (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 528. While Plaintiff checked the box on the Complaint form alleging that Defendant willfully failed to warn of a dangerous condition, the complaint lacks specific or general facts supporting any of these elements. Plaintiff does not provide details showing that Defendant had actual or constructive notice of a dangerous condition, that Defendant knew injury was probable, or that Defendant consciously failed to act to prevent harm. The Complaint relies on boilerplate and conclusory allegations without factual support. As such, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot establish a cause of action for premises liability based on willful failure to warn, and therefore, the demurrer should be sustained.

            

            Therefore, the Court sustains the demurrer to the third cause of action with leave to amend.

RULING

            The Court Denies the Motion to Compel Arbitration.

BACKGROUND

            On October 10, 2023, Plaintiffs Frank D Adams Jr., Menuét Adams, and Krista Adams filed the Complaint.

 

            On March 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint alleging causes of action for statutory elder abuse and neglect, negligence, and wrongful death.

 

            On April 2, 2024, Defendants Laibco, LLC. dba Las Flores Convalescent Hospital filed the instant motion to compel arbitration.

 

            On April 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Opposition.

 

            On August 12, 2024, the hearing was continued to October 15, 2024.

 

            On October 8, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The Federal Arbitration Act (the “FAA”) applies in both federal and state courts to contracts evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce. (9 U.S.C. §§ 1-2; Southland Corp. v. Keating (1984) 465 U.S. 1, 12.) The FAA preempts conflicting state law. (Preston v. Ferrer (2008) 552 U.S. 346, 353.) The party that contends the FAA applies bears the burden to demonstrate that the arbitration agreement is in a “‘contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce’ . . . .” (Woolls v. Super. Ct. (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b)¿Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿ 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

The instant case arises from the alleged negligent care Plaintiff Frank D Adams Jr. (Frank) obtained at Las Flores Convalescent Hospital. Before entering the facility, Frank granted Menuét Adams (Menuét) Durable Power of Attorney (DPOA) on February 23, 2021. The DPOA included powers over claims and litigation but not over healthcare decisions. Defendant requests that the Court compel arbitration based on the alleged signing of an arbitration agreement on December 13, 2022, by Menuét, which reads, in pertinent part:

 

Article 1 (1.1) Recitals: It is understood that any dispute as to medical malpractice, that is as to whether any medical services rendered under this contract were unnecessary or unauthorized or were improperly, negligently, or incompetently rendered, will be determined by submission to arbitration as provided by California law and not by a lawsuit or resort to court process except as California law provides for judicial review of arbitration proceedings. Both parties to this contract, by entering into it, are giving up their constitutional right to have any such dispute decided in a court of law before a jury and instead are accepting the use of arbitration…  It is also understood that this agreement similarly governs the submission to arbitration of any and all other claims arising, or in connection with, or relating in any way to the Admission Agreement (including, the validity, interpretation, construction, performance and enforcement thereof) or any service or goods provided by the Facility to the Resident, or which allege violations of the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act, the Unfair Competition Law, the False Advertising Law, the Consumer Legal Remedies Act, or which seek an award of treble damages, punitive damages or attorneys’ fees. (See Declaration of Raniyah Thomas (“Thomas Decl.”), Exhibit A, ¶ 1.1, pg. 1.)

 

1.     Existence of Valid Arbitration Agreement and Claims Covered by Arbitration Clause¿ 

 

“[T]he petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence . . . .” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.) “In determining whether an arbitration agreement applies to a specific dispute, the court may examine only the agreement itself and the complaint filed by the party refusing arbitration [citation]. The court should attempt to give effect to the parties’ intentions, in light of the usual and ordinary meaning of the contractual language and the circumstances under which the agreement was made.” (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.) “To determine whether a contractual arbitration clause requires arbitration of a particular controversy, the controversy is first identified, and the issue is whether that controversy is within the scope of the contractual arbitration clause.” (Titolo v. Cano (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 310, 316.) “Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration. The court should order them to arbitrate unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.” (California Correctional Peace Officers Ass’n v. State (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205.) 

 

[A] party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense. [Citation.] In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination. (Giuliano, supra, 149 Cal.App.4th at p. 1284.) 

 

 Defendant argues that the Arbitration Agreement (the Agreement) should be interpreted broadly, and that arbitration should be ordered unless it is explicitly clear that the agreement does not apply to the dispute. (Wickes v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.) Defendant also cites the specific language of the Agreement, which covers any disputes related to medical malpractice and any claims connected to or arising from the Admission Agreement or services provided. (Thomas Decl. Exhibit A.)

 

Defendant further supports its position with case law emphasizing the broad interpretation of arbitration agreements. (See, Wagner Construction Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 26.) Finally, Defendant argues that once the existence of an arbitration agreement is established, the burden shifts to the opposing party to prove it is unenforceable. (Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219.) Based on these principles, Defendant argues that the Agreement in question applies to the dispute and requires the matter to be settled in arbitration.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant has not met the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement, as Frank did not sign any such contract. (Garrison v. Superior Court (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 253, 263.) The party seeking to compel arbitration must prove that a valid arbitration agreement exists, and generally, a person not party to an arbitration agreement cannot be bound by it. (Flores v. Evergreen at San Diego, LLC (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 581.)

 

Plaintiffs assert that Defendant ignores the key issue that Frank never signed the Agreement, and that no valid agency was established that could bind him to the arbitration. An agency relationship requires actions by the principal, not just the agent. (Garcia v. KND Dev. 52, LLC (2020) 58 Cal. App. 5th 736; see also Civ. Code, § 2315.) In the instant case, Frank did not confer any authority to Menuét, who signed the agreement as a "responsible party." Additionally, Frank’s DPOA explicitly excluded healthcare decisions, further undermining any claims of agency. (Sitar Decl. Exhibit A.)

 

Plaintiffs point to case law, where courts have found no agency or contract in similar circumstances. (Pagarigan v. Libby Care Center, Inc. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 298; Yvonne Warfield v Summerville Senior Living, Inc (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 443.) Plaintiffs argue that Defendant has presented no evidence to show that Frank conferred agency to Menuét or that an arbitration agreement exists with him. Therefore, Plaintiffs contend that arbitration cannot be compelled.

 

In reply, Defendant argues that although Plaintiffs claim Menuét lacked the authority to enter into the Agreement under the DPOA due to its exclusion of healthcare decisions, this exclusion is irrelevant to the case. (Sitar Decl. Exhibit A.) The DPOA did not grant authority for healthcare decisions, but it did grant broad powers to Ms. Adams, including authority over "claims and litigation."(Sitar Decl. Exhibit A). This authority, Defendant argues, was sufficient for her to execute the Agreement on behalf of Mr. Adams.

 

Defendant further asserts that the scope of the DPOA, which included handling claims and litigation, was broad and comprehensive enough to cover the signing of contracts such as the Agreement. Defendant asserts that the California Supreme Court has found that if there is a broad enough DPOA, it can bind parties to Arbitration. (See Harrod v. Country Oaks Partners, LLC (2024) 15 Cal.5th 939, 966.)

 

In Hutcheson v. Eskaton Fountain Wood Lodge (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 937, the Court found that admission of a resident to a nursing home and signing an arbitration agreement as part of the intake process was a healthcare decision. (Hutcheson, 17 Cal.App.5th 937, 957.) Therefore, because the agent in Hutcheson did not have power of attorney for healthcare decisions, the arbitration agreement was invalid. (See, id.)

 

In Harrod v. Country Oaks Partners, LLC (2024) 15 Cal.5th 939, the Supreme Court of California found that the meaning of “health care decision” does not include the power to enter into optional, separate arbitration agreements.” (Harrod, supra, 15 Cal.5th 939, 965.) Therefore, Defendant argues that Menuét had the power to enter into the arbitration agreement, which Defendant asserts was optional, based on the DPOA, which granted Menuét power over all claims and litigation. (See id.; Sitar Decl. Exhibit A.) However, in Harrod, the Supreme Court referred to Hutcheson, stating, “We have no occasion to address Hutcheson’s ultimate concern: whether an agent with power over claims and litigation, but without power over health care decisions, may agree to arbitration with a health care facility with whom the agent had no right to contract for services in the first instance.” (Harrod, supra, 15 Cal.5th 939, Fn. 7.) 

 

Here, the DPOA signed by Frank explicitly does not grant power to his agent over healthcare decisions. (Sitar Decl. Exhibit A.) Therefore, because Menuét did not have the authority to make healthcare decisions for Frank, she also did not have the power to sign the admissions agreement on his behalf, as supported by Hutcheson and Harrod. As a result, she lacked the ability to bind Frank to an arbitration agreement related to his admission to Las Flores Convalescent Hospital pursuant to Hutcheson and Harrod. (See, Hutcheson, supra, 17 Cal.App.5th 937, 957; Harrod, supra, 15 Cal.5th 939, Fn. 7.) Thus, Defendant has failed to show the existence of a valid arbitration agreement. (Giuliano, 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.)

 

      The Court denies the Motion to Compel Arbitration.
