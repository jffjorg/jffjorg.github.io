DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies). 


Case Number: 23CHCV00319    Hearing Date: January 2, 2025    Dept: F49

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT F49

 

JANUARY 3, 2025

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23CHCV00319

 

Motion filed: 8/8/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Andriy Ishuninov 

RESPONDING PARTY: None.

NOTICE: OK.¿¿¿ 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order granting Defendant Andriy Ishuninov’s Motion to Strike to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint filed on July 18, 2024.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

This action arises from an alleged breach of a residential lease agreement by Defendant’s failure to provide conditions necessary for human habitation. (FAC ¶ 16.)

 

On February 3, 2023, Plaintiffs Michele Temm (“Michele”) and Peyton Temm, a minor, through her guardian ad litem Michele (“Peyton”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) initiated the action by filing a Complaint against Defendant Andriy Ishuninov (“Ishuninov” or “Defendant”). The Complaint alleged six causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract/Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment/Warranty of Habitability, (2) Tortious Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability, (3) Negligence, (4) Private Nuisance, (5) Violation of Unfair Business Practices, and (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), rendering moot the demurrer and motion to strike, filed by Defendant on May 29, 2024. The FAC asserts five causes of action against Defendant and Does 1 through 20: (1) Breach of Contract/Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment/Warranty of Habitability, (2) Tortious Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability, (3) Negligence, (4) Private Nuisance, and (5) Violation of Unfair Business Practices.

 

On August 8, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Strike (the “Motion”), seeking to strike portions of the FAC related to allegations and requests for relief for punitive damages.

 

No Opposition or Reply papers have been received by the Court.

 

ANALYSIS

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

A. Motion to Strike

 

            Defendant moves to strike the following portions of the FAC as they pertain to allegations and prayer for relief for punitive damages:

 

1.      Paragraph 42: “[T]he Defendants’ conduct was, based on information and belief, willful and intentional, done with oppression and malice against Plaintiffs, and undertaken with a conscious disregard to plaintiffs’ rights.” (FAC, p. 8:14-16.)

 

2.      Paragraph 43: “The Defendants’ conduct under these circumstances is despicable and such behavior warrants the imposition of punitive damages in a sum appropriate to punish the Defendants and to deter future similar misconduct.” (FAC, p. 8:17-19.)

 

3.      Paragraph 52: “Defendants’ conduct described above was willful, wanton, intentional, despicable, malicious, and initiated with malice and with the intent to knowingly take advantage of, oppress, and injure Plaintiffs. Defendants at all times acted with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of safety of Plaintiffs. Defendants were at all times aware that there was a high probability that their intentional and/or negligent failure to repair and maintain the Subject Property would injure Plaintiffs and cause them personal injury, emotional distress, and property damage. Plaintiffs are therefore entitled to an award of punitive and exemplary damages under and pursuant to Civil Code, Section 3294 and Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890.” (FAC, p. 10:8-16.)

 

4.      Paragraph 66: “The Defendants’ conduct warrants an award of punitive damages because the Defendants’ actions were willful, malicious and oppressive. The Defendants knew that dangerous conditions existed on the Subject Property, had the financial ability to fix the conditions, but did nothing to repair and remediate the dangerous conditions, despite their legal obligation to do so. Further, Plaintiffs are informed, believe and thereon allege that Defendants sought to punish the Plaintiffs for seeking redress about the physical condition of the Subject Property. The conduct of the Defendants under these circumstances was despicable and carried out with willful and conscious disregard of the rights and safety of Plaintiffs, which behavior warrants the imposition of punitive damages in a sum appropriate to punish the Defendants and to deter future similar misconduct.” (FAC, p. 12:7-16.)

 

5.      Prayer for Relief No. 4: “For exemplary and punitive damages according to proof[.]” (FAC, p. 14:13.)

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiffs’ allegations are conclusory and fail to present specific facts sufficient to support a claim of punitive damages under California law.

 

(1)    Punitive Damages

 

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “‘Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.’ [Citation.]” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210.)

 

“Malice” is conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) of Civil Code as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”

 

“As amended to include [despicable], the [Civil Code section 3294] plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests. The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.) The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.” (Ibid.) Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’” (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.) Further, “[t]here must be evidence that defendant acted with knowledge of the probable dangerous consequences to plaintiff’s interests and deliberately failed to avoid these consequences.” (Flyer’s Body Shop Profit Sharing Plan v. Ticor Title Ins. Co. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 1149, 1155.)

 

A motion to strike punitive damages is properly granted where a plaintiff does not state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, including allegations that defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Mere negligence, even gross negligence, is not sufficient to justify such an award” for punitive damages. (Kendall Yacht Corp. v. United California Bank (1975) 50 Cal.App.3d 949, 958.)

 

Defendant contends that the FAC contains no allegations of “fraud.” Additionally, while the FAC includes allegations of malice and oppression, Defendant argues that these claims are speculative and conclusory, lacking specific factual support to substantiate the punitive damages claim. (Mot. at p. 8.)

 

The Court observes that “[in] ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) However, the court does not assume the truth of conclusions of fact or law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Therefore, the Court examines not only the specific portions of the FAC targeted by Defendant’s Motion but also the factual allegations underlying the broader claim for general damages.

 

The FAC alleges that on or around November 29, 2020, Plaintiff Michele entered into a written lease agreement with Defendant for the premises located at 24585 Town Center Dr., Unit 4304, Santa Clarita, CA 91355 (the “Subject Property”). (FAC ¶¶ 4, 11.) On or around December 31, 2020, a water tank above the Subject Property broke, causing major flooding in Plaintiffs’ unit. (Id. ¶ 19.) Plaintiffs moved out of the Subject Property on January 7, 2021, after reaching an agreement with Defendant to pay a reduced rental until the Subject Property was repaired and remediated. (Id. ¶¶ 20, 21.) On February 3, 2021, Plaintiffs returned to the Subject Property, believing that remediation was completed. (Id. ¶ 21.) However, on February 4, 2021, Plaintiffs woke up disoriented, vomiting, and had diarrhea. Plaintiffs immediately vacated the Subject Property again and relocated to a hotel. (Ibid.) Subsequently, Plaintiffs informed Defendant that the issues remained unresolved and obtained a mold inspection result confirming the presence of mold at the Subject Property on or around February 10, 2021. (Id. ¶ 22.)

 

The FAC asserts that Defendant was aware of the uninhabitable conditions, had the means to make repairs, and purposefully failed to remedy known issues such as mold, and retaliated against Plaintiffs, resulting in Plaintiffs being ultimately forced to vacate on or around March 3, 2021. (Id. ¶¶ 25, 26.)

 

The Court has reviewed the allegations of the FAC and concludes that it does not plead ultimate facts that would establish malice or oppression. Notably, the FAC does not allege that Defendant owns the unit above the Subject Property or had prior knowledge of any preexisting conditions that caused the water damages. Even assuming the truth of the allegation that Defendant failed to remediate the Subject Property by March 3, 2021, the claims, when viewed in their entirety, do not demonstrate "despicable" conduct with the intent to harm or a willful and conscious disregard for Plaintiffs’ rights, as required to support a claim for punitive damages under California Civil Code § 3294.

 

Furthermore, Plaintiffs have not filed an Opposition to the Motion, effectively waiving any responses to the issues raised by Defendant.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the unopposed Motion to Strike.

 

B. Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend is generally available when “the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure . . . to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) However, the burden of proving the existence of a reasonable possibility to cure the defect by amendment is squarely on the plaintiff. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs have failed to file an Opposition or present any argument that the identified defects could be cured with reasonable possibility through further amendment. Consequently, Plaintiffs have not met their burden to justify leave to amend

 

Accordingly, the Motion is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Andriy Ishuninov’s Motion to Strike portions of the First Amended Complaint is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Arlene Aleman

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order sustaining Defendant Providence Holy Cross Medical Center’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The Demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff Arlene Aleman (“Plaintiff” or “Aleman”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Providence Holy Cross Medical Center, Audley Osbourne, M.D. (“Osbourne”), Brandon Hawkins, M.D., Brian Lee, M.D. (“Lee”), Andrew Behesnilian, M.D. (“Behesnilian”), Henry Shih, M.D. (“Shih”), and Does 1 to 20, alleging two causes of action: (1) Medical Malpractice, and (2) Medical Battery. On August 23, 2024, Plaintiff submitted a form amendment to the Complaint, correcting the name of Defendant Brandon Hawkins, M.D. to John Hawkins, M.D. (“Hawkins”). Subsequently, Defendant Lee filed his Answer to the Complaint on October 9, 2024.

 

On October 22, 2024, Defendant Providence Health System – Southern California, d/b/a Providence Holy Cross Medical Center (sued and served as Providence Holy Cross Medical Center) (“Providence”) filed the instant Demurrer (the “Demurrer”).

 

On November 19, 2024, the Court overruled a demurrer filed by Defendants Osbourne and Behesnilian on October 17, 2024. Subsequently, on December 18, 2024, Defendants Osbourne and Behesnilian filed their Answer to the Complaint.

 

On December 11, 2024, Plaintiff Aleman filed an Opposition to the Demurrer, and Providence replied on December 24, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.)

 

At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against them. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721 (Semole.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law[.]” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525 (Berkley).) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

A party filing a demurrer “shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) However, failure to meet and confer does not constitute grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)

 

Here, Providence’s counsel, Megan P. Best (“Best”), stated in her delcaration that on October 1, 2024, she sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel, Shirley Kenninger (“Kenninger”), outlining the grounds for the Demurrer and requesting dismissal of the battery cause of action. (Best Decl. ¶ 2.) On October 11, 2024, Best and Kenninger had a telephonic discussion regarding the meet and confer letter. During the discussion, Kenninger requested additional time to review the issues and granted Providence a one-week extension, until October 21, 2024, to respond to Plaintiff’s Complaint. (Id. ¶ 3.) On October 21, 2024, Kenninger informed Best that the battery cause of action would remain unchanged. (Ibid.)

 

Based on the above declaration, the Court finds that Providence has satisfied the requirements for an in-person or telephonic meet and confer, as mandated by the Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a).

 

B.     Second Cause of Action- Medical Battery

 

“There are three elements to a claim for medical battery under a violation of conditional consent: the patient must show his consent was conditional; the doctor intentionally violated the condition while providing treatment; and the patient suffered harm as a result of the doctor's violation of the condition. [Citation.]” (Conte v. Girard Orthopedic Surgeons Medical Group, Inc. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1269.) Further, “[w]here a doctor obtains consent of the patient to perform one type of treatment and subsequently performs a substantially different treatment for which consent was not obtained, there is a clear case of battery.” (Cobbs v. Grant (1972) 8 Cal.3d 229, 239 (Cobbs).)

 

Providence contends that the Second Cause of Action is subject to demurrer because Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to state the cause of action. (Dem. at p. 4.) Specifically, Providence argues that the Demurrer should be sustained on two grounds: (1) there are not any allegation regarding Providence in the battery causes of action, and (2) the battery cause of action merely duplicates the first cause of action for medical negligence.

 

The Court finds these arguments unpersuasive and will address each in turn.

 

(1)   Providence’s Liability for Medical Battery

 

“[A] nurse or physician may be the servant of a hospital, thus requiring the application of the doctrine of respondeat superior even though they are performing professional acts.” (Rice v. California Lutheran Hospital (1945) 27 Cal.2d 296, 304.) Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, employer may be vicariously liable for employee's torts committed within scope of employment. (Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons, Inc. (1986) 41 Cal.3d 962, 967; cf. Mayers v. Litow (1957) 154 Cal.App.2d 413, 418 [hospital not liable for acts of doctor who was an independent contractor].)

 

In California, courts assume that a physician appears to be an agent of the hospital. (Whitlow v. Rideout Memorial Hospital (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 631, 637 (Whitlow).) Civil Code section 2295 provides: “[a]n agent is one who represents another, called the principal, in dealings with third persons.” Agency may be “either actual or ostensible.” (Civ. Code, § 2298.) Actual agency occurs “when the agent is really employed by the principal.” (Civ. Code, § 2299.) Ostensible agency is found where “the principal intentionally, or by want of ordinary care, causes a third person to believe another to be his agent who is not really employed by him.” (Civ. Code, § 2300.)

 

Here, Providence highlights the definition of medical battery, stating that “battery is an intentional tort that occurs when a doctor performs a procedure without obtaining any consent,” citing Saxena v. Goffney (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 316, 324. (Dem. at p. 5.) Providence further asserts, without specificity, that Plaintiff has failed to allege any facts demonstrating that Providence committed medical battery. (Ibid.) In its Reply, Providence reiterates that “it is the duty of the physician ... to obtain the informed consent ... [and] to disclose to patient material information ... There is no cause of action for battery against Providence.” (Reply at p. 2.) Providence appears to argue that, as an entity rather than a natural person, it does not practice medicine and consequently, cannot be held liable for medical battery.

 

In the Opposition, Plaintiff argues that Providence is liable for Osbourne’s alleged medical battery due to the agency relationship between the two defendants. (Opp’n. at p. 4.) Plaintiff asserts that Providence was the ostensible agent of Defendant Osbourne, who allegedly committed medical battery. (Opp’n. at p. 4.) Additionally, Plaintiff claims that Defendant Osbourne relied on Providence to ensure the proper functioning of the equipment available to the doctors. (Ibid.)

 

The Court notes that, in the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Osbourne committed a medical battery when Osbourne went beyond the scope of consent given him by the plaintiff, Arlene Aleman. (Compl. ¶ 14.) Plaintiff further alleges that “Audley Osbourne MD, Brandon Hawkins MD, Brian Lee MD, Andrew Behesnilian MD, Henry Shih MD, were the agents, servants or employees of defendant Providence Holy Cross Medical Center.” (Id. ¶ 5.)

 

As observed in Whitlow, it is reasonable for a patient to assume that a doctor is an agent of the hospital. (Whitlow, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at p. 637.) Additionally, the Court finds that actual agency through employment, as defined under Civil Code section 2299, is sufficiently alleged by Plaintiff’s assertion that Defendant Osbourne and other doctors “were the agents, servants or employee of defendant [Providence].” (Compl. ¶ 5.)

 

Conversely, Providence’s Demurrer and Reply fail to engage with the legal analysis for potential liability under the doctrine of respondeat superior, thereby neglecting to address and refute a viable legal theory capable of withstanding its Demurrer.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds Providence’s argument unpersuasive. Under either actual or ostensible agency theories, the Court concludes that Plaintiff has alleged ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the Providence of the factual basis for the claim against them. (Semole, supra, 28 Cal.App.3d at p. 721.)

 

Therefore, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer, rejecting Providence’s first basis as grounds to sustain it.

 

(2)   Medical Battery Asserted as Duplicative of Medical Malpractice Claim

 

As noted in Providence’s Reply, in the seminal case of Cobbs, supra, 8 Cal.3d 229, the Supreme Court delineated the distinctions between the two causes of action, stating:

 

 “The battery theory should be reserved for those circumstances when a doctor performs an operation to which the patient has not consented. When the patient gives permission to perform one type of treatment and the doctor performs another, the requisite element of deliberate intent to deviate from the consent given is present. However, when the patient consents to certain treatment and the doctor performs that treatment but an undisclosed inherent complication with a low probability occurs, no intentional deviation from the consent given appears; rather, the doctor in obtaining consent may have failed to meet his due care duty to disclose pertinent information. In that situation the action should be pleaded in negligence.”

 

(Id. at pp. 240-241.)

 

In Cobbs, the Supreme Court held the facts supported a medical negligence case, reasoning that “[d]efendant performed the identical operation to which plaintiff had consented. The spleen injury, development of the gastric ulcer, gastrectomy and internal bleeding as a result of the premature absorption of a suture, were all links in a chain of low probability events inherent in the initial operation.” (Cobbs, supra, 8 Cal.3d at p. 241.)

 

However, the Court distinguishes Cobbs. Here, Plaintiff alleges that “[t]he fact is the Plaintiff consented to a medical procedure, on the condition that it was going to be done with robotic equipment and the Defendant [Osbourne] proceeded to attempt to remove the kidney stone a second time by placing another tube in the Plaintiff while she was sedated. This occurred when the first attempted procedure could not be completed due to the broken lens on the robotic equipment. Defendant [Osbourne] knew that the robotic equipment could not be used after he had attempted to use it only to discover the lens was broken so he then proceeded to attempt to remove the stone by putting in another tube which was unsuccessful.” (Id. ¶ 15.) “... Plaintiff was harmed by these actions and Defendant Audley Osbourne MDs’ conduct was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff Arlene Aleman’s harm...” (Id. ¶ 16.)

 

When construing the allegation liberally and assuming the facts as true, as required in reviewing a demurrer, the Court finds that the allegations support a reasonable inference that Defendant Osbourne did not perform the identical operation to which Plaintiff had consented. Plaintiff’s consent was conditioned upon the functional use of the robotic equipment. However, Defendant “[Osbourne] knew that the robotic equipment could not be used after he had attempted to use it only to discover the lens was broken so he then proceeded to attempt to remove the stone by putting in another tube which was unsuccessful.” (Compl. ¶ 15.)

 

Furthermore, the Court does not consider Providence’s incomplete argument that Plaintiff’s alleged injury was “[an] undisclosed inherent complication with a low probability,” which implicates a medical negligence cause of action. As previously explained, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged that Defendant Osbourne did not perform the identical operation to which Plaintiff had consented. Any disputes raised by Providence regarding the factual allegation or its assertions of additional facts outside the scope of the Complaint are not appropriate for consideration at this stage of the proceedings.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that the above factual allegations illustrate an action that sounds in medical battery. (See Berkey v. Anderson (1969) 1 Cal.App.3d 790 [the actual procedure was more complicated than the procedure to which plaintiff had previously consented]; Bang v. Charles T. Miller Hosp. (1958) 251 Minn. 427 [the actual procedural was a substantially different treatment when the plaintiff consented to a prostate resection but was uninformed that the procedure involved tying off his sperm ducts.])

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s allegations in the Complaint sufficiently state a cause of action for medical battery against Defendant Providence under the doctrine of respondeat superior.  The Court concludes that Providence has failed to meet its required burden to show that the Second Cause of Action is insufficiently stated.

 

            Therefore, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer on this ground.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Providence Health System – Southern California, d/b/a Providence Holy Cross Medical Center’s Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant Providence Health System – Southern California, d/b/a Providence Holy Cross Medical Center is ordered to serve and file its Answer to the Complaint within 30 days.

 

Moving party to give notice.
