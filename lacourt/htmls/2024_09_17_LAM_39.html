DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV37369    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 17, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV37369

MOTION: 

Motion for Protective Order

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant and Cross-Complainant Lisa Marie Vernola Salas, individually and as trustee of the Vernola Family Trust

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff Urban Soccer 5 Center LLC

 

MOTION

            Defendant and Cross-Complainant Lisa Marie Vernola Salas, individually and as trustee of the Vernola Family Trust (“Defendant”) moves to for a protective order against Plaintiff Urban Soccer 5 Center LLC (“Plaintiff”).  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Defendant seeks a protective order that: “Plaintiff . . . shall not demand, nor file any Motion for Further Responses against [Defendant] to the Set One Form Interrogatories, Set One Special Interrogatories, Set One Request to Produce, and Set One Request for Admissions. [¶] that [Plaintiff] shall not propound additional written discovery to [Defendant] on top of that already covered in the Set One Discovery Requests that total 297 questions . . . ; [¶] that [Plaintiff’s] counsel shall not in any Meet & Confer and/or correspondence continue to accuse Charles A. Mollis of ‘deliberate’ misconduct alleging ‘deliberate’ violation of the Discovery statutes . . . ; [¶] that any Further written discovery by Plaintiff shall be approved by a Discovery Referee under Code of Civil Procedure § 639, with the cost of said Referee to be borne [by] Plaintiff . . . ; [¶] the Discovery Referee shall be appointed by the Court, and that said Referee be a Retired Judge from either JAMS or ADR Services, Inc. as the Court deems suitable for this case . . . .” 

As a basis for this motion, Defendant cites Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010, 2030.090, and 2031.060.  The Court cannot grant relief that Plaintiff fails to specify in the notice of motion, or relief on bases other than those set forth in the notice of motion.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1010; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110, subd. (a); People v. American Sur. Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 719, 726.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 sets forth conduct that is subject to discovery sanctions.  It does not concern protective orders.  Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.090 permits the Court to enter a protective order regarding a set of interrogatories that “have been propounded . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.090.)  It does not permit the Court to enjoin Plaintiff from bringing discovery motions, or to prohibit Plaintiff from propounding hypothetical future discovery.   Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.060 permits the Court to enter a protective order regarding a set of document requests that “have been demanded . . . .”  It likewise does not permit the Court to enjoin Plaintiff from making discovery motions or to prohibit Plaintiff from propounding hypothetical future discovery.  Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for protective order is denied.

In the alternative, Defendant requests the Court appoint a discovery referee.  The Court denies this request.  Code of Civil Procedure section 639 provides “[w]hen the parties do not consent, the court may, upon the written motion of any party, or of its own motion, appoint a [discovery] referee” under certain circumstances, including “(5) When the court in any pending action determines that it is necessary for the court to appoint a referee to hear and determine any and all discovery motions and disputes relevant to discovery in the action and to report findings and make a recommendation thereon.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 639, subd. (a)(5).)  An order appointing a discovery referee must include “the exceptional circumstances requiring the reference, which must be specific to the circumstances of the particular case.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 639, subd. (d)(2).)  

The Court cannot appoint a discovery referee to oversee routine matters of discovery.  (Hood v. Superior Court (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 446, 449, fn. 4.)  The Court is not convinced this discovery dispute is extraordinary so as to justify the appointment of a discovery referee.  Rather, the evidence before the Court is of a typical discovery dispute between counsel who are not cooperating to complete discovery.  Therefore, the Court will rule on any pending discovery motions put before it and impose sanctions as appropriate.

The Court declines Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions in connection with the opposition to this motion.  The Court finds Plaintiff’s counsel failed to engage with Defendant’s counsel to informally resolve the discovery dispute that precipitated this motion.  Instead, Plaintiff’s counsel sent voluminous letters to Defendant’s counsel, which repeated the same arguments, verbatim.  When Defendant’s counsel requested to confer by telephone to better understand the dispute, Plaintiff’s counsel refused to do so unless Defendant’s counsel provided written statements in advance.  (Compendium of Evidence in Support of Motion for Protective Order, Exhibits J through O.) 

As such, the Court concludes Plaintiff’s counsel failed to meaningfully participate in the attempt to resolve this matter, which makes an award of sanctions unjust.  (Code of Civil Procedure, §§ 2033.080, subd. (d), 2031.060, subd. (h), 2030.090, subd. (d).)

Defendant is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 23STCV02667A    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 17, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV02667A

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Starbucks Corporation

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Princess Hodges

  

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Princess Hodges (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant Starbucks Corporation (“Defendant”) for sex discrimination and sexual harassment.  Plaintiff alleges her shift supervisor, Defendant Jamilh Roque (“Roque”), sexually harassed her.  Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative second amended complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action therein.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

EVIDENCE

            Plaintiff’s and Defendant’s evidentiary objections are overruled.

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Discrimination

            Plaintiff asserts causes of action for discrimination and retaliation under the Fair Employment and Housing Act, or FEHA.  To state a claim for discrimination under FEHA, the employee must demonstrate “(1) the employee's membership in a classification protected by the statute; (2) discriminatory animus on the part of the employer toward members of that classification; (3) an action by the employer adverse to the employee's interests; (4) a causal link between the discriminatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damage to the employee; and (6) a causal link between the adverse action and the damage.”  (Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  “A defending employer seeking summary judgment in a discrimination case may meet its burden by showing that one or more of these prima facie elements is lacking, or that the adverse employment action was based on legitimate nondiscriminatory factors.  [¶]  If the employer has met its burden by showing a legitimate reason for its conduct, the employee must demonstrate a triable issue by producing substantial evidence that the employer's stated reasons were untrue or pretextual, or that the employer acted with a discriminatory animus, such that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude that the employer engaged in intentional discrimination or other unlawful action.”  (Cucuzza v. City of Santa Clara (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1038, internal citation omitted.) 

In her operative complaint, Plaintiff alleges Defendant discriminated against her by “[d]ischarging, barring, refusing to transfer, retain, hire, select, and/or employ . . . in whole or in part on the basis of plaintiff’s sex.” (Second Amended Complaint, ¶ 17.)  The pleadings set the outer limits of materiality in a summary judgment proceeding.  (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382.)   Defendant advances the testimony of the manager of the store where Plaintiff worked, Denzell Jones (“Jones”), who states Plaintiff resigned her employment with Defendant via a text message to him.  (Declaration of Denzell Jones, ¶ 5.)  Jones authenticates the text messages he exchanged with Plaintiff in connection with her resignation.  (Ibid.)  In those messages, Plaintiff states Roque “came up to [Plaintiff] three times and rubbed ice on the exposed skin of [Plaintiff’s] neck and back until it melted despite [Plaintiff’s] obvious discomfort,” and Roque “has a habit of sneaking behind [Plaintiff] when [Plaintiff is] alone in the back room any saying ‘hey’ very closely to [Plaintiff’s] ear to startle [Plaintiff] which would be less weird had he not physically touched me the way he did today . . . .”  (Jones Decl., Exhibit A.) 

As defendants relate, at her deposition, Plaintiff testified Roque never mentioned Plaintiff’s gender.  (Declaration of Brett Young, Exhibit E, p. 105), and Plaintiff did not inform anyone else at the store Roque was scaring her (id., pp. 66, 76.)  In fact, Plaintiff did not inform Defendant of Rogue’s alleged harassment until after she had resigned.  Taken together, however, Defendant’s evidence demonstrates Defendant did not take any discriminatory actions against Plaintiff.  Defendant met its burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on her cause of action for discrimination.  Thus, Defendant shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her claim for discrimination.  In opposition, Plaintiff advances no evidence that Defendant discriminated against her by discharging her, refusing to transfer her, or failing to retain her as an employee because of her gender or sex.

In opposing the motion, Plaintiff presents her deposition testimony, in which she relates she resigned “[b]ecause it had become too distressing for [Plaintiff’s] emotional and mental health and [Plaintiff] didn't see any changes happening after [Plaintiff] brought the situation up to [Plaintiff’s] manager”  (Plaintiff Princess Hodges’ Evidence in Opposition to Defendant Starbucks Corporation Motion for Summary Adjudication [Sic] or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication (Plaintiff’s Evidence in Opposition), Exhibit 1 (Excerpts from Deposition of Princess Hodges), p. 62); the workplace was too distressing for Plaintiff because of harassment by Roque, as Roque would “would wait until [Plaintiff] was alone in the backroom on [her] break and he would sneak up behind [Plaintiff] and scare [Plaintiff], either say [Plaintiff’s] name or say, like, ‘Boo,’ or, like, ‘Hey’ or something.  And he would do it so close to the point where [Plaintiff] could feel his breath against [Plaintiff’s] ear. And often [Plaintiff] would, like, jump  back.  And so [their] bodies would sort of collide in that situation. And that would occur every shift that [Plaintiff] had with Jamilh. At minimum, one time per shift, but it would often happen more than one time” (id., pp. 63-64); Plaintiff worked with Roque for a total of 10 to 15 shifts (id., p. 70); Plaintiff believes Defendant discriminated against Plaintiff because of “those patronizing and demeaning comments that the male employees only directed towards [Plaintiff]” (id., p. 130); and Roque and Tristan discriminated against her because of her gender or sex (id., p. 131.)

Plaintiff argues she was constructively discharged.  However, as set forth above, Plaintiff admittedly did not tell Defendant about Roque’s alleged harassment until she resigned.  As such, Defendant could not have constructively discharged Plaintiff when she deprived Defendant of the opportunity to act on Roque’s alleged conduct at the time she informed Defendant of such conduct.  Likewise, Plaintiff’s argument Defendant failed to adequately investigate Plaintiff’s allegations against Roque is unavailing when she made no allegations against Roque until she resigned.  As such, any investigation Defendant conducted of Plaintiff’s allegations is irrelevant to Plaintiff’s claims.  Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her cause of action for discrimination. 

Second Cause of Action: Sexual Harassment

            Under FEHA, an employer is strictly liable for a supervisor’s harassment of a subordinate.  (State Dept. of Health Services v. Superior Court (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1026, 1042.)  For purposes of FEHA, a supervisor is anyone with authority to “hire, transfer, suspend, lay off, recall, promote, discharge, assign, reward, or discipline other employees, or the responsibility to direct them, or to adjust their grievances, or effectively to recommend that action, if, in connection with the foregoing, the exercise of that authority is not of a merely routine or clerical nature, but requires the use of independent judgment.”  (Gov. Code, § 12926, subd. (t).)  A lead worker who has responsibility to direct an employee's day-to-day duties may be a supervisor under FEHA.  (Chapman v. Enos (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 920, 930-931.)

In support of its motion, Defendant presents Roque’s declaration wherein he states, as a shift supervisor, he “could not hire, fire, demote, transfer, lay off, promote, discharge, suspend, or reward other partners.  . . .  Instead, [Roque’s] position at Starbucks required [Roque to] run the ‘daily play’ that was created either by [Roque] or . . . Jones.  In this capacity, it was [Roque’s] job to ensure partners were performing their various duties to Starbucks expectations, were working at the proper deployment or location in the store, and oversee shift operations during [Roque’s] work time.”  (Roque Decl., ¶ 3.)  Roque does not address what overseeing shift operations meant when Roque worked for Defendant.  Roque’s testimony does not show he did not direct the day-to-day duties of Plaintiff and other employees.  As such, this evidence does not meet Defendant’s burden to show it is not strictly liable for Roque’s alleged harassment of Plaintiff. 

However, as set forth above, Plaintiff admitted at deposition that Roque did not mention Plaintiff’s sex in his inappropriate comments to Plaintiff.  This evidence meets Defendant’s burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claim for sexual harassment against Defendant, because Plaintiff did not experience harassment based on her gender or sex.  Defendant shifts the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her cause of action for sexual harassment.

In opposition, Plaintiff advances her own deposition testimony.  At deposition, Plaintiff testified she perceived Roque’s harassment as being sexually motivated because “of the tone of voice that he would use when he would scare [Plaintiff] and the whole physical touching situation.”  (Plaintiff’s Evidence in Opposition, Exhibit 1, p. 69.)  Plaintiff also testified Roque was patronizing, and she could not say this was explicitly related to her gender or sex; but she did not witness similar comments being made to male employees.  (Id., Exhibit 1, p. 102.)  Although this suggests Roque’s behavior may have been based on Plaintiff’s sex, Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Roque’s behavior was “sufficiently severe or pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create a work environment that qualifies as hostile or abusive.”  (Dickson v. Burke Williams, Inc. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 1307, 1314.)  Plaintiff fails to advance evidence of harassment by Roque that would have “seriously affected the psychological well-being of a reasonable employee . . . .”  (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 610.)  Roque’s behavior, while unprofessional, was not so severe or persuasive as to create an abusive work environment.  “FEHA's prohibitions are not a ‘civility code’ . . . .”  (Sheffield v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Social Services (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 153, 161.) 

Plaintiff cites the so-called eggshell plaintiff rule and argues Plaintiff is more susceptible to psychological distress from harassment than the average person.  (See Rideau v. Los Angeles Transit Lines (1954) 124 Cal.App.2d 466, 471.)  However, Plaintiff cites no authority for the proposition this tenet of tort law that pertains to preexisting physical conditions applies to claims for emotional distress in statutory causes of action for sexual harassment under FEHA.  Likewise, while Jones suggested at his deposition Roque’s behavior, as described by Plaintiff,  violated Defendant’s sexual harassment policy, that does not make Roque’s behavior actionable under FEHA.  (See, e.g., Plaintiff’s Evidence in Opposition, Exhibit 4 (Excerpts from Deposition of Denzell Jones), pp. 68-70.) 

Plaintiff also advances Roque’s deposition testimony.  Roque testified at deposition it was his job to ensure the store ran smoothly during his shifts and to ensure employees he supervised took breaks.  (Plaintiff’s Evidence in Opposition, Exhibit 2, pp. 35-36.)  Roque also testified he trained the employees he supervised on how to make drinks or clean the machines, as well as how to multitask between warming food and operating the register.  (Id., Exhibit 2, p. 37.)  Again, as set forth above, triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Roque supervised Plaintiff.  However, that is immaterial, as Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Roque’s alleged harassment of Plaintiff was actionable.

Third Cause of Action: Retaliation

To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must establish “by a preponderance of the evidence, that retaliation for an employee's protected activities was a contributing factor in a contested employment action.  . . .  Once the plaintiff has made the required showing, the burden shifts to the employer to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.”  (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 718.) 

At deposition, Plaintiff testified “[t]here wasn’t really an opportunity to retaliate against [Plaintiff]” because Plaintiff resigned her employment with Defendant.  (Young Decl., Exhibit E, pp. 137-138.)  This evidence meets Defendant’s burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claim for retaliation against Defendant, because Plaintiff did not experience retaliation based on sex.  Defendant shifts the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her cause of action for retaliation.  In opposition, Plaintiff fails to advance any evidence Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff.  Thus, Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her claim for retaliation.

Fourth Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment, and Retaliation

Plaintiff’s claim for failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation is derivative of her claims for discrimination, harassment, and retaliation.  As Defendant met its burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on those claims, Defendant likewise met its burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on the cause of action for failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation.  As Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact exist as to the underlying causes of action, Plaintiff’s claims for failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation likewise fail.  (Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 288.) 

Fifth Cause of Action: Wrongful Constructive Termination

California law provides “a tort remedy when an employer's discharge of an employee contravenes the dictates of public policy.” (Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 167, 177.)  As set forth above, Defendant advances evidence Plaintiff resigned before informing Defendant Roque had harassed her.  As such, Defendant meets its burden to show it did not constructively discharge Plaintiff, because Defendant was unaware of any issue with Plaintiff’s employment until Plaintiff resigned.  Defendant shifts the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant constructively discharged Plaintiff.  Plaintiff fails to advance any evidence to show her work environment was so abusive that she had to resign.  As such, Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her cause of action for wrongful constructive discharge.

Sixth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

            In her operative complaint, Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s “discriminatory, harassing, and retaliatory actions against plaintiff constituted severe and outrageous misconduct and caused plaintiff extreme emotional distress.”  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶ 49.)  As set forth above, Defendant has met its burden of showing Defendant did not discriminate, harass, or retaliate against Plaintiff.  Defendant, therefore, has shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  Plaintiff fails to advance any such evidence.  Plaintiff, therefore, fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Punitive Damages

As Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her claims against Defendant, her derivative claim for punitive damages fails. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. DISCUSSION

Second Cause of Action: Sexual Harassment

To state a claim for hostile work environment harassment, Plaintiff must allege Defendant engaged in severe or pervasive harassment that unreasonably interfered with Plaintiff’s work performance.  (See Thompson v. City Of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 877.)

Defendant advances Plaintiff’s deposition testimony.  At deposition, Plaintiff testified she resigned “[b]ecause it had become too distressing for [Plaintiff’s] emotional and mental health and [Plaintiff] didn't see any changes happening after [Plaintiff] brought the situation up to [Plaintiff’s] manager” (Declaration of Brett D. And often [Plaintiff] would, like, jump  back.  And so [their] bodies would sort of collide in that situation. And that would occur every shift that [Plaintiff] had with Jamilh. At minimum, one time per shift, but it would often happen more than one time” (id., Exhibit B, pp. 63-64); she worked with Defendant a total of 10 to 15 shifts (id., Exhibit B, p. 70);  she did not inform anyone else at the store of Defendant’s behavior (id., Exhibit B, pp. 66, 76); and Defendant never mentioned Plaintiff’s gender or sex (id., Exhibit B, p. 105).  This evidence meets Defendant’s burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claim for harassment because Defendant’s behavior was not “sufficiently severe or pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create a work environment that qualifies as hostile or abusive.”  (Dickson v. Burke Williams, Inc. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 1307, 1314.) 

In opposition, Plaintiff advances her own deposition testimony.  At deposition, Plaintiff testified she perceived Defendant’s harassment as being sexually motivated because “of the tone of voice that he would use when he would scare [Plaintiff] and the whole physical touching situation.”  (Plaintiff’s Appendix of Evidence in Opposition to Defendant Jamilh Roque’s Motion for Summary Adjudication [sic] or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication, Exhibit 1 (Excerpts from Deposition of Princess Hodges), p. 69.)  Plaintiff also testified Defendant was patronizing, and she could not say this was explicitly related to her sex; but she did not witness similar comments being made to male employees.  (Id., Exhibit 1, p. 102.)  Although this suggests Roque’s behavior may have been based on Plaintiff’s gender or sex, Plaintiff fails to advance evidence of harassment by Defendant that would have “seriously affected the psychological well-being of a reasonable employee . . . .”  (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 610.)  Defendant’s behavior, while unprofessional, was not so severe or persuasive as to create an abusive work environment.  “FEHA's prohibitions are not a ‘civility code’ . . . .”  (Sheffield v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Social Services (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 153, 161.)

Los Angeles Transit Lines (1954) 124 Cal.App.2d 466, 471.)  However, Plaintiff cites no authority for the proposition that this tenet of tort law that pertains to preexisting physical conditions applies to claims for emotional distress in statutory causes of action for sexual harassment under FEHA.  Likewise, while Denzell Jones suggested at his deposition Defendant’s behavior, as described by Plaintiff, violated Defendant’s sexual harassment policy, that does not make Defendant’s behavior actionable under FEHA.   

Sixth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

            In her operative complaint, Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s “discriminatory, harassing, and retaliatory actions against plaintiff constituted severe and outrageous misconduct and caused plaintiff extreme emotional distress.”  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶ 49.)  As set forth above, Defendant has met his burden by showing Defendant did not harass Plaintiff.  Defendant, therefore, shifts the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  Plaintiff fails to advance any evidence that Defendant harassed Plaintiff in a severe and outrageous manner.  Plaintiff, therefore, fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Punitive Damages

As Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to her claims against Defendant, her claim derivative claim for punitive damages fails.

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is granted.
