DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00245    Hearing Date: June 24, 2024    Dept: P

Having considered plaintiffs’ motion for leave to file a supplemental complaint, defendant and cross-complainants’ opposition, and plaintiffs’ reply brief, the court rules as follows: the Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to File Supplemental Brief is tentatively GRANTED.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On April 1, 2021, Plaintiffs, James Atkins and Margaret G. Walker, individually, and as trustees of the Atkins-Walker Family Trust (collectively “Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, Karyn Chamberlain, Gutierrez & Sons Tree Service & Landscaping, Inc., and DOES 1 through 20. On June 17, 2021, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Willful Trespass; (2) Declaratory Relief; and (3) Elder Abuse.

On May 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion for Leave to File a Supplemental Complaint.

On June 10, 2024, Defendant and Cross-Complainants, Karyn Chamberlain and Michael Chamberlain filed an opposition brief.

I. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs have brought this case alleging that their property is adjacent to the Defendant’s property. Plaintiff notes that Defendant, Guiterrez & Sons Tree Service & Landscaping’s corporate officers, directors, supervisors and/or foreman ratified and approved the wrongful conduct alleged in the Complaint, including willful trespass, declaratory relief, and elder abuse.

Plaintiff contends that on March 2, 2021, Defendant Chamberlain hired Gutierrez landscaping to perform landscaping work. Plaintiffs allege that Gutierrez began cutting trees as directed by Defendant Chamberlain, but that they were on Plaintiffs’ property, cutting Plaintiffs’ trees and vegetation. (FAC. ¶¶ 10-11.)

Now, Plaintiffs wish to file a supplemental complaint.

II. LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 464, subdivision (a) provides, “[t]he plaintiff and defendant, respectively, may be allowed, on motion, to make a supplemental complaint or answer, alleging facts material to the case occurring after the former complaint or answer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 464, subd. (a).) A supplemental pleading is a mechanism used in order to assert facts which occur after the filing of the original complaint or cross-complaint. (Ibid.) A supplemental complaint does not supersede the original complaint, but merely adds new allegations in conjunction with the original complaint. (Hebert v. Los Angeles Raiders, Ltd. (1991) 23 Cal.App.4th 414, 426.) Thus, a supplemental pleading cannot allege facts to constitute an entirely new cause of action. (Flood v. Simpson (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 644, 647.) Denial of a motion may be proper on the basis that the supplemental pleading attempted to introduce new causes of action.  (Ibid.)  Leave to amend to file a supplemental complaint is addressed to the sound discretion of the court and will not be disturbed absent manifest abuse of that discretion. (Ibid.)

III. DISCUSSION

Moving Parties’ Arguments

Plaintiffs argue that they may make a supplemental complaint in order to allege facts material to the case occurring after the former complaint. Plaintiffs note that on September 15, 2023, Chamberlain once again trespassed on their property and cut, trimmed, and destroyed Plaintiffs’ foliage, simply because she felt the vegetation was “blocking her view.”

Responding Party’s Opposition

Defendant Chamberlain’s opposition brief argues this motion should be denied since Plaintiffs delayed more than eight (8) months to bring the motion. Further, Defendant Chamberlain contends that if this Court does grant the motion, it should vacate the jury trial set for August 20, 2024 and re-open discovery to allow Defendant the opportunity to conduct discovery based on the allegations in the proposed supplemental complaint.

Merits of the Motion for Leave to File Supplemental Complaint

Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must be accompanied by a separate declaration that specifies the following:

“(1) the effect of the amendment;

(2) why the amendment is necessary and proper;

(3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

(4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”

Here, the Court finds, as Defendant Chamberlain pointed out, that although the supplemental allegations are consistent with the original complaint and do not add any new legal theories or causes of action, Plaintiffs nonetheless waited eight (8) months to file this motion from when Defendant Chamberlain allegedly committed the same acts she is alleged to have commit in the original complaint. However, the declaration of Charles Peterson (“Peterson Decl.”), counsel for Defendant Chamberlain, indicates that supplementation is necessary and proper because at the time the operative complaint was filed, the misconduct at issue in the supplemental complaint could not have been alleged, as it had yet to occur, and that although this alleged misconduct took place in September 2023, it was not confirmed until November until November 30, 2023. The court finds substantial compliance with the Rules of Court.

Here, the factual allegations are functionally the same as those in the original complaint and the complaint does not state any new causes of action. Therefore, the proposed Second Amended Complaint is consistent with Code of Civil Procedure section 464(a).

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to File a Supplemental Complaint.

Having considered defendant’s motion to strike, plaintiff’s opposition, and defendant’s reply, the court rules as follows: the motion to strike portions of plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is GRANTED with leave to amend.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff, Daimel Garcia (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, Walmart, Inc., and DOES 1 through 100. On October 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Assault and Battery; and (3) Premises Liability.

On December 8, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s Complaint .

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief.

On April 29, 2024, Defendant filed a reply brief.

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

This action involves a personal injury allegedly arising out of an incident that occurred on July 13, 2023, when Plaintiff was shopping at the Walmart located at 22015 Hawthorne Blvd., Torrance, CA 90503 when he was allegedly stopped and assaulted by loss prevention and

security employees of Defendants. (FAC, ¶ 16.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant, Walmart owed a duty of reasonable care toward Plaintiff and others based on Defendants’ ownership of the property, their right to exercise control over the premises, their management and maintenance of the property, their contractual obligations, custom and practice in the industry, and the commission of affirmative acts that resulted in injury to Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 19.)

Plaintiff contends that Walmart breached its duty to Plaintiff individually, and by the acts of its agents and employees, they negligently, carelessly, and recklessly allowed loss prevention to engage in a physical fight with Plaintiff while on the subject premises. (FAC, ¶ 20.) Moreover, Plaintiff alleges that Walmart were aware or should have been aware of the dangerous and violent nature of the said personnel at the subject premises. (FAC, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff contends that the duties owed by Defendant include, but are not limited to: the duty to operate and manage the subject premises in a careful manner as to protect the physical safety and well-being of the persons and invitees; exercising due care in hiring managers and employees, including loss prevention and security; properly training and supervising staff responsible for such care; and taking whatever special actions and precautions required to maintain the safety of those persons at the subject premises. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Moreover, Plaintiff notes that Walmart also performed acts that affirmatively contributed to the creation of the unsafe situation or otherwise failed to remediate such circumstances. (FAC, ¶ 23.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.) “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Parties’ Arguments

Defendant argues that portions of Plaintiff’s FAC relating to punitive damages should be stricken as they do not adequately allege malice, oppression, or fraud. Further, Plaintiff contends that Plaintiff has failed to allege any specific allegations that any such behavior was ratified by Defendant’s officers, directors, or managing agents.

Responding Party’s Opposition

Plaintiff argues that sufficient facts are pled for Defendant and Mr. Ewing to be liable for punitive damages, but also, that Plaintiff has enough evidence that Defendant authorized and/or ratified the malicious batter/assault on Plaintiff.

Moving Parties’ Reply

On reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s opposition fails to present any facts pled in the FAC to satisfy the requirement imposed on Plaintiff by Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a).

Merits of the Motion to Strike

Preliminarily, this Court notes that Plaintiff, in his opposition brief, cites to deposition transcript and evidence to show that he is able to maintain a claim for punitive damages against Walmart. However, this Court notes that a motion to strike, like a demurrer, tests the sufficiency of the pleading on its face – that is, allegations contained in the four corners of the FAC. Plaintiff has not made any allegations referencing the deposition transcripts in his FAC, nor does he request this court take judicial notice of such. Thus, the Court will not be taking into consideration any reference to outside evidence not contained within the FAC.

A. Meet and Confer

California Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 requires that, before filing a motion to strike, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject of the motion for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the issues to be raised in the motion. The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(2).) The party must also file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(3).)

Here, counsel for Defendant, Walmart, filed a declaration indicating that on November 14, 2023, she met and conferred with Casey Kilday Esq., counsel for Plaintiff, both over the phone and by email on the dame day. (Declaration of Sayyora Badalbaeva (“Badalbaeva Decl.”), ¶ 4.) Having seen no argument in Plaintiff’s opposition brief, or a declaration from counsel for Plaintiff arguing otherwise, this Court finds that the parties have sufficiently met and conferred in good faith prior to Defendant bringing this action.

B. Punitive Damages

Here, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s language and prayer for punitive damages should be stricken as it is not specifically pled pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a), which authorizes punitive damages in non-contract cases “where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” As such, Defendant is moving to strike the following from Plaintiff’s FAC: (1) Page 3 – Factual Allegations, paragraphs 11-3; (2) Page 4 – Factual Allegations, paragraphs 14-15; (3) Page 8 – Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action, paragraphs 34 and 36; and (4) Page 11 – Prayers for Relief, paragraph 5.

“Malice [is defined as] conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights and safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” means “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” is “an

intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff. In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth. In ruling on a motion to strike, courts do not read allegations in isolation.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.) Something more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages. There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894.)

To plead a “willful and conscious disregard of the rights of others,” a plaintiff need only allege, “that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1211.) However, the definition of malice also requires that the conduct be despicable. “'Despicable conduct' has been described as conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. Such conduct has been described as '[having] the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.”' (American Airlines, Inc. v. Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 1017, 1050.) In cases involving conduct performed without intent to harm, a finding of malice requires proof by clear and convincing evidence that defendant's tortious wrong amounted to despicable conduct and that such despicable conduct was carried on with a ‘willful and conscious disregard’ of the rights or safety of others.” (See College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)

Specifically, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s FAC fails to assert allegations that any managing agent, officer, or director of Defendant authorized or ratified any act of oppression, fraud, or malice. Defendant asserts that although Plaintiff has alleged the Walmart security personnel assaulted the Plaintiff, there are no specific allegations of oppression, fraud, or malice alleged. Moreover, Defendant asserts that there are no specific allegations that in-store security employees qualify as managing agents for Walmart Inc.

This Court notes that “[a]n employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages. . . based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others, or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded, or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”(Civil Code, § 3294,subd.(b).) “[T]he imposition of punitive damages upon a corporation is based upon its own fault. It is not imposed vicariously by virtue of the fault of others.”(City Products Corp. v.

Globe Indemnity Co.(1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 31, 36.)“Corporations are legal entities which do not have minds capable of recklessness, wickedness, or intent to injure or deceive. An award of punitive damages against a corporation therefore must rest on the malice of the corporation’s employees. But the law does not impute every employee’s malice to the corporation. Instead, the punitive damages statute requires proof of malice among corporate leaders: the ‘officer[s], director[s], or managing agent[s].’”(Cruz v. Home Base (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 167 [citation omitted].) As to ratification, “[a] corporation cannot confirm and accept that which it does not actually know about.’”(Id.) (citing College Hospital, supra, at p. 726 [for ratification sufficient to justify punitive damages against corporation, there must be proof that officers, directors, or managing agents had actual knowledge of the malicious conduct and its outrageous character.].)

Here, Plaintiff’s FAC, as pled, alleges that Walmart’s owners, officers, managers, and shareholders had knowledge of the violent and dangerous nature of its employees because they were provided authority to exercise substantial professional judgment in the care of the subject premises and its patrons. (FAC, ¶¶ 13, 15, 24, 45.) While this is the language used to allege a claim for punitive damages, it is merely conclusory. Allegations supporting a request for punitive damages must be alleged with specificity; conclusory allegations without sufficient facts are not enough. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-42.) Plaintiff attempts to make up for this deficiency by arguing that Walmart failed to discipline any of their employees or to provide additional training to prevent violent encounters after the alleged harm done to Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 15.) However, this does not speak to actual knowledge of Walmart as to the alleged violent nature of its employees and is insufficient to support the claim for punitive damages.

Therefore, the Motion to Strike is GRANTED with twenty (20) days leave to amend by Plaintiff.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Walmart’s motion to strike with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

Moving Party is ordered to provide notice.

Having considered defendants’ demurrer, plaintiff’s opposition, and defendants’ reply, the court rules as follows: the Demurring Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the First, Second, Third, Fourth, Sixth, Eighth and Ninth causes of action with twenty (20) days leave to amend. I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On September 5, 2023, Plaintiff, Michelle Anaya (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, Verne Becker, South Bay Management Services, LLC, and DOES 1 to 5. II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

This case is based on Plaintiff’s allegations that on August 17, 2018, she entered into a written lease for the subject property for a term of one year, and thereafter continued on a month-to-month basis up until she vacated the subject property on May 4, 2022. (FAC, ¶14.) Plaintiff asserts that throughout her tenancy at the subject property, her requests for repairs were either ignored, denied, or completed in a sub-standard manner and without the appropriate permits from the City of Manhattan Beach. (FAC, ¶ 16.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

A. Demurrer

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.(Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.)

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

B. Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.) “When the

defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Parties’ Arguments

Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s First, Second, Third, Fourth, Sixth, Eighth, and Ninth causes of action fail to allege facts sufficient to state causes of action against them and are uncertain.

Responding Party’s Opposition

Plaintiff, in her opposition, concedes that Defendants’ demurrer to the First and Second causes of action are warranted and requests that the demurrer be sustained as to both of those causes of action with leave to amend. As to the Third, Fourth, Sixth, Eighth, and Ninth causes of action, Plaintiff argues that they are sufficiently plead.

Moving Parties’ Reply

On reply, Defendants argue that the FAC fails to allege any actual substantive facts to support a cause of action against them.

Merits of the Demurrer

A. Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)

This Court finds that Defendants have met and conferred in good faith prior to the filing of this demurrer. Defense counsel notes that on November 7, 2023, he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel about a planned demurrer and motion to strike, but that the parties were unable to reach any agreements.

B. Allegations against Landlord versus Property Managers

Demurring Defendants argue that at least five (5) of Plaintiff’s causes of action can only be pled against a landlord, not against property managers Mary E. Colin and South Bay Management Services, and thus argue the demurrer should be sustained as to those counts.

Plaintiff argues, in her opposition, that the case of Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903 is directly on point and shows that not only a landlord can be liable in a tort action, but so can his/her agent in the management of the landlord’s property.

In Stoibert the Court recited the following factual allegations in that plaintiff’s complaint to show that the rental agents acting for the owner assumed a duty toward her. (Stoiber, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d at 930.) The complaint in Stoiber alleged: “As agent of defendant [OWNER] (sic), defendant [PRINCIPAL OF RENTAL AGENCY], [RENTAL AGENCY] has assumed in the course of its agency, functions, duties, and responsibilities of defendant [OWNER] (sic), who resides in Connecticut. These duties, responsibilities, and functions include, but are not limited to, the following: 1. Collection of rental payments from the plaintiff; 2. Maintenance and management of the premises...” (Ibid.)

The court applied these facts to the test laid out in J’Aire Corp. v. Gregory (1979) 24 Cal.3d 799, which set forth the criteria to be considered by courts in determining whether a defendant stands in such a position to a plaintiff that he owes him an affirmative duty: “…(1) the extent to which the transaction was intended to affect the plaintiff, (2) the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, (3) the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, (4) the closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered, (5) the moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct and (6) the policy of preventing future harm.” (J’Aire Corp., supra, 24 Cal.3d at 804.)

In finding that a property manager may be liable in tort, the court noted, “the rental agents owed a duty of ordinary care towards the tenant because the transaction between the rental agent and the landlord was clearly intended to affect the tenants, and because harm would be foreseeable to the tenants if the rental agent did not properly perform his duty.” (Stoiber, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d at 930-31. It reasoned that “[w]hen the owner is located at some distance from the rental property as in the [Stoiber] case (Connecticut), the tenant’s only practical recourse is to complain to the rental agent. Imposition of a duty on the rental agent would as a matter of public policy encourage the agent to pass the complaints along to the owner or to take action to properly maintain the property, if this is party of his responsibilities as agreed with the owner.” (Id. at 931.)

In the case at bar, plaintiff makes no attempt to differentiate between the Defendants alleged – owner, rental agency, or principal of rental agency otherwise. In fact, Plaintiff’s FAC only makes mention of South Bay Management Service, LLC and Mary E. Colin by themselves when introducing them. Thereafter, Plaintiff combines three defendants, South Bay Management Services, LLC (“SBMS”), Mary E. Colin (“COLIN”), and landlord, Verne Becker (“BECKER”) into a combined Defendant listed throughout the FAC as “SBMS/COLIN/BECKER” or “Defendants.” There are no allegations that SBMS or COLIN stepped into the role of landlord for BECKER because the landlord was out of town, etc. As such, as pled, the FAC does not adequately allege that SBMS or Colin owed Plaintiff a duty independent from Becker.

This allegation deficiency is enough to rule the entire FAC as uncertain. As such, the demurrer is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted twenty (20) days leave to amend.

Merits of Motion to Strike

Because this Court has sustained the demurrer in its entirety, and the motion to strike relies on the same arguments made in the demurrer, and the motion to strike is made moot by this Court’s ruling on demurrer. The motion to strike as it relates to causes of action not sustained on demurrer is moot.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer in its entirety, but grants Plaintiff twenty (20) days leave to amend. Defendants’ motion to strike is moot as it relates to the causes of action sustained on demurrer.

B. Second Cause of Action for General Negligence

uncertain. In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiffs must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

Here, plaintiff has alleged a cause of action for negligence that is apparently the same as that alleged in the first cause of action, albeit with greater factual detail. Nonetheless, as a restatement of the same elements of the first cause of action, it is subject to demurrer.

Therefore, the demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

D. Third Cause of Action for Violation of Vehicle code section 20001(a)

Next, Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot maintain their cause of action for Violation of Vehicle Code section 20001(a) – Misdemeanor Hit and Run. “A violation of a state statute does not necessarily give rise to a private cause of action. Instead, whether a party has a right to sue depends on whether the Legislature has ‘manifested an intent to create such a private cause of action’ under the statute.” Lu v. Hawaiian Gardens Casino, Inc. (2010) 50 Cal. 4th 592, 596. A review of the relevant Vehicle Code section suggests no such right of action. The Court agrees. First, a misdemeanor violation of a vehicle code is not its own cause of action.

Therefore, the demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

E. Fourth Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Lastly, Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds they argue Plaintiff may not maintain a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress against them. “The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.) Further, “[i]n order to avoid a demurrer, the plaintiff must allege with ‘great [ ] specificity’ the acts which he or she believes are so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. [Citation.]” (Yau v. Santa Margarita Ford, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 144, 160-61.)

Specifically, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not alleged specific facts that Defendants acted with the intention of causing emotional distress, nor has she pled facts to establish severe or extreme emotional distress. This Court agrees. While Plaintiff has alleged the elements in a conclusory way, Plaintiff has failed to do so as to each element in a specific way. Generally, a cause of action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress is limited to egregious conduct that is directed at the plaintiff and proximately caused by defendant (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 905). However, there is one exception “when the defendant is

aware, but acts with reckless disregard, of the plaintiff and the probability that his or her conduct will cause severe emotional distress to plaintiff. Where reckless disregard of the plaintiff’s interests is the theory of recovery, the presence of the plaintiff at the time the outrageous conduct occurs is recognized as the element establishing a higher degree of culpability which, in turn, justifies recovery of greater damages by a broader group of plaintiffs than allowed on a negligent infliction of emotional distress theory.” (Id. at 905-906.)

Here, plaintiff’s allegations are wholly conclusory and allege no specific facts, not even who was driving the vehicle. Without more, Plaintiff is unable to sufficiently allege this cause of action.

Thus, the demurrer is SUSTAINED as to Plaintiff’s cause of action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress. Plaintiff is granted twenty (20) days leave to amend.

Merits of the Motion to Strike

Here, because the Motion to Strike seeks to strike: (1) Prayer for punitive damages at Page 3, paragraph 14(a)(2); (2) Paragraph 2 of the Second Cause of Action – General Negligence; (3) Entire Third Cause of Action (Violation of Vehicle Code 20001(a) – Misdemeanor Hit and Run Against Defendants DEBBIE MCALPHINE); and (4) Entire Fourth Cause of Action – INTENTIONAL INFLICTION OF EMOTIONAL DISTRESS (Against Defendants DEBBIE MCALHPINE), this Court notes that the analysis in Defendants’ demurrer is similar, if not the same, to that in the demurrer.

Plaintiff contends that fleeing the scene after the collision amounts to intentional, willful, and despicable conduct. However, in Brooks v. E.J. Willig Truck Transp. Co. (1953) 40 Cal.2d 669, the court held that a hit and run cannot give rise to damages unless the fact that the defendant fled the scene was “a proximate cause of further injury or death” beyond the accident itself. (Id. at 679.) In Brooks, the Supreme Court found that a hit and run instruction to the jury was warranted because there was no evidence in the record that the decedent died before the defendant drove away from the scene. (Id. at 680.) Therefore, the jury was in the best position to decide if fleeing the scene was a cause of further harm. (See id.) Brooks did not consider the issue of whether punitive damages can be imposed based on a hit and run. It did, however, hold that fleeing an accident scene only constitutes a tort if the act itself causes the plaintiff additional injury above and beyond the damages caused by the collision that preceded flight. (Id.)

Here, Plaintiff fails to allege any facts that fleeing the scene caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries. Thus, the Complaint is devoid of any facts to show that the act of hitting and then fleeing was in fact tortious conduct. If the act of hitting and then fleeing, in and of itself, does not give rise to a tort, then it logically follows that the act cannot give rise to a claim for punitive damages.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the motion to strike in its entirety and will allow twenty (20) days leave to amend.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer in its entirety, and allows Plaintiff twenty (20) days leave to amend. Further, Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED with leave to amend.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice.
