DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV29644    Hearing Date: September 6, 2024    Dept: 73

09/06/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

THE MACHINE GROUP, INC. v. NC DYNAMICS LLC (21STCV29644)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant:  Albro Lundy III (Baker, Burton & Lundy PC)

 

Counsel for Defendant/Cross-Complainant:  Jeffrey Goodfried (Saul Ewin LLP)

 

(1)   NC DYNAMICS’ Motion to COMPEL TMG TO RESPOND TO THE THIRD DEMAND FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE SUM OF $3,500 (filed 05/31/2024)

(2)   NC DYNAMICS’ Motion to COMPEL TMG TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES TO THE SECOND SET OF REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE SUM OF $3,000.00 (filed 05/31/2024)

(3)   NC DYNAMICS’ Motion to COMPEL TMG TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES TO THE THIRD SET OF FORM INTERROGATORIES AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE SUM OF $6,000.00 (filed 05/31/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to respond to the Third Demand for Production of Documents and Request for Sanctions is MOOT.

 

NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Second Set of Requests for Admissions and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED and DENIED in part. The request for sanctions is denied.

 

NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Third Set of Form Interrogatories and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED and DENIED in part. The request for sanctions is denied.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff The Machine Group (“TMG”) filed this action against Defendant NC Dynamics.

 

TMG’s Complaint alleges causes of action for breach of contract and recission.

 

On June 3, 2024, NC Dynamics filed a Second Amended Cross Complaint (“SACC”) against TMG and Nidec OKK USA Corporation, fka OKK USA Corporation.

 

NC Dynamics alleges the following causes of action in its SACC: (1) breach of contract; (2) fraud in the inducement; (3) negligence; (4) intentional interference with contractual relations; (5) intentional interference with prospective economic relations; (6) negligent interference with prospective economic relations; and (7) breach of warranty of fitness for particular purpose.

 

This case concerns a contract dispute for the sale and underperformance of specialized machinery.  NC Dynamics alleges the following in its SACC. NC Dynamics, the Defendant/Cross-Complainant, is a full-service manufacturing facility that manufactures products for aerospace companies.  According to NC Dynamics, NC Dynamics took steps to upgrade the manufacturing process in regard to an existing contract it had with Bombardier Aerostructures (“Bombardier”).  As part of that process, NC Dynamics requested bids from three companies to sell manufacturing equipment to NC Dynamics.  NC Dynamics also requested the companies to conduct time studies to establish the running time for the construction of specific titanium parts for the Bombardier contract. 

 

TMG, the Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant in this case, was one of the three companies to submit a bid, but the only company that offered to sell an upcoming machine from another company called OKK.  OKK conducted the time study. NC Dynamics contend that TMG ultimately won the bid based on a guarantee made by TMG President Richard Wackeen (“Wackeen”) that the parts would be manufactured in ten hours with the OKK machine and also upon the misrepresentation that OKK was aware that Wackeen was guaranteeing a ten hour running time.

 

NC Dynamics and TMG entered into two contracts for the purchase of two OKK machines.  The machines failed to construct the parts in the contracted ten-hour running time, and multiple attempts were made to reduce the running time. Because of the slow running time, NC Dynamics contend that they were forced to purchase a third machine from TMG to meet its production needs for the Bombardier contract.  NC Dynamics further allege that TMG continued to guarantee that the OKK machines would achieve the ten-hour running time despite knowing the OKK machine could do so.  NC Dynamics later learned that six weeks before the parties entered into the third contract, a TMG manager named Jim McCulloch (“McCulloch”) admitted in an email that the OKK machines would never achieve a running time.  McCulloch was present to witness the final run of the OKK machines, which at the lowest point exceeded 24 hours. As a result of the running time, NC Dynamics contend that they lost Bombardier, and others, as a current and future customer.  

 

In TMG’s Complaint, TMG alleges that NC Dynamics has an unpaid balance in the amount of $1,895,150.13 on the Second Machine and Third Machine, and has refused to make any further payments on these machines despite TMG’s demands.

 

On May 31, 2024, NC Dynamics filed three motions to compel against TMG:

·       NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to respond to the Third Demand for Production of Documents

o   TMG failed to serve responses

·       NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Second Set of Requests for Admissions

o   On August 31, 2023, NC Dynamics served its Second Set of Requests for Admissions which seek discovery that is directly relevant to this dispute. However, on October 23, 2023, TMG provided responses that contained boilerplate objections and evasive responses.

·       NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Third Set of Form Interrogatories

o   TMG’s responses to NC Dynamics’ Third Set of Form Interrogatories are evasive and incomplete, and the stated objections are without merit.

·       NC Dynamics requests sanctions.

 

In opposition, TMG argues that:

·       NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to respond to the Third Demand for Production of Documents

o   The motion is moot because TMG has since served responses

·       NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Second Set of Requests for Admissions

o   The requests at issue are defective

o   First, many of the RFAs seek admission of multiple conflicting fact patterns. Code. Civ. Proc., 2033.060(f) (“No request for admission shall contain subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive request…”)

o   A second general category of defect in the RFAs is that many seek admission of facts known only to other parties, often in matters where TMG would have no knowledge at all.

o   A third, smaller category of defective RFA’s relates to intentionally vague terms. TMG answered as best it could.

·       NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Third Set of Form Interrogatories

o   TMG provided further responses to interrogatory 15.1 and three other questions in order to avoid a motion

o   The motion seeks further responses to Form Interrogatory 17.1, and TMG challenges the propriety of further responses given the clearly defective nature of the respective Requests for Admission

 

In reply, NC Dynamics argues that:

·       NC Dynamics maintains that the Court should compel TMG to provide responses/further responses

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, counsel for NC Dynamics submitted a declaration attesting that the parties met and conferred. (Chow Decl. ¶¶ 9-12.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

B.      NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to respond to the Third Demand for Production of Documents

 

On May 31, 2024, NC Dynamics filed the instant motion to compel TMG’s responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Three, and request for sanctions.

 

On March 12, 2024, NC Dynamics served its Requests for Production of Documents, Set Three upon TMG. (Chow Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.)

 

TMG’s counsel states he was traveling at the time of service, and an administrative error led to the discovery being incorrectly calendared. (Semos Decl., ¶ 3.) On July 3, 2024, TMG served responses without objections to the Requests for Production of Documents, Set Three. (Semos Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.)

 

Since TMG has served responses, the Court finds the instant motion to compel responses is moot. When respondents serve untimely discovery responses after parties have filed motions to compel initial responses, courts have broad discretion as to the ruling and may hold that the motion is moot. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., v. Klugman (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 409.) Since the motion is moot, the Court does not award sanctions.

 

Thus, the Court finds NC Dynamics’ motion to compel TMG’s responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Three is MOOT.

 

C.      NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Second Set of Requests for Admissions

 

                                                               i.      Legal Standard

 

The notice of motion must be served within 45 days after service of the responses in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1013)); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c), 2033.290(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 736, 745.) The 45day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) However, the parties can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(c), 2033.290(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further responses may be brought based on responses to requests for admission (“RFA”) that: (1) provide evasive or incomplete answers; or (2) make unmeritorious or overly-generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)  However, a motion to compel further responses cannot compel the admission of matters already denied.  (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 821.) 

 

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the¿burden is on the responding party¿to justify any objection or failure fully to answer.¿(Coy v. Superior Court¿(1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also¿Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court¿(2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

                                                             ii.      Discussion

 

NC Dynamics seeks further responses to its Second Set of Requests for Admissions, RFA Nos. 32, 34, 36, 37, 38, 40, 52, 53, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61, 62, 63 and Document Request No. 53.

 

1.      Request Nos. 32, 34, 36, 52, 53, 62

 

The Court does not find these requests are code compliant.

 

For example, RFA No. 32 states “Admit that after the MACHINES were sold to NC DYNAMICS, TMG and/or 5TH AXIS had exclusive responsibilities for setting up the MACHINES to machine the BOMBARDIER PARTS.”

 

The Court agrees with TMG that Request Nos. 32, 34, 36, 52, 53 and 62 are compound and cannot be answered as phrased because it inquires about exclusive responsibility held by two entities. Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to RFA Nos.  32, 34, 36, 52, 53 and 62.

 

2.      Request Nos. 37

 

RFA No. 37 states “Admit that prior to the commencement of this litigation, TMG never instructed NC DYNAMICS to affix the BOMBARDIER PARTS horizontally in the MACHINES.”

 

TMG responded “Objection: The request is vague and ambiguous as to “instructed.” Without waiving said objection, Plaintiff responds as follows: NC Dynamics received the time study with horizontal images attached.”

 

The Court does not find the request is vague and ambiguous as to the term “instructed.” Thus, the Court GRANTS the motion to compel further response to RFA No. 37.

 

3.      Request Nos. 38, 40, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61, 63

 

The Court does not find these requests are code compliant.

 

For example, RFA No. 38 states “Admit that prior to the commencement of this litigation, 5TH AXIS never instructed NC DYNAMICS to affix the BOMBARDIER PARTS horizontally in the MACHINES.” Additionally, RFA No. 40 states “Admit that NC DYNAMICS never prohibited 5TH AXIS from affixing the BOMBARDIER PARTS horizontally in the MACHINES.”

 

Here, the requests ask TMG to speculate as to facts known to other parties. TMG responded that it lacked information sufficient to admit or deny this request. The Court agrees with TMG that Request Nos. 38, 40, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61 and 63 were properly objected to.

 

Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to RFA Nos. 38, 40, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61 and 63.

 

4.      Document Request No. 53

 

If NC Dynamics wishes to compel further response to Request for Production No. 53, it needs to file a motion for that response. CRC Rule 3.1345(a). Thus, motion to compel response to Request for Production of Document No. 53 is DENIED.

 

In sum, the Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to RFA Nos. 32, 34, 36, 38, 40, 52, 53, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61, 62, 63, Document Request No. 53 and the Court GRANTS the motion to compel further responses to RFA No. 37.

 

                                                           iii.      Sanctions

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(h), monetary sanctions shall be imposed where a party unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses unless the Court finds the party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

NC Dynamics seeks sanctions against TMG. However, since the Court grants and denies in part NC Dynamics’ motion, the Court does not find sanctions are warranted. Thus, NC Dynamics’ request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

D.     NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Third Set of Form Interrogatories

 

                                                               i.      Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”¿ CCP § 2030.010(a).¿ “Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.”¿ CCP § 2030.260(a).¿ 
¿ 

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered. (2) An exercise of the party's option to produce writings. (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.”¿ CCP § 2030.210(a).¿ “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”¿ CCP § 2030.220(c).¿ 

¿ 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).¿ 

 

                                                             ii.      Discussion

 

NC Dynamics seeks further responses to its Third Set of Form Interrogatories, Rog Nos. 12.7, 15.1, 17.1, 50.1, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6 and Document Request No. 51.

 

1.      Interrogatory Nos. 15.1 and 50.3

 

TMG asserts requests for further responses to interrogatory nos. 15.1 and 50.3 is moot as TMG provided further responses on August 1, 2024. NC Dynamics does not request further responses to these two interrogatories in their reply. Thus, the Court finds further responses to interrogatory nos. 15.1 and 50.3 are MOOT.

 

2.      Interrogatory Nos. 12.7

 

Interrogatory 12.7 states Have YOU OR ANYONE ACTING ON YOUR BEHALF inspected the scene of the INCIDENT? If so, for each inspection state:

(a) the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the individual making the inspection (except for expert witnesses covered by Code of Civil Procedure sections 2034.210-2034.310); and

(b) the date of the inspection.

 

TMG responded “Objection: This is vague and ambiguous and the scope is unclear. Without waiving said objection, Plaintiff responds as follows: Since the instigation of litigation, no.”

 

The Court agrees that the term “incident” is vague and is ambiguous as to the scope. Thus, the Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to interrogatory No. 12.7.

 

3.      Interrogatory No. 17.1

 

Interrogatory 17.1 states Is your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories an unqualified admission? If not, for each response that is not an unqualified admission:

(a) state the number of the request;

(b) state all facts upon which you base your response;

(c) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and

(d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.

 

TMG asserts that no response is required to this question because the respective RFA was properly objected to. As discussed above, the Court found that RFA Nos. 32, 34, 36, 38, 40, 52, 53, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61, 62, 63 were not code compliant. Thus, the motion to compel further responses to interrogatory No. 17.1 is DENIED.

 

4.      Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.4-50.6

 

NC Dynamics argues that TMG’s responses contain meritless objections, they are incomplete, and they fail to provide responses to each sub-part of these form interrogatories. TMG should be ordered to withdraw the meritless objections and provide and full and complete responses to all sub-parts of each of these form interrogatories.

 

The Court agrees and GRANTS the motion to compel further responses to interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.4-50.6.

 

5.      Document Request No. 51

 

If NC Dynamics wishes to compel further responses to Request for Production No. 51, it needs to file a motion for that response. CRC Rule 3.1345(a). Thus, motion to compel response to Request for Production of Document No. 51 is DENIED.

 

In sum, the Court finds further responses to interrogatory Nos. 15.1 and 50.3 are MOOT. The Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 12.7 and 17.1, and the Court GRANTS the motion to compel further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.4-50.6.

 

                                                           iii.      Sanctions

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(h), monetary sanctions shall be imposed where a party unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses unless the Court finds the party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

NC Dynamics seeks sanctions against TMG. However, since the Court grants and denies in part NC Dynamics’ motion, the Court does not find sanctions are warranted. Thus, NC Dynamics’ request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to respond to the Third Demand for Production of Documents and Request for Sanctions is MOOT.

 

NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Second Set of Requests for Admissions is GRANTED and DENIED in part. The Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to RFA Nos. 32, 34, 36, 38, 40, 52, 53, 55, 56, 57, 60, 61, 62, 63, Document Request No. 53 and the Court GRANTS the motion to compel further responses to RFA No. 37. The request for sanctions is denied.

 

NC Dynamics’ Motion to Compel TMG to provide further responses to the Third Set of Form Interrogatories is GRANTED and DENIED in part. The Court finds further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 15.1 and 50.3 are MOOT. The Court DENIES the motion to compel further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 12.7 and 17.1, and the Court GRANTS the motion to compel further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.4-50.6. The request for sanctions is denied.

 

TMG shall provide code-compliant responses within 20 days.

MARILEE MALES v. COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, et al. (23STCV09851)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Mark Valencia (Valencia & Cywinska, ALC)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Thomas Hurrell (Hurrell Cantrall LLP)

 

MOTION TO COMPEL PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS FROM DEFENDANT COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES AND REQUEST FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed 06/07/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses to request for production of document Nos. 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 73, and 74. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 20 days.

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record in the amount of $7,500.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within 20 days of the date of this order. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 2, 2023, Plaintiff Marilee Males (“Plaintiff”) filed this employment action against Defendants County of Los Angeles and Los Angeles County Department of Public Health (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

On August 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Religious Creed Discrimination – Failure to Accommodate In Violation of Cal. Gov. Code §12940(l)

2.      Retaliation in Violation of Cal. Gov. Code §12940(l)(4)

3.      Discrimination in Violation of Cal. Gov. Code §12940(l)(4)

4.      Retaliation in Violation of Cal. Gov. Code §12940(h)

5.      Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Retaliation in Violation of Govt. Code §12940(k)

6.      Labor Code §1102.5

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff is a Christian. Plaintiff has worked for Defendants from May 8, 2018 through January 2023. Specifically, Plaintiff was a Supervising Health Facilities Evaluator, Nursing, within the Los Angeles County Department of Health. Plaintiff requested an exemption from the COVID-19 vaccine on account of sincerely held religious beliefs. Plaintiff alleges she was wrongfully terminated. In the letter terminating Plaintiff’s employment, Defendants stated: “The basis for this action is your failure to comply with the Notice of Vaccination Requirement.”

 

On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Production of Documents from Defendant County of Los Angeles and Request for Attorneys’ Fees, arguing:

·       Plaintiff Males Request for Production of Documents to Defendant County of Los Angeles: # 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 73, and 74.

·       Pursuant to verified discovery received from the County of Los Angeles, the County states that in relation to Plaintiff’s department, as of April 14, 2023, there have only been 305 religious exemption requests, including Plaintiff’s request. (Decl. of Valencia, Ex. 1 – Supplemental Response to Special Interrogatory No. 4). Out of those applications, 87% were approved. 13% were denied.

·       Plaintiff seeks discovery of the other filed forms from the other 305 employees [the employees names and identifying information will be redacted, and assigned an identifying number for this litigation only], as the evidence will show that other applicants that had similar justifications to that of the Plaintiff had their accommodations granted. Such evidence is crucial, as it will prove that the County failed to accommodate Plaintiff, while accommodating others asserting nearly the same/identical justifications.

·       There are no privacy issues, especially given the fact that the names and id numbers of the employees will be redacted. Given the redactions, the invasiveness is minimal and privacy concerns are obviated. The justifications for the disclosure outweigh any purported privacy interest, and accordingly balance in favor of disclosure.

·       Plaintiff requests a recoupment of fees no less than $7,500.00.

Defendant County of Los Angeles filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff's Requests for Production, Nos. 68-74, have nothing to do with Plaintiff's own application, which is the only application in question in this case.

·       The information at issue contains personal information and names of the County's non-party employees, along with potentially going through their employment histories, including changes in employment status or accommodations to their employment. These requests “effectively seek the disclosure of confidential, personnel records of nonwitness third parties.” (Life Technologies Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 640, 652.)

·       While Plaintiff does suggest to redact the names of these employees, as well as their employee IDs, this does not cure the privacy issues at place. There may still be extremely sensitive and confidential information contained within the non-redacted portions.

·       Plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees should be denied.

Plaintiff filed a reply, arguing:

·       The requested evidence is crucial because it will show that other applicants who had similar explanations to that of the Plaintiff, were indeed granted a religious exemption. Such evidence could show that the County could have accommodated Plaintiff as they had accommodated others, but they failed to do so. (Out of 305 religious exemption requests in Plaintiff’s department, 87% were approved.)

·       Given the redactions, the invasiveness is minimal and privacy concerns are obviated.

·       Requests #68-74 are narrowly tailored. Plaintiff’s requests are not overbroad. They are limited to Plaintiff’s department. The County has already identified 305 religious exemption requests from Plaintiff’s department. Accordingly, the number is limited.

·       Furthermore, the discovery sought is very specific. Plaintiff is not seeking “ALL DOCUMENTS” but has specific forms being sought. Plaintiff is requesting specific forms, with the longest form being 3 pages. (Decl. of Valencia, Ex. 2-5.)

ANALYSIS

A.      Legal Standard

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

B.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel submits a declaration stating she sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter asking them to supplement discovery. Plaintiff’s counsel did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, however the Court will not deny Plaintiff’s motion on this basis. (See Obregon v. Sup. Ct. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434.) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits of the motion.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff is seeking responses to her Request for Production of Documents to Defendant County of Los Angeles Nos. 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 73, and 74.

Plaintiff seeks to compel the following requests:

RPD #68: All COVID-19 religious exemption APPLICATIONS submitted to YOU from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation. (“APPLICATION” means County of Los Angeles Religious Accommodation Request Form and supporting materials submitted along with Request Form.)

RPD #69: All “County of Los Angeles Denial of Request for Exemption” forms that YOU issued to those who applied for a COVID-19 religious exemption from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation.

RPD #70: Any forms that YOU issued to those who applied for a COVID-19 religious exemption from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023 approving the request for religious exemption, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation.

RPD #71: Any letters that YOU sent to YOUR employees informing them that YOU had approved their request for religious exemption from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation.

RPD #72: All “County of Los Angeles Religious Accommodation Clarification” forms that YOU received from those who applied for a COVID-19 religious exemption from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation.

RPD #73: All “Request for Reconsideration Following Department Denial of Exemption to COVID-19 Vaccinate Mandate” forms that YOU received from those who applied for a COVID-19 religious exemption from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation.

RPD #74: All “Notice of Final Determination for Exemption From the COVID19 Vaccination Policy” forms that YOU issued to those who applied for a COVID-19 religious exemption from Plaintiff’s department through January 25, 2023, with applicant’s names and corresponding id-numbers redacted with a substituted ID number for this litigation.

Defendant asserts the following objections: This request is vague and overbroad as to the use of the phrase "religious exemption." This request is also unduly burdensome, as it requests "all" APPLICATIONS through January 25, 2023. Such a request will also cause undue hardship on the Responding Party, as it would require the disclosure of hundreds of documents. Furthermore, this request also seeks documents subject to the official information privilege, and deliberative process privileges, as well as documents that would infringe on privacy rights of third-party employees, who are not part of this litigation. See Article I, Section 1 of the California Constitution; the U.S. Constitution; and Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal. 3d 652, 657. Lastly, the requested documents are contained within numerous respective employee personnel files, which further makes this request violative of privacy rights. As such, there are no responsive, unprivileged documents, that can be produced for this request.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to her case. The requested information is relevant to Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendant failed to accommodate her religious belief. The requests are narrowly tailored, not overbroad and Defendant has not shown that responding to these requests would be unduly burdensome.

 

Further, given that the names and ID numbers of the employees will be redacted, the Court finds that any privacy interests is minimal. (See Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 371 ("[protective measures, safeguards and other alternatives may minimize the privacy intrusion"].) In Defendant’s opposition, Defendant relies on Life Technologies Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 640. In Life Technologies Corp., The plaintiff sought information regarding: (1) the names of all employees terminated during a two-year period; (2) the department each employee worked for when terminated; (3) the date of termination; (4) the age of each employee at termination; (5) the reason for termination; (6) the identity (including name, address and telephone number) of all former Applied Biosystems employees still employed by the defendant after the reduction in force; and (7) whether the terminated employees were former employees of Applera or Applied Biosystems. (Id. at pp. 647- 648.) The defendant objected on the grounds that the information was irrelevant and implicated the privacy rights of third parties. (Id. at p. 648.) Here, Plaintiff is not seeking the names, identities, etc. of the other employees. Thus, the Court finds justifications for the disclosure outweigh any purported privacy interest.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses to request for production of document Nos. 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 73, and 74.

 

D.     Attorneys’ Fees

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§2031.300 and 2031.310, the court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for request of production of documents . . . unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification. (Cal. Civ. Pro. Code §2031.300(c).)

 

Plaintiff requests recoupment of fees no less than $7,500.00. Plaintiff’s counsel asserts he spent 17 hours of attorney work on the instant motion and his usual rate is $850 an hour. (Valencia Decl., ¶¶ 13-19.)

 

Since the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion, the Court finds sanctions are warranted. The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $7,500 against Defendant and its counsel of record.

 

DISPOSITION

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses to request for production of document Nos. 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 73, and 74. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 20 days.

FENIX ENTREPRENEUR, INC. v. SUN DO HONG (24STCV17180) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Justin Rodriguez (Justice Law Partners, Inc.)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Jacob Iloulian (Law Office of Jacob Illoulian) 

 

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT (filed 8/06/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff Fenix Entrepreneur, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant interpleader action against Defendant Sun Do Hong (“Defendant”).

 

The Complaint for Interpleader alleges the following. Martin Fierro, in his capacity as officer of Fenix Entrepreneur, Inc., entered into a Purchase Agreement on or about January 20, 2023 to purchase one-half (1/2) interest in the subject property located at 6836 Crenshaw Blvd. Los Angeles, CA 90043. The property prior to acceptance of Plaintiff’s offer to purchase one-half (1/2) interest subject property was vested as Joint Tenants between brother of Sun Do Hong and sister Sun Be Hong. Plaintiff deposited into Escrow a check for $5,000.00. Approximately 6 months after the opening of the Escrow, Sun Do Hong filed a Complaint to Partition the subject property with a corresponding Motion to have a Court Appoint a Referee to oversee the sale. Plaintiff is requesting the court to determine that it has a valid stake in the property based on their purchase agreement dated January 20, 2023.

 

On August 5, 2024, the Court found that the following cases, Hong v. Hong Case No. 23STCV12280 and Fenix Entrepreneur, Inc. v. Hong Case No. 24STCV17180, are related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 23STCV12280 is the lead case.

 

On August 6, 2024, Defendant filed the Demurrer to the Complaint, arguing:

·       Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action for interpleader:

o   Insufficient allegation of conflicting claims

o   Lack of proper stakeholder status

o   Failure to deposit property or money

·       The Complaint is also uncertain

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defense counsel failed to meet and confer

·        Plaintiff is a stakeholder

·       The Court should reconsider its May 1, 2024 ruling establishing a court supervised partition


Defendant did not file a reply.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating “I have reached out to Plaintiff to meet and confer regarding this demurrer. At the time of the signing of this declaration, we have not been able to resolve the issues of the demurrer.” (Iloulian Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendant’s counsel also filed a supplemental declaration attaching an email chain in which he and Plaintiff’s counsel emailed regarding scheduling a time to meet and confer. (Iloulian Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. 1.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to meet and confer.

 

Since Defendant’s counsel failed to meet and confer in person or by telephone, the Court finds the moving party has not met its obligation to meet and confer. However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).) Thus, the Court continues its discussion on the merits of the motion.

 

C.     Demurrer to Complaint

 

Defendant demurs to the Complaint for uncertainty and failure to state a cause of action.

 

As a general matter, “[d]emurrers for uncertainty are disfavored, and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.” (Lickiss v. Fin. Industry Regulatory Authority (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1125, 1135.) A demurrer for uncertainty does not address whether the pleading fails to “incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed at the uncertainty existing in the allegations actually made.” (Butler v. Sequeira (1950) 100 Cal.App.2d 143, 145-146.) Rather, a demurrer is intended to address whether a pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot understand the allegations actually made. (Id. at p. 146.)

 

Here, the Complaint is not incomprehensible because it generally alleges that Plaintiff has a stake in the subject property based on the purchase agreement dated January 20, 2023 between Plaintiff and Sun Be Hong. (Complaint, ¶¶ 1-8.) Defendant’s arguments largely pertain to failure to state sufficient facts. Thus, the demurrer for uncertainty is unsubstantiated and overruled.

 

Next, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action for interpleader.

 

When a person may be subject to conflicting claims for money or property, the person may bring an interpleader action to compel the claimants to litigate their claims among themselves. (CCP § 386(b); City of Morgan Hill v. Brown (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 1114, 1122.) Once the person admits liability and deposits the money with the court, he or she is discharged from liability and freed from the obligation of participating in the litigation between the claimants. (City of Morgan Hill, supra, 71 Cal.App.4th 1114, 1122.) C.C.P. § 386(b) requires only that the stakeholder file a verified pleading disclaiming any interest in the money or property claimed; it is the stakeholder's avowed disinterest in the interpleaded proceeds that gives him the right to interplead. (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 857, 873.)

 

CCP § 386 provides in relevant part:

(b) Any person, firm, corporation, association or other entity against whom double or multiple claims are made, or may be made, by two or more persons which are such that they may give rise to double or multiple liability, may bring an action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims. When the person, firm, corporation, association or other entity against whom such claims are made, or may be made, is a defendant in an action brought upon one or more of such claims, it may either file a verified cross-complaint in interpleader, admitting that it has no interest in the money or property claimed, or in only a portion thereof, and alleging that all or such portion is demanded by parties to such action, and apply to the court upon notice to such parties for an order to deliver such money or property or such portion thereof to such person as the court shall direct; or may bring a separate action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims. The action of interpleader may be maintained although the claims have not a common origin, are not identical but are adverse to and independent of one another, or the claims are unliquidated and no liability on the part of the party bringing the action or filing the cross-complaint has arisen. The applicant or interpleading party may deny liability in whole or in part to any or all of the claimants. The applicant or interpleading party may join as a defendant in such action any other party against whom claims are made by one or more of the claimants or such other party may interplead by cross-complaint; provided, however, that such claims arise out of the same transaction or occurrence.

. . .

(f) After any such complaint or cross-complaint in interpleader has been filed, the court in which it is filed may enter its order restraining all parties to the action from instituting or further prosecuting any other proceeding in any court in this state affecting the rights and obligations as between the parties to the interpleader until further order of the court

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiff has properly set forth an interpleader claim. A claim for interpleader must be brought by a neutral party seeking to resolve multiple conflicting claims by other parties. C.C.P. § 386(b) requires that the stakeholder file a verified pleading disclaiming any interest in the money or property claimed. Here, Plaintiff has not filed a verified pleading disclaiming any interest in the property. Instead, Plaintiff alleges that it has a stake in the subject property pursuant to the purchase agreement dated January 20, 2023 between Plaintiff and Sun Be Hong. (Complaint, ¶¶ 7-8.) Thus, Plaintiff is not a neutral party and is not the proper party to bring an interpleader claim.

 

Further, there is no indication in the Opposition that Plaintiff would be able to cure the defects of the Complaint. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Service (2000) 81 Cal. App. 4th 39, 43.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) There is no basis in law to grant leave to amend where, “the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Casterson v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 177, 190.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.
