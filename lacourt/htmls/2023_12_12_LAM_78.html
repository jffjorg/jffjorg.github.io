DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV46452    Hearing Date: December 12, 2023    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 
County of Los Angeles 
Department 78 
 
ANNETTE BREWER,
Plaintiff;  
vs. 
METHODIST HOSPITAL OF SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA,
Defendant. Case No.: 21STCV46452
Hearing Date: December 12, 2023 
 
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:  
PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE  
Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to requests for production is GRANTED as set forth below.
Within 20 days after the date of this order, Defendant must produce further verified responses and responsive documents as set forth below.
Sanctions in the amount of $1,350 are imposed jointly and severally on Defendant and Defendant’s counsel of record. Sanctions are payable 20 days after the date of this order.
Moving party to provide notice and to file proof of service of such notice within five court days after the date of this order.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for violations of the California Family Rights Act, violations of the Labor Code, wrongful termination, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiff alleges that she is a former employee of Defendant and worked as a secretary for outpatient surgery and then as a secretary/monitor technician for the orthopedics department. In June 2020, Plaintiff complained to her manager that her workload was increased and that she was working well-past her scheduled hours because the hospital was short-staffed. Plaintiff also complained that she was experiencing workplace bullying. In August 2020, Plaintiff was transferred to a different department where she began being verbally harassed by her new manager who accused her of not helping out with patient care despite the fact that she was not a nurse. Plaintiff’s work environment became unbearably hostile, and her manager would not relieve her for lunch or breaks. When Plaintiff complained, the manager yelled and berated Plaintiff in front of other employees. In September 2020, Plaintiff complained to human resources about her department’s lack of personal protective equipment. Plaintiff’s manager began making threatening gestures to her which she believed was retaliatory. In November 2020, Plaintiff became unwell at work and was tested for COVID-19. After she returned to work after receiving her test results, she was subjected to burdensome work conditions. In early December, 2020, Plaintiff fell ill and took medical leave in January 2021. When Plaintiff returned, she was terminated for taking medical leave. 
PROCEDURAL HISTORY
On December 21, 2021, Plaintiff Annette Brewer filed her Complaint against Defendant Methodist Hospital of Southern California.
On March 18, 2022, Defendant answered.
On August 18, 2023, the parties participated in IDC.
On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Requests for Production.
DISCUSSION 
Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s further responses to Requests for Production (“RPDs”).
I. Legal Standard
Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the Court may order a responding party to serve a further response to a request for production when the Court finds that any of the following apply: 
1. A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; 
2. A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or
3. An objection in the response is without merit or too general. 
To prevail, the party moving for the order must first offer specific facts demonstrating “good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) If “good cause” is shown by the moving party, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) 
Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.310, subdivision (h) authorizes a mandatory issuance of sanctions against “any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.310, subd. (h).) 
Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a). ) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)
II. Discussion
Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that she originally propounded RPDs on Defendant on October 26, 2022. (Banker Decl., ¶1.) On January 3, 2023, Defendant served its responses to the RPDs. (Id., ¶2.) On February 3, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel regarding deficiencies in its responses. (Id., ¶3.) On March 7, 2023, Defendant’s counsel agreed to produce further documents at the end of March and agreed to extend the deadline to file a motion to compel further responses to 10 days after Defendant produced additional documents. (Id., ¶4.) On April 3, 2023, Defendant represented it would produce documents by April 10, 2023. (Id., ¶5.) Plaintiff inquired after the missing responses again on April 18, 2023, May 11, 2023, and June 28, 2023. (Id., ¶¶6-7.) At an IDC in August 2023, Defendant’s counsel stated he would be producing documents shortly. (Id., ¶9.) As of the date of the filing of this motion, Defendant had not produced any documents. (Id., ¶10.) 
Defendant’s counsel represents that he served supplemental responses on November 27, 2023. (Garcia Decl., ¶¶3-4.) The responses are verified. (Opp., Exh. B.)
Plaintiff argues that the responses are still incomplete because Defendant failed to produce documents responsive to RPD 13. RPD 13 requests any and all documents referring to any insurance policies that may provide coverage for Defendant for the claims asserted by Plaintiff in this case. Because Defendant failed to respond completely to this request, Defendant must produce the declaration to its insurance policy. 
Plaintiff also argues that Defendant failed to produce records of communications between Plaintiff, her supervisor and Human Resources. Plaintiff refers to RPD numbers 26 and 28 which request documents referring to communications between Plaintiff and Kelly Rivers and Plaintiff and Anahid, respectively. Defendant’s further verified responses are confusing to the Court. Defendant must clearly state that no responsive documents exist, if that is the case. If any communications do exist, they must be produced. To the extent that the requested documents call for privileged information, a privilege log must be produced.
Plaintiff argues that the responses are not bates stamped and Defendant does not identify the specific request number to which the produced documents respond in violation of Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.280(a). Defendant must reproduce bates stamped responsive documents and indicate which request numbers the documents respond to.
With respect to sanctions, Defendant did not serve supplemental responses until November 2023, three months after Defendant agreed to produce supplemental responses. Additionally, Defendant provides no basis for failing to respond to RPD numbers 13, 26, and 28, and failing to serve bates stamped documents identified by request. The Court finds that Defendant’s conduct amounts to misuse of the discovery process. Plaintiff’s request for $1,350 in sanctions for three hours of attorney time at a rate of $450 per hour is granted.

[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:  
PLAINITFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION, SET ONE BY DEFENDANT POMONA HEALTHCARE AND WELLNESS
Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to Request for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED. Additionally, Plaintiff’s motion to compel Pomona Healthcare’s further response to FROG 17.1 is GRANTED.
Defendant is ordered to serve verified responses without objections within 10 days after the date of this order. 
Sanctions in the amount of $2,310 are imposed against Defendant. Sanctions are payable within 10 days after the date of this order.
Moving party to provide notice and to file proof of service of such notice within five court days after the date of this order.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for elder abuse and negligent hiring. Plaintiffs allege that Decedent Rosa Stephenson was a patient at Park Avenue Healthcare & Wellness Center in Pomona, California in February 2022. Defendants failed to turn Decedent every two hours to avoid the development of pressure sores, causing Plaintiff to develop severe pressure sores. Defendants’ facility was understaffed and there were no employees who could assess and treat Plaintiffs’ skin conditions. After Plaintiff was discharged from the facility, the facility was investigated by the California Department of Public Health, which found that the facility had violated federal regulations.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On June 22, 2022, Rosa Stephenson filed her Complaint against Pomona Healthcare & Wellness, LLC (“Pomona Healthcare”), Pomona Nursing & Healthcare Centre, LLC, Brius Management Co., and Rockport Administrative Services, LLC.
On August 15, 2022, Defendants answered.
On December 20, 2022, the parties participated in IDC.
On December 23, 2022, Rosa Stephenson passed away and the action was temporarily abated.
On August 3, 2023, the parties again participated in IDC.
On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff Penny Lyles, individually and as successor in interest to Rosa Stephenson, filed this motion to compel Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to Request for Admissions, Set One.
DISCUSSION 
Request for Admissions (“RFAs”), set one. 
I. Legal Standard
Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 2030.300, the Court may order a responding party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the Court finds that any of the following apply: (1) the answer to a particular interrogatory is incomplete or evasive, (2) the exercise of the option to produce documents is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is improper or inadequate, or (3) that an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. Such a motion “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040,” which “shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., sections 2016.040, 2030.300(b)(1).) 
Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300, subdivision (d) authorizes a mandatory issuance of sanctions against “any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300, subd. (d).) 
II. Discussion
Plaintiff moves to compel Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to RFA number 6.
Pomona Healthcare originally served responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories (“FROGs”), Set One on December 9, 2022. (Motion, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that the parties participated in IDC in December 2022 and agreed Pomona Healthcare would provide supplemental responses to RFA 6. (Partain Decl., ¶4.) However, the parties’ proposed order was never signed because Rosa Stephenson passed away shortly after the IDC. (Id.) At the August 3, 2023 IDC, Pomona Healthcare again agreed to provide further responses and failed to do so. (Id., ¶5.) 
Pomona Healthcare represents that it has already served a supplemental response to RFA 6. However, as Plaintiff points out, the supplemental response is not verified. Therefore, it is tantamount to no response at all, and the operative response is still the original response served in December 2022.
Plaintiff seeks further responses to RFA 6, which states “Please admit that the PLAINTIFF did not develop any pressure sores while she was a resident at the FACILITY.” 
Pomona Healthcare objected on the grounds that the term “develop any pressure sores” is vague. However, the term is not vague and refers to the pressure sores Plaintiff alleges Decedent suffered while she was under Defendant’s care. Therefore, Pomona Healthcare’s objection is without merit. The motion is granted.
Plaintiff also previously moved to compel Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to FROG number 17.1, which asked whether its responses to RFAs were unqualified admissions. Pomona Healthcare provided no response with respect to RFA 6. Because Pomona Healthcare is not required to respond to RFA 6, Pomona Healthcare must also provide a response to FROG number 17.1 with respect to RFA 6.
Sanctions are mandatory because Pomona Healthcare unsuccessfully opposed this motion. Plaintiff’s request for $2,310 in sanctions is granted.

The Court requires further briefing on the issue of the reasonableness of Plaintiff’s counsel’s billing. 
Here, Defendants propose to deduct 186.3 hours from Plaintiff’s counsels’ proposed billing.
