DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV33046    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

July 1, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV33046

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Huge USA, Inc. and Jin Ser Park

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Nam Suk Park
 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Nam Suk Park (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Huge USA, Inc. (“Huge USA”) and Jin Ser Park (“Park”) for wage and hour violations.  Park is the owner of Huge USA, which is a garment importing company. 

Plaintiff alleges Defendants hired Plaintiff to perform job duties, including “shipping, production, outsourcing and ordering fabrics, reporting manufacturing schedules, and monitoring logistics schedules” (Complaint, ¶ 10); Defendants paid Plaintiff a flat salary of $5,000 per month, and did not pay Plaintiff overtime compensation or provide Plaintiff with meal and rest breaks; (id., ¶¶ 11-19) ; and Park struck Plaintiff while Plaintiff worked for Defendants (id., ¶ 20). 

Defendants move for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action therein.  However, Defendants have not “repeated, verbatim, in the separate statement of undisputed material facts” each issue on which Defendants seek summary adjudication and identified “each supporting material fact claimed to be without dispute with respect to” each issue.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1350, subds. (b), (d).) Accordingly, the Court denies the motion for summary adjudication as procedurally improper. “There is little flexibility in the procedural imperatives of [Code of Civil Procedure section 437c], and the issues raised by a motion for summary judgment (or summary adjudication) are pure questions of law.  As a result, section 437c is unforgiving; a failure to comply with any one of its myriad requirements is likely to be fatal to the offending party.”  (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1607.)  Therefore, the court considers the motion as a motion for summary judgment only. 

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

DISCUSSION

First Through Fourth, Sixth, and Seventh Causes of Action

            Plaintiff’s first through fourth, sixth, and seventh causes of action are for wage and hour violations.  Defendants contend Plaintiff cannot prevail because Plaintiff was exempt under the managerial or executive exemption.  The executive exemption applies to “any employee: [¶] (a) Whose duties and responsibilities involve the management of the enterprise in which he/she is employed or of a customarily recognized department or subdivision thereof; and [¶] (b) Who customarily and regularly directs the work of two or more other employees therein; and [¶] (c) Who has the authority to hire or fire other employees or whose suggestions and recommendations as to the hiring or firing and as to the advancement and promotion or any other change of status of other employees will be given particular weight; and [¶] (d) Who customarily and regularly exercises discretion and independent judgment; and [¶] (e) Who is primarily engaged in duties which meet the test of the exemption. . . .  [¶] (f) Such an employee must also earn a monthly salary equivalent to no less than two (2) times the state minimum wage for full-time employment.”  (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 8, § 11040, subd. 1(A)(1).)  An employee is primarily engaged in executive duties if that employee spends more than half of his or her work time performing executive duties.  (Lab. Code, § 515, subd. (e).) 

            In Park’s declaration in support of the motion, he avers Plaintiff worked as Huge USA’s shipping manager, Plaintiff supervised warehouse workers, and Plaintiff’s primary duty was “to supervise the process of packing and shipping out [Huge USA’s] products stored in the warehouse for customer's and retailer's orders, in conjunction with the warehouse workers who usually number around ten (10) to twelve (12) employees.”  (Declaration of Jin Ser Park, ¶¶ 4, 5.)  Park further states, as shipping manager, Plaintiff’s job was to “review[] . . . Sales Order[s] and assign[] one of the warehouse workers to pack, box, and prepare the products in the Sales Order for shipping to the customer or retailer, or alternatively, . . . leave[] the Sales Order[s] in a stack for the warehouse workers to review and begin packing on their own” (id., ¶ 7); in situations in which “there are large numbers of Sales Orders at one time, and it is unlikely that [Huge USA] can take care of them all in a single day,” Plaintiff would use his “discretion and judgment and independently decide which Sales Orders to prioritize . . . by considering factors such as the size of the Sales Order (i.e., the number of products requested by the customer or retailer) and the date that the customer or retailer requested the products” (id., ¶ 8); Plaintiff had “authority to hire and terminate warehouse employees” provided Plaintiff notified Park, and Plaintiff had “authority to approve warehouse employees' requests for time off, sick leave, and vacation days, without seeking the approval of any other officer or employee of” Huge USA, as well as “authority to issue . . . Warning Notices to warehouse employees and decide when warehouse employees need to come early or stay after normal working hours” (id., ¶ 10); and, on or about May 1, 2021, Huge USA promoted Plaintiff to production director, which involved working in the company offices, interfacing with Huge USA’s vendors, and “overseeing the schedule for the arrival of goods from Chinese manufacturers” (id., ¶ 20). 

            Defendants’ evidence meets their burden of showing Plaintiff was exempt under the managerial or executive exemption and, therefore, shifts the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether that exemption applies.

In opposition, Plaintiff relies primarily on his own declaration.  In it, Plaintiff states he did not exercise any independent authority, and upper management had to approve his decisions.  (Declaration of Nam Suk Park, ¶ 4); had no authority to change the priority of sales orders or to assign sales orders to particular employees (id., ¶ 6), to hire, fire, or discipline other employees (id., ¶ 9), and to schedule employees (id., ¶ 11); and, even after he transitioned into another role, he had no authority to manage or oversee the production process or act independently (id., ¶¶ 12, 13, 15).  Plaintiff also related he was not proficient in English or Spanish , and therefore, he had no ability to oversee nor communicate with any of the warehouse workers.  (Id., ¶¶ 2, 6, 25.) 

In denying he exercised managerial powers as laid out above in the title 8, section 11040 regulation and Labor Code section 515, Plaintiff’s testimony sufficiently debunks evidence presented by Defendants and raises triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff was exempt under the managerial or executive exemption.  The Court, therefore, denies the motion for summary judgment. 

Defendants also advance evidence Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claims for meal and rest breaks because Defendants permitted Plaintiff to take such breaks.  (Jin Ser Park Declaration, ¶ 29.)  Plaintiff does not address this issue in his opposition.  Given such silence, Plaintiff has waived the right to argue that issue.  (Assad v. Southern Pacific Transportation Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1609, 1615.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff has failed to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether he can prevail on his third and fourth causes of action.  However, the Court must deny the motion for summary judgment, as Plaintiff raises triable issues of material fact as to other causes of action.

Fifth Cause of Action: Failure to Reimburse Expenses

Defendants propounded discovery on Plaintiff, in which Defendants asked Plaintiff to state all facts on which Plaintiff bases his fifth cause of action for failure to reimburse business expenses.  In response, Plaintiff stated, “Defendants required responding party to pay for the green card processing, including attorney’s fees and the advertisement required for the green card process with which responding party complied.”  (Declaration of Daniel E. Park, Exhibit 2.)  Plaintiff adds, in his declaration, Defendants agreed to sponsor his green card application, but Plaintiff paid the fees and costs.  (Declaration of Nam Suk Park, ¶ 17.)  “An employer shall indemnify his or her employee for all necessary expenditures or losses incurred by the employee in direct consequence of the discharge of his or her duties, or of his or her obedience to the directions of the employer . . . .”  (Lab. Code, § 2802, subd. (a).)  Plaintiff’s testimony raises triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff incurred expenses at Defendants’ direction, which Defendants failed to reimburse.  As such, Plaintiff raises triable issues of material fact as to whether he can prevail on his fifth cause of action.

Eighth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

            “The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

In the complaint, Plaintiff alleges, “During Plaintiff’s employment with Defendants, [Park] repeatedly physically harassed Plaintiff, including smashing Plaintiff’s neck and back, and grabbing Plaintiff’s belly without his consent.”  (Complaint, ¶ 20.)

In Park’s declaration in support of the motion, Park states, “I have on certain occasions tapped Plaintiff on the stomach and back.  However, when I did so, I did not intend to hurt, offend, embarrass, or otherwise harm Plaintiff.  I meant it as a friendly gesture. Also, Plaintiff never complained to me or to anyone to my knowledge about my tapping him on the stomach and back.”  (Declaration of Jin Ser Park, ¶ 30.)  Defendants also relate, at Plaintiff’s deposition, Plaintiff testified Park would “pinch [Plaintiff’s] stomach and grab [Plaintiff’s] stomach real hard so it would hurt, and he would say, ‘You piece of grease.· You butter.’”  (Declaration of Daniel E. Park, Exhibit 1, p. 94.)  Additionally, in response to Defendant’s interrogatory requests, Plaintiff related Park smashed his neck and back and grabbed his belly, referring to it as “chunk of meats.”  (Danile E. Park Declaration, Exhibit 2, Response to Special Interrogatory No. 49.)

Defendants’ evidence does not satisfy Defendants’ burden of demonstrating Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  The Court cannot conclude pinching and grabbing Plaintiff in the workplace while referring to him as grease, butter, and chunk of meat, all of which Plaintiff perceived as insults and which made Plaintiff feel shame and embarrassment, do not constitute extreme or outrageous conduct as a matter of law.  Thus, Defendants have failed to shift the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on his eighth cause of action.

Defendants rely on Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, which is not on point.  In that case, the defendant was the trustee of the decedent's trust, and the plaintiff was the decedent’s former wife. (Id., at p. 1039.)  The defendant made sexual advances toward the plaintiff, suggesting that he would authorize payment from the trust if the plaintiff would be “nice” to him, and adding the defendant would “get [the plaintiff] on [her] knees eventually” and have sex with her.  (Id., at p. 1040.)  The California Supreme Court reasoned, “Viewed in the context of plaintiff's legal battles, over a five-year span, with defendant and the two other trustees regarding their allocation of the trust funds, defendant's inappropriate comments fall far short of conduct that is so ‘outrageous’ that it ““‘“exceed[s] all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”””’ (Id., p. 1051.)  In this case, Plaintiff was Defendants’ employee, and not an opposing party in a contentious legal proceeding.  Conduct that is not outrageous between opposing parties in litigation could be outrageous in the workplace.  Further, Park’s conduct is not limited to insults, but also includes unwanted touching. 

For the same reason, Defendants’ reliance on Yurick v. Superior Court (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 1116 (Yurick) is misplaced.  In that case, the Court of Appeal held a supervisor telling an employee in the presence of others that anyone over 40, including the employee, was senile and the employee was a liar did not amount to outrageous conduct.  (Id., at p. 1129.)  Again, in this case, Park admittedly touched Plaintiff, and Plaintiff contends he perceived the unwanted touching was also intended to insult him.  Park’s conduct is more outrageous than the conduct that was at issue in Yurick.

Ninth Cause of Action: Battery

To state a claim for battery, Plaintiff must show Park intentionally acted in a way that resulted in harmful or offensive contact with Plaintiff’s body; Plaintiff did not consent to the contact; and the contact harmed Plaintiff.  (Brown v. Ransweiler (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 516, 526.)

As set forth above, Defendants’ own evidence demonstrates Park intentionally grabbed and pinched Plaintiff in a way that upset Plaintiff, and to which Plaintiff did not consent.  Defendants fail to meet their burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on his ninth cause of action for battery.  Defendants contend Plaintiff cannot show Park intended the unwanted touching.  However, the only evidence on this point is Park’s declaration.  As such, the Court exercises its discretion and determines an additional basis for denying summary judgment is because, in this instance, “a material fact is an individual's state of mind, or lack thereof, and that fact is sought to be established solely by the individual's affirmation thereof.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (e).) 

Tenth Cause of Action: Unfair Business Practices           

      Defendants’ only argument regarding the tenth cause of action for unfair business practices is it fails as derivative of the causes of action for wage and hour violations.  (See Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 28.)  As set forth above, Plaintiff raises triable issues of material fact as to whether he can prevail on his wage and hour claims.  As such, Plaintiff likewise raises triable issues of material fact as to whether he can prevail on the tenth cause of action for unfair business practices.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied.

Defendants are ordered to serve notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service concerning such notice.
