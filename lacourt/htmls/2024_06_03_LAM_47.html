DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 23STCV09430    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     June 3, 2024               TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Sung Sik Lim v. Peter Eunsang Kim

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV09430           

 

(1) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(2) MOTINO TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Sung Sik Lim

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 5/29/24

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/27/23: Complaint filed.

·         12/29/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for fraud and breach of contract. The parties were equal owners of a corporation, Papilion, Inc., whose sole asset was a restaurant named Concerto. On January 31, 2020, the Restaurant’s original lease expired, and the landlord commenced an unlawful detainer action. Defendant wished to contest the unlawful detainer, and contracted with Plaintiff to purchase Plaintiff’s interest in the business and defend the unlawful detainer action. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant never made the agreed-upon payments for Plaintiff’s interest in the business.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production propounded to Defendant.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

           

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART with respect to Requests Nos. 1 and 11 and otherwise DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

            Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories propounded to Defendant.

 

Legal Standards

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to an interrogatory when the court finds that: “(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete[;] (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate[; or] (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

A party making a motion to compel further responses must include a declaration stating facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally the issues presented by the motion before filing the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.310(b).) 

 

            The Declaration of Dennis McPhillips attached to the Motion states that the parties met and conferred electronically between January 10 and February 20, 2024 regarding this dispute and agreed to schedule an Informal Discovery Conference before the Court on April 15, 2024. (Declaration of Dennis McPhillips ISO Mot. ¶ 16 Exhs. C-G.) However, Defendant never prepared an IDC statement and did not appear at the IDC. (Id. ¶ 17.) Plaintiff has therefore satisfied the statutory meet-and-confer requirements.

 

Timeliness

 

A motion to compel a further response must be noticed within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(c), see also Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409; Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.) Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel further responses. (Id.) The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.)

 

            The meet-and-confer correspondence provided by Plaintiff’s counsel includes an express agreement by Defendant’s counsel to an extension of the time to file this motion to 25 days after the Informal Discovery Conference. (McPhillips Decl. Exh. D.) As the Informal Discovery Conference took place on April 15, 2024, the deadline to file this motion was May 10, 2024, by operation of this agreement. The motion was filed and served on May 2, 2024, well ahead of that deadline. The Motion is therefore timely.

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff propounded special interrogatories to Defendant via email on November 8, 2023. (McPhillips Decl. Exh. A.) Defendant responded on December 7, 2023 with only objections and a refusal to answer the interrogatories while Defendant’s demurrer to the Complaint was still pending. (Id. Exh. B.) As Defendant has not responded to this motion, Defendant has failed to justify these objections. Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an order compelling further responses.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff requests sanctions only against Defendant in the amount of $3,435.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions on any attorney engaging in the misuse of the discovery process by requiring that attorney to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300(d) requires the Court to impose sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

            In the Court’s view, invalid or unsupported objections are more properly imputed to legal counsel, who is expected to understand the procedural and substantive requirements for an objection, and not the client on whose behalf those objections are asserted. An award of sanctions that has not been properly requested in a notice of motion does not comply with the statutory requirements and is a violation of the opposing party’s right to due process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.040.) As counsel has not been named in the Notice of Motion as a target of sanctions, the Court is without authority to impose sanctions upon Defendant’s counsel, and the Court finds that the imposition of sanctions on Defendant would be unjust in this instance.

 

//

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production sent to Defendant.

 

Legal Standards

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to a demand for inspection when the court finds that: “(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the moving party to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) These facts must also be set forth in a separate statement filed by the moving party. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(c).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

A party making a motion to compel further responses must also include a declaration stating facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally the issues presented by the motion before filing the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

 

            The Declaration of Dennis McPhillips attached to the Motion states that the parties met and conferred electronically between January 10 and February 20, 2024 regarding this dispute and agreed to schedule an Informal Discovery Conference before the Court on April 15, 2024. (Declaration of Dennis McPhillips ISO Mot. ¶ 16 Exhs. C-G.) However, Defendant never prepared an IDC statement and did not appear at the IDC. (Id. ¶ 17.) Plaintiff has therefore satisfied the statutory meet-and-confer requirements.

 

Timeliness

 

A motion to compel further responses to requests for production must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

The meet-and-confer correspondence provided by Plaintiff’s counsel includes an express agreement by Defendant’s counsel to an extension of the time to file this motion to 25 days after the Informal Discovery Conference. (McPhillips Decl. Exh. D.) As the Informal Discovery Conference took place on April 15, 2024, the deadline to file this motion was therefore May 10, 2024, by operation of this agreement. The motion was filed and served on May 2, 2024, well ahead of that deadline. The Motion is therefore timely.

 

Good Cause

 

            Plaintiff propounded requests for production to Defendant via email on November 8, 2023. (McPhillips Decl. Exh. A.) Request No. 1, which seeks any communications between Defendant and Plaintiff since December 2, 2015, (Exh. A. No. 1), and Request No. 12, which seeks any correspondence regarding acquisition of the Concerto Restaurant (Id. No. 12) are facially relevant to the allegations in the pleadings. However, nothing in either the original Complaint nor the First Amended Complaint justifies the remaining overarching requests, which seek, in essence, all Papilion and Concerto financial records and other business documents in Defendant’s possession. Plaintiff’s conclusory assertion that they are relevant to Plaintiff’s claim that Defendant operated the business without paying Plaintiff the money owed does not suffice to establish good cause for such wide-ranging requests. The Court will therefore only address the sufficiency of Defendant’s responses to Requests Nos. 1 and 12.

 

Defendant’s Responses

 

            Defendant responded to the requests at issue by asserting boilerplate objections and refusing to respond to the request while Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint remained pending. As Defendant has not responded to this motion, Defendant has failed to justify these objections. Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an order compelling further responses.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff also requests sanctions against Defendant for asserting invalid objections to the requests for production. However, as Plaintiff did not fully prevail on this motion, Plaintiff is not entitled to sanctions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART with respect to Requests Nos. 1 and 12 and otherwise DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

           

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART with respect to Requests Nos. 1 and 12 and otherwise DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

            Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

            Moving Party to give notice.

HEARING DATE:     June 3, 2024               TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Robert Golightly v. Pacific Coast Sustainable Assets, LLC, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV28108           

 

DEMURRER TO ANSWER

 

MOVING PARTY:               Cross-Complainants Pacific Coast Sustainable Assets, LLC, and Yajac Agriculture, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Cross-Defendant Robert Golightly

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         10/24/22: Complaint filed.

·         08/04/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         03/25/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Pacific Coast Sustainable Assets, LLC and Yajac Agriculture, LLC as to Robert Golightly

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract. Plaintiff contracted with Defendants to manage and operate a farm owned by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that he was terminated in violation of the terms of his contract.

 

Cross-Complainants demur to the Answer to the Cross-Complaint filed by Robert Golightly.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Cross-Complainants’ Demurrer to the Answer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to all affirmative defenses, except for the legal defense of failure to state a cause of action.

 

            Cross-Defendant shall have 20 days from the date of this order to file a First Amended Answer.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Cross-Complainants demur to the Answer to the Cross-Complaint filed by Robert Golightly.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 430.20 provides:

 

A party against whom an answer has been filed may object, by demurrer as provided in Section 430.30, to the answer upon any one or more of the following grounds:

 

(a)  The answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

(b)  The answer is uncertain. As used in this subdivision, “uncertain” includes ambiguous and unintelligible.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 430.20.)

 

            Regarding the standards for pleading an affirmative defense:

 

 (1) In General. An affirmative defense must be pleaded in the same manner as if the facts were set forth in a complaint. In other words, the general requirement of stating the ultimate facts applies and, where particularity in pleading is necessary in a complaint, it is equally necessary in an affirmative defense involving the issue. (Bruck v. Tucker (1871) 42 C. 346, 352; Greiss v. State Inv. & Ins. Co. (1893) 98 C. 241, 244, 33 P. 195; Bradbury v. Higginson (1914) 167 C. 553, 557, 140 P. 254 [if matter set up is equitable cause of action, answer must contain all averments essential to statement of cause of action as such]; see 19B Am.Jur. P.P. Forms (2007 ed.), Pleading, §85 et seq.)

 

(2) Argumentative Denials. The affirmative form in which the defendant phrases his or her answering averments does not make them new matter. If they merely contradict essential allegations of the complaint, they are simply denials in affirmative form (“argumentative denials”). (Frisch v. Caler (1862) 21 C. 71, 74; Goddard v. Fulton (1863) 21 C. 430, 436; Rancho Santa Margarita v. Vail (1938) 11 C.2d 501, 543, 81 P.2d 533; Jolley v. Clemens (1938) 28 C.A.2d 55, 65, 82 P.2d 51, supra, §1077.)

 

(6 Witkin Cal. Proc. Plead § 1122.)

 

            Affirmative defenses that are mere legal conclusions are insufficient:

 

The . . . answer alleged merely: “Plaintiff’s claims are barred in whole or in part by the doctrine of waiver,” and “Plaintiff's causes of action, or some of them, are barred by the doctrine of laches because plaintiff unreasonably delayed bringing suit, causing defendants to reasonably rely on the status quo.”

 

These affirmative defenses consist of legal conclusions that could survive neither a demurrer nor a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Citations omitted.)

 

(Westly v. Board of Administration (2003) 105 Cal. App. 4th 1095, 1117.) Rather than being “proffered in the form of terse legal conclusions,” affirmative defenses must plead facts “averred as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and are alleged in the complaint.” (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384.)

 

A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a pleading is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. (See Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the pleading is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond--i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.  (See Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) "The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts."  (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet-and-confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Faryan Afifi states that he sent a meet-and-confer letter to Cross-Defendant’s counsel on April 25, 2024 via email, requesting a substantive response to the objections raised by April 29, 2024. (Declaration of Faryan A. Afifi ISO Demurrer ¶2; Exh. 1.) Cross-Defendant’s counsel responded that he would get back to Cross-Complainants’ counsel by “the latter part of next week” on April 26, 2024, ignoring the given deadline. (Id. ¶ 3 Exh. 2.) As of May 3, 2024, the date the demurer was filed, Cross-Defendant’s counsel had not followed up. (Id.) The Court therefore finds that Cross-Complainants have satisfied their statutory meet-and-confer obligations.

 

//

 

//

Analysis

 

            Cross-Complainants demur to the Answer to the Cross-Complaint with respect to each of the eight affirmative defenses asserted based on a failure to state facts sufficient to support a defense and for uncertainty.

 

1.      First Affirmative Defense: Failure to State Facts Sufficient to Support a Cause of Action

This defense, mislabeled as an affirmative defense, alleges that Cross-Complainants’ causes of action fail because they have failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for each of the causes of action asserted. (Answer ¶ 2.) “The defense of failure to state a claim is, in essence, an argument that the plaintiff has not met its burden of alleging the elements of its claims.” (LL B Sheet 1, LLC v. Loskutoff (2019) 362 F.Supp. 3d 804, 818.) It is not an affirmative defense that relies on the pleading of new factual allegations, so it is not subject to a demurrer that challenges the sufficiency of the underlying allegations.  Accordingly, the demurrer to this defense is not well-taken and is overruled.    

 

2.      Second Affirmative Defense: Unclean Hands

 

This affirmative defense alleges that Cross-Complainants are barred from recovering against Cross-Defendant by reason of their own conduct under the doctrine of unclean hands. (Answer ¶ 3.) Cross-Complainants demur to this defense for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense. The Court concurs with Cross-Complainants. This defense is merely a conclusory legal assertion without supporting facts. Cross-Defendant has failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

3.      Third Affirmative Defense: Laches

 

This affirmative defense alleges that Cross-Complainants are barred from recovering against Cross-Defendant under the doctrine of laches. (Answer ¶ 4.) Cross-Complainants demur to this defense for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense. The Court concurs with Cross-Complainants. This defense is merely a conclusory legal assertion without supporting facts. Cross-Defendant has failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

4.      Fourth Affirmative Defense: Waiver

 

This affirmative defense alleges that Cross-Complainants are barred from recovering against Cross-Defendant under the doctrine of waiver. (Answer ¶ 5.) Cross-Complainants demur to this defense for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense. The Court concurs with Cross-Complainants. This defense is merely a conclusory legal assertion without supporting facts. Cross-Defendant has failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

//

5.      Fifth Affirmative Defense: Statute of Limitations

 

            This affirmative defense alleges that Cross-Complainants’ action is barred by unspecified statutes of limitations. (Answer ¶ 6.) Cross-Complainants demur to this defense for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

In pleading a statute of limitations, “it is not necessary to state the facts showing the defense, but it may be stated generally that the cause of action is barred by the provisions of Section ____ (giving the number of the section and subdivision thereof, if it is so divided, relied upon) of the Code of Civil Procedure.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 458.) In other words, there are two ways to plead the statute of limitations. (Brown v. World Church (1969) 272 Cal.App.2d 684, 691.) “[F]irst by alleging all of the facts showing that the action is barred . . . and indicating that the lateness of the commencement of the action is being urged as a defense.” (Ibid.) Second, “to specify the applicable section, and, if such section is divided into subdivisions, to specify the particular subdivision or subdivisions thereof.” (Ibid. [holding that the defendants had failed to properly plead the statute of limitations because “Section 337 of the Code of Civil Procedure has two subdivisions and defendants failed to specify under which subdivision of the section they allegedly come”]) 

 

Here, Cross-Defendant has neither alleged the specific statutory section applicable, along with any necessary subsections, nor any facts showing the action is barred by any statute of limitations. As a result, Cross-Defendant’s affirmative defense does not qualify as a sufficient pleading of a statute of limitations defense.  The demurrer to this affirmative defense must be sustained.

 

6.      Sixth Affirmative Defense: Judicial Admissions

 

This affirmative defense alleges that the claims asserted by Cross-Complainants are barred by the judicial admissions made by Yajac Agriculture and Yael and Uzzi Reiss in Yajac Agriculture, LLC v. Epstein (LASC Case No. BC582745.) (Answer ¶ 7.) Cross-Complainants demur to this defense for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense. The Court concurs with Cross-Complainants. This defense is merely a conclusory legal assertion without supporting facts. Cross-Defendant has failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

7.      Seventh Affirmative Defense: Estoppel

 

This affirmative defense alleges that Cross-Complainants are barred from recovering against Cross-Defendant under the doctrine of estoppel. (Answer ¶ 8.) Cross-Complainants demur to this defense for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense. The Court concurs with Cross-Complainants. This defense is merely a conclusory legal assertion without supporting facts. Cross-Defendant has failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

//

8.      Eighth Affirmative Defense: Irreconcilable Conflict

 

This affirmative defense alleges that Cross-Complainants are barred from recovering against Cross-Defendant because there is an irreconcilable conflict between Yajac Agriculture and Yael Reiss on the one side and Pacific Coast Assets, LLC on the other side. (Answer ¶ 9.) As Cross-Complainants argue, “Irreconcilable conflict” is not an affirmative defense. Further, although the answer makes several assertions regarding “Yael Reiss’ intentional creation of a false February 3 2012 Operating Agreement,” and the resulting consequences, Cross-Defendant’s answer does not set forth any factual support for those assertions and sets forth no colorable legal theory under which they might serve as a defense, affirmative or otherwise. The Court therefore concurs with Cross-Complainants that this cause of action is not adequately pled.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) California law imposes the burden on the party who filed the pleading to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims.  (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the [pleading] shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Here, Cross-Defendant has not demonstrated the manner by which the Answer might be amended to properly state his defenses against the claims in the Cross-Complaint. However, in light of the liberal standard for permitting amendment of the pleadings, and the defects in the answer arising merely from a paucity of alleged facts, the Court will exercise its discretion to permit leave to amend the answer.

 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Cross-Complainants’ Demurrer to the Answer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to all affirmative defenses, except for the legal defense of failure to state a cause of action.

 

            Cross-Defendant shall have 20 days from the date of this order to file a First Amended Answer.

HEARING DATE:     June 3, 2024                           TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         John & Sheryl Associates d/b/a Discovery World Early Education Center v. CYP LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV30527           

 

MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant CYP LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff John & Sheryl Associates d/b/a Discovery World Early Education Center.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract that was filed on December 14, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant, as the landlord of property leased by Plaintiff, refused to approve Plaintiff’s request to install new playground equipment without adequate justification.

 

Defendant moves for reconsideration of the Court’s March 29, 2024 ruling denying Defendant’s special motion to strike.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant moves for reconsideration of the Court’s March 29, 2024 ruling denying Defendant’s special motion to strike.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 1008 provides, in relevant part:

 

a) When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.

 

* * *

 

(c) If a court at any time determines that there has been a change of law that warrants it to reconsider a prior order it entered, it may do so on its own motion and enter a different order.

 

(d) A violation of this section may be punished as a contempt and with sanctions as allowed by Section 128.7. In addition, an order made contrary to this section may be revoked by the judge or commissioner who made it, or vacated by a judge of the court in which the action or proceeding is pending.

 

(e) This section specifies the court’s jurisdiction with regard to applications for reconsideration of its orders and renewals of previous motions, and applies to all applications to reconsider any order of a judge or court, or for the renewal of a previous motion, whether the order deciding the previous matter or motion is interim or final. No application to reconsider any order or for the renewal of a previous motion may be considered by any judge or court unless made according to this section.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(a), (c)-(e) (bold emphasis added).)

 

Jurisdiction

 

            Defendant does not bring this motion as a formal motion for reconsideration under section 1008 but rather styles it as a request for the Court to reconsider the March 29, 2024 ruling on its own motion pursuant to subdivision (c). Plaintiff argues that such a request is improper because subdivision (c) does not permit Defendant to petition the Court for reconsideration, and because Defendant has not complied with the procedural requirements of subdivisions (a) and (b). Even so, the Court has discretion to reconsider an order on its own motion under both section 1008(c) and its “constitutionally derived authority.” (Le Francois v. Goel¿(2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1096.)  Section 1008 limits the parties’ ability to file repetitive motions to reconsider a matter already decided; it does not limit the court’s ability to reconsider its prior interim orders to correct its own errors. (Id. at pg. 1109.) The Court therefore has jurisdiction to consider the request on its merits.

 

Analysis

 

            Defendant contends that the Court should reconsider its March 29, 2024 ruling denying Defendant’s special motion to strike the Complaint. In that motion, Defendant argued that this action, asserting claims for breach of contract and breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, arose from statements made by Defendant in connection with an issue under consideration by a judicial body. The Complaint alleges that Defendant cited health and safety claims asserted by Plaintiff in a separate action concerning the interior of the subject property as justification to deny Plaintiff’s request to approve installation of outdoor playground equipment. (Complaint ¶¶ 9, 13, 15, 23.) Defendant argued that these justifications grounded Plaintiff’s claims in protected activity under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. The Court rejected that contention, finding that the gravamen of Plaintiff’s claims in this action arose from Defendant’s refusal to approve Plaintiff’s planned construction of an outdoor playground. (March 29, 2024 Minute Order p.5.) With respect to the breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, the Court specifically found that the issue in this case vis a vis Defendant’s good faith or lack thereof is whether the structural integrity claims asserted in the first lawsuit have any relation to Plaintiff’s request to build a playground, and that this relationship is not a matter that was under consideration or review in the first lawsuit. (Id. p. 6.)

 

            Defendant argues that the Court accepted oral representations by Plaintiff’s counsel that Plaintiff withdrew a request to make interior renovations that had been simultaneously pending. (See Declaration of Mark K. Worthge ISO Mot. Exh. 1. p.8:1-11.)  According to Defendant, that representation is not supported by evidence and, at the time Defendant denied the renovation request, those interior improvements were part of the request and had not been withdrawn. Defendant’s argument entirely misconstrues the Court’s ruling and misapplies the standard for a special motion to strike under section 425.16. In the first instance, it is Defendant’s burden, not Plaintiff’s, to demonstrate that the action is based on protected activity. (Kajima Engineering & Construction, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 921, 928; Rivero v. American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees, AFL-CIO (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 913, 919.)  Further, in evaluating whether any cause of action arises from protected conduct, it is proper for a court to consider, in addition to any declarations submitted, the relevant allegations of the complaint.  (Bel Air Internet, LLC v. Morales (2018) 20 Cal. App. 5th 924, 936.)  Here, the complaint plainly attacks Defendant’s allegedly unjustified interference with Plaintiff’s efforts to install new outdoor playground equipment, not to do interior renovations, and thus provides an adequate basis for the Court’s first-prong determination.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 9, 12-14, 16, 20, 22.) Second, as the Court stated in its ruling on March 29, Defendant’s justifications for denying the renovation request are not the injury-producing conduct on which the action is based; it is the denial itself that is the alleged cause of the injury on both causes of action. With respect to the breach of contract claim, Defendant’s justifications are entirely collateral, as the denial itself is an action, not a statement.

 

            With respect to the alleged breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, the Court found that the dispute—i.e., whether Defendant’s denial of the request was done in good faith—centers on whether the renovations at issue here bear any connection to the first lawsuit concerning the interior of the building. Defendant contended, as it does here, that the matters are related and thus that it had a good-faith basis to deny the request. Plaintiff contends that there is no relation and that Defendant’s justification was a mere excuse for a bad-faith denial. This question is not “under consideration or review” in the first action.

 

            The Court did not err in its March 29, 2024 ruling and declines Defendant’s invitation to reconsider that decision.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.
