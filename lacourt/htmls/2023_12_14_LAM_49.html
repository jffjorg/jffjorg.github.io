Case Number: 18STCV04752    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: 49

Kurt Knutsson, et al. v. Judith Salkow Shapiro, et al.

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION
 

MOVING PARTIES: Defendant Judith Salkow Shapiro, APC and Defendant San Pasqual Fiduciary Trust Company

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc. bring this suit against the law corporation of their deceased former lawyer, Judith Salkow Shapiro, for legal malpractice and fraud in the handling of an underlying employment and right of publicity action against KTLA, Local TV, and other television stations.  The operative Second Amended Complaint asserts causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Professional Negligence/Legal Malpractice, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, and (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty.  

Defendant Judith Salkow Shapiro, APC and Defendant San Pasqual Fiduciary Trust Company now move for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication. Plaintiffs opposed. After the September 26, 2023, hearing on this motion, the court continued the hearing and invited supplemental briefing. The court has read and considered Defendants’ and Plaintiffs’ supplemental briefs of 11/22/23 and 12/06/23, respectively, and now rules as follows.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED. Defendants’ alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication is also DENIED.

Plaintiff to give notice, unless waived.

DISCUSSION:

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

I. Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing motions for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent's claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294).  Thus, summary judgment is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c); Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.)

As to each claim as framed by the Complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A defendant has met its burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if it demonstrates the absence of any single essential element of plaintiff’s case or a complete defense to plaintiff’s action.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(o)(2); Bacon v. Southern Cal. Edison Co. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 854, 858.)  Once the defendant moving party has met the burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material facts exists as to a cause of action or a defense thereto.  (§ 437c(o)(2).)

II. Judicial Notice

IV. Analysis

A. Background

This legal malpractice action arises out of now deceased attorney Judith Shapiro’s representation of Plaintiffs in Knutsson, et al. v. KTLA, et al. Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. BC500792 (the “Underlying Action”). There, Plaintiffs brought right of publicity claims against KTLA, Local TV, and other television stations. Initially, Plaintiffs defeated Local TV’s motion for summary judgment of the right of publicity claims. However, Local TV then successfully petitioned the Court of Appeal for a writ of mandate. As a result, summary judgment was granted against Plaintiffs, and on April 11, 2017, Knutsson was ordered to pay $327,171 in attorney’s fees to Local TV. 

Now, in the Second Amended Complaint, Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc., allege Defendants “failed to obtain the learning or skill necessary to evaluate the claim they included for violation of California Civil Code Section 3344 (Use of Another’s Name, Voice, Signature, Photograph or Likeness in Advertising or Soliciting Without Prior Consent), nor did they associate with competent counsel or refer the matter to other counsel with sufficient learning and skill to evaluate such a claim.” (SAC ¶ 22.) Following an appeal, Plaintiffs “were ordered to pay attorneys’ fees to Local TV, LLC in the sum of $327,171.00 plus post judgment interest of 10% per annum, which required Plaintiffs to obtain litigation financing that required them to give up a substantial percentage of their ultimate recovery in the underlying litigation.” (Id. ¶ 32.) 

B. Statute of Limitations and Continuing Representation

1. Background Authorities

At issue in this motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication is whether Plaintiffs’ claims are barred by the applicable statute of limitations. Code of Civil Procedure section 340.6 provides that the statute of limitations for legal malpractice is one year.  However, subsection (a)(2) provides that the time to bring a legal malpractice claim “shall be tolled during the time…[t]he attorney continues to represent the plaintiff regarding the specific subject matter in which the alleged wrongful act or omission occurred.”  

Continuous representation is an objective standard, and “the limitations period can be tolled even though the client is aware of the attorney’s negligence.”  (Nielsen, 157 Cal. App. 4th at 1049 [“Continuity of representation ultimately depends, not on the client's subjective beliefs, but rather on evidence of an ongoing mutual relationship and of activities in furtherance of the relationship.”].)  The analysis is “when the client actually has or reasonably should have no expectation that the attorney will provide further legal services. [Citation.]”  (Id. at 1050.)  Thus, on a motion for summary judgment that invokes “continuous representation,” the question is “whether, using an objective standard, there are triable issues of fact about whether there was an ongoing relationship and activities in furtherance of the relationship.”  (Id. at 1050.)  

To illustrate this standard, the court turns to a discussion of the relevant case law. In Nielsen v. Beck (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 1041, a client sued its former attorney for legal malpractice. (Id. at 1048.)  The trial court granted the attorney’s motion for summary judgment, concluding that the lawsuit was barred by the one-year statute of limitations.  In doing so, the trial court rejected the client’s argument that the “continuous representation” rule of section 340.6(a)(2) had tolled the limitations period.  

On appeal, the Court of Appeal held the trial court improperly granted the motion for summary judgment.  Notably, even though a substitution of attorney form had been filed, there was evidence “from which a trier of fact could conclude that the relationship between [the attorney and client] had not been severed at the time the substitution of attorney form was signed.”  (Id. at 1051.)  This evidence included telephone calls in which the attorney rendered “professional services” in the form of “negotiations, options, and strategy.”  (Id.) The Court rejected the attorney’s argument that he was merely being “cordial,” stating this “creates sufficient evidence that may convince a trier of fact to the contrary.”  (Id. at 1052.)  In addition, the court explained that the substitution of attorney form was “was not the key to this analysis.  A reasonable trier of fact could conclude that the parties intended that [the attorney] no longer act as litigation counsel, but he was expected to continue to provide transactional advice, as evidenced by the three September 2004 telephone calls.”  (Id.)

The Nielsen Court also addressed the scope of the representation, of which there were also disputes of fact.  “In addition to the issue of timing, in order to fall within the tolling provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 340.6, subdivision (a)(2), the attorney must have continued ‘to represent the plaintiff regarding the same specific subject matter[ ]’ (§ 340.6, subd. (a)(2), italics added) as to which the negligence occurred.”  (Id. at 1052-53.) “The continuing representation tolling provision ‘is not applicable when there is a continuing relationship between the client and the attorney involving only unrelated matters. [Citation.]’”  (Id. at 1053.) In that case, the court found the attorney’s “representation in the bankruptcy case and [] representation in the unlawful detainer proceedings can be seen to be intertwined and related, having overlapping objectives and purposes. The legal representation could be seen to have arisen from the same event.”  (Id. at 1054.) Hence, there were disputes of fact that precluded summary judgment.  (Id.)

Defendants argue two other cases are controlling and support their motion. First, in Flake v. Neumiller & Beardslee (2017) 9 Cal. App. 5th 223, 226, the Court of Appeal addressed whether the trial court properly granted summary judgment to a former attorney in malpractice suit based on the statute of limitations on the ground that the client could not have had an objectively reasonable expectation the attorney was continuing to represent him after the attorney served a motion to withdraw. The Court answered that in the affirmative, explaining:

[Counsel’s motion to withdraw] would indicate to any objectively reasonable client that Neumiller's representation of Flake was over, as it had been completely assumed by another. Whether that relationship-ending event is properly characterized as a true “abandonment” by Neumiller or not, the motion definitively informed Flake that Sinclair was already handling the pending postjudgment motions and would handle the appeal. Although Sinclair later denied the truth of Neumiller's statements, and Flake may have (subjectively) thought otherwise, any objectively reasonable client would have understood on receipt of the motion to withdraw that Neumiller had stopped working on the case. Therefore, Sinclair's declaration did not create a triable issue of material fact, because it does not inform as to Flake's objectively reasonable belief as of the time Flake was served with the motion to withdraw.

Nor did Flake's declaration raise any triable issues, because he did not dispute that he received the motion to withdraw and associated papers, which, as stated, clearly signaled to an objectively reasonable reader that Neumiller was no longer providing legal services for Flake.

(Id. at 231.)

Second, in Nguyen v. Ford (2020) 49 Cal. App. 5th 1, 8, the trial court sustained a defendant attorney’s demurrer in a malpractice action based on the statute of limitations. On appeal, the Court explained that the “continuous representation exception applies only to the attorney's representation of the plaintiff in the ‘specific subject matter’ in which the alleged wrongdoing occurred.” Thus, “[t]o determine whether the continuous representation exception applies, “ ‘[t]he inquiry is not whether an attorney-client relationship still exists but when the representation of the specific matter terminated.’ ” (Id. at 12.) 

Affirming the trial court’s order, the Court of Appeal explained that the attorney’s filing of a notice of withdrawal in the underlying case placed the plaintiff on objective notice that the representation had ended. (Id. at 15.) The court also noted that the plaintiff “d[id] not assert any ongoing legal contacts with [the attorney] related to the matter.” (Id.) “For example, Nguyen does not allege that she continued to communicate with Ford about the district court case; Ford made any legal appearances on her behalf in the district court case; or Ford sent her legal bills related to ongoing work in the district court case. Nguyen does not assert Ford told Nguyen she was continuing to represent her in the district court case.” (Id.)

While Flake and Nguyen are instructive, this court does not read those cases as providing a bright line rule that a notice to withdraw ends the tolling under section 340.6. Indeed, the Nguyen court itself stated that “[t]he end of an attorney-client relationship is not always signaled by a bright line.” (Id. at 230.) “[T]he formal act of withdrawing does not demarcate the end of the professional relationship in the context of the legal malpractice statute of limitations.” (Id.) Rather, the question is highly fact intensive. With these principles in mind, we turn to the instant facts.

C. Facts of the Instant Matter

In support the motion for summary judgment, Defendants present evidence Plaintiffs suffered actual injury in the underlying action no later than April 11, 2017, when an award of attorneys’ fees was entered against Plaintiff. (SSUMF 13.) 

That much appears undisputed.  Plaintiffs filed this malpractice suit on November 13, 2018. Thus, based plainly on a one-year statute of limitations from the date of actual injury of April 11, 2017, Plaintiffs’ action is untimely. Therefore, Plaintiffs must establish a triable issue as to the tolling of the statute of limitations to survive summary judgment.

On June 16, 2017, Shapiro filed a “Notice of Withdrawal” in the underlying action. Shapiro passed away on November 11, 2017, following a bout with pancreatic cancer. In support of their argument against tolling, Defendants contend by November 10, 2017 at the very latest—the day before Shapiro’s death—Plaintiffs did not have an objectively reasonable expectation that Shapiro was continuing to represent them in the Underlying Action. They present evidence that Shapiro had filed a notice of withdrawal in the Underlying Action on June 16, 2017, and had communicated that fact to the Plaintiffs. Shapiro explained to Knutsson in detail her reasons for withdrawing. After withdrawing, Shapiro did not attend any hearings or handle any legal filings, had not been copied on case correspondence, and had not billed Knutsson for any new legal work. Shapiro reminded Knutsson on more than one occasion that he had outstanding legal bills for her prior work in the underlying case.

Defendant presents further evidence that Shapiro informed Knuttson of her health issues, including her pancreatic cancer diagnosis, at least as early as August or September of 2017. Therefore, Defendants argue that “any objectively reasonable client would have understood Shapiro’s notice of withdraw to be the end of her representation in the Underlying Action.” (Mtn. 10: 18-20.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs counters that Shapiro continued to advise Knutsson about the KTLA case after the Notice of Withdrawal. Indeed, in her email to Knutsson accompanying her Notice of Withdrawal, Shapiro wrote she would “be happy to give” her “comments or suggestions” on a potential amended pleading. (SSDMF 15, Exh. 20.) Plaintiffs also present evidence that Shapiro had a history of exaggerating her ailments, and threatened to withdraw so frequently that the withdrawal could hardly be taken literally. (Knutsson Decl. ¶ 14.)

Plaintiffs also present evidence of continued communications with Shapiro regarding the subject matter of the ongoing litigation. “In late October or early November 2017,” Knutsson called Shapiro to discuss his second deposition scheduled for a few weeks later. (Knutsson Decl. ¶ 18.) Knutsson “wanted to know why [he] had to have [his] deposition taken a second time” and whether he should fight it. (Id.) Shapiro advised him “it would not be worth it to try to prevent the deposition from going forward,” and “launched into criticisms” of Knutsson’s performance at his first deposition. (Id.) Shapiro advised Knutsson on how to handle himself at the second deposition. (Id.) Plaintiffs note only after Shapiro passed—and with no one now advising Knutsson to preserve the right of publicity claims—did Knutsson advise his attorney dismiss those claims. (Id. ¶ 25.) 

The disputed facts here are distinguishable from Flake and Nguyen, discussed in section (B)(1), supra. In those cases, the Court held a notice of withdrawal placed the plaintiff on objective notice that the representation had ended. But in those cases, it appears there were little, if any, communications between the attorney and client after the withdrawal. That is not the case here.

Here, Knutsson and Shapiro stayed in contact after Shapiro formally withdrew as Knutsson’s attorney in the underlying action. On at least one phone call, they discussed the case and Shapiro advised Knutsson how to conduct himself for an upcoming deposition. Knutsson recites other instances where he discussed the case with Shapiro. A review of the evidence suggests that Knutsson and Shapiro maintained a cordial relationship after the withdrawal. One might even call the two friends. This blurs the line between the friendship and attorney-client relationship. 

Knutsson presents further evidence he learned of Shapiro’s death on or about November 12, 2017, and was “completely shocked at the news.” (Knutsson Decl. ¶ 21.) Although he was aware of her pancreatic cancer diagnosis, he believed “that people could live for years after they were diagnosed with that form of cancer.” (Id.) Knutsson also states that Shapiro “had a history of exaggerating her health issues,” which she would sometimes use to her advantage in litigation. (Id. ¶ 22.) There is also no evidence in the record that Knutsson knew Shapiro was “no longer lucid” in the days before her death. While the tolling standard is an objective one, it is still based on the facts known from the client’s perspective.

Considering the above, this court finds the existence of triable issue as to the continuing representation tolling doctrine. In so concluding, this court is mindful of its duty to “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

Finally, because the court finds a triable issue of fact as to whether Plaintiffs’ claims are timely within the one-year statute of limitations period, the court need not determine at this time whether a longer limitations period might apply to Plaintiffs’ fraud claim(s).

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED. Defendants’ alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication is also DENIED.

D. Other Observations

Although not raised in the briefing, the court notes the following point for sake of thoroughness on the statute of limitations issue. It is undisputed that Judith Salkow Shapiro passed away on November 11, 2017. It cannot be disputed that the representation stopped as of this date, as she could not continue to represent clients after her death. Therefore, viewing the evidence in the light most favorable to Plaintiffs, November 11, 2017, was the absolute last day that the one-year statute of limitations could begin running. 

Moving forward exactly one year, November 11, 2018, fell on a Sunday. The next day—Monday, November 12, 2018—was Veterans Day, an observed court holiday. Plaintiffs then filed their complaint the following day on Tuesday, November 13, 2018. 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 12a(a), “[i]f the last day for the performance of any act provided or required by law to be performed within a specified period of time is a holiday, then that period is hereby extended to and including the next day that is not a holiday.” “Holidays within the meaning of this code are every Sunday and any other days that are specified or provided for as judicial holidays in Section 135.” (CCP § 10.)

Therefore, because Sunday, November 11, 2018, and Monday, November 12, 2018, were “holidays” as that term is used in the Code of Civil Procedure, Plaintiffs had until Tuesday, November 13, 2018, to file this action. Plaintiffs did, in fact, file this action on that last day. The action was therefore timely for the purposes of this motion.

Of course, the trier of fact will still determine whether the statute of limitations had, in fact, expired, before November 13, 2018, based upon Plaintiff’s tolling theory of “continued representation."  This Court may consider bifurcating that issue if there is a trial in this matter.

Case Number: 20STCV47887    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: 49

Felipe Mejia Iniguez, et al. v. A.G. Layne, Inc., et al.

MOTION TO QUASH DEPOSITION SUBPOENA ON NON-PARTY WITNESS SAUNDRA EDWARDS
 

MOVING PARTY:  Plaintiffs Maria G. Mejia, individually and as successor-in-interest to Decedent Felipe Mejia Iniguez; Rodrigo Mejia; Rodolfo Mejia; and Rosalva Mejia

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Univar Solutions USA, Inc.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiffs Felipe Mejia Iniguez and Maria G. Mejia filed the instant action against various manufacturers of alleged toxic chemical products.  Felipe worked in mannequin manufacturing where he alleged he was exposed to harmful chemical products that caused renal cell carcinoma.  Felipe passed away from the cancer after filing this suit; his wife, as his surviving spouse and successor-in-interest, continues the suit on his behalf.  The First Amended Complaint also adds Felipe’s adult children as Plaintiffs, and asserts causes of action against the chemical manufacturers for (1) negligence, (2) strict liability – warning defect, (3) strict liability – design defect, (4) fraudulent concealment, (5) breach of implied warranties, and (6) loss of consortium.

Plaintiffs now move to quash the deposition subpoena for the production of business records on non-party Saundra Edwards by Defendant Univar Solutions USA, Inc. Defendant opposed.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Quash is GRANTED.

Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION:

Motion to Quash Third Party Subpoena

I. Legal Standard

A court may quash a subpoena entirely or partially, and issue an order to protect parties, witnesses or consumers from unreasonable or oppressive demands including violations of privacy. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1.) 

II. Analysis

Plaintiffs move to quash Defendant Univar Solutions USA Inc.’s Subpoena for the Production of Business Records served on Plaintiff Rosalva Mejia’s psychotherapist, Saundra Edwards. Plaintiff Rosalva Mejia is the daughter of the decedent Felipe Mejia Iniguez.

The Subpoena at issue seeks:

(1) Any and all WRITINGS OR DOCUMENTS RELATING TO [Rosalva Mejia]’s care, treatment, examination and/or consultation with YOU, including but not limited to all office, emergency room, inpatient and outpatient charts and notes; correspondence; patient histories; diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment; laboratory tests and results; prescriptions; radiological reports and diagnostic studies; photographs; patient consent forms; logs; counseling; physical therapy records, including sign-in sheets and all descriptions of exercises prescribed; appointment records; and insurance documents.

(2) Any and all itemized statements of the billing charges and/or consolidated statement of benefits, with diagnostic and procedure codes, to include the total charges private or governmental; any amounts written off by the provider, and any amounts that are the patient’s responsibility; payment history and any insurance billing or payments information; and emergency room physicians’ bills.

(3) Any and all COMMUNICATIONS between YOU and [Rosalva Mejia].

(4) Any and all COMMUNICATIONS YOU and any third party RELATING TO [Rosalva Mejia].

(Brust Dec. Ex. “E”)

First, Plaintiffs contend the mental health records are protected pursuant to Plaintiff’s Constitutional right of privacy. Second, Plaintiffs argue the records are protected by the psychotherapist-patient privilege. (Evidence Code § 1014.) Third, Plaintiffs argue the records are not relevant to the issues because Plaintiff has not placed her mental condition in controversy above and beyond “that distress typically associated with a wrongful death cause of action.” (Mtn. 7: 26-27.) 

In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs have put communication and treatment relevant to the mental or emotional condition of patient, Rosalva Mejia at issue, by claiming mental injuries and naming her therapist Saundra Edwards as a witness in discovery. It contends that Plaintiff’s mental health records are relevant to the subject matter of the lawsuit, that Plaintiff waived any existing privilege, and the records are not protected by any right to privacy.

Evidence is relevant if it “ha[s] any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.” (Evid. Code § 210.)  “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (CCP § 2017.010; Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301 [“discovery is not limited to admissible evidence”].) 

“The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious. [Citation.] The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy. A court must then balance these competing considerations.” (Williams v. Superior Ct. (2017) 3 Cal. 5th 531, 552.)

Here, Plaintiff identified Ms. Edwards as a witness in response to Univar’s form interrogatories as someone with knowledge of the occurrence giving rise to this action. In her special interrogatory number 7, Plaintiff again identified Ms. Edwards as someone with knowledge of the facts concerning Decedent’s exposure to chemical products.

A plaintiff may waive the psychotherapist-plaintiff privilege by placing her mental condition at issue in the lawsuit. (Cal. Evid. Code § 1016). The exception provides in relevant part that “[t]here is no privilege…as to a communication relevant to an issue concerning the mental or emotional condition of the patient.”  (Id.)

Expanding on this section, the California Supreme Court has explained that “plaintiffs are ‘not obligated to sacrifice all privacy to seek redress for a specific [physical,] mental or emotional injury’; while they may not withhold information which relates to any physical or mental condition which they have put in issue by bringing [a] lawsuit, . . . they are entitled to retain the confidentiality of all unrelated medical or psychotherapeutic treatment they may have undergone in the past.” (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 864 [citation and footnote omitted].) However, “. . . privacy interests may have to give way to [an] opponent’s right to a fair trial. Thus courts must balance the right of civil litigants to discover relevant facts against the privacy interests of persons subject to discovery.” (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 842.) 

Importantly, when determining damages in a wrongful death action, the jury may not consider a plaintiff’s “grief, sorrow, or mental anguish” or “pain and suffering.” (CACI 3921.) “California cases have uniformly held that damages for mental and emotional distress, including grief and sorrow, are not recoverable in a wrongful death action.” (Krouse v. Graham (1977) 19 Cal.3d 59, 72, internal citations omitted.)

Based on the express exclusion of this evidence from a jury’s consideration, Defendant has failed to demonstrate the evidence sought is “reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010.) This court does not mistake discoverable evidence for admissible evidence.  However, given the privacy interests at issue, Defendant needed to clearly articulate how the records would lead to discovery of admissible evidence. It has not done so. Merely bringing a wrongful death action is insufficient, since the jury may not consider a plaintiff’s “grief, sorrow, or mental anguish” or “pain and suffering.” (CACI 3921.)

To conclude, when balancing Plaintiff’s privacy interests against Defendant’s interest in discovering the documents at issue, the balance does not shift in favor of disclosure. 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Quash is GRANTED.

III. Sanctions

In making an order pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, “the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

The Court declines to award expenses as the motion was not made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification.

Moving party to give notice.

Derek Paul Deakin v. Robert Allan Kahn

CROSS-COMPLAINANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT
 

MOVING PARTY: Cross-Complainant Robert Allan Kahn

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Cross-Defendant Derek Paul Deakin

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiff Derek Deakin alleges he retained attorney Defendant Robert Kahn to represent him in a personal injury action. Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached the parties’ fee agreement be retaining a portion of the settlement owed to Plaintiff. 

Defendant has filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff for declaratory relief. Defendant/Cross-Complainant seeks a judicial declaration over the parties’ rights to $269,984.74 currently held in Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s client trust account. 

Cross-Complainant now moves for summary judgment. Cross-Defendant opposed. The matter first came for hearing on November 2, 2023. After the hearing, the court continued the motion and allowed Cross-Defendant to file a supplemental opposition addressing the defects raised at the hearing and in the tentative ruling. The court has read and considered Cross-Defendant’s supplemental filings of November 6, 2023, as well as Cross-Complainant’s December 5, 2023, response thereto, and now rules as follows.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Cross-Complainants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Cross-Complainant is ordered to file a [Proposed] Judgment consistent with this ruling.

Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION:

Motion for Summary Judgment

I. Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843. In analyzing motions for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent's claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.  Thus, summary judgment is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c); Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741. 

Where a plaintiff moves for summary judgment, the burden is to produce admissible evidence on each element of a “cause of action” entitling him or her to judgment. (CCP § 437c(p)(1); see Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (1995) 37 CA4th 1282, 1287, 44 CR2d 335, 337 (citing text) (disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 C4th 826, 107 CR2d 841)).  This means that plaintiffs who bear the burden of proof at trial by a preponderance of evidence must produce evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not.  At that point, the burden shifts to defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(1)).

II. Analysis

A. Moving Party’s Burden

In analyzing motions for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent's claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

Summary judgment is appropriate in a declaratory relief action when only legal issues are presented for the court's determination. (Gafcon, Inc. v. Ponsor & Associates (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1388, 1401–1402.)

Cross-Complainant Kahn seeks the following declaratory judgment:

For a declaratory judgment that Kahn is entitled to the $269,984.74 held in his client trust account, and Deakin is not entitled to any of the $269,984.74, and that Kahn may disburse $260,984.74 to himself in payment of the balance of the attorney’s fees he earned pursuant to the Fee Agreement.
(Cross-Complaint, Prayer 1.)

In support of the motion, Kahn presents evidence that he and Deakin entered into a written Retainer Agreement on June 15, 2018, pursuant to which Deakin retained Kahn to represent him in a personal injury case involving injuries Deakin sustained in a motorcycle accident on June 6, 2018. (SSUMF 1.) The retainer provided that Kahn’s fee for his legal services would be thirty-three and one-third percent if the matter settled more than 120 days before the first date set for trial, or forty percent if the matter settled less than 120 days before the first date set for trial. (SSUMF 2.) The retainer also provided that Kahn could recover his costs. (SSUMF 3).

On August 16, 2018 Kahn filed an action on Deakin's behalf entitled Deakin v. Tassiopulos, LASC Case No. BC717886. (SSUMF 5.) Deakin advised Kahn of his desire to net between $875,000 and $1,000,000. (SSUMF 12.) As the case progressed, Deakin continued to lower his demand. (SSUMF 13.) Kahn advised Deakin that the gross amount he was offering would net Deakin less than his desired $875,000 to $1,000,000. (Id.) 

On or about August 23, 2011, Deakin’s case settled for $1,675,000, after Deakin authorized Kahn to accept the Code of Civil Procedure 998 offer made by the defendant in that amount. (SSUMF 7.) Deakin accepted the settlement with knowledge of the approximate amount he would net. (SSUMF 14.) Kahn deposited the funds into his client trust account. (SSUMF 8.) 

Because the case settled less than 120 days before the first date set for trial (February 18, 2020), Kahn was entitled to a 40 percent fee and reimbursement of costs in the amount of $94,592.02. (SSUMF 9, 10.) Despite requests from Deakin, Kahn never agreed to reduce his fee. (SSUMF 11, 16.) 

On September 20, 2022, Kahn disbursed $644,021.60 to Deakin with Deakin's approval. (SSUMF 20.) On January 3, 2023, Kahn disbursed another $162,565.44 to Deakin to cover one of Deakin’s medical liens. (SSUMF 24.) On or about January 16, 2023, Kahn paid himself out of this trust account a total of $494,592.02 ($400,000 for fees and $94,592.92 for costs). (SSUMF 23.) Therefore, there remains $269,984.74 in contested fees in the trust account. (SSUMF 23.) 

Considering this evidence, Kahn has met his initial burden to establish he is entitled to declaratory relief. This switches the burden to Deakin to establish a triable issue.

B. Opposing Party’s Burden

1. Procedural Requirements of Opposition to MSJ

The opposition to a motion for summary judgment or adjudication must consist of (1) a memorandum in opposition, (2) a separate statement in opposition, and (3) evidence in opposition. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1350(e).) The separate statement in opposition “must unequivocally state whether [each] fact is ‘disputed’ or ‘undisputed.’” (Id., Rule 3.1350(f)(2).)

In opposition, Cross-Defendant first filed a document titled “Cross-Defendant Derek Deakin’s Responses and Supporting Evidence To Robert Kahn’s Separate Statement in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment.” (See 09/08/23 Filing.) While it resembles a separate statement, if fails to identify which facts are disputed or undisputed. Deakin did not submit a memorandum of points and authorities. He has likewise did not submit any admissible evidence in support of his opposition. 

In light of these deficiencies, the court released a Tentative Ruling stating its intention to continue the hearing to allow Deakin the opportunity to file a code-compliant opposition. At the conclusion of the November 2, 2023, hearing on this motion, this court continued the motion and permitted Deakin to file a supplemental opposition no later than November 30, 2023.

Only four days later, on November 6, 2023, Cross-Defendant filed two documents: (1) a “Separate Statement of Disputed Facts and Supporting Evidence in Opposition of Cross-Complainant’s Motion for Summary Judgment” and (2) “Responses and Supporting Evidence in Opposition of Motion for Summary Judgment [sic].”   [FN 1]

As an initial matter, Cross-Defendant has again not included a declaration or affidavit with his opposition. His 09/12/23 “Responses and Supporting Evidence” and 11/06/23 supplemental “Responses and Supporting Evidence” do, however, include a “Verification” stating:  Cross-Defendant “declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.” (Response p. 16; supplemental Response p. 12.) 

Cross-Defendant’s supplemental Separate Statement contends each of Cross-Complainant’s twenty-seven material facts are disputed. Upon review, many of the “disputed facts” are not competently in dispute. For example, the moving party provided the following material fact:

On August 18, 2022, Kahn advised Deakin in writing that if he accepted $1.7 million, he would net approximately $737,407.26. (SSUMF 14.) 

The undisputed fact is clearly supported by the August 18, 2022, email where Kahn writes to Deakin that he would “would net approximately $737,407.26 on a $1.7MM settlement.” (Kahn Dec. ¶ 5, Ex. 7.)

In his response to the separate statement, Deakin says this undisputable fact is disputed. He states:

Disputed. Deakin went into shock by this email as Kahn breached their Oral Agreement. Therefore, Deakin is seeking restitution and unjust enrichment. Unjust enrichment occurs when Party A confers a benefit upon Party B without Party A receiving the proper restitution required by law (See LII, Wex, “unjust enrichment”, 2023). A fair jury trial is requested to determine if a quasi-contract exists as California courts typically differ on whether cause of action for unjust enrichment is a legitimate claim.
(Cross-Complainant’s 11/06/23 Separate Statement, 14.) 

Suffice it to say, this fact is not disputed, and Cross-Defendant’s explanation does not show otherwise. This is far from the only example. It is also noted that Deakin has not provided any “additional” facts in dispute. 

As the Court of Appeal recently stated, “[t]he opposing party's responses to the separate statement must be in good faith, responsive, and material. Responses should directly address the fact stated, and if that fact is not in dispute, the opposing party must so admit. It is completely unhelpful to evade the stated fact in an attempt to create a dispute where none exists.” (Beltran v. Hard Rock Hotel Licensing, Inc., 2023 WL 8430581, at *6 (Cal. Ct. App. Dec. 5, 2023 [certified for publication].) 

These factors alone are sufficient grounds to grant the motion for summary judgment in Cross-Complainants’ favor. Indeed, trial courts should “not hesitate to disregard attempts to game the system by the opposing party claiming facts are ‘disputed’ when the uncontroverted evidence clearly shows otherwise.” (Id. at *7.) “When a moving party makes the required prima facie showing, the opposing party's failure to comply with this requirement may, in the court's discretion, constitute a sufficient ground for granting the motion. (CCP § 437c(b)(3); see Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mut. Cas. Co. (2009) 170 Cal. App. 4th 554, 568.) 

Be that as it may, for matters of fairness, the court proceeds to consider Cross-Defendant’s evidence, to the extent it is possible to do so. 

2. Opposition on the Merits

First, Cross-Defendant contends his signature on the attorney fee Agreement is a forgery. But he has provided no competent evidence to support that contention. A party seeking to avoid summary judgment “must produce substantial responsive evidence sufficient to establish a triable issue of material fact on the merits…” (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App. 4th 151, 163 [bare assertion that moving party “fabricated” evidence is insufficient to avoid summary judgment])(emphasis added). And the opposing party cannot controvert the moving party's declarations by evidence “based on speculation, imagination, guess work, or mere possibilities.” (Doe v. Salesian Soc. (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 474, 481.)

Second, Cross-Defendant also fails to demonstrate that the Agreement is ambiguous. It provides that “Client agrees to pay for the services herein described, the fee shall be thirty-three and one-third percent (33 1/3%) of any and all gross amounts recovered if the matter is settled more than 120 days before the first date set for trial. If the matter settled less than 120 days before the first date set for trial, or goes to trial, the fee will be forty (40%) percent.” (SSUMF 2, Exh. 1.) This agreement means what it says. 

Third, Cross-Defendant argues that the fee Agreement was superseded by an oral agreement by which Kahn represented he would receive between $875,000 and $1,000,000 in the settlement. On July 26, 2022, Kahn wrote to Deakin by email: 

“This will confirm that you have authorized me to negotiate a settlement that will net you between $850,000 and $1 million and that whatever that number turns out to be it will be more than my fee. So for example, if you net 900,000 my fee has to be less than 900,000 etc.” (Cross-Complainants’ supplemental Response and Supporting Evidence, Exh. H.)

This email does not demonstrate any modification to the fee Agreement. Nor does it represent any “promise” to recover a certain amount. Based on this email in context of the other communications between the parties, this representation can be viewed as little more than an aspirational understanding between them. 

Finally, Deakin has failed to submit evidence by which a reasonable trier of fact can find wrongdoing by Kahn when he accepted a settlement on Deakin’s behalf. The undisputed evidence shows that on August 23, 2022, Deakin authorized Kahn to accept the opposing party’s section 998 offer.  Deakin wrote to Kahn by email: “I will accept the offer of $1,675,000 and arbitrate your fee.” (Kahn Decl. ¶ 7, Exh. 7, 402.) This came after Kahn had explained to Deakin that acceptance of an offer around that amount would net him less than $800,000. (Id., 316.) Kahn also explained to Deakin the “consequences of not accepting” the section 998 offer if he did not obtain a more favorable verdict at trial. (Id., 357.) In short, Deakin was not “coerced” to accept the settlement. Rather, his attorney advised him that the offer on the table was the best he would get, and that it was in his best interest to accept it. 

This court is mindful of its duty to “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) But a responding party’s evidence still must be “substantial.” (See Sangster, supra, 68 Cal.App. 4th at 163.) 

Considering the evidence, Cross-Defendant has failed to carry his burden to establish a triable issue. In other words, there is no evidence by which a reasonable trier of fact could find in Cross-Defendant’s favor on the Cross-Complaint.

Accordingly, Cross-Complainants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Cross-Complainant is ordered to file a [Proposed] Judgment consistent with this ruling.

Moving party to give notice.

Case Number: 23STCV08151-2    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: 49

Derek Paul Deakin v. Robert Allan Kahn

CROSS-COMPLAINANT’S MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST DEREK PAUL DEAKIN
 

MOVING PARTY: Cross-Complainant Robert Allan Kahn 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Cross-Defendant Derek Paul Deakin

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiff Derek Deakin alleges he retained attorney Defendant Robert Kahn to represent him in a personal injury action. Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached the parties’ fee agreement be retaining a portion of the settlement owed to Plaintiff. 

Defendant has filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff for declaratory relief. Defendant/Cross-Complainant seeks a judicial declaration over the parties’ rights to $269,984.74 currently held in Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s client trust account. 

Cross-Complainant now moves to quash a subpoena issued to US Bank. Cross-Defendant filed a response on October 18, 2023, and a supplemental response on November 27, 2023.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Motion to Quash is GRANTED.

An award of monetary sanctions is issued in favor of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Kahn, and against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Deakin, in the total amount of $60.00 for costs.  The request for attorney's fees is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION:

Motion to Quash Subpoena

I. Legal Standard

A court may quash a subpoena entirely or partially, and issue an order to protect parties, witnesses or consumers from unreasonable or oppressive demands including violations of privacy. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1.) 

II. Analysis

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Kahn moves to quash the subpoena issued on US Bank, seeking records from Kahn’s client trust account. Kahn contends the records sought pertain only to other clients (and not Derek Deakin), and therefore, are unrelated to the instant action.

Evidence is relevant if it “ha[s] any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.” (Evid. Code § 210.)  “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (CCP § 2017.010; Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301 [“discovery is not limited to admissible evidence”].) 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Deakin opposes the motion. Deakin admits this is his third attempt to subpoena the records from US Bank.  Based on the timing and amount of the checks deposited into Kahn’s client trust account, Deakin theorizes that the money may be linked to his case. Deakin contends he is “reasonably suspicious Kahn is self-dealing and strongly believes Kahn is utilizing a diversion to distract Deakin” from the amounts he is owed. (Response 9: 24-26.)

Deakin’s theory lacks substance. It appears the amount of money is not in dispute. The parties know what the case settled for, how much has been disbursed to Deakin, and how much has gone toward medical liens. The only question is how or if the remaining disputed fund should be apportioned.

Considering this, the settlements or funds retained by Kahn for other clients are plainly irrelevant to the present case between Deakin and Kahn. Deakin contends otherwise, but has failed to articulate a clear basis for subpoenaing the records for Kahn’s other clients. Therefore, the subpoena is not “reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010.)

The court is mindful that the legislature promotes a “very liberal and flexible standard of relevancy,” such that any “doubts as to relevance should generally be resolved in favor of permitting discovery.” (Williams, supra, 3 Cal. 5th at 542.) But here there is no doubt—the records sought are not relevant to this case.

Accordingly, Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Motion to Quash is GRANTED.

Moving party to give notice.

III. Sanctions

In making an order pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, “the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

Deakin filed two oppositions to the motion to quash: a response and a supplemental response. Neither articulated any valid basis to subpoena records from US Bank for matters wholly unrelated to Deakin’s case. 

The court can only conclude the motion was opposed in bad faith and/or without substantial justification. 

Kahn seeks $15,000 in sanctions against Deakin, calculated as follows: “Kahn expended 10 hours reviewing the Subpoena and the US Bank account records to and communicating with representatives of US Bank to determine whether any of the requested records could possibly be relevant to this case. He expended 2 hours preparing this motion and anticipate spending 1 hour preparing a Reply to Deakin’s anticipated opposition, and 2 hours preparing for and attending the hearing of this motion.” (Mtn. 4: 16-20.) Kahn “values his time at no less than $1,000 per hour based on his 44 years of trial experience.” (Id. 4: 20-21.)

However, as a general rule, attorneys representing themselves are not entitled to recover attorney’s fees, either by contract or sanctions. (See Trope v. Katz (1995) 11 Cal. 4th 274, 292 [reasoning that the “usual and ordinary meaning of the words ‘reasonable attorney's fees’ is the consideration that a litigant pays or becomes liable to pay in exchange for legal representation”].)

Therefore, attorney Kahn cannot recover attorney’s fees for acts time incurred in pro per. The sanctions are limited to the reasonable costs incurred in bringing this motion, in this case, the $60.00 filing fee. 

Accordingly, an award of monetary sanctions is issued in favor of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Kahn, and against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Deakin, in the total amount of $60.00 in costs.   This Court makes no findings as to whether the claimed 15 hours was a “reasonable amount of time” in connection to this motion, nor does it make any finding as to whether the requested $1,000.00 per hour was a reasonable hourly rate.

Moving party to give notice.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
