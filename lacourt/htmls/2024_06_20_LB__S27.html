DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 23LBCP00091    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Prior Hearing

The Court was originally scheduled to hear this matter on 4/19/24.  Prior to the hearing, the Court issued a 16-page tentative ruling addressing each of the parties’ arguments.  The Court ultimately adopted the tentative ruling as its final ruling.  The Court incorporates the 4/19/24 ruling into this order by reference. 

 

The ruling included, in pertinent part, the following findings and instructions:

Petitioner argued, before the hearing officer, that the District violated his due process rights by deliberating in a closed, rather than open (public) forum.  The hearing officer addressed and rejected this contention at page 8, lines 2-6, but did not meaningfully discuss the contention. 

 

Petitioner contends that Gov Code §54957(b)(1) allows for closed sessions if the employee requests them, but he contends he did NOT request a closed session, and therefore it was a violation of his due process rights for the board to deliberate in a closed session.  LBUSD contends §§54957(a) and (b)(1) permitted deliberation in a closed forum. 

 

These sections provide:

(a) This chapter shall not be construed to prevent the legislative body of a local agency from holding closed sessions with the Governor, Attorney General, district attorney, agency counsel, sheriff, or chief of police, or their respective deputies, or a security consultant or a security operations manager, on matters posing a threat to the security of public buildings, a threat to the security of essential public services, including water, drinking water, wastewater treatment, natural gas service, and electric service, or a threat to the public's right of access to public services or public facilities.

(b)(1) Subject to paragraph (2), this chapter shall not be construed to prevent the legislative body of a local agency from holding closed sessions during a regular or special meeting to consider the appointment, employment, evaluation of performance, discipline, or dismissal of a public employee or to hear complaints or charges brought against the employee by another person or employee unless the employee requests a public session.

(2) As a condition to holding a closed session on specific complaints or charges brought against an employee by another person or employee, the employee shall be given written notice of his or her right to have the complaints or charges heard in an open session rather than a closed session, which notice shall be delivered to the employee personally or by mail at least 24 hours before the time for holding the session. If notice is not given, any disciplinary or other action taken by the legislative body against the employee based on the specific complaints or charges in the closed session shall be null and void.

 

Subdivision (a), on its face, does not apply.  There was no threat, as defined in that section, that required the proceedings to take place in a closed session.  Subdivision (b)(1) does apply, and clearly permits a closed session, but the fact that the subdivision specifically states, “unless the employee requests a public session” appears to mean it’s up to the employee to have the proceedings be open or closed.  Especially when read in connection with (b)(2), which specifically provides for 24 hours’ notice of the right to an open, rather than closed, proceeding, the conclusion that the option was the employee’s, not the employer’s, appears clear. 

 

The District cites various cases in its opposition, but none are directly on point, as none considered a situation where an employee asked for an open session and was denied the request.  The closest case to being on point is Kolter v. Commission of Professional Competence of Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 1346, 1353, which was cited by the District to support its conclusion that the District was not required to deliberate in open session or to invite Petitioner into closed session to observe deliberations. 

 

In Kolter, the school board held TWO sessions prior to dismissing the employee.  The board first held a closed session, where it deliberated on the issue of whether or not to initiate the termination process.  Thereafter, the board issued the required 24-hour notice, and then convened a second time, with the employee present and in an open session, to actually vote to terminate the employee.

 

The problem with Kolter is that the proceedings in this case that were closed were the actual termination proceedings, not proceedings concerning whether or not to initiate termination proceedings.  The proceedings therefore violated the Code, and, per the express language of the Code, the proceedings are null and void.

The final issue is the remedy to be imposed.  Petitioner seeks reinstatement of his employment plus back pay.  Additionally, in the prayer portion of the petition, Petitioner seeks attorneys’ fees, costs of suit, and punitive damages. 

 

Neither party meaningfully briefs the appropriate relief in the event that a closed, rather than open, hearing was held.  The Court is continuing the hearing on this writ for six weeks, to Thursday, 5/30/24 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27.  Counsel must meet and confer forthwith concerning the appropriate remedy in an attempt to avoid the necessity of a further hearing.

 

If Counsel are unable to come to agreement concerning the appropriate remedy, then at least two weeks prior to the hearing, each party must submit a brief concerning the sole issue of remedy.  The briefs must be no more than five pages each.  The parties must provide on point authority concerning whether Petitioner is entitled to reinstatement, back pay, costs, attorneys’ fees, and/or punitive damages. 

 

2.     Supplemental Briefs

On 5/30/24, each party filed a supplemental brief.  Petitioner’s brief addressed the remedies to be imposed, as Petitioner was ordered to do.  Respondent’s brief does not address remedies.  Instead, Respondent’s brief contends the prior order is in error, and asks the Court to deny the petition. 

 

Because writs of mandate are complicated and technical, the Court has considered Respondent’s supplemental brief, despite the fact that it did not address the issue presented by the Court in its prior ruling.  Respondent relies primarily on two cases in its supplemental brief, neither of which were cited in the original opposition papers.  The cases are Bollinger v. San Diego Civil Services Commission (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 568 and Ricasa v. Office of Administrative Hearings (2018) 31 Cal.App.5th 262, 278. 

 

In Bollinger, the Court considered a situation where there was a public evidentiary hearing, but then the commission deliberated in private to determine the penalty to be assessed.  The trial court granted the petitioner’s application for writ of mandate, finding the proceedings were required to be open.  The court of appeals reversed, holding that the evidentiary portion of the hearing was required to be open, but that the deliberations were permitted to be closed.

 

In Ricasa, the employee pleaded guilty to various charges, and the board ultimately deliberated to determine whether the guilty plea supported termination.  The employee argued the deliberations were required to be public, and sought a writ of mandate.  The court of appeals held that the deliberations were permitted to be closed because they did not amount to an evidentiary hearing, but instead to a discussion of whether the guilty plea, which was an evidentiary admission, supported termination.

 

Thus, when Bollinger and Ricasa are read together, they lead to the conclusion that proceedings weighing evidence must be open, but proceedings to deliberate on the actions to be taken in light of the evidence can be closed.  Respondent herein establishes that Petitioner was given the opportunity to be heard concerning the charges against him, and only the deliberations were closed. 

 

The Court therefore reverses its prior tentative ruling and denies the petition for writ of mandate.

Case Number: 23LBCV00046    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Julio Contreras filed this action against Defendant, City of Long Beach for damages arising out of an automobile accident that occurred on 7/10/22.  Plaintiff filed the complaint on 1/09/23. 

 

2.     Prior Hearing on Motion for Leave to Amend

On 10/12/23, the Court heard and denied Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend.  Plaintiff sought leave to amend the complaint to add claims by two additional plaintiffs, Luis and Marco Bonilla.  The Court ruled, in pertinent part, as follows:

Plaintiff, when he served Defendant with his government tort claim, also included the claims of two third parties, Luis and Marco Bonilla, both of whom were also involved in the subject accident.  When Plaintiff filed his complaint, however, he erroneously failed to include the Bonillas in the action.  Plaintiff concedes the statute of limitations has run as to the Bonillas’ claims, but seeks leave to add them to his complaint because the failure to include them was the result of his attorney’s mistake or neglect. 

 

Plaintiff does not cite any on point authority permitting him to add two previously un-named plaintiffs whose claims are barred by the statute of limitations to the complaint.  The Court has considered the various possible mechanisms to name the new plaintiffs, and finds Plaintiff cannot do so.

…

The claims clearly do not relate back to the named plaintiff’s claims.  Amendments naming a new party plaintiff are permissible if there is no change in the claim being asserted: e.g., amendments to reflect an assignment of the claim; or, on plaintiff's death, to substitute their executor or administrator.  Plaintiffs who lack standing to sue on a claim or cause of action may seek leave to amend their complaint to substitute a plaintiff with standing to sue (the “real party in interest”). Such an amendment is authorized under CCP § 473(a)(1), which allows amendments “by adding or striking out the name of any party.” See Branick v. Downey Sav. &Loan Ass'n (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 243.  However, where the amendment adds a plaintiff whose claim is independent of the claims previously asserted, the amendment does not “relate back” to the original complaint. I.e., the plaintiff proposed to be substituted may not “state facts which give rise to a wholly distinct and different legal obligation against the defendant.”  Id. at 243.

 

Plaintiff seeks to add wholly distinct claims by other plaintiffs, and not to merely substitute the new plaintiffs’ claims for his own.  The proposed amended complaint, therefore, does not relate back to the original complaint, and amendment is not proper under §473(a).

…

While Plaintiff does not expressly mention §473(b), he does contend he should be granted leave to amend because his attorney made a mistake or neglected to name the other two plaintiffs when he filed the complaint.  §473(b) governs relief from attorney errors.  Pursuant to Life Sav. Bank v. Wilhelm (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 174, 178, §473(b) does not provide a mechanism for relief when the attorney error was failure to comply with the applicable statute of limitations. 

            …

Plaintiff also argues relief should be granted pursuant to Gov Code §946.6.  §946.6 permits a plaintiff whose application for leave to file a late claim was denied to then file a petition, with the Superior Court, for relief from failure to file a timely claim.  §946.6 does not have any application to this case, because the proposed new plaintiffs did not file any application for leave to file a late claim, and did not initiate this action by way of a petition for relief.  If the proposed new plaintiffs have any procedural mechanism to assert their claims, such as a petition for relief, they are free to file any such petition.  They have failed, however, to show that §946.6 permits them to proceed in this action by way of a motion for leave to amend to add claims that are barred by the statute of limitations. 

 

3.     Motion to Intervene

a.     Parties’ Positions

On 5/07/24, Plaintiff filed this motion to intervene.  The motion seeks an order permitting the Bonillas to intervene in this action.  On 6/06/24, Defendant filed timely opposition to the motion.  Defendant contends the Bonillas do not meet the elements for mandatory intervention, and should not be permitted discretionary intervention because the case is on the eve of trial.  In the event the Court grants the motion, Defendant asks for a six-month trial continuance to prepare for the newly-added claims.

 

b.     Law Governing Intervention

Intervention is the procedure whereby someone not previously served as a party to an action (or ordered joined as a party) may nevertheless become a party. An intervention takes place when the court grants leave to a nonparty to join the plaintiff in claiming what is sought by the complaint; or to unite with the defendant in resisting the plaintiff's claims; or to demand anything adverse to both parties.  CCP §387(b).  Intervention is either as of right or permissive.

 

A nonparty has the right to intervene in litigation between others where the nonparty claims an interest in the property or transaction involved in the litigation, and is so situated that any judgment rendered in the nonparty's absence “may impair or impede that person's ability to protect that interest.” CCP § 387(d)(1)(B); Crestwood Behavioral Health, Inc. v. Lacy (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 560, 572-573. 

 

If proper procedures are followed, the court has discretion to permit a nonparty to intervene in litigation pending between others, provided:

•           The nonparty has a direct and immediate interest in the litigation; and

•           The intervention will not enlarge the issues in the case; and

•           The reasons for intervention outweigh any opposition by the existing parties. CCP §387; Truck Ins. Exch. v. Superior Court (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 342, 346. 

 

The party seeking to intervene must have a direct and immediate interest in the outcome of the litigation; i.e., he or she must stand to gain or lose by direct operation of the judgment.  City & County of San Francisco v. State of Calif. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1030, 1037. 

 

Whether intervention is of right, or only permissive, the party seeking to intervene must make “timely” application to the court.  CCP § 387(d)(1).  Timeliness of the motion to intervene is determined by the totality of the circumstances, with a focus on three primary factors: (a) the stage of the proceedings; (b) the prejudice to other parties from the delay in seeking to intervene; and (c) the reason for the delay. Prejudice to the existing parties from the delay is the most important consideration in determining whether the motion is timely.  Crestwood Behavioral Health, Inc. v. Lacy (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 560, 574-576. 

 

c.     Intervention as a Matter of Right

As noted above, an intervention takes place when the court grants leave to a nonparty to join the plaintiff in claiming what is sought by the complaint; or to unite with the defendant in resisting the plaintiff's claims; or to demand anything adverse to both parties.  CCP §387(b).  A nonparty has the right to intervene in litigation between others where the nonparty claims an interest in the property or transaction involved in the litigation, and is so situated that any judgment rendered in the nonparty's absence “may impair or impede that person's ability to protect that interest.” CCP § 387(d)(1)(B).

 

The Bonillas simply do not meet the definition of interveners as a matter of right detailed above.  They do not seek to join the plaintiff in claiming what is sought by the complaint; instead, they seek to bring their own independent claims for their own unique damages.  And to the extent an automobile accident is a “transaction,” their claims arise out of the same accident as Plaintiff’s claims, but “any judgment rendered in [the Bonillas’] absence” would not “impair or impede [the Bonillas’] ability to protect that interest. 

 

d.     Discretionary Intervention

As noted above, discretionary intervention can only be permitted if intervention will not enlarge the issues in the case.  Such is not the case here, as permitting the Bonillas to assert their claims would clearly change the scope of litigation.

 

e.     Timeliness

The motion is denied because the Bonillas failed to show that mandatory or permissive intervention applies, as discussed above.  Additionally, the Court notes that their motion was not timely made.  Plaintiff first filed this action on 1/09/23.  This motion is set to be heard almost a year and a half later. 

 

f.      Final Note

The Court notes that, while not mentioned in the opposition, it seems granting the motion would be an act in futility.  CCP §387(f) and Timberidge Enterprises, Inc. v. City of Santa Rosa (1978) 86 Cal.App.3d 873, 879-880 make clear that a defendant has the right to challenge a complaint in intervention by filing a demurrer.  If the Court were to permit the Bonillas to intervene, Defendant would clearly demur on the ground that the complaint in intervention is not timely, and the demurrer would be sustained without leave to amend.  It is not in the interest of justice to permit intervention, which would necessitate a continuance of the currently scheduled FSC and trial dates, only to have the complaint in intervention dismissed on demurrer. 

 

g.     Conclusion

The motion for leave to intervene is denied because Plaintiff failed to show mandatory or discretionary intervention is appropriate, because the motion was not timely filed, and because the motion, if granted, would ultimately result in dismissal of the complaint in intervention.

Case Number: 23LBCV00974    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jesus and Diana Renteria filed this action against Defendants, Pinner Construction Co., Inc.., et al. on 6/06/23.  Plaintiffs’ FAC alleges the defendants, and each of them, provided negligent drawings, plans, specifications, safety instructions, and designs for Plaintiff to use in constructing a building.  As a result, the building Plaintiff was working on collapsed, causing him to sustain grave injuries.  His wife sues for loss of consortium. 

 

Plaintiffs have added numerous doe defendants to the action, including CH2M Hill Engineers, Inc., which it named as Doe One on 9/07/23.  Many defendants have pending cross-complaints against the other defendants. 

 

2.     Demurrer

  1. Subject Cross-Complaint

Kern filed a cross-complaint on 7/26/23.  The cross-complaint is against various entities, including CH2M.  It includes causes of action for equitable indemnity, contribution and apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief. 

 

  1. Legal Standard

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares Counsel met and conferred extensively, but were unable to come to agreement concerning the issues presented by way of the demurrer.  The demurrer will therefore be heard on its merits. 

 

  1. Grounds for Demurrer

CH2M demurs to Kern’s cross-complaint on four grounds.  First, it contends Kern failed to plead any specific facts against CH2M that would support a claim for equitable indemnification.  Second, it contends Kern failed to file a certificate of merit, and this failure is fatal to its claims against CH2M.  Third, it contends the cross-complaint for contribution and apportionment of fault is not ripe.  Finally, it contends the cause of action for declaratory relief fails to state a claim for various reasons, which will be discussed below.

 

  1. Specific Facts

CH2M demurred to Plaintiffs’ complaint.  The Court ruled on the demurrer, in pertinent part, as follows:

Pursuant to Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527, a plaintiff can plead negligence against a defendant in general terms.  While the plaintiff cannot simply allege “the defendant’s negligence caused the plaintiff injury,” substantial detail is not required.  Id.  In this case, Plaintiff alleges the various defendants negligently provided the subject drawings, plans, specifications, safety instructions, and designs (negligence cause of action) and owned, maintained, managed, and/or operated the subject premises (premises liability cause of action).  At the pleading stage, this is sufficient.  The parties can resolve issues relating to the specifics of the claims through discovery.  The demurrer is overruled. 

 

CH2M’s demurrer to Kern’s cross-complaint mirrors its demurrer to Plaintiffs’ complaint.  CH2M again argues Kern failed to plead specific facts to support the conclusion that CH2M played a role in Plaintiffs’ injuries.  Specifically, CH2M argues, “Kern fails to satisfy the first element of its claim for equitable indemnity as to CH2M Hill because Kern has not alleged – and cannot allege – any facts to support that CH2M Hill is liable to Plaintiffs.” 

 

The Court finds the cross-complaint mirrors the language in the complaint, which the Court already ruled was sufficient.  The cross-complaint is essentially one for negligence, seeking indemnification to the extent any other entity’s negligence caused Plaintiffs’ damages.  CH2M has not cited any case holding that, in the indemnification context, a more strict pleading standard is applied than is applied in the main complaint; the language in the cross-complaint is typical of cross-complaints for indemnification, and issues relating to the defendants’ relative fault will be determined at trial.  The demurrer on this ground is overruled.

 

  1. Certificate of Merit

Kern concedes it failed to file a certificate of merit with its cross-complaint, and concedes this is fatal to its claim for equitable indemnification against CH2M.  It provides authority, however, for the position that the correct relief under the circumstances is sustaining the demurrer with leave to amend and requiring a certificate of merit.  See Price v. Dames & Moore (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 355, 357.  CH2M cites nothing to the contrary in reply.  The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend on this ground.

 

  1. Contribution and Apportionment of Fault

CH2M argues a claim for apportionment of fault and contribution is only ripe if and when there is a judgment in the main complaint.  Neither party cites a case directly on point.  There is no case cited where a court of appeals decided whether a trial court acted properly in dismissing a claim or failing to dismiss a claim for apportionment of fault and/or contribution from a cross-complaint.

 

The Court notes that these causes of action are routinely pled in cross-complaints.  Indeed, CH2M filed a cross-complaint on 6/03/24, and it includes a cause of action for “equitable apportionment,” which appears to be the same thing as a claim for apportionment of fault.  Notably, Judicial Council Form PLD-PI-002 is a form cross-complaint for use in personal injury cases, and it includes a cause of action for apportionment of fault. 

 

The demurrer is overruled.

 

  1. Declaratory Relief

Kern’s cross-complaint for declaratory relief consists only of two paragraphs.  The first paragraph incorporates the prior allegations by reference.  The second paragraph reads:

Cross-complainant is informed and believes, and thereupon alleges that an actual controversy exists between the parties concerning their respective rights and duties because cross-complainant contends and cross-defendants deny, that cross-defendant is responsible, in whole or in part, for the plaintiff’s injuries and damages, if any, arising from the occurrence described in plaintiff’s complaint.

 

The demurrer is sustained on two grounds.  First, it is not clear what declaration Kern is seeking.  Second, to the extent Kern is merely seeking a declaration that the other cross-defendants must indemnify it and/or contribute to Plaintiff’s damages, the cause of action is duplicative and superfluous in light of the first two causes of action in the cross-complaint.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if Kern can cure both defects. 

 

3.     Conclusion

CH2M’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as set forth fully above.  Kern must file an amended cross-complaint within twenty days.  CH2M must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter. 

 

Any other cross-defendant who wishes to file a responsive pleading to the amended cross-complaint may do so within the statutory time thereafter.  Any cross-defendant who answered the cross-complaint but does not timely respond to the amended cross-complaint will have its answer to the cross-complaint deemed its answer to the amended cross-complaint.

Case Number: 23LBCV01255    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Eric Cleveland filed this action against Defendants, Cancun Properties LLC, Anjai Savire LLC, and Sirenitas Restaurant, Inc. for violations of the ADA and UNRUH, contending he has osteoarthritis and Defendants’ property is in violation of the ADA and UNRUH because it needs to have an asphalt overlap in the parking area and needs to have work done on the disabled parking space for the property. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel

a.     Procedural History

Plaintiff filed this motion to compel on 4/15/24, setting it for hearing on 6/20/24.  The motion seeks to compel further responses to RPDs, further responses to FROGs, further responses to RFAs, and the depositions of David Yashar and Fernaz Esther Fard. 

 

On 6/07/24, Defendant filed opposition to the portion of the motion seeking to compel the depositions of Yashar and Fard.  On 6/08/24, Defendant filed opposition to the portion of the motion seeking to compel further responses to RPDs, FROGs, and RFAs. 

 

On 6/12/24, Plaintiff filed reply papers in response to the opposition. 

 

b.     Depositions

Plaintiff propounded demands for depositions on 2/27/24, pursuant to which he noticed the depositions of David Yashar and Farnaz Esther Fard.  The parties met and conferred repeatedly about deposition dates, but then on 3/20/24, Defense Counsel contended, for the first time, that Brett Daniels was the PMK for Cancun Properties and asked about a date for Daniels’s deposition.  On 3/22/24, Defendant clarified that it believes Plaintiff should depose ONLY Daniels and not Yashar or Fard.  On 4/15/24, having failed to resolve the issue, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel. 

 

Defendant opposes the motion, arguing Yashar and Fard are merely investors in Cancun, such that they lack personal knowledge of any details of this case.  Defendant argues the motion seeks to compel an “apex deposition,” which is improper under Liberty Mutual and its progeny. 

 

In both the moving papers and the reply papers, Plaintiff correctly notes that Defendant never served any objections to the deposition demand, and never filed a motion for a protective order concerning the depositions.  The cases cited by Defendant that collectively stand for the position that apex depositions are often improper were decided in the context of motions for protective orders.  Absent such motion, Defendant needed to, at minimum, serve timely objections to the deposition demand.  CCP §2025.450 requires the Court to grant the motion to compel when the deponent, having failed to serve a timely and valid objection, fails to appear at a properly noticed deposition.  Such is the case here, and the motion is granted.

 

The Court notes, additionally, that Yashar and Yard do not appear to fall into the category of persons whose depositions are precluded under Liberty Mutual and its progeny.  The seminal case concerning apex depositions is Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court, (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1282.  In that case, the plaintiff had suffered personal injuries on the job.  Liberty Mutual was the workers’ compensation carrier.  The plaintiff was suing Liberty Mutual for fraud and IIED concerning the claims handling process.  The plaintiff sought to depose the CEO of Liberty Mutual and cited, as a reason, the fact that the CEO had been CC’d on correspondence.  The trial court denied a motion for a protective order, and the Court of Appeals reversed.  It held that a deposition of the CEO was not appropriate unless the plaintiff showed the CEO had unique and superior knowledge of the facts, and the discovery could not be obtained by less intrusive means.  It held that the “head of a large national corporation will generally not have knowledge of a specific incident or case handled several levels down the corporate pyramid.”

 

Defendant failed to show that it is a large corporation, or that Yashar and/or Yard are apex persons in a large organization such that they would not have knowledge of the workings of the corporation.  Defendant contends Yashar and Yard are “mere investors,” but this does not make them akin to the CEO of a large insurance company being sued for claims handling.  If Yashar and Yard truly lack any substantive knowledge, then their depositions will be very short, but they failed to show that Plaintiff is not entitled to depose them.

Plaintiff’s complaint includes causes of action for:

Assault and battery – John Doe

Negligent hiring, retention, training, and supervision – Swift

Negligence – John Doe and Swift

Negligent entrustment – Swift

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he and Plaintiff’s attorney met and conferred telephonically, but were unable to resolve the issues presented by way of the demurrer.  The meet and confer requirement is therefore satisfied. 

 

  1. Negligent hiring, training, and retention

Defendant demurs to the negligent hiring, training, and retention cause of action, contending Plaintiff did not plead facts showing Defendant knew its security guard was dangerous prior to the incident at issue in this case. 

 

At ¶38, Plaintiff makes general allegations that the security guard was unfit for his employment and that Defendant knew he was unfit, as well as that he had a long history of criminal activity and police had been dispatched on numerous occasions prior to the incident.  There are, however, no facts pled to support this allegation.  Neither party briefs whether or to what extent a plaintiff must plead specific facts, as opposed to mere conclusions, to support a claim for negligent hiring, training, and retention. 

 

The Court researched the issue and located the case of Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1054, wherein the Court held (citations omitted):

The eighth cause of action is labeled "Negligent Hiring, Training and/or Supervision." In essence, plaintiff asserts that ABC's negligence in either hiring or supervising Marshall resulted in the sexual assault and on that basis ABC can be held liable.  California case law recognizes the theory that an employer can be liable to a third person for negligently hiring, supervising, or retaining an unfit employee. (citations.) Liability is based upon the facts that the employer knew or should have known that hiring the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm materializes. (citations).  This is where plaintiff's complaint flounders.

 

Plaintiff alleged that ABC knew or should have known that "Marshall engaged in the purchase and use of serious, mind-altering illegal drugs, that defendant Marshall used his position at ABC to gain sexual favors, and that use of a 'casting couch' is common within the entertainment industry."  Given the harm that plaintiff suffered--a brutal sexual assault after having been surreptitiously drugged--the pleading allegations are insufficient to allege a cause of action. ABC's knowledge that Marshall personally used "serious mind-altering drugs" does not equate with knowledge that he would surreptitiously use drugs to place a prospective employee into a situation of helplessness before violently assaulting him. Nor does ABC's knowledge that Marshall used his position "to gain sexual favors" have material relevance to this matter. Use of the word "gain" is consistent with the quid pro quo form of sexual harassment but that is not the basis of plaintiff's claim. The "casting couch" allegation suffers from a similar infirmity. That is, knowledge that Marshall used his position of authority to extract or to coerce sexual favors is not knowledge that he would first drug and then attack a potential employee. In the context of negligent hiring, those are qualitatively different situations. In sum, the cornerstone of a negligent hiring theory is the risk that the employee will act in a certain way and the employee does act in that way. Plaintiff has failed to allege those necessary facts.

 

Similarly here, because Plaintiff alleges no facts in support of his conclusions, the Court finds the cause of action is insufficiently pled.  The demurrer is sustained.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiff can plead facts, as opposed to conclusions, showing Defendant knew of the security guard’s lack of fitness for his job prior to the incident.  The Court notes that Plaintiff may not presently have those facts, in which case he should not amend the complaint at this time, but should seek leave to amend the complaint if and when he discovers such facts during the course of the action.  The Court will permit discovery into this issue, and encourages the parties to stipulate to the filing of an amended complaint if and when these facts come to light. 

 

  1. Negligence

Both parties appear to agree that the negligence cause of action sounds in vicarious liability, whereas the negligent hiring/training/supervision/retention and negligent entrustment causes of action sound in direct liability.  Defendant correctly notes that the complaint is highly confusing in this regard, because the cause of action for negligence contains numerous allegations that go to direct liability, rather than vicarious liability.  The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff must re-plead the negligence cause of action in a way that clarifies that he is seeking relief on a vicarious basis only in connection with the cause of action.

 

  1. Negligent Entrustment

The negligent entrustment cause of action suffers from the same defects as the negligent hiring, training, supervision, and retention cause of action.  There are no facts, as opposed to conclusions, pled to support the allegation that Swift negligently entrusted its automobile to John Doe.  The demurrer is sustained with the same conditional leave to amend detailed above.

The demurrer is sustained as set forth fully above.  The motion to strike is moot.  Plaintiff must file an amended complaint within twenty days.  Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.
