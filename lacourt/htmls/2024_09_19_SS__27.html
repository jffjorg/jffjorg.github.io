DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV28515    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DISMISSAL

Hearing Date: 9/19/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV28515 BERTHA GONZALEZ NUNEZ vs JOSE ALFARO et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DISMISSAL IS GRANTED 

 

On April 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel did not appear at the hearing due to technological issues. Plaintiff had arranged for coverage, and the retained counsel intended to appear remotely. However, on the day of the hearing, counsel was placed in the "waiting room" by CourtConnect and waited for some time before being disconnected. After unsuccessful attempts to reconnect, counsel called the Court and was informed that the servers were down (Exhibit 2).

 

The Court continued the hearing to June 26, 2024, but once again, neither Plaintiff nor counsel appeared, leading to the dismissal of the action without prejudice. Plaintiff alleges that the failure to appear was caused by excusable neglect, specifically due to regrettable miscommunication within the team and the failure to secure an attorney to cover the hearing. Plaintiff now moves the court for mandatory relief of the dismissal based on counsel’s mistake or neglect.

 

Section 473, subdivision (b) states in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.... Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Italics added.) 

 

Section 473, subdivision (b), provides for two distinct types of relief.  “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,” the court has discretion to allow relief from a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” a party or his or her attorney. Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,” the court shall vacate any “resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  The range of attorney conduct for which relief can be granted in the mandatory provision is broader than that in the discretionary provision, and includes inexcusable neglect.  But the range of adverse litigation results from which relief can be granted is narrower. Mandatory relief only extends to vacating a default which will result in the entry of a default judgment, a default judgment, or an entered dismissal.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, In (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615–616. Mandatory relief applies here, as Plaintiff’s counsel has submitted a declaration attesting to his mistake or neglect. The neglect, in the form of non-appearance, led to the Court’s dismissal of the case. Dismissal is the type of court order that qualifies for mandatory relief. Additionally, this motion was filed within six months of the dismissal, which occurred in June 2024. All requirements for mandatory relief have been met and the court GRANTS this motion.  The Court sets a non-appearance case review re default judgment for _____________.

MOTION FOR COSTS AND ATTORNEY’S FEES

Hearing Date: 9/19/24

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV04367 EDWIN SARGENTI vs CITY OF LONG BEACH, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant City of Long Beach

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION FOR COSTS AND ATTORNEY’S FEES IS DENIED

 

Background

The action stems from Plaintiff Edwin Sargenti’s fall while operating a Lime electric scooter on a City of Long Beach sidewalk after entering into a Lime user agreement. The City is designated as a third-party beneficiary of this Agreement. On May 14, 2024, the Court granted Defendant City of Long Beach’s Motion for Summary Judgment against Plaintiff. Defendant served the Notice of Entry of Judgment on July 5, 2024. On August 6, 2024, Defendant moved the court to award it $54,708.06 in costs and $329,208.75 in attorney’s fees, pursuant to the indemnity clause of the agreement and Code of Civil Procedure § 1032(b).

Costs

Code of Civil Procedure section 1032 establishes that a defendant dismissed from an action is a prevailing party and, as a matter of right, is entitled to recover costs in any action or proceeding unless a statute specifically states otherwise. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a)(4).) A defendant is considered the "prevailing party" whether the dismissal is voluntary or involuntary. (Santisas v. Goodin (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 606.) Furthermore, a defendant is entitled to costs whether the dismissal is with or without prejudice. (Cano v. Glover (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 326, 331.) 

A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.5, or the date of service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is earlier. The memorandum of costs must be verified through a statement from the party, attorney, or agent certifying that, to the best of their knowledge, the listed costs are correct and were necessarily incurred in the case. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a)(1).)

Defendant references Rule 3.1702 to support the contention that this statute allows for the filing of a motion for costs; however, Rule 3.1702 does not govern the deadline for filing a memorandum of costs. Rule 3.1702, titled "CLAIMING ATTORNEY'S FEES," specifies in subdivision (a), "Application," that the rule applies in civil cases to claims for statutory attorney's fees and contractual attorney's fees, unless otherwise provided by statute. Neither subdivision (b) nor (c) of Rule 3.1702 mentions a memorandum of costs. The only mention of a memorandum of costs in Rule 3.1702 is in subdivision (e), "Attorney's fees fixed by formula," which states that if a party is entitled to statutory or contractual attorney's fees that are predetermined without court intervention, those fees must be claimed in the memorandum of costs. This provision facilitates the claiming of attorney's fees through the memorandum of costs when the fees are fixed.

This specific provision is irrelevant here as the attorney fees in question are not fixed by a formula or a predetermined amount.

Rules 3.1700 and 3.1702 establish distinct procedures for asserting and contesting claims within their respective scopes. Rule 3.1700 imposes relatively brief periods for the filing of a memorandum of costs and motions to tax costs, whereas Rule 3.1702 provides a more extended period for filing motions for attorney's fees in unlimited civil actions. Conflicts between Rules 3.1700 and 3.1702 concerning attorney’s fees are resolved in favor of Rule 3.1702 because it is deemed specific relative to the general nature of Rule 3.1700. In such cases, the specific rule prevails unless a contrary intention is clearly evident. (Kaufman v. Diskeeper Corp., (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1, 9.)

Kaufman, as relied upon by Defendant, does not substantiate the position that a motion to tax costs, which is subject to a 15-day deadline, can be filed concurrently with a motion for attorney fees under a 60-day deadline. Defendant has not contested this point in the reply. Consequently, the relevant deadline for claiming costs is 15 days after Defendant served the Notice of Entry of Judgment, in accordance with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a)(1). The time provisions for filing a memorandum of costs are mandatory. (Sanabria v. Embrey (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 422, 426.) Therefore, Defendant's request for costs is untimely and is denied.

Attorney’s Fees

Defendant relies on sections 1.4.7, 1.8, 1.15, and 8.1 of the user agreement between Lyft and Plaintiff for the recovery of City of Long Beach's attorney fees incurred in this matter. The Court notes that these provisions in the contract are indemnification and assumption of risk clauses, and not attorney fees provisions where the prevailing party can collect attorney fees. Defendant is attempting to collect attorney fees pursuant to the indemnity and assumption of risk provisions in the agreement.

The Court will analyze each provision in turn:

Section 1.4.7. Indemnification. To the fullest extent permitted by applicable law and without limiting Your indemnification obligations set forth in Section 8 of this Agreement, You agree to indemnify and hold harmless Lime from any tickets, citations, fines, penalties and administrative fees incurred as a result of Your use of a Vehicle, and any and all consequences, claims, demands, causes of action, losses, liabilities, damages, injuries, fees, costs and expenses, penalties, attorneys’ fees, judgments, suits settlements or disbursements of any kind or nature arising from: (a) Your negligence or willful misconduct; or (b) Your breach of this Agreement. 

Section 1.4.7 applies exclusively to Lime, as the specific language indicates, “You agree to indemnify and hold harmless Lime from any tickets.” This provision is inapplicable to the City.

Sections 1.8, 1.15, and 8.1 potentially apply to the City as these sections refer to a "released party" which encompasses the City, or the provisions do not specify to whom they apply, allowing for broader applicability.

Section 8.1 states:

“You agree to defend, indemnify, and hold harmless the Released Persons from and against any and all consequences, claims, demands, causes of action, losses, liabilities, damages, injuries, fees, costs and expenses, penalties, attorneys’ fees, judgments, suits settlements, and/or disbursements of any kind, or nature whatsoever, whether foreseeable or unforeseeable, and whether known or unknown, that directly or indirectly arise from or are related to any claim, suit, action, demand, or proceeding made or brought against any Released Person, or on account of the investigation, defense, or settlement thereof, arising out of or in connection with, whether occurring heretofore or hereafter: (i) Your use or misuse of the Services, Products, and/or related equipment, (ii) Your User Submissions; (iii) Your use of the Services and Your activities in connection with the Services; (iv) Your breach or alleged breach of this Agreement or any Additional Terms;

Then there are the assumption of risk provisions stating:

1.15. Assumption of Liability by You: YOU AGREE THAT YOU ARE SOLELY RESPONSIBLE AND LIABLE FOR ANY MISUSE, CONSEQUENCES, CLAIMS, DEMANDS, CAUSES OF ACTION, LOSSES, LIABILITIES, DAMAGES, INJURIES, FEES, COSTS AND EXPENSES, PENALTIES, ATTORNEYS’ FEES, JUDGMENTS, SUITS AND/OR DISBURSEMENTS OF ANY KIND, OR NATURE WHATSOEVER, WHETHERFORESEEABLE OR UNFORESEEABLE, AND WHETHER KNOWN OR UNKNOWN, AS A RESULT OF USING ANY OF THE SERVICES

1.8. Compliance with Laws: YOU ARE SOLELY RESPONSIBLE AND LIABLE FOR ANY VIOLATION OF ANY LAWS, RULES, REGULATIONS, AND/OR ORDINANCES CAUSED BY YOUR USE OF THE SERVICES, INCLUDING IMPROPER RIDING AND/OR PARKING, AND YOU AGREE THAT YOU ARE SOLELY RESPONSIBLE AND LIABLE FOR ANY AND ALL CONSEQUENCES, CLAIMS, DEMANDS, CAUSES OF ACTION, LOSSES, LIABILITIES, DAMAGES, INJURIES, COSTS AND EXPENSES, PENALTIES, ATTORNEYS’ FEES, JUDGMENTS, SUITS, FEES (INCLUDING IMPOUNDING FEES CHARGED BY ANY LOCAL GOVERNMENT) AND/OR DISBURSEMENTS OF ANY KIND, OR NATURE WHATSOEVER, WHETHER FORESEEABLE OR UNFORESEEABLE, AND WHETHER KNOWN OR UNKNOWN, AS A RESULT OF USING ANY OF THE SERVICES.

“Generally, indemnity is defined as an obligation of one party to pay or satisfy the loss or damage incurred by another party.” (Rideau v. Stewart Title of California, Inc., (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1287, 1294.) Generally, an indemnification provision allows one party to recover costs incurred defending actions by third parties, not attorney fees incurred in an action between the parties to the contract. (Rideau, supra, 235 Cal.App.4th at p. 1298.) Courts look to several indicators to distinguish third party indemnification provisions from provisions for the award of attorney fees incurred in litigation between the parties to the contract. The key indicator is an express reference to indemnification. A clause that contains the words “indemnify” and “hold harmless” generally obligates the indemnitor to reimburse the indemnitee for any damages the indemnitee becomes obligated to pay third persons, that is, it relates to third party claims, not attorney fees incurred in a breach of contract action between the parties to the indemnity agreement itself. (Carr Business Enterprises v. City of Chowchilla, (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 1, 20.)

Broad language such as “‘Seller ... agrees to indemnify and save buyer ... harmless from any and all losses ... including ... reasonable attorney's fees ... arising from any cause or for any reason whatsoever’ does not provide for attorney fees in an action between the parties for breach of contract.” (Alki Partners, LP v. DB Fund Servs., LLC (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 574, 601.) Similarly, an indemnification clause in which one party promised to “indemnify” the other from “‘any, all, and every claim’ which arises out of ‘the performance of the contract’ ” (Citing Myers Building Industries, Ltd. v. Interface Technology, Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 949, 974) deals only with third party claims, and cannot support an award of attorney fees in an action for breach of contract between the parties to the agreement. The Myers court held that, considering the ordinary meaning of the words “indemnify” and the context of the provisions, the contract could not be construed as separately providing for attorney fees in an action between the parties. (Ibid.) The Myers court determined the indemnity provision there did not afford a right to attorney fees incurred in breach of contract litigation between the parties to the agreement, even though, the contract provided the indemnity agreement was to be enforced “[t]o the fullest extent permitted by law.” (Id. at pp. 963–964.)

Although the provisions at issue use broad language similar to that in Myers such as "any, all, and every claim which arises out of the performance of the contract ... to the fullest extent permitted by law” or "harmless from any and all losses ... including ... reasonable attorney's fees ... arising from any cause or for any reason whatsoever," Myers (as well as Alki) denied attorney’s fees.

In this case, both express references to indemnification and assumption of risk are evident in the titles of the provisions. The provisions are not titled as attorney fee provisions, and Defendant has not provided such a provision. There is also usage of terms like "indemnify" or "hold harmless," which are standard for indemnity agreements in section 8.1, and terms like "YOU ARE SOLELY RESPONSIBLE AND LIABLE," which are typical for assumption of risk provisions under sections 1.15 and 1.8. Furthermore, within the provisions themselves, it does not contain any language that is typical of an attorney’s fee provision in a lawsuit between the parties, such as “the prevailing party can recover attorney’s fees.” The purposes of attorney fee provisions, indemnity provisions, and assumption of risk provisions all differ: an attorney fee provision governs disputes between the parties; an indemnity provision applies when a third party, not the parties themselves, sues one of the parties; and an assumption of risk provision involves one party acknowledging and accepting the inherent risks of an activity or service, thereby limiting the liability of the provider for those risks. Essentially, these are standard indemnity and assumption of risk provisions, although very broad in language; they lack any specific mention that in a lawsuit between the parties, the prevailing party would be entitled to attorney’s fees.

Defendant cites Hom v. Petrou (67 Cal.App.5th 459, 472), but in that case, there was an attorney fee provision between the contracting parties stating, "Should any dispute arise from this Lease or the tenancy hereby created, and the parties cannot settle it between themselves, then the prevailing party will be entitled to reimbursement of its reasonable attorneys’ fees." Under this agreement, the third-party beneficiaries were able to recover fees against one of the parties. This is distinguishable from the present case, where no such attorney fee provision exists for the prevailing party.

Thus, attorney’s fees are denied.

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV31561 KEVIN JOSHUA ROGOFF et al. vs LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY et al. 

Moving Party: Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transit Authority and Marcelino Efrocino Cipriano Barrozo 

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿Kevin Rogoff

Ruling: MOTION FOR SANCTIONS¿IS GRANTED 

 

On August 26, 2021, Plaintiffs Kevin Joshua Rogoff, Fanny Valenzuela, Nefretiri Rogoff and Judith Rogoff filed the instant action for negligence and motor vehicle negligence against, among others, Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transit Authority and Marcelino Efrocino Cipriano Barrozo (collectively referred to herein as “Defendants”). On October 17, 2023, Defendants’ counsel personally served Plaintiffs’ counsel with a Notice of Demand for an Independent Medical Examination (IME) of Kevin Rogoff, scheduled for November 16, 2023. The notice specified that “any continuance or cancellation of this examination must be received no later than 48 hours prior to the examination date” and that “failure to provide such notice will render Plaintiff responsible for any fees charged due to late cancellation.” On November 14, 2023, Defendants’ counsel emailed Plaintiffs’ counsel to confirm that the IMEs were proceeding as scheduled for November 16, 2023. On November 15, 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel responded, claiming non-receipt of the IME demands and stating that Plaintiff Kevin Rogoff would not be able to attend. As a result, Defendants incurred a $1,500 late cancellation fee for his nonappearance at the IME with Dr. Nelson. (Exhibit 3.) On November 15, 2023, Defendants’ counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter to Plaintiffs’ counsel requesting reimbursement for the cancellation fee charged by Dr. Nelson. Plaintiffs’ counsel failed to respond to this meet-and-confer attempt. On May 22, 2024, Kevin Rogoff was ordered to attend a second IME for his right hip on May 28, 2024, with Dr. Nelson. However, on May 28, 2024, Mr. Rogoff failed to appear for the court-ordered IME. Neither Mr. Rogoff nor his attorney’s office provided any notice to Defendants. Defendants were informed of Mr. Rogoff’s failure to appear by Dr. Nelson’s office, and another $1,500 no-show fee was incurred. (Exhibit 9.) Defendants’ counsel made multiple attempts, through emails and meet-and-confer efforts, to seek reimbursement from Plaintiffs’ counsel for the cancellation fees resulting from Plaintiff’s non-appearance and late cancellation. Plaintiffs’ counsel did not respond to any of these attempts. Defendants now move the Court for sanctions against Plaintiff Kevin Rogoff and his counsel, in the amount of $3,960. 

Plaintiff argues that this motion should be denied because it contains a typographical error, referring to “Nefretiri Rogoff” instead of the correct name, “Kevin Rogoff,” in the notice of the motion. However, this is a minor error, as the motion and caption clearly identify the sanctions as being sought against Kevin Rogoff. Moreover, it is well-settled that the appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and opposition to the motion on its merits constitutes a waiver of any defects or irregularities in the notice of motion. (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697.) Defendant contested the motion on its merits, thereby waiving any notice defects. 

Furthermore, Plaintiff does not dispute the sequence of events presented by Defendants but requests that sanctions not be imposed because Plaintiffs’ counsel lost contact with Plaintiff regarding the second IME. However, despite losing contact with Plaintiff, Plaintiffs’ counsel did not communicate this to Defendants’ counsel 48 hours before the IME to potentially reschedule the IME and avoid incurring cancellation fees. Plaintiff’s counsel also did not substantively respond to Defendants meet and confer attempts after the IME inquiring into the reason for Mr. Rogoff’s nonappearance.  Plaintiff did not present any argument in the opposition regarding the late cancellation of the first IME. Plaintiff also failed to provide any justification for not giving the 48-hour notice before the IME date to avoid cancellation fees, as stated in the notice. The Court does not find the imposition of sanctions unjust. 

Plaintiff further argues that the $3,000 cancellation fee is unreasonable and unsupported. However, Defendant has provided invoices from Dr. Nelson, showing that $1,500 was charged for each cancellation (Exhibits 3, 9). It is reasonable and common for a doctor to charge such fees for either a no-show or a last-minute cancellation, and the Court finds the amount of sanctions reasonable and substantiated. 

Moreover, Defendant has made multiple attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the cancellation fees, as evidenced by Exhibits 4, 6, and 8, but received no responses. Consequently, Defendant had to file this motion to recover the cancellation fees.  

The Court finds that sanctions in the amount of $3,000 for the cancellation fees are appropriate based on Plaintiff’s late cancellations, failure to appear at the scheduled IME, failure to provide timely communication to avoid the cancellation fees, and the lack of response to Defendants' meet-and-confer efforts. While Plaintiff is correct that sanctions are not intended to punish a party, in this case, the $3,000 sanctions are not to punish but to reimburse Defendant for the cancellation fees incurred due to Plaintiff's nonappearance and late cancellations. Additionally, the $960 sanction is to compensate Defendant for attorney’s fees incurred in seeking recovery of the cancellation fees. Plaintiff could have avoided this motion by responding to Defendant’s counsel's numerous meet and confer attempts, but failed to do so over a long period of time. The Court thus orders sanctions in the amount of $3,960 against Plaintiff and his attorney, jointly and severally, payable to Defendant within 20 days from today.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

Hearing Date: 9/19/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV07704 MARILYN GOTTESMAN vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al. 

Moving Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED 

 

Background

On September 27, 2011, at approximately 8:30 a.m., Plaintiff Marilyn Gottesman tripped and fell due to a sidewalk displacement located at 8124 Regis Way in Los Angeles. Defendant City of Los Angeles moves the Court for summary judgment on the basis that the displacement which caused Plaintiff to fall is a trivial defect.

 

Undisputed Facts

On September 27, 2021, at approximately 8:30 A.M., Plainitff tripped and fell on the sidewalk at/near 8124 Regis Way in Los Angeles. Plaintiff tripped and fell as a result of her right foot hitting an uplift in the sidewalk. The day of her fall was the first time that Plaintiff had walked on the side of the block where she fell. Plaintiff was walking a dog (a Siberian husky) on a 5- to 6-foot-long leash. The leash was in Plaintiff’s left hand and the dog was on Plaintiff’s left side. Prior to the trip-and-fall accident, Plaintiff and her dog stopped about 5 to 6 feet from the uplift.  As Plaintiff and her dog resumed the walk, Plaintiff scanned the area in front of her but did not see the uplift. A red circle on Exhibit 2-3 to the transcript of Plaintiff’s deposition depicts the area where Plaintiff tripped. At the location of the Plaintiff’s accident, the sidewalk comprises concrete and measures approximately 5 feet wide. The sidewalk was firm and did not have any jagged or loose edges.  At the time of the accident, there were no obstructions blocking Plaintiff’s view of the sidewalk. The lighting conditions were sufficient for the uplift to have been seen.

Legal Standard

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

 

¿               The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)¿ 

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court overrules Plaintiff’s Objections No. 1 and 2. The parties did not make any other objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling, as per CCP § 437c(q).

 

Premises Liability 

 

The Trivial Defect Doctrine 

 

“Property owners are required to maintain land in their possession and control in a reasonably safe condition and to use due care to eliminate dangerous conditions on their property.  But a property owner is not liable for damages caused by a minor, trivial, or insignificant defect on its property.  The so-called ‘trivial defect doctrine’ recognizes that persons who maintain walkways, whether public or private, are not required to maintain them in an absolutely perfect condition.  The duty of care imposed on a property owner, even one with actual notice, does not require the repair of minor defects.”  (Fajardo v. Dailey (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 221, 226 [cleaned up].)  

 

“In limited circumstances a court may determine a walkway defect is trivial as a matter of law.  Where reasonable minds can reach only one conclusion—that there was no substantial risk of injury—the issue is a question of law, properly resolved by way of summary judgment.  But where sufficient evidence has been presented so that reasonable minds may differ as to whether the defect is dangerous, summary judgment is inappropriate.”  (Id. at p. 226.)   

   

California Courts have developed two substantially similar tests to determine whether a sidewalk defect is trivial, i.e., not dangerous, as a matter of law.  In Stathoulis v. City of Montebello (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 559, 567-68, the Court of Appeal set out a two-part test. “First, the court reviews evidence regarding the type and size of the defect.  If that preliminary analysis reveals a trivial defect, the court considers evidence of any additional factors such as the weather, lighting and visibility conditions at the time of the accident, the existence of debris or obstructions, and plaintiff’s knowledge of the area.  If these additional factors do not indicate the defect was sufficiently dangerous to a reasonably careful person, the court should deem the defect trivial as a matter of law ....” (See also Nunez v. City of Redondo Beach (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 749, 758 [adopting two-step framework]; Huckey v. City of Temecula (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1105 [same].) 

 

More recently, in Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102, 110, the Court of Appeal read Government Code section 830.2 to require a more “holistic” approach, stating, “[a]lthough we agree with the premise that the size of the defect is the primary determinant of triviality, as discussed below, we modify the prevailing two-step framework into a holistic, multi-factor analysis.”  The Court will follow Stack’s holistic approach, although the result would be the same under either test.

 

1. Size of the Defect

 

“We begin with the most important factor: the defect’s size.” (Stack, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 110 [cleaned up].)  “Sidewalk elevations ranging from three-quarters of an inch to one and one-half inches have generally been held trivial as a matter of law.” (Huckey, supra, 37 Cal.App.5th at p. 1107; see Stathoulis, supra, 164 Cal.App.4th at p. 568 [“Several decisions have found height differentials of up to one and one-half inches trivial as a matter of law.”])  A more “accurate encapsulation” of the law, according to Stack, is that “when the size of the depression begins to stretch beyond one inch the courts have been reluctant to find that the defect is not dangerous as a matter of law, i.e., that it is minor or trivial.”  (Stack, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 112.)  “That said, there is no firmly fixed arbitrary measurement in inches below which a defect is trivial as a matter of law and above which it becomes a question of fact whether or not the defect is dangerous.  This is because a court should not rely solely upon the size of the defect ... although the defect’s size ‘may be one of the most relevant factors’ to the court’s decision.”  (Id. at pp. 112–113 [cleaned up].)

 

The parties provide slightly different measurements of the height of the displacement. Plaintiff’s expert measures it at 1 ¼ inches and Defendant’s expert measures it at slightly over 1 inch.

Upon closer examination of the experts’ declarations, the Court finds that the declarations are not actually in conflict with each other, as they measure different sections of the displacement. Mr. Gsell, Plaintiff’s expert, measured three sections of the displacement on the concrete slab, from the far right side (IMG_7177), to the middle (IMG_7179), and approximately seven inches left of the center slab (IMG_7182). The respective measurements were 1 ¼, ¾, and 5/8 inches (Gsell Decl. ¶ 8, 9). These measurements indicate that the displacement is most pronounced on the right and lessens as one moves to the left of the slab.

  However, Plaintiff's argument that the displacement causing her fall measured 1 ¼ inches is unsupported by her own expert’s findings and Plaintiff’s own testimony.  Mr. Gsell's declaration does not specify whether any of these measurements correspond to the exact location where Plaintiff fell. He did not assert that the location of Plaintiff’s fall was at the 1 ¼ inch displacement; rather, he provided measurements from three disparate locations across the slab. No rationale was provided for choosing these specific locations, other than to demonstrate the change in elevation from right to left. This is further confirmed by an examination of IMG_7177, showing that the measurement hugs the very edge of the concrete slab.

In her deposition, Plaintiff testified that at the time of her trip-and-fall, she was walking "towards the right side… middle to right side" of the sidewalk, and she "circled" the approximate area on which she tripped and fell on a photograph depicting the subject sidewalk condition (City’s Compendium of Evidence, Ex. 3 – Transcript of Plaintiff’s Deposition, at transcript page 44:4-14; Ex. 6 – Blanchette Declaration, ¶ 6 and Ex. B). Plaintiff did not testify that she tripped on the very right edge of the sidewalk where it measures 1 ¼ inches in height.

On the other hand, Defendant’s expert, Blanchette, accounted for Plaintiff's testimony that she was walking from the middle to the right side of the sidewalk and measured the displacement at the approximate location where Plaintiff's foot caught the displacement (Blanchette Decl. ¶ 8). It shows a measurement slightly above one inch, specifically 1.03125 inches, as Defendant clarified in their reply.

Although Plaintiff argues that her expert measured the displacement in 2021 whereas Defendant’s expert measured it in 2023, Blanchette has declared that the conditions are substantially similar (Blanchette Decl. ¶ 6). Upon examination of both experts' investigations, the Court finds that the conditions were substantially similar; no work appears to have been performed on the displacement, nor has there been any change. Furthermore, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence to suggest that the displacement has shrunk, contrary to the common experience that displacements typically grow over time.

Here, there is really no contradictory testimony from the two experts. The measurements were taken at different locations, with Plaintiff’s expert measuring the farthest right of the slab while Defendant's expert took measurements to the left of that point. Given the nature of the displacement, which is highest on the far right and decreases as you move left, it is unsurprising that the two measurements differ. Because Plaintiff’s expert did not state that the right edge is where Plaintiff fell, and this is not supported by Plaintiff’s own testimony, the Court finds that Defendant’s expert’s measurement, which takes into account Plaintiff’s testimony, reflects the height of the displacement where Plaintiff fell.

It is undisputed that the sidewalk pavement displacement in question here was 1and 1/4 inch at its highest point, tapering off in height towards the right. (City’s Exh. H, I, J.) Considering the size of the defect alone, without any aggravating factors, the displacement is trivial even at its highest point and is even less of an issue at the place where Plaintiff tripped.  The Court finds the size of the displacement to be about 1 inch. 

 

2.  Aggravating Factors 

 

The Court next considers additional factors.  “Beyond size, additional factors courts typically consider in assessing a sidewalk condition’s triviality as a matter of law are: the nature and quality of the defect (including whether it has jagged breaks or cracks); whether anything was obstructing or concealing the defect (for instance, an object, debris, or other substance); the lighting and weather conditions at the time of the incident; whether the defect has caused other accidents; and plaintiff's familiarity with the area.”  (Stack, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 115.)   

 

At the time of the accident, there were no obstructions blocking Plaintiff’s view of the sidewalk (Def's Ex. 3 - Gottesman Dep., at 35:13-16, 35:20-36:5). At the time of Plaintiff’s fall, the lighting conditions were more than sufficient for the uplift to have been seen and avoided (Def's Ex. 6 - Blanchette Dec ¶ 7). The subject sidewalk condition is firm, stable, and does not have any jagged, loose edges (Def's Ex. 6 - Blanchette Dec ¶ 8). The City did not receive any notifications, complaints, or requests for sidewalk repair at/near 8124 Regis Way, Los Angeles prior to Plaintiff’s alleged September 27, 2021 accident (Def's Ex. 7 – Declaration of Glenn Lacoure ¶ 4; see also, Def's Ex. 4 – City’s Special Interrogatories to Plaintiff, Set One, at Interrogatory Nos. 7-9; Def's Ex. 5 – Plaintiff’s Responses to City’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, at Responses to Interrogatory Nos. 7-9).

Although Plaintiff argues that the displacement must be assessed holistically, she either does not dispute the presence of aggravating factors or has failed to present substantial evidence of such factors. First, Plaintiff does not dispute that there were no obstructions blocking her view of the sidewalk at the time of the accident (Plf’s Response to UMF 12). Plaintiff also does not contend that lighting or visibility was an issue (Plf’s Response to UMF 13). Both Mr. Blanchette’s and Mr. Gsell’s photographs from their inspections show that there is no debris, plants, or roots covering the sidewalk; there are no jagged or loose edges, and the sidewalk appears to be stable. No contrary evidence has been provided, nor is there any evidence that another individual fell at the same spot.

As for Plaintiff’s familiarity with the sidewalk, she had walked in the neighborhood where she fell twice a day since 2017, but she had not previously walked on the side of the street where the incident occurred. This information is supported by evidence in Defendant’s Compendium, Ex. 3 – Gottesman Dep., at 25:13-15; 26:2-9; 25:16-19. This factor is considered neutral.

Overall, the court finds the displacement is question measures at just very slightly above 1 inch, and there were no aggravating factors. Thus, the motion for summary judgment is granted, as the Court finds the sidewalk displacement to be trivial.

MOTION TO DISMISS AND MOTIONS TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES

Hearing Date: 9/19/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV02001 BRYSON PHINALI ARRINGTON vs SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA GAS COMPANY et al.

Moving Party: Defendant SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA GAS COMPANY 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO DISMISS IS DENIED;

MOTIONS TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES ARE GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART

 

Background

 

On June 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging a natural gas explosion that occurred on February 1, 2021, in Los Angeles, California. On May 13, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel, and Plaintiff has since been appearing in the case pro per. On May 16, 2024, Defendant SoCalGas served its Form Interrogatories (Set No. Two), Special Interrogatories (Set No. Two), Requests for Admission (Set No. One), and Requests for Production of Documents (Set No. Two) to Plaintiff. On July 10, 2024, Defendant attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff regarding outstanding discovery responses and gave Plaintiff an extension to provide the outstanding discovery responses by July 19, 2024. As of July 31, 2024, SoCalGas has not received discovery responses and now moves to dismiss (the Court presumes, though the motion does not specifically reference “terminating sanctions,” that the motion to dismiss is effectively a request for terminating sanctions, or, in the alternative, to compel discovery responses.

Terminating Sanctions

A terminating sanction is a “drastic measure which should be employed with caution.” (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 793.) “A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.) Terminating sanctions are premature here, as there has not been any violation of a court order, thus no history of abuse exists.

Motions To Compel Initial Responses

Although the Court declines to impose terminating sanctions,  the Court finds it appropriate to grant Defendant’s motion to compel. As a preliminary matter, the Court will note that the format and content of the motion leave much to be desired, as it did not cite any statute or case law as grounds to compel initial responses. However, motions to compel initial responses are fairly straightforward, and the grounds of the motion are evidenced by the timeline provided by Defendant and Plaintiff’s failure to serve discovery responses as stated in defense counsel’s declaration attached to the present motion.

 

Legal Standard

 

1.  Requests for Production and Interrogatories

 

A defendant may make a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling without leave of court at any time. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (a).) A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)       

      

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)  The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

2.  Request for Admissions

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”  CCP § 2033.010.  “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”  CCP § 2033.250(a).

 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c). A motion to deem RFA admitted does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Discussion

 

It is undisputed that Plaintiff did not timely respond to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set No. Two), Special Interrogatories (Set No. Two), Requests for Admission (Set No. One), and Requests for Production of Documents (Set No. Two). Plaintiff did not file an opposition or any filings indicating to the Court that the responses at issue have been served. Thus, the Defendant’s motions to compel responses to Form and Special Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents are granted. Plaintiff is hereby ordered to serve verified and complete responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set No. Two), Special Interrogatories (Set No. Two), and Requests for Production of Documents (Set No. Two) without objections within 20 days of today.

The Court denies Defendant’s request to compel regarding Defendant’s Requests for Admission (Set No. One).  Nowhere in the motion does Defendant indicate it is seeking to have the RFA deemed admitted, and the CCP does not provide for an order to compel responses to an RFA.  (Again, the Court notes that the format and content of the motion leave much to be desired).

DEMURRER

Hearing Date: 9/19/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV18819 ALEJANDRA QUINTERO vs PERCH RESTAURANT, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: DEMURRER IS SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

Background

On August 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants Perch Restaurant, Pershing Square Downtown LA; Citrus Hospitality Management; JMF Pershing Square LLC; JMF Enterprise LLC; JMF Enterprises V LLC; and DOES 1 through 100, alleging three causes of action: (1) general negligence, (2) premises liability, and (3) products liability. Plaintiff alleges she incurred injuries at Defendants’ restaurant after encountering a conduit or piping for an outdoor patio heater.

On February 2, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants' demurrer with leave to amend as to Plaintiff's third cause of action for product liability. In reaching its decision, the Court recognized California’s strict products liability precedents which have established "a bright-line legal distinction" that imposes liability only on entities responsible for introducing an injury-producing product into the stream of commerce. (Taylor v. Elliott Turbomachinery Co., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 564, 576.)

The Supreme Court of California concluded that it would be improper to impose strict liability on the hotel proprietor for injuries caused by an alleged defect in hotel premises that the proprietor did not build or market. (Peterson v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1185 ,1188.) The Court further explained that “although many potentially defective products are used in hotel or restaurant setting, the mere circumstance that it was contemplated customers of these businesses would use the products ... or be benefited by them does not transform the owners of the business into the equivalent of retailers of the products. [Citation]” (Id., at pp. 1199-1200.) “The mere foreseeability of injury to users of a defective product was not sufficient justification for imposing strict liability outside the stream of commerce.” (O’Neil v. Crane Co., (2012) 53 Cal.4th 335, 349.)

Furthermore, the Court held that in attempting to meet the pleading requirements for a product liability case, Plaintiff made several allegations, such as "Defendants placed the item in the stream of commerce" or "Defendant manufactured and installed component parts which were incorporated into the final product and were also defective." However, the Court found these to be legal conclusions and not to be considered by the Court.

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint. Pershing Hill LLC, doing business as Perch and erroneously sued as Perch Restaurant; JMF Enterprises V LLC; JMF Pershing Square LLC; and Citrus LLC, erroneously sued as Citrus Hospitality Management ("Defendants"), now demur to the product liability cause of action in the second amended complaint (SAC). Based on a side-by-side comparison of the FAC and the SAC, the Court determined that the only new information pled in the SAC is as follows:

“Defendants, and each of them, are the owners of multiple restaurants. They are also the builder of restaurants (i.e., construct the restaurants and "build them out from scratch". As an owner/ builder of multiple restaurants, they participated in and conducted the design, construction, and building of the subject restaurant, as well as multiple other restaurants. After designing, constructing, building the restaurant, Defendants leased the property for use to their other codefendants, whom are alleged to be alter-egos of each other. As a builder engaged in the business of constructing (i.e., manufacturing), as well as leasing properties to each other, products liability attaches.”

“Additionally, with regards to the product alleged to be defective in this Complaint, Defendants, and each of them, rebuilt, repurposed, rewired, redesigned, reconstructed, and reconditioned the product, thus assuming the role analogous to that of a manufacturer.”

Discussion

On February 2, 2024, the Court found that the First Amended Complaint (FAC) did not allege that Plaintiff was responsible for making or marketing the defective product, and merely because customers would use the products or benefit from them does not transform the owners of the business into retailers of the products, or part of the stream of commerce  per the bright-line rule under Taylor v. Elliott Turbomachinery Co., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 564, 576, where liability is imposed only on those entities responsible for placing an injury-producing product into the stream of commerce. In the SAC, Plaintiff attempts to circumvent the issue that Defendant did not make or market the product at issue, by alleging that “Defendants, and each of them, rebuilt, repurposed, rewired, redesigned, reconstructed, and reconditioned the product, thus assuming a role analogous to that of a manufacturer.”  The Court first notes that Defendants were previously found not to be within the stream of commerce as to the original unmodified product. Plaintiff’s new theory seems to be that Defendants reconstructed or created a new defective product. However, Plaintiff failed to allege facts that this newly reconstructed product was put into the stream of commerce. Although Plaintiff made conclusory statements in the SAC such as "Defendants placed the item in the stream of commerce," these were the same statements made in the FAC that the Court, on February 2, 2024, already determined to be legal conclusions. In what has been newly alleged in the SAC, there is no factual allegation that this newly reconstructed product was part of a distribution chain. No distributors, retailers or consumers are identified, nor was it alleged that this modified product is being sold to consumers. All that is alleged seems to be that the product was used by the Defendants in their own restaurants, which Plaintiff alleges are alter egos of each other.

The Court in O'Neil v. Crane Co. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 335, 349, emphasized that strict products liability should be imposed only on those entities responsible for placing a defective product into the stream of commerce. This is the crucial distinction between the present case and Kriegler v. Eichler Homes, Inc. (1969) 269 Cal.App.2d 224, a case that Plaintiff heavily relies on. In Kriegler, the defendant was involved in the mass production of homes that were placed within a commercial distribution chain, with the houses ultimately sold to consumers. Here, Defendants are not within the stream of commerce for the original product as the Court has previously ruled. After the product's reconstruction, there are no allegations—other than those that the Court ruled were legal conclusions—of any distribution chain, stream of commerce, or customers to whom this newly reconstructed product could be sold to.

This is Plaintiff's third complaint, and Plaintiff has had ample opportunity to comply with the pleading standards. Also, from the facts alleged, it does not appear that further amendments can cure the defect. Should Plaintiff, during discovery, discover facts that demonstrate Defendant was manufacturing this heater, placing it in a chain of distribution, and selling it to consumers, the Court may then consider Plaintiff's request to amend the complaint. As of now, the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.
