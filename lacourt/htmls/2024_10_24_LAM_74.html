DEPARTMENT 74 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 



 




Case Number: 19STCV30239    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: 74

The Wonderful Company LLC v. Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company, et al.

Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

BACKGROUND 

            Plaintiff The Wonderful Company LLC (“TWC”) sues Defendant Lucile Packard Children’s Hospital Stanford (“LPCH”) for alleged improper billing and pricing of medical services, and Defendant Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company (“Anthem”) for failing to question those practices in breach of its contractual and fiduciary obligations as TWC’s third-party administrator.  

The operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) asserts causes of action for (1) breach of contract (against Anthem), (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing (against Anthem), (3) breach of fiduciary duty (against Anthem), (4) intentional misrepresentation (against all defendants), (5) negligent misrepresentation (against all defendants), (6) concealment (against all defendants), (7) violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code Section 17200, et. seq. (against all defendants), and (8) negligence (against Anthem).

            On January 5, 2024, LPCH filed the instant motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication.

On August 16, 2024, TWC filed its opposition.

On August 23, 2024, LPCH filed its reply.

            The jury trial is set for November 4, 2024.  

JUDICIAL NOTICE

            No request for judicial notice has been filed.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

            The court rules on TWC’s objections to LPCH’s evidence as follows. Objection Nos. 1-37, 39-40, 42-51, 53-81, 83-89, 91, 93, and 98 are overruled. Objection Nos. 38, 41, 52, 82, 90, 92, and 94-97 are sustained.

LPCH’s Objection Nos. 1-2, 6, 7, 9-11, 13-18, and 25-26 are overruled. Objection Nos. 3, 4-5, 8, 12, 19-24, and 27-31 are sustained.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

DISCUSSION 

LPCH moves for summary judgment or summary adjudication of the SAC’s fourth (4th) cause of action for intentional misrepresentation, fifth (5th) cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, sixth (6th) cause of action for concealment, and seventh (7th) cause of action for violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code Section 17200, et. seq. (i.e.., the Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”)).

Misrepresentation

In the fourth and fifth causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation, respectively, TWC alleges that LPCH made misrepresentations regarding the medical charges of two pediatric patients, IC and JJ, [1] including the following.

(a)   Charging for ventilation management services when this service was never performed;

(b)   Charging for urinalysis testing allegedly performed by the laboratory when these services were performed bedside, and therefore never rendered by the laboratory; and

(c)   Double billing for professional services, twice a day, for 35 days, contrary to coding standards that LPCH represents it follows.

(SAC, ¶¶ 48, 53.)  

According to the evidence LPCH has produced, TWC, in its discovery responses, “identified [only] five different categories of charges that it claims LPCH unlawfully billed for services not rendered [to IC and/or JJ]. [Those categories] are: (a) charges for ventilation management services that TWC claims are not documented in the medical records and therefore were not provided; (b) charges for cross-match and typing services that TWC claims are not documented in the medical records and therefore not provided; (c) charges for critical care services under CPT Codes 99291 and 99292 for which TWC claims there is no documentation to support that the level of care billed was actually provided by LPCH; (d) charges for urinalysis testing conducted by a lab when documentation allegedly does not support that the testing was actually done in the lab; and (e) a charge for a Pediatric Intensive Care Unit (‘PICU’) Level 3 stay.” (LPCH’s compendium of evidence, filed March 14, 2024 (“LCOE”), Tab 1 – Declaration of Kristien Karmarkar (“Karmarkar Decl.”), ¶ 8 [emphasis added].)

“‘“The elements of fraud [i.e., intentional misrepresentation], which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or “scienter”); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.”’ [Citation.]” (Tenet Healthsystem Desert, Inc. v. Blue Cross of California (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 821, 837 (“Tenet”).)

 “[The tort of negligent misrepresentation is similar to fraud, except that it does not require scienter or an intent to defraud.” (Tenet, supra, 245 Cal.App.4th at p. 845.) 

A.    Falsity

To prove that there was no false representation with regard to charges associated with ventilation management services for JJ[2], LPCH submits the declaration of its Executive Director of Revenue & Billing Integrity, Kristien Karmarkar (“Karmarkar”)[3], who testifies as follows.

Karmarkar located each ventilation management charge for JJ and confirmed that medical documentation supported each charge at issue. (LCOE, Tab 1 – Karmarkar Decl., ¶¶ 10-13.) Specifically, for each ventilation management services charge, she located flowsheets, which, contained, among other things, (1) input into the electronic medical record (EMR) by a respiratory therapist each time they checked a ventilator setting or made an adjustment and, (2) a time stamp showing the time a setting was made or adjusted. (Karmarkar Decl., ¶ 11; Exhibit A [a chart Karmarkar testifies shows the date of service for each ventilation management along with flowsheets that supported each charge].)

Based on her review and approximately 18 years of experience at LPCH, Karmarkar found nothing that supported TWC’s allegations that LPCH charged for ventilation management services for JJ that were not performed. (Karmarkar Decl., ¶ 14.)

The court finds that through Karmarkar’s declaration, LPCH has met its burden of showing that it did not make false representations with regard to the ventilation management services in dispute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

However, the court also finds that TWC has met its burden of showing triable issues exist regarding whether LPCH misrepresented the ventilation management services charges. In response to a deposition question asking whether LPCH could bill for a nurse that provides ventilation management services instead of a respiratory therapist, Karmarkar answered no. (TWC’s compendium of evidence (“TCOE”), Tab B – Declaration of Ashley L. Milnes (“Milnes Decl.”), Exhibit E – Karmarkar Vol. II Depo., p. 41:10-23.) According to TWC’s expert, Georgeann Edford (“Edford”), “most of the ventilation management services were performed by nurses, and thus should have been part of the PICU room rate and not separately reimbursable.” (TCOE, Tab A – Edford Decl., ¶ 14.) LPCH has not disputed or objected to that evidence. Therefore, based on TWC’s evidence, there is a triable issue of material fact regarding whether LPCH properly billed TWC (through Anthem) for JJ’s ventilation management services. In addition, LPCH’s Vice President of Revenue Operations Shawn Tienken (“Tienken”) was asked by counsel: “Would you agree with me that if LPCH does not document a service, it cannot charge for it”?” (TCOE, Tab B, Exhibit C – Tienken Depo. Vol. II, p. 172:12-13.) Tienken answered: “That is the standard. That is my understanding, yes.” (Id. at p. 172:14-15.) LPCH has also disputed or objected to Edford’s opinion that “the flowsheets in JJ’s medical records are essentially blank for multiple dates of service, with no information nor data provided, which indicates that no services were provided, provided, which indicates that no services were provided for a multi-hour period.” (Edford Decl., ¶ 14.) In its reply, LPCH argues that the “SAC does not allege fraud based on lack of medical necessity.” (Reply, p. 3:6-7.) However, LPCH’s argument does not address the inference that can be drawn from Edford and Tienken’s testimonies (i.e., there is a triable issue regarding whether LPCH should have billed for some of JJ’s ventilation management services because some flowsheets did not contain any information and LPCH only bills those services that are supported by documentation).

The court also finds that triable issues exist with regard to charges for critical care under CPT codes 99291 and CPT code 99292. (See fn. 3 above for definition of CPT codes.) Karmarkar testifies that “CPT codes 99292 and 99291 are used to report the total duration of time spent by a physician providing critical care services to a critically ill or critically injured patient. …. Critical care, as defined by the American Medical Association (‘AMA’) is the delivery by a physician(s) or other qualified healthcare professional for medical care for a critically ill/injured patient in which there is an acute impairment of one or more vital organ systems, such that there is a probability of imminent or life—threatening deterioration of the patient’s condition.” (LCOE, Tab 1 – Karmarkar Decl., ¶ 36.) Karmarkar admits that LPCH’s documentation was not sufficient to support a critical service charge for JJ for three days, September 8 through 10, 2017. (Karmarkar Decl., ¶ 37; fn. 3.) Given Tienken’s testimony that LPCH cannot charge for a service it has not documented, there is a triable issue regarding whether there was falsity in some critical services charges.

Unlike the charges for ventilation management services and critical services, however, the court finds that TWC has not met its burden of showing triable issues exist as to charges for IC’s crossmatch and typing services. LPCH has met its initial burden of showing that there was no falsity with regard to those services through Karmarkar’s declaration, which states (among other things) that Karmarkar located the results for each electronic crossmatch, ABO/RH Typing, antibody screen, and HLA tests that TWC identified, and reviewed the supporting documentation (e.g., the results page) for each of those tests. (Karmarkar Decl., ¶¶ 19-33.) Based on her review of the records and experience at LPCH, Karmarkar did not find anything that supports TWC’s allegation that LPCH charged for crossmatch and typing services for JJ or IC when the service was not performed. (Karmarkar Decl., ¶ 4.) In opposition, TWC argues that Karmarkar’s declaration did not lay a foundation for her knowledge of the “highly technical ‘material facts’ she attempts to present.” (Opposition, p. 8:3-4.) The court disagrees. (See Footnote 3 of this Ruling for a discussion of Karmarkar’s experience.) TWC also argues that, at most, “the Motion shows testing may have occurred [citation], but not that it was medically necessary and supported by the medical documentation.” (Opposition, p. 8:5-7.) However, TWC contradicts itself because it also states that it “is not disputing the medical necessity of the services provided to IC and JJ. ” (Opposition, fn. 3.) In addition, the court has sustained LPCH’s objection to the evidence TWC relies on to argue that the crossmatch and typing services were not supported by sufficient documentation. (See Ruling on LPCH’s Evidentiary Objection No. 19 above.)

In any event, even though the court has found that there are no triable issues as to the crossmatch and typing services charges, that finding is no consequence because (1) summary adjudication is only proper if it disposes of an entire claim, not factual allegations, and, (2) here, triable issues exist regarding whether LPCH made false representations in its charges for JJ’s ventilation management and critical services. (Catalano v. Superior Court (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 91, 97 [“summary adjudication is meant to dispose of an entire substantive area”].) For the same two reasons, the court need not address at this time whether there was falsity with regard to the remaining categories of charges (i.e., urinalysis testing and PICU Level 3).

B.     Scienter or Negligence

LPCH argues that TWC cannot prove scienter or negligence because “TWC cannot establish that the representations were known by those individuals to be false at the time, or were made recklessly or negligently.” (Motion, p. 14:3-6.)

            As stated above, “[t]he tort of negligent misrepresentation does not require scienter or intent to defraud.” (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 173.) Instead, “[i]t encompasses ‘[t]he assertion, as a fact, of that which is not true, by one who has no reasonable ground for believing it to be true’ [citation], and ‘[t]he positive assertion, in a manner not warranted by the information of the person making it, of that which is not true, though he believes it to be true’  [citations].” (Id. at p. 174; Fox v. Pollack (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 954, 962 [“Negligent misrepresentation is a form of deceit, the elements of which consist of (1) a misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance thereon by the party to whom the misrepresentation was directed, and (5) damages” (emphasis added)].)

Here, TWC has produced evidence raising triable issues of material fact regarding whether LPCH had reasonable grounds for believing that all of its charges were true given that  Tienken testified that LPCH cannot charge for undocumented services and TWC’s expert has testified some charges did not have documentation. Therefore, the court finds LPCH’s “negligence” argument unpersuasive.

As for scienter, “[t]he requisite state of mind … for actual fraud or intentional misrepresentation is disbelief in the truth of the statement, i.e., knowledge of falsity.” (Anderson v. Deloitte & Touche (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1468, 1476 [italics in original].)

LPCH argues that TWC cannot establish scienter because “[t]he charges for services provided to JJ and IC were put through the normal charge and claim review process, which ran each charge through a set of rules to check for errors and required all errors to be manually reviewed and cleared before LPCH created a claim. [Citation.]” (Motion, p. 14:15-18.)

To support that argument, TWC cites Paragraph 17 of Tienken’s declaration. (Motion, p. 14:18.)

However, the court has sustained TWC’s objection to that testimony on lack of foundation, hearsay, and speculation grounds because Tienken failed to, among other things, explain how he arrived at the conclusion that all charges were put through the normal charge and claim review process. (See Ruling on TWC’s Evidentiary Objection No. 94.)

LPCH also argues that TWC cannot establish scienter because (1) charges are ultimately billed based on the documentation that LPCH’s doctors and nurses provide, and (2) those professionals did not have any incentive to make misrepresentations because they are not compensated based on the amount charged for their services. (Motion, p. 14:7-13.)

However, there is a triable issue of material fact regarding whether LPCH had knowledge of falsity because (1) Tienken testified that LPCH does not charge for services that are unsupported by documentation and (2) Edford testified some charges were unsupported by documentation yet billed to TWC.

For those reasons, the court finds LPCH’s scienter argument unpersuasive.

C.    Reliance

“Because ‘reliance is the causal mechanism of fraud’ [citation], … [a] pleading [must have] facts showing [and a plaintiff must ultimately prove] actual reliance, that is, that the plaintiff suffered economic injury as a result of his or her reliance on the truth and accuracy of the defendant’s representations.” (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 228 [emphasis added] (“Chapman”).) “[A]ctual reliance, or causation, [can] inferred from the misrepresentation of a material fact.” (Id. at p. 229.)

Here, the court finds triable issues exist as to actual reliance because, (1) as explained above, TWC has met its burden of showing that there is a question of fact regarding whether LPCH misrepresented material facts (i.e., the charges in dispute), and (2) LPCH has not presented evidence disproving the allegation that TWC suffered economic injury as a result of the alleged misrepresentations.

“‘“Besides actual reliance, [a] plaintiff must also show ‘justifiable’ reliance, i.e., circumstances were such to make it reasonable for [the] plaintiff to accept [the] defendant’s statements without an independent inquiry or investigation.” [Citation.] The reasonableness of the plaintiff’s reliance is judged by reference to the plaintiff’s knowledge and experience. [Citation.] “‘Except in the rare case where the undisputed facts leave no room for a reasonable difference of opinion, the question of whether a plaintiff’s reliance is reasonable is a question of fact.’ [Citations.]” [Citation.]’ [Citation.]” (Public Employees’ Retirement System v. Moody’s Investors Service, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 643, 672 [original italics removed; emphasis added].)

Here, LPCH suggests that TWC cannot establish justifiable reliance because the defendant “did not even know that TWC was the party ultimately responsible for payment. [Citation.] As far as LPCH knew, its bills were going to Anthem as an insurer responsible for covering the cost of JJ’s and IC’s treatment, not as a [third-party administrator] of any employer’s self-funded plan.” (Motion, p. 15:22-18.)

However, as TWC alludes to in its opposition, “[t]he maker of a fraudulent misrepresentation is subject to liability for pecuniary loss to another who acts in justifiable reliance upon it if the misrepresentation, although not made directly to the other, is made to a third person and the maker intends or has reason to expect that its terms will be repeated or its substance communicated to the other, and that it will influence his conduct in the transaction or type of transaction involved.’ [Citation.]” (Geernaert v. Mitchell (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 601, 605–606, quoting Restatement Second of Torts section 533 [italics in original].)

Here, the court finds that TWC has met its burden of showing that triable issues exist regarding whether LPCH had reason to expect that the charges would be communicated to TWC. According to TWC’s evidence, Anthem and LPCH entered into a Centers of Medical Excellence Agreement (the “CME Agreement”). (TCOE, Tab B – Milnes Decl., ¶ 2; Exhibit A – a copy of the agreement.) The agreement defines “‘Other Payors’ …. [to include] …. self-administered or self-insured programs providing Health Benefit Plans, or employers or insurers” (Id. at p. 3), and states that “Anthem shall use its best efforts to assure Other Payors compensate Facility and Medical Group in accordance with the terms and conditions of this Agreement.” (CME Agreement, p. 10, Section 2.21.) TWC argues that since it falls within the definition of “Other Payors,” a triable issue exists regarding whether LPCH had reason to know that TWC would rely on the charges. In its reply, LPCH argues that Section 2.21 only applies to “non-Anthem entities that, pursuant to ‘arrangements’ with Anthem, make direct payments to LPCH,” and that “TWC had no such ‘arrangement’ and made no payments directly to LPCH.” (Reply, pp. 8:24-9:2.) However, the court has not found that restriction in the language of Section 2.21, and LPCH has not provided any evidence to support its interpretation. (See Brown v. Goldstein (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 418, 432–433 [“‘The interpretation of a contract involves “a two-step process: First the court provisionally receives (without actually admitting) all credible evidence concerning the parties” intentions to determine ‘ambiguity,’ i.e., whether the language is ‘reasonably susceptible’ to the interpretation urged by a party. If in light of the extrinsic evidence the court decides the language is ‘reasonably susceptible’ to the interpretation urged, the extrinsic evidence is then admitted to aid in the second step – interpreting the contract. [Citation.]” [Citation.]’ [Citations]”].) Therefore, the court finds there is a question of fact regarding whether LPCH had reason to know that TWC would rely on the charges.

LPCH argues that TWC could not have relied on the billing information that Anthem conveyed because TWC had no choice but to reimburse Anthem for those charges. (Motion, p. 15:22-24.) However, that argument does not disprove reliance or TWC’s allegation that it ended up reimbursing Anthem for charges that were never supposed to be asserted by LPCH in the first place.

LPCH also argues that TWC cannot establish reliance because Anthem was not TWC’s agent. The court finds LPCH’s agency arguments unpersuasive in light of the Restatement Second of Torts section 533 (mentioned above).

For those reasons, summary adjudication of the fourth and fifth causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation, respectively, is denied.

Concealment

“[T]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are:(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

“[T]here are two causation elements in a fraud cause of action. First, the plaintiff’s actual and justifiable reliance on the defendant’s misrepresentation [e.g., concealment] must have caused him to take a detrimental course of action. Second, the detrimental action taken by the plaintiff must have caused his alleged damage.” (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1062; CACI No. 1901 – Concealment.)

Here, TWC’s concealment claim alleges that (1) LPCH had a duty to disclose material facts regarding its CDM[4], (2) LPCH breached that duty by failing to disclose (among other things) that it had increased its CDM by more than 7% year over year, and (3) as a result of that concealment, TWC “was forced to pay LPCH’s inflated rates.” (SAC, ¶¶ 58-67.)

The court finds that LPCH has met its burden of establishing that the reliance element of TWC’s concealment claim cannot be established, and TWC has failed to meet its burden of showing a triable issue of material fact exists as to that issue for the following reasons. It is undisputed that “[r]ather than paying the CDM rates for JJ’s and IC’s claims, TWC paid the discounted rates negotiated by Anthem.” (UMF ¶ 103.) Therefore, TWC could not have relied on the CDM rates to pay the alleged “inflated rates” in the CDM. TWC argues in its opposition that that undisputed fact is “immaterial.” (Opposition, p. 18:7-9.) The court disagrees in light of the law cited above and the allegations in the SAC.

            For those reasons, summary adjudication of the sixth cause of action for concealment is granted.

Violation of the UCL

“‘To bring a UCL claim, a plaintiff must show either an (1) “unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice,” or (2) “unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising.”’ [Citation.]” (Adhav v. Midway Rent A Car, Inc. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 954, 970.)

Here, LPCH argues in its moving papers that summary adjudication of the UCL claim is proper because the “claim covers exactly the same ground as the claims for intentional and negligent misrepresentation, and should be rejected for the same reasons [as those other claims].” (Motion, p. 20:23-24.)

However, the court has found that triable issues exist as to the misrepresentation claims.

Therefore, summary adjudication of the seventh cause of action for violation of UCL is denied.

 

CONCLUSION 

The request for summary adjudication of the sixth cause of action for concealment is granted. The request for summary judgment and summary adjudication of all other claims is denied.

BACKGROUND 

             Plaintiff The Wonderful Company (TWC) filed a Complaint against defendants Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company (Anthem) and Lucile Packard Children’s Hospital Stanford (LPCH).  TWC contracted with Anthem to provide its employees with access to Anthem’s provider network and negotiated rates.  Two children of TWC employees received medical care at LPCH and, upon receiving the bill, TWC had an outside auditor review the charges and alleged overbilling.  TWC sued Anthem and LPCH for improper billing and a violation of contractual and fiduciary obligations.

            TWC filed the Second Amended Complaint and asserted eight causes of action against Anthem: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty: (4) Intentional Misrepresentation; (5) Negligent Misrepresentation; (6) Concealment; (7) Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code section 17200, et seq.; and (8) Negligence. On July 3, 2024, Anthem filed its motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.

LEGAL STANDARD

¿¿            [A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. “” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

           

DISCUSSION 

Breach of Contract

The elements of Breach of Contract are (a) existence of a contract; (b) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (c) defendant’s breach (or anticipatory breach); and (d) resulting damage.  (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N. Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) The parties only dispute whether Anthem breached its contractual obligations.

TWC alleges that Anthem breached their contract by:

a)      Failing to properly process and adjudicate the claims

b)      Failing to notify TWC under Anthem’s notification and payment policies

c)      Failing to prevent the drastic increases in LPCH’s chargemaster

d)      Failing to notify Anthem of LPCH’s “excessive, outlier” contract rates.

(SAC, ¶¶ 30, 31.)

According to the evidence Anthem has produced, Anthem was not required to prevent increases in the LPCH’s chargemaster and had no obligation to notify Anthem of its contract rates from confidential contracts.  (Braje Decl., Ex. “K”, Howe Dep. 354:10-23, 440:21-441:24.; Braje Decl., Ex. “W”, Rutledge Dep. 168:4-13, 200:25-201:5, 202:22-204:18; Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”.)  Anthem also produced evidence that it followed its own procedures with respect to processing, notification and investigating and reviewing the claims.  (Walsh Decl., ¶ 18-31; Rodriquez Decl. ¶¶ 27-35, Ex. “E”.) 

To prove there was no breach of contract when Anthem declined to provide the CME Agreement between Anthem and LPCH, Anthem submits the depositions of Howe and Rutledge who both testify that Anthem considers its contracts with providers to be “proprietary” and that TWC’s contract with Anthem did not allow TWC to see the underlying contracts with providers.  It also submits the Administrative Services Agreement, which protects provider agreements as proprietary. The Court finds that through the Howe and Rutledge depositions, Anthem has met its initial burden of showing that it was not contractually required to provide TWC with the CME Agreement.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).)

To prove there was no breach of contract because Anthem followed its notification and adjudication policies, Anthem submits the declaration of its Director of Health Benefits Organization Claims Operations for the Central and West Markets, Bridget Walsh (Walsh), who testified Anthem had a multistep auditing process, which included reviewing claims for coding and charges and the accuracy of the claim based upon any applicable contract.  (Walsh Decl., ¶ 11-16.)  Anthem then issued multiple high dollar notifications for the claims to TWC to begin the audit process.  (Walsh Decl., ¶ 22-40.)  Anthem completed its audit process, which included calculating the case rate for the treatment, the stoploss payment, the outlier rate, total allowance and the deduction for prior claim payments.  (Walsh Decl., ¶ 29.) Anthem meets its initial burden of showing that it met its obligation of notification to TWC. 

Anthem also submitted the Karmarkar declaration that states that $7,700 of overcharge from incorrect code billing occurred but fails to meet its burden showing that it reviewed the coding charges and accuracy of the claims based on the CME Agreement. (Braje Decl., Ex. “X”, Karmarkar Decl. ¶¶ 37, 38, 44.) Thus, triable issues exist regarding whether Anthem properly adjudicated the claims for coding and accuracy under its policies. Summary adjudication is proper only if it disposes of an entire claim.  (Catalano v. Superior Court (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 91, 97 [“summary adjudication is meant to dispose of an entire substantive area”].) Anthem has not done so. Thus the court denies summary adjudication of breach of contract.

 

Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

In the second cause of action, TWC alleges that Anthem breached the duty of good faith and fair dealing by:

a)      Entering into an agreement with LPCH which unjustly enriched LPCH;

b)      Allowing LPCH to overcharge patients and employees;

c)      Failing to notify TWC of high dollar claims;

d)      Misrepresenting that claims were “clean and payable”; and

e)      Misleading TWC that Anthem attempted to secure a discount from LPCH

(SAC, ¶¶ 34-38.)

            According to the evidence Anthem produced, Anthem retained complete control over their contracts with providers, including the charge agreements.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, ASA Art. 2 § s.)  Anthem also presents evidence, as discussed above, that it followed the procedures for adjudication of claims, and therefore, did not misrepresent the “clean and payable” nature of the claims. 

The elements for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing are: (a) the existence of a contractual relationship; (b) implied duty; (c) breach; and (d) causation of damages.  (E.g., Smith v. San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49. See also Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.)  Parties only dispute if Anthem’s actions constituted a breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

A.     Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

To prove there was no breach with respect to the contract and charges, Anthem submits the ASA, which includes a provision that allows Anthem to build and maintain their provider network as it wishes.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, ASA Art. 2 § s.)  The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing does not extend to create obligations beyond what was contemplated by the contract.  (Pasadena Live v. City of Pasadena (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1089, 1094.) 

The Court finds that through the ASA, Anthem has met its burden of showing that it did not breach the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing regarding the contract provisions with LPCH. 

To prove there was no breach with respect to the failure to notify and the representations regarding the billing statements from LPCH, Anthem submits the same evidence as discussed above.  Similarly to the above discussion, Anthem submits evidence that the claims went through their adjudication procedures and that there were several later-identified coding errors.

Anthem also presents evidence through the ASA that the billing mistakes were within the margin of error covered in the quality assurance guarantee and, therefore, cannot be a violation of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, Schedule C.) 

Anthem is incorrect in their assessment of the ASA’s quality assurance provision.  The ASA’s quality assurance provision provides that if the audits that Anthem performs fail to comply with the Performance Guarantees (97% accurate) Anthem would be required to pay a penalty.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A.”)  A penalty requirement does not prohibit TWC from seeking legal relief for inaccurate audits.  It also does not protect Anthem from a breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Anthem does not present any additional evidence that their auditing process successfully audited the LPCH bills. 

The Court finds that Anthem has not met its burden of showing that it did not violate the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing when is assured TWC that the billing was free of errors because of its adjudication process. Accordingly, the court denies summary adjudication. 

 

Breach of Fiduciary Duty

In the third cause of action, TWC alleges that Anthem owed a fiduciary duty because Anthem was the only party with access with sufficient information and expertise required to review the reasonableness of charges.  TWC alleges that Anthem breached that duty by:

a)      Entering into an agreement which unjustly enriched LPCH;

b)      Approving inappropriate invoices from LPCH;

c)      Overcharging TWC enrollees; and

d)      Threatening the loss of TWC’s access to Anthem’s insurance network unless TWC paid the invoices.

(SAC, ¶¶ 41-44.)

According to the evidence Anthem produced, Anthem did not owe TWC a fiduciary duty because both companies were sophisticated parties whose contract did include a fiduciary duty provision.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, Art. 27 § a; Rodriquez Decl., ¶¶ 11-13; Braje Decl., “F”, Resnick Dep. 24:9-25:13, 28:68.) 

The elements of a breach of fiduciary duty are (a) the existence of a fiduciary duty, (b) breach of that duty, and (c) damages caused by the breach.  (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.)  Anthem only disputes whether a fiduciary duty exists between the parties. 

A.     Fiduciary Duty

A fiduciary duty exists when either a party (1) knowingly undertakes a fiduciary duty or (2) enters into a relationship which imposes a duty as a matter of law.  (City of Hope Nat’l Med. Ctr. V. Genentech, Inc. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386.) 

A fiduciary duty may arise under common law when a party voluntarily accepts or assumes the confidence of the other party.  (Hasso v. Hapke (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 107, 140.)

A contract does not necessarily create a fiduciary duty, even if the parties have unequal bargaining power or asymmetrical information.  (City of Hope 43 Cal.4th at 387-88.) The existence of a fiduciary duty is typically a question of law.  (Marzec v. Pub. Emps’ Ret. Sys. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 889, 915.)  But “it is a question of fact whether one is… a party to a confidential relationship that gives rise to a fiduciary duty under common law.  (Hasso 227 Cal.App.4th at 140.)

To prove that there was no fiduciary duty imposed on Anthem under the ASA, Anthem submits several sections of the ASA which includes the following provisions.

The ASA allows TWC to access Anthem’s contractual relationships and negotiated rates.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, ASA Art. 2 § s; Braje Decl., Ex. “D”, Rutledge Dep. At 131:13-132:2.)  Anthem is “strictly” an independent contractor who administers certain aspects of TWC’s health plans, including the negotiation of all communications with network providers (“responding to Provider inquiries, negotiating rates with Providers or auditing Providers”), and the adjudication of appropriateness of the claims.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, ASA Art. 2 § s.)  Finally, Anthem’s contracts with providers, which are used to adjudicate the claims, constitute a “nonpublic trade secret” and is not available to TWC.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, ASA p. 2, Art. 2 § s.)

The Court finds that Anthem has not met its burden of showing that, as a matter of law, it did not owe a fiduciary duty to TWC.  The Court also finds that Anthem has not met its burden of showing that, as a matter of fact, it did not owe a fiduciary duty to TWC given it took sole responsibility for negotiating contracts with providers, which TWC did not have access to, sole responsibility for communicating with providers, and their complete control over the adjudication process. 

For these reasons, summary adjudication of the third cause of action is denied.

 

Misrepresentation

In the fourth and fifth causes of action, TWC alleges that Anthem made misrepresentations regarding appropriateness of the medical charges of the pediatric patients, I.C. and J.J..  (SAC, ¶ 49.)

            According to the evidence Anthem has produced, TWC never relied on the billing statements produced by Anthem.  (Rodriguez Decl., Exs. “E”, “F”, “G”; Rodriguez Decl., ¶¶ 36-41; Braje Decl., Ex. “B”, Rodriguez Dep. 60:4-21; Braje Decl., Ex. “D”, Rutledge Dep. 285:1-4; Braje Decl., Howe Dep. 226:20-24; Braje Decl., Calderone Dep. 112:3-21.)  Additionally, Anthem argues that none of the alleged misrepresentations were material, and Anthem did not have the required scienter.

“‘The elements of fraud [i.e., intentional misrepresentation], which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or “scienter”); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ [Citation.]” (Tenet Healthsystem Desert, Inc. v. Blue Cross of California (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 821, 837 (“Tenet”).) 

 “[The tort of negligent misrepresentation is similar to fraud, except that it does not require scienter or an intent to defraud.” (Tenet, supra, 245 Cal.App.4th at p. 845.)   

A.     Misrepresentation

A misrepresentation is “material” if a “reasonable man would attach importance to its existence or non-existence in determining his choice of action.  (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 977.)

Anthem identifies four misrepresentations: (a) transplant type, (b) LPCH’s discount, (c) Anthem’s review of the claims, and (d) LPCH’s services and Anthem’s payment policies.  TWC only identified the last two as material misrepresentations in the operative complaint.  (SAC, ¶ 49.)

To prove that there were no false representations regarding Anthem’s review of the claims, Anthem presents the same evidence as above: (1) the claims went through Anthem’s audit process and (2) the process was more than “rechecking the math”.  (Walsh Decl., ¶ 18-31; Rodriquez Decl. ¶¶ 27-35, Ex. “E.”)  Anthem also included the evidence that LPCH identified coding issues.  (Braje Decl., Ex. “X”, Karmarkar Decl. ¶¶ 37, 38, 44.)  Anthem also presents the quality assurance provision as evidence that Anthem does not guarantee that all claims are 100% accurate.  (Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”, Schedule C.)

As discussed above, Anthem is not alleging that the misrepresentation was that the claims went through the process, but instead that the process was not as thorough as Anthem represented, and that Anthem repeatedly assured TWC that the claims were correct despite the coding mistakes that LPCH and Anthem include in their evidence.  Similarly as discussed above, Anthem provides the quality assurance guarantee, which imposes a penalty against Anthem if their adjudications are less than 97% accurate. 

            The Court finds that Anthem meets its initial burden of showing that it did not represent the claims as 100% accurate; instead, it represented that the claims had been through their adjudication procedure.  (¿¿Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2)¿¿.) 

            However, the Court also finds that Anthem did not meet its burden of showing that the claims had been properly adjudicated in the procedure.  Regardless of whether Anthem only guarantees that all claims over a specific time period are 97% accurate, Anthem also guarantees that their adjudication process evaluates claims for deficiencies.

            Scienter

Anthem argues that TWC cannot prove scienter or negligence because TWC cannot prove that Anthem had knowledge of every single line item in a 100+ day stay.  (Motion, p. 16.)

As stated above, “[t]he tort of negligent misrepresentation does not require scienter or intent to defraud.” (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 173.) Instead, “[i]t encompasses ‘[t]he assertion, as a fact, of that which is not true, by one who has no reasonable ground for believing it to be true’ [citation], and ‘[t]he positive assertion, in a manner not warranted by the information of the person making it, of that which is not true, though he believes it to be true’  [citations].” (Id. at p. 174; Fox v. Pollack (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 954, 962 [“Negligent misrepresentation is a form of deceit, the elements of which consist of (1) a misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance thereon by the party to whom the misrepresentation was directed, and (5) damages” (emphasis added)].) 

Anthem’s main contention is that TWC does not provide proof that Anthem was aware of any inaccuracies in the billing statements.  (Motion, p. 16.) 

Anthem produced evidence that the claims went through a thorough and multistep review by specially trained auditors. (Walsh Decl. ¶¶ 2, 27-28, 39.)  After the review process, Anthem assumes that every line item is correct.  (Miles Decl., Ex. “F”, Walsh Depo. 45:6-12.)  A reasonable company that contracts to review the entirety of claims would follow its review processes and procedures, including reviewing line items and reviewing for coding errors and application of the appropriate contract billing amounts.  Anthem does not provide evidence or legal authority establishing that a reasonable corporation would not review each line item as a claim as outlined by their own policies.

To support the argument that Anthem had the appropriate scienter for intentional misrepresentation, TWC presents evidence that Anthem did not check to see if the billing was properly adjudicated based on the CME and that TWC alerted Anthem to issues with the claim after their third-party audit revealed several compliance and coding issues.  (Milnes Decl., Ex. “F”, Walsh Depo. 104:12-105:4; Milnes Decl., Ex. “H”, CERiS Audit Report.)

Therefore, Anthem’s argument that it was unaware of the accuracy of the line items goes directly against its claim that the review process was thorough, and the third-party audit sent to it by TWC before TWC paid Anthem for the claims.

For those reasons, the Court finds Anthem’s scienter argument unpersuasive.

B.     TWC’s Reliance on Anthem’s Representations

Anthem argues that TWC cannot prove reliance because “TWC admits that it never believed the bills that Anthem sent were true or correct.”  (Motion, p. 11.) 

As stated above, both Negligent and Intention Misrepresentation require that the injured party actually relied on the misrepresentation.

Anthem presents evidence of email communications with TWC indicating that TWC was skeptical of the total bills and requested additional information about the claim and charges from the day it received the bill.  (Rodriguez Decl., Exs. “E”, “F.”)

            The Court finds that Anthem meets its initial burden of showing that TWC questioned the validity of the billing from the moment it reviewed the claims.

            However, TWC submits evidence that TWC relied on Anthem’s representations that the claims were properly adjudicated despite their concerns when they reimbursed Anthem for the claims.  (Milnes Decl., Ex. “F”, Walsh Depo. Vo. I 46:2-7; Milnes Decl., Ex. “L”, Walsh Depo. Vo. II 151:7-153:17; Milnes Decl., Ex. “C”, Tienken Depo. II at 174:8-13, 223:21-224:14; Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “F”; Milnes Decl., Exs. “R” and “S”; Milnes Decl., Ex. “T”, Rutledge Depo. Vol. II 329:19-330:10; Miles Decl., Ex. “U”, Howe Depo. Vol. I 225:8-226”4.)

            For those reasons, the Court finds Anthem’s reliance argument unpersuasive. 

 

Concealment

            In the sixth cause of action, TWC alleges that (1) Anthem failed to disclose facts related to its investigation that it could have never discovered itself, (2) Anthem knew about the claims for over a year and failed to notify TWC, and (3) Anthem concealed information regarding LPCH’s pricing, including the following:

a)      The above market contract prices with LPCH; and

b)      The increase in LPCH’s chargemaster above 7%.

(SAC, ¶¶ 58-61.)

            The elements of concealment are largely the same as intentional misrepresentation: (1) concealment, (2) a duty to disclose, (3) intentional concealment or suppression of the fact with intent to defraud, (4) plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would have changed its course of action if it had known, and (5) resulting damage.  (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.)

            Anthem’s argument against concealment is derivative of its argument against misrepresentation.  Anthem argues that either it had no duty to disclose or that the concealments were immaterial.

            Anthem presents evidence that the ASA protects the CME Agreement with LPCH from disclosure and does not require Anthem to disclose any additional information about the changes in LPCH’s chargemaster, which was publicly available.  (Braje Decl., Ex. “K”, Howe Dep. 354:10-23, 440:21-441:24.; Braje Decl., Ex. “W”, Rutledge Dep. 168:4-13, 200:25-201:5, 202:22-204:18; Rodriguez Decl., Ex. “A”; Braje Decl. Ex. “X”, Karmarkar Decl. ¶ 50.)  Anthem also presents evidence that it followed its policies regarding notification of the high dollar claims.  (Walsh Decl., ¶ 18-31; Rodriquez Decl. ¶¶ 27-35, Ex. “E”.)

            The Court finds that Anthem has met its burden of showing that the duty to disclose element of TWC’s concealment claim cannot be established, and TWC has failed to meet its burden of showing a triable issue of material fact exists.  TWC alleges that Anthem failed to reveal that it had not conducted a “thorough executive level review” and that it did not disallow a single claim.  (Opp., p. 13.)  But does not cite to authority or a portion of the ASA that establishes that Anthem had a duty to disclose this information. 

            Therefore, summary adjudication of the seventh cause of action for concealment is granted.

 

Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code section 17200, et seq.

            The Unlawful Business Practices cause of action covers both prongs of the Unfair Business Practices statute.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.)  The Fraud Prong requires (1) defendant’s affirmative misrepresentation, conduct or business practice, or omission in violation of a duty of defendant to disclose, that (2) is likely to deceive members of the public.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.)

            The Unfairness Prong requires (1) a business practice; (2) that is unfair, lawful or fraudulent; and (3) an authorized remedy.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.)

            Anthem’s sole argument as to the Unlawful Business Practices cause of action is that it should be adjudicated as the fraud claims.  (Motion, p. 20.) 

Given the Court’s finding that triable issues remain as to the misrepresentation claims, the Court denies summary adjudication of the seventh cause of action for the violation of the Business & Profession Code section 17200.

 

Negligence

            The elements of a Negligence cause of action are (1) legal duty owed to the Plaintiff; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff.  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)

            Anthem does not present any specific facts or argument regarding the negligence claim in the Moving Papers.

            Therefore, the court denies summary adjudication as to the eighth cause of action for negligence. 

 

CONCLUSION 

The court grants summary adjudication of the seventh cause of action for concealment and denies summary adjudication of the other causes of action.

The court denies Anthem’s motion for summary judgment.

Defendant shall give notice.

CONCLUSION
