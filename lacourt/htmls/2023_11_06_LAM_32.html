DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV04529    Hearing Date: November 6, 2023    Dept: 32

 

CRAIG SALLIN, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

JOE SAMUEL BAILEY, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  20STCV04529

  Hearing Date:  November 6, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiffs’ motion for summary judgment or adjudication

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On February 4, 2020, Plaintiffs Craig Sallin, Croesus Investments, LLC, Linda Snyder-Kelbaugh, Sun Coast Distributing, Inc., Sun Pacific Group, Inc., and Jerry James filed this action for breach of contract against Defendants Joe Samuel Bailey and Renew Spinal Care, Inc. The operative First Amended Complaint was filed on March 27, 2020.   

            The FAC asserts a single cause of action for breach of written settlement agreement. The FAC alleges that Plaintiffs invested money to Defendants and that the parties reached a settlement resolving disputes over the amount owed. This action stems from Defendants’ alleged failure to pay the amounts required by the settlement agreement.  

            On July 28, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion for summary judgment or adjudication in the alternative. Defendants filed their opposition on October 23, 2023. Plaintiffs filed their reply on November 1, 2023.

 

 

 LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the plaintiff moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to establish each element of the cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Once the plaintiff has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Defendants’ Objections to Plaintiffs’ Evidence

  • The Court does not consider Defendants’ objections because they fail to “[q]uote or set forth the objectionable statement or material” as required by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1354(b)(3).

Plaintiffs’ Objections to Defendants’ Evidence:

  • The following objections are overruled: 1-15, 28, 31, 33, 34.
  • The remaining objections are not pertinent to the Court’s ruling on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(q).)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiffs seek summary judgment on their breach of contract claim, as well as summary adjudication of Defendants’ usury defense.

I. Breach of Contract

            To establish breach of contract, a plaintiff must show: (1) the contract existed, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)

            a. Terms of the Agreement

            Here, the parties entered into a Settlement Agreement on July 24, 2019. (Plntf.’s Undisputed Facts (UF) 10.) The Agreement calls for Defendants to pay nine investors a total principal of $6 million as full and final satisfaction of all claims between the investors and Defendants. (UF 12.) Plaintiffs are six of the nine total investors and collectively hold a 79% interest in the obligations owed by Defendants under the Agreement. (UF 15.) The unpaid principal balance and any accrued interest will become due and payable in full on July 24, 2024, five years from the date of execution. (UF 12.) The Agreement lists an interest rate of 10% per annum. (Ibid.) The Agreement calls for Defendants to pay the investors the first $1 million received from the final judgment in the case Bailey v. St. Louis by September 30, 2019. (Ibid.) In July 2019, the Agreement was amended to make the $1 million due by February 29, 2020. (UF 13.) Defendants are also required to pay 25% of all collections from Bailey v. St. Louis. (UF 12.) Additionally, Defendants are required to pay the investors $1,000 with respect to each surgery performed at Defendants’ medical clinics. (Ibid.)  

            b. Defendants’ Purported Breach

            Plaintiffs contend that Defendants breached the Agreement by not making any payments to date, despite receiving $12 million from the Bailey v. St. Louis case and performing 624 surgeries. (UF 14, 16, 23.) Based on their 79% interest, Plaintiffs contend they are entitled to $2.3 million from Bailey v. St. Louis and $492,960 from the surgeries. (Ibid.)

                        1. Collections from Bailey v. St. Louis         

            Plaintiffs’ only evidence of any recovery or collection from Bailey v. St. Louis is the investors’ recollection of a purported phone call where Bailey supposedly admitted to receiving $12 million from the case. (See UF 16.) However, Bailey denies making such a call and denies receiving any money from Bailey v. St. Louis. (Bailey Decl. ¶ 51.) Therefore, there is a triable issue as to whether Bailey has actually collected any funds from Bailey v. St. Louis. As a result, it cannot be determined as a matter of law that Defendants breached the Agreement by failing to pay the $1 million paydown or 25% of collections, or failing to report “significant developments” in the Bailey v. St. Louis case.

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendants at least breached the obligation to report “significant developments” in regards to collection efforts in the Bailey v. St. Louis case. But again, Plaintiffs have no evidence of any significant developments beyond the purported receipt of $12 million, which is disputed. It is also a factual issue what is considered a “significant development.”

                       

2. Surgery Profits

            Plaintiffs’ basis for claiming $1,000 per surgery comes from Section 2(d)(i) of the Agreement. (UF 18.) This section provides that “Sam [Bailey] shall cause Newco to pay the lender-investors $l,000 for each surgery performed by Newco. The payments shall be made quarterly with respect to those surgeries on which Newco has received payment. It is contemplated that the $1,000 per surgery shall come from Sam’s interest in the surgical profits generated by Newco.” (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, § 2(d)(i).) According to the Agreement, Newco is a company that Bailey intended to form as a successor to Renew Spinal Care, Inc. to provide minimally invasive spinal surgery. (Id., § 2(d).)  

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendants have failed to pay the investors for 624 surgeries, resulting in $492,960 owed to Plaintiffs. (UF 23.) Plaintiffs arrived at this number by estimating that Defendants performed 13 surgeries per month on average based on 113 surgeries performed between September 2020 and June 2021. (UF 21.) Using the average of 13 surgeries per month, Plaintiffs estimate that Defendants performed 624 surgeries over the four years since the execution of the Settlement Agreement. (UF 23.)

            However, the Agreement requires payment for “surgeries on which Newco has received payment.” (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, § 2(d)(i).) This means surgeries that were actually performed and paid for. The Agreement does not contemplate payments based on an estimate of surgeries. It cannot be determined as a matter of law that Defendants owe $492,960 for 624 surgeries because Plaintiffs have no evidence that Defendants actually performed this number of surgeries.

Furthermore, the Agreement requires payment for “each surgery performed by Newco.” (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, § 2(d)(i).) Plaintiffs’ evidence references surgeries performed by unspecified “Defendants’ Medical Clinics.” (See UF 21.) Plaintiffs bear the initial burden on summary judgment, and they have not satisfied their burden of proving that Newco performed surgeries so as to trigger the payment provision. Plaintiffs introduce reply evidence of Bailey’s interrogatory responses supposedly admitting that a company he formed, Sonospine Surgery, is Renew’s successor and therefore considered “Newco” under the Agreement. This evidence was not filed with Plaintiffs’ moving papers and will not be considered. (See Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537 [“new evidence is not permitted with reply papers. This principle is most prominent in the context of summary judgment motions”].)      

Because of the factual disputes discussed above, Plaintiffs have not established a breach of contract as a matter of law. Therefore, the Court is precluded from granting summary judgment for Plaintiffs.  

II. Usury

The California Constitution limits the interest rate on a loan or forbearance to the higher of 10% per annum or 5% per annum plus a certain prevailing rate. (Cal. Const., Art. XV § 1.) “The essential elements of usury are: (1) The transaction must be a loan or forbearance; (2) the interest to be paid must exceed the statutory maximum; (3) the loan and interest must be absolutely repayable by the borrower; and (4) the lender must have a willful intent to enter into a usurious transaction.” (Ghirardo v. Antonioli (1994) 8 Cal.4th 791, 798.)

a. Waiver

            1. Applicable Law

Plaintiffs argue that Defendants have waived the usury defense because the Settlement Agreement contains a mutual release and wavier. (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, §§ 4, 5.) Plaintiffs rely on Credit Finance Corp. v. Mox (1932) 125 Cal.App. 583, 586, which held that “in the absence of a showing of fraud or undue influence, a compromise agreement, like a judgment, is decisive of the rights of the parties thereto, and ‘[e]ven though there had been usury in the original transaction, it would seem that upon a settlement upon which each of the parties released the others from all claims and liabilities of every character then existing, the right to rely upon a previous usurious transaction would cease to exist.’”  

However, a release provision does not constitute a waiver of usury claims where “exempting [a party] from the consequences of usury would violate public policy.” (Hardwick v. Wilcox (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 975, 988.) A contract violates public policy when its object is to exempt anyone from liability for a violation of law. (Id. at p. 989, citing Civ. Code, § 1668.) Therefore, if an agreement is “an extension of the underlying usurious loan transaction,” construing a release provision as a waiver of usury would wrongly allow a party to “benefit from his illegal contract and retain the usurious interest he extracted.” (Ibid.) This was not the case in Mox because “[i]n Mox, the parties had a bona fide dispute as to whether the loans in question were usurious, and resolved that dispute in a settlement agreement.” (Hardwick, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th at p. 990.)

In sum, Mox precludes a party from arguing that a settlement agreement is tainted by an underlying usurious transaction if the very purpose of the settlement agreement is to resolve the dispute over usury and remove the taint. However, Mox does not stand for the proposition that a usury claim is waived in every case involving a settlement agreement with a release provision. Instead, under Hardwick, a settlement agreement cannot waive usury if it is an extension of a usurious transaction or “itself violate[s] the usury law.” (Hardwick, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th at p. 990.)

            2. Application to this Case

In this case, the parties did not have a bona fide dispute over usury, and the Settlement Agreement was not a resolution of a preexisting usury dispute. Instead, the facts suggest that the Agreement “itself violated the usury law.” (See Hardwick, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th at p. 990.) Defendants point out that the total principal amount loaned by the Plaintiffs in 2014 and 2015 was $930,000, which turned into $4.7 million owed by July 2019, the date of the settlement agreement. Defendants contend that the Settlement Agreement extended the due dates for the loans in exchange for fixing $3,810,000 in usurious interest. Based on the amounts stated in the complaint and attached settlement agreement, Defendants calculate that each Plaintiff is seeking to recover an interest rate between 16.57% and 197.29% for the various loans that they made to Defendants.

A reasonable trier of fact may conclude that the Agreement created usury rather than resolving an existing dispute over usury. Therefore, there is a triable issue over whether Defendants waived the usury defense.  

b. Whether the Underlying Transaction Consisted of Loans

Plaintiffs argue that the underlying transactions were investments, not loans. Plaintiffs contend that between 2014 and 2016, the nine investors “invested in a medical receivables financing program offered by Defendants.” (UF 1.) Plaintiffs claim that “Defendants solicited the investments and represented to Plaintiffs that the receivables were in the process of being paid by various insurance companies. Pursuant to Pledge Agreements signed by Defendants, the INVESTORS were to be repaid from the payments Defendants received with respect to said receivables.” (UF 3.)

On the other hand, Defendants argue that the investors were never given any membership interest in Renew and were not entitled to any profits earned by Renew. (Def.’s Resp. to UF 3.) Instead, under the pledge agreements, “each plaintiff received a fixed payment within a fixed amount of time.” (Ibid.; Bailey Decl., Ex. 1.) The pledge agreements also provided for conditions of default on repayment. (Ibid.) A reasonable trier of fact may interpret these as loans rather than investments. Furthermore, the Settlement Agreement states that “[t]he Lender-Investors made the following loans/investments in the form of a series of loans to Sam or Renew or both.” (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, § A.) The recital proceeds to list the “loans” made by each of the nine investors. (Ibid.) Therefore, there is a triable issue over whether Plaintiffs made loans subject to usury law.     

 

 

c. Converting a Non-Usurious Transaction into a Usurious One

Plaintiffs argue that “[a] modification of a nonusurious transaction (e.g., a credit sale) is not subject to usury even if modified to raise the interest rate to a usurious rate.” (Mtn. 13:12-14.) However, the case relied upon, Ghirardo v. Antonioli (1994) 8 Cal.4th 791, does not stand for the proposition that any non-usurious transaction may be converted to a usurious one and remain immune from usury law. First, the court in Ghirardo “beg[a]n with the important premise, which Ghirardo does not dispute, that his purchase of the property from Gay was exempt from the usury law” because the transaction did not “involve[] a loan or forbearance.” (Id. at p. 803.) By contrast, there is a dispute in this case over whether the underlying transactions were loans subject to usury law. Furthermore, Ghirardo was decided based on the credit sale exception because “the owner of property, whether real or personal, has a perfect right to name the price on which he is willing to sell, and to refuse to accede to any other. He may offer to sell at a designated price for cash or at a much higher price on credit, and a credit sale will not constitute usury however great the difference between the two prices.” (Id. at p. 804.) Plaintiffs articulate no basis to apply the credit sale exception to the transactions at issue in this case.

Similarly, in DCM Partners v. Smith (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 729, 731, “[t]he court held that the original transaction constituted a credit sale that was exempt from the usury laws, and that it retained its exempt status when the note was modified to provide for increased interest.” Again, in this case, the underlying transactions are not exempt credit sales. Neither Ghirardo nor DCM supports Plaintiffs’ suggestion that an investment can be converted to a loan that is immune from usury. (See Mtn. 14:5-7.)

 

 

 

CONCLUSION

            For the reasons articulated above, there are disputes of material fact which preclude summary judgment or adjudication of Plaintiffs’ breach of contract claim and Defendants’ usury defense. Therefore, Plaintiffs’ motion is DENIED.

JANE DOE,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

LUIS DIAZ ROJAS,

                        Defendant.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV41020

  Hearing Date:  November 6, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s motions to compel further responses to form interrogatories and requests for production   

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On December 30, 2022, Plaintiff Jane Doe filed this action against Defendant Luis Diaz Rojas stemming from an alleged sexual assault.

            On October 13, 2023, Defendant filed the instant two motions to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production. Plaintiff filed her opposition on October 24, 2023. Defendant filed his reply on October 30, 2023.  

LEGAL STANDARD

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).)

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete; (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate; (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).)  

MEET AND CONFER

            A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b).) The Court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Koorenny Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. 3, 5.)

DISCUSSION

I. Form Interrogatories

An answer to an interrogatory must be as complete and straightforward as possible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220(a).) Form Interrogatories are drafted and approved by the Judicial Council and seek basic information fundamental to routine discovery. (See Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1250.) A party “is entitled to demand answers to its interrogatories, as a matter of right . . . [and] the burden of justifying any objection and failure to respond remains at all times with the party resisting an interrogatory.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.)

            In her responses, Plaintiff asserted a variety of boilerplate objections for which no explanations are provided in her opposition. Plaintiff’s opposition argues that “a lot of defendant’s requests for discovery are irrelevant, invasive and absurd!” (Opp. 3:13-15.) Plaintiff also reiterates her objections and simply declares that they are valid. (Opp. 9:1-9.) These generic statements do not actually address the substance of any of the interrogatories and fail to demonstrate which interrogatories are irrelevant or invasive, or how. It is Plaintiff’s burden to justify her objections. (See Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 541.) Plaintiff has failed to do this. Therefore, further responses are required for the Form Interrogatories.

II. Requests for Production

            The Requests for Production seek documents relevant to Plaintiff’s claims. The requests seek the documentary basis for Plaintiff’s allegations, photos of Plaintiff and Defendant together, proof of payments from Defendant to Plaintiff, documents showing phone calls or texts between Plaintiff and Defendant, documents showing sexual intercourse between Plaintiff and Defendant, and all documents identified in Plaintiff’s responses to form interrogatories.

Defendant has established good cause through his separate statement, which also explains why Plaintiff’s responses are improper. Plaintiff has not filed a responsive separate statement, nor does Plaintiff’s opposition brief address the substance of the requests or her responses. Plaintiff’s objections are without merit. To the extent Plaintiff is unable to comply with a request, her response must abide by Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230. Therefore, further responses are required for the Requests for Production.    

CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motions to compel further responses are GRANTED. Plaintiff shall provide further responses to the subject Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production within 15 days. Sanctions are denied.
