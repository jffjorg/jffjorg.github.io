DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV02778    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Morgan Harris,

 

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Nima Fahimian, M.D. Inc., et al.

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    24VECV02778                   107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

 

Motion to Quash Subpoena Duces Tecum

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

This is a medical malpractice case. (Complaint p. 3.) On June 12, 2024, Plaintiff Morgan Harris (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Bespoke Treatment, LLC (“Bespoke Treatment”), Nima Fahimian MD Inc. (“Fahimian”), and Does 1 to 10, alleging a cause of action for general negligence.

 

On October 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion seeking an order quashing, or in the alternative, a protective order, on the subpoenas duces tecum served by Fahimian and Bespoke Treatment (the “Motion”). On November 1, 2024, Fahimian filed an opposition. On November 4, 2024, Bespoke Treatment filed an opposition. Plaintiff filed no replies.

 

II.              PROCEDURAL ISSUES

 

  1. Timing

 

Citing Code of Civil Procedure sections 1005, subdivision (b) and 1010.6, subdivision (a)(3)(B), Fahimian argues Plaintiff’s Motion is untimely because Plaintiff did not serve the Motion sixteen days before the hearing. (Fahimian Opposition pp. 7-8.) Fahimian states the hearing is set for November 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s Motion was due to be served no later than October 21, 2024, but Plaintiff served the Motion on October 23, 2024. (Fahimian Opposition pp. 7-8.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b) provides that “all moving and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least 16 court days before the hearing.”  “Any period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

 

Plaintiff’s proof of service shows Plaintiff served the notice of motion by email on October 22, 2024. October 22, 2024, is seventeen court days before the hearing.  However, “[a] trial court has broad discretion to accept or reject late-filed papers.” (Jack v. Ring LLC (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1186, 1210.) Accordingly, this court exercises its discretion and considers the Motion.

 

Also, Plaintiff filed this Motion against two Defendants, so it should have been filed as two separate motions. (See Duran v. St. Luke’s Hospital (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 457, 460 [”But while it is mandatory for the court clerks to demand and receive statutorily required filing fees, it is not… a jurisdictional defect if the precise fee is not collected.”])¿¿ According to the rules, in the future, Plaintiff is required to pay another filing fee in this scenario.

 

  1. Meet and Confer

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿1987.1 does not require that parties meet and confer before the filing of a motion to quash a deposition subpoena. Thus, no declaration by Plaintiff attesting to any efforts thereto is required. 

 

  1. The Separate Statement 

 

Fahimian argues this court should deny the Motion because Plaintiff failed to file a separate statement. (Fahimian Opposition p. 8.)

 

Motions to compel or quash production of documents at depositions must be accompanied by a separate statement unless no response has been provided to the discovery. (CRC 3.1345(a)(5), (b)(1).) 

 

Plaintiff filed no separate statement with the Motion. Thus, this court finds that this requirement has not been met and denies the Motion without prejudice.

 

  1. Sanctions

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1987.2, subdivision (a) states, “in making an order pursuant to motion made under… Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”

Plaintiff seeks $8,770 in monetary sanctions against Defendants. (Motion p. 11.) Plaintiff is pro per and is therefore not entitled to recover attorneys’ fees as a discovery sanctions. (See Kravitz v. Superior Court (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 1015, 1020.) (Motion p. 12.) This court also finds that Fahimian and Bespoke Treatment did not oppose the motion in bad faith or without substantial justification and declines to award Plaintiff the filing fee as a sanction.

Bespoke Treatment seeks $2,250.00 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. (Bespoke Treatment Opposition pp. 15-16.) This court finds that Plaintiff did not file the Motion in bad faith or without substantial justification and therefore declines to award Bespoke Treatment monetary sanctions.

 

III.            CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies Plaintiff’s Motion.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

  

Demurrer Without Motion to Strike

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

¿ 

Defendants Habib Talebi and Mery Tabrizi (“Defendants”) demur to the complaint brought by Plaintiff Maboud Nourkhah (“Plaintiff”).

 

This court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend.

 

I.                BACKGROUND¿ 

 

On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants alleging causes of action for breach of contract and “faluse [&] defamation in society[.]” (Complaint p. 2.)

 

On October 4, 2024, Defendants demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint. No opposition has been filed.

 

On October 30, 2024, this court deferred ruling on the demurrer and continued the hearing to November 15, 2024. (10/30/24 Minute Order.)

II.             PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

a.     Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40(a).)

Plaintiff attached no proof of service to the complaint. (Complaint.) Accordingly, this court cannot evaluate whether Defendants’ demurrer is timely. Regardless, this court exercises its discretion and considers the demurrer. (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 750 [stating, “the lower court acted within its broad discretion by considering defendant's demurrer, notwithstanding plaintiff's claim that it was untimely.”])

b.     Meet and Confer

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)¿

 

Defendants’ counsel submits a declaration stating counsel “called the plaintiff to attempt to resolve the issues raised by this demurrer but was unable to do so.” (Axelrod ¶ 2.) Defendants’ counsel also called Plaintiff twice, but states “Plaintiff essentially refused to speak to me.” (Axelrod ¶ 2.)

This court finds that Defendants have satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).) A party may also demur to a complaint because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

 

Demurrer for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION¿ 

 

a.     Breach of Contract

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state a claim for breach of contract. (Demurrer p. 7.)

 

The elements of a breach of contract claim are: (1) existence of a contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendants’ breach; and (4) resulting damage. (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N. Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

 

Plaintiff’s complaint contains no factual allegations supporting the elements of a breach of contract cause of action. Accordingly, this court sustains the demurrer as to Plaintiff’s breach of contract cause of action.

 

b.     Defamation

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state a claim for defamation. (Demurrer p. 8.)

 

“The elements of a defamation claim are: (1) a publication that is; (2) false; (3) defamatory; (4) unprivileged; and (5) has a natural tendency to injure or causes special damage.” (J-M Manufacturing Co., Inc. v. Phillips & Cohen LLP (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 87, 97.)

 

Plaintiff’s complaint contains no factual allegations supporting the elements of a defamation cause of action. Accordingly, this court sustains the demurrer as to Plaintiff’s defamation cause of action.

 

c.      Uncertainty

 

Defendants argue both of Plaintiff’s causes of action are uncertain. (Demurrer pp. 7-8.)

 

“Even as against a special demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to ‘set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.’” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co., supra, 205 Cal.App.4th at p. 858.)

 

Plaintiff alleges no facts in the complaint. Further, Plaintiff states that Plaintiff is a “corporation qualified to do business in California” and “defendant is a corporation or unincorporated association and its principal place of business is here.” (Complaint.)  None of the parties in this action appear to be corporations.

 

Accordingly, this court finds that the complaint is so vague and unclear that the nature, source, and extent of the claims being brought against Defendants cannot be understood.

 

V.              LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303-304.)¿ 

 

This court finds that there is a reasonable possibility that Plaintiff can successfully amend the complaint. Accordingly, this court grants Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

VI.           CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer and grants Plaintiff leave to amend.
