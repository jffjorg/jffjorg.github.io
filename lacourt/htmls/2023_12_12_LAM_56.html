DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 18STCV06106    Hearing Date: December 12, 2023    Dept: 56

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

JIE LIU,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

HONGQIANG ZHOU, et al.,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.: 18STCV06106

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION TO ENFORCE SUBPOENA

 

Date: December 12, 2023

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff

 

The Court has considered the moving papers.  No opposition papers were filed.  Any opposition papers were required to have been filed and served at least nine court days before the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b).

 

BACKGROUND

            On December 15, 2020, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $40,678.70. 

 

            On October 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to enforce a deposition subpoena that was served on Defendant (the “Motion”). 

 

 

DISCUSSION

            Under CCP section 2025.450, if, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party, without having served a valid objection, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.  (CCP § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  The motion must both: (1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice; and (2) include a meet and confer declaration pursuant to CCP section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.  (CCP § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1)-(2); see Leko v. Cornerstone Building Inspection Service (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1109, 1124 (the statute also applies when the deponent simply fails to appear).)

 

            On January 6, 2023, Defendant was served with a subpoena (the “Subpoena”).  (Declaration of Edward C. Tu (“Tu Decl.”) ¶ 8.)  On August 24, 2023, the Court ordered Defendant to produce documents in compliance with the Subpoena by September 15, 2023.  (Tu Decl. ¶ 18.)  As of the filing of the Motion, Defendant had not produced any responses or documents.  (Tu Decl. ¶ 19.)

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

JINHEE KIM,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

B&K ENTERPRISE, INC., et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

      CASE NO.:  23STCV06136

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Date: December 12, 2023

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

Judge: Holly J. Fujie

 

 

MOVING PARTIES: Defendants B&K Enterprise, Inc. (“B&K”); Soojin Kelly Kim (“Soojin”); and Brandon Kim (“Brandon”) (collectively, “Moving Defendants”)[1]

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff

 

The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

This action arises out of a sublease arrangement.  The currently operative second amended complaint (the “SAC”) alleges: (1) breach of oral agreement; (2) wrongful retention of security deposit under Civil Code section 1950, subdivision (c); (3) money had and received; (4) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment; and (5) unfair competition under Business and Professions Code section 17200.

 

In relevant part, the SAC alleges: In 2019, Plaintiff and Moving Defendants entered into an oral agreement (the “Sublease Agreement”) for Plaintiff to sublet a second-floor commercial space (the “Premises”) from Moving Defendants.  (See SAC ¶¶ 8, 18.)  Under the Sublease Agreement, Plaintiff agreed to make monthly rent payments of $40,000 for the duration of Moving Defendants’ master tenancy, during which time Plaintiff would operate a karaoke business and utilize Moving Defendants’ services and infrastructure.  (See id.) 

 

Plaintiff took possession of the Premises in 2019.  (SAC ¶ 19.)  Plaintiff and Moving Defendants performed under the Sublease Agreement for about three years.  (SAC ¶ 20.)  On or about February 22, 2023, however, Brandon presented Plaintiff with a new written sublease agreement that indicated that the Premises would be sublet to new tenants, and demanded that Plaintiff vacate the Premises within seven days.  (See SAC ¶¶ 20-21.)  On information and belief, the new subtenants pay a larger amount in monthly rent, which motivated Moving Defendants to force Plaintiff to vacate the Premises.  (See SAC ¶¶ 20, 46.)

 

On November 6, 2023, Moving Defendants filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) to the fifth causes of action on the grounds that the SAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.  Moving Defendants also filed a motion to strike (the “Motion”) portions of the SAC.

 

 

 

DEMURRER

Meet and Confer

The meet and confer requirement has been met for the Demurrer and Motion.

 

Legal Standard

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)  The court accepts as true all material factual allegations and affords them a liberal construction, but it does not consider conclusions of fact or law, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or judicially noticed facts.  (Shea Homes Limited Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254.)  With respect to a demurrer, the complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded.  (Rodas v. Spiegel (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 513, 517.)  A demurrer will be sustained without leave to amend if there exists no reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) 

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”)

The UCL prohibits any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200; see Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 610.)  To show a violation of the UCL, a plaintiff must establish: (1) a loss or deprivation of money or property sufficient to qualify as injury in fact, i.e., economic injury; and (2) that that economic injury was the result of, i.e., caused by, the unfair business practice or false advertising that is the gravamen of the claim.  (Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310, 322.)  A breach of contract may form the predicate for a UCL claim, provided it also constitutes conduct that is unlawful, or unfair or fraudulent.  (Puentes v. Wells Fargo Home Mortgage, Inc. (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 638, 645.)  To show a business practice is unfair, the plaintiff must show the conduct threatens an incipient violation of an antitrust law, or violates the policy or spirit of one of those laws because its effects are comparable to or the same as a violation of the law, or otherwise significantly threatens or harms competition.  (Byars v. SCME Mortgage Bankers, Inc. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1134, 1147.)   

 

The UCL claim is rooted in Moving Defendants’ alleged breach of the Lease Agreement and the resulting damages Plaintiff incurred.  (See SAC ¶¶ 52-54.)  The Court is not persuaded that the facts in the SAC are sufficient to allege that the breach of the Lease Agreement was also unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent.  Plaintiff’s opposition (the “Demurrer Opposition”) does not clearly articulate how the facts of the SAC fall within the legal framework of the unfairness prong of the UCL.  Plaintiff has not shown the ability to amend this claim to state a valid cause of action.  The Court therefore SUSTAINS the Demurrer to the fifth cause of action without leave to amend. 

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

Legal Standard

Under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 436, a motion to strike either: (1) strikes any irrelevant, false or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strikes any pleading or part thereof not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule or order of court.  (CCP § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

 

            The Motion seeks to strike allegations from paragraphs 28 and 46 of the SAC.  Paragraph 26 concerns punitive damages and paragraph 48 alleges that Defendants Soon Han Pak, Chung Hyuk Pak, and 3855 Wilshire Blvd Property, LLC (collectively, the “3855 Wilshire Defendants”) assisted Moving Defendants’ scheme to terminate the Lease Agreement.  (See SAC ¶¶ 28, 46.) 

 

            The request for punitive damages is not alleged in connection with a particular claim, and Plaintiff does not identify a cause of action that may form the basis for a punitive damages award.  (See Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)  Paragraph 46 is alleged in connection with the fourth cause of action.  The Court previously sustained the 3855 Wilshire Defendants’ demurrer to the SAC’s fourth cause of action.  In addition, Plaintiff does not offer any authority that demonstrates a legal basis for imposing secondary liability in breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment actions.  The Court therefore GRANTS the Motion without leave to amend.
