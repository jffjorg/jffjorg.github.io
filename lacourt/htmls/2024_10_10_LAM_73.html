DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30828    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 73

10/10/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

YEDAM, INC., et al. v. DANIEL LEE, et al. (21STCV30828)

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainants/opposing party:  Dale Park (Law Offices of Dale J. Park)

Counsel for Cross-Defendants/moving party: Frederick Lee (Frederick W. Lee Law Firm APC)

 

CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (SET ONE) PROPOUNDED ON CROSS-COMPLAINANT DANIEL N. LEE AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed on 07/01/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee is granted in part and denied in part.  Further responses to Request Nos. 1-12, 14-18 and 25 are DENIED. Further responses to Request Nos. 19-24 are GRANTED.

 

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Yedam, Inc. (“Yedam") and Eun Kyung Choi (“Choi") brought this breach of loyalty and unfair business practices action against Daniel N. Lee (“Lee") and Jin Sook Lee (together, “the Lees"), and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“DM Cosmetics").

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 5 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Declaratory Relief.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Yedam imports and purchases products including candles and scented oils that are sold at various retail locations such as department stores in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 9.) On or about February 2017 Defendant Daniel Lee began working for Yedam in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 10.) In his capacity as an employee, Defendant Daniel Lee was significantly involved in dealing with: a) manufacturers that produced candles, scented oils or related products; b) the manufacturers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; c) the buyers at the department stores in Korea that purchased such products from Yedam; d) the buyers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; and e) the retail locations in both the United States of America and South Korea where such items were sold at. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Notwithstanding his duty of loyalty, Defendant Daniel Lee, in conjunction with his wife Defendant Angela Lee, have misused Yedam’s proprietary information in order to enrich themselves financially. (FAC, ¶ 24). Defendants’ actions have caused Buyers for Department Stores in South Korea to cease purchasing products from Plaintiff Yedam and/or have caused the closure of retail locations in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 26).

 

The Lees and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“Cross-Complainants" or “Defendants”) cross-complained against Yedam, Choi, Olive House LLC, and Lamis Tania, LLC (“Cross-Defendants"). On August 10, 2023, Cross-Complainants filed their third amended cross-complaint (“TACC”) asserting 8 causes of action against Cross-Defendants: (1) Breach of Oral Contract I; (2) Breach of Oral Contract II; (3) Breach of Oral Contract III; (4) Breach of Oral Contract IV; (5) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (6) Trademark Infringement; (7) Unfair Competition in Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq. and California Trademark Law; and (8) Declaratory Relief.

 

The TACC alleges the following. Prior to 2017, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Choi owned Yedam. (TACC, ¶ 11.) Yedam was experiencing serious financial problems. (Ibid.) In February of 2017, Choi approached Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lee asking for help. (Id., ¶ 12.) Choi proposed Lee serve as Yedam’s general manager, whereby Lee would oversee the Korean market and Choi would oversee the Chinese market. (Ibid.) In exchange, Lee would be paid a $10,000 per month salary. (Ibid.) In addition, Choi would give Lee 49% ownership of Yedam if Lee could help Yedam “overcome [its] financial hardship and become profitable." (Ibid.) Lee agreed and moved from the US to Korea to serve as Yedam’s general manager. (Id., ¶ 13.) To help Yedam solve its financial issues, Lee loaned Yedam $70,000.00 in operating capital. (Id., ¶ 14.) Choi promised that the $70,000.00 would be paid back when Yedam become profitable, along with 7% interest per annum from April 1, 2017. (Id., ¶ 12.) Lee successfully managed Yedam thereafter, and Yedam went from net losses in 2016 and 2017 to net profits of over a million dollars per year by the end of 2019. (Id., ¶ 15.) However, in July of 2019, Yedam faced supply chain issues when the “Milk House Candle" company in the United States refused to renew its exclusive distribution contract with Yedam. (Id., ¶ 16.) In response, the Lees created a new brand of merchandise named “Dani Mackenzie" under DM Cosmetics, Inc. (Id., ¶ 17.) The Lees trademarked Dani Mackenzie in the United States in May of 2020. (Id., ¶ 18.) Around that same time, Choi proposed that Yedam purchase Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics in exchange for Yedam having the right to use the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 19.) Choi told the Lees that there was no need to have a written licensing and distribution agreement because Choi would transfer 49% of Yedam’s ownership interest to Lee by the end of December 2020. (Id., ¶ 20.)

 

Dani Mackenzie became highly popular in Korea, and Yedam’s sales and profit soared to the point that Yedam was valued at over $50 million. (Id., ¶ 22.) On December 20, 2020, Lee demanded that Choi transfer 49% of Yedam to him. (Id., ¶ 23.) Lee also demanded that Choi and Yedam repay the $70,000 operating capital loan with 7% interest from April 1, 2017. (Ibid.) However, Choi terminated Lee from Yedam shortly thereafter and refused to convey the 49% ownership share. (Id., ¶ 25.) Further, Choi and Yedam stopped purchasing Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics and started using other suppliers to manufacture products bearing the Dani Mackenzie trade name. (Id., ¶ 28.) On March 18, 2021 and April 14, 2021, Cross-Complainants demanded that Cross-Defendants cease and desist manufacturing, supplying and/or selling products under the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 32.)

 

A.      Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents (Set One)

 

On July 1, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed the Motion to Compel Further Answers to Request for Production of Documents (Set One) against Daniel Lee, arguing:

·       The responses are not code compliant.

·       Monetary sanctions in the amount of $6,010.00.

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Responses are code-compliant and the requests contain vague and ambiguous terms.

·       Sanctions should be awarded to Daniel Lee in the amount of $2,160.00.

No reply was filed.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, counsel for Cross-Defendants submitted a declaration attesting that the parties discussed the alleged deficient responses via email but an agreement was not reached. (Muriella Decl., ¶¶ 8-11.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

B.      Legal Standard

¿  

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

C.      Discussion

 

Cross-Defendants request further responses to Requests Nos. 1-12, 14-25.

 

Requests Nos. 1-12 and 14 seek all documents identified in response to corresponding Interrogatories. Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee responded by stating that no documents were identified in the underlying interrogatories, thus, he is unable to comply with this demand. Cross-Defendants fail to show why further responses are warranted. Since Cross-Complainant Lee has stated why he cannot comply with these demands, the Court denies Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

 

Requests Nos. 15-18 and 25 seek documents or ESI relating to communications at any time from April 29, 2019 up to and including the present between Cross-Complainant Lee and any buyer of the PRODUCT. Cross-Complainant objects to each request stating that the term “PRODUCT” is vague, ambiguous, overbroad, too general and too indefinite. The Court agrees. Cross-Defendants define “PRODUCT” as candles, diffusers and/or scented oils. However, Cross-Defendants’ definition is overbroad, and it is unclear specifically what candles, diffusers and/or scented oils they are referring to. For instance, if Cross-Defendants’ are only referring to the products bearing the trademark, then it should be clearly set forth. The Court denies Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

 

Requests Nos. 19-24 seek documents or ESI relating to communications at any time from April 29, 2019 up to and including the present between Cross-Complainant Lee and Eun Kyung Choi regarding either Yedam, Inc., DM Cosmetics, or Dani Mackenzie. Cross-Complainant objects to each request stating that “regarding Yedam, Inc.”, “regarding DM Cosmetics” and “regarding Dani Mackenzie” are vague, ambiguous, overbroad, too general and too indefinite. However, the Court finds that further responses are warranted. The discovery sought is relevant to the subject matter of this case. Cross-Defendants have specified the scope of time for which they are seeking production. Further, while Cross-Complainant provides boilerplate objections and states in a conclusory manner that they will suffer undue hardship in searching these documents, Cross-Complainant fails to provide any evidence as to this undue hardship. The party objecting to a discovery request on this basis bears the burden of supplying evidence of “the quantum of work required.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549.)¿¿The Court grants Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

 

In sum, Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee is granted in part and denied in part.  Further responses to Request Nos. 1-12, 14-18 and 25 are DENIED. Further responses to Request Nos. 19-24 are GRANTED.

 

D.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Both parties request the Court impose monetary sanctions. Here, the motion is granted and denied in part. The Court declines to impose sanctions.  

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee is granted in part and denied in part.  Further responses to Request Nos. 1-12, 14-18 and 25 are DENIED. Further responses to Request Nos. 19-24 are GRANTED.

 

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied.

KENT TRAN v. UTOMO TANI, et al. (23STCV08305)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants/moving party:  Sally Chan (West Themis Law, PC)

Counsel for Defendants/Cross-Complainant/ opposing party: T. Randolph Catanese (Catanese and Wells)

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE THE TRIAL DATE AND RELATED DEADLINES (filed 09/10/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Motion to Continue the Trial date and related deadlines is GRANTED. Dates  to be provided 10-10-24.

Related deadlines are to follow the new trial date.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On April 13, 2023, Plaintiff Kent Tran (“Plaintiff”) filed his Complaint against Defendants Utomi Tani aka Jimmy Tani (“Jimmy”); Yong Sook Kim; Jimmy Tani, Jr.; Lan Sam; Goodland Global Farm Inc.; JT and KT Development LLC; Maie JT & KT Development LLC; True Garlic, Inc.; Goodland Global Mining, Inc.; and nominal Defendant Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc. dba NBC Seafood Restaurant (“collectively Defendants”).

 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint alleging the following causes of action:

1)      Intentional misrepresentation/fraud

2)      Conversion/embezzlement

3)      Declaratory relief

4)      Partition by sale

5)      Violation of Welfare and Institutions Code § 15600 et seq – Financial Elder Abuse

6)      Intentional infliction of emotional distress

7)      Breach of fiduciary duty

8)      Conspiracy

9)      Fraudulent transfer/constructive fraud

10)  Negligent misrepresentation/fraud

11)  Fraud by concealment

12)  Breach of written contract

13)  Breach of oral contract

14)  Inspection of records

15)  Accounting

16)  Unjust enrichment

17)  Involuntary dissolution of corporations

18)  Fraudulent transfer

19)  CA Penal Code Section § 496(A)

20)  Cancellation of instrument pursuant to Civ. Code § 3412

 

The Complaint alleges the following. In or about 2008, Plaintiff and Jimmy formed several companies, including Goodland Global Farm Inc., (“Goodland”), JT and KT Development LLC, and Maie JT and KT Development LLC. Plaintiff and Jimmy had agreed to own the companies equally as partners. Plaintiff and Jimmy also decided to jointly invest in another company, NBC Seafood Restaurant (“NBC”) with Kam Choi Lin, Chang Ho Chen, Wei Lin Zheng and Wai Hung Szeto. Plaintiff alleges that Jimmy engaged in fraud and falsely misrepresented to Plaintiff, among other things, that Plaintiff owned 50% of the Companies when he actually did not, that the Companies were in need of loans and not profitable in order to induce Plaintiff to obtain personal loans, for Plaintiff to sign documents that were related to taking out loans in 2021, and that Plaintiff needed to vacate and sell his home to save the Companies.

 

On February 15, 2024, Defendants (“Cross-Complainants”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Kam Choi Lin, Chang Ho Chen, Wei Lin Zheng and Wai Hung Szeto (collectively “Cross-Defendants”).

 

Cross-Complainants filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1)      Equitable Indemnity and Contribution

2)      Indemnity

3)      Declaratory relief

 

Cross-Complainants, Cross-Defendants and Plaintiff are shareholders of NBC Seafood Restaurant. The FACC alleges that that any liability held against them pertaining to Plaintiff’s allegations, is due to the negligence, carelessness, acts, or omissions of Cross-Defendants. Cross-Complainants are seeking indemnification and contribution for any loss suffered and damages awarded to Plaintiff. Cross-Complainants also seek a declaration that Jimmy has not violated the Bylaws of Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc., and that his actions or omissions were ratified and consented to by Cross-Defendants.

 

On September 10, 2024, Defendants and Cross-Complainants’ filed a Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Deadlines, arguing:

·       Good Cause Exists to Grant the Motion to Continue the Trial Date Because of the Ongoing Illness of Defendant and Cross-Complainant Yong Sook Kim.

·       Good Cause Exists to Grant the Motion to Continue the Trial Date Because of Defendants' and Cross-Complainants' Inability to Obtain Essential Testimony, Documents and Other Material Evidence Despite Diligent Efforts

·       The Case is Not Yet At Issue and This is the First and Only Anticipated Request for Continuance by Defendants and Cross-Complainants.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff Kent Tran’s Age and Health Mandate Expedited Trial Under California Code of Civil Procedure Section 36.

o   At 73, Plaintiff has faced ongoing health problems, including surgeries and medical treatments since 2018. Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 36, a party over 70 with substantial interest in a case is entitled to trial preference if health concerns warrant it, preventing prejudice to their litigation interests.

·       While the Defendants cite the medical condition of Ms. Yong Sook Kim as their primary reason for requesting a continuance, the evidence they provide is neither specific nor substantial enough to justify such a lengthy postponement.

·       Unavailability of Essential Testimony and Documents Does Not Justify Delay

·       While it is true that some legal and factual issues have not yet been fully developed, this is a direct result of Defendants' persistent refusal to engage in timely discovery and their continuous efforts to obstruct the discovery process.

 

Defendants and Cross-Complainants filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff Kent Tran's Request for Trial Preference Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 36 is Improper and Should be Disregarded.

o   It is brought in an incorrect manner, and the request is not supported by any declaration of the Plaintiff, counsel, or attending physician.

·       Defendant and Cross-Complainant Yong Sook Kim is an Essential Party for Trial. She is directly sued in the SAC.

·       The Unavailability of Essential Testimony and Documents Constitutes Good. Cause to Continue the Trial Date.

 

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A party seeking a continuance of the date set for trial must make the request for a continuance by a noticed motion or an ex parte application as soon as reasonably practical once the necessity for the continuance is discovered.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332(b).)¿ The request for continuance may be granted on an affirmative showing of good cause.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332.)¿

 

The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial. (See generally, Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (d)(1)-(11).) Additionally, factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; the proximity of the trial date; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c), (d).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Defendants and Cross-Complainants move for an order continuing the November 25, 2024 trial date and related deadlines for a period of six months or any date thereafter.

 

Defendants and Cross-Complainants assert that due to illness, it is anticipated that Defendant and Cross-Complainant Yong Sook Kim would not be able to attend the current trial date. In her declaration, Defendant and Cross-Complainant Yong Sook Kim states that she was required to go on an emergency basis to the hospital on August 30, 2024 and on September 5, 2024, her medical doctor advised that she would need additional rest and time to rehabilitate until mid-2025. (Kim Decl., ¶ 4.) Kim submitted a letter from her doctor stating her multiple medical morbidities and that postponing the court date about 6 months would accommodate her medical need. (Kim Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. 1.) Defendants and Cross-Complainants also contend that this is the first request for a trial continuance and the case is relatively new having been filed on April 13, 2023. They argue that there has been little discovery in this case and Defendants and Cross-Complainants have been unable to obtain essential testimony, documents and other material evidence. For example, Plaintiff’s deposition has not been taken yet. (Catanese Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds there is good cause to continue trial. Defendant and Cross-Complainant Yong Sook Kim is a party in this action and states her serious illness would preclude her from attending the current trial date. “The death or serious illness of a trial attorney or a party should, under normal circumstances, be considered good cause for granting the continuance of a trial date.” (Hernandez v. Superior Ct. (2004) 115 Cal. App. 4th 1242, 1247–48.) Further, Defendants and Cross-Complainants have stated they have been unable to obtain essential discovery. Additionally, there has been no previous trial continuance, the case is relatively new and the relatively short length of the requested continuance all weigh in favor of a trial continuance.

 

Moreover, Plaintiff’s opposition does not sufficiently allege prejudice. Plaintiff also argues that preference should be given to Plaintiff pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 36, but fails to provide any evidence in support of any health issues.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds the interests of justice are best served by a trial continuance. The Court GRANTS the Motion to Continue the Trial and related deadlines.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Motion to Continue the Trial date and related deadlines is GRANTED.  Dates to be provided 10-10-24. Related deadlines are to follow the new trial date.

ALADDIN DINAALI v. BRIDGE PROPERTY MANAGEMENT, LC, et al. (24STCV01673)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Aladdin Dinaali (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  David Samani (Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP)

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed on 8/13/24 by Defendants Bridge Property Management, LC, James E. Murphy, and Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants Bridge Property Management, LC, James E. Murphy and Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP’s motion for attorney fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Defendants $15,400 in attorney fees and costs. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff Aladdin Dinaali (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Bridge Property Management, LC (“Bridge”); Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP (“LBBS”); James E. Murphy (“Murphy”); American Legal Investigation Services Nevada, Inc.; Gabriela Melendez; and Deyber Jimenez.

 

The basis of this lawsuit is that in April 2022, Bridge, through its counsel, Murphy, sued Plaintiff in Nevada (the “Nevada action”) for defamation and business disparagement. In the Nevada action, Bridge alleged that Plaintiff damaged its reputation by creating two websites that falsely stated that criminals resided in Bridge’s property and that the company is racist. Plaintiff allegedly sent e-mails to Bridge’s employees directing them to visit the websites. Bridge dismissed the case in May 2023.

 

In October 2022, Plaintiff sued Bridge and Murphy in Los Angeles Superior Court, case number 22STCV32840 (“First California Action”). Plaintiff alleged that he was wrongfully sued in Nevada, Bridge allegedly filed a false police report against him for criminal prosecution, Bridge conspired with its attorney and employees to falsely accuse him of violating various statutes, and that his website constitutes protected activities. In January 2023, the court dismissed the case.

 

In January 2024, Plaintiff brought this current lawsuit alleging that the Nevada action was filed by Defendants frivolously and unlawfully. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

 

C/A 1: Conspiracy

C/A 2: Malicious Prosecution

C/A 3: Abuse of Process

C/A 4: Unfair Business Practices (Civ § 1770)

C/A 5: Unfair Business Practices (BPC § 17200)

C/A 6: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

C/A 7: Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

On March 25, 2024, Defendants Bridge, LBBS and Murphy (collectively “Bridge Defendants”) filed a Special Motion to Strike (“anti-SLAPP”).

On June 14, 2024, this Court entered an order granting the Bridge Defendants anti-SLAPP motion.

On August 13, 2024, the Bridge Defendants filed their motion for attorneys’ fees, arguing:

·       The Bridge Defendants are entitled to a fee award as prevailing party on the anti-SLAPP motion, they respectfully request that the Court grant this Motion and award them $15,400 in attorneys’ fees and costs.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing: 

·       The Bridge Defendants failed to meet and confer with Plaintiff about their motion or else Plaintiff would have withdrawn the complaint

·       Defendants committed perjury in their declarations and are subject to criminal prosecution

 

Bridge Defendants filed a reply, arguing:

·       There is no requirement to meet and confer prior to filing an anti-SLAPP motion.

·       Plaintiff, without providing any evidence, asserts that defendants committed perjury in their declaration. This baseless and unsupported statement cannot be used to deny Defendants’ motion.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (c) states that “[i]n any action subject to subdivision (b), a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover his or her attorneys’ fees and costs.”

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

As the prevailing party on their anti-SLAPP motion, the Bridge Defendants move for an award of mandatory attorneys’ fees and costs against Plaintiff in the amount of $15,400.

 

The Court notes that in Plaintiff’s opposition, he asserts that the motion should be denied because (1) the Bridge Defendants “failed to meet and confer between their filing or else Plaintiff would have withdrawn the complaint”; and (2) “Defendants committed perjury in their declarations and are subject to criminal prosecution should they wish to continue with this motion.” (Oppos., p. 2.) However, Plaintiff fails to provide any authority or evidence in support of his statements. The Court does not find Plaintiff’s assertions as grounds to deny the Bridge Defendants’ instant motion.

 

Since the Court granted the Bridge Defendants’ anti-SLAPP motion, the Bridge Defendants are entitled to attorneys’ fees and costs pursuant to CCP § 425.16(c).  

 

a.     Hourly Rate

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) 

 

The Bridge Defendants’ seek to recover fees for their counsel’s billed time in connection with their anti-SLAPP and the instant motion. The Bridge Defendants’ counsel, Patrik Johansson, states his hourly rate is $425 per hour. (Johansson Decl., ¶ 13.) 

 

The Court finds that the rates requested by the Bridge Defendants are reasonable and not higher than rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and expertise. Mr. Johansson attests to his experience and the reasonableness of the rates. (Johansson Decl., ¶¶ 5, 13-14.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

b.     Reasonable Hours Incurred

 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.”  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) 

 

While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) 

 

The Bridge Defendants move for attorney fees and costs in the total amount of $15,400 incurred in connection with their successful anti-SLAPP motion and the instant motion for attorney fees and costs. The Bridge Defendants note they are only seeking 2/3 of the fees and cost incurred as Defendant LBBS is self-represented.

 

The Bridge Defendants assert the total time spent on the anti-SLAPP motion took 45.2 hours. (Johansson Decl., ¶ 9.) However, since the Bridge Defendants only seek 2/3 of the total fees, the Bridge Defendants are seeking only fees incurred for 30 hours (2/3 of 45.2 hours). (Johansson Decl., ¶ 10.) Therefore, at a rate of $425 per hour, the total fees sought in connection with the anti-SLAPP motion is $12,750 (30 x 425.)

 

The Bridge Defendants also seek fees incurred on the instant motion. The Bridge Defendants anticipate spending 6 hours total on the instant motion. (Johansson Decl., ¶ 13.) Thus, the total fees sought in connection with the instant motion is $1,700 (2/3 of 6 x 425.)

 

Having analyzed the instant motion, the anti-SLAPP motion, and pleadings, the Court finds that the fees sought by the Bridge Defendants are reasonable and do not include excessive time billed or duplicative entries. “Parties who litigate with no holds barred in cases such as this, in which the prevailing party is entitled to a fee award, assume the risk they will have to reimburse the excessive expenses they force upon their adversaries.” (Stokus v. Marsh (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 647, 653-654.) Here, the Court does not find that the time billed by defense counsel was excessive.

 

c.      Costs

 

The Bridge Defendants also seek the costs incurred with their anti-SLAPP motion and the instant motion. The Bridge Defendants incurred $1,305 in appearance fees, and $60 for the filing fee for the anti-SLAPP motion. (Johansson Decl., ¶ 11.) They also incurred an additional $60 in motion fees for this fee motion. (Ibid.) The Bridge Defendants are seeking $950 of these fees (2/3 of $1,425.) The Court grants the Bridge Defendants’ request for costs.

 

In sum, the Bridge Defendant’s Motion for Award of Attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in connection with their successful anti-SLAPP motion and the instant motion is GRANTED in the total amount of $15,400.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants Bridge Property Management, LC, James E. Murphy and Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP’s motion for attorney fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Defendants $15,400 in attorney fees and costs.

PULLIAM v. CITY OF LOS ANGELES POLICE DEPARTMENT (24STCV18869)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff: Alander Pulliam (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant:  Jeremy Warren (City Attorney)

 

(1)   DEFENDANT’S Demurrer TO complaint (filed 9/05/24)

(2)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DISCOVERY RESPONSES AND FOR DISCOVERY OF WITNESS (filed 8/07/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery Responses and for Discovery of Witness.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff Alander Lee Pulliam Jr. filed this action against Defendant City of Los Angeles Police Department.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Violation of California Civil Code Section 51.7 (Ralph Civil Rights Act of 1976)

2.      Battery

3.      Assault

4.      False Arrest and Imprisonment

5.      Invasion of Privacy

 

The Complaint alleges the following. “This complaint concerns an incident that occurred on July 26, 2024, and which involved, among other things, blatant and unjustifiable misconduct by Los Angeles Police Department Officers Antonio J. Velasco and Christian A. Castro.” (Complaint, ¶ 1.) “Plaintiff was subjected to an unmerited arrest and officers executed an unlawful search of plaintiff's belongings without cause or justification.” (Complaint, ¶ 2.) Plaintiff seeks compensatory and punitive damages.

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Discovery Responses and for Discovery of Witness, arguing:

·       Plaintiff seeks the discovery of personnel files of Officers Velasco and Castro; incident reports regarding the July 26, 2024 incident; surveillance reports regarding the July 26, 2024 incident; witness information regarding the July 26, 2024 incident.

·       Plaintiff seeks this information under California Code of Civil Procedure Sections 2030.290 and 2031.310.

 

No opposition or other reply was filed.

 

On September 5, 2024, Defendant City of Los Angeles (“Defendant” or “City”) filed a demurrer to the Complaint, arguing:

·       The Los Angeles Police Department (“LAPD”) cannot sue or be sued because it does not exist separate and apart from the City of Los Angeles and does not have its own legal identity. (See Alcala v. City of Corcoran (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 666, 669-670.) Therefore, a suit against the LAPD is tantamount to a suit against the City of Los Angeles. Thus, the LAPD should be dismissed.

·       Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to allege compliance with the Government Tort Claims Act. Gov’t. §§810 et. seq.

·       Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain therefore the City demurs to the entire Complaint

·       Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to adequately allege a statutory basis for imposing tort liability against the City, which is a public entity. Gov. Code, §§ 815, subd. (a), 815.6.

 

No opposition or other reply was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Defendant’s Demurrer

 

a.       Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating that on August 29, 2024, he spoke with Plaintiff Alander Lee Pulliam Jr., who is representing himself, regarding the grounds of the demurrer.  (Warren Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation and the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

b.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

c.       Discussion

 

 

 

First, the City Defendant asserts that although Plaintiff has named the LAPD as a defendant in this action, the LAPD cannot be sued in state court because it does not exist separate and apart from the City Defendant, and thus, the LAPD does not have its own legal identity. (See Alcala v. City of Corcoran (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 666, 669-71.) The City Defendant avers that a lawsuit against the LAPD is tantamount to a lawsuit against the City Defendant. Plaintiff does not dispute this assertion or otherwise provide any explanation for why he can sue LAPD separately from the City Defendant. Thus, LAPD is not a properly named defendant in this matter.

 

Next, the City Defendant argues the demurrer should be sustained because Plaintiff failed to plead his compliance with the Government Tort Claims Act.  The Court agrees.  Except as provided in Sections 946.4 and 946.6, a written claim must be presented to a public entity and must be denied or deemed denied before a suit for money or damages may be brought against the public entity on a cause of action for which a claim is required to be presented.¿ (Gov. Code, § 945.4; Munoz v. State of California (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1767, 1777.)¿¿ The claim must be filed within six months.¿ (Gov. Code, § 911.2, subd. (a); Munoz, supra, 33 Cal.App.4th at p. 1777.)¿ Here, the Complaint is devoid of any allegations regarding Plaintiff’s compliance with the Government Tort Claim Act.   

 

Finally, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s Complaint is uncertain and fails to state any causes of action. The first page of the Complaint states Plaintiff is alleging causes of action for: Violation of California Civil Code Section 51.7 (Ralph Civil Rights Act of 1976); Battery; Assault; False Arrest and Imprisonment; and Invasion of Privacy. However, the Complaint does not contain any facts in support of these causes of action. Plaintiff only references an incident on July 26, 2024 and provides conclusory allegations of misconduct, without providing any specific facts in support. CCP section 430.10(f) provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous and unintelligible. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) Not only does Plaintiff fail to state a cause of action, but the Court finds the Complaint is ambiguous such that Defendant cannot determine what is at issue.

 

Accordingly, based on the foregoing independent reasons, the City Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.  

 

B.      Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery

 

In light of the Court’s ruling on Defendant’s demurrer, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery. As discussed above, Plaintiff’s operative Complaint is uncertain and fails to state any causes of action. Further, Plaintiff brings his instant motion against the LAPD, which is not a properly named Defendant.

 

Therefore, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery Responses and for Discovery of Witness.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Discovery Responses and for Discovery of Witness.
