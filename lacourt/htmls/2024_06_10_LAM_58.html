DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV04046    Hearing Date: June 10, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce G. Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             June 10, 2024

Case Name:                Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP v. Great Host International, Inc.  

Case No.:                    23STCV04046

Motion:                       Motion to Set Aside and Vacate Default Judgment

Moving Party:             Defendant Great Host International

Opposing Party:          Plaintiff Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP

Tentative Ruling:      The Motion to Vacate and Set Aside Default pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473, subdivision (d) and 473.5 is denied.  

             

Plaintiff Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP (Plaintiff), a law firm, brought this action against their former client, Defendant Great Host International, Inc. (Defendant) for failure to pay attorneys’ fees in excess of $100,000.00.

After Defendant failed to file an Answer, default was entered against Defendant on May 26, 2023. The Court entered default judgment on July 21, 2023.

On January 18, 2024, Defendant moved to set aside the entry of default judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). Plaintiff opposed the motion. On February 21, 2024, the Court denied the motion to set aside and vacate the default and default judgment.

 

         Now, Defendant moves to set aside and vacate the default and default judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473, subdivision (d) and 473.5. Plaintiff again opposes the motion.

 

         The Motion to Vacate and Set Aside Default is denied.

        

         Plaintiff’s objections to Defendant’s declaration of Ali Zeini are ruled as follows: Nos. 1, 6, 9 are sustained, No. 2-3 are overruled, No. 4-5, 7-8 are sustained as to “nor any other officer of Great Host”.

 

         Further, the Court sustains Plaintiff’s objection to Defendant’s Supplemental Declaration of Ali Zeini, submitted with Defendant’s reply. The declaration is neither signed under penalty of perjury nor does it state the place and date of execution. It is thus not competent evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2015.5; People v. United Bonding Ins. Co. (1969) 272 Cal.App.2d 441, 446 [rejecting purported declaration lacking place or date of execution]; Dodge v. Free (1973) 32 Cal.App.3d 436, 444 [undated declaration invalid].)

 

Analysis

 

         Defendant seeks relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473, subdivision (d) and 473.5.

 

         Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) provides that the Court may “set aside any void judgment or order.” Section 473.5, subdivision (a) provides that a party may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend when “service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party to defend the action.”

 

            As noted in the prior motion to set aside, Defendant’s president, Ali Zeini, represents that he learned that a default judgment in this action had been entered against Defendant Great Host International, Inc. on August 1, 2023. (Def.’s RJN Ex. A [Zeini Decl., ¶ 3.) He further states Defendant never received service of the complaint and summons in this action, and he never authorized Sam Palacios to accept any documents on behalf of Defendant for the purposes of service. (Def.’s RJN Ex. A [Zeini Decl., ¶¶ 3-4].)

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues the motion to set aside based on Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) and 473.5 are untimely. This argument is well taken.

 

            As a preliminary matter, the motion to set aside is based on improper service; Defendant does not argue the judgment is void on its face – at least until reply. However, although the reply asserts the default judgment is “void on its face” Defendant does not make any argument challenging the judgment except as to service.

 

To be sure, a motion to set aside a judgment void on its face may be brought at any time. (Pittman v. Beck Park Apartments Ltd. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1009, 1021.) “In determining whether an order is void for purposes of section 473, subdivision (d), courts distinguish between orders that are void on the face of the record and orders that appear valid on the face of the record but are shown to be invalid through consideration of extrinsic evidence. ‘This distinction may be important in a particular case because it impacts the procedural mechanism available to attack the judgment [or order], when the judgment [or order] may be attacked, and how the party challenging the judgment [or order] proves that the judgment [or order] is void.’ [Citation.]” (Pittman, supra, 20 Cal.App.5th at pp. 1020-1021.) Here, Defendant relies on the extrinsic evidence that “Sam Palacios is a general office employee with no authority to receive any communications on behalf of Great Host.” (Zeini Decl., ¶ 5.) By relying on a extrinsic evidence to show the default judgment is invalid, Defendant cannot claim the default judgment is void on its face. Thus, Defendant’s motion to set aside is subject to the time limits of Sections 473, subdivision (d) and 473.5.

 

            First, under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5, “a party can make a motion showing a lack of actual notice not caused by avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, but such motion must be made no later than two years after entry of judgment, and the party must act with diligence upon learning of the judgment.” (Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 175, 180 [emphasis added]; Code Civ. Pro., § 473.5, subd. (a).)

 

            Similarly, “[w]here a party moves under section 473, subdivision (d) to set aside “a judgment that, though valid on its face, is void for lack of proper service, the courts have adopted by analogy the statutory period for relief from a default judgment” provided by section 473.5, that is, the two-year outer limit.” (Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 175, 180.) This includes the two-year outer time limit. (Ibid.) It also includes the “reasonable time” limit. (Schenkel v. Resnik (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th Supp. 1, 3-4.) Because the same “reasonable time” language requiring diligence in the filing of the set-aside motion is found in both sections 473 and 473.5, courts have held that “the same interpretation should be given to the reasonable time requirement set forth in both sections.” (Schenkel v. Resnik, supra, 27 Cal.App.4th Supp. at 4.)

 

            There is no dispute that this second motion for relief was brought within two years of the entry of default judgment. Thus, the Court considers only whether the motion was brought in a “reasonable time” after discovery of the default judgment.

 

The determination of a reasonable time depends upon the circumstances of each case. (Caldwell v. Methodist Hospital (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1521, 1524 [finding that a three-month delay in seeking to set aside dismissal of action is unreasonable].)

 

            In this case, Defendant has not met its burden of showing that it brought this second motion within a reasonable time. (Younessi v. Woolf (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 1137, 1145 [a ‘ “moving party ... must show diligence in making the motion after discovery of the default.” ’]”.)

 

             Here, as the opposition notes, default judgment was entered on July 21, 2023. Defendant admitted that it first learned of the default judgment on August 1, 2023 and filed its first motion under Section 473, subdivision (b), on January 18, 2024 – 5 months and 18 days or 170 days later. The motion was denied on February 21, 2024 and, although represented by counsel, Defendant waited until May 10, 2024 to file the instant motion – an additional 79 days.

 

            Despite this being the second attempt to set aside the default judgment, the moving papers contain no explanation for this delay in bringing either motion. In fact, it is only in reply that Defendant attempts to provide some explanation. The Court sustained the objection to Zeini’s supplemental declaration, and therefore, the purported explanation is inadmissible.  But even if the Court considered it, Zeini’s declaration is suspiciously vague and conclusory.[1] Zeini states that after discovering the default judgment on August 1, 2023, he travelled abroad on a pre-planned vacation to Lebanon from August 15, 2023 to September 15, 2023. (Zeini Suppl. Decl., ¶ 5.) Then, without providing a specific date, Zeini states that “Shortly after [his] return, [he] was screened and diagnosed with skin cancer, for which [he] started receiving treatment on January 3, 2024.” (Id. ¶ 6.) Further, he suggests that no one else in the company was able to address this default judgment because Great Host’s other corporate officers, Salim Zeini and Zack Zeny, are Zeini’s children, “so [his] cancer diagnosis and treatment were disruptive to all of Great Host’s upper management.” (Id. ¶ 7.) Finally, Defendant asserts that it was also preparing for trial in the JSS Almonds Matter, which was set for October 2, 2023 – but notes that on September 29, 2023 the trial was continued to January 2, 2024. (Id. ¶ 8.)

 

            None of this reply evidence – even if the Court considered it and credited it – demonstrates diligence in pursuing relief. Zeini’s supplemental declaration fails to show that the described incidents significantly interfered with Defendant’s ability to file a motion, justifying the delay. There are also weeks that are simply unaccounted for when Defendant had ample time to take action, such as the time between the discovery of the default judgment and Zeini’s vacation. Far shorter delays have been held to be unreasonable when there was no satisfactory explanation for them. (Younessi v. Woolf, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 1145 [seven weeks]; Mercantile Collection Bureau v. Pinheiro (1948) 84 Cal.App.2d 606, 607-609 [two months].)

 

Based on the foregoing, the motion to set aside the entry of default and default judgment based on Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) and 473.5 is denied.

Conclusion

 

            The motion to set aside the default judgment is denied.


[1]           Even the portion of the declaration that asserts when and how Defendant discovered the default judgment is vague. Paragraph 3 of the supplemental declaration fails to identify who at Great Host purportedly received notice, and who sent the email with the notice of default. There is no evidence of personal knowledge supporting the assertion. Even if the supplemental Zeini declaration had been properly executed, most of it would be inadmissible, and little of it is credible.


Case Number: 24STCV02795    Hearing Date: June 10, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce G. Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             June 10, 2024

Case Name:                Snyder v Cardwell

Case No.:                   24STCV02795

Matter:                        Motion to Compel Arbitration

Moving Party:             Defendants James Cardwell, James Cummings, Dweller, LLC, Monty the Dog Productions, Inc., Big Movie, LLC, and Leonard Maurice Foley III

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Stephen Snyder

Tentative Ruling:      The Motion to Compel Arbitration is granted; the matter is stayed pending resolution of the arbitration.

 

This action arises from a transaction to acquire the rights to a feature film. Plaintiff Stephen Snyder (Plaintiff) sued Defendants James Cardwell, James Cummings, Dweller, LLC, Monty the Dog Productions, Inc., Big Movie, LLC, Leonard Maurice Foley III, and Mark Styslinger for fraudulently inducing Plaintiff into executing an Acquisition and Release Agreement where the Defendants acquired all rights, entitlements, controls and all other interests which are associated with the motion picture titled “Dweller.”

 

On February 20, 2024, Defendant Mark Styslinger (Styslinger) specially appeared and moved to quash service of summons based on a lack of personal jurisdiction. Plaintiff opposed the motion to quash, contending that there is personal jurisdiction over Styslinger. The motion to quash for lack of personal jurisdiction was granted.

 

            On May 13, 2024, Defendants James Cardwell, James Cummings, Dweller, LLC, Monty the Dog Productions, Inc., Big Movie, LLC, and Leonard Maurice Foley III (Defendants) filed a motion to compel arbitration pursuant to the parties’ arbitration agreement. Plaintiff opposes the motion to compel arbitration.

 

            The motion to compel arbitration is granted; the matter is stayed pending the outcome of arbitration.  

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, a court may order arbitration of a controversy if it finds that the parties have agreed to arbitrate that dispute. Because the obligation to arbitrate arises from contract, the court may compel arbitration only if the dispute in question is one in which the parties have agreed to arbitrate. (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 352.) Since arbitration is a favored method of dispute resolution, arbitration agreements should be liberally interpreted, and arbitration should be ordered unless the agreement clearly does not apply to the dispute in question. (Id. at p. 353; Segal v. Silberstein (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 627, 633.)

 

Analysis

 

Existence of a Valid Agreement

 

In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, a court must determine two threshold matters: first, whether a valid agreement to arbitrate exists; and second, whether that agreement encompasses the dispute at issue. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.)  

 

            By way of background, on February 19, 2023, Plaintiff Snyder and Defendant Dweller, LLC entered into the Acquisition and Release Agreement (Agreement) for the acquisition of the rights and interest in the film titled, Dweller. (Cardwell Decl., ¶ 1, Ex. A; FAC, ¶ 17, Ex. 1, ¶ 11.)

 

Pursuant to the Agreement, Plaintiff agreed to arbitrate any dispute. Specifically, the Agreement provides in relevant part:

 

“The parties agree that the laws of the State of California shall be applied in construing this Agreement and in enforcing the rights and remedies of Snyder and Buyer. In the event of a dispute between Snyder and Buyer, each party waives its right to file suit in a court of law and agrees that any dispute will be resolved through binding arbitration (in the County of Los Angeles) using the rules of the American Arbitration Association and applying California Law.” (Cardwell Decl., ¶¶ 1-2, Ex. A; FAC, ¶ 17, Ex. 1, ¶ 11.)

 

            “A party seeking arbitration must prove the existence of an agreement to arbitrate by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Hotels Nevada v. L.A. Pacific Center, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 754, 765.) It may meet its burden by complying with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1330, which provides: “A petition to compel arbitration ... pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.”

            Here, Defendants have met their burden of demonstrating the existence of an arbitration agreement.

 

Plaintiff does not dispute signing this Agreement, or that his claims fall within the scope of this Agreement.[1] Rather, in opposition, Plaintiff makes several arguments claiming the Agreement is unenforceable.

 

            First, Plaintiff argues – citing Mansouri v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 633 – that the motion to compel arbitration should be denied because Defendants failed to make a formal demand for arbitration prior to bringing this motion. This argument is not well taken. In Hyundai Amco America, Inc. v. S3H, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 572, the Court of Appeal clarified and distinguished the ruling in Mansouri – specifically explaining that the language in Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 governs and under this statute it was sufficient that a party filed a lawsuit rather than commencing arbitration proceedings as required by the agreement to establish a party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy. (Hyundai Amco America, Inc. v. S3H, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 572, 577.)

 

            Second, Plaintiff argues that the Agreement – and by extension the arbitration provision – has been rescinded. In support, Plaintiff cites Paragraphs 18 to 21, 27, and 102 to 108 of the First Amended Complaint (FAC).[2] The substance of these paragraphs is that Plaintiff never received any consideration for the Agreement such that Plaintiff validly rescinded the Agreement.

 

             Civil Code section 1689, subdivision (b) provides: “A party to a contract may rescind the contract in the following cases: ... [¶] ... [¶] (2) If the consideration for the obligation of the rescinding party fails, in whole or in part, through the fault of the party as to whom he rescinds.”

 

“If a contract includes an arbitration agreement, and grounds exist to revoke [or, more accurately, rescind] the entire contract, such grounds would also vitiate the arbitration agreement. Thus, if an otherwise enforceable arbitration agreement is contained in an illegal contract, a party may avoid arbitration altogether.” (Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 29–30.)

 

            Here, specifically, Plaintiff argues that he never received any monetary compensation in exchange for the Agreement and the film, Dweller, was never distributed in any capacity. (5/28/24 Synder Decl., ¶¶ 2-10.) As result, Plaintiff argues the Agreement demonstrates a failure of consideration.

 

The Agreement states:

 

“As consideration for Snyder relinquishing all Rights applicable to the Picture and for providing the applicable releases and representations; Buyer agrees to finance the cost of distribution of the Picture known as “Dweller”. Snyder acknowledges such Considerations are sufficient to compensate Snyder for his obligations herein.”

 

Plaintiff also alleges that the parties had an oral agreement that required “a theatrical release with a minimum guarantee of 750 screens nationwide in all of the major markets for “Dweller” (5/28/24 Synder Decl., ¶ 4.) This representation was made prior to the execution of the Agreement. (5/28/24 Synder Decl., ¶ 13 [questioning Cardwell why this theatrical release requirement was not included in the Agreement].)

 

            However, the failure of the film to be distributed does not clearly show a failure by Defendant to finance the distribution – as required by Civil Code section 1689. (Civ. Code, § 1689 [“through the fault of the party as to whom he rescinds”].) The evidence is unclear how a failure of financing occurred and only indicates that there was no distribution. Thus, the evidence before the Court on this motion – verified allegations and Synder’s vague declaration – are insufficient to show an adequate basis for rescission that would support rescission of the arbitration provision as well.

 

            Additionally, Plaintiff argues that he executed the Agreement under fraud and duress.

 

            Contrary to the reply, Plaintiff is correct that fraud in the inducement of a contract –where there are specific claims of fraud in the inducement with respect to the arbitration provision – is an issue to be resolved first by the Court.

 

            “California law distinguishes between fraud in the ‘execution’ or ‘inception’ of a contract and fraud in the ‘inducement’ of a contract ... in the former case ‘ “the fraud goes to the inception or execution of the agreement, so that the promisor is deceived as to the nature of his act, and actually does not know what he is signing, or does not intend to enter into a contract at all, mutual assent is lacking, and [the contract] is void. In such a case it may be disregarded without the necessity of rescission.” ’ ” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 415.) “[C]laims of fraud in the execution of the entire agreement are not arbitrable under either state or federal law. If the entire contract is void ab initio because of fraud, the parties have not agreed to arbitrate any controversy.” (Id. at p. 416.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff’s fraud arguments are based on the assertion that Cardwell – being a licensed attorney and in the position of power over the transaction – used excessive pressure and exerted his dominance in multiple occasions and specifically in demanding that Plaintiff not retain an attorney. (Snyder Declaration, ¶¶ 12-22; Thorne Decl., ¶¶ 4-12.) Such claims, however, are of the same general type our Supreme Court in Rosenthal held to be insufficient for fraud in the execution.

 

            “Fraud in the inducement, by contrast, occurs when ‘ “the promisor knows what he is signing but his consent is induced by fraud, mutual assent is present and a contract is formed, which, by reason of the fraud, is voidable. In order to escape from its obligations the aggrieved party must rescind.” ’ ” (Rosenthal, supra, 14 Cal.4th at p. 415.) “[F]raud in the inducement relating to other contractual terms does not render the arbitration agreement unenforceable, even when it might justify rescission of the contract as a whole. By entering into the arbitration agreement, the parties established their intent that disputes coming within the agreement's scope be determined by an arbitrator rather than a court; this contractual intent must be respected even with regard to claims of fraud in the inducement of the contract generally.” (Id. at p. 416.)

 

            However, as explained by the United States Supreme Court in Prima Paint Corp. v. Flood & Conklin Mfg. Co. (1967) 388 U.S. 395, “if the claim is fraud in the inducement of the arbitration clause itself—an issue which goes to the ‘making’ of the agreement to arbitrate—the federal court may proceed to adjudicate it. But the [Federal Arbitration Act] does not permit the federal court to consider claims of fraud in the inducement of the contract generally.” (Id. at pp. 403–404.)

 

Plaintiff submits evidence that he requested the removal of the arbitration agreement but Cardwell told him: “Arbitration clauses are ‘standard and necessary in all contracts in order for them to be valid.” James Cardwell also stated “because arbitration clauses are standard in all contracts, the arbitration clause must remain in the contract or there is no deal. Take it or leave it.” (5/28/24 Synder Decl., ¶ 13(d).) Plaintiff repeats that he was told that arbitration clauses were “mandatory in all contracts.” (5/28/24 Synder Decl., ¶ 13(e).)

 

This fraud argument defies credulity. As Plaintiff’s own pro per opposition papers demonstrate, Plaintiff is capable of conducting legal research and understanding the case law and statutes. Thus, simple research and – candidly – common sense would quickly refute Cardwell’s purported assertion that arbitration provisions are required in all contracts to be valid. To believe otherwise would mean that there could be no valid court claim for breach of contract where every contract must contain an arbitration provision and be arbitrated. Further, attached to the Complaint and FAC are numerous agreements between Plaintiff and other parties relating to Dweller over the years; some of these contracts (which appear to be drafted by Plaintiff) contain arbitration provisions and some do not. For example, the March 5, 2023 Copyright Assignment contract between Synder and Thorne does not contain an arbitration provision whereas the June 7, 2017 Writer Story Agreement sent by Synder to his writers did contain an arbitration provision.

 

Plaintiff is not an unsophisticated party and as such, this claim for fraud in the inducement is not based on reasonable reliance. As such, based on the evidence before this Court, the claim for fraud in the inducement of the arbitration provision is unpersuasive.

 

            Additionally, Plaintiff argues that he is an indigent litigant and therefore cannot be subject to arbitration. However, this argument – even if true – alone does not prohibit compelling this matter to arbitration.

 

            For example, in Roldan v. Callahan & Blaine (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 87, indigent plaintiffs were excused from paying fees for an ordered arbitration, because “to rule otherwise might effectively deprive them of access to any forum for resolution of their claims.” (Roldan, supra, 219 Cal.App.4th at p. 96.) The Roldan court stated that while it could not “order the arbitration forum to waive its fees” and did not “have authority to order [the defendants] to pay [the] plaintiffs’ share of those fees,” it did have authority to “give [the defendants] a choice: if the trial court determines that any of [the] plaintiffs is unable to share in the cost of the arbitration, [the defendants] can elect to either pay that plaintiff's share of the arbitration cost and remain in arbitration or waive [their] right to arbitrate that plaintiff's claim.” (Ibid.)

 

Roldan provides that a trial court should first estimate the anticipated cost of the previously ordered arbitration proceeding, and then determine whether any of the plaintiffs are financially able to pay their pro rata share of that cost. (Ibid.) If the trial court determines that any plaintiff is unable to do so, it should order that the defendant has the option of either paying that plaintiff's share of the arbitration cost or waiving the defendant's right to arbitrate that plaintiff’s case and allow the case to proceed in court. (Ibid.; see also Hang v. RG Legacy I, LLC, supra, 88 Cal.App.5th at pp. 1253-1255; Weiler v. Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Investment Services, Inc. (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 970.)

            Relevant to the issues here, Defendants assert that, “in the event that the court determines that Plaintiff is unable to pay the cost of arbitration, [Defendants] will agree, by declaration, to pay the costs of Plaintiff’s arbitration.” (6/3/2024 Response, 8:17-19.) Thus, Plaintiff’s alleged inability to pay is not a basis for denying this motion.

 

            Lastly, Plaintiff argues that the Agreement is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable.

 

            If a court finds as a matter of law that a contract or any clause of a contract is unconscionable, the court may refuse to enforce the contract or clause, or it may limit the application of any unconscionable clause so as to avoid any unconscionable result. (Civ. Code § 1670.5, subd. (a).) “An agreement to arbitrate, like any other contract, is subject to revocation if the agreement is unconscionable.” (Carmona v. Lincoln Millennium Car Wash, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 74, 83 [citing Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 98].)

 

            “The general principles of unconscionability are well established. A contract is unconscionable if one of the parties lacked a meaningful choice in deciding whether to agree and the contract contains terms that are unreasonably favorable to the other party. [Citation.] Under this standard, the unconscionability doctrine ‘ “has both a procedural and a substantive element.” ’ [Citation.] ‘The procedural element addresses the circumstances of contract negotiation and formation, focusing on oppression or surprise due to unequal bargaining power. [Citations.] Substantive unconscionability pertains to the fairness of an agreement's actual terms and to assessments of whether they are overly harsh or one-sided.’ [Citation.] [¶] Both procedural and substantive unconscionability must be shown for the defense to be established, but ‘they need not be present in the same degree.’ [Citation.] Instead, they are evaluated on ‘ “sliding scale.” ’ [Citation.] ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to’ conclude that the term is unenforceable. [Citation.] Conversely, the more deceptive or coercive the bargaining tactics employed, the less substantive unfairness is required. [Citations.] A contract's substantive fairness ‘must be considered in light of any procedural unconscionability’ in its making. [Citation.] ‘The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.’ ” (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 125–126.) “The burden of proving unconscionability rests upon the party asserting it.” (OTO, supra, 8 Cal.5th at p. 126.)

 

             Plaintiff argues that the contract was unilaterally drafted by Cardwell “where Snyder was never given an opportunity to negotiate or ask for better terms or consult with an attorney.” (Opp., 10: 18-20.) This argument is somewhat undermined by Synder’s own declaration where he states he questioned the arbitration provision and requested its removal suggesting he did have an opportunity to negotiate its terms even if those changes were not implemented. Given the nature of the Agreement – a film distribution agreement – the Court is not inclined to find the type of inherent procedural unconscionability found in a typical consumer and employment contracts. The facts and circumstances of this transaction do not support Plaintiff’s claim that he had “no reasonable alternative” but to enter into this agreement on the terms provided.

 

            Thus, Plaintiff has not identified any procedural unconscionability in the Agreement and Plaintiff’s unconscionability argument fails for this reason. (Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1243.) Nonetheless, the Court will address the substantive unconscionability argument, as well.

           

Here, Plaintiff also argues the Agreement is substantively unconscionable. However, Plaintiff identifies no specific provision in the arbitration provision that is substantively unconscionable. Rather, Plaintiff only repeats the argument that he is unable to pay for arbitration and that “other provisions within the Agreement also subjects it to substantive unconscionability” with identifying anything specific.

 

Plaintiff relies on Gutierrez v. Autowest, Inc. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 77 and Frausto v. Lawyers for Employee and Consumer Rights, APC (Cal. Ct. App., Feb. 17, 2021, No. B305183) 2021 WL 613146, at *1 to argue that a contract of adhesion that requires the consumer to post arbitration fees is unconscionable. However, Frausto v. Lawyers for Employee and Consumer Rights, APC is an unpublished case and thus not citable. Moreover, these cases are inapplicable where the contract at issue is not a consumer contract (or an employment contract).

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff has failed to meet his burden of showing the Agreement is unenforceable based on unconscionability.

CONCLUSION

 

            Accordingly, the Court grants Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration. The motion to compel arbitration is granted; the matter is stayed pending the outcome of arbitration.
