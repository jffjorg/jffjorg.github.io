DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01451    Hearing Date: April 17, 2024    Dept: W

YURIDIA LEDESMA, et al. v. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION ATTENDANCE OF A PERSON MOST QUALIFIED AND CUSTODIAN OF RECORDS OF DEFENDANT AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC., AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:        April 17, 2024                                                Trial Date:       June 10, 2024

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        22VECV01451

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Yuridia Ledesma and Ofelia Arambula

Responding Party:     Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On September 30, 2022, Plaintiffs Yuridia Ledesma and Ofelia Arambula filed a complaint against Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. for violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty. Plaintiffs entered into a warranty contract with American Honda Motor Co., Inc. regarding a 2020 Honda Accord. Plaintiff alleges Defects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves including but not limited to exterior, electrical, engine, and steering.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified and Custodian of Records of Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiffs Yuridia Ledesma and Ofelia Arambula move this court, pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(a), for an order compelling Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. to produce a Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian of Records to be deposed in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.230. Plaintiffs move this court for an order awarding monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record, Bowman and Brooke LLP for Defendant’s willful and intentional failure to comply with the relevant code sections of the Discovery Act. Plaintiffs move for an order for sanctions in the amount of $1,830.00.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2025.450, if after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action, without having served a valid objection, fails to appear for examination, or proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document. (CCP § 2025.450(a).) A motion to compel the deposition of a party to the action must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, or, when the deponent failed to attend the deposition, a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(2).)

 

Plaintiffs argue even though Plaintiffs have served a notice and sent out several written communications in an effort to meet and confer, Defendant has and continues to fail to produce its PMQ. Additionally, Plaintiffs attempted to meet and confer before filing this motion and made numerous efforts to informally resolve this issue but no alternative dates have still not been provided.

 

In opposition, Defendant AHM contends it is not refusing to provide a corporate witness in this matter. Rather, despite Plaintiffs’ belief that AHM has unlimited capacity to produce witnesses on unilaterally noticed deposition dates, defense counsel has indicated on multiple occasions that AHM has limited witness resources available due to the number of filings by Plaintiffs’ counsel for depositions of AHM’s person(s) most qualified in Song-Beverly type cases. Moreover, as recently as March 28, 2024, AHM has offered an available date for the deposition of AHM’s person most qualified on May 17, 2024. (Hancox Decl. ¶12.)

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to compel deposition of Defendants’ PMQ. Both sides agree that that Defendant’s PMQ should be deposed. 

 

The parties are ordered to meet and confer regarding availability. The deposition date is to be no earlier than sixty days from the date of this motion and no more than 120 days.

 

The request for sanctions is DENIED. The court will revisit the issue of sanctions if the plaintiff is not able to take the PMQ’s deposition within 120 days.


Case Number: 22VECV02132    Hearing Date: April 17, 2024    Dept: W

SUZANNE M. CURTIS v. TRANSAMERICA PREMIER LIFE INSURANCE COMPANY, et al.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERSTION

 

Date of Hearing:        April 17, 2024                                     Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        22VECV02132

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Suzanne M. Curtis

Responding Party:     Defendants Transamerica Life Insurance Company and Transamerica Premier Life Insurance Company n/k/a Transamerica Life Insurance Company

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 23, 2022, Plaintiff Suzanne M. Curtis filed a complaint against Defendants AIG American General Assurance Company, American General Assurance Company, American International Group, Inc., Transamerica Life Insurance Company, Transamerica Premier Life Insurance Company, L. Dorais, and Brian Forsyth for breach of contract and fraud.

 

Plaintiff dismissed AIG American General Assurance Company on January 17, 2023.  

 

Defendants Transamerica Life Ins. Co and Transamerica Premier Life Insurance company demurred to the complaint on statute of limitations grounds and that demurrer was sustained without leave to amend on March 1, 2024.  By minute order that same date, both defendants were dismissed.   On March 11, 2024, plaintiff filed an ex parte application to have a motion for reconsideration heard, which was heard on March 11 and the motion set for hearing on April 17, 2024.  The court treated the ex parte application as the moving papers.   Defendants filed a timely opposition.  Plaintiff filed a document entitled a Motion for Reconsideration on April 10, 2024, which was in fact a reply brief.  

 

Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied. 

 

discussion

 

Defendants first argue that the motion for reconsideration must be denied because the order of dismissal following the sustaining of a demurrer without leave to amend is a

final judgment. See Desai v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1115 (order

dismissing defendant following an order sustaining that defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend is a final judgment); see also Cal Code of Civ. Proc. § 581(d) (“All dismissals ordered by the court shall be in the form of a written order signed by the court and filed in the action and those orders when so filed shall constitute judgments and be effective for all purposes….”).  Because the court did not sign the order of dismissal and no judgment was ever entered, the court finds this motion for reconsideration properly before the court.  

 

Nonetheless, the motion must be denied on its merits.   Code of Civil Procedure § 1008(a) “requires that a motion for reconsideration be based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212, citing Civ. Proc. Code § 1008(a).)  A declaration setting forth the new or different facts, circumstances or law must be filed in support of the motion. CCP § 1008(a); Branner v. Regents of Univ. of Calif. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1043, 1048 (“motion to reconsider was invalid when filed and served because the motion failed to contain an affidavit or declaration in support of the motion.”).  Although plaintiff did file a declaration with her original ex parte application, that declaration did not set forth any new or different facts, circumstances or law.   Instead, plaintiff merely sought to reargue the original demurrer and to complain that she believed she had been given inadequate time to argue her position at the hearing on the demurrer.  A party’s mere disagreement with the court's order or argument that the court misinterpreted the law or the facts does not justify a motion for reconsideration:

 

What respondent essentially argues is that section 1008 does not apply when the litigant disagrees with the trial court's ruling. Since in almost all instances, the losing party will believe that the trial court's “different” interpretation of the law or facts was erroneous, to interpret the statute as the respondent urges would be contrary to the clear legislative intent to restrict motions to reconsider to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or authority that was not previously considered by it.

 

(Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500, as modified on denial of reh'g (Apr. 3, 1995).

 

Accordingly, plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied.  Defendants to submit a judgment of dismissal within 10 days. 

 


Case Number: 23VECV01079    Hearing Date: April 17, 2024    Dept: W

ELLEN S. DURANT, et al. v. CONSTANCE ADLER GALLOWAY, et al.

 

Defendant CONSTANCE ADLER GALLOWAY’s motion to quash service of summons and complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        April 17, 2024                                    Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01079

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Constance Adler Galloway

Responding Party:     None

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 10, 2023, Plaintiffs Ellen S. Durant and David E. Durant filed a complaint against Defendants Constance Adler Galloway, Hanibal Design Carlisle, and Urban Bilial Carlisle for breach of contract, intentional misrepresentation, and financial elder abuse.  Plaintiffs filed an amended complaint on January 19, 2024.  Plaintiffs allege Ellen, who is 89 years old, purchased a 3-bedroom condominium. David is Ellen’s son. Plaintiffs further allege Constance befriended Ellen and Ellen considered Constance like a daughter. Constance informed Ellen she could act as a property manager for the condominium. Plaintiffs allege in 2020, Constance signed a lease with Defendants Hanibal and Urban to rent one of the bedrooms in the condominium. In 2021, Plaintiffs decided to sell the property. Shortly thereafter, the friendship between Ellen and Constance deteriorated. Plaintiffs allege then Constance orchestrated a police action, where police disrupted Ellen’s convalescence and quiet enjoyment of the Property. Moreover, Constance accepted rent from Defendants Hanibal and Urban against Plaintiffs’ wishes. Plaintiffs wired $9,000 to Defendants Hanibal and Urban to have them vacate the property; however, Hanibal and Urban have failed to vacate the property.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Constance Adler Galloway’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Constance Adler Galloway moves the court to quash service of summons and complaint on the grounds Defendant was not properly served.

 

“[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.]” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.) “[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper” but only if it “complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.” (Id. at 1441-1442.) When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) “A court lacks jurisdiction over a party if there has not been proper service of process.” (See Ruttenberg v. Ruttenberg (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 801, 808.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs will not be able to meet their burden that service of process was effectuated against Defendant Galloway pursuant to statute. This court granted Defendant’s motion to vacate default when Plaintiffs attempted service at Defendant’s daughter’s residence. Pursuant to the order and the demurrer on the complaint heard the same day, Plaintiffs had 120 days to file an amended complaint and remedy service of process on Defendant Galloway properly. However, Plaintiffs served the FAC on Defendant via email while the undersigned just had open-heart surgery in the hospital.

 

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to quash service of summons. To properly serve a defendant, there are four methods for serving a summons within the State of California: (1) personal service – Code Civ. Proc. §415.10; (2) substitute service – Code Civ. Proc. §415.20; (3) mail service – Code Civ. Proc. §415.30; and (4) service by publication – Code Civ. Proc. §415.50. Plaintiffs did not serve the summons and complaint via one of the established four ways.

 

Accordingly, the motion to quash service of summons and complaint is GRANTED.
