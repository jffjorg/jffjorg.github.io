DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV19816    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: B

HEARING DATE: Wed., Oct. 23, 2024 JUDGE/DEPT: Moskowitz/Dep. B

CASE NAME: ABDUL THOMPSON, et al. vs. ALEJANDRO BERNAL BENGE

CASE NUMBER: 21STCV19816

PROCEEDINGS: EXPEDITED PETITION FOR APPROVAL OF MINOR’S COMPROMISE

MOVING PARTY: Petitioner Abdul Thompson, on behalf of minor Claimant Dale Thompson

RESP. PARTY: None

EXPEDITED PETITION FOR APPROVAL OF

MINOR’S COMPROMISE OF A DISPUTED CLAIM

(CCP § 372, CRC, rules 7.950, 7.950.5)

TENTATIVE RULING:

The Expedited Petition filed on behalf of minor Claimant Dale Thomspon is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

I. Background

On May 26, 2021, Plaintiff Dale Thompson (“Claimant”) by and through his guardian ad

litem Abdul Thompson (“Petitioner”) filed an action for general and motor vehicle negligence against Defendant Alejandro Bernal Benge (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 15. Petitioner was appointed as guardian ad litem for Claimant on November 19, 2021.

On March 21, 2023, Petitioner filed a Notice of Settlement.

On June 5, 2024, the matter was assigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East. Petitioner was ordered to give notice to all parties in this action within 10 days of service of this notice and file proof of service thereof within 12 days of this notice. The Court notes that Petitioner has failed to file proof of service of the Notice of Case Reassignment.

On June 5, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Expedited Petition to Approve Minor’s Compromise of Disputed Claim on behalf of Claimant.

II. Legal Standard

Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim. (Prob. Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; CCP § 372.) “‘[W]ithout trial court approval of the proposed compromise of the ward’s claim, the settlement cannot be valid. [Citation.] Nor is the settlement binding [on the

minor] until it is endorsed by the trial court.’” (Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.) A minor, like Claimant, “shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.” (CCP § 372(a)(1).) Alternatively, the petitioner may file a declaration demonstrating that he or she has a right to compromise the minor’s claim under Cal. Probate Code § 3500.

Regarding the substance of the Petition, to obtain court approval of the settlement of a minor’s claims, the petitioner must file a complete and “verified petition for approval of the settlement and must disclose ‘all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise.’ [Citations.]” (Barnes v. Western Heritage Ins. Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 249, 256; CRC Rule 7.950.) (Italics added.)

Under CRC Rule 7.950.5, an expedited petition for minor’s compromise without a scheduled hearing is permitted as long as Petitioner uses the required Judicial Council forms and meets certain additional conditions.

III. Discussion & Conclusion

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive

$4,000. $1,000 in attorney’s fees will be deducted from Claimant’s settlement amount. (MC-350EX Item 17c.)

The fee agreement between Petitioner and Petitioner’s counsel is reasonable as it represents a 25% contingency fee of the proposed $4,000 settlement. (MC-350EX Attachment 14a.) However, Petitioner does not satisfy CRC 7.950.5 because they fail to disclose information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise. Notably, Petitioner and Alaysha Thompson are to receive $13,000 and $5,000, respectively. Petitioner provides no declaration or other admissible evidence pertaining to the reasonableness of Claimant’s settlement amount in relation to theirs.

Accordingly, the Expedited Petition filed on behalf of minor Claimant Abdul Thompson is DENIED and this hearing is continued to [date].

Case Number: 22STCV05398    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: B

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

JERRY LEE DORRIS, AN INDIVIDUAL, Plaintiff, vs. FOOD 4 LESS OF CALIFORNIA, A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, et al., Defendants. ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 22STCV05398 [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: FOOD 4 LESS’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT Dept. B DATE: OCTOBER 23, 2024 TIME: 8:30 A.M. COMPLAINT FILED: FEBRUARY 14, 2022 TRIAL DATE: FEBRUARY 10, 2025

I. BACKGROUND

On February 14, 2022, Plaintiff Jerry Lee Dorris (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Food 4 Less of California, Inc. (“Food 4 Less”), the Kroger Company (“Kroger”), and Does 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) general negligence; and (2) premises liability. The complaint arises from a March 9, 2020, incident where Plaintiff allegedly sustained serious injuries when they slipped and fell on an unknown substance while on Defendants’ premises.

On June 5, 2024, the matter was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On August 5, 2024, Food 4 Less filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On October 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

On October 17, 2024, Food 4 Less filed their reply.

II. MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Food 4 Less moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint because Defendant alleges they did not have notice, actual or constructive, of the dangerous condition that caused Plaintiff’s injuries. (Notice of Motion, p. 2: 9-12.) Therefore, Food 4 Less argues that they do not owe Plaintiff a duty as a matter of law. (Ibid.)

III. LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).)

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to

show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [it is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) “The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.)

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence.

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections

On October 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed evidentiary objections to various portions of the declaration of Alondra Carcamo (“Carcamo”), the store leader at the time and location of Plaintiff’s alleged injuries.

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections are sustained as to No. 9. No. 9 properly objects to Food 4 Less’s Exh. G, the Sweeps Floor Inspection Report, dated March 9, 2020, which lacks authentication. (Cal. Evid. Code § 1414.)

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections are overruled as to Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, 11, and 12. These objections largely rely on Carcamo’s lack of foundation or personal knowledge. However, as store leader of the location where Plaintiff was injured, and as responding management to Plaintiff’s injuries at the time they occurred, Carcamo demonstrates sufficient foundation and personal knowledge of relevant store policies and procedures.

Defendant’s Burden

Food 4 Less meets their initial burden.

Food 4 Less moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint because Defendant alleges they did not have notice, actual or constructive, of the dangerous condition that caused Plaintiff’s injuries. (Notice of Motion, p. 2: 9-12.) Therefore, Food 4 Less argues that they do not owe Plaintiff a duty as a matter of law. (Ibid.)

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence:¿duty, breach, causation, and damages.¿ (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)¿Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.¿(Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)¿If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition reasonably safe for [customers’] use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to

avoid the harm.”¿(Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)¿The existence and scope of a property owner’s duty are legal questions for the court.¿(Annocki, supra, 232 Cal.App.4th¿ at p. 36.)¿

“Because the owner is not the insurer of the visitor’s personal safety . . . the owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is a key to establishing its liability.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206.) “[W]here the plaintiff relies on the failure to correct a dangerous condition to prove the owner’s negligence, the plaintiff has the burden of showing that the owner had [actual or constructive] notice of the defect in sufficient time to correct it.” (Ibid.) To establish liability for negligence, “[t]here must be some evidence . . . to support the conclusion that the condition had existed long enough for the proprietor, in the exercise of reasonable care, to have discovered and remedied it.” (Girvetz v. Boys’ Market (1949) 91 Cal.App.2d 827, 829; Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1206.)

Plaintiff alleges that he sustained injuries when he slipped and fell on Defendant’s premises. (Food 4 Less Separate Statement of Facts “F-SSF” Nos. 1-3.) Video surveillance shows that, at approximately 2:58 p.m. on the day of the incident, an unidentified customer drops a grocery item on the ground. (Ibid. at No. 10; Exh. E [video surveillance].) This action resulted in the spilled spaghetti sauce which Plaintiff slipped on. (Ibid. at No. 11; Exh. E.) Approximately five minutes later, at 3:03 p.m., Plaintiff comes into view of the camera and slips on the spilled sauce. (Ibid. at Nos. 12 and 13; Exh. E.) Video surveillance shows that there were no employees at or near the area to observe the creation of the condition which caused Plaintiff’s incident five minutes later. (Ibid. at No. 14; Exh. E.) The video also shows that no employees passed through the area during the interim five minutes. (Ibid. at No. 15; Exh. E.) On the day of the incident, Food 4 Less was not informed by any employee or customer that the subject area needed

cleaning or care. (Ibid. at No. 16; Carcamo Decl. ¶ 8.) Food 4 Less only learned of the incident after Carcamo responded to Plaintiff’s incident. (Ibid. at No. 17; Carcamo Decl. ¶ 9.)

Furthermore, Food 4 Less provides evidence that the subject aisle was sufficiently inspected and cleared of any debris 45 minutes prior to Plaintiff’s incident. The video evidence shows store employee Antonio Dominguez sweeping the subject aisle at 2:13 p.m. and leaving it clear of any foreign substances. (Carcamo Decl. ¶¶ 6b. and 6c.) Store policy dictates that courtesy clerks complete sweeps of the store every 60 minutes. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)

Based on the foregoing, Food 4 Less provides sufficient evidence demonstrating a lack of actual or constructive notice of the spill which caused Plaintiff to slip. Video surveillance shows that the dangerous condition only existed for five minutes before Plaintiff’s incident. Furthermore, video surveillance shows that no employees were at or near the dangerous condition before Plaintiff slipped.

Accordingly, Food 4 Less meets its initial burden. The burden now shifts to Plaintiff.

Plaintiff’s Burden

In opposition, Plaintiff presents three main arguments: (1) Food 4 Less has only provided video from one camera angle, suggesting Food 4 Less is hiding evidence (see Plaintiff’s Response to F-SSF “PR-F-SSF" No. 14); (2) the video shows a person who “appears to be an employee” and who appears to look down the aisle with the spill (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts “P-SSF” Nos. 19 and 20); (3) Food 4 Less should have been inspecting the aisles every 10 or 15 minutes; and (4) manipulation of the video cannot be “ruled out.” (P-SSF No. 31.)

Plaintiff fails to meet their burden.

Plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Consumer Cause, Inc., supra, 91

Cal.App.4th at 468.) Here, all four of Plaintiff’s arguments rely on allegations without specific factual support. Plaintiff claims Food 4 Less might have more camera angles of the incident but provides no facts suggesting Food 4 Less’s deliberate efforts to conceal such evidence during discovery. Plaintiff also fails to address the future possibility of obtaining such evidence. There is no evidence suggesting the person who glances down the aisle is a Food 4 Less employee, as Plaintiff claims. Plaintiff’s claim that it’s industry standard to inspect aisles every 10 or 15 minutes is purely conjecture. (See P-SSF Nos. 16 and 17.) Finally, Plaintiff admits that manipulation of the video evidence “cannot be proven.” (P-SSF No. 31.) Therefore, Plaintiff solely opposes this motion with allegations and speculation. “If the plaintiff cannot [set forth specific facts], summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467.)

V. CONCLUSION

No triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Food 4 Less had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

TENTATIVE RULING

NOAH CVENGROS, vs. SAM TUCKER. Case No.: 22STCV14538 Hearing Date: OCTOBER 23, 2024

Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club’s motion to disqualify counsel is granted.

Background

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff Noah Cvengros (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Sam Tucker (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10 alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence.

On April 18, 2024, Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club (“Interinsurance”) filed an application to intervene. It was granted on April 19, 2024.

On June 6, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B in Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On September 4, 2024, Interinsurance brought the instant motion to disqualify Christpher L. Rudd as counsel for any party in this action.

On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

On October 16, 2024, Interinsurance filed their reply.

Discussion

Interinsurance moves to disqualify Christopher L. Rudd as counsel for any party in this action.

Cal. Code of Civ. Procedure § 128(a)(5) states that “[e]very court shall have the power . . . to control in furtherance of justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto.” (CCP § 128(a)(5).) “‘A trial court's authority to disqualify an attorney derives from the power inherent in every court [t]o control in furtherance of

justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto. [Citations.]’ [citation] ‘. . . The paramount concern must be to preserve public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar. The important right to counsel of one's choice must yield to ethical considerations that affect the fundamental principles of our judicial process.’ [citation]” (Kirk v. First American Title Ins. Co. (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 776, 791-792.)

In ruling on a motion to disqualify, the court should weigh: (1) the party's right to counsel of choice; (2) the attorney's interest in representing a client; (3) the financial burden on a client of change of counsel; (4) any tactical abuse underlying a disqualification motion; and (5) the principal that the fair resolution of disputes requires vigorous representation of parties by independent counsel. (Mills Land & Water Co. v. Golden West Refining Co. (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 116, 126.) Whether an attorney should be disqualified is a matter addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court. (Henriksen v. Great American Savings & Loan (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 109, 113.)

Interinsurance moves to disqualify Attorney Rudd on the basis that counsel represents both Plaintiff and Defendant, constituting a nonwaivable conflict of interest pursuant to Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct (“RPC”) Rules 1.7(a) and 1.7(d). RPC 1.7(a) and 1.7(d) state as follows:

“(a) A lawyer shall not, without informed written consent from each client and compliance with paragraph (d), represent a client if the representation is directly adverse to another client in the same or a separate matter.

. . .

(d) Representation is permitted under this rule only if the lawyer complies with paragraphs (a), (b), and (c), and . . . (3) the representation does not involve the assertion of a claim by one client against another client represented by the lawyer in the same litigation or other proceeding before a tribunal.”

RPC Rules 1.7(a) and (d)(emphasis added).

Attorney Rudd holds himself out as counsel for both Plaintiff and Defendant in this action. (See Motion Exh. F [Rudd’s January 2, 2024, letter, claiming representation of both].) Therefore, Attorney Rudd is in direct violation of RPC Rule 1.7(d).

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Interinsurance (1) has no standing to bring the instant motion and (2) its delay in doing so unduly prejudices Plaintiff. However, interinsurance has proper standing. “[W]here an attorney’s continued representation threatens an opposing litigant with cognizable injury or would undermine the integrity of the judicial process, the trial court may grant a motion for disqualification, regardless of whether a motion is brought by a present or former client of recused counsel.” (Kennedy v. Eldridge (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1197, 1205.) Here, Interinsurance would be unduly prejudiced by Attorney Rudd’s representation of both Plaintiff and its insured, giving Interinsurance standing to bring this motion. Even if that was not the case, Attorney

Rudd’s direct violation of RPC Rule 1.7(d) undermines the integrity of the judicial process, warranting disqualification. Furthermore, Interinsurance filed this motion less than four months after entering the suit as an intervening party. Either way, untimeliness is not a defense to disqualification pursuant to RPC 1.7(d).

Accordingly, Interinsurance’s Motion to Disqualify Counsel must be granted.

CONCLUSION

Interinsurance’s Motion to Disqualify Counsel is GRANTED.

Moving Party to give notice.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Re: 22STCV18770 Joselyn Espinoza vs Carlos Pizza Food Services, Inc., et al.

Plaintiff Joselyn Espinoza’s Request for Default Judgment

Defendants: Carlos Pizza Food Services, Inc.

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Joselyn Espinoza’s request for default judgment against Defendant Carlos Pizza Food Services, Inc. is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a May 15, 2021, assault in which Plaintiff Joselyn Espinoza (“Plaintiff”) alleges an employee of Defendant Carlos Pizza Food Services, Inc. (“Defendant”) pushed Plaintiff, a lawful patron of Defendant, to the ground and punched her in the face, causing Plaintiff injury.

On June 8, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Carlos Pizza Food Services, Inc. (“Defendant”), Marian de Jesus (“de Jesus”), and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”), alleging six causes of action: (1) assault; (2) battery; (3) premises liability; (4) negligence; (5) negligent supervision; and (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On July 1, 2024, Defendant entered default.

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendant.

LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules

of Court, rule 3.1800.)

ANALYSIS

Plaintiff seeks monetary damages against Defendant in the amount of $85,989.50, representing $85,000 in general and special damages and $989.50 in costs.

Plaintiff’s request is denied for the following deficiencies:

1. Plaintiff’s general damages are 7.5x special damages. This ratio is unreasonably high as general damages are usually limited to a maximum of 5x special damages.

2. Plaintiff fails to provide prima facie evidence supporting the amount of damages sought. While Plaintiff provides a recommendation for future surgery and ongoing therapy, they fail to provide any estimates of said treatments. (See Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361.)

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment has now been denied for deficiencies twice. Accordingly, the Court will set an OSC re: Dismissal for failure to timely obtain default judgment. Plaintiff is put on notice that further deficient filings risk dismissal pursuant to Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 583.410 and CRC 3.110(h).

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Defendant Carlos Pizza Food Services, Inc. is DENIED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
