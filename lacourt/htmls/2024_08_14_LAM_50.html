DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV23520    Hearing Date: August 14, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

c&m investment group, ltd.,  

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

HARVEY CHAMPLIN,

                        Defendant.

Case No.:

21STCV23520

Hearing Date:

August 14, 2024

Hearing Time:

8:30 a.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR TRIAL SETTING PREFERENCE PURSUANT TO CAL. CIV. PROC. CODE § 36

 

 

Background

Plaintiffs C&M Investment Group, Ltd. and Karlin Holdings Limited Partnership (jointly, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action on June 23, 2021 against Defendant Harvey Champlin (“Defendant”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) intentional interference with contractual relations and (2) intentional interference with prospective economic benefit.

Trial in this matter is currently set for September 25, 2024.

Defendant now moves for trial setting preference. Plaintiffs oppose. 

Discussion

Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (a) provides as follows:

“(a) A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings:

(1) The party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole.

(2) The health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.”

If the Court makes these findings, trial preference is mandatory–the Court lacks discretion to deny the relief. (Koch-Ash v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 689, 692.) The purpose of section 36, subdivision (a) is “to safeguard litigants beyond a specified age against the legislatively acknowledged risk that death or incapacity might deprive them of the opportunity to have their case effectively tried and the opportunity to recover their just measure of damages or appropriate redress.” (Kline v. Superior Court (1991) 227 Cal.App.3d 512, 515.) Upon the granting of a motion for trial preference, “the court shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (f).) 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36.5, “[a]n affidavit submitted in support of a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36 may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party. The affidavit is not admissible for any purpose other than a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36.”

In the motion, Defendant asserts that he is “entitled to trial preference under CCP § 36(a) based on his age and deteriorating health.” (Mot. at p. 3:20-21.) Defendant states that he is currently 80 years old. (Champlin Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant asserts that his “advanced age and serious medical comorbidities are such that preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing his interest in the litigation.” (Mot. at p. 5:6-7.) Defendant’s counsel states in his supporting declaration that “[b]ased on my discussions and experiences with Champlin and review of medical documentation, I am informed and believe that he has been diagnosed with Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (‘COPD’) and utilizes a pacemaker for management of cardiac issues.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶ 3.)

Defendant’s counsel states that “COPD is a progressive respiratory condition that impairs function of the lungs, leading to difficulty breathing, wheezing, fatigue, chronic bronchitis, and irreversible damage to the airways. COPD worsens over time due to irreversible damage to the airways and lungs and is exacerbated by the aging process. COPD also poses an increased risk of developing heart disease.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendant’s counsel further states that “Champlin’s advanced age and medical conditions pose an increased risk of exacerbation and complications associated with COPD and cardiac issues. For instance, Champlin had a COPD attack during his deposition on September 27, 2022, requiring the proceeding to be immediately halted and resumed the next day.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶ 5.)

Defendant’s counsel states that “[a]s Champlin ages and his health declines, his ability to participate in physical activities, including attending lengthy court proceedings for which he is the sole named defendant, will be impaired…Given Champlin’s age and health comorbidities, there is an increased likelihood of hospitalization and, even worse, death as time progresses.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.) Defendant’s counsel states that “Champlin is an immunocompromised individual who is at greater risk of illness given the COVID-19 pandemic.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶ 8.)

Defendant asserts that “[a]s the sole named Defendant in this action, it is undisputed Champlin has a substantial interest in this action as a whole.” (Mot. at p. 3:2-3.) Defendant further asserts that “[t]he Court should grant Champlin preferential status with respect to the existing September 25, 2024 trial date.” (Mot. at p. 6:8-9.)

In the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that “to qualify for preference under § 36(a), [Defendant] must show that his health is ‘such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing [his] interest in the litigation.’ Mr. Champlin does not carry this burden.” (Opp’n at p. 1:3-5.) Plaintiffs argue that “[a]part from comments about the nature of COPD, Mr. Jenkins provides no facts indicating that Mr. Champlin’s conceded health issue would impose any prejudice to schedule the trial in a way that also accommodates the schedule of Plaintiffs’ principal.” (Opp’n at p. 1:9-11.)

But as discussed, Defendant’s counsel states that “[g]iven Champlin’s age and health comorbidities, there is an increased likelihood of hospitalization and, even worse, death as time progresses.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶ 7.) Defendant’s counsel states that “[a]s Champlin ages and his health declines, his ability to participate in physical activities, including attending lengthy court proceedings for which he is the sole named defendant, will be impaired.” (Jenkins Decl., ¶ 6.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendant has shown that he is over 70 years of age, that he has a substantial interest in the action as a whole, and that his health “is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing [Defendant’s] interest in the litigation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).) Thus, the Court grants Defendant’s motion for trial setting preference.

Conclusion 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for trial setting preference is granted. As discussed, trial is currently set for September 25, 2024, which is within 120 days of the date of this Order.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this Order.

Background

Plaintiff Siranush Akherdyan (“Akherdyan”) filed this action on April 25, 2023 against Defendants Masis Tamazyan (“Tamazyan”) and Elen Tahmasian (“Tahmasian”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) declaratory relief re: real property resulting trust and (2) “in the alternative,” declaratory relief re: deed of trust priority.

On July 7, 2023, Tahmasian filed a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendants Siranush Akherdyan, a.k.a Siranush Akhverdyan, Soorage Tomasian, and Tamazyan. The Cross-Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) fraudulent conveyance, (2) quiet title, (3) cancellation of instrument, (4) negligence, and (5) declaratory relief.  

Tamazyan and Tahmasian (jointly, the “Moving Parties”) now move for judgment on the pleadings as to both causes of action of Akherdyan’s Complaint. Akherdyan opposes.

Discussion

As an initial matter, the Court notes that the Moving Parties’ counsel’s declaration filed in support of the motion provides, inter alia, that “[o]n May 23, 2024, I sent a letter to Plaintiff’s attorney, Manuel Duran, identifying deficiencies in the Complaint filed by Plaintiff Siranush Akherdyan. In that letter, I outlined the reasons why the Complaint fails to state a viable cause of action against my clients, including the applicability of the Statute of Frauds and the failure of Plaintiff’s allegations regarding a deed of trust to establish ownership of the property at issue…To date, I have not received any response from Mr. Duran indicating that Plaintiff will amend or dismiss her Complaint to address these deficiencies.” (Kakoian Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.)

Mr. Kakoian’s declaration does not demonstrate that the parties met and conferred by telephone, in person, or by video conference regarding the Moving Parties’ motion for judgment on the pleadings.

 Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 439, subdivision (a), “[b]efore filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings. If an amended pleading is filed, the responding party shall meet and confer again with the party who filed the amended pleading before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings against the amended pleading.” (Emphasis added.) Such meeting and conferring must be done in good faith with an effort to try to resolve the issues subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings.
