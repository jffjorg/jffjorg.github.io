DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV20321    Hearing Date: April 9, 2024    Dept: B

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Thelidria Calhoun, through her successor in interest Dimitria Calhoun

(1)   Demurrer to FAC

(2)   Motion to Strike

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The demurrer to the First Cause of Action in the FAC is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  The demurrer to the Third and Fourth Causes of Action are SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.  

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 78 and prayer at 2 and 4.  The motion to strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 95 and prayer at 8.

BACKGROUND

On May 28, 2021, plaintiff Thelidria Calhoun, in and through her successor-in-interest Dimitria Calhoun, filed a complaint against Torrance Memorial Medical Center, Transitional Care Unit, Kindred Hospital Southbay, and Memorial Hospital of Gardena for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) gross negligence, (4) corporate negligence, (5) willful misconduct, (6) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, and (7) wrongful death.

On November 14, 2022, at the FSC, the court noted that there was no proof that defendants had been served.  The minute order reflects that there being no appearances by or for either side, nor communication with the court as to why there are no appearances by counsel or parties, the court ordered that the FSC be taken off calendar/vacated.  The court stated further that if there is no appearance at the trial scheduled for November 28, 2022, the court may dismiss the case pursuant to CCP §581(b)(3).

On November 28, 2022, on the trial date, there were no appearances, and the court ordered the case dismissed without prejudice.

On May 17, 2023, the case was transferred from the PI Hub to Dept. M.

On June 30, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s motion to vacate and set aside the dismissal.

On September 25, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) willful misconduct, (4) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, and (5) wrongful death.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center demurs to the First, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and plaintiff lacks standing.

The FAC alleges that Thelidria Calhoun was born in 1935.  FAC, ¶17.  On December 5, 2017, Thelidria was admitted to Torrance Memorial Medical Center due to shortness of breath and pneumonia.  On December 8, 2017, she was moved to ICU.  Id., ¶22.  Her condition continued to deteriorate during her stay at Torrance Memorial to March 2018, when she was transferred to Transitional Care Unit (“TCU”).  She was released from TCU on October 23, 2018, even though she was not stable.  She was extremely weak and when she was taken to her bedroom the attendants did something that hurt her neck.  She continued to decline and was readmitted to Torrance Memorial in December 2018.  Id., ¶23. 

The FAC further alleges that on January 27, 2019, Thelidria was transferred to Kindred for rehabilitation of her hip and to help wean her off her ventilator.  During a cardiology consultation on February 25, 2019, it was noted that she had “a recent cervical neck fracture, multiple orthopedic fractures, malnutrition, interstitial lung disease, acute on chronic respiratory failure status post tracheostomy with trach collar, decubitus wound with wound Vac.”  She required frequent repositioning every two hours for pressure relief on the decubitus wounds she suffered.  Id., ¶28.  On March 5, 2019, she was taken to Memorial Hospital of Gardena after 911 was called.  Id., ¶29.  On April 1, 2019, Thelidria’s daughter Dimitria Calhoun demanded a meeting with the CEO of defendant Kindred and that Thelidria be transferred to Kindred Orange, but they refused to do so.  She informed the CEO and other department heads that Thelidria was not being turned, that she had severe bed sores, that she looked swollen, and that her GI tube was leaking, but the treatment stayed the same.  By April 30, 2019, she had developed an infection and was taken to ICU.  Id., ¶31.  By May 12, 2019, Kindred’s nursing staff indicated that Thelidria’s pressure ulcer had yellow slough and drainage, indicating infection.  Id., ¶32.  On May 15, 2019, Kindred completed a wound care consult that indicated that the two scattered Stage 4 pressure ulcers progressed to being unstageable.  Id., ¶33.  She died on June 1, 2019 from septic shock secondary to the large, infected, necrotic sacral decubitus ulcer.  Id., ¶45.

            Standing

Defendant argues that plaintiff does not have standing as to the first four causes of action because she has not filed a declaration under CCP §377.32.  The court notes that plaintiff filed a declaration pursuant to CCP §377.32 on March 26, 2024.

 

            First Cause of Action for Elder Abuse

In order to state a cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act, “a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.” Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 789.   There must be a showing of recklessness, fraud, malice, or oppression.  Id. at 783.

“[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.”  Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407 (citing Covenant Care).  “Allegations of misconduct directed against one defendant . . . do not state a cause of action against another defendant against whom the allegations of misconduct are not directed.”  Id. at 410 (citation omitted).  “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence.”  Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal. App. 4th 336, 347 (citation omitted).  Citing to the content of the definition of “neglect” in Welf. and Inst. Code §15610.57(b), the Supreme Court stated, “[w]hat they each seem to contemplate is the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder’s basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.”  Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148, 58.  “What seems beyond doubt is that the Legislature enacted a scheme distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together – claims of professional negligence and neglect.”  Id. at 159.

The FAC alleges that upon admission to Torrance Memorial on December 8, 2017, Thelidria’s skin was clear of any breakdown and she did not have any preexisting pressure ulcers.  FAC, ¶26.  A Braden Skin Risk assessment should have been done as the score would have alerted the nursing staff, indicating that she was at risk of developing pressure ulcers.  Between December 8 and January 18, 2018, she was in ICU and on a ventilator.  She developed an infection in her GI track.  She was unable to eat and on December 30, 2017, she developed an abscess on her right side.  She was not being turned every two hours and this caused bed sores.  By January 30, 2018, she had to have a feeding tube put in because she had not been eating.  On February 2, 2018, physical therapy was started and she could stand with assistance.  In March 2018 she was moved to TCU.  Id., ¶27.

The FAC further alleges that in December 2018, Thelidria was taken to Torrance Memorial where she underwent surgery to repair her hip.  Her nurses repeatedly failed to properly clean Thelidria.  Her daughter regularly visited and found her mother lying in feces.  On January 27, 2019, Thelidria was transferred to Kindred Hospital Southbay.  Id., ¶28.

Defendant argues that plaintiff has not alleged egregious acts of misconduct or facts amounting to authorization or ratification.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to meet the elements.  Plaintiff alleges two admissions at Torrance Memorial.  The allegations do not show oppression, fraud, or malice or a conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (unstageable bedsore) as to either of the admissions.  Further, the allegations are insufficient as to ratification as they are conclusory.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Third Cause of Action for Willful Misconduct

Defendant argues that there does not exist a cause of action for willful misconduct and no facts are alleged to support the cause of action.

In her opposition, plaintiff concedes the Third Cause of Action should be dismissed.

The court finds that a cause of action for “willful misconduct” does not exist.  See Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal. App. 4th 518, 526 (“The parties have argued extensively about whether a tort called ‘willful misconduct’ is recognized in California.  It is not a separate tort, but simply ‘an aggravated form of negligence, differing in quality rather than degree from ordinary lack of care.’”) (citation and internal quotations omitted).

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Fourth Cause of Action for violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights

Defendant argues that the statute does not apply to it because it is not a skilled nursing facility; rather it is a general “acute care facility.”  Defendant points out that Health and Safety Code §1430(b) and CCR §72527 only apply to skilled nursing facilities or intermediate care facilities.

In her opposition, plaintiff states that she will not object to a ruling by the court sustaining the demurrer without leave to amend.

The court finds that Patient’s Bill of Rights does not apply to defendant, which plaintiff concedes.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Fourth Cause of Action.

 

            Motion to Strike

            Defendant requests that the court strike the claim for punitive, exemplary, and treble damages from the FAC at paras. 78 (under First Cause of Action) and 95 (under Third Cause of Action) and prayer at 2, 4 (under the First Cause of Action), and 8 (under Third Cause of Action for willful misconduct).

            In light of the ruling on the demurrer, the motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 78 and prayer at 2 and 4.  The motion to strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 95 and prayer at 8.

ORDER

The demurrer to the First Cause of Action in the FAC is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  The demurrer to the Third and Fourth Causes of Action are SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 78 and prayer at 2 and 4.  The motion to strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 95 and prayer at 8.

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

BACKGROUND

On May 28, 2021, plaintiff Thelidria Calhoun, in and through her successor-in-interest Dimitria Calhoun, filed a complaint against Torrance Memorial Medical Center, Transitional Care Unit, Kindred Hospital Southbay, and Memorial Hospital of Gardena for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) gross negligence, (4) corporate negligence, (5) willful misconduct, (6) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, and (7) wrongful death.

On November 14, 2022, at the FSC, the court noted that there was no proof that defendants had been served.  The minute order reflects that there being no appearances by or for either side, nor communication with the court as to why there are no appearances by counsel or parties, the court ordered that the FSC be taken off calendar/vacated.  The court stated further that if there is no appearance at the trial scheduled for November 28, 2022, the court may dismiss the case pursuant to CCP §581(b)(3).

On November 28, 2022, on the trial date, there were no appearances, and the court ordered the case dismissed without prejudice.

On May 17, 2023, the case was transferred from the PI Hub to Dept. M.

On June 30, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s motion to vacate and set aside the dismissal.

On September 25, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) willful misconduct, (4) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, and (5) wrongful death.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Kindred demurs to the Second Cause of Action for Negligence and the Fourth Cause of Action for Violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights on the ground that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The FAC alleges that Thelidria Calhoun was born in 1935.  FAC, ¶17.  On December 5, 2017, Thelidria was admitted to Torrance Memorial Medical Center due to shortness of breath and pneumonia.  On December 8, 2017, she was moved to ICU.  Id., ¶22.  Her condition continued to deteriorate during her stay at Torrance Memorial to March 2018, when she was transferred to Transitional Care Unit (“TCU”).  She was released from TCU on October 23, 2018, even though she was not stable.  She was extremely weak and when she was taken to her bedroom the attendants did something that hurt her neck.  She continued to decline and was readmitted to Torrance Memorial in December 2018.  Id., ¶23.

As to Kindred, the FAC alleges that on January 27, 2019, Thelidria was transferred to Kindred for rehabilitation of her hip and to help wean her off her ventilator.  During a cardiology consultation on February 25, 2019, it was noted that she had “a recent cervical neck fracture, multiple orthopedic fractures, malnutrition, interstitial lung disease, acute on chronic respiratory failure status post tracheostomy with trach collar, decubitus wound with wound Vac.”  She required frequent repositioning every two hours for pressure relief on the decubitus wounds she suffered.  Id., ¶28.  On March 5, 2019, she was taken to Memorial Hospital of Gardena after 911 was called.  Id., ¶29.

On April 1, 2019, Thelidria’s daughter Dimitria Calhoun demanded a meeting with the CEO of defendant Kindred and that Thelidria be transferred to Kindred Orange, but they refused to do so.  She informed the CEO and other department heads that Thelidria was not being turned, that she had severe bed sores, that she looked swollen, and that her GI tube was leaking, but the treatment stayed the same.  By April 30, 2019, she had developed an infection and was taken to ICU.  Id., ¶31.  By May 12, 2019, Kindred’s nursing staff indicated that Thelidria’s pressure ulcer had yellow slough and drainage, indicating infection.  Id., ¶32.  On May 15, 2019, Kindred completed a wound care consult that indicated that the two scattered Stage 4 pressure ulcers progressed to being unstageable.  Id., ¶33.  She died on June 1, 2019 from septic shock secondary to the large, infected, necrotic sacral decubitus ulcer.  Id., ¶23, 45.

 

            Second Cause of Action for Negligence

Defendant argues that the cause of action is barred by the one year statute of limitations under CCP §340.5 (“In an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person’s alleged professional negligence . . . .”).  Defendant asserts that it is a healthcare provider, and that the allegations against it relate to professional negligence.  Defendant contends that the complaint was not filed until May 28, 2021, almost two years after decedent died on June 1, 2019.

A review of the factual allegations is that Kindred was operating as a skilled nursing facility.  (FAC ¶ 15.)  On January 27, 2019 Mrs. Calhoun was transferred to Kindred for rehabilitation of her hip and to help wean her off her ventilator.  (FAC ¶ 28.)  Plaintiff complains that Kindred failed to properly reposition Mrs. Calhoun frequently enough .  (FAC ¶ 28, 29, 42.)  In short, the conduct that is alleged to have been provided by Kindred was that of medical care.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the cause of action is not barred by the applicable statute of limitations, which is two-years under CCP §335.1.  Plaintiff contends that this cause of action is for general negligence, not medical malpractice.

“The FAC is pled with sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for general negligence. Defendant Kindred’s demurrer mistakenly asserts that Plaintiffs’ negligence claim (the second cause of action), is for medical malpractice.  This is incorrect.  Plaintiffs’ negligence claim is for general negligence, an additional theory of liability.”  (Opposition pg. 3:2-5.)

Other than making this naked assertion, Plaintiff directs to the Court to nothing that appears to be “general negligence” rather than the provision of medical care.

The court finds that the cause of action alleges medical care as the factual basis for the claim of negligence and therefore it is governed by the statute of limitations governing medical malpractice, not general negligence.  The claim is time barred on its face.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND the Second Cause of Action.

            Fourth Cause of Action For Violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights

Defendant argues that the statute does not apply to it because it is not a skilled nursing facility; rather it is a general “acute care facility.”  Defendant points out that Health and Safety Code §1430(b) and CCR §72527 only apply to skilled nursing facilities or intermediate care facilities.

“Plaintiffs hereby concede that the Fourth Cause of Action does not apply to Defendant Kindred, and they will not object to a ruling by the Court sustaining the Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action without leave to amend.”  (Opposition pg. 2:3-6.)

The court finds that Patient’s Bill of Rights does not apply to defendant.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Fourth Cause of Action.

 

ORDER

The demurrer to the Second Cause of Action in the FAC is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMENED.  The demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion to Vacate Dismissal

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The dismissal entered on August 21, 2023 is set aside and vacated. 

BACKGROUND

On March 1, 2023, Andy Guy filed a complaint against Timothy Roth and Roth Management Group for (1) breach of contract, (2) fraud – concealment, (3) intentional misrepresentation, and (4) conversion.

On August 21, 2023, a Request for Dismissal was filed and the case was dismissed with prejudice.  It is that Dismissal that Plaintiff now seeks to have vacated on the basis that he did not authorize Defendants’ counsel to file it after Plaintiff’s counsel provided it to Defendants’ counsel.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §473(b) states, in relevant part:  “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a . . . dismissal . . . taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief . . . shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the . . . dismissal . . . . was taken. . . .”

“[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

DISCUSSION

            The Court begins by emphasizing what it is ruling upon, and what it is not ruling upon.  This Plaintiff’s motion seeks to set aside a dismissal that Plaintiff claims was filed without his permission.  This ruling only addresses that issue.  It does not reach the issue claimed by Defendant that the parties had entered into an enforceable settlement agreement which Defendant contends obligated Plaintiff to dismiss this case with prejudice.

            Pursuant to CCP §473(b), plaintiff Andy Guy requests that the court set aside and vacate the dismissal with prejudice entered on August 21, 2023.

            Plaintiff contends that he did not authorize the filing of the request for dismissal.  Plaintiff’s former counsel Erik Woodbury states in his declaration that he did not file the request for dismissal.  Rather it was defense counsel Einar Johnson that filed it.  Woodbury explains that in July 2023, defense counsel sent Woodbury a “draft settlement agreement,” which he sent to plaintiff to review.  Woodbury prepared and executed a form CIV-110 request for dismissal and sent it to Johnson “to hold in trust and not file . . . unless and until Mr. Guy executed the draft settlement agreement or a revised version thereof.”  Plaintiff did not sign the “draft settlement agreement.”  Woodbury states that he never received a service copy of the request for dismissal and did not know that defense counsel had filed it until Woodbury received a letter from plaintiff’s current counsel.  He states further that plaintiff never authorized him or defense counsel to file a request for dismissal.  According to plaintiff’s declaration, he states that in July 2023, he reviewed the “draft settlement agreement” and declined to settle and did not execute the agreement.  He states that he never authorized defense counsel to file a request for dismissal or for his counsel to prepare it.  Andy Guy decl.

            In opposition, defendants argue that the case had settled, and that one of the terms of the settlement was that plaintiff dismiss the case with prejudice.  Defendants assert that on July 9, 2023, plaintiff’s former attorney Woodbury sent an email to defense counsel Johnson stating that plaintiff had accepted defendants’ offer, which, defendants argue, resulted in a binding written settlement agreement.  Counsel sent emails back and forth, and on July 22, 2023, defense counsel sent to plaintiff’s counsel a settlement agreement signed by defense counsel and defendants.  Amongst further email correspondence, on August 17, defense counsel stated to plaintiff’s counsel that plaintiff was in breach of the agreement.  In another email, defense counsel “formally declared” plaintiff to be in breach of the agreement and filed the request for dismissal that had been signed by plaintiff’s counsel.

            In reply, plaintiff disputes that an enforceable settlement agreement exists because he did not sign one.  Plaintiff reiterates that he did not authorize defense counsel to file a request for dismissal.

            The issue of whether or not the parties entered into an enforceable settlement agreement shall not be addressed in this motion.  That issue may be raised in a proper motion seeking a determination on that issue.

The court finds that plaintiff has met his burden of showing that the filing of the Request for Dismissal by Defendants’ attorney was the result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, and/or neglect.  The evidence indicates that the Request for Dismissal was filed without plaintiff’s or plaintiff’s counsel’s knowledge.  Whether the purported settlement agreement is enforceable, which defense counsel contends he acted under, is not at issue as to this motion and the Court declines to consider any terms of such an agreement at this time as part of this motion.

The motion is GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.  The Court orders that the dismissal with prejudice entered on August 21, 2023 is set aside and vacated.

            The Court shall discuss with the parties the possibility of referring this matter for immediate mediation.

            A Case Management Conference and Trial Setting conference is set for  May 17, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.  All parties mut file a timely Case management Conference Statement.

            Unless notice is waived at the hearing by all parties, Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 23TRCV02413    Hearing Date: April 9, 2024    Dept: B

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Sandi Flores

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Artemio Salas

Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 RULING

The motion is GRANTED.   Plaintiff Artemio Salas is ordered (1) to serve on defendant a verified response without objections to plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and (2) to produce all documents and things in plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to defendant’s request, within 15 days.

The court orders that plaintiff pay defendant a monetary sanction in the amount of $1,190 within 30 days.

BACKGROUND

On July 25, 2023, plaintiff Artemio Salas filed a complaint against Sandi Flores for (1) quiet title, (2) breach of contract, (3) recovery by one under the doctrine of estoppel, (4) declaratory relief, and (5) clerk of the court sign all necessary documentation.

On March 18, 2024, plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  So, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487. 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Sandi Flores requests that the court compel plaintiff Artemio Salas to respond to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

            The complaint alleges that plaintiff seeks to quiet title to two separate pieces of property that are owned by the parties.  One property is located at 1137 W. El Segundo Blvd., Gardena and the other property is located at 1717 Agate Circle, Bullhead City, Arizona.  The parties were in an unmarried relationship and were co-habitants for approximately eighteen years.  The parties purchased the property in El Segundo in 2017 using plaintiff’s personal funds and was used solely as a rental income property.  The Arizona property was primarily used as a vacation property.  Plaintiff used approximately $150,000 to remodel both properties.  The parties’ relationship ended in May 2023.  The complaint alleges that defendant has wrongfully collected all the rental payments from the El Segundo property and defendant is wrongfully attempting to sell 50% of the El Segundo property.

            Defendant asserts that on November 10, 2023, defendant served plaintiff with written discovery.  Responses were due by December 15, 2023.  When no response was received, on January 9, 2024, defense counsel sent an email to plaintiff’s counsel, requesting responses and production of documents by January 16.  As of the filing date (February 2), defense counsel had not received responses or documents.

            In opposition, plaintiff contends that he has served responses beginning March 29 and continuing to April 2, and is diligently working to gather documentary evidence to produce.  Plaintiff also asserts that his failure to timely respond “lies at the fee of prior counsel.”  Plaintiff states in his declaration that he sought input and assistance from his prior counsel to timely and properly respond to the discovery requests, but that his prior counsel did not assist him to respond or prepare his responses to the requests.

            The court finds that defendant properly served written discovery and plaintiff failed to timely serve responses, and thus has waived objections.  Although it appears that plaintiff has served at least some responses, it is unclear to what extent plaintiff has produced responsive documents.

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

            Defendant requests $1456.65 ($650/hr.) in sanctions against plaintiff.  The court finds that $1190 ($450 x 2.5 hrs., filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against plaintiff.

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiff Artemio Salas is ordered (1) to serve on defendant a verified response without objections to defendant’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and (2) to produce all documents and things in plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to defendant’s request, within 15 days.

The court orders that plaintiff pay to defendant a monetary sanction in the amount of $1,190 within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
