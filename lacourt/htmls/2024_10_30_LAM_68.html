DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STLC05464    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 10-30-24

Case #: 22STLC05464

Trial Date: 1-13-25

 

AUGMENT ASSESSMENT

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff, Rick Ladd

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant, County of Los Angeles, et al.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Augment the Assessment Appeals Board Administrative Record

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Rick Ladd owns certain real property identified as 3912 West 106th St., Inglewood. Plaintiff alleges the County of Los Angeles improperly assessed property taxes for the years 2019 through 2022, for a balance in excess of $16,781.52.

 

On August 17, 2022, and May 1, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed a complaint and first amended complaint for Refund of Property Taxes, Accounting, and Declaratory Relief. On May 2, 2023, the action was reclassified as unlimited. On September 5, 2023, the court (Department 85 writs and receivers) sustained the demurrer to the accounting and declaratory relief cause of action without leave to amend, overruled the demurrer to the refund of property taxes causes of action, and determined the action was not properly before the department. On September 7, 2023, the action was transferred to Department 68.

 

On January 24, 2024, the court denied the motion to compel further responses to Request for Production of Documents, numbers 3, 5-10, and granted the motion to compel further responses to Requests for Admissions, numbers 8-11, 14, 18-21, and 30-33, from defendants County of Los Angeles, et al.

 

RULING: Denied

Plaintiff Rick Ladd, in pro per, moves to Augment the Assessment Appeals Board Administrative Record or Alternatively Issue a Writ of Administrative Mandamus Compelling Augmentation. Plaintiff seeks to augment the Administrative Record consisting of items identified as Exhibits P-24 through P-30, in order to allow review of the complete record regarding the assessment process. Defendants County of Los Angeles, et al., counters that the sole remaining issue for consideration before the court involves the calculation of the refund amount. Plaintiff is now precluded from seeking de novo review of the entire underlying assessment process. Allowing an augmentation of the record would constitute improper new evidence now precluded due to the failure to present said information at the time of the assessment hearing. The court electronic filing system shows no reply at the time of the tentative ruling publication cutoff.

 

The first amended complaint challenges the correction of a property valuation from $590,000 to $275,000. [First Amend. Comp., ¶ 9.] Plaintiff maintains the Los Angeles County Assessment Appeals Board failed to timely comply with the provisions of California Revenue and Taxation Code sections 1604, et seq. [First Amend. Comp., ¶ 10.] The first cause of action specifically and exclusively alleges an “unlawful method of assessment” seeking “de novo review of the method of assessment applied...” [First Amend. Comp., ¶ 16.]

 

“The assessment of property for the purpose of taxation is a function of the executive branch of the government and the judiciary has no power or jurisdiction to inquire as to the actual value of property to determine whether or not it has been properly assessed. (Citation.) ... The duty of determining the value of the property and the fairness of the assessment is confided to the appropriate county board of equalization. In discharging this duty, the board's determination upon the merits of the controversy is conclusive. The taxpayer has no right to a trial de novo in the superior court to resolve conflicting issues of fact as to the taxable value of his property. (Citation.) If a plaintiff taxpayer claims only that the assessor and the board of equalization erroneously applied a valid method of determining full cash value, the decision of the board is equivalent to the determination of a trial court and the trial court in turn may review only the record presented to the board. The trial court may overturn the board's decision only when no substantial evidence supports it, in which case the actions of the board are deemed so arbitrary as to constitute a deprivation of property without due process. (Citation.) In those cases where the substantial evidence test is to be applied, the trial court does not weigh the evidence in the administrative record nor does it exercise its independent judgment. Rather, it reviews the entire record to determine if there is substantial evidence to support the findings of the administrative agency. (Citation.)

 

“Where the legality of the valuation method used by the assessor is in issue, the scope of judicial review is not limited to the evidence. (Citation.) When the taxpayer challenges the validity of the valuation method itself, the issue raised is a question of law. That question is whether the challenged method of valuation is arbitrary, in excess of discretion, or in violation of the standards prescribed by law. (Citation.) Reviewing courts must presume the assessing officers have performed their duties and have assessed all properties fairly and upon an equal basis. (Citation.) Where taxes are claimed to be excessive, before they can be set aside there must be evidence to show the assessments were fraudulently or mistakenly made or that an improper method of valuation was pursued. Put another way, specific valuations and methods of valuation employed are reviewable only for arbitrariness, abuse of discretion, or failure to follow the standards prescribed by the Legislature. (Citation.)

 

“When a board of equalization purports to decide a question of law or refuses to hear a case on the ground it involves only a question of law to be decided by the courts, a taxpayer has the right to resort to the courts for determination of such question. (Citation.) Thus, if the issue is a legal one the judicial inquiry is pursued independently of the board's decision and unrestricted by the usual limits on the scope of review. The court is not restricted to the transcript of the proceedings before the board, but may receive additional evidence bearing on the legal question. (Citations.)”

 

(Norby Lumber Co. v. County of Madera (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 1352, 1362–1363.)

 

The operative complaint comes to the court after the writs and receivers department overruled the demurrer to the first cause of action and sustained the demurrer without leave to amend as to the remaining two causes of action. The court specifically reviewed the administrative record, and categorically found no challenge to the actual wrongfulness of any assessment, but in fact a sole claim for miscalculation of the properly refunded amount. The court therefore held the case shall only proceed on the issue of the tax refund. A tax refund case is properly filed in the superior court. (Little v. Los Angeles County Assessment Appeals Bds. (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 915, 923 accord Sunrise Retirement Villa v. Dear (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 948, 955 (footnote 2.)

 

Under the substantial evidence standard, the court cannot consider new and additional information beyond the administrative hearing transcript. (Western States Petroleum Assn. v. Superior Court (1995) 9 Cal.4th 559, 579; see FlightSafety Intern., Inc. v. Los Angeles County Assessment Appeals Bd. (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 712, 721.) All information was submitted at the time of the hearing. (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 18, § 313, subd. (e).) “We have repeatedly held that issues not presented at an administrative hearing cannot be raised on review.” (Niles Freeman Equipment v. Joseph (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 765, 787.) “Exhaustion of administrative remedies is a jurisdictional prerequisite to a judicial action challenging any planning decision. (Citation.) If a party wishes to make a particular methodological challenge to a given study relied upon in planning decisions, the challenge must be raised in the course of the administrative proceedings. Otherwise, it cannot be raised in any subsequent judicial proceedings.” (San Franciscans Upholding the Downtown Plan v. City & County of San Francisco (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 656, 685.) The court finds no legal or factual support for an alternative “legal” challenge allowing the introduction of further, proposed evidence to the record.

 

The motion is therefore DENIED.

 

Trial set for January 13, 2025.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff, Limelight International, Ltd., et al.

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant, D. Masin Consulting, LLC

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Limelight International Ltd., (Limelight) a Hong Kong based company authorized to conduct business in California alleges an unspecified defaulted balance owed by defendant RLoom LLC (RLoom) in 2022. On February 8, 2023, RLoom and defendant D. Masin Consulting, LLC (D. Masin) executed a “General Assignment for the Benefit of All Creditors,” whereby all assets were subject to liquidation for payment of outstanding debts. Also on February 8, 2022, D. Masin and RLoom Design Corp. aka Newstyle Fashion Inc. also entered into an “Asset Purchase Agreement, whereby said assets were assigned by RLoom to D. Masin were sold to Newstyle for $365,000. Limelight maintains an asset value of at least $9,000,000, including its claimed outstanding debt.

 

On October 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed its complaint for Malicious Prosecution, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Fraudulent Conveyance, Successor Liability, Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and Conspiracy to Fraudulently Transfer Property. Following certain defendants answer to the complaint, the parties stipulated to the filing of a first amended complaint. The first amended complaint filed on January 3, 2024, alleges Malicious Prosecution, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Fraudulent Conveyance, Successor Liability, Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and Conspiracy to Fraudulently Transfer Property. On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the first cause of action for Malicious Prosecution.

 

On February 20, 2024, the court overruled the demurrer to the first amended complaint. Defendants answered the first amended complaint on March 1, 2024.

 

RULING: Granted.

Request for Judicial Notice: Granted.

 

Plaintiff Limelight International, Ltd., et al. moves to compel further responses on Request for Production of Documents (set one), numbers 1-10, against Defendant D. Masin Consulting, LLC. Plaintiff maintains the objections lack merit, the promises to respond fail to statutorily comply, and promised production otherwise never occurred. D. Masin Consulting, LLC in opposition maintains that following the filing of the motion, all non-privileged material was produced as promised, and only attorney client and attorney work privileged documents remain withheld, which are identified in a privilege log. Plaintiff in reply reiterates the deficiency of the responses, including lack of code compliance thereby insuring full responsive production. Plaintiff denies production of a complete privilege log. Plaintiff requests sanctions as a result of the delayed production. Plaintiff alternatively moves for the appointment of a discovery referee if the court is inclined to deny the motion, thereby necessitating a second motion.

 

“Whether a particular response does resolve satisfactorily the issues raised by a motion is a matter best determined by the trial court in the exercise of its discretion, based on the circumstances of the case.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 408–409.) While D. Masin Consulting, LLC in opposition represents full production of non-privileged material and a privilege log, the counter position in reply challenges the completeness of said further production, code compliance and a complete privilege log.

 

The court declines to summarize and address each and every disputed item, at least in part due the inability to conduct an item-by-item examination of the alleged continuing deficiencies. The court accepts the representations of Plaintiff regarding unaccounted for documents in the privilege log, and insufficient statutory compliance. The court therefore GRANTS the motion for purposes of compelling complete responses and production, even if a substantial majority of production occurred, and the missing documents were inadvertently or intentionally omitted.

 

The court orders the parties to further meet and confer in order to address the deficiencies raised by Plaintiff in reply within 15 days of this order. Defendant D. Masin Consulting, LLC to provide further code compliant responses, full non-privileged production, and an updated privilege log accounting for all withheld items within 15 days of the lapsed meet and confer period.

 

Even if D. Masin Consulting, LLC were to establish completeness at oral argument, thereby somehow vitiating the necessity of ordering further meet and confer and production, the court adheres to a policy that whenever a motion prompts a response demonstrating validity to the motion, the court will impose sanctions in favor of the moving party. Given the delayed production, withheld documents, and failure to produce a privilege log until after the filing of the motion, the court imposes the statutory minimum of $1,000 in sanctions joint and several against D. Masin Consulting, LLC and counsel of record. Payable within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.050, subd. (a), 2031.310, subd. (h); 2031.320, subd. (d).)

 

Two more motions to compel further responses to document demands scheduled for October 31, and December 5, 2024. Plaintiff in reply alternatively moves for potential appointment, but the court finds the circumstances insufficiently support consideration barring a more articulated showing, such as a requirement of direct review over each and every sought after challenged item for review of issues like relevance, privilege, third party privacy, etc. Such a showing requires a hearing under Code of Civil Procedure section 639, subd. (a)(5); (Taggares v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal. App. 4th 94, 105.) The parties may alternatively stipulate to a discovery referee. The court can tailor any such order to specific parties, with fee splitting and sanctions awards vested with the referee, if applicable. The court reserves the right to sua sponte set an OSC re: Appointment of Referee if potential future discovery disputes greatly expand need and further usurp court resources.

 

Plaintiff to give notice to all parties.

MOTION TO QUASH

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants City of Redondo Beach Police Department

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Kelly Nicole Deckers

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Quash Service of the Summons and First Amended Complaint

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Kelly Deckers was formerly employed by Redondo Beach Police Department as an Emergency Dispatcher. Plaintiff alleges a cancer diagnosis required subsequent accommodation, which was not provided. The hostile work environment “forced” resignation from the position in November 2023.

 

On March 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint for 1. Discrimination Based On Physical Disability (Govt. Code §12940(A)); 2. Hostile Work Environment (Govt. Code §12940 Et Seq.); 3. Retaliation In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940 Et Seq.; 4. Failure To Prevent Discrimination (Govt. Code §12940(K)); 5. Harassment (Govt. Code §12940(J); 6. Failure To Accommodate Physical Disability In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940(M); 7. Failure To Engage In A Good Faith Interactive Process In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940(N); And 8. Wrongful Constructive Termination In Violation Of Public Policy In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940 et seq.; 9. Failure To Pay Premium Double Time Wages Due (Cal. Labor Code §§ 510, 558, 1194, 1198); 10. Failure To Provide Timely Off Duty Meal Periods (Cal. Labor Code §§ 226.7, 512); 11. Failure To Provide Rest Periods (Cal. Labor Code § 226.7); 12. Failure To Maintain Records And Provide Accurate Itemized Wage Statements (Cal. Labor Code §226); 13. Failure To Pay Wages Due Upon Termination (Cal. Labor Code §§201-203); 14. Failure To Reimburse Business Expenses (Cal. Labor Code § 2802); 15. Failure To Provide Day Of Rest (Cal. Labor Code §§ 551, 552); 16. Failure To Allow Inspection Of Employment Records (Cal. Labor Code §1198.5); 17. Defamation; and 18. Unfair Competition (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§17200 Et Seq. On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the seventeenth cause of action for Defamation.

 

On June 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint for 1. Discrimination Based On Physical Disability (Govt. Code §12940(A)); 2. Hostile Work Environment (Govt. Code §12940 Et Seq.); 3. Retaliation In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940 Et Seq.; 4. Failure To Prevent Discrimination (Govt. Code §12940(K)); 5. Harassment (Govt. Code §12940(J); 6. Failure To Accommodate Physical Disability In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940(M); 7. Failure To Engage In A Good Faith Interactive Process In Violation Of Govt. Code §12940(N); and 8. Defamation.

 

On August 22, 2024, the court denied the special motion to strike the first amended complaint brought by City of Redondo Beach.

 

RULING: Denied.

Request for Judicial Notice: Granted.

Defendant City of Redondo Beach Police Department moves to quash service of the first amended complaint. The “Police Department” moves to quash on grounds said department works as a part of the City of Redondo Beach, rather than a separate government entity subject to independent designation as a defendant. Plaintiff in opposition maintains service was proper, and the status of the police department is not properly considered in a motion to quash service. Defendant in reply maintains the motion to quash constitutes the proper method for challenge.


The court takes judicial notice of the Secretary of State roster and concludes the Redondo Police Department lacks a separate, independent legal entity status apart from the City of Redondo Beach. While the “Police Department” appears as part of the City of Redondo Beach without a separate charter, given the opposition, the court declines to make a determination regarding a lack of jurisdiction on the basis of the unchallenged service or typical jurisdictional requirements under the minimum contact standard. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).) Whether the subject opposition ultimately mounts to a resource wasteful distinction without a difference or not, the propriety of the “Police Department” as a separately named defendant appears more properly addressed in a demurrer, rather than a jurisdictional issue in a motion to quash given the general appearance of the City of Redondo Beach. (Vann v. City and County of San Francisco (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 1013, 1023-1026.)

 

The motion to quash service is DENIED.

 

Motion to deem admissions admitted, and multiple demurrers and a motion to strike between December 5, 2024, and January 27, 2025.
