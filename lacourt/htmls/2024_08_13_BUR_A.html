DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

REQUESTING ORAL ARGUMENT PER CRC 3.1308

The Court will attempt to post all Tentative Rulings at least the day prior to the hearing by 3:00 p.m.; however, the Court does not post Tentative Rulings for all matters.  

The Court will indicate in the Tentative Ruling whether the Court is requesting oral argument.  For cases where the Court is not requesting argument, then the Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) where the Court requests notice of intent to appear.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, a party seeking argument should notify "all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue."  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is requested.   

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Notice of the ruling must be served as indicated in the tentative.  Remote appearances are permitted for all law and motion unless otherwise indicated by the Court.  

 

Case Number: 23BBCV02519    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: A

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH CENTRAL DISTRICT - BURBANK

DEPARTMENT A

 

TENTATIVE RULING

AUGUST 13, 2024

MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23BBCV02519

 

MP:  

Levon Anayan (Defendant)

RP:  

Empire Fire and Marine Insurance Company (Intervenor)

 

NOTICE:

 

The Court is not requesting oral argument on this matter.  The Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) whereby notice of intent to appear is requested.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, no argument is requested and any party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.  

 

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

 

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

This is an action between Liana Ordaz (Ordaz) and Levon Anayan (Anayan) relating to an automobile collision between the parties. Before the Court is a motion to intervene by Empire Fire and Marine Insurance Company (Empire). On February 23, 2024, the Court granted Empire’s motion to intervene in this matter. Empire contended that, as the insurer for Anayan’s vehicle, it has an interest in this litigation. The basis for this claim is Empire’s belief that Ordaz and Anayan staged the collision in order to defraud their insurance companies.

 

Before the Court is a Motion for Protective Order filed by Anayan. Anayan seeks to exclude from evidence two recorded statements made to Empire in the course of their investigation. Anayan’s sole argument is that these statements are the subject of attorney-client privilege. Empire opposes the motion.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order. (C.C.P. § 2025.420, (a).) The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under C.C.P. § 2016.040. (Id.)

 

The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (C.C.P. § 2025.420(b).) Moreover, there are “no time limits within which an application for a protective order must be filed” (Stadish v. Superior Court (1999) 71 Cal. App. 4th 1130, 1144.)

 

C.C.P. § 2025.420(b), provides a nonexclusive list of permissible directions that may be included in a protective order. “[T]he burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought.” (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Anayan asks the Court grant a protective order with respect to discovery requests served on Empire by Plaintiff. Specifically, Anayan requests that two recorded calls between himself and agents of Empire be excluded from discovery. The first recorded statement is contained within a November 14, 2022 phone call between an Empire claims adjuster and Anayan. (Bozoghlian Decl. ¶ 6.) The second recorded statement was taken on February 10, 2023. (Bozoghlian Decl. ¶ 7.) During this second conversation Anayan, now represented by counsel, answered various questions under penalty of perjury from an Empire investigator. (Bozoghlian Decl. ¶ 7.)

 

The discovery request was from plaintiff to the intervenor.  After defendant’s counsel objected to disclosure of the telephone calls with intervenor.  (Miller Decl. ¶17).  Subsequently, plaintiff’s counsel called defendant’s counsel and advised that plaintiff was not requesting the production of the recorded calls in question.  Mr. Miller, plaintiff’s counsel advised the intervenor was insisting on producing these calls.  (Miller Decl. ¶19). 

 

At this time, the Court finds it unnecessary to analyze whether the statements are protected by an attorney-client privilege or whether there is an exception thereto if one does exist.  Ultimately, plaintiff was seeking discovery from intervenor, and plaintiff likewise has the right to further define what is sought.   Since plaintiff is not seeking the recorded calls in question, intervenor has disclosed their existence and can comply with what is requested by plaintiff without transgressing upon defendant’s claimed privilege.  Intervenor does not explain why turning over information that is not requested is needed to comply with the discovery request.  Intervenor may fully comply without turning over the recorded calls in question.  Defendant also seeks an order that prevents the disclosure of the existence of the calls, the Court does not find a basis to issue such an order, only the content of the calls and the recordings should not be provided.   The law permits the disclosure of the existence of items, which is often done with a privilege log.  That would then permit the Court to make further rulings to the extent necessary whether the items are in fact privledged.

 

The Court believes the requested relief can be granted without opining on the validity of defendant’s attorney-client privilege claim.  Accordingly, the motion for protective order is GRANTED.   However, the request is granted for the limited purpose of responding to the current discovery requests from plaintiff, and not for any other purpose.  Whether these statements may be admitted at trial or should be subsequently disclosed for some other reason is not being addressed.   The bottom line is that plaintiff is not requesting the recorded calls and has so advised intervenor.  Intervenor should not include information that is not being requested.

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Kamo Arustamyan (Plaintiff) bring this action against Nissan-Infiniti LT LLC (Nissan) for claims arising out of the purchase of a 2021 Infiniti Q50 (the Subject Vehicle). The Complaint alleges, among other things, that he was damaged by the terms of the Lease Agreement, and he was fraudulently induced into signing. The vast majority of Plaintiff’s claims concern his allegation that he was improperly excluded from any insurance payout when the vehicle was rendered a total loss following a December 23 ,2021 incident.

 

Nissan now moves to compel Plaintiffs’ claims against them to arbitration. Nissan argues these claims are subject to arbitration pursuant to the agreement contained in the Lease Agreement signed between Plaintiff and the dealership, which was subsequently assigned to Nissan. Plaintiff has filed no opposition to this motion, and indicated they would not do so in a July 31, 2024 Case Management Statement.   

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

C.C.P. § 1281.2 states: “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement arbitrate the controversy exists.”

 

A party seeking to compel arbitration has the initial burden to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, the existence of a valid and enforceable arbitration agreement. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.) If the moving party has met its initial burden, then the burden shifts to respondents to prove the falsity or unenforceability of the arbitration agreement. (Id.) 

 

Moving party has filed a notice of non-opposition.   The Court notes, failure to file an opposition to the motion indicates the other parties' acquiescence that the motion is meritorious. CRC Rule 8.54(c)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

The Arbitration Clause

 

As evidence of an arbitration agreement between the parties, Nissan submits a copy of the Motor Vehicle Lease Agreement (Lease Agreement) between Plaintiff and Glendale Infiniti (Dealership). Nissan further states that the Lease Agreement between Plaintiff and Glendale Infiniti was thereafter assigned to Nissan-Infiniti LT and serviced by Nissan Motor Acceptance Company, LLC (NMAC). (Blakney Decl. ¶¶ 2, 5.)

 

The Lease Agreement contains an arbitration clause at paragraph 31 which reads in relevant part:

 

1. Either you or we may choose to have any dispute between us decided by arbitration and not in court or by jury trial.

 

2. If a dispute is arbitrated, you will give up your right to participate as a class representative or class member on any class claim you may have against us including any right to class arbitration or any consolidation of individual arbitrations.

 

3. Discovery and rights to appeal in arbitration are generally more limited than in a lawsuit, and other rights that you and we would have in court may not be available in arbitration….

 

…[a]ny claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Clause, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action…

 

…This Arbitration Clause shall survive any termination, payoff or transfer of this lease….

 

(Blakney Decl. Exh. A, p. 4.)

 

The Plaintiff has declined to oppose the motion.  Pursuant to CRC Rule 8.54(c), the failure to file an opposition to the motion indicates the other parties' acquiescence that the motion is meritorious.

As such, the Court need not conduct its own independent analysis seeking to substitute its judgment and legal analysis to find fault, if any, with Plaintiff’s motion.  

 

As such, the Court finds Nissan has provided sufficient evidence that Plaintiff’s dispute is subject to arbitration and the burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to show falsity or unenforceability. Plaintiff, having submitted no opposition to this motion, has not upheld his burden. Given that the arbitration clause is present in Plaintiff’s Complaint, the Court sees no facial claim for falsity. Further, the language of the arbitration agreement appears to bind both parties equally and is not facially unconscionable.  

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

 

Stay

 

Once arbitration has been compelled, in whole or in part, a stay of proceedings is mandatory if the issues in the arbitration and the pending action overlap. (C.C.P. § 1281.4 (if a court “has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.”)) 

 

“The purpose of the statutory stay [under section 1281.4] is to protect the jurisdiction of the arbitrator by preserving the status quo until arbitration is resolved. In the absence of a stay, the continuation of the proceedings in the trial court disrupts the arbitration proceedings and can render them ineffective.” (Federal Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1370, 1374-1375 (citations omitted).) 

 

As the Court grants the motion to compel arbitration in its entirety, the Court also grants the motion to stay the proceedings pending arbitration.

TENTATIVE RULING

AUGUST 13, 2024

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION & MOTION FOR TRIAL PREFERENCE

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 24NNCV01901

 

MP:  

Bellefontaine Health Center, Abraham Bak and Menachem Gastwirth (Defendants)

RP:  

Weslie Hofland (Plaintiff)

 

NOTICE:

 

The Court is not requesting oral argument on this matter.  The Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) whereby notice of intent to appear is requested.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, no argument is requested and any party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.  

 

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at

(818) 260-8412.

 

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Weslie Hofland (Plaintiff) brings this action against Bellefontaine Health Center (Bellefontaine), Abraham Bak (Bak) and Menachem Gastwirth (Gastwirth) (collectively Defendants). Plaintiff alleges that he was injured as the result of negligence and elder abuse due to the Defendants. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that he was injured in two separate falls at Defendants’ independent care facility.  Plaintiff argues that the injury was the result of Defendants’ failure to observe fall risk protocol, among other things. Bak and Menachem are alleged to be the owners and operators of Bellefontaine.

 

Before the Court is Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration. Defendants argue that Plaintiff is bound to arbitrate his claims against them because he signed an arbitration agreement on April 24, 2024 (Defendants’ Exhibit A). Plaintiff opposes the motion to compel and Defendants reply.

 

Also before the Court is a motion by Plaintiff for trial preference based on his age and health conditions. Defendants oppose this motion and Plaintiff replies.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

C.C.P. § 1281.2 states: “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement arbitrate the controversy exists.” A party seeking to compel arbitration has the initial burden to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, the existence of a valid and enforceable arbitration agreement. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.) If the moving party has met its initial burden, then the burden shifts to respondents to prove the falsity or unenforceability of the arbitration agreement. (Id.)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

General

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff does not dispute the existence of an arbitration agreement, but opposes this motion for three reasons: 1) that Plaintiff lacked the mental capacity to contract at the time he signed the agreement, 2) that Plaintiff’s electronic signature was not appropriately authenticated, and 3) that Plaintiff cannot afford arbitration. The Court will address each of the grounds in turn beginning with Plaintiff’s capacity.

 

Legal Standard for Determining Capacity

 

“‘A judicial determination that a person is totally without understanding, or is of unsound mind, or suffers from one or more mental deficits so substantial that, under the circumstances, the person should be deemed to lack the legal capacity to perform a specific act, should be based on evidence of a deficit in one or more of the person's mental functions ….’ (Prob. Code, § 810(c).)” (Algo-Heyres v. Oxnard Manor LP (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 1064, 1070.)

 

“Probate Code section 811 requires that incapacity to contract be supported by evidence of a deficit in at least one of four areas: alertness and attention, information processing, thought processes, and ability to modulate mood and affect. The deficit must ‘significantly impair the person's ability to understand and appreciate the consequences of his or her actions with regard to the type of act or decision in question.’” (Id. [citations omitted].)

 

“The capacity to make a decision requires the person have the ability to communicate the decision verbally or by other means, and to understand and appreciate the rights and responsibilities affected by the decision, the probable consequences, and the ‘significant risks, benefits, and reasonable alternatives involved in the decision.’” (Id. [citations omitted].)

 

Evidence of Incapacity

 

Plaintiff alleges that he suffered two falls prior to his admission to the Defendants’ facility. Plaintiff alleges he fell on April 7, 2024 and was hospitalized thereafter. (Compl. ¶ 24.) Plaintiff also alleges that he fell on April 22, 2024, and thereafter received treatment including staples to the back of his head. (Compl. ¶ 24.) The fall which is the subject of this lawsuit is alleged to have occurred on April 25, 2024 at Defendants’ facility.  (Compl. ¶ 30.) Another fall is alleged to have occurred on May 4, 2024, also at Defendants’ facility. (Compl. ¶ 31.)

 

As evidence of Plaintiff’s inability to contract, he offers several records. The Court notes that a good deal of these records occur after Plaintiff’s April 25, 2024 fall and struck his head. Given that Plaintiff is tasked with demonstrating he lacked capacity to contract at the time the contract was entered, medical documentation after the April 25, 2024 fall is less persuasive.

 

As concerns records prior to the April 25 fall, Plaintiff submits his April 22, 2024 admission record to Defendants’ facility. (Garcia Decl. Exh. 2.) This record shows that Plaintiff suffered from a traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage without loss of consciousness. (Id.) It also shows that Plaintiff was diagnosed with “mild cognitive impairment of uncertain or unknown etymology.” (Id.)   In an April 30, 2024 page from the Initial History and Physical, Plaintiff is noted as having “mild dementia,” which typically does not appear in a short period and likely existed at the time the agreement was signed.

 

Plaintiff also submits an excerpt from Defendants’ care plan. This excerpt is dated April 22, 2024 and appears to be part of the intake procedure. (Garcia Decl. Exh. 3.) This excerpt notes Plaintiff as being a risk to wander, stating that he was going through his suitcase as if he had somewhere to be. (Id.)

 

The remainder of Plaintiff’s evidence of his incapacity is dated after the April 25 fall. Plaintiff first submits an “Initial History and Physical” which is dated April 30, 2024 and presumably was prepared in his hospitalization after the April 25 fall. (Garcia Decl. Exh. 1.) This record indicates, via checkbox, that Plaintiff was assessed to not have the capacity to understand or make decisions. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff also submits an excerpt from Defendants’ care plan dated May 22, 2024. (Garcia Decl. Exh. 4.) This excerpt documents that Plaintiff was “unaware of safety needs” and “wandering”. (Id.) The Court notes this entry also comes after the alleged May 4, 2024 fall.

 

Plaintiff submits a Social Services Admission Assessment dated April 26, 2024. (Garcia Decl. Exh. 5.) This assessment indicates, via checkbox, that Plaintiff always required the assistance of others when reading instructions pamphlets or other written materials from his doctors. (Id.) It is also indicated via checkbox that Plaintiff was only able to express concrete needs, understand spoken communication, and able to follow simple commands. (Id.) This document indicates that Plaintiff has a durable power of attorney for both healthcare and financial means but is silent as to any details. (Id.) No documents evidencing this power of attorney, or who holds it, are attached.

 

While Plaintiff does submit the declaration of Shahab Attarchi M.D. in support of this opposition, nothing in the declaration attests to Plaintiff’s mental capacity at the time of entering the arbitration agreement. (Garcia Decl. Exh. 6.)  Furthermore, Plaintiff’s family advised that Plaintiff will sign his own admission paperwork and never sought a conservator for him, possibly because they had a power of attorney but did not exercise it.  The Garcia declaration likely was intended to be utilized for the purpose of obtaining trial preference, rather than showing Plaintiff’s mental incapacity.

 

In contrast to Plaintiff’s submissions, Defendant submits the declaration of its Admissions Director Angelica Galvan (Galvan). Galvan states that she provided Plaintiff with the agreement, and he signed it in her presence. (Galvan Decl. ¶ 2.) Galvan further states that she felt the medication Plaintiff was on when he was admitted would have impaired his ability to sign, and so she waited several days after his admission to present him with the agreement. (Id.) Galvan also states that she spoke to Plaintiff’s daughter prior to presenting the agreement and that Plaintiff’s daughter informed her Plaintiff would sign his own documentation. (Id.) Galvan states that prior to presenting the agreement she reviewed Plaintiff’s medical records and found no record indicating he lacked capacity. (Id.)

 

Discussion

 

In considering the totality of the evidence, the Court does not find that Plaintiff has provided sufficient evidence to rebut the presumption of his capacity to contract. None of the records predating Plaintiff’s fall in Defendants’ facility indicate anything more than minor difficulty in understanding. The admissions records noted that Plaintiff had mild cognitive impairment, which does not equate to a lack of capacity to understand his contractual rights. Further, Plaintiff being prone to wander does not suggest a significant impairment of his ability to understand the consequences of his action.

 

As concerns the post-fall evidence, the Court finds this insufficient to demonstrate a significant impairment. None of the post-fall evidence includes detail regarding Plaintiff’s supposed mental incapacity. Plaintiff’s capacity to understand is presented via checkbox in the April 30 “Initial history and Physical”, with no explanation as to why a lack of capacity was determined. Further, the Social Services Admission form only demonstrates Plaintiff’s own belief that he lacked capacity. Nothing in the Social Services Admission indicates that an evaluation of capacity, other than Plaintiff’s own, was ever conducted.

 

As concerns the existence of a power of attorney (POA), the Court has already noted that no evidence of this is attached to Plaintiff’s opposition. If it is Plaintiff’s position that Defendants should have contracted with the holder of Plaintiff’s POA, they have not provided the evidence to support this claim. Plaintiff’s opposition is silent as to why Plaintiff’s daughter indicated that Plaintiff could review and sign the paperwork.

 

Lastly, the May 22 care plan excerpts only speak to Plaintiff’s tendency to wander. There is no explanation as to how Plaintiff is “unaware of his safety needs” and, even if there were, it is not clear that such an evaluation results in his incapacity to contract. Further, there is doubt cast on this evidence by virtue of it being dated after the two falls in Defendants’ facility.

 

In short, the Court finds Plaintiff’s evidence insufficient to demonstrate that his capacity to contract was significantly impaired when he agreed to arbitrate his claims. Plaintiff simply has not presented a medical diagnosis indicating that he suffered from a condition which significantly impaired his cognitive abilities.

 

As such, Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

 

Electronic Signature

 

The Court briefly addresses Plaintiff’s argument that the motion to compel should be denied based on Defendants’ failure to authenticate the electronic signature on the arbitration agreement. The Court finds this argument has been sufficiently addressed by the declaration of Galvan, wherein she specifically states that Plaintiff signed the agreement in her presence.

 

Arbitration Fees

 

Plaintiff also opposes arbitration on grounds of his inability to afford the arbitral process. Plaintiff requests the Court find that Defendant be required to pay for Plaintiff’s portion of the arbitration fees upon showing of his indigent status.

 

Upon determination by a trial court that a plaintiff lacks the financial resources to share the cost of arbitration, the trial court may order the defending party to pay plaintiff’s share or waive the right to arbitrate. (Roldan v. Callahan & Blaine (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 87, 96.) “Where a party to a contract with an arbitration provision opposes a motion to compel arbitration on the ground of inability to pay the costs, the moving party can ask leave to conduct limited discovery directed only to the opponent's financial circumstances.” (Hang v. RG Legacy I, LLC (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 1243, 1256 [citation omitted].)

 

Here, Plaintiff submits the declaration of his daughter Natalie Hofland (Natalie). Natalie states that the only bank account Plaintiff maintains is a joint checking account in both of their names. (Natalie Decl. ¶ 5.) As of May 31, 2024, the balance on this account was $2,743.50. (Natalie Decl. Exh. 2.) As of June 30, 2024, the balance was $183.25 (Natalie Decl. Exh. 1.)  Natalie states that Plaintiff’s only source of income is Social Security in the amount of $2,249.10 a month. (Natalie Decl.¶ 4.) Natalie attests that Plaintiff currently pays $8,070.00 per month in rent at a separate care facility. (Natalie Decl. ¶ 6.) Natalie states that Plaintiff’s income no longer covers his basic needs. Lastly, Natalie states that Plaintiff has no real property and no assets or investments. (Natalie Decl. ¶¶ 8-9.) 

 

Plaintiff also submits the declaration of his counsel Stephen Garcia (Garcia). Garcia states that in his firm’s experience, an arbitration of this kind typically would cost at least $5,000 per day and last at least seven days. (Garcia Decl. ¶ 4.) Garcia also attaches arbitration billing statements from previous clients in an elder abuse case totaling $37,750, $42,950.00, $98,112.50, $23,060.00, and $98,200.00. (Garcia Decl. Exhs. 8-12.)

 

The Court disagrees with Defendants’ contention that Plaintiff is inaccurately representing his financial position. Defendant argues that Plaintiff provides no explanation for his transfers of $1,400 per month to his daughter, as exhibited in the Zelle transfers documented in the two bank statements provided. The Court does not find the failure to explain these transfers negates Plaintiff’s showing that his current income and bank account balance would not allow him to afford the arbitration process. Further, the Court finds Defendants’ argument that Plaintiff could have transferred large sums of money out of this account to appear indigent is without merit. Defendants have presented no basis to believe that such a transfer occurred. Plaintiffs’ daughter has testified under penalty of perjury that Plaintiff maintains no major assets and lives purely off his social security check. Defendant has provided no title search or any other publicly available documentation which would refute these statements.

 

In short, the Court finds Plaintiff has shown inability to pay for the arbitral process such that Defendant must either pay Plaintiff’s share of the arbitration costs or waive the right to arbitration.

 

Motion for Trial Preference

 

In light of the Court granting the motion to compel arbitration, Plaintiff’s motion for trial preference is MOOT; however, the Court would have been inclined to grant the preference.
