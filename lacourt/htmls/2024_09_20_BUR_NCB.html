LEGAL STANDARD

CCP § 473(a)(1) states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” 

            CRC rule 3.1324 requires a motion seeking leave to amend to include a copy of the proposed pleadings, to identify the amendments, and to be accompanied by a declaration including the following facts:

            1) The effect of the amendment;

            2) Why the amendment is necessary and proper;

            3) When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

            4) The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

The Court’s discretion regarding granting leave to amend is usually exercised liberally to permit amendment of pleadings.  (Nestle v. Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.)  If a motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend.  (Morgan v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff moves to file the proposed SAC.  A copy of the proposed SAC is attached as Exhibit 1 to the motion papers.  A redlined version of the SAC is attached as Exhibit 2. 

            In support of the motion, Plaintiff provides the declaration of counsel Damion Robinson.  Mr. Robinson states that the purpose of the amendment is to separately state and clarify the theories of liability that Plaintiff asserts in this action and to clarify that they sound in both negligent and intentional conduct.  (Robinson Decl., ¶4.)  He states that the SAC seeks to add the 9th through 13th causes of action for negligent misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty to safeguard, breach of fiduciary duty to supervise, and negligent supervision and retention.  (Id., ¶3.)  He states that the new counts are based on the same general subject matter and underlying facts as the FAC.  (Id., ¶4.)  Mr. Robinson states that he was recently retained by Plaintiff and that his firm substituted in as counsel on July 8, 2024.  (Id., ¶5.)  He states that based on a review of the file and consultation with Plaintiff and prior counsel, he determined that it would be prudent to amend the FAC to separately state Plaintiff’s claims for negligent misrepresentation and negligent conduct on the part of Defendants CMM LLP and Robert Schreiber in failing to adequately supervise Defendant Gregory Mogab or to safeguard Plaintiff’s trust assets from misuse or inappropriate investments solicited by Mr. Mogab.  (Id., ¶6.)  He states that no party will be prejudiced by the amendment because trial is scheduled for February 18, 2025, the parties have taken modest discovery, and discovery has largely been deferred awaiting a sale of the property.  (Id., ¶7.)  He states that counsel for CMM LLP and Mr. Schreiber indicated that they would not oppose the filing of the SAC, while the other Defendants have not responded to Mr. Robinson’s emails regarding the filing of the SAC.  (Id., ¶8, Ex. 3.) 

            Based on the moving papers, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file the proposed SAC.  Mr. Robinson’s declaration addresses the factors under CRC Rule 3.1324.  In addition, the Court recognizes the liberal policy in allowing amendment.  “[T]he court's discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.  The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”  (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.)

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

            Plaintiff Hani Mammo (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he hired and retained Defendant ABA Law Group (“ABA”) to represent him and his businesses in several legal matters from 2021 to 2023.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Christopher Reyes was an attorney who passed away in December 2023 and his estate is believed to be Defendant Estate of Christopher Reyes (“Reyes”).  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Bianca Santamaria (“Santamaria”) was Reyes’ wife and that she willfully took and received money and property from Reyes with the intent to defraud Plaintiff and other creditors.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants Edward Damas (“Damas”) and Kurt Fredric Zimmerman (“Zimmerman”) are attorneys who worked on Plaintiff’s case.

  • LASC Case No. 23STCV01410, Racquel Haynes et al. v. Bushra Mammo et al.: Plaintiff alleges that Defendants were grossly negligent, failed to defend or engage in the matter, and failed to report the matter to Plaintiff’s insurance.
  • LASC Case No. 22CHRO00618, Lopez v. Mammo: Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to defend or file the papers necessary to terminate and expunge the case. 
  • LASC Case No. BC604605, Jorge Vargas v. Mammo, et al.: Plaintiff alleges that Defendants only appeared to a single hearing and then failed to attend any further hearings.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ gross negligence caused them to miss several trial dates, such that judgment was entered against Plaintiff in excess of $600,000 in December 2022.

DISCUSSION

            Zimmerman demurs to the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action in the FAC on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts. 

  1. Statute of Limitations

Zimmerman argues that the 1st to 3rd causes of action fail because they are time-barred. 

The statute of limitations period for legal malpractice claims is state in CCP § 340.6:

(a) An action against an attorney for a wrongful act or omission, other than for actual fraud, arising in the performance of professional services shall be commenced within one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts constituting the wrongful act or omission, or four years from the date of the wrongful act or omission, whichever occurs first. … Except for a claim for which the plaintiff is required to establish the plaintiff's factual innocence, the time for commencement of legal action shall not exceed four years except that the period shall be tolled during the time that any of the following exist:

(1) The plaintiff has not sustained actual injury.

(2) The attorney continues to represent the plaintiff regarding the specific subject matter in which the alleged wrongful act or omission occurred.

(3) The attorney willfully conceals the facts constituting the wrongful act or omission when those facts are known to the attorney, except that this subdivision shall toll only the four-year limitation.

(4) The plaintiff is under a legal or physical disability that restricts the plaintiff's ability to commence legal action.

(5) A dispute between the lawyer and client concerning fees, costs, or both is pending resolution under Article 13 (commencing with Section 6200) of Chapter 4 of Division 3 of the Business and Professions Code. …

(b) In an action based upon an instrument in writing, the effective date of which depends upon some act or event of the future, the period of limitations provided for by this section shall commence to run upon the occurrence of that act or event.

(CCP § 340.6.) 

            Zimmerman argues that the FAC alleges facts that Plaintiff was involved in several lawsuits in 2020 and 2021 and that Defendants abandoned him in 2021 and 2022.  (FAC, ¶¶24, 26.)  He argues that the only case in which he had a meaningful connection was in Case No. 20CHCV00262, wherein Plaintiff began representing himself in October 2021.  (Dem. at pp.4-5.)  (The Court notes that Zimmerman refers to a request for judicial notice, but no request for judicial notice document was filed to accompany the demurrer to the FAC papers.) 

However, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants collectively were involved in each of his 7 cases and that they abandoned him or committed malpractice in each of the cases.  Plaintiff alleges that he met with Zimmerman and Reyes several times in 2021 and 2022 and that Defendants (Zimmerman and Reyes most often) would tell Plaintiff they were working on his cases and there was nothing Plaintiff needed to do.  (Id., ¶¶28-29.)  Plaintiff alleges that he reasonably relied on his legal counsel’s statements and assurances that they never missed hearings or failed to tell him about judgments entered against him.  (Id., ¶¶30-31.)  Plaintiff also alleges facts that he learned in late-2023 that he could search for court cases online, wherein he discovered that there were missed trials, missed hearings, sanctions, and legal malpractice by Defendants.  (Id., ¶¶32-34.)  The Court also notes that several of the cases include case numbers beginning in “23-“, which indicates that they were filed in the year 2023. 

At the pleading stage and taking the allegations as true, the Court finds that the FAC sufficiently alleges facts showing that Plaintiff discovered the facts regarding Defendant’s alleged malpractice in late 2023.  This action was initially filed on January 3, 2024.  Further, Plaintiff has alleged facts that Defendants concealed the status of his cases and reassured him that his cases were being handled.  Taking the truth of the allegations at the pleading stage, the Court finds that the allegations are sufficient to allege claims within the statute of limitations period.  “In order for the bar of the statute of limitations to be raised by demurrer, the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows merely that the action may be barred.”  (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Services (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315-16.)

The demurrer on the basis of the statute of limitations is overruled. 

  1. 1st cause of action for Negligence

The elements of a negligence cause of action are “duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.”  (Carlsen v. Koivumaki (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 879, 892.)  In a legal malpractice action arising from a civil proceeding, the elements are (1) the duty of the attorney to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as a member of his or her profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) proximate causal connection between the breach and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the attorney's negligence.”  (Jones v. Whisenand (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 543, 550.)  “Proof of legal malpractice requires proof not only of negligence by the lawyer but also of causation, a trial within a trial to establish that, but for the lawyer's negligence, the client would have prevailed in the underlying action.”  (United Community Church v. Garcin (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 327, 334.) 

In the 1st cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that he was Defendants’ client and that by virtue of the attorney-client relationship, Defendants owed him a duty to act with the utmost good faith and in his best interest.  (FAC, ¶39.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants grossly failed to meet the standards of care when representing him in the 7 underlying cases and thereby breached their duty to Plaintiff.  (Id., ¶¶40-41.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants caused the losses and injuries by their abandonment of the cases in which they represented Plaintiff and but-for Defendants’ failures to act, Plaintiff would not have suffered damages in excess of $850,000.  (Id., ¶¶42-43.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ negligence was a deviation from the regular standard of care and their acts/omissions fell below the standard of care for comparable attorneys who practice in the community.  (Id., ¶¶44-45.)

Zimmerman argues that Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged causation as the FAC mischaracterizes the underlying litigation matters.  Namely, Zimmerman argues that in the only action he was actively involved in (20CHCV00262), Plaintiff represented himself, which caused the case to be abandoned.  As to the remaining cases, Zimmerman argues that he was not involved in those actions, but rather Reyes was involved as counsel.  (Dem. at pp.8-10.)  Again, these arguments rely largely in part on Zimmerman’s request for judicial notice, but no request for judicial notice was filed with the demurrer papers.  Even if the Court were to take judicial notice of the documents, the Court would only take judicial notice of the existence of the documents, but not for the truths of the matters asserted therein.  Thus, although Zimmerman argues that the judicially noticeable documents may show a certain record of Plaintiff representing himself in propria persona or that certain records show that Zimmerman’s name was not included in captions or court orders, the Court will not take the truths of those matters asserted in a vacuum.  These matters are better left for the summary judgment or trial stage where such contentions and evidence may be substantiated or opposed/disputed.  

In the prior ruling on the demurrer, the Court sustained the demurrer to the 1st cause of action in the complaint, stating that Plaintiff failed to allege sufficient facts regarding causation that Plaintiff would have prevailed in the underlying actions or in the defense of the underlying actions.  Plaintiff has done so now with his additional “but-for” allegations in paragraph 40.  For example, Plaintiff has alleged fact that but-for Defendants’ negligence and inaction in Case No. 23CHCV00863, Plaintiff would have prevailed in the matter and not been forced to incur legal fees, costs, and delay in stating and protecting his rights.  (FAC, ¶40 at 10:21-27.)  He alleges that Defendants caused his loss and injuries by abandoning Plaintiff and that but for their failures to act, he would not have been injured in an amount over $850,000.  (Id., ¶¶42-43.) 

The Court finds these allegations sufficient to support negligence/legal malpractice.  The demurrer to the 1st cause of action is overruled. 

  1. 2nd cause of action for Breach of Oral Contract

The essential elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) “An oral contract may be pleaded generally as to its effect, because it is rarely possible to allege the exact words.”  (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

Zimmerman argues that the FAC does not allege that Plaintiff made an offer to Zimmerman to represent Plaintiff in the underlying action, or that Zimmerman accepted the offer.  Zimmerman also argues that the FAC alleges oral contracts or a single oral contract, as well as facts that Plaintiff entered into the oral contract for himself and several businesses, but the businesses have not been included as parties to the action. 

In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges that he hired and retained ABA, he entered into a contract for legal services with ABA to represent himself and several businesses he owns (Mammo Enterprises LLC and others), and ABA assigned Reyes and Zimmerman to work as lead counsel on Plaintiff’s cases.  (FAC, ¶¶2, 25.)  In the 2nd cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants entered into oral contracts (plural) during in-person meetings in 2020 to represent Plaintiff in the 7 matters.  (FAC, ¶49.)  Plaintiff alleges that the material terms of the contract (singular) were that Defendants would legally represent Plaintiff in the pending legal matters and Plaintiff would pay them at a rate of $400/hour billed periodically or monthly.  (Id.)  He alleges that as new lawsuits arose, they were added to the representation agreement/contract between the parties. (Id.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to fulfill their contractual obligations to represent Plaintiff, and that Plaintiff fulfilled his contractual duties by paying them.  (Id., ¶50.) 

Here, the allegations are somewhat unclear whether there was only a single oral contract or multiple oral contracts.  In addition, the FAC does not allege whether ABA (and its attorneys, Reyes and Zimmerman) agreed to represent Plaintiff and his entities, or just Plaintiff himself.  Plaintiff should allege further facts regarding the nature of the contract(s) and the terms. 

As such, the demurrer to the 2nd cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

  1. 3rd cause of action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty  

The caption of the FAC states that the 3rd cause of action is for negligence – legal malpractice, but the body of the FAC states that the 3rd cause of action is for breach of fiduciary duty.  (See FAC at pp. 1, 14.) 

To state a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, Plaintiff must allege: (1) the existence of a fiduciary relationship; (2) its breach; and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach.  (Roberts v. Lomanto (2003) 112 Cal. App. 4th 1553, 1562.)  A fiduciary relationship is any relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is in duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.  (Wolf v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 625, 629.)  “The relation between attorney and client is a fiduciary relation of the very highest character, and binds the attorney to most conscientious fidelity ....”  (Pierce v. Lyman (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1102.)  The basic fiduciary obligations include undivided loyalty and confidentiality. (Id.)  Such fiduciary duty includes the obligation to render a full and fair disclosure to the client of all facts which materially affect his rights and interests.  (Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176, 188-189.) 

In the 3rd cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that all Defendants had an attorney-client relationship with Plaintiff and they each owed him a fiduciary duty to act with the utmost good faith and in his best interests and legal representation – a duty which they breached by failing to represent him, abandoning his legal matters, and making false and willfully misleading statements to Plaintiff.  (FAC, ¶53.)  He alleges that these actions fell below the fiduciary duty owed under the attorney-client relationship to Plaintiff.  (Id.)  Plaintiff alleges that the attorneys and employees of ABA failed to follow the common standards of care which a reasonable attorney or staff member would have undertaken.  (Id., ¶54.) 

Zimmerman argues that a breach of fiduciary duty claim in the attorney-client context will only lie where an attorney converts his client’s business opportunities, obtains an unfair advantage at his client’s expense, or has a conflict of interest.  (Dem. at p.14.)  He argues that Plaintiff’s allegations that ABA and its attorneys/staff failed to follow the common standards of care that a reasonable attorney would have undertaken (FAC, ¶54) only supports negligence. 

There may be some overlap between a breach of fiduciary duty and negligence claim, given the elements of each of these causes of action, and this in itself will not render a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action as duplicative of a negligence cause of action.  However, here, Plaintiff has not alleged facts that are distinct from the legal malpractice cause of action.  The only factual difference is that Plaintiff has alleged that he had an attorney-client relationship with Defendants that was the basis of the fiduciary duty.  Further facts should be alleged to support the nature of the fiduciary relationship and how Defendants (and Zimmerman) breached that duty.   

The demurrer to the 3rd cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.   

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant Kurt Zimmerman’s demurrer to the FAC is overruled as to the 1st cause of action.  The demurrer is sustained with 20 days leave to amend as to the 2nd and 3rd causes of action.

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

Plaintiffs Vigan Sardariani (“Sardariani”) and V&S Complete Auto Repairs, Inc. (“V&S”) allege that this action involves a 22-year-old lease relationship between Sardariani and Defendant Roland Markarian (“Markarian”) concerning real property located at 7139 Foothill Blvd., Tujunga, CA 91042 (the “Foothill Property”).  The Court will call this the “Fraud Action” in its subsequent discussion, based on the Court’s review of its nine causes of action.

In the Fraud Action, Plaintiffs allege that Markarian rented the Foothill Property in Tujunga on May 1, 2002 subject to the lease agreement with Sardariani.  Plaintiffs allege that Markarian sued Plaintiffs on May 11, 2022 in Pasadena Case No. 22PDUD00815, Markarian v. Sardariani.  Plaintiffs alleged that on June 8, 2016, Markarian, without notice to Plaintiffs, transferred ownership of the leased property to Defendant RRRM Holding Company, LLC (“RRRM”).  Plaintiffs allege that Markarian and RRRM persuaded Plaintiffs’ sublessor, Defendants Ara Ahanjanyan and Arman Artunyan, to breach their sublease with Plaintiffs.  Plaintiffs allege that on December 29, 2023, RRRM initiated an unlawful detainer action in Burbank Case No. 23BBCV03087, RRRM Holding Company, LLC v. Sardariani.  Plaintiffs allege that RRRM has made false representations that it leased property to Sardariani, that rent had increased (without proper notice), and that RRRM performed all terms and conditions on the lease when it had not. 

The first amended complaint (“FAC”), filed March 14, 2024, alleges causes of action for: (1) fraud; (2) intentional concealment; (3) negligent misrepresentation; (4) breach of duty of good faith and fair dealing; (5) breach of contract; (6) tortious interference with prospective economic advantage; (7) negligent interference with prospective economic advantage; (8) intentional interference with contractual relations; and (9) inducing breach of contract.

On July 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an Amendment to Complaint Naming Doe No. 1, naming Rejina Mehdian as Doe 1.

B.     Demurrers on Calendar

There are 4 demurrers on calendar.  On August 16, 2024, Defendants Roland Markarian, RRRM Holding Company, LLC, Ara Ohanjanyan, and Arman Arutyunyan (“Defendants”) filed 4 demurrers to Plaintiffs’ FAC. 

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiffs Sardariani and V&S filed a single opposition to the demurrers. 

On September 13, 2024, Defendants filed a reply brief.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants filed 4 demurrers to the FAC.  The 4 demurrers are identical in content, such that the Court will consider the 4 demurrers together.  Defendants demur to the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action for fraud, intentional concealment, and negligent misrepresentation. 

A.    1st cause of action for Fraud by Sardariani against Markarian

To allege a cause of action for fraud, the requisite elements are: (1) a representation, usually of fact, which is false; (2) knowledge of its falsity; (3) intent to defraud; (4) justifiable reliance upon the misrepresentation; and (5) damage resulting from that justifiable reliance.  (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal. App. 3d 59, 72-73.)  This cause of action is a tort of deceit and the facts constituting each element must be alleged with particularity; the claim cannot be saved by referring to the policy favoring liberal construction of pleadings.  (Committee on Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216.)  Since the claim must be pleaded with particularity, the cause of action based on misrepresentations must allege facts showing how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the misrepresentations were tendered.  (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.)

In the 1st cause of action, Plaintiffs allege that at the time of entry into the lease, Markarian made false representations to Sardariani that harmed Sardariani, including: (a) Markarian would abide by the terms of the lease; (b) Markarian would provide Sardariani with a right of first refusal to purchase the property; (c) Markarian would charge only the amount of rent at the time of the expiration of the first lease term on a month-to-month holdover tenancy; (d) Markarian would permit Sardariani to sublet the property; (e) the original lease agreement between Sardariani and Markarian evidences the foregoing false statements of Defendants that were false at the time they were made or were made with reckless disregard for their falsity; (f) the subtenants and their site manager were advised on behalf of Markarian by counsel Raymond Zakari to stop paying rent to Sardariani and start issue sublease rent checks to Markarian, and Markarian through Mr. Zakari induced the subtenants to sign a new lease directly with Markarian or RRRM; and (g) in 2018, Markarian falsely claimed rent for April 2018 was not paid.  (FAC, ¶20.)  Sardariani alleges that Markarian represented to him that these facts were true, when they were actually false.  (Id., ¶21.)  Sardariani alleges that Markarian intended him to rely on the representations to induce him to rent the premises, and Sardariani reasonably relied on the false representations.  (Id., ¶¶22-23.)  

            Defendants demur to the 1st cause of action, arguing that it is duplicative of the 5th cause of action for breach of contract.  They argue that the alleged misrepresentations are with respect to their purported breaches under the lease. 

            The representations in paragraph 20(a) to (d) essentially amount to allegations for breach of contract or breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing regarding performance of the lease agreement.  In addition, subsections (e) to (g) do not appear to be “misrepresentations” that were made by Markarian to Sardariani, but are rather facts regarding Markarian’s conduct.  With respect to subsection (g), Markarian’s statement that Sardariani did not pay rent in April 2018 occurred after the parties entered into the lease agreement on May 1, 2002, such that it is unclear how this particular “misrepresentation” could have been relied upon by Sardariani to enter into the lease agreement for rental of the subject premises.  (See FAC, ¶¶20, 22.) 

            As currently worded, the fraud cause of action fails to allege facts regarding the misrepresentations that were made by Markarian to Sardariani at the time the parties entered the lease agreement.  The 1st cause of action also fails to allege the misrepresentation(s) with the requisite specificity. 

In opposition, Sardariani argues that Markarian concealed the transfer of the property so that Sardariani would be unaware that his right of first refusal had been triggered; however, this is not an allegation for misrepresentation that was made in connection with the 1st cause of action for fraud.  

            For these reasons, the demurrers to the 1st cause of action are sustained with leave to amend. 

B.     2nd cause of action for intentional concealment by Sardariani against Markarian and RRRM

The elements for fraudulent concealment are the following: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact; (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff; (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.  (Lovejoy v. AT&T Corp. (2004) 119 Cal. App. 4th 151, 157-158.)  This too is a cause of action for deceit and the facts constituting each element must be alleged with particularity; the claims cannot be saved by referring to the policy favoring liberal construction of pleadings. (Committee on Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216.)

In the 2nd cause of action, Sardariani alleges that Markarian and RRRM “concealed certain information as alleged more fully hereinabove, including the unnoticed transfer of ownership of the premises.”  (FAC, ¶27.)  Sardariani alleges that Markarian and RRRM intentionally failed to disclose certain facts that were known only to them and that Sardariani could not have discovered or, alternatively, they disclosed some facts to Sardariani but intentionally failed to disclose other facts.  (Id., ¶¶28-29.)  Sardariani alleges that he did not know of the concealed facts and that Markarian and RRRM intended to deceive Sardariani by concealing the facts.  (Id., ¶¶30-31.)  Sardariani alleges that had the omitted information been disclosed, he reasonably would have behaved differently.  (Id., ¶32.) 

Markarian and RRRM argue that Sardariani has not alleged what “certain fact” was concealed or, alternatively, what facts were concealed and which facts were not concealed.  (FAC, ¶¶27, 29.) 

Based on the allegations of the 2nd cause of action, it is ambiguous as to the nature of the concealment.  While, in part, Sardariani alleges that the concealment involved the unnoticed transfer of ownership of the premises, Sardariani also ambiguously alleges that “some facts” were disclosed but that Markarian and RRRM failed to disclose “other facts.”  (FAC, ¶29.)  The nature of what facts were disclosed and what other facts were not disclosed have not been alleged.  Thus, the Court will sustain the demurrers with leave to amend as to the 2nd cause of action so that Sardariani may allege additional facts to plead the elements with the requisite specificity. 

C.     3rd cause of action for negligent misrepresentation by Sardariani against Markarian and RRRM

To allege a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, the requisite elements are: (1) a misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact; (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true; (3) with intent to induce another's reliance on the fact misrepresented; (4) ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance thereon by the party to whom the misrepresentation was directed; and (5) damages.  (B.L.M. v. Sabo & Deitsch (1997) 55 Cal. App. 4th 823, 834.)  Negligent misrepresentation, unlike fraud, does not require knowledge of falsity.  (Apollo Capital Fund, LLC v. Roth Capital Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 226, 243.)  However, like fraud, “[e]ach element in a cause of action for. . . negligent misrepresentation must be factually and specifically alleged.”  (Cadlo v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (2004) 125 Cal.App.4th 513, 519.)

            In the 3rd cause of action, Sardariani alleges that he was harmed because Markarian and RRRM negligently misrepresented certain facts as alleged in the FAC.  (FAC, ¶36.)  Sardariani alleges that they represented to Sardariani that certain facts were true, when the representations were not true.  (Id., ¶¶37-38.)  He alleges that though Markarian and RRRM may have honestly believed that the representations were true, they had no reasonable grounds to believe the representations were true.  (Id., ¶39.)  Sardariani alleges that Markarian and RRRM intended for Sardariani to rely on the representations and that Sardariani was harmed.  (Id., ¶¶40-41.) 

            For the reasons discussed above, the demurrers to the 3rd cause of action are sustained with leave to amend.  Again, the misrepresentations should be pled with the requisite specificity and the “certain facts” Sardariani alleges in the FAC should be identified and alleged.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants Roland Markarian, RRRM Holding Company, LLC, Ara Ohanjanyan, and Arman Arutunyan’s four demurrers to the FAC are sustained with 20 days leave to amend.  The Case Management Conference is continued to November 25, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

Plaintiffs Edward Gurevich, Zoya Gurevich, and Irina Siegel (“Plaintiffs”) allege that they were involved in a motor vehicle collision with Defendant Steven Schneiderman (“Defendant”) on February 15, 2023 near Riverside Dr and Coldwater Canyon Avenue in Los Angeles.  Plaintiffs allege that Mr. Gurevich was driving with passengers Ms. Gurevich and Ms. Siegel towards an intersection, when Defendant turned into an adjacent lane attempting to veer around vehicles that were stopped for a red traffic signal and struck Plaintiffs’ vehicle. 

            The complaint, filed April 2, 2024, alleges causes of action for: (1) general negligence; and (2) motor vehicle.

B.     Cross-Complaint

On May 9, 2024, Mr. Schneiderman filed a cross-complaint against Mr. Gurevich for: (1) indemnification; (2) declaratory relief; and (3) apportionment of fault.

C.     Motion on Calendar

On August 15, 2024, Mr. Gurevich filed a demurrer to the cross-complaint. 

On September 5, 2024, Mr. Schneiderman filed an opposition brief.

On September 11, 2024, Mr. Gurevich filed a reply brief.

DISCUSSION

            Mr. Gurevich demurs to the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action in the cross-complaint.  Mr. Gurevich argues that the cross-complaint is vague and “[e]ach of the three alleged causes of action in defendant/cross-complainant’s cross-complaint fail to indicate, with any level of specificity, the specific individuals’ damages the cross-complainant is seeking relief from. [¶] Said differently, the cross-complaint is vague and ambiguously written as it fails to indicate that Edward Gurevich’s [sic] cross-complaint against Edward Gurevich seeks indemnification, declaratory relief and apportionment of fault with regard to plaintiffs’ [sic] Zoya Gurevich and Irina Siegal’s damages.”  (Dem. at 3:11-16.)  Mr. Gurevich argues that the cross-complaint refers to “[cross-]defendants” generally, but fails to state that Mr. Schneiderman is seeking relief from Mr. Gurevich for damages claimed by Ms. Zurevich and Ms. Siegal.  Other than a recitation of the law on demurrers and counsel’s meet and confer efforts, there are no further arguments raised in the demurrer.  

            In the 1st cause of action for indemnity, Cross-Complainant Mr. Schneiderman seeks indemnity against Cross-Defendant Mr. Zurevich in the event that Mr. Schneiderman is found responsible to “Plaintiff, or to anyone else, as a result of the incidents and occurrences described in Plaintiff’s complaint….”  (XC, ¶6.)  In the 2nd cause of action for declaratory relief, Mr. Schneiderman seeks declaratory relief in the form a judicial determination regarding the percentage of fault between Mr. Schneiderman and each Cross-Defendant (Mr. Gurevich and Roes 1-25) for the damages suffered by the “Plaintiff.”  (Id., ¶¶8-9.)  In the 3rd cause of action for apportionment of fault, Mr. Schneiderman alleges that if he is found liable to “Plaintiff, each Cross-Defendant should be required to pay a share of Plaintiff’s judgment which is in proportion to the comparative negligence of that Cross-Defendant….”  (Id., ¶10.)

            The cross-complaint is not so vague that Mr. Gurevich is unable to respond, particularly as these causes of action are generally raised a cross-complaint, and such claims are typically based on the outcome of the underlying complaint.  However, the Court recognizes that the cross-complaint is vague as to which Plaintiff or Plaintiffs is responsible for the action.  Cross-Complainant uses the singular term “Plaintiff” without indicating which one.    Thus, the Court will sustain the demurrer to the cross-complaint so that Mr. Schneiderman can clarify and identify which of the three Plaintiffs are subject to the cross-complaint (or to allege that all three are subject to the cross-complaint), as well as properly refer to Plaintiff/Plaintiffs in the plural or singular form. This is a relatively simple issue to fix and the only ground upon which Mr. Gurevich has demurred. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Edward Gurevich’s demurrer to the cross-complaint is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

Cross-Defendant shall provide notice of this order.
