The heading on your e-mail should contain the case name, number, hearing date, and that you submit. The message should indicate your name, contact information, and the party you represent. Please note, the above e-mail address is to inform the court of your submission on the tentative ruling. All other inquiries will not receive a response.

If there are no appearances by either side and no submission on the Court's tentative ruling, the matter will be placed OFF CALENDAR. 

Due to overcrowding concerns of COVID-19, all parties shall make every effort to schedule a remote appearance via LACourtConnect (https://my.lacourt.org/laccwelcome) for their next hearing. The parties shall register with LACourtConnect at least 2 hours prior to their scheduled hearing time.

Case Number: 22STLC03523    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Mojaver v. Bayard, et al.

DEMURRER

(CCP § 430.10, et seq.)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Michael J. Bayard’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On May 25, 2022, Plaintiff Michael M. Mojaver (“Plaintiff”), in propria persona, filed the Complaint in this action against Defendant Michael J. Bayard (“Defendant”). Proof of service by mail was filed on February 28, 2023 and April 10, 2023. The case came for trial on November 22, 2023, at which time the Court placed the trial off calendar and set an order to show cause regarding Plaintiff’s failure to enter Defendant’s default and default judgment. (Minute Order, 11/22/23.)

 

The Motion to Quash was filed by Defendant on February 1, 2024 and was initially set for hearing in Department 25 in the Spring Street Courthouse. On February 29, 2024, the case was transferred to Department 26 in the Spring Street Courthouse. The Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Quash Service of the Summons and Complaint on April 9, 2024. (Minute Order, 04/09/24.)

 

At the Order to Show Cause Re Failure to File Proof of Service of Summons on April 30, 2024, Plaintiff requested additional time to serve Defendant. (Minute Order, 04/30/24.) The Order to Show Cause was continued to October 29, 2024. (Ibid.) On June 10, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint, thereby submitting themselves to the jurisdiction of this Court. No opposition to the Demurrer has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

The Demurrer is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. (Demurrer, Backus Decl., Exh. A.) The Demurrer is brought on the basis that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action and is uncertain. (Notice of Demurrer, pp. 2:1-3:4 [citing Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e), (f)].) Special demurrers, however, are not permitted in the limited jurisdiction court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) Therefore, the Court will not rule on the demurrer for uncertainty.

 

Allegations in the Complaint

 

The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) Breach Of Contract; 2) Breach Of Agreement; (3) Promissory Estoppel; (4) Unjust Enrichment; (5) Quantum Meruit; (6) Inflection Of Emotional Stress; and (7) Open Book Account, based on the following facts. Plaintiff was harmed by Defendant’s breach of a contract with the American Arbitration Association (“AAA”). (Compl., pp. 1:22-2:1.) Plaintiff further alleges that he was a primary party to that contract. (Id. at p. 2:3-4.) Defendant was to provide “neutral, unbiased, and impartial oversight over the matter that was a subcontract between Plaintiff and AAA.” (Id. at pp. 2:21-24.) Defendant violated the terms of the contract by allowing a bogus and untimely counterclaim to be filed, by refusing to ban ex parte communications, and by failing to be unbiased and impartial, thereby causing Plaintiff financial harm. (Id. at p. 4:1-24.)

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant asks that the Court take judicial notice of the following documents:

 

1.      Mojaver’s Complaint on behalf of Newport Construction, Inc. against general contractor, C.W. Driver LLC, LASC Case No. 23AHCV00452, consisting of a two-page Form Complaint and a 12-page Complaint For Damages (March 2, 2023);

2.      Mojaver’s Notice of Termination and Dissolution of Newport Construction, Inc. (March 20, 20);

3.      Order Granting Defendants’ Motion to Stay LASC Case No. 23AHCV00452 pending Completion of Arbitration (June 29, 2023);

4.      Mojaver’s Affidavit to Disqualify Judge Oldendorf per CCP § 170.6 (February 20, 2024);

5.      Mojaver’s Motion for Disqualify Judge Oldendorf pursuant to CCP §§ 170.1-170.9 (February 20, 2024);

6.      Judge Oldendorf Order Denying the Affidavit and Motion to Disqualify (February 21, 2024);

7.      Defendants’ Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (Final Arbitration Award, Exhibit 8, pages 111 to 113) (March 12, 2024);

8.      Court Order Granting Defendants’ Petition to Confirm Award (March 22, 2024);

9.      Judgment in favor of Defendants and against Mojaver in the Second Lawsuit (April 9, 2024); and

10.  AAA Construction Industry Arbitration Rules and Mediation Procedures

The request for judicial notice asks the Court to take judicial notice pursuant to Evidence Code sections 451 to 453, specifically, section 452, subdivisions (c), (d), and (h). With respect to court records, the Court cannot take judicial notice of the facts contained therein and may only take judicial notice of the existence of those records. (See Adams v. Bank of America, N.A. (2020) 51 Cal.App.5th 666, 673 [citing Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1569–1570].) The Court’s judicial notice of these documents is limited accordingly.

 

Demurrer to Complaint

 

Defendant argues that the claims in the Complaint are barred by the doctrine of arbitral immunity, as articulated by the Court of Appeals:

 

“As our Supreme Court has stated: Arbitrators are judges chosen by the parties to decide the matters submitted to them … Arbitrators have been extended the protection of judicial immunity because they perform the function of resolving disputes between parties, or of authoritatively adjudicating private rights. (In re Marriage of Assemi (1994) 7 Cal.4th 896 909 [30 Cal. Rptr. 2d 265, 872 P.2d 1190]) … It has long been recognized that, in private arbitration proceedings, an arbitrator enjoys the benefit of an arbitral privilege [of immunity] because the role that he or she exercises is analogous to that of a judge. … There is hardly any aspect of arbitration law and practice more settled, both in domestic and international relations, than the immunity of arbitrators from court actions for their activities in arriving at their award. … This rule—immunizing arbitrators in private contractual arbitration proceedings from tort liability—is well established in California. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 650).” (internal quotations omitted).

 

(La Serena Properties, LLC v. Weisbach (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 893, 901.) Arbitrators, therefore, are immunized from suit for their activities in reaching an arbitration award. Here, the Complaint alleges that Defendant improperly allowed a bogus counterclaim to be filed in violation of the statute of limitations, set the evidentiary hearing for two days instead of one day, and was biased. These allegations are barred by the aforementioned doctrine of immunity, which is broadly applied to different causes of action, including failure to disclose, fraudulent concealment, breach of contract, unfair business practices and negligence. (See id. at 899.) As the allegations in support of Plaintiff’s causes of action pertain to Defendant’s activities reaching the arbitration award, they are barred by arbitral immunity. Nor has Plaintiff filed any opposition demonstrating that the doctrine of arbitral immunity does not apply or that other facts can be alleged to overcome said immunity.

 

Conclusion

 

Therefore, Defendant Michael J. Bayard’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

Case Number: 23STLC04893    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Romex Textiles, Inc. v. Promises, Promises, Inc., et al.

RECONSIDERATION

(CCP § 1008)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants Promises, Promises, Inc. and Eugene Michael Hardy’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

 

 

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Romex Textiles, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for breach of contract and common counts against Defendants Promises, Promises, Inc. (“Defendant PPI”) and Eugene Michael Hardy (“Defendant Hardy”) on August 3, 2023. The First Amended Complaint was filed on October 23, 2023. On January 25, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint with leave to amend. (Minute Order, 01/25/24.) The Motion to Strike was accordingly deemed moot. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff filed the Second Amended Complaint on February 13, 2024. On April 16, 2024, Defendants filed a Demurrer to, and Motion to Strike Portions of, the Second Amended Complaint. The Demurrer and Motion to Strike came for hearing on May 14, 2024 at which time the Court heard argument from Plaintiff and took the matter under submission. (Minute Order, 05/14/24.) On May 20, 2024, the Court overruled the Demurrer and ordered Defendants to answer within 20 days. (Minute Order, 05/20/24.)

 

Defendants filed the instant Motion for Reconsideration on May 30, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on June 24, 2024 and Defendants replied on June 28, 2024.

 

Discussion

 

The Motion is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a), which states in relevant part:

 

When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).) The Court lacks the jurisdiction to reconsider a prior ruling, on motion of a party, where the motion does not comply with the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1008. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (e); Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1106.) Additionally, there is a diligence requirement: “Courts have construed section 1008 to require a party filing an application for reconsideration or a renewed application to show diligence with a satisfactory explanation for not having presented the new or different information earlier.” (Even Zohar Construction & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 839.)

 

Defendants move for reconsideration of the order overruling their Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff was a suspended corporation that could not oppose the Demurrer; (2) the Court did not consider their reply or request for judicial notice; and (3) new law supports reconsideration.

 

In support, Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of the following: (1) this Court’s May 20, 2024 ruling on submitted matter in Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23STLC04893, filed in this Court on May 20, 2024; (2) the California Secretary of State’s May 20, 2024 Certificate of Status regarding Plaintiff Romex Textiles, Inc.; (3) the May 20, 2024 Entity Status Letter from the State of California Franchise Tax Board regarding Plaintiff; (4) the January 26, 2022 Audit Assessment of Plaintiff by the City of Los Angeles Office of Finance; (5) the Complaint in City of Los Angeles v. Romex Textiles, Inc., Los Angeles Superior

Court Case No. 24STCP00704, filed in this Court on March 7, 2024; and (6) the October 10, 2022 Notice of Judgment Lien filed with the California Secretary of State against Plaintiff in connection with the judgment of $9,044,383.56 entered against Plaintiff in Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 19STCV40836. The request is granted as to Exhibits 1 and 2. The request is denied as to exhibits 3 to 6 for lack of relevance.

 

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to the declaration of Attorney Hardy are ruled on as follows: No. 1 overruled; Nos. 2-9 sustained.

 

Regarding the first basis for reconsideration, Plaintiff’s corporate status at the time of the Demurrer, the opposition demonstrates that Plaintiff has been conditionally revived by the Secretary of State as of June 24, 2024. (Opp., Binafard Decl., Exh. 1.) “[A] suspended corporation which pays its taxes and obtains a certificate of revivor during the pendency of an action may be allowed to carry on the litigation, even to the extent of validating otherwise invalid prior proceedings.” (Gar-Lo, Inc. v. Prudential Sav. & Loan Assn. (1974) 41 Cal.App.3d 242, 244.) That Plaintiff’s corporate status was suspended when it opposed the Demurrer, therefore, is not grounds for reconsideration of the Court’s ruling.

 

Defendants also argue that because alter ego is an equitable doctrine, Plaintiff’s deception in appearing at the demurrer hearing while suspended is grounds to bar those allegations. In support of this argument, Defendants cite Quick v. Pearson (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 371 for the proposition that “a party who seeks equity must also do equity.” (Quick v. Pearson (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 371, 380.) The Court of Appeals explained what it means to “do equity” as follows: “‘The unclean hands doctrine “closes the doors of a court of equity to one tainted with inequitableness or bad faith relative to the matter in which he seeks relief.” ’ [citations omitted].” (Ibid.) The question of bad faith, therefore, does not concern Plaintiff’s litigation conduct. Rather, it concerns Plaintiff’s conduct with respect to the parties’ business transactions.

 

Regarding the second basis for reconsideration, Defendants rely on cases that are distinguishable from this action. Specifically, in those cases, the trial courts failed to consider memoranda filed or arguments made by the moving party but also that the moving parties could not have presented earlier. In Johnston v. Corrigan (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 553, the Court of Appeals affirmed the trial court’s ruling granting reconsideration of the cross-defendants’ motion for attorney’s fees following an anti-SLAPP motion. Reconsideration was proper because the trial court had previously and incorrectly ruled that the cross-defendants had not filed any points and authorities in support of their special motion to strike; in the motion for reconsideration, the cross-defendants demonstrated that they had, in fact, filed said points and authorities. (Johnston v. Corrigan (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 553, 555.)

 

In Kalivas v. Barry Controls Corp. (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1152, the Court of Appeals ruled that reconsideration should have been granted when a party, as a result of being misled by a confusing courtroom rule, filed no opposition to a motion for summary judgment and did not appear at the hearing. (Kalivas v. Barry Controls Corp. (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1152, 1161.) Finally, in Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, the family law court erred in denying reconsideration when the moving party’s attorney was barred from introducing evidence of a stay issued in a related foreclosure action. (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1447-1448.)

 

Here, Defendants were provided opportunities to present their arguments: they filed a reply in support of the Demurrer, which the Court’s ruling expressly acknowledged. (Minute Order, 05/14/24.) Defendants also had the opportunity to appear at the hearing and argue but instead submitted on the tentative ruling, which was to overrule the Demurrer as to Defendant PPI but sustain it as to Defendant Hardy. (Minute Order, 05/14/24.) The Motion for Reconsideration does not provide a satisfactory explanation for failing to raise these arguments earlier.

 

Finally, Defendants point to two new cases as “new law” that support reconsideration. Neither case constitutes “new law” in the sense that they stand for a proposition never previously articulated. The Motion points to Campbell v. Los Angeles Unified School District (2024) 2024 WL 2281587, at *1 for the proposition that courts have the ability to sustain demurrers without leave to amend. This has long been established by case law. In the other case, D.K. v. Office of Administrative Hearings (2024) 2024 WL 2147805, at *1, Defendants rely on the Court of Appeals’ ruling taking judicial notice of an involuntary medication order. Again, there is no question of matters this Court could take judicial notice at the time of the hearing on the Demurrer. Therefore, this case is not new law as contemplated by the reconsideration statute. (See Ovitz v. Schulman (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 830, 848 [law is not new if it relies on earlier decisions that invoke the same principle].)

 

 

 

Conclusion

 

Therefore, Defendants Promises, Promises, Inc. and Eugene Michael Hardy’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

Case Number: 23STLC06236    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 26

 

Lupa-Chazan v. Garcia, et al.

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, SET TWO; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(CCP §§ 2031.300, 2023.030)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Daniel Lupa-Chazan’s Motion to Compel Further Responses, Without Objection, to Request for Production, Sets One and Two, and Request for Sanctions is DENIED.

    

ANALYSIS:

 

On September 28, 2023, Plaintiff Daniel Lupa-Chazan, (“Plaintiff”), in pro se, filed this action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence against Defendants Alicia Garcia (“Defendant Alicia”) and Sergio Garcia (“Defendant Sergio”). Following Defendants’ Motion to Strike, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint on March 11, 2024.

 

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Sets One and Two, and Request for Sanctions on June 11, 2024. Defendants filed an opposition on June 24, 2024 and Plaintiff replied two days later.

 

Discussion

 

Upon receipt of responses to discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that the responses are evasive, incomplete, an objection is without merit or too general. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) A motion for an order compelling further responses shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2)) and notice of the motion shall be given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or any specific later date to which the parties have agreed in writing, or the party requesting the order waives any right to compel further responses (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).). Defendant served responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Sets One and Two, on April 29, 2024 and June 10, 2024, respectively. The instant motion was timely filed 43 days after service of the responses to Set One. The Motion is also accompanied by a declaration demonstrating that Plaintiff sent defense counsel meet and confer letters on April 30, 2024 and June 10, 2024. (Motion, Lupa-Chazan Decl., Exh. C.) The parties are at an impasse regarding the discovery responses. (Id. at ¶4.)

 

Motions to compel further responses to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, and requests for admission must include a separate statement providing all the information necessary to understand each discovery request and response at issue. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345.) The separate statement must be full and complete so that no person is required to review any other document in order to determine the full request and full response. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(c).) The Motion is accompanied by a separate statement. (Motion, pp. 11-13.)

Finally, a motion requesting an order compelling further responses to a demand for production of documents must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) If good cause is shown, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) “[T]he party who seeks to compel production has met his burden of showing good cause simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (Ibid. [citing Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2001) Discovery, ¶¶ 8:1495 to 8:1495.10, pp. 8H 24 to 8H 26]. Upon a fact-specific showing of relevance, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify its objections. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Sergio’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 1 and 2, and Set Two, No. 3. Plaintiff also moves to compel Defendant Alicia’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, No. 1. The Court will consider each request in turn.

 

Defendant Sergio, Set One, Request No. 1 asks for production of “Sergio Garcia’s medical records.” The First Amended Complaint alleges that Defendant Sergio has Type 2 Diabetes and that drivers with this condition are responsible for alerting their car insurance company and taking their medication. (FAC, p. 3:8-24.) It further alleges that Defendant Sergio did not take his medication on the date of the accident despite knowing a consequence might be hypoglycemia, and that experiencing hypoglycemia while driving would make it probable that Defendant Sergio would lose control of his truck and cause an accident. (Id. at p. 4:1-12.) Defendant Sergio allegedly continued to drive despite having multiple opportunities to pull over and address his hypoglycemia. (Id. at p. 4:14-26.) Despite the allegations that Defendant Sergio’s medical condition is relevant to the subject motor vehicle accident, Plaintiff has not shown good cause for the production of Defendant Sergio’s medical records without limitation. Defendant Sergio rightly objected that this request is overbroad in scope and time but the Motion does not respond to this particular objection.  The allegations regarding Defendant Sergio’s Type 2 Diabetes only refer to the date of the accident but the request seeks all of his medical records regardless of the date or the conditions addressed. The request to compel a further response by Defendant Sergio to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Request No. 1, is denied.

 

Defendant Sergio, Set Two, Request No. 3 similarly asks for “Sergio P. Garcia’s medical records pertaining to his Type 1 diabetes, high blood pressure, bi-polar disorder, and obesity.” Defendant Sergio objects that the request is overbroad in scope and time, relevance, and medical privacy. First, the Court notes that the First Amended Complaint does not allege that Defendant Sergio has Type 1 Diabetes, high blood pressure, or bi-polar disorder. (FAC, p. 3:3-9.) The relevance of any of these conditions to this lawsuit, therefore, has not been demonstrated by Plaintiff. Also, although Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Sergio’s obesity is a factor in causing his Type 2 Diabetes, the Motion does not provide facts otherwise linking the obesity allegation to the accident.

 

In the reply, Plaintiff argues that Defendant Sergio’s medical records are sought to investigate a pattern and practice of failing to manage his health conditions that demonstrate a willful and knowing disregard for the safety of others. The reply cites case law for the proposition that privacy rights must be “weighed in the balance” against competing interests. (Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 26.) The balancing of these interests, however, is not set forth in either the moving or reply papers. The balancing test considers “the strength of the countervailing interest sufficient to warrant disclosure of private information varying according to the strength of the privacy interest itself, the seriousness of the invasion, and the availability of alternatives and protective measures.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 556.) Here, a privacy interest in medical records is extremely strong, and the proposed invasion into Defendant Sergio’s entire medical history is quite serious. Plaintiff does not offer any consideration of available alternatives or protective measures, such as obtaining information through interrogatories, requests for admission, or depositions, or agreeing to a protective order. Therefore, the request to compel a further response by Defendant Sergio to Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, Request No. 3, is denied.

 

Defendant Sergio, Set One, Request No. 2 asks for Defendant Sergio’s driving history, to which he again objected on grounds of overbreadth in time and scope, as well as relevance and privacy. Plaintiff’s request for production of documents regarding Defendant Sergio’s driving history is also problematic because it is without any limitation. Plaintiff argues that the request is reasonable to evaluate whether Defendant Sergio has a history of DUIs, careless and/or reckless driving, or whether he has incurred other accidents due to his alleged health conditions. No authority is cited in the Motion regarding obtaining a defendant’s entire driving history. In the reply, Plaintiff cites a non-existent statute (Code of Civil Procedure section 3032.210 et seq.) and general case law regarding the duty to conduct a reasonable investigation to obtain responsive information (Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1496, 1504) and that “the scope of permissible discovery is one of reason, logic and common sense.’ ” (Seahaus La Jolla Owners Assn. v. Superior Court (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 754, 767). The request to compel a further response by Defendant Sergio to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Request No. 2, is denied.

 

Finally, Defendant Alicia, Set One, Request No. 1 asks for production of “Current vehicle registration for 2021 Chevrolet Silverado, California license plate number 78975N3,” which was the vehicle involved in the accident on March 26, 2023. Defendant Alicia only responded with a relevance objection. The First Amended Complaint alleges that the 2021 Chevrolet Silverado involved in the accident is registered to both Defendants, making them jointly and severally liable. (FAC, p. 6:1-2.) In the Motion, Plaintiff further explains that he was unable to obtain the registration at the time of the accident because Defendant Sergio was unconscious. While this explains Plaintiff need for the registration of the subject vehicle at the time of the accident on March 26, 2023, it does not explain why the demand seeks the “current” vehicle registration. The request to compel Defendant Alicia’s further response to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Request No. 1, is denied.

 

Plaintiff requests an award of monetary sanctions in connection with the instant Motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010 and 2031.310. In light of the the Motion being denied, the request for an award of monetary sanctions is also denied.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Daniel Lupa-Chazan’s Motion to Compel Further Responses, Without Objection, to Request for Production, Sets One and Two, and Request for Sanctions is DENIED.

 

 

Moving party to give notice. 

 

 

 


Case Number: 24STCP00466    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Forward Financing, LLC v. Bianca, et al.

PETITION TO CONFIRM ARBITRATION AWARD

(CCP § 1285, et seq.)

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Petitioner Forward Financing, LLC’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE TO ALLOW PETITIONER TO FILE AND SERVE AN AMENDED PETITION THAT CORRECTS THE DEFECTS NOTED HEREIN.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On February 14, 2024, Petitioner Forward Financing, LLC (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (the “Petition”) against BLC, Inc. dba Bellacures (“BLC”) and Respondent Bianca Christopher (“Respondent”). No proof of service of, nor opposition to, the Petition has been filed to date.

 

Legal Standard

 

“Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.  The petition shall name as respondent all parties to the arbitration and may name as respondents any other persons bound by the arbitration award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.) “If a petition or response under this chapter is duly served and filed, the court shall confirm the award as made, whether rendered in this state or another state, unless in accordance with this chapter it corrects the award and confirms it as corrected, vacates the award or dismisses the proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.)

 

A response to a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award that seeks to vacate or correct the award must be served and filed no later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the respondent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1288.2.)

 

Discussion

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4 requires that the Petition and Notice of Hearing be served on Respondent “in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice” or “[i]f the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made . . . [s]ervice within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4, subds. (a), (b).)

 

First, the Petition does not name all parties to the arbitration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1285. The Petition only names Respondent but does not name BLC. (See Pet., ¶1 and Attachment 8(c).) Second, no proof of service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing has been filed as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4. The arbitration agreement does not provide for the manner of service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing, which therefore must be served in the manner of service for a summons. (Pet., Attachment 4(b).) Accordingly, the Court finds that the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4 are not satisfied.

 

Conclusion

 

Petitioner Forward Financing, LLC’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE TO ALLOW PETITIONER TO FILE AND SERVE AN AMENDED PETITION THAT CORRECTS THE DEFECTS NOTED HEREIN.

Case Number: 24STLC02545    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Robertson’s Ready Mix, Ltd. v. Apex Design Concrete, Inc., et al.

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS-COMPLAINT

(CCP § 428.50)

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Shuyuan Zhang’s Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint is GRANTED. THE PROPOSED CROSS-COMPLAINT IS TO BE FILED AND SERVED WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff Robertson’s Ready Mix, Ltd. (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for automobile subrogation against Defendant Shuyuan Zhang (“Defendant Zhang”) and others. Defendant Zhang filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint on May 9, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50 provides:

 

“(a) A party shall file a cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint.

 

(b) Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial.

 

(c) A party shall obtain leave of court to file any cross-complaint except one filed within the time specified in subdivision (a) or (b).  Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50.) Defendant Zhang moves for leave to file a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, and Defendants Apex Design Concrete LLC and Calixto Andrew Franco III. The Motion is brought on the grounds that the cross-complaint arises out of the same incident that gives rise to the Complaint. (Motion, Deng Decl., p. 2:4-12.) Based on the supporting declaration and lack of any opposition, the Court finds that leave to file a cross-complaint is in the interests of justice.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Shuyuan Zhang’s Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint is GRANTED. THE PROPOSED CROSS-COMPLAINT IS TO BE FILED AND SERVED WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.
