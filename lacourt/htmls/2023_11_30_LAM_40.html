DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV01110    Hearing Date: November 30, 2023    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

 

County of Los Angeles

 

Department 40

 

KAREN FLORES,

                                Plaintiff,

v.

EVERBRANDS, INC., a California corporation; EVERBRANDS, INC., a Delaware corporation; MICHEAL FLORMAN, an individual; YON LAI, an individual, and DOES 1-25,

                               Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          23STCV01110

 Hearing Date:   11/30/23

 Trial Date:        9/3/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

1)     Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set 1), Sent to Michael Florman (General) and Request for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,620.51 Against Michael Florman and his Attorney of Record Allyson Thompson (CRS ID: 4116)

2)     Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set 1), Sent to Everbrands, Inc. (Employment) and Request for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,134.01 Against Everbrands Inc. and their Attorney of Record Allyson Thompson (CRS ID: 5535)

 

Background Allegations

Pleadings

            Plaintiff Karen Flores (Plaintiff) sues Defendants Everbrands, Inc., a California Corporation (Everbrands CA), Everbrands, Inc., a Delaware Corporation (Everbrands DE) (collectively, Everbrands, Inc.), Michael Florman (Florman), Yon Lai (Lai), and Does 1-25 pursuant to a January 18, 2023 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Failure to Pay a Minimum Wage, (2) Failure to Pay Overtime, (3) Failure to Provide rest Breaks, (4) Failure to Provide Meal Breaks, (5) Inaccurate Wage Statements, (6) Waiting Wage Penalties (Cal. Labor Code §§ 201-203), (7) Unfair Competition and Unlawful Business Practices – (Cal. Bus. Code § 17200), and (8) Failure to Provide Records.

            The claims arise from allegations that when Plaintiff was employed by Defendants, she was paid less than the applicable minimum wage, was not compensated for overtime, and was not compensated for and/or provided meal and rest breaks.

           

Motions Before the Court

            These motions, the third and fourth in a series of nine motions to compel filed by Plaintiff, arise from discovery served by Plaintiff on July 24, 2023, as to Defendant Michael Florman and Everbrands, Inc. The discovery at issue served on Defendant Michael Florman were Form Interrogatories-General, while the discovery served on Everbrands, Inc. were Form Interrogatories-Employment. Both were served on Allyson K. Thompson, Esq., and Laura P. Birnbaum, Esq., Plaintiff’s counsel. On September 11, 2023, substantive responses to both sets of discovery were received by Plaintiff from Defense counsel, however the responses were not verified.

            Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter on September 19, 2023, but Defendant did not respond. On October 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed the following motions that are presently before the Court: (1) Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set 1), Sent to Michael Florman (General) and Request for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,620.51 Against Michael Florman and his Attorney of Record Allyson Thompson (CRS ID: 4116) and (2) Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set 1), Sent to Everbrands, Inc. (Employment) and Request for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,134.01 Against Everbrands Inc. and their Attorney of Record Allyson Thompson  (CRS ID: 5535) (hereinafter, “Motions”). Opposition Papers to both Motions were filed by Defendant on November 15, 2023. Plaintiff then filed Reply Papers on November 21, 2023. 

 

Discussion

 

Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Interrogatories  

            If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subds. (b), (c).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (CCP § 2030.290, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

            If a motion to compel responses to interrogatories is filed, the Court may impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c).)

 

Analysis - MOOT

Plaintiff Flores filed this motion for the express purpose of obtaining a court order that does two things: (1) “order Defendant to provide verified responses, without objection to the outstanding discovery,” i.e., FROGs, Set One; and (2) “issue sanctions in the amount of $3,620.51 against Defendant and their attorney of record, jointly and severally.” (See Mot., p. 6.)

It is true that, as served on September 11, 2023, Everbrands CA’s responses to FROGs, Set One, were no responses at all because they were unverified. (Mot., Shirdel Decl., Ex. 3 [Verification page stating verification would follow responses to FROGs, Set One]; see Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.250, subd. (a); Blue Ridge, supra, 202 Cal.App.3d at p. 344; Appleton, supra, 206 Cal.App.3d at p. 236.)

However, on October 26, 2023—after Plaintiff filed her motion on October 2, 2023—Defendant Florman verified Everbrands CA’s responses to FROGs, Set One. That verification appears to have been first served on Plaintiff Flores on November 14, 2023, when Everbrands CA’s opposition was filed. (Opp’n, p. 6, Thompson Decl. Ex. H.)

As a result, the September 11, 2023 responses have been supplemented with a verification that makes them facially code-compliant.

Plaintiff Flores’s motion instead argues that the September 11, 2023 responses are a nullity and that Everbrands CA must present a completely new set of interrogatory responses that are unqualified by objections, which have allegedly been waived. (Mot., p. 5.)

The Court disagrees with Plaintiff. Instead, the Court finds that this motion has been MOOTED.

Plaintiff Flores brought this motion to obtain verified responses to FROGs, Set One. The verification attached to the opposition brought the September 11, 2023 responses up to Code. Plaintiff thus has her verified interrogatory responses and has not provided authority to the effect that, where substantive responses to written discovery are made with verification to follow, a failure to verify within a certain period of time renders the initial unverified responses a nullity such that subsequent verification cannot remedy the lack of verification. The proper vehicle to challenge the sufficiency of the now-verified September 11, 2023 responses to FROGs, Set One, is to file a motion to compel further responses to FROGs, Set One.

The Court does not reach the question of whether objections to FROGs, Set One, were waived through lack of verification, but the Court does note that the opposition contains facts that would support relieving Everbrands CA of any waiver, should it exist. (Opp’n, Thompson Decl., ¶¶ 4-11; Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a)(2).)

 

Sanctions- DENIED

            Although the motions are moot at this point, the issue of sanctions still remains. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348 subd. (a).)

While the Court recognizes the Plaintiff’s frustration at receiving these verifications late, the Court finds that the imposition of sanctions in this case would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. section 2030.300 subd. (d).) Defendant argues that sanctions should be denied because of an inadequate service and meet and confer, claiming the motions they were sent only by email without Plaintiff confirming the correct email address as required by Code Civ. Proc. section 1010.6. They further point out that multiple mistakes were made in the proofs of service, including the usage of a name that does not belong in this case. Further, Defendants argue that the Motions should be denied because Plaintiff did not engage in a good faith meet and confer when they only sent over one letter during a time when Defendants’ counsel was heavily involved in preparing a motion for summary judgment in a different case. (Declaration of Allyson Thompson, ¶ 5.)

The Court accepts Defendant’s representations that the failure to provide the verifications was inadvertent, and finds that the verifications were thereafter provided. Awarding sanctions in every case where a motion to compel is filed before the responses are provided paints with too broad a brush. When ruling on requests for sanctions in discovery, the Court is called upon to distinguish between cases in which a party is either stonewalling and failing to adhere to its duty to provide discovery, or taking needlessly antagonistic or unreasonable positions in discovery on the one hand, and inadvertence and mistakes on the other. Rare is the attorney who has never made a mistake during litigation; not all such mistakes should result in sanctions. On the other hand, where one party is taking unreasonable positions and failing to cooperate, sanctions are an important tool in gaining cooperation or creating consequences.

Moreover, the amount of sanctions requested in these two motions is excessive, where Plaintiff has filed at least three motions on the subject of the failure to provide verifications which are identical but for the precise item of written discovery at issue.

Therefore, the Court finds that the imposition of sanctions in this matter would be unjust.

The Court again encourages the parties to use the Court’s Informal Discovery Conference program which in this case, could have saved Plaintiff from having to file at minimum the two instant motions to compel and the one heard on November 29, 2023.

Having now reviewed the past four motions, the Court declines to send the parties to a discovery referee at this time, since the main issue at least in three of the four motions so far appears to be the issue of sanctions. The Court will retain its right to consider a referee in the future should the nature of the discovery disputes merit it.          

Conclusion

            Plaintiff’s two MOTIONS are DENIED as MOOT and both requests for sanctions are DENIED.
