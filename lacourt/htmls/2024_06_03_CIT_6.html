DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV01453    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Ibrahim Mobarak, et al. v. Lourdes Juarez Garrison 

Cross-Defendant Ibrahim Mobarak’s Motion to Strike Portions of Cross-Complaint 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS Cross-Defendant Ibrahim Mobarak’s motion to strike portions of the cross-complaint without leave to amend. Cross-Defendant is ordered to serve and submit a proposed Order of Dismissal within ten calendar days of this order. 

             Cross-Defendant Ibrahim Mobarak is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within ten calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is an auto accident case. On May 10, 2023, plaintiffs Ibrahim Mobarak (Ibrahim), Mia Mobarak, Kristian Mobarak, and Berlynn Mobarak (collectively, Plaintiffs or Cross-Defendants) filed this action against defendant Lourdes Juarez Garrison (Defendant or Cross-Complainant) and Does 1 to 10, alleging causes of action for motor vehicle and general negligence. 

On November 27, 2023, Cross-Complainant filed the operative cross-complaint against Ibrahim and Roes 1 to 25, alleging causes of action for apportionment of fault, indemnification, and declaratory relief. 

On April 26, 2024, Ibrahim moved to strike portions of the cross-complaint. On May 23, 2024, Cross-Complainant opposed the motion. Ibrahim did not reply. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

            “Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(2).) “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id., § 436.) 

PRELIMINARY ISSUES 

            The Court notes that Cross-Complainant filed and served the opposition on May 23, 2024, more than three days after it was due on May 20, 2024. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) The Court exercises its discretion to still consider the opposition, but admonishes Cross-Complainant to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure going forward. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (d); Juarez v. Wash Depot Holdings, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1197, 1202.) 

DISCUSSION 

Meet and Confer 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a), Ibrahim was required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference before bringing this motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) Ibrahim provided no declaration  evidencing any meet-and-confer efforts. (Id., § 435.5, subd. (a)(3).) Nevertheless, the Court may not deny a motion to strike for failure to adequately meet and confer. (Id., § 435.5, subd. (a)(4).) The Court will still consider the motion to strike, but admonishes Ibrahim to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure going forward. 

Analysis 

Ibrahim seeks to strike each cause of action in the cross-complaint on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, is duplicative of the affirmative defenses pled in the answer to the complaint, and is otherwise improper against Ibrahim. With respect to the First Cause of Action for indemnification, Ibrahim contends it is equivalent to comparative negligence. Ibrahim then argues that it is improper because Cross-Complainant has asserted affirmative defenses of voluntary assumption of risk, negligence of others, apportionment of fault, lack of causation, and failure to wear proper seatbelts and safety restraints. Ibrahim contends that even if he bears some comparative fault, it will be reflected in a reduction of the total liability by virtue of Cross-Complainant’s affirmative defenses. Ibrahim also contends the passenger Plaintiffs are fault-free, but even if they are comparatively negligent, the answer asserts the affirmative seatbelt defense. Ibrahim contends the cross-complaint is proper as to the Roe cross-defendants, but not as to Ibrahim or the passenger Plaintiffs. 

In opposition, Cross-Complainant contends Ibrahim has not demonstrated that any allegations are false, irrelevant, or improper, or that the cross-complaint violates any laws, court rules, or order of the Court. Cross-Complainant contends that the purported duplicative nature of the affirmative defenses alleged in the answer to the complaint is a novel theory of law, and that there is no rule or statute cited that allows for striking the pleading via a motion to strike. Cross-Complainant otherwise contends the cross-complaint properly pleads all required elements for indemnification. 

The Court finds Ibrahim’s motion to be well taken. Equitable indemnity is an equitable doctrine that apportions responsibility among tortfeasors responsible for the same indivisible injury on a comparative fault basis. (American Motorcycle Assn. v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 578, 598.) “[T]he equitable indemnity doctrine originated in the common sense proposition that when two individuals are responsible for a loss, but one of the two is more culpable than the other, it is only fair that the more culpable party should bear a greater share of the loss.” (Id. at p. 593.) “Although the body of law defining and applying principles of equitable indemnity has not fully gelled but is still evolving, one thing is clear: The doctrine applies only among defendants who are jointly and severally liable to the plaintiff.” (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc. v. Forcum/Mackey Construction, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852, emphasis added.) Thus, “a right of equitable indemnity can arise only if the prospective indemnitor and indemnitee are mutually liable to another person for the same injury.” (Fremont Reorganizing Corp. v. Faigin (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1153, 1177, emphasis added.) 

Cross-Complainant alleges in its indemnification (equitable indemnity) count that in the principal action (the Complaint) Ibrahim alleges conduct entitling him to compensatory damages against Cross-Complainant. (Cross-Complaint, First Cause of Action, ¶ 7.b.) Cross-Complainant further alleges that if found liable, his liability would be based solely upon a derivative form of liability resulting from an obligation imposed upon him by law, and therefore he would be entitled to complete indemnity from each cross-defendant, which includes only Ibrahim as the cross-defendant named. (Cross-Complaint, First Cause of Action, ¶ 7.c.) There are no allegations that Cross-Complainant and Ibrahim are liable to another person for the same injury, only that Ibrahim is liable to Cross-Complainant for the injuries Ibrahim suffered. Thus, there is no basis for equitable indemnity. 

Moreover, this First Cause of Action is duplicative of affirmative defenses in Cross-Complainant’s answer. The answer asserts affirmative defenses of comparative fault alleging any damages suffered by Ibrahim are the result of the comparative fault of Ibrahim and must be adjusted accordingly. (Answer – Tenth, Eleventh Affirmative Defenses.) The Second Cause of Action for apportionment of fault likewise alleges that if Cross-Complainant is liable for any damages suffered by Ibrahim, those damages were caused in whole or in part by Ibrahim and Ibrahim should be required to pay a share of Ibrahim’s judgment in proportion to his comparative negligence. (Cross-Complaint, Second Cause of Action, ¶ 8.a., b.) Thus, the Second Cause of Action against Ibrahim also duplicates Cross-Complainant’s affirmative defenses — both allege Ibrahim, not Cross-Complainant, is responsible for his own damages (Answer – First, Tenth, Eleventh Affirmative Defenses.) Therefore, Cross-Complainant can obtain the remedy he seeks via his affirmative defenses and the Cross-Complaint is unnecessary. (See Lauriedale Associates, Ltd. v. Wilson (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1439, 1444 [The cross-complaint was properly dismissed as unnecessary because equivalent relief was available through affirmative defenses asserted by defendant].) 

Finally, "when an issue can be raised by means of an affirmative defense, a trial judge may therefore dismiss a declaratory relief claim raising the same questions in the cross-complaint." (C.J.L. Construction, Inc. v. Universal Plumbing (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 376, 391; see also Welfare Investment Co. v. Stowell (1933) 132 Cal.App. 275, 276-278 [trial judge "properly" struck cross-complaint when all of the issues raised by that pleading were likewise alleged as affirmative defenses].) Here, the Third Cause of Action for declaratory relief seeks a declaration regarding liability alleging that the subject accident was caused in whole or in party by Ibrahim’s negligence. (Cross-Complaint, Third Cause of Action, ¶ 9.) As with the first and second causes of action, the Third Cause of Action raises the same issues of comparative negligence/fault asserted in the Answer as affirmative defenses. Thus, the Third Cause of Action can be stricken. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion to strike the First, Second and Third Causes of Action in the Cross-Complaint without leave to amend as to Ibrahim. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Cross-Defendant Ibrahim Mobarak’s motion to strike portions of the cross-complaint without leave to amend. Cross-Defendant is ordered to serve and submit a proposed Order of Dismissal within ten calendar days of this order. 

             Cross-Defendant Ibrahim Mobarak is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within ten calendar days of this order.


Case Number: 23PSCV01590    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Xchange USA Inc. v. MEG Home Plus, LLC, et al. 

1 – Cross-Defendants’ Special Motion to Strike (Anti-SLAPP);

2 – Defendants’ Demurrer to Xchange USA, Inc. and Xchange Logistics Corporation’s First Amended Complaint; and

3 – Defendants’ Motion to Strike Xchange USA, Inc. and Xchange Logistics Corporation’s First Amended Complaint 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court DENIES Xchange USA, Inc.’s and Xchange Logistics Corporation’s special motion to strike (anti-SLAPP) and DENIES the request for attorney’s fees and costs. 

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the First Amended Complaint with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

The Court DENIES the motion to strike as to the allegations in the FAC pertaining to the addition of Xchange Logistics as a plaintiff and the purportedly immaterial allegations, and GRANTS the motion with respect to the prayer for special damages. Accordingly, the Prayer for Relief, ¶ A, p. 7:3-4, is stricken with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a breach of contract action. On May 26, 2023, plaintiff Xchange USA Inc. filed this action. On March 26, 2024, plaintiffs Xchange USA Inc. (Xchange USA) and Xchange Logistics Corporation (Xchange Logistics) (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed the operative First Amended Complaint (FAC) against defendants MEG Home Plus (MEG), LLC, Jie Li, Cynthia Li, Jing Li (Jing) (collectively, Defendants), and Does 1 to 25, alleging causes of action for breach of contract and common count: open book account. 

On February 15, 2024, cross-complainant Meg filed the operative cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants Xchange USA, Xchange Logistics, and Mia Zhang, and Roes 1 to 99, alleging causes of action for fraud, breach of contract, conversion, and cancellation of instrument. 

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND – Anti-SLAPP 

On April 2, 2024, Xchange USA and Xchange Logistics (collectively, Moving Parties) filed a special motion to strike (anti-SLAPP) against each cause of action in the cross-complaint. On March 29, 2024, MEG opposed the motion.[1] On April 19, 2024, Moving Parties replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Anti-SLAPP 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 permits the court to strike causes of action arising from an act in furtherance of the defendant's right of free speech or petition, unless the plaintiff establishes that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16.) 

“The anti-SLAPP procedures are designed to shield a defendant’s constitutionally protected conduct from the undue burden of frivolous litigation.” (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 393 (Baral), italics in original.) “The anti-SLAPP statute does not insulate defendants from any liability for claims arising from the protected rights of petition or speech.  It only provides a procedure for weeding out, at an early stage, meritless claims arising from protected activity.” (Id., at p. 384, italics in original.) 

“Resolution of an anti-SLAPP motion involves two steps. First, the defendant must establish that the challenged claim arises from activity protected by section 425.16. If the defendant makes the required showing, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate the merit of the claim by establishing a probability of success.” (Baral, supra, 1 Cal.5th at p. 384, citation omitted.) The California Supreme Court has “described this second step as a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure.’ The court does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims. Its inquiry is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment. It accepts the plaintiff’s evidence as true, and evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law. ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’” (Id., at pp. 384-385, internal citations omitted.) 

            Where a cause of action is predicated on both protected and unprotected activity, the court may strike those parts predicated on protected activity, even if the moving party sought to strike the entire cause of action. (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1011 (Bonni).) Allegations that are merely incidental and provide context without a right to recovery are not subject to the anti-SLAPP statute. (Id., at p. 1012.) 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS – Anti-SLAPP 

            The Court SUSTAINS MEG’s evidentiary objection numbers 4 and 30 as to the language, “the breach was not cured, and there was no notice that the breach would be cured,” on the grounds that they assert improper legal conclusions. (See Towns v. Davidson (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 461, 472.) The Court OVERRULES all of MEG’s other evidentiary objections. 

DISCUSSION – Anti-SLAPP 

Timeliness 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (f), requires that a special motion to strike be filed within 60 days of service of complaint, or later with court permission. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (f).) Here, Moving Parties filed and served their motion on April 2, 2024, which is less than 60 days from the time MEG served the cross-complaint on them. (Proof of Service (2/20/24) [Xchange USA]; Proof of Service (2/28/24) [Xchange Logistics].) The Court notes that MEG did not file a proof of service of the cross-complaint as to Cross-Defendant Mia Zhang, who does not otherwise seem to have appeared in this action. Accordingly, the motion is timely. 

            First Prong – Protected Activity 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (e), defines protected activity as, “(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e).) While filing a lawsuit is an exercise of the constitutional right of petition, an anti-SLAPP motion against a cross-complaint must show that it alleges acts in furtherance of the right of free speech or petition in connection with a public issue. (Kajima Engineering and Const., Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 921, 929 (Kajima).) 

            Summary of Arguments – Protected Activity 

Moving Parties contend that the harm complained of in the cross-complaint arises out of protected activity. Moving Parties contend Xchange[2] and MEG entered into a partial settlement agreement (PSA) related to the original complaint, which gave Xchange the right to auction off MEG’s cargo if MEG breached the PSA. Moving Parties contend MEG breached the PSA, Xchange gave proper notice to cure, and MEG failed to cure. Moving Parties contend Xchange properly provided notice to all relevant parties and scheduled the action for February 22, 2024. Moving Parties then contend MEG filed the cross-complaint on February 15, 2024, alleging fraud, breach of contract, and conversion, and alleging that the harm for each of those causes of action involved MEG defending itself against the original complaint, and taking legal action to stop Xchange Logistics from selling MEG’s goods at auction. Moving Parties contend the auction was a remedy provided under the PSA. 

In opposition, MEG contends most of the evidence is inadmissible, and that the motion fails to show that MEG entering into a contract with Xchange Logistics arises from Xchange USA’s petitioning activity. MEG contends there is no evidence that shows the allegations of entering into the PSA six months before litigation as having any connection to a judicial proceeding. MEG then contends the motion fails to show that activities relating to auctioning MEG’s goods arises from Xchange USA’s petitioning activity. MEG contends Xchange USA’s name is missing from the notice of auction and that there is no evidence Xchange USA actually auctioned off any goods. MEG contends Xchange Logistics was never a party to this action until MEG filed the cross-complaint. 

MEG further contends that the auctioning activities are not covered under the terms of the PSA and that the claims still do not arise from Xchange USA’s petitioning activities. MEG contends there is no evidence of any breach of the PSA. MEG then contends the PSA expired at the time of the auction, and that it terminates 75 days from the “Delivery Date” (as defined in the PSA), and that the Delivery Date was changed to October 16, 2023, so the auction of the goods on February 22, 2024, was past the deadline. MEG contends that the claims at issue do not arise from suing in court, which MEG admits is petitioning activity. MEG contends that it only requests attorney fees as damages for defending against Xchange USA’s lawsuit against Xchange Logistics, and that therefore Xchange USA cannot ask these claims to be stricken since they are not brought against Xchange USA. MEG also contends Xchange Logistics cannot ask the allegations to be stricken because the purported petitioning activity is of Xchange USA. 

            Analysis – Protected Activity 

The Court finds that Moving Parties’ actions in connection with the PSA are not constitutionally protected activities under Civil Code section 425.16. “A breach of contract claim can arise from protected activity if the action allegedly breaching the contract is itself protected. [Citations.]” (Bonni, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 1025, italics added.) None of the legal authorities Moving Parties cited in their moving papers directly support their contention that performing under a contract is a constitutionally protected activity for purposes of an anti-SLAPP motion. (See 19:3-21:3.) In fact, the legal authority that the Court found tends to show that performing or failing to perform under a contract by itself is not a protected activity. “The simple breach of contract alleged in the instant case does not arise from such constitutionally protected activity.” (Applied Business Software, Inc. v. Pacific Mortgage Exchange, Inc. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1118 (Applied Business).) 

Moreover, even if a settlement agreement is entered into in connection with a judicial proceeding, its breach does not necessarily constitute a protected activity. (Applied Business Software, Inc. v. Pacific Mortgage Exchange, Inc. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1117-1118 (Applied Business).) More specifically, the Second District of the Court of Appeal stated that: 

[T]he gist of plaintiff's complaint is not that defendant did something wrong by acts committed during the course of the underlying federal action, but rather that defendant did something wrong by breaching the settlement agreement after the underlying action had been concluded. Under the explanatory provisions in subdivision (e) of section 425.16, defendant's entering into the settlement agreement during the pendency of the federal case was indeed a protected activity, but defendant's subsequent alleged breach of the settlement agreement after the federal case was concluded is not protected activity because it cannot be said that the alleged breaching activity was undertaken by defendant in furtherance of defendant's right of petition or free speech, as those rights are defined in section 425.16. Thus, the instant suit is based on alleged conduct of the defendant that is not protected activity. 

(Applied Business, supra, 164 Cal.App.4th at p. 1118, italics added.) 

            The fact that the parties entered into the PSA in connection with the original complaint during the pendency of this action does not automatically bring it within the scope of a constitutionally protected right under Civil Code section 425.16. (See Id.) 

Furthermore, the Court also does not find MEG’s allegations regarding the auctioning of its goods to be directed at protected activity. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 17, 22, 30.) This is merely performance under an agreement and, as noted above, the parties’ respective performances in connection with the PSA by themselves are not constitutionally protected activity, even if the PSA was entered into during pending litigation. (See Applied Business, supra, 164 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1117-1118.) Additionally, MEG’s claims are not based on the PSA, but rather are based on the service contract from December 2022 between Xchange Logistics and MEG. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 17, 20, Ex. B.) 

Finally, the Court does not find that MEG’s damage claims regarding defending itself against Moving Parties’ litigation brings it within the scope of a protected activity. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶ 17.) “[T]o determine the applicability of the anti-SLAPP statute, we look to the allegedly wrongful and injurious conduct of the defendant, rather than the damage which flows from said conduct.” (Renewable Res. Coal., Inc. v. Pebble Mines Corp. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 384, 396-397, italics in original.) Here, the allegedly wrongful and injury producing conduct is MEG being deceived into entering into the service contract with Xchange Logistics, (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 16), Xchange Logistics’ failure to perform under the service contract, (Id., ¶ 22), and Xchange Logistics’ wrongfully withholding MEG’s goods and planning to auction off those goods, (Id., ¶¶ 29-30). 

The Court also notes the following language from the Court of Appeal in Kajima: 

“A compulsory cross-complaint on a ‘related cause of action’ against the plaintiff (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.30, subd. (a)) would rarely, if ever, qualify as a SLAPP suit arising from petition activity. By definition, a ‘related cause of action’ is ‘a cause of action which arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause of action which the plaintiff alleges in his complaint.’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.10, subd. (c), italics added.) The SLAPP suit is not ‘related’ to the transaction or occurrence which is the subject of the plaintiff's complaint, but arises out of the litigation process itself.”

(Kajima, supra, 95 Cal.App.4th at p. 934.) 

The cross-complaint is based on the same set of facts as Moving Parties’ original complaint and the FAC. In fact, they involve the same parties and have claims based on the same service contract between MEG and Xchange Logistics. (FAC, Ex. 2; Cross-Complaint, Ex. B.) Thus, at most, the cross-complaint is a compulsory cross-complaint and not a SLAPP suit. (See Kajima, supra, 95 Cal.App.4th at p. 934.) 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court finds Moving Parties have not met their moving burden in showing that the cross-complaint is directed toward protected activity. The Court therefore need not consider the second prong regarding whether Plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the merits. (See Applied Business, supra, 164 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1118-1119.) 

Therefore, the Court DENIES Moving Parties’ anti-SLAPP motion. The Court further DENIES Moving Parties’ request for attorney’s fees and costs, given its denial of the anti-SLAPP motion. 

CASE NAME:  Xchange USA Inc. v. MEG Home Plus, LLC, et al. 

2 – Defendants’ Demurrer to Xchange USA, Inc. and Xchange Logistics Corporation’s First Amended Complaint; and

3 – Defendants’ Motion to Strike Xchange USA, Inc. and Xchange Logistics Corporation’s First Amended Complaint 

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND – Demurrer 

            On April 16, 2024, Defendants MEG, Jie Li, and Jing Li aka Cynthia Li (collectively, Moving Defendants) demurred to the First Amended Complaint. On May 16, 2024, Plaintiffs opposed the demurrer. On May 23, 2024, Moving Defendants replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Demurrer 

            A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (Code Civ. Proc., § 422.10; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Id.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862, disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287 [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery. (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-972.) The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint, or in a superseded complaint in the same action. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”]) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.)  

DISCUSSION – Demurrer 

Meet and Confer 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), Moving Defendants were required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference before bringing this demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) Counsel for Moving Defendants indicates having met and conferred by telephone on April 15, 2024, which the Court finds sufficient. (Wenyao Decl., ¶ 2.) 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract 

“A cause of action for breach of contract requires pleading of a contract, plaintiff's performance or excuse for failure to perform, defendant's breach and damage to plaintiff resulting therefrom. [Citation.] A written contract may be pleaded either by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. [Citation.]” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) 

Moving Defendants demur to the First Cause of Action for breach of contract on the grounds that it is uncertain. Moving Defendants contend the First Cause of Action is uncertain because it cannot be ascertained what the theory of liability is. More specifically, Moving Defendants contend each party is alleged to be acting as an agent or alter ego for any other party, which leads to virtually limitless combinations by which any particular defendant could be liable. Moving Defendants contend an additional issue of uncertainty is that the FAC alleges all of the Defendants are all the other Defendants’ alter ego, but the individual Defendants cannot be the alter ego of other individuals. Moving Defendants also contend it cannot be ascertained how Moving Party is liable. Moving Defendants further contend the First Cause of Action is also uncertain because it cannot be ascertained which contract is at issue, as there are two different contracts attached to the FAC. 

In opposition, Plaintiffs contend the First Cause of Action is not uncertain because a plain reading makes it clear that the breach of contract claim is directed at all individuals as alter egos of the corporate entity, and that nothing in the FAC suggests the individual Defendants are alter egos of each other. Plaintiffs contend the FAC specifically identifies Exhibit 2 as the contract that was breached. 

The Court finds the First Cause of Action is subject to demurrer for uncertainty. While demurrers for uncertainty are generally disfavored and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond, (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Ins. Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848), the Court finds the First Cause of Action is sufficiently ambiguous here. The FAC attaches two written contracts and numerous invoices from before January 1, 2023, i.e., before the allegedly effective date of the second contract. (See FAC, ¶¶ 17-18, 26, 28, Exs. 2-3, pp. 37-46.) The FAC does not allege that the second contract superseded the first, nor does there appear to be any terms in the second contract indicating that it superseded the first contract. (See Id., ¶¶ 17-30, Exs. 1-2.) 

Furthermore, Plaintiffs are not parties to both contracts. (See FAC, Exs. 2-3.) If Plaintiffs are only suing on the second contract as they contend in their opposition, then Xchange USA arguably should not be a party to this action since Xchange USA is not a party to the second contract. (See Id., Ex. 2.) Therefore, given that the FAC is based on two different contracts, the First Cause of Action is subject to demurrer for uncertainty. (See Zumbrun v. University of Southern California (1972) 25 Cal.App.3d 1, 9 [trial court properly sustained demurrers for uncertainty based on two contracted related causes of action having been improperly combined into one].) Also, given the combined causes of action into one, this cause of action should be split into two. (See Id.) 

Finally, the FAC is uncertain as to the basis for liability as to each defendant. The FAC alleges “Plaintiffs and all Defendant (as alter egos of one another) entered into a written agreement.” (See FAC, ¶ 26.) The agreements attached as exhibits 1 and 2 to the FAC state that such agreements are between MEG Home Plus. None of the other defendants are named as parties to the agreement. "Alter ego" is not a cause of action, and a plaintiff need not plead the words "alter ego" in a complaint to recover from an individual as the alter ego of a corporate entity. (Favila v. Pasquarella (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 934, 946. "A claim against a defendant, based on the alter ego theory, is not itself a claim for substantive relief, e.g., breach of contract or to set aside a fraudulent conveyance, but rather, procedural, i.e., to disregard the corporate entity as a distinct defendant and to hold the alter ego individuals liable on the obligations of the corporation where the corporate form is being used by the individuals to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, or promote injustice." (Hennessey's Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1359; see also Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.) A plaintiff "must allege sufficient facts to show a unity of interest and ownership, and an unjust result if the corporation is treated as the sole actor." (Leek, supra, 194 Cal.App.4th at p. 415.) To allege alter ego, a party must allege that the alleged alter ego dominated and controlled the other party; that a unity of interest and ownership existed between the parties; that the entity was a mere shell and conduit for Defendant’s affairs; that entity was inadequately capitalized; that entity failed to abide by the formalities of corporate existence; that the alleged alter ego used the entity’s assets as her own; and that recognizing the separate existence of the entity would promote injustice. (Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 235.) The allegation of ultimate facts is sufficient to allege alter ego. (Rutherford Holdings, LLC, 223 Cal.App.4th at 236 [citing Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550].) 

Here, the FAC fails to specifically identify the parties to the agreement and the specific parties alleged to be liable on an alter ego theory. Plaintiffs must allege sufficient facts to establish an alter ego theory as to each defendant alleged to be an alter ego. Here, in paragraphs 13 through 16 of the FAC, Plaintiffs make alter ego allegations as to specific factors that could aid a court in finding alter ego liability, but it is unclear in the First and Second causes of action which party Plaintiffs is alleging is the alter ego of which other party. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the First Cause of Action with leave to amend. 

Second Cause of Action – Common Count: Open Book Account 

“[A] common count, to be sufficiently pleaded, must state not only the indebtedness of the defendant but also directly or impliedly the relationship or the express or implied legal principle upon which a promise to plaintiff is predicated.” (Vaughn v. Certified Life Ins. Co. of Cal. (1965) 238 Cal.App.2d 177, 181.) While common count claims are generally not subject to demurrer, they are subject to demurrer when they are based on the same facts as the specifically pleaded claims. (Orloff v. Metropolitan Trust Co. (1941) 17 Cal.2d 484, 489.) 

Moving Defendants demur to the Second Cause of Action for common count: open book account on the grounds that it is uncertain. Moving Defendants contend this cause of action is the same as the First Cause of Action and is defective for the same reasons. Plaintiffs’ opposition does not respond to this argument. 

The Court finds this cause of action subject to demurrer for uncertainty, as it is based on the same facts as the First Cause of Action, including the same two contracts. (FAC, ¶¶ 31-34, Exs. 1-2.) The Court further construes Plaintiffs’ lack of argument on this cause of action as a tacit admission that Moving Defendants’ arguments here are meritorious. (See Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410; C. Opposing the Motion—and Rebutting the Opposition, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 9(I)-C, ¶¿9:105.10 .) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Second Cause of Action with leave to amend. 

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND – Motion to Strike 

            On April 16, 2024, Moving Defendants moved to strike the First Amended Complaint. On May 16, 2024, Plaintiffs opposed the motion to strike. On May 23, 2024, Moving Defendants replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Motion to Strike 

            “Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(2).) “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id., § 436.) 

DISCUSSION 

Meet and Confer 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a), Moving Defendants were required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference before bringing this demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) Counsel for Moving Defendants indicates having met and conferred by telephone on April 15, 2024, which the Court finds sufficient. (Wenyao Decl., ¶ 2.) 

Improperly Added Plaintiff 

“A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 472, subd. (a).) “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding… name of any party…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); Pasadena Hosp. Assn., Ltd. v. Superior Ct. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1031, 1035.) 

Moving Defendants contend the FAC improperly added Xchange Logistics as a plaintiff to this action, and that Plaintiffs should have first obtained leave of court to amend the complaint and add Xchange Logistics as a plaintiff. In opposition, Plaintiffs contend that Plaintiff[3] exercised its right under Code of Civil Procedure section 472 to amend the original complaint, and that an amendment to a complaint may encompass adding or deleting parties to the complaint under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1). 

The Court finds the addition of Xchange Logistics as a plaintiff to the FAC was permitted under Code of Civil Procedure sections 472, subdivision (a). (See Code Civ. Proc., § 472, subd. (a).) Section 472 provides in pertinent part that "[any] pleading may be amended once by the party of course, and without costs, at any time before the answer or demurrer is filed, or after demurrer and before the trial of the issue of law thereon . . . ." (Code Civ. Proc. § 472(a).) "This section states the right to amend at the outset, for a limited period, without leave of court." (5 Witkin, Cal. Procedure (3d ed. 1985) Pleading, § 1119, p. 536.) In general, an amendment may correct any part of the pleading. (Id., § 1108, p. 526.) "Thus[,] . . . [the] parties may be changed or new parties brought in." (Ibid.)” (Gross v. Dep't of Transp. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 1102, 1105.) 

“Section 472 does not limit what types of amendments may be made of course and without leave of court. The only limitations expressed in section 472 are that the amendment be made before the answer or demurrer is filed, or after the demurrer but before it is heard. …[It] therefore follows that under § 472 any part of the pleading may be changed, without leave, including the addition of a new party." (Id. [internal quotations omitted].) 

Xchange USA had the right to amend its original complaint once as a matter of course per section 472, subdivision (a), which it did when it filed the FAC on March 26, 2024. Such amendment could include the addition of a new plaintiff.

Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as to the allegations in the FAC pertaining to the addition of Xchange Logistics as a plaintiff. 

Prayer for Special Damages 

Moving Defendants contend the prayer for special damages in the FAC should be stricken because the FAC does not support any request for special damages. In opposition, Plaintiffs contend the request for special damages should not be stricken because Plaintiff[4] is seeking recovery of its contract damages, specifically transportation and logistics costs, plus storage costs. Plaintiffs contend it should be foreseeable that if MEG breached, Xchange[5] would have a possessory lien per Commercial Code sections 7209 and 7307, which would in turn lead to further storage charges. 

A plaintiff is entitled to recover all reasonable and necessary expenses to which he may have been put by reason of a breach of contract. If special damages are the natural consequences of the breach, they may be recovered. (Walpole v. Prefab Mfg. Co. (1951) 103 Cal.App.2d 472, 489-490.)  [230 P.2d 36].) The FAC alleges only that “Plaintiffs were harmed in an amount not less than $1.3 million dollars….” (FAC, ¶¶ 30, 34.) Plaintiffs then pray for special damages in an amount not less that $1.3 million dollars. (FAC, Prayer for Relief ¶ A.) Plaintiffs fail to allege with any particularity, facts showing that these damages "directly and naturally" resulted "in the ordinary course of events" from the breach of the contract. ( Civ. Code, § 1787.) Plaintiffs’ arguments in the Opposition are not considered facts pled in the FAC. 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS the motion to strike the prayer for special damages, with leave to amend. 

Immaterial Allegations 

Moving Defendants contend the other allegations should be stricken as immaterial. In opposition, Plaintiffs contend they are unable to reasonably respond to the argument that portions of the FAC are not drawn in conformity with the laws, rules of court, or court order, as Moving Defendants do not explain which of the laws, rules, or orders were violated. Plaintiffs also contend they are unable to reasonably respond to the argument that portions of the FAC are immaterial, since they do not explain why they believe each portion is immaterial, and some of the challenged portions of the FAC contain the actual contracts executed between the parties and the amount of damages sought. Plaintiffs contend the contracts provide background information as well, which contract was breached. Plaintiffs contend they present the state of the dispute after the breach and attempts at resolution. 

The Court agrees with Plaintiffs. Moving Defendants fail to provide the Court with adequate grounds to consider the motion to strike allegations as immaterial. 

Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion to strike the allegations as immaterial. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES Xchange USA, Inc.’s and Xchange Logistics Corporation’s special motion to strike (anti-SLAPP) and DENIES the request for attorney’s fees and costs. 

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the First Amended Complaint with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

The Court DENIES the motion to strike as to the allegations in the FAC pertaining to the addition of Xchange Logistics as a plaintiff and the purportedly immaterial allegations, and GRANTS the motion with respect to the prayer for special damages. Accordingly, the Prayer for Relief, ¶ A, p. 7:3-4, is stricken with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.
