Case Number: 22STLC02264    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 25

 

Hearing Date:                         Monday, July 8, 2024

Case Name:                             STATE FARM MUTUAL AUTOMOBILE INSURANCE COMPANY v. MARVIN SOSA-REYES; and DOES 1 to 10

Case No.:                                22STLC02264

Motion:                                   Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings  

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company  

Responding Party:                   Unopposed

Notice:                                    IMPROPER. The notice of motion does not provide the grounds for the motion.

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.     

                                               

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 5, 2022, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Marvin Sosa-Reyes (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10 alleging a cause of action for Subrogation.

 

The Complaint alleges the following: Robin Godfrey (the “Insured”) had a policy of insurance with Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) On or about May 2, 2021, Defendant caused the Insured to incur a loss that was covered under the policy. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) Defendant negligently operated a motor vehicle and caused an accident. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) The Insured suffered damages in the amount of $7,648.33. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) As required by both the insurance policy and Plaintiff’s duty of good faith and fair dealing with its insured, Plaintiff investigated the loss, determined it was covered, and paid the claim. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) The total loss was $7,648.33. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Plaintiff seeks subrogation of its payment from all Defendants. (Complaint, ¶ 8.)

 

On October 24, 2022, Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint setting forth a general denial and five affirmative defenses.

 

On November 1, 2022, Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint against Rosemary Armenta and Roes 1 to 10, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Indemnity; (2) Contribution; and (3) Declaratory Relief.

 

On May 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to deem the Requests for Admission, Set One, it propounded on Defendant as admitted.

 

On June 26, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to deem its Requests for Admission, Set One as admitted. (06/26/23 Minute Order.)

 

Pursuant to its June 26, 2023 order, the following matters in Plaintiff’s RFAs were admitted:

 

1.      Admit that at the time of the INCIDENT, you were driving a motor vehicle. (For purposes of these requests, the term INCIDENT refers to the accident upon which this action was based occurring on or about May 2, 2021 at or near STRANWOOD AVE NEAR RINALDI ST MISSION HILLS, CA 91345).

2.      Admit that at the time of the INCIDENT, you failed to drive with reasonable care.

3.      Admit that your driving was the sole cause of the COLLISION with plaintiff’s INSURED VEHICLE. ANALYSIS

 

I.          Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

A.                Meet and Confer Requirement

            Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, a moving party's counsel must meet and confer, in person, by telephone, or by videoconference with counsel for the party who filed the pleading “that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (CCP § 439(a).) “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to grant or deny the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (CCP § 439(a)(4).)

            Plaintiff’s counsel, Tristan P. Espinosa (“Espinosa”), declares that on March 26, 2024, his office served a meet and confer letter on Defendant. (Espinosa Decl., ¶ 1; Ex. 1.) To date, Mr. Espinosa has received no contact from Defendant. (Espinosa Decl., ¶ 2.)    

            The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has not been met as counsel did not meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by videoconference. The Court, however, will assess the merits of the motion for judgment on the pleadings.

The Court reminds the parties of the need to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure.

B.        Judicial Notice 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice pursuant to Evid. Code §§ 452 and 453. The Court therefore takes judicial notice of Defendant’s Answer, Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Admissions, and the Court’s June 26, 2023 order granting Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Admissions.

C.         Legal Standard

“A party may move for judgment on the pleadings.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (b)(1).) “If the moving party is a plaintiff, [the motion may only be made on the grounds] that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(A).) In assessing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, a court “may consider matters that may be judicially noticed.” Pang v. Beverly Hosp., Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 986, 990, citation omitted. A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be filed by a plaintiff if “the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the plaintiff to demur to the answer has expired.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f)(1).) A motion pursuant to CCP § 438 “may be made . . . if a pretrial conference order has been entered pursuant to Section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (e).)

 “A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999. “A motion for judgment on the pleadings is akin to a general demurrer; it tests the sufficiency of the complaint to state a cause of action.” Wise v. Pacific Gas and Elec. Co. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 725, 738.  

“Judicial admissions may be made in a pleading, by stipulation during trial, or by response to request for admission.” Barsegian v. Kessler & Kessler (2013) 215 Cal.Ap.4th 446, 451, citations omitted. “[I]n discovery when a party propounds requests for admission, any facts admitted by the responding party constitute judicial admissions.” Id. at p. 452. “A judicial admission is therefore conclusive both as to the admitting party and as to that party’s opponent.” Ibid., emphasis in original. “[I]f a factual allegation is treated as a judicial admission, then neither party may attempt to contradict it—the admitted fact is effectively conceded by both sides.” Ibid., emphasis in original.

1.         Appropriateness of Granting the Instant Motion   

 

            Plaintiff argues that the Court’s order granting its motion to deem admissions which, when read with the facts that have been deemed admitted, remove any issue raised by Defendant under a general denial or any affirmative defenses raised in his Answer. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at p. 3:23-26.)   

 

            The Court finds that the Notice of Motion is defective as it merely states that Plaintiff “will and does hereby move for an order granting Plaintiff’s Judgment on the Pleadings against Defendant MARVIN SOSA-REYES. Said motion will be based on this Notice, the Memorandum of Points and Authorities, Declaration of Tristan P. Espinosa, Esq., and the Request for Judicial Notice which establish that Plaintiff is entitled to such relief.” (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:21-25.)

 

            However, “[a] notice of motion must state in the opening paragraph the nature of the order being sought and the grounds for the issuance of the order.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1110(a).) Here, the Notice of Motion is void of any indication of the basis for Plaintiff’s request for judgment on the pleadings. Given that Plaintiff is the moving party, the motion can only be made on the grounds “that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.” (CCP § 438(c)(1)(A).) Here, Plaintiff does not even argue that the Complaint states facts to constitute a cause of action against Defendant. Plaintiff also does not explicitly argue that the Answer fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the Complaint. To the contrary, Plaintiff argues that, due to the Court’s order granting its motion as to deem the RFAs as admitted, its motion for judgment on the pleadings is proper.

 

            The Court is unsure whether Plaintiff is asking the Court to determine that the Complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action along with a determination that—based on the Court’s June 26, 2023 Minute Order—Defendant’s Answer fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a defense. “[D]ue process requires a party to be fully advised of the issues to be addressed and to be given adequate notice of what facts it must rebut in order to prevail.” Fenn v. Sherriff (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1466, 1481.

 

            Additionally, the Court finds that Plaintiff is improperly relying on the truth of deemed requests for admissions to seek judgment on the pleadings against Defendant. The Court notes that on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the Court analyzes the pleadings and does not rely on deemed admissions granted through discovery motions. Barker v. Hull (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 221, 224. Plaintiff’s cited authority also does not support the proposition which it advances. The case of Barsegian, supra, 215 Cal.App.4th 446 is inapposite as such case addressed judicial admissions in the context of a motion to compel arbitration and not a motion for judgment on the pleadings. Plaintiff’s citation to Addy v. Bliss (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 205 is also inapposite as such case involved judicial admissions concerning a motion for summary judgment.

 

It appears that Plaintiff is arguing that there is no factual dispute concerning the Complaint or Defendant’s Answer based on the Court’s June 26, 2023 order. The appropriate motion for Plaintiff to file is not a motion for judgment on the pleadings, but rather a motion for summary judgment. If Plaintiff contends that there are no triable issues of fact, then Plaintiff should file a motion for summary judgment since a motion for summary judgment allows the Court to determine whether there is any triable issue of material fact. Saldana v. Globe-Weis Systems Co. (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 1505, 1511. Only the legal sufficiency of allegations in a pleading are tested pursuant to a motion for judgment on the pleadings. Lee v. Kotyluk (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 719, 728. Here, pursuant to the instant motion, Plaintiff improperly requests that the Court make a factual determination that certain issues of fact have been determined in Plaintiff’s favor pursuant to the Court’s ruling on a discovery motion, and therefore judgment should enter in Plaintiff’s favor and against Defendant. Thus, the basis for the instant motion is improper.  

 

II.        Conclusion

           

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.

 

            The Moving party is ordered to give notice of this Court’s Ruling and to attach a copy of the Court's Tentative Ruling, as exhibit “A” to said notice.

BACKGROUND

 

On February 1, 2023, Plaintiff State Farm General Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Jesus Lucas (“Defendant”) and Elizabeth Torres (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10 alleging one cause of action for Subrogation.

 

On November 3, 2023, Defendant Elizabeth Torres filed an Answer to the Complaint setting forth a general denial and four affirmative defenses.

ANALYSIS

 

A.         Legal Standard

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250(a).)

 

When a party fails to serve a timely response to requests for admission “[t]he party to whom the request was directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc § 2033.280(a).) “The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).) “It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction . . . on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated [the] motion.” Id.

 

Any party may obtain discovery . . . by inspecting, copying, testing, or sampling documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically stored information in the possession, custody, or control of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010, subd. (a).) Where a party fails to timely respond to demand for inspection, copying, testing or sampling “[t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b) provides that “[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”

In opposition, defense counsel states that Defendant moved to Brazil and has not been able to respond and they do not have her contact information.

 

Because Plaintiff properly served the request for discovery, and Defendant has failed to provide responses, the Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to an order establishing the truth of the matters in the request for admission served on Defendant. Therefore, the motion is granted.

 

Plaintiff also requests sanctions. Sanctions are mandatory in connection with a motion to deem the truth of the matter in the requests for admission admitted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).) Thus, the request for sanctions in connection with this motion is granted. As such, the Court imposes monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $460 ($200 per hour, for two hours, plus $60 filing fee) to be paid within 20 days of this order.

Case Number: 23STLC07120    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Monday, July 8, 2024

Case Name:                             LAURA ELENA VALENCIA v. HUGO VALENCIA, JR.

Case No.:                                23STLC07120

Motion:                                   Demurrer

Moving Party:                         Defendant Hugo Valencia, Jr.

Responding Party:                   None 

Notice:                                    OK 

 

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Defendant Hugo Valencia, Jr.’s demurrer is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED without leave to amend in part.

 

The Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the cause of action for negligence per se and SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the cause of action for negligent entrustment.

 

Defendant’s Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint to be filed within 20-days.

 

BACKGROUND 

 

On November 7, 2023, Plaintiff Laura Elena Valencia (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Hugo Valencia, Jr. (“Defendant”) for (1) negligence, (2) strict liability (Civil Code section 3342), (3) negligence per se, and (4) negligent entrustment.

 

The complaint alleges that on November 7, 2021, Plaintiff was attacked and scratched by an unsupervised, off-leash dog owned by Defendant when she was standing in her front yard. Plaintiff alleges that the dog left Defendant’s property through an open gate.

 

On June 7, 2024, Defendant filed this demurrer. No opposition has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.    Legal Standard 

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law ….” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525).) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters; therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.) 

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Defendant demurs to the causes of action for negligence per se and negligent entrustment in the complaint, arguing they fail to state sufficient facts and are uncertain.

 

The Plaintiff’s Complaint provides:

 

“On November 7, 2021, the Plaintiff, Laura Elena Valencia (Plaintiff) while standing in her front yard located at 10709 Amery, South Gate, California, was attached and scratched by an unsupervised, off-leash dog that is owned, kept, harbored, controlled and/or maintained by Defendant, at 10715 Amery, south Gate, California.

 

The dog, unsupervised and off-leash left the Defendant’s property through an open gate. The attack on the Plaintiff was unprovoked and without warning.  The sudden and vicious attack by Defendant’s dog, thereby, caused the Plaintiff to suffer severe lacerations and bodily injuries to her back, shoulders and legs which has left Plaintiff scared and emotionally frightened. Defendant breached his duty as a dog owner and was negligent as follows: Failure to maintain his animal under proper restraint and control; failure to give full time and attention; failure to obey leash laws and statutes; reckless endangerment; failure to pay proper attention to protect the public from his dog; failure to take preventative measures such as by use of muzzle; failure to warn; failure to entrust the dog to a person competent to maintain control and restraint; maintain adequate fencing to keep the dog from leaving the property and running loose and viciously attacking Plaintiff in her own yard.”

 

I.                    Third Cause of Action for Negligence Per Se

 

Defendant argues this cause of action fails because negligence per se is a rebuttable evidentiary presumption and not an independent cause of action. 

 

            Plaintiff alleges in her Complaint for Negligence Per Se that Defendant violated municipal authority including but not limited to Los Angeles Municipal Code Section 53.06 and 53.34.1 and Los Angeles County Code Section 10.32.010. 

 

“’Negligence per se’ is an evidentiary doctrine codified at Evidence Code section 669. Under subdivision (a) of this section, the doctrine creates a presumption of negligence if four elements are established: (1) the defendant violated a statute, ordinance, or regulation of a public entity; (2) the violation proximately caused death or injury to person or property; (3) the death or injury resulted from an occurrence the nature of which the statute, ordinance, or regulation was designed to prevent; and (4) the person suffering the death or the injury to his person or property was one of the class of persons for whose protection the statute, ordinance, or regulation was adopted. These latter two elements are determined by the court as a matter of law.” (Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1285, internal citations omitted.)  “Thus, the doctrine of negligence per se does not establish tort liability.  Rather, it merely codifies the rule that a presumption of negligence arises from the violation of a statute which was enacted to protect a class of persons of which the plaintiff is a member against the type of harm that the plaintiff suffered as a result of the violation.  Even if the four requirements of Evidence Code section 669, subdivision (a), are satisfied, this alone does not entitle a plaintiff to a presumption of negligence in the absence of an underlying negligence action.”  (Ibid., internal citations omitted, emphasis in original.)  “Accordingly, to apply negligence per se is not to state an independent cause of action. The doctrine does not provide a private right of action for violation of a statute.  Instead, it operates to establish a presumption of negligence for which the statute serves the subsidiary function of providing evidence of an element of a preexisting common law cause of action.”  (Id. at 1285-86, internal citations omitted.) 

 

“ ‘The presumption of negligence created by Evidence Code section 669 concerns the standard of care, rather than the duty of care.’  In order for the presumption to be available, ‘either the courts or the Legislature must have created a duty of care.’  ‘[A]n underlying claim of ordinary negligence must be viable before the presumption of negligence of Evidence Code section 669 can be employed.... “[I]t is the tort of negligence, and not the violation of the statute itself, which entitles a plaintiff to recover civil damages.” ’ ” (Millard v. Biosources, Inc. (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1353, citations omitted, original italics.)   

 

Notwithstanding, a party can still allege a negligence per se cause of action.  In Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, the California Supreme Court noted that, while the plaintiff's allegations of violations of statutes did not state a separate cause of action in the sense that they did not allege a different violation of a primary right, i.e. the right to be free from bodily harm, they could still be alleged either together in a single count, or as separate counts.  (Id. at 413.)  Likewise, in Millard, the Court of Appeal found that, although negligence per se was not a true independent cause of action, plaintiff nevertheless needed to plead allegations that supported the negligence per se theory in order to rely on that theory in opposition to summary judgment. Since plaintiff had failed to make any such allegations in the complaint, he could not rely on a negligence per se theory to defeat summary judgment. (Millard, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at 1353.)

 

Although Defendant is correct that negligence per se is not a separate “cause of action” in the sense of alleging a different violation of a primary right, Plaintiff nevertheless can allege negligence per se as a separate count of negligence.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the cause of action for negligence per se is OVERRULED.

 

II.                 Fourth Cause of Action for Negligent Entrustment

Defendant argues the facts in Plaintiff’s Complaint arise out of an alleged dog attack, which is inapplicable to any claim for negligent entrustment. Defendant also argues the cause of action for negligent entrustment is duplicative of the cause of action for negligence because it is based on the same facts.

“Every owner of a motor vehicle is liable and responsible for the death or injury to person or property resulting from a negligent or wrongful act or omission in the operation of the motor vehicle, in the business of the owner or otherwise, by any person using or operating the same with the permission, express or implied, of the owner.”  (Vehicle Code § 17150.)  “Liability arises solely from the owner’s status as owner of the vehicle, not from any independent fault or wrongdoing on the owner’s part.”  (Galvis v. Petito (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 551, 555 (emphasis in original).) 

 

To hold a defendant liable for negligent entrustment, the plaintiff must prove that (1) the driver was negligent in operating the vehicle; (2) that the defendant was an owner of the vehicle operated by the driver; (3) that the defendant knew, or should have known, that the driver was incompetent or unfit to drive the vehicle; (4) that the defendant permitted the driver to use the vehicle; and (5) that the driver’s incompetence or unfitness to drive was a substantial factor in causing harm to the plaintiff.  (Jeld-Wen, Inc. v. Superior Court (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 853, 863-64.) 

 

Under the theory of negligent entrustment, liability is imposed on¿the vehicle¿owner or permitter because of the owner’s independent¿negligence¿and not the¿negligence¿of the driver. (Safeco Ins. Co. v. Gilstrap¿(1983) 141 Cal.App.3d 524, 530.) A person who places or entrusts his motor vehicle in the hands of another “whom he knows, or from circumstances is charged with knowing, is incompetent or unfit to drive, may be held liable for an injury inflicted by the use made thereof by that driver, provided the plaintiff can establish that the injury complained of was proximately caused by the driver's disqualification, incompetency, inexperience or recklessness.” (Flores v. Enterprise Rent-A-Car Co.¿(2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 1055, 1063.)

 

There are no allegations that would show anyone entrusted a vehicle to another. Further, there are no allegations of entrusting the dog to another person either. It is unclear how this cause of action applies in this case.

 

In addition, Plaintiff has already alleged a cause of action for negligence based on the same facts as Plaintiff’s claim for negligent entrustment.  Thus, the negligence and negligent entrustment causes of action are duplicative.  (Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501 (stating that a demurrer may be sustained when a cause of action is duplicative of another cause of action and “thus adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery”; see Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.) 

 

As such, the demurrer to the cause of action for negligent entrustment is SUSTAINED on these grounds without leave to amend. 

 

 

 Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is OVERRULED as to third cause of action for negligence per se and SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the fourth cause of action for negligent entrustment.

 

Defendant’s Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint to be filed within 20-days.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On March 15, 2024, Plaintiff JUNG HONG PARK dba HS EXPRESS AMERICA, INC. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant HANMI BANK and DOES 1 to 10 (“Defendants”), alleging Defendants proceeded with an online transfer despite recognizing the account as suspicious and without informing Plaintiff.

 

On June 10, 2024, Defendant Hanmi Bank filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint. The demurrer is unopposed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

“The primary function of a pleading is to give the other party notice so that it may prepare its case [citation], and a defect in a pleading that otherwise properly notifies a party cannot be said to affect substantial rights.” (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240.)¿

 

“A¿demurrer¿tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Ivanoff v. Bank of America, N.A.¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 719, 725.) The Court looks to whether “the complaint alleges facts sufficient to state a cause of action or discloses a complete defense.” (Id.) The Court does not “read passages from a complaint in isolation; in reviewing a ruling on a demurrer, we read the complaint ‘as a whole and its parts in their context.’ [Citation.]” (West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 804.) The Court “assume[s] the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations, facts that reasonably can be inferred from those expressly pleaded and matters of which judicial notice has been taken.” (Harris, supra, 56 Cal.4th p. 240.) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)¿¿¿

¿          

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).)¿¿¿

¿          

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)¿¿

 

 

III.              Meet and Confer

 

Prior to filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41.)¿¿¿

 

Here, Defendant Hanmi Bank advances the declaration of its counsel of record, Wil J. Rios, attesting to the meet and confer efforts made in regard to the issues raised in the demurrer prior to filing as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. Specifically, Rios states that he sent a meet and confer email to Plaintiff on May 6, 2024 outlining the deficiencies of the Complaint and requesting Plaintiff voluntarily dismiss the Complaint. (Rios Decl., ¶3, Ex. A.) Plaintiff decided not to dismiss the Complaint to address the deficiencies identified by defense counsel. (Id. at ¶4.)

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant Hanmi Bank has fulfilled the meet and confer requirement.

 

IV.              Discussion

 

Defendant Hanmi Bank demurs to the entire Complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to allege enough facts that would be sufficient to constitute any cause of action against it.

 

The Complaint alleges as follows:

 

“If the bank proceeded with an online transfer despite recognizing the account as suspicious, the responsibility may lie with the bank. Banks operate detection system for suspicious transactions or accounts to prevent money laundering, and it is their duty and responsibility to operate such systems to block suspicious transactions and notify the relevant parties.” (Attachment 1, ¶1.)

 

“However, despite being aware of this fact, the representative of Hanmi Bank proceeded with the online transfer without informing the client, and therefore, the bank should bear responsibility for this act.” (Id. at ¶2.)

 

“In the case of California, banks are required to comply with strong regulations and procedures for preventing money laundering and financial crimes. In California, banks are obligated to submit a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR), which mandates reporting of suspicious transactions or activities to the domestic Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN).” (Id. at ¶3.)

 

“Therefore, if the bank proceeded with an online transfer without informing the client despite recognizing the account as suspicious, it appears that the bank did not properly comply with the regulations on its own. Consequently, the bank executed such a request without any doubt. This may be a violation of financial regulations in California, and the bank must take responsibility.” (Id. at ¶4.)

 

“Accordingly, the plaintiff seeks a judgment requiring Hanmi Bank to fully compensate for the amount transferred.” (Id. at ¶5.)

 

On the face of the Complaint, Plaintiff fails to state sufficient facts to support a legally cognizable cause of action against Defendant Hanmi Bank. In fact, the Complaint does not identify any causes of actions for which the allegations are being brought under. The attachment to the Complaint does not list any causes of action. While the Complaint references violations of financial regulations that Defendant Hanmi Bank might have violated, it does not identify which financial regulations have been violated. Likewise, the alleged facts are too vague to determine what financial regulations are being referenced. As such, it is unclear what claims Plaintiff is alleging to adequately put Defendant Hanmi Bank on notice of what defenses it might raise in response. Nonetheless, Plaintiff does allege facts concerning an obligation under FinCEN, which suggests that the Complaint is capable of reasonable amendment.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant Hanmi Bank’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.
