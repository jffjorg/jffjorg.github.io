DEPARTMENT 86 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23LBCP00344    Hearing Date: July 25, 2024    Dept: 86

MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

 

Date:               7/25/24 (1:30 PM)

Case:                           Supporters Alliance for Environmental Responsibility v. Long Beach, et al. (23LBCP00344)

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Petitioner Supporters Alliance for Environmental Responsibility’s Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.

 

Petitioner moves for a protective order that it not be required to respond to a deposition notice propounded by real party in interest Marina Shores Owner, LP for petitioner’s person(s) most qualified or that the topics for inquiry and for document production be limited to the issue of standing. (See Drury Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. A.) Real party contends that the deposition notice is probative of petitioner’s standing.

 

As an initial matter, the Court addresses petitioner’s contentions regarding the limits of discovery in an action for administrative mandamus.  Petitioner is correct that “discovery under section 1094.5, unlike general civil discovery, cannot be used to go on a fishing expedition looking for unknown facts to support speculative theories.” (Pomona Valley Hospital Med. Ctr. v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 93, 102.)  Thus, discovery in an administrative mandamus proceeding “may reasonably be limited to inquiries calculated to yield evidence which through no fault of the offeror does not appear in the administrative record,” including evidence improperly excluded at the administrative hearing or evidence that could not have been produced at the hearing with the exercise of reasonable diligence.  (Ibid.)  Here, however, real party does not seek to augment the record with evidence that could not have been presented during the administrative proceedings under CCP § 1094.5(e). Rather, real party seeks to examine petitioner’s standing to bring the instant petition. Extra-record evidence may be admitted to negate an affirmative defense of standing. (See Western States Petroleum Assn. v. Superior Court (1995) 9 Cal.4th 559, 575, fn. 5; Running Fence Corp. v. Superior Court (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 400, 424, fn. 14.)

 

Petitioner appears not to dispute that discovery is allowed in administrative mandamus proceedings on the issue of standing. (Mtn. at 8; Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702-03 [affirming trial court’s imposition of issue sanction establishing that petitioner lacked standing in CEQA action for failure to submit to deposition where real party should inquire about standing].) However, petitioner does appear to dispute the extent to which real party is entitled to examine petitioner’s standing.

 

In the motion, petitioner notes that it has offered to provide its Articles of Incorporation and By-laws, proof of corporate good standing, and the name of at least one member living or working in Long Beach. (Drury Decl. ¶ 9(b).) According to the Opposition, petitioner did so.  (Opp at 4; Friedman Decl. ¶ 2 & Exs. A & B.)

 

In terms of additional discovery, petitioner contends that only the following topics set forth in the deposition notice are pertinent to its standing:

 

17. The bases of the allegations of Paragraph 10 of SAFER’s First Amended Petition in this action, specifically, that SAFER has “members who live and/or work in or near the City of

Long Beach.”

18. The following information concerning each member of SAFER who SAFER contends lives and/or works in or near the City of Long Beach:

(a) The location of such place of residence and/or place of work, as applicable;

(b) The distance from the member’s place of residence and/or place of work, as applicable, and the PROJECT (as defined below);

(c) The member’s knowledge about the PROJECT;

(d) The member’s knowledge about the allegations of SAFER’s First Amended Petition in this action; and

(e) All communications between the member and SAFER, or between the member and any other person or entity, concerning the Project.

 

Petitioner also contends that only the following document requests are pertinent to its standing:

 

REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 14: DOCUMENTS sufficient to show the bases of the allegations of Paragraph 10 of SAFER’s First Amended Verified Petition in this action, specifically, that SAFER has “members who live and/or work in or near the City of Long Beach.”

 

REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 15: As to each member who SAFER alleges lives and/or works in or near the City of Long Beach, DOCUMENTS sufficient to show:

(a) The location of such place of residence and/or place of work, as applicable;

(b) The distance from the member’s place of residence and/or place of work, as applicable, and the PROJECT (as defined below);

(c) The member’s knowledge about the PROJECT; and

(d) The member’s knowledge about the allegations of SAFER’s First Amended Petition in this action.

 

Petitioner, however, cannot limit real party’s discovery only to topics and documents petitioner deems relevant to standing.  In Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, the real party sought to take the deposition of the petition’s person most qualified on the issue of standing. The real party was informed and believed: (1) that the petitioner was a shell corporation, which consisted of two members; (2) that the address used by petitioner was a law corporation; (3) that the petitioner had no assets; and (4) that, in prior lawsuits where money was awarded to the petitioner, the money was given to the law corporation. (Creed-21, 18 Cal.App.5th at 693.) The trial court ordered the petitioner to produce the person most qualified at deposition and produce all requested documents. (Id. at 695.) After the petitioner failed to produce the PMQ, despite multiple opportunities, the Court of Appeal found that the trial court did not abuse its discretion in deeming the petitioner to lack standing. (Id. at 702-03.)

 

As was the case in Creed-21, the Court finds that the discovery sought by real party here is reasonably tailored based on the real party’s information and belief. Topics such as petitioner’s shareholders, directors, and corporate officers; compliance with corporate formalities; the relationship and communications between a union and petitioner; communications between petitioner and any person who testified in support of petitioner’s objections during the public hearings; and objections to development projects submitted by petitioner are probative of whether petitioner is a shell corporation. Topics such as revenues and expenses of petitioner; capitalization, assets, and liabilities of petitioner; and petitioner’s bank accounts and financial statements are probative of whether petitioner has any assets or is a mere shell. Topics such as distributions to shareholders and other members, the number of lawsuits filed by petitioner, the breakdown of how many lawsuits were resolved at trial versus settlement; the terms of settlements; and monies obtained by petitioner in connection with lawsuits it has filed are probative of whether petitioner receives monies awarded from the cases it brings, as opposed to petitioner’s counsel or some other undisclosed third party for which petitioner may be a mere instrumentality or shell.

 

While a corporation may have a right of associational privacy (see Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 852 [“[C]ompelled disclosure of an individual's private associational affiliations and activities . . . frequently poses one of the most serious threats to the free exercise of this constitutionally endowed right [of association]”]), any corporate privacy right is outweighed by respondent’s right to conduct discovery. (SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 756 [“The discovery’s relevance to the subject matter of the pending dispute and whether the discovery ‘“appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence” ’ is balanced against the corporate right of privacy”].)

 

Discovery about settlements is not privileged under Evidence Code § 1152 because it is not being admitted to prove liability for loss or damage. (See Evid. Code § 1152(a).)

 

To the extent that questions asked at deposition or certain responsive documents are subject to the attorney-client privilege, the proper remedy is to object to the question at deposition or object to production of the privileged document; it is not to bar the deposition and document production altogether. Attorneys can instruct their clients not to answer based on attorney-client privilege. (CCP § 2025.460(a).) The merit of an objection based on privilege can be adjudicated in a motion to compel answer at deposition. (CCP § 2025.480.)

 

While petitioner contends that certain evidence is sufficient to establish standing, real party is entitled to “test the pleadings.” (Burke v. Superior Court (1969) 71 Cal.2d 276, 281.) This includes petitioner’s allegations of standing. (SAP ¶¶ 10, 11, 13; see, e.g. SJJC Aviation Services, LLC v. City of San Jose (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 1043, 1057-58 [“[W]here the claim of ‘citizen’ or ‘public interest’ standing is driven by personal objectives rather than ‘broader public concerns,’ a court may find the litigant to lack such standing….’”].)

 

The motion is DENIED.

Petitioner Los Angeles Sunshine Coalition seeks a writ of mandate directing respondent City of Los Angeles to make secondary employment forms for officers at Hollywood Station from 2019, 2020, and 2021 available for inspection.

 

I.       Factual Background

 

            In June 2021, petitioner Los Angeles Sunshine Coalition submitted a request for public records pursuant to the California Public Records Act (“CPRA”), Request 21-4348, to the Los Angeles Police Department (“LAPD” or “Department”) through its agent and under a pseudonym. (Riskin Decl. ¶ 3 & Pet. Ex. 1.) The request sought secondary employment (i.e., moonlighting, paid details, or off-duty work) forms within the possession and control of the LAPD for officers at Hollywood Station from 2019 through 2021. (Pet. Ex. 1.)  Before engaging in off-duty, secondary employment work, Department policies require LAPD personnel to submit for review and approval “Permits for Secondary Employment” and, if in the film industry, a “Motion Picture/Television Filming Work Permit” (collectively “Permits for Secondary Employment” or “PSE”). (Gillani Dec. ¶ 2-5.)

 

Prior to the filing of the instant Petition, respondent City of Los Angeles had failed to provide any determination on petitioner’s request. (Pet. ¶ 9; Ans. ¶ 9.) Since the initiation of this action, respondent responded with a determination that key information sought in the request is exempt from disclosure due to privacy (Gov. Code § 7927.700) and public interest (Gov. Code § 7922.000). (Hong Decl. ¶ 2 & Ex. 101.)

Respondent provided petitioner with an Excel spreadsheet of multiple non-individualized responsive categories of information extracted from responsive Permits for Secondary Employment: officer rank, division, dates the permits were signed and approved, expiration dates, the type of secondary employment, and the names of the commanding officers who approved the permits. (Gillani Decl. ¶ 7.) Respondent withheld the actual copies of responsive Permits for Secondary Employment, as well as all other categories of information contained therein, including the officer’s name and serial number, the secondary employer’s name, location, and contact information; the days and hours of off-duty employment; and a description of secondary employment duties to be performed. (Gillani Decl. ¶¶ 4, 5, 7.)

 

II.      Procedural History

 

            On May 16, 2023, petitioner filed a Verified Petition for Writ of Mandate. On June 21, 2023, respondent filed an Answer. On May 28, 2024, petitioner filed an opening brief. On June 25, 2024, respondent filed an opposition. On July 10, 2024, petitioner filed a reply.

 

III.     Standard of Review

 

Pursuant to the CPRA, individual citizens have a right to access government records. In enacting the CPRA, the California Legislature declared that “access to information concerning the conduct of the people’s business is a fundamental and necessary right of every person in this state.” (Gov. Code § 7921.000; see also Cal. Const. Art. I, Sec. 3(b); County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 57, 63.)

 

“[E]very person has a right to inspect any public record” of a state or local agency subject to statutory exemptions. (Gov. Code § 7922.530(a); see also Gov. Code § 7920.510(h) [definition of “local agency” includes agent of a city].) The California Constitution mandates that the CPRA be “broadly construed,” while any statute “that limits the right of access” must be “narrowly construed.” (See Cal. Const. Art. I, Sec. 3(b)(2); see also Nat’l Lawyers Guild v. City of Hayward (2020) 9 Cal.5th 488, 507.) The CPRA “does not allow limitations on access to a public record based upon the purpose for which the record is being requested, if the record is otherwise subject to disclosure.” (Gov. Code § 7921.300.)

 

“Any person may institute a proceeding…for a writ of mandate…to enforce that person’s right…to inspect or receive a copy of any public record or class of public records.” (Gov. Code § 7923.000.) “To establish an agency has a duty to disclose under [the CPRA], the petitioner must show that: (1) the record ‘qualif[ies] as [a] ‘public record[ ]’ …; and (2) the record is ‘in the possession of the agency.’” (Anderson-Barker v Sup.Ct. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 528, 538.) “Whether a record falls within the statutory definition of a ‘public record’ involves a ‘distinct inquiry’ from whether the agency is in possession of that record…. The duty to disclose applies only when the petitioner has satisfied both elements.” (Id. at 539.)

 

CPRA exemptions must be narrowly construed, and the agency bears the burden of showing that a specific exemption applies. (Sacramento County Employees’ Retirement System v. Superior Court (2013) 195 Cal.App.4th 440, 453.) A public agency also has the burden to demonstrate that it properly withheld records on the grounds they are non-responsive to a CPRA request or do not constitute public records. (ACLU of Northern Cal. v. Sup. Ct. (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 55, 83-86.) “Because the agency has full knowledge of the contents of the withheld records and the requester has only the agency's affidavits and descriptions of the documents, its affidavits must be specific enough to give the requester ‘a meaningful opportunity to contest’ the withholding of the documents.” (Id. at 83.)

 

IV.     Analysis

 

A.           Evidentiary Matters

 

Respondent’s evidentiary objections are OVERRULED.

 

B.           Public Record

           

            The CPRA defines “public records” as “any writing containing information relating to the conduct of the public’s business prepared, owned, used, or retained by any state or local agency regardless of physical form or characteristics.” (Gov. Code § 7920.530(a).) “But the mere possession by a public agency of a document does not make the document a public record.” (Coronado Police Officers Ass’n v. Carroll (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1006.)

 

Relying on Coronado, respondent contends that the PSEs sought by petitioner are not public records because they pertain “only to private, off-duty functions largely independent of public employment and do not shed light into how the Department operates or fulfills its official duties.” (Opp. at 6:9-11.) In Coronado, a police officers association sought access to a database created and maintained by the San Diego Public Defender’s Office (“PDO”). The database was “comprised of information contained in the Public Defender’s existing client files that is supplemented with information gathered from other public information sources, such as court files, civil service proceedings, peace officer reports and newspaper articles.” (Coronado, 106 Cal.App.4th at 1005.) The database included impeachment evidence used and shared by PDO defense attorneys. (Ibid.) The trial court denied the association’s CPRA writ petition, and the Court of Appeal affirmed. The Court of Appeal held, in part, that the database was not a public record, even though it was prepared, used, and retained by a local agency.  This was because the PDO’s core function—the representation of indigent criminal defendants—is a private function, because “a public defender is not a state agent, but rather acts as a private attorney when representing clients.” (Id. at 1006-12, discussing Polk County v. Dodson (1981) 454 U.S. 312 [public defender does not act under color of state law when performing lawyer’s tradition functions as defense counsel].) The Court thus reasoned that the database at issue “represents a logical application of the traditional functions of defense counsel” because the information therein is “retained and used to assist in the defense of existing and future clients,” which are “private functions to which the Public Defender is entitled to maintain a level of independence equivalent to a private attorney.”  (Coronado, 106 Cal.App.4th at 1008-09.)

 

            As observed by the Coronado court, the “critical question” in determining if a petitioner is seeking public records is “whether the information contained therein relates to the conduct of the ‘public’s business.’” (Coronado, 106 Cal.App.4th at 1006.)  Here, unlike the database in Coronado, the PSEs at issue are directly related to the public’s business with respect to the restrictions on a police officer’s employment set forth in Penal Code § 70.

 

Penal Code § 70(a) prohibits state or local employees from asking, receiving, or agreeing to receive any reward for doing an official act. However, Penal Code § 70(c)(1) allows peace officers to obtain casual or part-time employment as a private security guard or patrolman for a public entity while off duty provided that the peace officer “is subject to reasonable rules and regulations of the agency for which he or she is a peace officer.” Similarly, Penal Code § 70(d)(1) allows peace officers to obtain casual or part-time employment as a private security guard or patrolman for a private employer while off duty so long as certain conditions are met, including: “(A) The peace officer is in his or her police uniform. [¶] (B) The casual or part-time employment as a private security guard or patrolman is approved by the county board of supervisors with jurisdiction over the principal employer or by the board’s designee or by the city council with jurisdiction over the principal employer or by the council’s designee. [¶] (C) The wearing of uniforms and equipment is approved by the principal employer. [¶] (D) The peace officer is subject to reasonable rules and regulations of the agency for which he or she is a peace officer.” (Pen. Code § 70(d)(1)(A-D).)

 

            The Department requires its personnel to submit a PSE before engaging in off-duty, secondary employment. (Gillani Decl. ¶¶ 1, 2.) In its opposition, the Department described the process of approval as follows:

 

In compliance with legal requirements and Department policies, Supervisors and Commanding Officers review and investigate new and renewal permit applications to assure that off-duty, secondary employment is not prohibited with no conflicts of interest or interference with primary employment responsibilities, and that the employee officer is qualified and possesses any required licenses or permits.

 

(Gillani Decl. ¶ 2.) Thus, although a PSE may pertain to private employment, there can be no serious doubt that the Department’s required submission, review, and approval of PSEs pursuant to Department policies and legal requirements, including review for interference with primary employment responsibilities (i.e., duties working as LAPD police officer), is a public function.  (See Commission on Peace Officer Standards & Training v. Superior Court [“CPOST”] (2007) 42 Cal.4th 278, 288 n.3 [finding “undisputed” that records and information possessed by the state agency Commission were public records that “related to the public’s business” because “the Commission uses them to monitor the compliance . . . with Commission regulations

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that the PSEs requested by petitioner are public records.

           

C.           Exemptions Claimed by Respondent

 

Respondent argues that, even if PSEs are public records, such records may be withheld because: (1) the records are considered “personal data” contained in a peace officer’s confidential personnel file under Penal Code §§ 832.8(a)(1) and 832.7(a); (2) disclosure of the requested records would constitute an unwarranted invasion of privacy; and (3) the public interest served by not disclosing the records outweighs any public interest in disclosure.

 

1.            Personal Data

 

The CPRA “does not require disclosure of records, the disclosure of which is exempted or prohibited pursuant to federal or state law, including, but not limited to, provisions of the Evidence Code relating to privilege.” (Gov. Code § 7927.705.)

 

Under Penal Code § 832.7(a), “the personnel records of peace officers and custodial officers and records maintained by a state or local agency pursuant to Section 832.5, or information obtained from these records, are confidential and shall not be disclosed in any criminal or civil proceeding except by discovery pursuant to Sections 1043 and 1046 of the Evidence Code.” (Pen. Code § 832.7(a).) “Personnel records” includes records relating to “[p]ersonal data, including marital status, family members, educational and employment history, home addresses, or similar information.” (Pen. Code § 832.8(a)(1).)

 

Our Supreme Court has examined what constitutes peace officer personnel records exempt from CPRA disclosure and concluded that “only the types of information enumerated in section 832.8” so qualifies.  (CPOST, 42 Cal.4th at 293.)  With respect to section 832.8(a)(1)’s “employment history . . . or similar information,” the Court has adopted the view that such information “refers only to information about the individual’s previous employment” and not employment information that “relates to the officer’s then-current status with [the] department.”  (Id. at 294, emphasis in original.)  In so holding, our Supreme Court quoted and adopted the Court of Appeal’s conclusion in City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 883 that section 832.8, subdivision (a):

 

presents a list of general pieces of information that might be found on any resume or job application, i.e., the subdivision (a) list would include basic status or identifying information as he or she came to the job.  It does not include any information that would be specific to the current job. . . Information that is specific to the employee’s current status as a peace officer would not be “similar information” to the other information covered by the statute.

 

(CPOST, 42 Cal.4th at 295, quoting City of Los Angeles, 111 Cal.App.4th at 891-92.)

 

In light of such guidance, the PSE forms at issue, which are part of the process for approval of simultaneous, secondary employment that is consistent with LAPD polices, constitute information relating to an employee’s current status as a peace officer.  As such, they do not constitute a personnel record contemplated by section 832.8. Accordingly, the information sought by petitioner is not privileged under Penal Code §§ 832.7(a) or 832.8(a)(1) and cannot be exempted from CPRA disclosure on that basis.

 

2.            Privacy

 

The CPRA “does not require disclosure of personnel, medical, or similar files, the disclosure of which would constitute an unwarranted invasion of personal privacy.” (Gov. Code § 7927.700.)  As discussed above, PSEs do not constitute personnel records. PSEs also do not constitute medical files on their face. Accordingly, the question is whether PSEs are “similar files, the disclosure of which would constitute an unwarranted invasion of personal privacy.”

 

            “The public’s interest in the qualifications and conduct of peace officers is substantial, a circumstance that both diminishes and counterbalances any expectation officers may have that their names and employment as peace officers will be confidential.” (CPOST, 42 Cal.4th at 299.) Contending that secondary employment is separate from primary employment with LAPD, respondent contends that the PSEs concern “private economic affairs completely unrelated to the individual’s public employment with the Department.” (Opp. at 14:5-7.)  The Court disagrees.  For secondary off-duty employment with a private employer, peace officers must be in their police uniforms, have approval of the municipal governing body or its designee, and be subject to the reasonable rules and regulations of LAPD. (Pen. Code § 70(d)(1)(A), (B), (D).) Because peace officers work in uniform and are subject to LAPD regulations and rules while engaged in secondary employment, secondary employment implicates the qualifications and conduct of peace officers, in which the public has an interest.

 

The PSEs do not constitute an unwarranted invasion of personal privacy.

 

3.            Balancing of Interests

 

Government Code § 7922.000 states: “An agency shall justify withholding any record by demonstrating that the record in question is exempt under express provisions of this division, or that on the facts of the particular case the public interest served by not disclosing the record clearly outweighs the public interest served by disclosure of the record.”

 

            Respondent contends that disclosure of the PSEs would allow members of the public to search the name of any officer named in the PSEs in other databases to discover their domiciled city, county, state, and zip code. (Bislig Decl. ¶ 2.) Respondent contends this would facilitate locating the officer by any member of the public seeking to inflict harm. This speculative concern is insufficient to warrant exemption from disclosure of the PSEs. “‘A mere assertion of possible endangerment’ is insufficient to justify nondisclosure.” (POST, 42 Cal.4th at 302, quoting CBS, Inc. v. Block (1986) 42 Cal.3d 646, 652.) While undercover officers may be entitled to anonymity (POST, 42 Cal.4th at 301), respondent does not discuss—let alone persuade—that officers at the Hollywood Station require anonymity to perform their duties or to protect themselves. Respondent thus fails to show that disclosure of the names of officers engaged in secondary employment “would increase the threat to officer safety presented by those with a generalized hostility toward law enforcement officers.” (Id. at 303.)

 

            On the other hand, as discussed above, by virtue of the regulation of secondary employment under Penal Code § 70, the public has an interest in learning about secondary employment of peace officers. As one example, the public has an interest in determining whether secondary employment contributes to fatigue of officers or otherwise interferes with or impairs the performance of their duties.  The public may also have an interest in which officers may have an employment relationship with a person or entity that might influence the actions or judgment of the officer.  Absent any particularized and concrete safety concern posed by the disclosure of PSEs, the Court finds the public interest in secondary employment subject to approval by LAPD for its officers outweighs any interest in nondisclosure.

 

V.      Conclusion

 

            Respondent having failed to demonstrate the application of any exemption, the Court finds that petitioner is entitled to inspect the requested PSEs, which are public records.  The petition is GRANTED. Pursuant to Local Rule 3.231(n), petitioner shall prepare, serve, and ultimately file a proposed judgment and proposed writ of mandate.

II.      Procedural History

            On August 14, 2023, the instant case was deemed related to Lawrence L. Welk, Jr. v. Vanowen Genpar, LLC et al., Case No. 23STCP02326.

 

            On May 22, 2024, petitioner filed an opening brief.[1] On June 24, 2024, respondent filed an opposition. On July 10, 2024, petitioner filed a reply.

 

III.     Standard of Review

 

CCP § 1085(a) provides: “A writ of mandate may be issued by any court to any inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or person, to compel the performance of an act which the law specially enjoins, as a duty resulting from an office, trust, or station, or to compel the admission of a party to the use and enjoyment of a right or office to which the party is entitled, and from which the party is unlawfully precluded by that inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or person.”

 

“There are two essential requirements to the issuance of a traditional writ of mandate: (1) a clear, present and usually ministerial duty on the part of the respondent, and (2) a clear, present and beneficial right on the part of the petitioner to the performance of that duty.” (California Assn. for Health Services at Home v. State Dept. of Health Services (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 696, 704.) Corporate inspections are governed under CCP § 1085. (Wolf v. CDS Devco (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 903, 912.)

 

Corporations Code § 1602 states: “Every director shall have the absolute right at any reasonable time to inspect and copy all books, records and documents of every kind and to inspect the physical properties of the corporation of which such person is a director and also of its subsidiary corporations, domestic or foreign.” “Upon refusal of a lawful demand for inspection, the superior court of the proper county, may enforce the right of inspection with just and proper conditions ….” (Corp. Code § 1603.)

 

“The petitioner always bears the burden of proof in a mandate proceeding brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 1085.” (California Correctional Peace Officers Assn. v. State Personnel Bd. (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1133, 1154.)

 

IV.     Analysis

 

1.            Joint Opening Brief

 

The opening brief is a joint opening brief for Lynn Welk, Jr. v. The Welk Group, Inc., Case No. 23STCP01994, and Lawrence L. Welk, Jr. v. Vanowen Genpar, LLC et al., Case No. 23STCP02326. While the two cases are related, they are not consolidated. (8/14/23 Minute Order.) Accordingly, this ruling only pertains to petitioner Lynn Welk’s case.

 

2.            Merits

 

It is undisputed that petitioner is a director of respondent and has inspection rights under Corporations Code § 1602. For petitioner to obtain the corporate documents she seeks, she must show that she “made a demand for inspection, which was refused.” (Fowler v. Golden Pacific Bancorp, Inc. (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 205, 217, citing Corp. Code §§ 1602, 1603.)

 

As noted above, respondent has produced documents. Petitioner still seeks: (1) a complete list of all bank account numbers and the associated financial institution, including opening and closing date (if applicable) associated with respondent; (2) the complete banking records (to include deposit receipts, internal check requests, and internal correspondence pertaining to deposit receipts and checks requests), complete bank statements, and cancelled checks for all bank accounts associated with respondent; and (3) all QuickBooks data held by respondent, its agents, accountants, or bookkeepers pertaining to respondent. (See Proposed Order.)

 

Petitioner requested copies of the following documents:

 

1. The Operating Agreement(s) for any L.L.C.’s to which W.G.I. [i.e., respondent] is, directly or indirectly, a party, other than those for VanOwen and Wilshire, which I already have.

 

2. The Articles of Incorporation, By-laws, and any shareholders agreement(s) of W.G.I., or its subsidiaries, including, but not limited to, any amendments or proposed amendments to each of the preceding documents.

 

3. All board records for the past three years, including, but not limited to, any materials provided to the Board of Directors before board meetings, and complete copies of all minutes of every General and Special Board meeting(s).

 

4. All organization charts or any other such documentation reflecting the entity structure of W.G.I. and its wholly and partially owned subsidiaries.

 

5. Any annual financial statements for W.G.I. and its wholly and partially owned subsidiaries for each of the following years: 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, and interim 2023.

 

6. A schedule of all intellectual property rights held by W.G.I., including, but not limited to, the rights to the Lawrence Welk programs, music, trademarks, and copyrights.

 

7. Any records concerning any transaction(s) between or among W.G.I. on the one hand and any Related Party on the other hand. For the purposes of this request, a “Related Party” includes, but is not limited to, Jonathan Fredericks, Jeffrey Welk, Stephen Baron, David Mack, Kevin Welk, and any entity owned or controlled by each of these individuals.

 

8. The budget for 2023 for W.G.I., including, but not limited to, any of its subsidiaries.

 

9. Any appraisals(s) for any real property in which W.G.I. has any interest.

 

10. Any material agreements) that W.G.I. has with (1) any outside advisor or manager, including any property manager; or (2) any party concerning its non-real property assets; (3) any attorney or law firm; or (4) accountant or accounting firm.

 

11. All agreements relating to “other partnership income” as reported on account 9208001.

 

12. W.G.I.’s federal and State tax returns, whether originally filed or amended, for 2019, 2020, 2021, and 2022 including all statements, schedules, and attachments.

 

(Lynn Welk Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. A.)

 

            Petitioner does not cite which, if any, of her requests encompass the documents sought in her proposed order. Notably, bank records, bank statements, and checks are not listed in petitioner’s requests. Nor are QuickBooks data. Accordingly, petitioner fails to show that respondent refused to produce the documents which she seeks in her writ petition.

 

In the reply, petitioner argues that respondent is narrowly interpreting her requests to exclude QuickBooks records. Petitioner appears to argue that her request for financial statements (No. 5), records of transactions between respondent and any Related Party (No. 7), budgets (No. 8), and tax statements including statements, schedules, and attachments (No. 12) include QuickBooks data. (Reply at 3:27-4:4.) Petitioner provides no evidence that these documents are contained in QuickBooks. (See Opening Br. at 7:22-23 [“QuickBooks is widely used accounting software used by businesses to manage acceptance of payments, payments of bills and invoices, and which includes payroll functions”].) Moreover, as phrased in petitioner’s proposed order, petitioner seeks “all QuickBooks data” held by respondent. Petitioner’s desire for QuickBooks data is untethered to any request that she sent respondent in April 2023. The Court declines to rewrite petitioner’s request for her in order to capture something for which she did not ask.

 

Moreover, in petitioner’s April 5, 2024 meet and confer letter, petitioner gave no indication that any QuickBooks data was missing. Indeed, with respect to financial statements and transactions between respondent and any “Related Party,” as respondent represents, petitioner asked respondent to confirm that all responsive documents were produced. (Baird Decl. ¶ 23 & Ex. R.) Petitioner does not point to any communication to respondent indicating that, prior to the filing of the Opening Brief, petitioner requested any QuickBooks data. Accordingly, petitioner has not shown that respondent refused to produce QuickBooks data.

 

Absent a “refusal of a lawful demand for inspection,” no writ of mandate shall issue.

 

V.      Conclusion

 

The First Amended Petition is DENIED. Pursuant to Local Rule 3.231(n), respondent The Welk Group, Inc. shall prepare, serve, and ultimately file a proposed judgment.

(Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 2.) He is also a limited partner of respondents WRE Vanowen L.P. (“Vanowen LP”) and WRE Wilshire L.P. (“Wilshire LP”), holding approximately 9% of the limited partnership interests in each entity. (Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 3.) The other member or limited partner in each entity is respondent Welk Real Estate, Inc., a wholly owned subsidiary of respondent Welk Group, Inc. (Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 4.)[1]

Petitioner’s requests of the two limited partnerships (“LP”) were made as a limited partner, and his requests of the two limited liability companies (“LLC”) were made as a member. The parties dispute whether full and complete production to petitioner’s requests has been made.

III.     Standard of Review

 

CCP § 1085(a) provides: “A writ of mandate may be issued by any court to any inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or person, to compel the performance of an act which the law specially enjoins, as a duty resulting from an office, trust, or station, or to compel the admission of a party to the use and enjoyment of a right or office to which the party is entitled, and from which the party is unlawfully precluded by that inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or person.”

 

“There are two essential requirements to the issuance of a traditional writ of mandate: (1) a clear, present and usually ministerial duty on the part of the respondent, and (2) a clear, present and beneficial right on the part of the petitioner to the performance of that duty.” (California Assn. for Health Services at Home v. State Dept. of Health Services (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 696, 704.) Corporate inspections are governed under CCP § 1085. (Wolf v. CDS Devco (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 903, 912.)

 

The rights of a member of an LLC to inspect and copy records of the LLC are set forth in Corporations Code § 17704.10.[3] Section 17704.10 provides in relevant part:

 

(a) Upon the request of a member or transferee, for purposes reasonably related to the interest of that person as a member or a transferee, a manager or, if the limited liability company is member-managed, a member in possession of the requested information, shall promptly deliver, in writing, to the member or transferee, at the expense of the limited liability company, a copy of the information required to be maintained by paragraphs (1), (2), and (4) of subdivision (d) of Section 17701.13, and any written operating agreement of the limited liability company.

 

(b) Each member, manager, and transferee has the right, upon reasonable request, for purposes reasonably related to the interest of that person as a member, manager, or transferee, to each of the following:

 

(1) To inspect and copy during normal business hours any of the records required to be maintained pursuant to Section 17701.13.

 

(2) To obtain in writing from the limited liability company, promptly after becoming available, a copy of the limited liability company's federal, state, and local income tax returns for each year….

….

 

(g) In any action under this section or under Section 17713.07, if the court finds the failure of the limited liability company to comply with the requirements of this section is without justification, the court may award an amount sufficient to reimburse the person bringing the action for the reasonable expenses incurred by that person, including attorney's fees, in connection with the action or proceeding.

 

The rights of a limited partner of an LP to inspect and copy records of the LP are set forth in section 15903.4, which provides in relevant part:

 

(a) On 10 days’ demand, made in a record received by the limited partnership, a limited partner may inspect and copy any information required to be maintained pursuant to Section 15901.11 during regular business hours in the limited partnership’s principal office. The limited partner need not have any particular purpose for seeking the information.

 

(b) Subject to subdivision (g) [governing trade secrets and other information the disclosure of which would be harmful to the limited partnership], during regular business hours and at a reasonable location specified by the limited partnership, a limited partner may obtain from the limited partnership, which may be transmitted via electronic transmission, and inspect and copy true and full information regarding the state of the activities and financial condition of the limited partnership and other information regarding the activities of the limited partnership as is just and reasonable if:

 

(1) the limited partner seeks the information for a purpose reasonably related to the partner’s interest as a limited partner;

 

(2) the limited partner makes a demand in a record received by the limited partnership, describing with reasonable particularity the information sought and the purpose for seeking the information; and

 

(3) the information sought is directly connected to the limited partner’s purpose.

 

“The petitioner always bears the burden of proof in a mandate proceeding brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 1085.” (California Correctional Peace Officers Assn. v. State Personnel Bd. (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1133, 1154.)

2.            Merits

 

It is undisputed that petitioner is a member of Vanowen LLC and Wilshire LLC and has inspection rights under section 17704.10. It is also undisputed that petitioner is a limited partner of Vanowen LP and Wilshire LP and has inspection rights under section 15903.4.

 

For petitioner to obtain the corporate documents he seeks, he must show that he “made a demand for inspection, which was refused.” (Fowler v. Golden Pacific Bancorp, Inc. (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 205, 217, citing Corp. Code §§ 1602, 1603; see also McIntyre v. Sonoma Valley Unified School Dist. (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 170, 180, quoting Fair v. Fountain Valley School Dist. (1979) 90 Cal.App.3d 180, 186 [“’[T]he party who seeks a writ of mandate has the burden of proving that the official body which fails to perform an act has thus violated its duty toward him by denying him a clear and present right’”].)

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that petitioner also seeks an order against respondents Welk Real Estate, Inc. and Welk Group, Inc. Petitioner never served requests on these two respondents. (Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 5 & Exs. A-D.) Petitioner does not respond to respondents’ contention that Welk Real Estate, Inc. and Welk Group, Inc. appear to be improperly named as respondents. (See Opp. at 1, fn. 1; see generally Reply.) Accordingly, it appears these two respondents never refused any request to produce documents.

 

With respect to the two LLCs and the two LPs on whom petitioner served requests, respondents have produced documents. Petitioner still seeks: (1) a complete list of all bank account numbers and the associated financial institution, including opening and closing date (if applicable) associated with respondents; (2) the complete banking records (to include deposit receipts, internal check requests, and internal correspondence pertaining to deposit receipts and checks requests), complete bank statements, and cancelled checks for all bank accounts associated with respondents; and (3) all QuickBooks data held by respondents, their agents, accountants, or bookkeepers pertaining to respondents (See Proposed Order.)

 

With respect seeking a list of all bank account numbers and associated financial institutions, petitioner does not cite to which of his requests the list corresponds. (See Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 5 & Exs. A-D.) Nevertheless, on May 13, 2024, petitioner asked respondents for a list of all bank account numbers and the associated financial institution, including opening and closing dates. (Baird Decl. ¶ 18 & Ex. 12.) Respondents maintain they provided this information. (Opp. at 10.) However, respondents did not cite any evidence indicating that they provided the requested information as they claim. “An attorney’s argument in pleadings is not evidence.” (Hebberd-Kulow Enterprises, Inc. v. Kelomar, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 272, 283.)  Because the list of bank accounts is arguably within the scope of bank statements requested in No. 5 of petitioner’s requests, and because respondents do not argue that petitioner never requested the list of accounts, the Court orders respondents to provide the list sought in the first category contained in petitioner’s proposed order. 

 

With respect to petitioner’s contention that he is entitled to “complete banking records,” the Court notes that, in No. 5 of petitioner’s requests, petitioner requested “[b]ank records, bank statements, cancelled checks, savings accounts, money-market accounts, investment accounts, books, records, general ledgers, general journal and quarterly and annual profit and loss and balance sheet financial statements” for the respective entity and/or the “Property,” defined as any real property purchased, owned or controlled by the entity, “for the periods 2017-2022 and interim 2023 whether internally prepared or prepared by outside accountant(s).” (Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 5 & Exs. A-D [definition of “Property” in No. 1].)

 

            Petitioner contends that there were references to certain bank accounts in records produced by respondents but that the statements and cancelled checks for those accounts have not been received. (McGonigle Decl. ¶ 4; Smith Decl. ¶¶ 3-6.) Petitioner does not explain how such references indicate that the accounts are associated with respondents or the Property. Without seeing the actual references to accounts, the Court cannot ascertain whether the purportedly missing bank statements and checks are within the scope of No. 5 in petitioner’s requests.

 

Moreover, petitioner now defines “banking records” to include deposit receipts, internal check requests, and internal correspondence pertaining to deposit receipts and check requests. Respondents contend such items were never requested. (Opp. at 10, fn. 6.) The Court agrees. (See Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 5 & Exs. A-D.) Petitioner only now expands the definition of “banking records” after having made his requests and having had opportunity to clarify.  The Court thus does not find that petitioner has met his burden to demonstrate respondents should have understood his request for “bank records” to include deposit receipts, “internal” check requests, or “internal” correspondence relating to deposit receipts and check requests. The Court will not order production of the second category of documents contained in petitioner’s proposed order.

 

            With respect seeking QuickBooks data, in No. 20 of petitioner’s requests, petitioner requested “‘QuickBooks’ or other electronic data file including passwords, if it exists, for the years 2017-2022 and interim 2023” for the respective entity and/or the “Property,” defined as any real property purchased, owned or controlled by the entity. (Lawrence Welk Decl. ¶ 5 & Exs. A-D [definition of “Property” in No. 1].) Respondents contend that such data is the property of third parties and is not in respondents’ possession, custody, or control. (Baird Decl. ¶ 15 & Ex. 10.) Petitioner contends that such data inputs “are not proprietary to or the property of any QuickBooks entity, but rather the data inputted into QuickBooks is the property of the end user” and therefore is readily available from respondents’ accountants or bookkeepers. (Opening Br. 7:27-8:4.)

 

In the opposition, respondents represent that their “counsel previously informed petitioner’s counsel ‘[a]s to the QuickBooks, any electronic accounting files are the property of third parties and are not in Respondents’ possession, custody, or control. The only electronic accounting files for these entities are those created for them by the property manager (Lincoln Property Management Company, f/k/a RiverRock) and other third parties.’” (Opp. at 11:1-5.) If that is the case, then it may be true that respondents did not refuse to provide the requested QuickBooks data. Instead, it may be that respondents were not able to produce the data inputs because a property manager has possession, custody, and control over them. However, “[a]n attorney’s argument in pleadings is not evidence.” (Hebberd-Kulow Enterprises, Inc. v. Kelomar, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 272, 283.) Furthermore, respondents do not state why they cannot obtain the QuickBooks data from the property manager, who would appear to be their agent. Accordingly, the Court orders production of the third category contained in petitioner’s proposed order.

 

            Lastly, the Court notes respondents’ assertion that no writ should issue because they are willing to perform without coercion. (Opp. at 11:7-18; see Bruce v. Gregory (1967) 65 Cal.2d 666, 671 [“[The] remedy of Mandamus will not be employed where the respondents show that they are willing to perform the duty without the coercion of the writ].”) Nonetheless, because respondents have not sufficiently justified not producing certain documents requested by petitioner, the Court will issue an order for respondents to produce documents.

DEMURRER TO VERIFIED PETITION

FOR WRIT OF MANDATE

 

Date:               7/25/24 (1:30 PM) 

Case:                           Eric Pelton v. County of Los Angeles et al. (24STCP00050) 

  

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Respondent County of Los Angeles’ Demurrer to Verified Petition for Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED.

 

Respondent’s requests to take judicial notice of the Los Angeles County Charter and the Civil Service Rules of the County of Los Angeles are GRANTED. (Morgado v. City and County of San Francisco (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1, 13, fn. 8 [court must take judicial notice of county and city charters]; Olson v. County of Sacramento (1974) 38 Cal.App.3d 958, 964 [taking judicial notice of civil service commission rules].)

 

Petitioner Eric Pelton seeks a writ of mandate directing respondent Civil Service Commission (“Commission”) to set aside the dismissal of his appeal. (Pet. ¶ 6; Prayer ¶ 2.) In March 2022, while his appeal was pending, petitioner retired. (Pet. ¶ 1.) On October 4, 2023, the Commission dismissed the appeal, finding it had lost jurisdiction due to Pelton’s retirement in March 2022. (Pet. ¶ 6.)


Petitioner’s retirement while his appeal before the Commission was pending divested the Commission of jurisdiction. “A civil service commission created by charter has only the special and limited jurisdiction expressly authorized by the charter.” (Zuniga v. Los Angeles County Civil Service Com. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1255, 1259, quoting Hunter v. Los Angeles County Civil Service Com. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 191, 194.) Under the Los Angeles County Charter, which applies in this case, once an employee voluntarily separates from service, the Civil Service Commission is without jurisdiction to adjudicate the merits of any appeal. (Zuniga, 137 Cal.App.4th at 1261.) This is the case even if the employee was a current employee at the time of the appeal. (Ibid.; see also County of Los Angeles Dept. of Health Services v. Civil Service Com. of County of Los Angeles (Latham) (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 391, 401 [“Zuniga stands for the bright line proposition that, where an employee retires during the pendency of a civil service appeal, her future status as an employee by definition is no longer at issue…. [T]he Commission has authority to address only matters involving a member of the civil service, and a person who has retired is no longer a member of the civil service”].)

 

Relying on Hudson v. County of Los Angeles (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 392, petitioner contends that the Zuniga rule does not apply because of purported “exigent circumstances.” (Pet. ¶ 5.) Hudson does not set forth any exception from the Zuniga rule based on “exigent circumstances.” In Hudson, pending an appeal from discharge from employment, the public retirement system granted the employee deputy sheriff a disability retirement and she withdrew her contributions after having been told that she was ineligible due to lack of the required numbers of years of service. (Hudson, 232 Cal.App.4th at 396-97.) The Court of Appeal found that the bright line rule of Zuniga and Latham did not apply under the circumstances presented in that case, including the Los Angeles County Sheriff’s Department initiating the employee’s disability retirement, the Commission ordering the employment restored, the Sheriff’s Department agreeing and returning the plaintiff to work in an unsworn position pending her appeal, the plaintiff actively seeking the public retirement system Los Angeles County Employees Retirement Association’s (“LACERA”) reevaluation of its finding of disability so that she could return to work, and LACERA’s refusal to reevaluate the disability on the ground that the plaintiff was no longer a member after having already withdrawn her retirement contributions. (Id. at 396-99, 406.) Because the plaintiff’s status as an employee was at issue, the Court of Appeal declined to apply the Zuniga/Latham rule. (Id. at 413.)

 

Here, there is no allegation in the Petition that petitioner’s status as an employee was at issue. There is no allegation that the Department of Parks and Recreation (“DPR”), petitioner’s employer, or the Commission ever gave any indication that petitioner could return to employment. Rather, the DPR terminated petitioner’s employer for not having registered with Fulgent Genetics and having vaccinated against COVID-19. (Pet. ¶¶ 1, 8.) While petitioner’s appeal was pending, he retired “[d]ue to exigent and financial circumstances.” (Pet. ¶ 1.) While the employee in Hudson had withdrawn her retirement contributions due to economic need (Hudson, 232 Cal.App.4th at 397), the Hudson court in no way indicated that economic need would allow the Commission to retain jurisdiction of an appeal when the employee has retired. (Deiro v. Los Angeles County Civil Service Commission (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 925, 933.) “The essence of the Zuniga/Latham rule is that a plaintiff's future status as an employee is not at issue after he has retired. In the ordinary case, this is…true…of retirees who choose to retire after years of service: their future status as employees is not at issue, unless they or their former employer choose to place it in issue.” (Id. at 934.) “The existence of a case like Hudson…does not change the general rule. It is the exception, on egregious facts—and it was clear throughout the long saga that the plaintiff's future status as an employee was at issue.” (Ibid.) There are no egregious facts alleged here. This is a straightforward case where the Commission had “no authority to order reinstatement or any other relief to a retired person whose future status as an employee is not at issue.” (Id. at 927.)

 

Petitioner alleges that he “was encouraged to retire…to procure a minimal retirement benefit…with the explicit understanding that said application would not impact [his] ability to be restored to service through the Civil Service Commission processes, upon prevailing at hearing.” (Pet. ¶ 1.) “An employee who claims that a resignation has been obtained by duress, fraud, or undue influence, may appeal to the director of personnel, setting forth in writing the facts substantiating the allegation, within 10 business days of the acceptance of the resignation by the appointing power.” (RJN Ex. 2 at 117 [Civil Service Rule 18.09].) To the extent that petitioner contends that his retirement was obtained by duress, fraud, or undue influence, petitioner has not alleged that he appealed the acceptance of his retirement. Accordingly, petitioner cannot argue that his retirement is void.

 

The other cases on which petitioner relies are unavailing because jurisdiction was defined under rules other than the Los Angeles County charter and civil service rules at issue here. (Cf. SEIU, Local 1021 v. County of San Joaquin (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 449 [Memorandum of Understanding between county and union]; Hughes vs. County of San Bernardino (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 542 [San Bernardino County personnel rules]; Hall-Villareal vs. City of Fresno (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 24 [Fresno City Charter].)

 

Because the Court finds the Commission lost jurisdiction, petitioner fails to allege in his second cause of action that there was a prejudicial abuse of discretion, denial of a fair trial, or conduct without or in excess of jurisdiction that would warrant administrative mandamus under CCP § 1094.5 arising from the Commission’s dismissal of petitioner’s appeal. Relatedly, because the Commission had no jurisdiction to hear petitioner’s appeal, petitioner fails to state a ministerial duty to support his claim for traditional mandamus under CCP § 1085 in his first cause of action.

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Although petitioner states that “leave to amend should be granted” (Opp. at 2), petitioner fails to identify how the defects in the petition might be reasonably cured by amendment.  Before allowing leave to amend, the Court will hear a proffer from petitioner as to how the petition can be amended to address the defects set forth herein.
