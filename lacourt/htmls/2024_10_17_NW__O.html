I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Clara Victoria Cruz (“Plaintiff”) alleges she was walking in the parking lot of Defendant Mrs. Gooch’s Natural Food Markets erroneously sued as Whole Foods Market California, Inc. (“Mrs. Gooch’s”) when she tripped and fell over a parking block, sustaining significant injuries. (See Complaint, pg. 4, ¶ GN-1.)

Plaintiff initially filed this action against Mrs. Gooch’s and Defendant 14311 Ventura Development, LLC (“Ventura Development”) but later expanded her action to include Ventura Paving Management, Inc. (“Ventura Paving”).

Ventura Development filed a Cross-Complaint against Mrs. Gooch’s. Mrs. Gooch’s filed a Cross-Complaint against Ventura Development. Ventura Paving filed a Cross-Complaint against Ventura Development.

Ventura Paving now moves for summary judgment as to all causes of action in Plaintiff’s Complaint or, alternatively, for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for premises liability.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On March 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging (1) General Negligence; and (2) Premises Liability.

On July 5, 2023, Ventura Development filed its Answer.

On July 11, 2023, Ventura Development filed its Cross-Complaint, alleging (1) Express Indemnity; (2) Implied Indemnity; (3) Contribution; (4) Breach of Contract; (5) Declaratory Relief Duty to Defend; and (6) Declaratory Relief Duty to Indemnify.

On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff amended her Complaint to change Whole Foods Market California Inc.’s name to Mrs. Gooch’s Food Markets, Inc.

On July 24, 2023, Mrs. Gooch’s filed its Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

On August 11, 2023, Mrs. Gooch’s filed its Answer to Ventura Development’s Cross-Complaint.

On October 13, 2023, Mrs. Gooch’s filed its Cross-Complaint, alleging (1) Express Indemnity; (2) Implied Contractual Indemnity; (3) Equitable Indemnity; and (4) Declaratory Relief.  

On October 23, 2023, Ventura Development filed its Answer to Mrs. Gooch’s Cross-Complaint.

On February 26, 2024, Plaintiff amended her Complaint to add Ventura Paving as a Defendant.

On April 3, 2024, Ventura Paving filed its Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

On April 3, 2024, Ventura Paving filed its Cross-Complaint, alleging (1) Equitable Indemnity; (2) Contribution; and (3) Declaratory Relief.

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff amended her Complaint to add Pacific Star Capital, LLC (“Pacific”), as a Defendant.

On July 30, 2024, Ventura Paving filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment (“Motion”).

On August 2, 2024, Ventura Paving dismissed Pacific from its Cross-Complaint.

On August 22, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Pacific from her Complaint.

On October 3, 2024, Ventura Development filed an Opposition to Ventura Paving’s Motion.

On October 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition to Ventura Paving’s Motion.

On October 9, 2024, Ventura Paving filed its Replies.

On October 11, 2024, Mrs. Gooch’s filed a Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (See Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing such motions, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) Thus, summary judgment or summary adjudication is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) [1]; Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.) 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate or establish an essential element. (See Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must be denied where the moving party’s evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition or where the opposition is weak. (See Leyva v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475; Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384 & 387.) When a plaintiff is the moving party, the plaintiff has a higher burden: it must “present evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not.” (See Aguilar, 25 Cal.4th at 845 (emphasis added).) “The burden of the moving party is to persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find.” (PP Mortgage, Ltd. v. Bizar (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 773, 776 (emphasis in original) (cleaned up).)

Once the moving party has met its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material fact exists to a cause of action or a defense thereto. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(o)(2).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

As a preliminary matter, Ventura Development and Mrs. Gooch’s are not party to this Motion. However, both parties filed papers opposing the Motion and Plaintiff’s Opposition, respectively. Neither party addressed why the Court should consider their filings or how they have standing to oppose. Accordingly, the Court will not consider their filings for the purposes of the Motion.

A.             Evidentiary Issues

The Court will rule on Ventura Paving’s evidentiary objections despite Ventura Paving’s failure to comply with Rules of Court, rule 3.1354(b)(3), which requires a party “[q]uote or set forth the objectionable statement or material” in its evidentiary objections.

The Court OVERRULES Ventura Paving’s evidentiary objections Nos. 5-7, 8-11, 13-18 and SUSTAINS Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 12.  

B.             Undisputed Material Facts

On March 30, 202[1], Plaintiff visited the Whole Food Market located at 14311 Ventura Blvd, Sherman Oaks, CA (“Property”). (See Plaintiff’s Response to Ventura Paving’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) No. 1.) While visiting, Plaintiff tripped and fell over a parking block (“parking block” or “wheel stop”) in the parking lot. (See UMF No. 2.) The parking block Plaintiff tripped and fell over was painted blue, in the exact same shade of blue as the rest of the striping in the area. (See Ventura Paving’s Response to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Additional Undisputed Material Facts (“AUMF”) No. 10.)

Ventura Paving does not own, operate manage, supervise or maintain the Property. (See UMF No. 5.) Mrs. Gooch’s leases the Property from Ventura Development. (See AUMF Nos. 22, 26.) Ventura Paving is a subcontractor hired in June 2020 by Ventura Development to inter alia, “stripe all handicap parking spaces, arrows, and the hash only designated to the handicap spaces, according to plans.” (UMF No. 6.) In or about early September 2020, Ventura Development also hired Ventura Paving to install wheel stops in the handicap designated parking spaces. (See UMF No. 7.) In September 2020, Ventura Paving provided, installed and painted the wheel stops at the handicap designated parking spaces in the parking lot at the Property. (See UMF No. 8.) Ventura Paving was not notified of any problem with the wheel stop at issue until served with the instant lawsuit on February 29, 2024. (See UMF No. 11.)

C.             Ventura Paving is Entitled to Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action for Premises Liability.

Plaintiff levies two claims against Ventura Paving: general negligence and premises liability. (See Complaint.) Ventura Paving moves for summary judgment as to both causes of action or, alternatively, for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s premises liability claim only.

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.” (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.) “Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1714(a).) “The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises; accordingly, mere possession with its attendant right to control conditions on the premises is a sufficient basis for the imposition of an affirmative duty to act. But the duty arising from possession and control of property is adherence to the same standard of care that applies in negligence cases.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158 (cleaned up).) 

1.               Summary Judgment – Completed and Accepted Doctrine

Ventura Paving argues it has no duty to Plaintiff under either cause of action and is thus entitled to summary judgment. (See Motion, pp. 3-6.) Ventura argues it “owed no duty to Plaintiff because the law is clear that when a contractor's work has been completed and accepted by the owner, the contractor is not liable to third persons for injury suffered by reason of a patent defect caused by the contractor's work. (Boswell v. Laird (1857) 8 Cal. 469; Sanchez v. Swinerton & Walberg Co. (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1461.).” (Motion, pg. 3.) In support of its argument, Ventura Paving submits evidence (1) it was a subcontractor hired by Ventura Development to perform painting and striping of the parking lot and to install wheel stops at the Property; (2) it completed the work it was hired to perform no later than October 31, 2020; (3) the completed work was accepted; and (4) Ventura Paving was not notified of any issues with the parking block until it was served with the instant lawsuit on February 29, 2024. (See Motion, pg. 4, UMF Nos. 6-9.)

The Court finds Ventura Paving has not met its burden of proof under the completed and accepted doctrine and thus has not met his burden in negating the element of duty underlying Plaintiff’s claims. “[W]hen a contractor completes work that is accepted by the owner, the contractor is not liable to third parties injured as a result of the condition of the work, even if the contractor was negligent in performing the contract, unless the defect in the work was latent or concealed. The rationale for this doctrine is that an owner has a duty to inspect the work and ascertain its safety, and thus the owner’s acceptance of the work shifts liability for its safety to the owner, provided that a reasonable inspection would disclose the defect. Stated another way, when the owner has accepted a structure from the contractor, the owner’s failure to attempt to remedy an obviously dangerous defect is an intervening cause for which the contractor is not liable. The doctrine applies to patent defects, but not latent defects. If an owner, fulfilling the duty of inspection, cannot discover the defect, then the owner cannot effectively represent to the world that the construction is sufficient; he lacks adequate information to do so.” (Neiman v. Leo A. Daly Co. (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 962, 969 (“Neiman”)(cleaned up).)

Ventura Paving has presented no evidence the parking block was a patent defect that Ventura Development had awareness of, or should have had awareness of upon reasonable inspection, prior to the incident. Ventura Paving’s only evidence in support of its UMF No. 10, which states “Ventura Paving Management Inc.’s work at 14311 Ventura Blvd., Sherman Oaks, CA was completed and accepted by 14311 Ventura Development LLC, no later than October 31, 2020, the day the store opened,” is the declaration of its CEO Benjamin Lopez (“Lopez”), whereby Lopez merely restates the contents of UMF No. 10. (UMF No. 10, Declaration of Benjamin Lopez filed July 30, 2024, ¶ 7.)

The Court of Appeal’s reasoning in Neiman is instructive here. In Neiman, the lack of stripes on a set of stairs was deemed a patent defect because “[i]t is undisputed [appellee’s] plans and specifications called for contrast marking stripes to be placed on the stairs at the Main Stage.” (Neiman, 210 Cal.App.4th at 969.) As such, “[t]he alleged defect is patent as a matter of law; it would be discovered by the inspection on owner would make in the exercise of ordinary care and prudence in ensuring that obvious safety measures called for in the plans and specifications were completed.” (Neiman, 210 Cal.App.4th at 970 (cleaned up).) Here, however, Ventura Paving has presented no evidence that the parking block at issue was supposed to be painted a certain color or be placed in a certain position or that Ventura Development knew or should have known the parking block at issue was supposed to be painted a certain color or be placed in a certain position.

Nor has Ventura Paving presented evidence the parking block presented an obvious and apparent danger. (See Sanchez v. Swinerton & Walberg Co. (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1461, 1470–1471 (“Sanchez”) (emphasis added).) In Sanchez, discussed by Ventura Paving in support of its argument, the Court of Appeal found standing water on a stairwell “obvious and apparent to any reasonably observant person, as would the danger the water might create slippery surfaces and cause one to slip and fall. In the context of a patent defect, the word ‘patent’ refers to the patency of danger and not merely to exterior visibility. Here, the danger itself was patent as a matter of law; it would be discovered by the inspection an owner would make in the exercise of ordinary care and prudence for, as a matter of ordinary prudence, one would test whether water accumulated on the uncovered landings of stairways into buildings. The obvious nature of the defect would allow the owner and users to take steps to remedy the condition or to take precautions against injury.” (Id. (cleaned up) (emphasis added).)

Here, the only evidence Ventura Paving presents that comes close to addressing the nature of the parking block (whether patent or latent) is its evidence in support of UMF No. 14, which states, “Plaintiff has produced photographs which depict the blue wheel stop in the same condition as it was when Plaintiff tripped and show that the wheel stop was open, obvious and visible.” (UMF No. 14.) The evidence in support of UMF No. 14, however, consists of photos of the parking block and a portion of Plaintiff’s deposition testimony discussing the photos. (See id.) At most, these documents support an argument that the parking block was “exteriorly visible.” (Sanchez, 47 Cal.App.4th at 1470.) They do not, however, establish that the parking block presented an obvious and apparent danger. (See id.)

In sum, the Court not persuaded by Ventura Development’s evidence that the parking block is a patent defect and that Ventura Paving is thereby absolved of any potential liability to Plaintiff by virtue of the completed and accepted doctrine. Accordingly, the Court DENIES Ventura Paving’s Motion for Summary Judgment on the grounds of the completed and accepted doctrine.  

2.               Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action for Premises Liability Cause of Action is Appropriate.

 “The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises; accordingly, mere possession with its attendant right to control conditions on the premises is a sufficient basis for the imposition of an affirmative duty to act. But the duty arising from possession and control of property is adherence to the same standard of care that applies in negligence cases.” (Kesner, 1 Cal.5th at 1158 (cleaned up).) 

Ventura Paving argues the parking block constituted an open and obvious harm such that Plaintiff cannot establish that Ventura Paving breached a duty to Plaintiff. (See Motion, pp. 4-6.)[2] Ventura Paving also argues that since it did not own, lease, occupy, or control the Property and thus cannot be held liable to Plaintiff under a theory of premises liability. (See id. at 6-8.) In support of its arguments, Ventura Paving submits evidence that (1) Plaintiff wasn’t paying attention when she tripped on the parking block; (2) there was no car parked in the handicap designated parking area when Plaintiff tripped; (3) the parking block was an open an obvious condition; and (4) Ventura Paving did not own, lease, occupy or control the Property. (See UMF Nos. 5, 12-14.)

The Court finds Ventura Paving has submitted evidence sufficient to meet its burden on summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for premises liability. Ventura Paving has submitted evidence that it does not own, lease, occupy or control the Property. (See UMF No. 5.) Ventura Paving has submitted evidence that it was a subcontractor hired to perform the work of striping all handicap parking spaces and install the parking blocks in said spaces, but that its work was completed in October of 2020. (See UMF No. 6-7, 10.) Ventura Paving has thus submitted admissible evidence that it does not own, possess, or control the Property and the burden shifts to Plaintiff to show a triable issue of material fact.[3]  

Plaintiff has not shown there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether Ventura Paving owns, leases, occupies, or controls the property. To the contrary, Plaintiff does not dispute that Ventura Paving does not own, lease, occupy, or control the Property; nor does Plaintiff dispute that Ventura Paving was hired to perform work that it ostensibly completed. (See UMF Nos. 5, 6-7, 10.) In fact, Plaintiff submits evidence that supports Ventura Paving’s position, as she submits evidence that Ventura Development owns the Property and Mrs. Gooch’s leases the Property from Ventura Development. (See AUMF Nos. 22, 26.)

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Ventura Paving’s Motion as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for premises liability only.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Ventura Paving’s Motion for Summary Judgment on the grounds of the completed and accepted doctrine but GRANTS Ventura Paving’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for premises liability.

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case arises out of a multi-motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs Marisela Garza (“Garza”) and Irene Carreto Sandoval (“Sandoval”)(collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege they were walking on the side of the street when Defendant Briana Martinez (“Martinez”) negligently drove a vehicle (“Car 1”) through a red light and collided with Defendant Brian Baker’s (“Baker”) vehicle. III.         LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 877.6(a)(1)[1], provides that, on noticed motion, “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors.” “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 877.6(c).) Although determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.¿(Id.)¿

If a motion is opposed, the moving party must show by competent evidence all Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488 (“Tech-Bilt”) factors. (See Mediplex of Cal. v. Superior Court (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 748, 753, fn.4; Greshko v. Los Angeles (1987) 194 Cal.App.3d 822.) The Tech-Bilt factors are: (1) A rough approximation of plaintiffs' total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability; (2) The amount paid in settlement; (3) A recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than if found liable after a trial; (4) The allocation of the settlement proceeds among plaintiffs; (5) The settlor’s financial condition and insurance policy limits, if any; and (6) Evidence of any collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct between the settlor and the plaintiffs aimed at making the nonsettling parties pay more than their fair share. (See Tech-Bilt, 38 Cal.3d at 499.)

Parties challenging the good faith nature of a settlement agreement have the burden to demonstrate that it lies so far “out of the ballpark” of the Tech-Bilt factors that it is inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute. (Nutrition Now, Inc. v. Superior Court (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 209, 213-214.)

Where a good-faith settlement is unopposed, counsel’s bare-bones declaration is sufficient for a good-faith determination. (See City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.)

IV.          ANALYSIS

Montano and Ramos’ (“Settling Defendants”) uncontested Motion sets forth the background of the case and the grounds for good faith settlement via the declaration of their attorney, Christopher Nalbantian (“Nalbantian”). (See Declaration of Christopher Nalbantian filed September 13, 2024 (“Nalbantian Decl.”).) Because the Motion is unopposed, the Court need not consider and weigh the Tech-Bilt factors.

Nalbantian declares the $30,000.00 settlement agreement accounts for the $15,000.00 per person maximum policy limits of Settling Defendants’ automobile liability insurance policy with Allstate Northbrook Indemnity Company. (See Nalbantian Decl., ¶¶ 11-12, Ex. G.) Nalbantian declares this settlement was made in good faith and “is within the ballpark of their proportionate liability based on the potential liability of the other parties” as Settling Defendants “were 0% liable for the collision.” (Id. at ¶ 13.) Nalbantian declares Martinez “was 100% liable for Plaintiffs’ damages as she ran a red light which caused the collision with…[Baker]…who then hit…[Montano’s]…vehicle, who was at a complete stop at a red traffic light and pushed him into the vehicle that was parked on Mason Avenue.” (Id. at ¶ 10.) Nalbantian further declares most of Sandoval’s medical bills were covered by Medi-Cal, Garza’s medical bills were $6,096.11, and neither Plaintiff is claiming loss of income or earning capacity. (See id. at ¶¶ 6-9, Exs. A-C, E, F.) Finally, Nalbantian declares Settling Defendants’ policy limits are sufficient to cover and exceed their proportionate share of Plaintiffs’ damages, that the parties have agreed to bear their own attorney’s fees and costs, and that the settlement agreement was reached following an arm’s length negotiation through insurance claims representatives. (See id. at ¶¶ 15, 17, 20.)

Accordingly, Settling Defendants have adequately established the settlement was entered into in good faith and their Motion is GRANTED.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Settling Defendants’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Motion To Quash

Code of Civil Procedure § 418.10(a)(1)[1] provides, “[a] defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion… [t]o quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” A motion to quash must be granted if the court finds that either (1) there is no basis for exercising personal jurisdiction over the defendant or (2) service on the defendant was improper. (See Ziller Elecs. Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1229.)  

“When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process the burden is on the plaintiff to prove . . . the facts requisite to an effective service.”¿(Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413 (internal quotations omitted).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

Defendant moves to quash Plaintiffs’ service of summons. (See Motion.) As an initial matter, Defendant contends Plaintiffs’ written Opposition should not be considered until it is filed and served at least a day before the hearing. (See Motion at 3-4: 18-9.) (See also Rules of Court 3.1327(c).) Plaintiffs filed their Opposition on October 9, 2024, and served said Opposition on Defendant by mail. (See Opposition at 11.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1010.6(a)(1)(D), Plaintiffs properly served Defendant with the Opposition when it was filed and electronically transmitted to the Court. (See id.); (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 1010.6(a)(1)(D).)

Plaintiffs argue Defendant was personally served with the summons and complaint. (See Opposition at 5: 14-15.) To effectuate service on a residence, the “summons may be served by personal delivery of a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete at the time of such delivery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 415.10.)

Additionally, if, by reasonable diligence, the copy of the summons and complaint cannot be personally delivered to the person to be served, a copy of the summons and complaint can be left at the person’s dwelling house or usual place of abode, business, or mailing address “in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address… at least 18 years, of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and of the complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 415.20; see also American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 389 (wherein the court reasoned, “Two or three attempts to personally serve a defendant at a proper place ordinarily qualifies as ‘reasonable diligence.’”).)

Code of Civil Procedure § 417.10 requires an affidavit of the person making the service showing time, place, and manner of service and facts, which recites the name of the person receiving the copy of the summons and complaint. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 417.10.) Additionally, “[a] summons in an action for unlawful detainer of real property may be served by posting if upon affidavit it appears to the satisfaction of the court in which the action is pending that the party to be served cannot with reasonable diligence be served in any manner specified in this article other than publication and that: (1) A cause of action exists against the party upon whom service is to be made or he is a necessary or proper party to the action; or (2) The party to be served has or claims an interest in real property in this state that is subject to the jurisdiction of the court or the relief demanded in the action consists wholly or in part in excluding such party from any interest in such property.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 415.45.)

Plaintiffs bear the burden of proving the service complied with the law. (See Summers v. MacClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.) Here, the Court finds they have done so. On September 20, 2024, a registered California process server, Danny J. Heinz (“Mr. Heinz”), waited at the alleged home of Justin Massion’s father located at 24686 Cordillera Drive, Calabasas, California 91302 (“Cordillera Property”). (See Opposition at 2: 10-21; see also Ex. 1 at 6-7.) At 1:00 p.m., Mr. Heinz approached a tall man in his 30s with a ponytail who drove a white Corvette, who had just arrived at the Cordillera Property and was the same man Mr. Heinz saw and attempted to speak to when serving a Three-Day Notice to Quit on August 30, 2024, at the Subject Property. (See Opposition at 2: 17-25.) Mr. Heinz had previously learned Defendant is a tall man in his 30s who sometimes puts his hair into a ponytail and drives a white Corvette. (See id.: 8-9.) Mr. Heinz identified himself as a process server to Defendant. (See id. at 2-3: 25-1.) He informed him he was being served with a summons and complaint, along with court documents for himself and unknown occupants of the Subject Property, but Defendant refused to take the documents as he walked towards the Cordillera Property door. (See id.) Mr. Heinz then notified Defendant he was served while walking after him and dropped the documents on the porch while Defendant was on the porch. (See id. at 3:1-5; see also Ex. 2 at 8-10.) Mr. Heinz also stated he had attempted to serve Defendant on multiple occasions between September 12, 2024, and September 20, 2024, driving by the Subject Property “many times” and the Cordillera Property seven times, and knocking on the Cordillera Property door at least three times when he saw the white Corvette in the driveway, but no one answered. (See id., at 10-16.) Thus, Plaintiffs convincingly argue Defendant was served because Mr. Heinz also recognized him from previously posting the Three Day Notice to Quit

Additionally, the affidavit of service from Mr. Heinz creates a rebuttable presumption of proper service under Evidence Code § 647 because Mr. Heinz is a registered California process server. (See Evid. Code § 647; see also Floveyor Internat., Ltd. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 795.) The Proof of Personal Service, filed on October 9, 2024, and Mr. Heinz's declaration within the Opposition both attest to and comply with Code Civ. Proc. § 415.10 because a copy of the summons and complaint were placed on the porch where Defendant was after Defendant refused to accept the documents, and Mr. Heinz identified himself as a process server informing Defendant he was served. (See Proof of Personal Service; see Opposition at 2-3:22-5.) The signed declaration of Mr. Heinz complied with Code of Civil Procedure section 417.10 as it included the showing time being 1:00 p.m., the place being the Subject and Cordillera Properties, and manner of service and facts reciting Defendant’s name receiving the copy of the summons and complaint as it was dropped where the Defendant was after he refused to accept the documents. (See id. at 2-3: 3-8.)

Defendant, who filed his Motion before Plaintiffs filed their Proof of Personal Service, claims Plaintiffs “only” posted the summons and complaint amounting to a failure to perform service and cites actual notice as an insufficient substitute for service. (See Motion at 3: 2-4, 10-16.) He has not filed a Reply. As a result, Defendant has not provided sufficient evidence to rebut the presumption created by Evid. Code § 647. Therefore, Plaintiffs have met their burden in proving personal service was effectuated, establishing jurisdiction for purposes of the present Motion.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion to Quash. Defendant has 10 days to file a responsive pleading pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10(c).
