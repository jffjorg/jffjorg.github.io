DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV01260    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: P

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint

Moving Party: Plaintiff Alexandria Covarrubias

Responding Party: None

RULING

The Court considered the moving papers. Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On April 21, 2023, Plaintiff Alexandria Covarrubias (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Island Pro Painting, Inc.; Tofiga Pro Painting, Inc.; Spartan Education d/b/a Spartan College of Aeronautics & Technology; City of Inglewood; and DOES 1-50, inclusive, alleging

causes of action for (1) Dangerous Condition of Public Property; (2) Negligence; (3) Negligence: Premises Liability; and (4) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, or Retention of Employee.

On December 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed amendments to the complaint, to add Rexford Industrial Realty (“Rexford”) and HC 8911 Aviation, LLC as Doe Defendants.

On March 6, 2024, Rexford filed a demurrer to the complaint. Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) on April 25, 2024. The FAC included the same causes of action as the original complaint.

On June 24, 2024, Rexford filed a demurrer to the FAC. On July 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On September 9, 2024, the demurrer was sustained with leave to amend.

On September 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion. As of October 16, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On July 5, 2022, Plaintiff slipped and fell on a wet floor in Spartan College. (FAC, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges that the floor was wet because Defendants Island Pro Painting and Tofiga Pro Painting, in the course of their work, created a puddle of water and failed to clean it up. (FAC, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff was unaware of the puddle because there were no warning signs. (FAC, ¶ 14.)

LEGAL STANDARD

Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure sections 473, subd. (a), and 576. The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified. [Citation.]” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422.) Notwithstanding the “policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party...’ [citation]. A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).)

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Argument

Plaintiff argues that Defendants will not be prejudiced because she seeks only to remove two causes of action, amend fictious names, and clarify existing allegations. Thus, there will only be minimal delays in the proceedings.

Merits of the Motion

Plaintiff seeks to amend the FAC to remove the first cause of action for Dangerous Condition of Public Property; remove the third cause of action for Negligence: Premises Liability; identify Doe Defendants; and clarify existing allegations.

Plaintiff’s motion substantially complies with the requirements of Rule 3.1324. The motion states that this amendment is necessary to (1) comply with a prior court ruling; (2) allow Plaintiff to clarify her causes of action; and (3) properly present her case. Plaintiff provides a copy of the proposed Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) and identifies the differences between the SAC and the FAC.

Plaintiff argues that Defendants will not be prejudiced by the filing of the SAC because they have less causes of action to defend against, and existing allegations are reorganized for clarity but not substantively changed. Further, any delay that will occur is necessary, as one defendant appeared in the action only recently.

Given the minimal changes to the FAC and the lack of opposition from any Defendant, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a second amended complaint.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED.

Plaintiff is ordered to separately file the SAC within 5 days and to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 24TRCV01789    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

Moving Party: Defendant Enterprise Rent-a-Car Company of Sacramento, LLC

Responding Party: Plaintiff Merclyn Georgia Brown

RULING

The Court considered the moving papers and opposition. Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff Merclyn Brown (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Enterprise Rent-a-Car Company of Sacramento, LLC (“Defendant”); Artem Khakhaev (“Khakhaev”); and DOES 1-50, inclusive alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence; (2) Negligence per se; and (3) Negligent Entrustment.

On September 13, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint and a request for judicial notice. Plaintiff filed an opposition on October 10, 2024.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On June 6, 2022, Plaintiff and Khakhaev were involved in a motor vehicle collision. (Complaint, ¶ 1.) Plaintiff alleges that the collision occurred due to Khakhaev making an unsafe right turn. (Complaint, ¶ 8.)

Khakhaev was driving a Freightliner commercial vehicle, which Plaintiff alleges was owned by Defendant. (Complaint, ¶¶ 7, 27.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant knew or should have known that Khakhaev was a reckless driver but allowed him to drive the Freightliner anyway. (Complaint, ¶ 27.)

LEGAL STANDARD

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.)

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. §

436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

DISCUSSION

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of a Statement of Information filed with the Secretary of State, which indicates that Defendant is in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles.

The court may take notice of official acts of the State of California, pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (c). In addition, the court may take judicial notice of the fact that Enterprise Rent-a-Car is engaged in the business of renting vehicles as it is not reasonably subject to dispute and is capable of immediate and accurate determination. (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (h).)

Thus, the court takes judicial notice of the Statement of Information and the facts contained within.

Moving Party’s Argument

Defendant contends that Plaintiff only makes conclusory and uncertain allegations. Further, a rental car company is not vicariously liable for the negligent acts of its customers under federal law.

Opposing Party’s Argument

Plaintiff argues that she has pleaded sufficient facts to maintain all causes of action. Further, the demurrer is premature as there are factual disputes that need to be resolved during discovery. Primarily, Plaintiff seeks information about whether Khakhaev had the proper certification or license to operate the Freightliner.

Merits of the Motion

A. Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)

Defense counsel declares that he sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel and followed up with a telephone call. However, the parties were unable to reach an agreement.

Accordingly, the court finds that the parties have satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

B. Whether the Claims are Barred by 49 U.S.C. section 30106, subd. (a) (the” Graves

Amendment”)

Defendant demurs to the complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff has not stated a cause of action against it, as all claims of negligence are barred by the Graves Amendment.

The Graves Amendment states:

“(a) In general. An owner of a motor vehicle that rents or leases the vehicle to a person (or an affiliate of the owner) shall not be liable under the law of any State or political subdivision thereof, by reason of being the owner of the vehicle (or an affiliate of the owner), for harm to persons or property that results or arises out of the use, operation, or possession of the vehicle during the period of the rental or lease, if--

(1) the owner (or an affiliate of the owner) is engaged in the trade or business of renting or leasing motor vehicles; and

(2) there is no negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner (or an affiliate of the owner).”

Defendant claims it is a car rental business as proven by the Statement of Information. While the court takes judicial notice of the facts within the Statement of Information because they are not reasonably the subject of dispute, the Graves Amendment does not bar the allegations of this complaint. Federal law only protects such businesses from the independent negligent acts of its customers. Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant was negligent because it allowed an incompetent, “careless and reckless driver” to operate a Freightliner it owned.

Thus, the Graves Amendment does not bar Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant.

C. Sufficiency of the Allegations

Defendant demurs to the complaint on the basis that Plaintiff makes only boilerplate allegations of negligence. Further, Defendant argues that the negligence per se claim must fail because Plaintiff does not allege that Defendant was the one driving the car.

Negligence may be pleaded generally. (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 407-408.) Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant negligently entrusted its vehicle to an incompetent, reckless driver, causing Plaintiff’s injury. These allegations, though bare, are sufficient to survive a demurrer. (See Pultz v. Holgerson (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 1110, 1117 [“The negligence element may be generally pleaded; the breach of duty of care may be alleged by stating the act was negligently done.”])

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED in its entirety.


Case Number: 24TRCV02254    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

Moving Parties: Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc.

Responding Party: None

RULING

Having considered the moving papers, the motion to compel binding arbitration is GRANTED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On July 5, 2024, Plaintiff Gimi George (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Defendant”) and DOES 1-10, inclusive alleging five causes of action for violations of Statutory Obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act Civ. Code sections 1790-1795.8.

On August 16, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion. As of October 16, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On November 21, 2023, Plaintiff purchased a Tesla Model Y (the “Vehicle”) manufactured by Defendant. (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

Plaintiff received an express written warranty which stated that if a defect developed during the warranty period, Plaintiff could deliver the Vehicle to a repair shop to make repairs. (Complaint, ¶ 9.)

During the warranty period, several defects developed. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) However, Defendant failed to repair the Vehicle after several attempts. (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

LEGAL STANDARD

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) California law favors enforcement of valid arbitration agreements. (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 97.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.)

The Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies to any contract evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce which contains an arbitration clause. (Wolls v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) Section 2 of the FAA provides that arbitration provisions shall be enforced, save upon grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Ibid.) A state court may refuse to enforce an arbitration clause on the basis of generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability. (Ibid.) A state court, however, may not defeat an arbitration clause by applying state laws applicable only to arbitration provisions. (Ibid.)

For the FAA to apply, a contract must involve interstate commerce. (Ibid.) When it applies, the FAA preempts any state law rule that stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of the FAA’s objectives. (Carbajal v. CWPSC, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 227, 238.) A party asserting FAA preemption bears the burden to present evidence establishing a contract with the arbitration provision affects interstate commerce, and the failure to do so renders the FAA inapplicable. (Ibid.) Evidence must be presented, in the form of declarations or other evidence, that establishes that the contract affects interstate commerce. (Ibid.) “[S]ince arbitration is a matter of contract, the FAA also applies if it is so stated in the agreement.” (Davis v. Shiekh Shoes, LLC (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 956, 963.)

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) Thus, in assessing a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the [motion] bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284, citation omitted.)

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

DISCUSSION

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of the complaint filed by Plaintiff. The court may take notice of its own file, pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (d).

Defendant’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

Moving Party’s Argument

Defendant argues that Plaintiff consented to the terms of an online Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“Order Agreement”) and a Retail Installment Sales Contract (“Sale Contract”), both of which including arbitration provisions. Both the FAA and California law require Plaintiff to arbitrate this dispute.

Merits of the Motion

A. Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

In support of the motion, Defendant submits the Declaration of Raymond Kim (“Kim”). Kim declares that when Plaintiff purchased the Vehicle, he signed two agreements containing arbitration provisions. (Kim Decl., Exh. 1 and 2.) The Order Agreement contains an arbitration provision on page 3, which provides Plaintiff with the opportunity to opt out, but Plaintiff did not exercise that option. (Kim Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff also signed the Sale Contract, which has an arbitration on page 5. (Kim Decl., ¶¶ 8, 12.)

The Order Agreement’s Arbitration provision provides that the parties agree that if a dispute arises between the parties and it is not resolved within 60 days, the parties agree to an arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association. Any fees incurred are to be paid by Tesla Motors, Inc. (Kim Decl., ¶ 6.) The Sale Contract’s arbitration provision provides that any dispute arising from the sale and purchase of the Vehicle; Plaintiff’s credit application; or the condition of the Vehicle is to be resolved in binding arbitration. (Kim Decl., ¶ 12.) The provision also states that the arbitration agreement is governed by the FAA.

The court notes that the Order Agreement only shows that Plaintiff was the customer and does not contain his signature. The Sale Contract, however, does show that it was e-signed by the Plaintiff. The court cannot bind the Plaintiff to an arbitration provision that he did not sign. Accordingly, the court will determine whether the parties have entered a binding arbitration agreement under the Sale Contract alone.

The court finds that with the Sale Contract, Defendant has met its burden of proving that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties. Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish any facts necessary to show that the agreement should not be enforced.

B. Grounds for Compelling Arbitration

The court finds that the language of the Sale Contract’s arbitration provision covers this dispute, as it arises from the condition of the vehicle. Given the instant motion is unopposed, Plaintiff has not established by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to his defense.

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration.

B. Stay of Proceedings

Code Civ. Proc. section 1281.4 provides that if the court has ordered the arbitration of a controversy, it “shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” Pursuant to Section 1281.4, therefore, the Court stays this action pending conclusion of the arbitration proceedings.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration is GRANTED. The court STAYS this action pending conclusion of the arbitration process.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
