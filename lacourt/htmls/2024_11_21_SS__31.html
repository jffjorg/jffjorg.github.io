DEPARTMENT 31 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV24940    Hearing Date: November 21, 2024    Dept: 31

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Kerry Bensinger, Department 31

 

 

HEARING DATE:     November 21, 2024                           TRIAL DATE:  March 10, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Gus Murra v. LAPRAAC, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV24940

 

 

MOTION FOR NEW TRIAL AFTER SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTIES:           Plaintiffs Gus Murra, Jacob Palacios, Victor Brown, Jerry D. Fritz, Louie Gener

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant City of Los Angeles

 

 

I.          FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

This is a consolidated action involving allegations that the City of Los Angles (City) and the Los Angeles Police Department (LAPD) by and through their actual and/or ostensible agent, Los Angeles Police Revolver and Athletic Club (LAPRAAC), negligently purchased and sold firearms and negligently supervised LAPRAAC’s employees who purchased and sold firearms. Plaintiffs Gus Murra (Murra), Jacob Palacios (Palacios), Victor Brown (Brown), Jerry D. Fritz (Fritz), and Louie Gener (Gener) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), were sworn officers of the Los Angeles Police Department (LAPD).  LAPRAAC is a private entity that sells firearms and police equipment, among other items.  LAPRAAC operates a gun store at the LAPD Police Academy campus located at 1880 North Academy Drive, Los Angeles, CA 90012.  LAPRAAC is a federally licensed firearms dealer.  The City and LAPD are not.

 

Between 2018 and 2020, each Plaintiff separately purchased firearms from LAPRAAC in private sales transactions.  During that same time period, the manager of LAPRAAC’s gun store was allegedly trafficking in stolen weapons and/or stealing guns from LAPRAAC’s inventory and selling them on the side.  In 2020, LAPD’s Commercial Crimes Division, Burglary Section (CCD) contacted each Plaintiff regarding their respective firearms purchases from LAPRAAC.  CCD informed each Plaintiff the firearm they purchased from LAPRAAC had been reported stolen.  Criminal investigations ensued. 

 

During the pendency of the criminal investigation, Murra, Palacios, and Gener were indefinitely stripped of their LAPD badges and placed on residential assignment; Brown was reassigned to another department with work restrictions. 

 

Plaintiffs allege LAPRAAC acted negligently in its oversight of its firearms sales. As relevant here, Plaintiffs allege LAPRAAC was the actual and/or ostensible agent of the City and the LAPD.[1]  And if so, the City is liable for the negligent acts of its agent (actual or ostensible) and its employees.  Plaintiffs further allege having suffered economic damages, emotional distress, and reputational harm.

 

            Plaintiffs’ Complaints

 

On July 7, 2021, Murra filed a Complaint against LAPRAAC, the City, and LAPD.  Murra’s Complaint is the lead case.  On August 16, 2021, Brown, Fritz, and Gener each filed a Complaint against Defendants.  Murra, Brown, Fritz, and Gener’s respective complaints each allege three causes of action: (1) Negligence, (2) Negligence (Gov. Code §§ 815.2 and 820), and (3) Negligent Supervision, Hiring, or Retention (Gov. Code §§ 815.2 and 820).  Plaintiffs’ First Cause of Action for Negligence is asserted against LAPRAAC. As relevant here, Plaintiffs’ Second and Third Causes of Action are asserted against the City and LAPD. 

 

Palacios filed his Complaint against Defendants on August 16, 2021.  Palacios’s First Amended Complaint (FAC), filed on September 8, 2022, is his operative pleading.  The FAC asserts the same first three causes of action and adds a fourth cause of action against the City for Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code § 1102.5.

 

Motions for Summary Judgment

 

The City filed five motions: four motions for summary judgment against Murra, Brown, Fritz and Gener, and a fifth motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication against Palacios’s FAC.  Palacios asserted a retaliation claim.  With the exception of Palacios’s retaliation claim, Plaintiffs bring identical negligence and negligent supervision, hiring, or retention claims against the Defendants.  Because the negligence claims all turned on the issue of whether LAPRAAC was the agent or ostensible agent of the City or LAPD with respect to LAPRAAC’s firearms sale and transactions, the court addressed the arguments together.

 

On August 19, 2024, the court granted each of the City’s motions for summary judgment.

 

The Instant Motion

 

On October 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this Motion for New Trial After Summary Judgment. 

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

            A motion for new trial asks the trial court to reexamine one or more issues of fact or law after a trial and decision by judge or jury.¿ (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 656, 657.)¿ A new trial motion is also available to challenge judgments disposing of an action without trial where an issue of fact or law has been decided, such as in the case of summary judgment.¿ (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 858.)  “The right to a new trial is purely statutory, and a motion for a new trial can be granted only on one of the grounds enumerated in the statute.”  (Fomco, Inc. v. Joe Maggio, Inc. (1961) 55 Cal. 2d 162, 166.)  “As the motion for a new trial finds both its source and its limitations in the statutes, the procedural steps prescribed by law for making and determining such a motion are mandatory and must be strictly followed.”  (Mercer v. Perez (1968) 68 Cal.2d 104, 118 [cleaned up]; Pacific Trends Lamp & Lighting Products, Inc. v. J. White Inc. (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 1131, 1135.) 

 

As set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 657, the grounds upon which a new trial may be ordered include: 

 

1.      Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial. 

2.      Misconduct of the jury; and whenever any one or more of the jurors have been induced to assent to any general or special verdict, or to a finding on any question submitted to them by the court, by a resort to the determination of chance, such misconduct may be proved by the affidavit of any one of the jurors. 

3.      Accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against. 

4.      Newly discovered evidence, material for the party making the application, which he could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered and produced at the trial. 

5.      Excessive or inadequate damages. 

6.      Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law. 

7.      Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 657.) 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiffs argue a new trial is warranted for two reasons: (1) the court improperly shifted the burden to Plaintiffs of proving an ostensible agency relationship between LAPRAAC and the City, and (2) Plaintiffs offered evidence to create a triable issue of fact as to the existence of an ostensible agency.  In essence, Plaintiffs move for a new trial based on irregularity in the proceedings.[2]  “A new trial may be granted where there is an ‘[i]rregularity in the proceedings.’ (§ 657, subd. (1).) An ‘irregularity in the proceedings’ is a catchall phrase referring to any act that (1) violates the right of a party to a fair trial and (2) which a party cannot fully present by exceptions taken during the progress of the trial, and which must therefore appear by affidavits.
(Montoya v. Barragan (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1229–30, citations omitted.). For the reasons stated herein, Plaintiffs fail to show a new trial is warranted.3  

 

A.  Whether the City Held LAPRAAC as its Ostensible Agent

 

To establish ostensible agency, a plaintiff must show (1) that the defendant intentionally or carelessly created the impression of agency; (2) that plaintiff reasonably believed such agency; and (3) that plaintiff was harmed because he or she reasonably relied on the belief.  (CACI No. 3709; Civ. Code § 2300; Associated Creditors’ Agency v. Davis (1975) 13 Cal.3d 374, 399 [“There are three requirements necessary before recovery may be had against a principal for the act of an ostensible agent. The person dealing with the agent must do so with belief in the agent's authority and this belief must be a reasonable one; such belief must be generated by some act or neglect of the principal sought to be charged; and the third person in relying on the agent’s apparent authority must not be guilty of negligence.”].)   “Ostensible agency cannot be established by the representations or conduct of the purported agent; the statements or acts of the principal must be such as to cause the belief the agency exists.”  (American Way Cellular, Inc. v. Travelers Property Casualty Co. of America (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 1040, 1053.)

 

In ruling in the City’s favor, the court narrowed the issue to whether the City or the LAPD presented LAPRAAC as its ostensible agent for the sale of firearms.  After reviewing the briefing and holding two hearings, the court concluded LAPRAAC was not the City’s nor the LAPD’s ostensible agent for this narrow purpose.  Moreover, the court concluded that Plaintiffs’ belief in LAPRAAC’s ostensible authority was not reasonable as a matter of law.

 

Now, Plaintiffs argue “the City did not meet its initial burden of negating Plaintiffs’ claims that LAPRAAC was the ostensible agent of defendant City.”  (Mot. pg. 1.)  Not so.  The City offered evidence to establish the following: “LAPRAAC is a separate entity (City’s Undisputed Material Facts (UMF) 22, 26); LAPRAAC’s gun store is located on LAPRAAC property, and is wholly owned, operated and staffed by LAPRAAC (UMF 13, 15); with respect to the LAPRAAC gun store which is the subject of this lawsuit, including the sale of guns, LAPRAAC is not an agent of the City (UMF 18); LAPRAAC is not authorized by the City to contract for the City, nor is it authorized to do any acts for the City with respect to the gun store (UMF 24, 25); the City is not involved in LAPRAAC’s operations in any way (UMF 26); and each Plaintiff admitted in responses to the City’s Request for Admission that there are no documents pertaining to the purchase of the subject firearms which identifies the City as the seller. (UMF 23 (Murra); UMF 23 (Brown); UMF 40 (Palacios); UMF 40 (Fritz); UMF 33 (Gener).)  In short, the City showed LAPRAAC and the City are separate entities, the City plays no role in LAPRAAC’s operations, and LAPRAAC did not have authority to act as the City’s agent, ostensible or otherwise.”  (Minute Order, 8/19/24.)  

 

The City met its initial burden to show no triable issue of material fact existed.  And once the burden shifted, Plaintiffs failed to make a prima facie showing there was triable issue on the existence of an ostensible agency.  Plaintiffs did not point to any statements made by the City or the LAPD to create the impression that LAPRAAC was authorized to sell firearms on the LAPD’s behalf.  Similarly, no evidence was submitted to show the City or the LAPD were authorized to sell firearms.  Given the foregoing, any belief in an ostensible agency for the sale of firearms was unreasonable.  The court arrived at this conclusion after the parties were afforded an opportunity to submit additional briefing and the benefit of two hearings.  Plaintiffs did not submit any evidence to direct a different result. 

 

In sum, the court did not improperly shift the burden to Plaintiffs.  And, as before, Plaintiffs do not offer any new arguments (and certainly no new evidence) to demonstrate the existence of a triable issue.[3]  

 

IV.       CONCLUSION

 

            The Motion for a New Trial is DENIED.

CASE:                         Lorraine Flores v. Casey’s Cars, Inc., et al.

 

ASE NO.:                    24STCV13371

 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL BINDING ARBITRATION AND STAY ALL PROCEEDINGS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Casey’s Cars, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Lorraine Flores

 

 

I.          FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

            On May 29, 2024, Lorraine Flores (Flores or Plaintiff) filed this action against Casey’s Cars, Inc. (Defendant), among others, for (1) Violations of the Consumers Legal Remedies Act, (2) Intentional Misrepresentation, (3) Concealment, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, and (5) Violations of the Unfair Competition Law.  Flores alleges she purchased a used 2020 Land Rover Range Rover (the Vehicle) from Defendant based on Defendant’s representations that there were no issues with the Vehicle.  The Vehicle came with an extended warranty.  However, shortly after the purchase, the Vehicle presented with issues which needed $4,509.19 in repairs.  Defendant declined to help Flores with the repairs, claiming the issues were not covered by extended warranty. 

 

            On September 24, 2024, Defendant filed this motion to compel arbitration and stay all proceedings.

 

            On November 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. 

 

On November 15, Defendant filed a reply.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability.  (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72.)  Under both the FAA and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract.  (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)  Arbitration agreements can be invalidated by “generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability.”  (AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333, 339.)

 

“California law strongly favors arbitration.  Through the comprehensive provisions of the California Arbitration Act (Code Civ. Proc., § 1280 et seq.), the Legislature has expressed a ‘strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a speedy and relatively inexpensive means of dispute resolution.  As with the FAA (9 U.S.C. § 1 et seq.), California law establishes a presumption in favor of arbitrability.  An agreement to submit disputes to arbitration “is valid, enforceable and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 125 (OTO).)

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate the agreement should not be enforced, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue.  (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, the court can compel parties to an arbitration agreement to arbitrate their dispute.  

 

III.      DISCUSSION

 

            There is no dispute Plaintiff signed an agreement for purchase of the Vehicle which contains an arbitration clause.  There is also no dispute the Federal Arbitration Act governs the arbitration clause.  Plaintiff argues several terms in the agreement are unconscionable.  Because the court finds there is a valid delegation clause, the court will compel Plaintiff’s claims to arbitration.  

 

A.    The Delegation Clause

 

“Although threshold questions of¿arbitrability¿are ordinarily for courts to decide in the first instance under the [Federal Arbitration Act (FAA)], the ‘[p]arties to an¿arbitration agreement¿may agree to¿delegate¿to the arbitrator, instead of a court, questions regarding the enforceability of the agreement.’”  (Pinela v. Neiman Marcus Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 227, 239 (Pinela) quoting¿Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 241.)  “Just as the arbitrability of the merits of a dispute depends upon whether the parties agreed to arbitrate that dispute, … so the question who has the primary power to decide arbitrability turns upon what the parties agreed about that matter.”  (Aanderud v. Superior Court (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 880, 891 (Aanderud).)  The delegation issue is a “gateway” question.  (Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson (2010) 561 U.S. 63, 69 (Rent-A-Car).)  

 

“There are two prerequisites for a delegation clause to be effective.  First, the language of the clause must be clear and unmistakable. Second, the delegation must not be revocable under state contract defenses such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability.  The ‘clear and unmistakable’ test reflects a ‘heightened standard of proof’ that reverses the typical presumption in favor of the arbitration disputes.”  (Aanderud, supra, 13¿Cal.App.5th at p. 892 (cleaned up).)  Thus, “[u]nless the parties clearly and unmistakably provide otherwise, the question of whether the parties agreed to arbitrate is to be decided by the court, not the arbitrator.’”  (Pinela, supra, at pp. 239-40 (cleaned up).) 

 

Here, Plaintiff clearly and unmistakably agreed to commit questions of arbitrability to the arbitrator.  The Agreement states, in relevant part, “Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, any allegation of waiver of rights under this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute) . . . between you and us …, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship . . . shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action.”  (Humphries Decl., Ex. A, p. 5.)

 

The language is clear and unmistakable.  The delegation clause reserves to the arbitrator the issue whether the agreement is enforceable.  For this reason, the court compels Plaintiff’s claims to arbitration.  Plaintiff may present his unconscionability argument to the arbitrator.[1]

 

IV.       CONCLUSION

 

             Given the foregoing, Defendant Casey’s Cars, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay All Proceedings is GRANTED.  The action is stayed pending the conclusion of the arbitration.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)  The court sets a post-arbitration status conference for August 21, 2025, at 9:00 a.m.

(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1551, 1559–60 citing (Rent-A-Center, supra, 561 U.S. at p. 70 [“when a party is claiming that an arbitration agreement is unenforceable, it is important to determine whether the party is making a specific challenge to the enforceability of the delegation clause or is simply arguing that the agreement as a whole is unenforceable. If the party's challenge is directed to the agreement as a whole--even if it applies equally to the delegation clause--the delegation clause is severed out and enforced; thus, the arbitrator, not the court, will determine whether the agreement is enforceable.  [¶] In contrast, if the party is making a specific challenge to the delegation clause, the court must determine whether the delegation clause itself may be enforced (and can only delegate the general issue of enforceability to the arbitrator if it first determines the delegation clause is enforceable).”].)

CASE:                                Malibu Psychic, a d.b.a. of George Merino, et al. v. Malibu Psychic, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                      24STCV13786

 

 

MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS

     

 

MOVING PARTY:              Defendants Malibu Psychic Spiritual Center, Inc., Alexus Miller,

                                                and Carol Stanton

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Malibu Psychic, a dba of George Merino, and George Merino

 

 

I.          INTRODUCTION

 

            On May 31, 2024, plaintiffs Malibu Psychic, a d.b.a. of George Merino, and George Merino (collectively, Plaintiff) commenced this action against defendants Malibu Psychic Spiritual Center, Inc. (erroneously sued as Malibu Psychic) (MPSC), Alexus Miller (erroneously sued as Alexis Miller) (Miller), Andrew Adams (Adams), Carol Stanton (Stanton), and Diane Ray (Ray) (collectively, Defendants). 

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Verified First Amended Complaint (FAC against Defendants for (1) Violation of Common Law Trademark Rights Under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 14245; 92) Trademark Dilution and Injury to Business Reputation Under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 14330 to 14335; (3) Unfair Competition and False Advertising Under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§17200 and 17500, et seq., (4) Trespass to Land; (5) Trespass to Land; (6) Conversion by Vandalism; (7) Conversion by Vandalism; (8) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (9) Application and Request for Injunctive Relief.

 

            On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Proof of Service of Summons showing “Alexis Miller” was served with the summons and complaint by personal service on August 2, 2024.  On the same day, Plaintiff filed Proofs of Service of Summons showing MPSC, Stanton, Adams, and Ray were served with the summons and complaint by substituted service on August 2, 2024.

 

On September 23, 2024, MPSC, Miller, and Stanton (hereafter, Moving Defendants) filed this Motion to Quash Service of Summons.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition.  Moving Defendants filed a reply.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

            Personal service may be accomplished by personally delivering a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)  If a copy of the summons and complaint cannot, with reasonable diligence, be personally delivered to the person being served, substitute service may be effected by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at the person’s “dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address ... in the presence of ... a person apparently in charge ... and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail ... to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20, subd. (b).) 

 

A defendant must file a motion to quash service of summons on or before the last day on which the defendant must plead unless the time is extended by stipulation or by a judge’s order for good case.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)  Although the notice of motion must designate a hearing date not more than 30 days after the notice is filed, scheduling a hearing date beyond the 30-day time period does not deprive the court of jurisdiction to consider the merits of the motion.  (Olinick v. BMB Entertainment (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1286, 1295-96.) 

 

Filing a proof of service by a registered process server creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper.  (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390 (Zara); Evid. Code, § 647 [“The return of a process server registered pursuant to ... the Business and Professions Code upon process or notice establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return”].)  This evidentiary presumption also applies in unlawful detainer actions and is “consistent with the purpose of the unlawful detainer procedure to afford a relatively simple and speedy remedy for specific landlord-tenant disputes.”  (Palm Property Investments, LLC v. Yadegar (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 1419, 1427 (Palm Property).) However, the presumption only arises if the proof of service complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.  (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441-42.)  Proof of service of summons may be impeached by evidence that contradicts it.  (City of Los Angeles v. Morgan (1951) 105 Cal.App.2d 726, 731.) 

 

When a defendant moves to quash service of summons, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.”¿  (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

 

            Moving Defendants seek an order quashing service of the summons and FAC on the following grounds:

 

1.      The Proof of Service forms indicate the complaint in this action was filed.  However, the complaint was superseded by a First Amended Complaint, and there is no proof of service for that amended complaint.

2.      The service package was given to Jennifer Dimitro, a relative of Ms. Miller, who was present at the business location while Ms. Miller stepped out. Ms. Dimitro does not work at Malibu Psychic Spiritual Center, Inc. and is not a proper person for substitute service.

3.      Ms. Miller was never personally served; the service package was given to Ms. Dimitro.

4.      Mr. Adams, Ms. Stanton and Ms. Ray are neither shareholders nor employees of Malibu Psychic Spiritual Center, Inc., and that business location is not proper to serve them. In fact, Mr. Adams primary residence is in Washington state, Ms. Adams resides in Nevada, and Ms. Stanton resides in Nevada.

 

(See Notice of Motion.)  

 

            At the outset, the court notes the proof of service forms and the attached declaration states Plaintiff’s process server Alexandra R. Rista (Rista) served the Complaint, not the FAC.  Defendants argue this is a fatal blow to Plaintiffs’ argument.  Not so. Rista submitted another declaration in support of Plaintiff’s opposition which indicates she served and mailed a copy of the FAC to MPSC, Adams, Stanton, and Ray.  While not an ideal situation for Plaintffs, Rista’s subsequent declaration provides sufficient ballast to keep the Plaintiffs’ case afloat.

 

            Because each defendant is situated differently, the court addresses each defendant in turn.

 

            Defendant Alexus Miller

 

            Plaintiff filed a proof of service stating Miller was personally served with process. Moving Defendants argue otherwise.  In support, Moving Defendants offer the declaration of Miller and her cousin, Jennifer Dimitro.  Miller states she stepped out of the business to attend to her children when Rista purportedly effectuated personal service.  Dimitro, who was visiting MPSC at the time, was left alone at the shop to greet Rista and to ask Rista to wait.  The legal papers were left at the shop.  (Miller Decl., ¶ 2.)  Dimitro confirms this version of events, and further states that she explained to Rista that she was not Miller.  (Dimitro Decl., ¶¶ 2-6.) 

 

            Plaintiff contests each of the foregoing points.  In support, Plaintiff offers the declaration of their registered process server, Rista, who states she first met Miller on July 31, 2024, at MPSC; Miller identified herself as “Alexus Miller.”  (Rista Decl., ¶ 3.)  Thereafter, Rista returned to MPSC on August 2, 2024, and personally served Miller with the summons and FAC on that date.  (Rista Decl., ¶ 5.)  

 

The affidavits are in conflict.  Miller states unequivocally that she was not personally served. Her position is supported by Dimitro.  Rista states unequivocally that she personally served Miller.  Rista submits text messages with Miller and a photo purportedly of Miller opening the front door of MPSC.  (See Rista Decl., Exs. 5 and 6.). However, the text messages do not provide a physical description of Miller or demonstrate that the person Rista met at the store was Miller.  The texts do provide some circumstantial evidence in support of Plaintiff’s point.  But Rista does not provide a physical description of Miller.  Further, the purported photo of Miller opening the door at MPSC is not helpful.  The door obscures a view of the person.  If there is an individual in the photo, the court cannot tell who it is, let alone glean the person’s physical characteristics. 

 

In short, the court cannot resolve this conflict based on the affidavits alone.  An evidentiary hearing is necessary.  The court will discuss with counsel possible dates for the evidentiary hearing.  The court will allocate forty-five minutes to each side to cross-examine witnesses on the narrow issue whether Miller was personally served.  The parties are to submit declarations (which can be the same ones or new ones) which shall serve as the direct testimony for each witness to be called.  The parties are to provide the court with their declarations and all exhibits, excluding impeachment and rebuttal exhibits.  The declarations and exhibits shall be provided to the court in a three ring notebook at least 5 court days in advance of the hearing.  Counsel are to exchange notebooks at least seven court days in advance of the hearing.  

 

Defendant MPSC

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of service stating MPSC was served by substituted service.  Moving Defendants contend MPSC was not properly served because (1) Dimitro, and not Miller, was served with process, (2) Dimitro is not MPSC’s agent for service of process, and (3) there is no proof of service of the FAC.

 

The court is not persuaded.  “If a copy of the summons and complaint cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered to the person to be served, …, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at the person’s dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and of the complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20(b), emphasis added.)

 

Here, Rista attached a declaration of diligence indicating she attempted personal service on July 24, 2024, July 31, 2024, August 1, 2024, and August 2, 2024.  (See Declaration of Diligence, Proof of Service (Stanton), 9/18/24.)  Based upon Dimitro’s Declaration, MPSC was served by substitute service because Dimitro was apparently in charge of the place of business when Rista arrived and served her with the papers.[1]  Rista thereafter mailed a copy of the summons and FAC to MPSC’s address.  Plaintiff properly effectuated substituted service on MPSC. 

 

Accordingly, the motion to quash as to MPSC is DENIED.

 

Defendant Andrew Adams

 

Plaintiff purportedly served Adams by substituted service.  Moving Defendants argue Adams is neither a shareholder nor employee of MPSC.  The point is supported by Miller’s declaration.  (Miller Decl., ¶ 3.)  Miller further states that Adams resides primarily in Washington state where he does construction work.  (Id.).

 

Plaintiff argues to the contrary.  In support, Plaintiff offers Rista’s declaration wherein she states that she conducted an internet search regarding Adams which revealed that he resided with Miller at 1509 South Wooster Avenue, Apt. #1, Los Angeles, California 90035, was  Miller’s husband, and the registered owner of defendant MPSC.  (Rista Decl., ¶ 7.)(emphasis added.)  Plaintiff offers his own declaration wherein he states that he hired a team of private investigators who determined Adams was an owner of MPSC.  (Merino Decl., ¶ 5.) 

 

The court finds the presumption of proper service as to Adams is rebutted.  Rista does not provide any evidence to corroborate the results of her internet search regarding Adams.  The reliability of that information cannot be determined.  Further, Plaintiff’s evidence regarding Miller’s ownership interest was obtained in June of 2023.  The information is stale.  In other words, Plaintiff does not submit any proof to indicate Adams owned or worked at MPSC at the time this lawsuit was filed. 

 

Accordingly, the motion to quash as to Adams is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Carol Stanton

 

Plaintiff purportedly served Stanton by substituted service.  Moving Defendants argue Stanton is neither a shareholder nor employee of MPSC.  The point is supported by Miller’s declaration.  (Miller Decl., ¶ 4.)  Miller further states that Stanton resides primarily in Texas.  (Id.)

 

Plaintiff argues to the contrary.  In support, Plaintiff offers his declaration wherein he states that he hired a team of private investigators who determined Stanton was an owner of MPSC.  (Merino Decl., ¶ 5.)  Further, Stanton personally placed her name on a business card to the private investigators, which demonstrates that Stanton worked at MPSC.  (Merino Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 8.)  Plaintiffs also include a photo of Stanton working the storefront counter of MPSC.  (See Merino Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 9.)

 

The court finds the presumption of proper service as to Stanton is rebutted.  As Plaintiff acknowledges, the name “Jackie” appears on the business card, not “Carol”.  Plaintiff does not provide any proof to show “Jackie” is Carol Stanton.  Further, Plaintiff’s evidence regarding Stanton’s ownership interest and working the storefront counter of MPSC was obtained in June of 2023.  The information is stale.  In other words, Plaintiff does not submit any proof to show Stanton owned or worked at MPSC at the time this lawsuit was filed in 2024. 

 

Accordingly, the motion to quash as to Stanton is GRANTED.

 

 Defendant Diane Ray

 

Plaintiff purportedly served Ray by substituted service.  Moving Defendants argue Ray is neither a shareholder nor employee of MPSC.  The point is supported by Miller’s declaration.  (Miller Decl., ¶ 5.)  Miller further states that Ray resides primarily in Nevada.  (Id.)

 

Plaintiff argues to the contrary.  In support, Plaintiff offers his declaration wherein he states that he hired a team of private investigators who determined Ray was an owner of MPSC.  (Merino Decl., ¶ 5.)  According to Plaintiff, Ray personally placed her name “Diane” on a business card to the private investigators, which demonstrates that Ray worked at MPSC.  (Merino Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 8.) 

 

The court finds the presumption of proper service as to Ray is rebutted.  Plaintiff’s evidence regarding Ray’s ownership interest and working the storefront counter of MPSC was obtained in June of 2023.  The information is stale.  In other words, Plaintiff does not submit any proof to show Ray owned or worked at MPSC at the time this lawsuit was filed in 2024. 

 

Accordingly, the motion to quash as to Ray is GRANTED.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION 

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Quash is Granted in Part, Denied in Part.  The motion is granted as to Andrew Adams, Carol Stanton, and Diane Ray.  The motion is denied as to Malibu Psychic Spiritual Center, Inc. 

 

The court will discuss possible dates for an evidentiary hearing with respect to Alexus Miller.  The hearing is limited to the narrow question whether Miller was personally served with process.
