Case Number: 19STCV46989    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

adela sanchez , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

kia motors america , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

19STCV46989

 

 

Hearing Date:

December 16, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion to compel further deposition of domingo perez

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                Defendant Kia America, Inc., f/k/a Kia Motors America, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Plaintiffs Adela Sanchez and Livia Larios

Motion to Compel Further Deposition of Domingo Perez

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Kia America, Inc., f/k/a Kia Motors America, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order compelling the further deposition of Domingo Perez (“Perez”) on the ground that counsel for plaintiffs Adela Sanchez and Livia Larios (“Plaintiffs”) instructed Perez not to answer various questions.

The court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Perez to answer the questions set forth as Excerpts Nos. 1-2 and 7-9 in Defendant’s separate statement because counsel for Plaintiffs, in the opposition papers, has not shown that there existed an attorney-client relationship for the purposes of Perez’s deposition, such that the information sought was not subject to the attorney-client privilege.

“Ordinarily, when a party seeks legal advice from a lawyer, and the lawyer provides such advice, an attorney-client relationship is formed.”  (Wood v. Superior Court of San Diego County (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 562, 581.)  “The attorney-client relationship requires something more than simply speaking to an attorney about a legal matter.  [Citations.]  To be a client for purposes of the privilege, a person must ‘consult[] a lawyer for the purpose of retaining the lawyer or securing legal service or advice from him in his professional capacity . . . .’”  (Ibid. [internal citations omitted] [emphasis in original]; Evid. Code, § 951.)

First, at his deposition, Perez testified “No” to the question “Have you retained an attorney to represent you for this deposition?” (Alarcon Decl., Ex. A, Perez, p. 9:19-21.)  Perez did not correct his deposition testimony.  Moreover, Perez did not submit a declaration stating that (1) he did not understand the question or the term “retain,” or (2) he consulted with counsel for Plaintiffs for the purposes of securing legal service or advice from counsel.  (Woods, supra, 46 Cal.App.5th at p. 581; Opp., p. 7:1-8.)

Second, the court finds that counsel for Plaintiffs has not established the existence of an attorney-client relationship between counsel and Perez in their declaration.

Counsel for Plaintiffs have submitted a declaration stating, in relevant part, the following: “I met with Mr. Perez in preparation for the deposition and subsequently defended his deposition on October 16, 2024.  I represented Mr. Perez as his attorney for the purpose of the deposition.  Mr. Perez provided substantive testimony on facts pertaining to his knowledge regarding the Subject Vehicle.  All of my communications with Mr. Perez were made under the protection of the attorney-client privilege.”  (Reed Decl., ¶ 6.)  The court finds that this declaration is insufficient to establish an attorney-client relationship between Perez and counsel for Plaintiffs.  Counsel did not state facts establishing the existence of such a relationship, including that Perez consulted with counsel for the purpose of securing legal services or advice, and instead merely stated, in conclusory fashion, that counsel represented Perez as his attorney for the purpose of deposition.  (Ibid.)

            The court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Perez to answer the questions set forth in Excerpts Nos. 3-4 and 6 because Plaintiffs’ counsel has withdrawn the objections to those questions.  (Pl. Sep. Statement, pp. 8:2-6, 8:15-21, 11:10-15.)

The court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Perez to answer the question set forth as Excerpt No. 5 in the Separate Statement because the court finds, after considering Perez’s right to privacy in his financial information, his objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in these circumstances, the threatened intrusion, and the legitimate and countervailing interests that disclosure serves, that Perez’s privacy concerns are outweighed by Defendant’s need to obtain the requested information in order to calculate damages that may be awarded to Plaintiffs in this action.  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 2 Cal.5th 531, 552 [setting forth framework for evaluation potential invasions of privacy].)

ORDER

            The court grants defendant Kia America, Inc., f/k/a Kia Motors America, Inc.’s motion to compel further deposition testimony of Domingo Perez.

            The court orders Domingo Perez to appear for and testify at a deposition to be taken for counsel by defendant Kia America, Inc., f/k/a Kia Motors America, Inc. on ____________, 2024.  The court further orders that, at that deposition, Domingo Perez shall answer the questions set forth in Excerpt Nos. 1-9, as set forth in the separate statement filed by defendant Kia America, Inc., f/k/a Kia Motors America, Inc. on November 12, 2024.

            The court orders defendant Kia America, Inc., f/k/a Kia Motors America, Inc. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s request for court judgment by default

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                Plaintiff Thiago Tambuque      

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       n/a

Request for Court Judgment by Default

Plaintiff Thiago Tambuque (“Plaintiff”) requests that the court enter default judgment in his favor and against defendant Eden Atias (“Defendant”) in the total amount of $52,183.70, consisting of $39,856.92 in damages, $948.20 in interest, $3,478.50 in costs, and $7,900 in attorney’s fees.

First, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to recover damages in the amount of $10,000 in connection with the first cause of action for willful misclassification in violation of Labor Code section 226.8.  (Tambuque Decl., ¶¶ 6-7; Lab. Code, § 226.8, subds. (a)(1) [it is unlawful for any employer to engage in the willful misclassification of an individual as an independent contractor], (b) [if the Labor and Workforce Development Agency or a court issues a determination that an employer has engaged in a violation, the employer shall be subject to a civil penalty of not less than $5,000 and not more than $15,000 for each violation].)  The court notes that, although Plaintiff has requested $15,000 in penalties, Plaintiff only prayed for $10,000, such that $10,000 is the maximum amount that the court may award.  (Compl., ¶ 25 [alleging that Plaintiff is entitled to penalties in the amount of $10,000]; Sass v. Cohen (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 1032, 1040 [“The ‘relief granted’ in the default judgment ‘cannot exceed’ what the plaintiff ‘demanded in the [operative] complaint’”]; Brief, p. 7:15-17 [requesting $15,000 for the intentional misclassification as an independent contractor].)

The court also notes that, in Noe v. Superior Court (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 316, 338 (Noe), the court held, in construing the language of Labor Code section 226.8, that “[t]here is no language suggesting a private plaintiff may bring a direct action under section 226.8 action or that a misclassified employee may collect the penalties described within the statute.”  But section 226.8 has been amended since Noe was decided.  As amended, it appears that an employee may recover the penalties provided by section 226.8.  (Lab. Code, § 226.8, subd. (g)(2) [in an enforcement pursuant to this statute, the Labor Commissioner or public prosecutor “may alternatively recover the penalties . . . as damages payable to the employee.  An employee is entitled to recover either the damages as provided for in this section or to enforce a civil penalty, as set forth in subdivision (a) of Section 2699, but not both, for the same violation”] [emphasis added]; Noe, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at p. 339 [finding that there was no private right of action other than through a PAGA claim under section 226.8 because, inter alia, it “contain[ed] no language suggesting the penalty is recoverable directly by employees”].)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to recover from Defendant (1) $2,764.32 in unpaid minimum wages, and (2) $2,764.32 in liquidated damages for failure to pay minimum wages.  (Lab. Code, §§ 1197, 1194, 1194.2, subd. (a); Tambuque Decl., ¶ 8; Compl., ¶ 35.)

Third, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to recover $47.88 for missed meal and rest periods.  (Lab. Code, § 226.7; Tambuque Decl., ¶ 9; Compl., ¶ 46.)

Fourth, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to recover the requested $250 in damages for three failures on the part of Defendant to provide to Plaintiff an accurate wage statement.  (Lab. Code, § 226, subd. (e)(1) [allowing for an employee to recover penalties not to exceed $4,000 for violation of section 226 in the amounts of $50 for the first violation and an additional $100 for each subsequent violation], Tambuque Decl., ¶¶ 4 [Plaintiff was promised weekly payment], 8 [Plaintiff worked for Defendant for four weeks], 9 [Plaintiff did not receive any wage statements].)

Fifth, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to recover damages in the amount of $3,830.40 in waiting time penalties since, as set forth above, Defendant did not pay to Plaintiff all wages due.  (Lab. Code, § 203 [if an employer willfully fails to pay wages of an employee who is discharged, the wages shall continue as a penalty from the due date until paid or an action is commenced, but not for more than 30 days]; Tambuque Decl., ¶¶ 8 [Plaintiff received only $300 in compensation], 10 [Defendant did not pay Plaintiff for the work that he performed].)

Sixth, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to recover indemnification for necessary expenditures incurred by Plaintiff in the discharge of his duties for Defendant in the amount of $200.  (Lab. Code, § 2802, subd. (a); Tambuque Decl., ¶ 9 [Plaintiff was not compensated for the use of his personal computer, phone, and phone service which amounted to $200].)

Seventh, the court finds that Plaintiff has not proven that he is entitled to damages in the amount of $15,000 in connection with his wrongful termination cause of action because (1) he did not present adequate argument, authority, or evidence establishing that he has suffered damages in that amount, and (2) to the extent that Plaintiff seeks to recover punitive damages from Defendant, Plaintiff was required to, but did not, submit proof of Defendant’s financial condition.  (Kim v. Westmoore Partners, Inc. (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 267, 272 [“it is incumbent on the plaintiff to prove up his damages, with actual evidence”] [emphasis in original]; Compl., ¶ 66 [requesting, in connection with the wrongful termination cause of action, “exemplary damages . . . in an amount of $15,000”]; Soto v. BorgWarner Morse TEC Inc. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 165, 195 [“Evidence of a defendant’s financial condition is a legal precondition to the award of punitive damages”].)

Eighth, the court finds that the requested costs are recoverable as costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivisions (a)(1) and (a)(4).  (CIV-100, ¶ 7.)

Ninth, the court finds that the interest calculations filed on December 2, 2024 are incorrect.

Plaintiff requests prejudgment interest, at the rate of 10 percent per annum, on the following damages: $5,528.64 for lost minimum wages and liquidated damages, $47.88 for failure to provide meal and rest breaks, and $200 for failure to reimburse business expenses.  (Dec. 2, 2024 MC-030 [asserting he is owed $5,776.62 in wages based on those damages].) However, Plaintiff may only recover 10 percent interest on $5,528.64 for the nonpayment of wages.  (Lab. Code, § 218.6 [“In any action brought for the nonpayment of wages, the court shall award interest on all due and unpaid wages at the rate of interest specified in subdivision (b) of Section 3289 of the Civil Code, which shall accrue from the date that the wages were due and payable . . . .”].)  Plaintiff may recover 7 percent interest on the total $247.88 in damages for failure to provide meal and rest breaks and failure to reimburse business expenses.  (Ibid.; Naranjo v. Spectrum Security Services, Inc. (2022) 13 Cal.5th 93, 122 [“for purposes of prejudgment interest under Labor Code section 218.6, a section 226.7 suit is also not an ‘action brought for the nonpayment of wages,’ as section 218.6 requires, and so does not qualify for that statute’s higher rate”].)  The court finds that Plaintiff may recover prejudgment interest in the total amount of $992.02, consisting of 10 percent interest as to the $5,528.64 for the 635-day period of March 17, 2023 (the date of breach) through December 16, 2024 (amounting to $961.83) and 7 percent of interest on the remaining $247.88 for the same period (amounting to $30.19).  (Tambuque Decl., ¶ 10 [Plaintiff’s last day of work was March 17, 2023]; Dec. 2, 2024 MC-030 [requesting interesting from date of March 17, 2023].)

Tenth, the court awards Plaintiff reasonable attorney’s fees in the amount of $985.71 pursuant to Local Rule 3.214.

Finally, the court notes that Plaintiff has now dismissed all Doe defendants.  (Dec. 2, 2024 Req. for Dismissal, ¶¶ 1, 5.)

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has proven that he is entitled to judgment in his favor and against Defendant in the total amount of $25,313.23, consisting of (1) $19,856.92 in damages and penalties as set forth above, (2) $3,478.58 in costs, (3) $992.02 in prejudgment interest, and (4) $985.71 in attorney’s fees.

The court will modify, sign, and file the proposed Judgment lodged by Plaintiff on December 2, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
