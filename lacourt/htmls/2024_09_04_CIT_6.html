DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV00428    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Maria Angela Rodriguez v. Mercado Latino, Inc., et al. 

1 – Defendants Mercado Latino, Inc., Grosac, Inc., Richard Rodriguez, and Roberto Rodriguez’s Demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint (Joined by Defendant Jorge Rodriguez); and

2 – Defendants Mercado Latino, Inc., Grosac, Inc., Richard Rodriguez, and Roberto Rodriguez’s Motion to Strike Portions of the Third Amended Complaint (Joined by Defendant Jorge Rodriguez) 

TENTATIVE RULING           

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action of the Third Amended Complaint without leave to amend as to Moving Parties and Jorge. The Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the Second Cause of Action. Defendants must file an answer within 20 days of the Court’s order. 

The Court GRANTS the motion to strike as to page 1, lines 27 through 28 of the TAC, and page 13, lines 19 through 20 as to Moving Parties and Jorge. The Court otherwise DENIES the motion. 

Moving Parties are ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days. 

BACKGROUND 

This is an involuntary dissolution action. On May 21, 2021, Plaintiff Maria Angela Rodriguez (Plaintiff) filed this action against Defendants Mercado Latino, Inc. (Mercado Latino), Grosac, Inc. (Grosac), Richard Yarruths Rodriguez (Richard), Roberto Rodriguez (Roberto), Jorge Rodriguez (Jorge) (collectively, Defendants), and Does 1 through 50. On July 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (TAC), alleging causes of action for involuntary dissolution of Mercado Latino and Grosac, breach of fiduciary duty, financial elder abuse, and accounting. 

On July 29, 2024, Defendants Mercado Latino, Grosac, Richard, and Roberto (collectively, Moving Parties) demurred to and moved to strike portions of the TAC. On July 30, 2024, and August 15, 2024, Jorge joined Moving Parties’ motions. On August 21, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the demurrer and motion to strike. On August 27, 2024, Moving Parties replied to both oppositions. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Demurrer 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (Code Civ. Proc., § 422.10; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Id.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862, disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287 [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery. (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-972.) The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint, or in a superseded complaint in the same action. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”]) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE – Demurrer 

            The Court GRANTS Moving Parties’ requests for judicial notice. (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (d).) However, the Court takes judicial notice only as to the existence, content, and authenticity of such documents; it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted therein. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal. App. 5th 389, 400.)           

DISCUSSION – Demurrer           

            Meet and Confer 

            Per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), Moving Parties were required to meet and confer telephonically or in person before bringing this demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The Court finds Moving Parties’ meet-and-confer efforts to be sufficient. (Leight Decl., ¶ 2, subd. (b).) 

Second Cause of Action – Involuntary Dissolution (Grosac) 

To state a cause of action for involuntary dissolution, the plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating the plaintiff is a person entitled to bring such an action and the grounds for such relief, as set forth in Corporations Code section 1800. (Corp. Code, § 1800.) 

“Generally, if a verified complaint contains allegations fatal to a cause of action, a plaintiff cannot cure the defect by simply omitting those allegations in an amended pleading without explanation. [Citation.] This principle is known as the sham pleading doctrine. But amendment in this manner is allowed where a plaintiff clearly shows that the earlier pleading is the result of mistake or inadvertence. [Citations.]” (JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. v. Ward (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 678, 690; see also Pierce v. Lyman (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1109 [sham pleading doctrine also applies to unverified complaints].) “When the plaintiff pleads inconsistently in separate actions, the plaintiff's complaint is nothing more than a sham that seeks to avoid the effect of a demurrer. [Citation.]” (Cantu v. Resol. Tr. Corp. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 857, 877-878, italics in original.) 

Moving Parties demur to the Second Cause of Action for involuntary dissolution of Grosac on the grounds that the TAC fails to state a cause of action based on the sham pleading doctrine. Moving Parties contend Plaintiff made contradictory allegations in a prior probate action (LASC Case No. 17STPB08193; Request for Judicial Notice, Exs. 1-4) in which Plaintiff alleged her father gave her some shares in Grosac and does not mention having invested $50,000.00 of her own money in exchange for an equal ownership interest in Grosac. Moving Parties also contend the allegations of the $50,000.00 investment are missing from the original complaint and First Amended Complaint. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends Moving Parties’ sham pleading argument is barred by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (b), and that it is not a sham pleading. Plaintiff contends the Second Cause of Action of the TAC is almost identical to the previous versions of the complaint, and Defendants were parties to the prior probate petitions, so they could have raised these arguments in their prior demurrers, but they did not. Plaintiff then contends the Second Cause of Action is not a sham pleading because the allegations upon which Defendants base their arguments are not material and do not create a defect or an unexplained inconsistency. Plaintiff also contends the original complaint and First Amended Complaint do in fact allege that she financially contributed to the purchase of the real property held by Grosac, and that the investment issue was not relevant to the probate case or inconsistent with the pleadings in this case. 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s arguments regarding Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (b), persuasive. Moving Parties did not raise this sham pleading issue in their prior demurrers. The probate action was filed in 2018 and involved the Moving Parties. (See Requests for Judicial Notice, Exs. 1-4.) Moving Parties’ reply papers do not dispute they were aware of the probate action either. (Reply, pp. 4-5; Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410; C. Opposing the Motion—and Rebutting the Opposition, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 9(I)-C, ¶ 9:105.10; see also Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215 [“Contentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority. [Citation.]”]) While some grounds for attacking the complaint like jurisdiction and failure to state a claim are not waivable for failure to object by demurrer or answer, (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.80), the argument Plaintiff failed to previously raise is the sham pleading argument, not the failure to state a cause of action or lack of jurisdiction. Moving Parties’ demurrer to the Second Cause of Action must therefore be overruled. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (b).) 

Moreover, even if the Court were to consider Moving Parties’ substantive arguments, the Court does not find the absence of allegations from the probate case petitions regarding the $50,000.00 investment to be an inconsistent allegation for purposes of the sham pleading doctrine. The petitions in the probate case were focused on transferring certain real property into a trust so that it could be distributed. (Requests for Judicial Notice, Ex. 1, ¶ 14; Id., Ex. 2, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff’s ownership interest in Grosac does not appear to have been a concern in the probate case. The Court also does not necessarily find allegations of a $50,000.00 investment versus a gift of the Grosac stock to Plaintiff to be inconsistent allegations either, as the Court reads them to be alternative theories by which Plaintiff came to allegedly own the stock. (TAC, ¶¶ 16, 23; Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. 2, ¶ 13; Williams v. S. California Gas Co. (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 591, 598 [alternative factual allegations based on alternative legal theories is permissible].) 

Furthermore, Plaintiff correctly contends that she previously alleged having contributed money or assets to Grosac. (Compl., ¶ 43; FAC, ¶¶ 63, 70.) The Court sustained multiple demurrers in part because Plaintiff had alleged insufficient facts, so it would be unfair to punish Plaintiff for alleging additional facts, such as the $50,000.00 investment. (See Order re Tentative Ruling (2/27/24); Order re Tentative Ruling (6/10/24).) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the Second Cause of Action of the Third Amended Complaint. 

Fourth Cause of Action – Financial Elder Abuse 

“Financial abuse” of an elder or dependent adult occurs when a person or entity does any of the following: 

(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. 

(2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. 

(3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70. 

(Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30, subd. (a).) 

Elder abuse claims are statutory causes of action and therefore must be pleaded with particularity. (See Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790, citing Lopez v. Southern Cal. Rapid Trans. Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795). 

Sham Pleading and Insufficient Damage Allegations 

Moving Parties demur to the Fourth Cause of Action for financial elder abuse on the grounds that it fails to state a cause of action and is a sham pleading. Moving Parties reiterate their arguments here that this is a sham pleading because there were no allegations in the probate petitions or earlier versions of the complaint in this action of an agreement between Plaintiff and Defendants regarding Plaintiff investing $50,000.00 into Grosac to purchase real property and receive shares in return that would be equal to her brothers’ interests. Moving Parties contend the allegations in the probate action are inconsistent because she alleged in that case that she obtained an equal ownership interest as a gift from her father. Moving Parties also contend the allegations of the TAC lack the specificity required for a financial elder abuse claim. 

Moving Parties further contend the only damages alleged pertain to Defendants’ refusal to provide Plaintiff the Grosac stock certificates or other evidence of her ownership, but these allegations are a sham because Plaintiff sought in the probate action to have title to the real property transferred from Grosac to the trust, which would prevent any purchaser from buying the property. Moving Parties also contend the TAC does not allege the majority shareholders agreed to the sale of that property or that anyone offered to buy Plaintiff’s interest in Grosac. 

In opposition, Plaintiff reiterates that the sham pleading argument was not raised in prior demurrers. Plaintiff contends that the Fourth Cause of Action is not a sham for the same reasons set forth above, namely that the prior allegations Moving Parties reference are not material, they do not create an unexplained inconsistency, Plaintiff previously alleged her financial contributions to Grosac in the prior versions of the complaint and there was no reason to allege the $50,000.00 investment in the probate action because it was not a probate issue. Plaintiff then contends she adequately alleged harm and argues that the applicable heightened pleading standard for financial elder abuse is particularity, not specificity. Plaintiff further contends the TAC alleges that Defendants refused to provide her with her Grosac stock certificates, they deny she owns Grosac stock, and they refuse to reimburse her for her investment. 

The Court rejects Moving Parties’ sham pleading arguments for the same reasons set forth above. Nevertheless, the Court finds the TAC fails to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action for financial elder abuse. The Court sees two primary problems here, namely that the onus was on Graciliano to transfer the shares to Plaintiff, not Defendants, and that Plaintiff still does not allege any facts evidencing damages. The TAC alleges that Jorge acknowledged in writing in 2005 that Graciliano wanted each of his children to be equal shareholders in Grosac, and that he would gift enough shares to Plaintiff to make her an equal shareholder. (TAC, ¶ 20.) The TAC also alleges that Graciliano signed a notarized document stating that it was his wish Plaintiff would receive his and his wife’s shares in Grosac. (TAC, ¶ 22.) The TAC then alleges in a conclusory fashion that Graciliano left his children as the sole shareholders of Mercado and Grosac, each having an equal 25% share. (TAC, ¶ 23.) The problem here is that the shares at issue belong (or belonged) to Graciliano, so the onus of transferring the shares to Plaintiff as alleged fell on Graciliano, not Defendants. Thus, even assuming the allegations of the TAC are true, Defendants are not at fault for Graciliano’s failure to transfer the shares to Plaintiff. 

As for Plaintiff having been allegedly contacted by persons and entities interested in buying the City of Industry property, that does not damage Plaintiff because the TAC alleges Grosac owns the property, not Plaintiff. (TAC, ¶ 29.) The TAC also does not allege that anyone offered to purchase Plaintiff’s shares in Grosac. (Id.) Moreover, as stated previously, it is unclear how Plaintiff suffered harm due to the alleged withholding of Plaintiff’s stock certificates. Stock certificates are merely evidence of ownership, and the rights to the stock may exist without the certificates. (Lowe v. Copeland (1932) 125 Cal.App. 315, 325 (Lowe).) Plaintiff is still free to sell whatever shares she has in Grosac, even if the she does not have the certificates. (Cf Id. [“a certificate is only evidence of ownership, which may exist without it…”].) To the extent Plaintiff alleges she demanded her stock certificates, that is still of no avail to Plaintiff because she simultaneously alleges that Defendants deny Plaintiff has any such rights. (TAC, ¶ 50, subd. (i); Lowe, supra, 125 Cal.App. at p. 325, italics added [“a demand is necessary to put a corporation in default for failure to deliver certificates of stock where the right thereto has not been denied [citations]…”]) 

Furthermore, the authority Plaintiff cited does not state that holding onto stock certificates constitutes financial elder abuse. Keading v. Keading (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 1115 involved a defendant who caused the elder to execute new stock certificates shortly before the elder died. (Id., at p. 1128.) Mears v. Crocker First Nat. Bank of San Francisco (1948) 84 Cal.App.2d 637 involved conversion, not financial elder abuse. (Id., at p. 644.) 

Statute of Limitations 

“While a demurrer based on statute of limitations lies where the dates in question are shown on the face of the complaint, if those dates are missing, there is no ground for a general demurrer. [Citation.]” (United W. Med. Centers v. Superior Ct. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 500, 505.) The statute of limitations for financial elder abuse is four years from the when the plaintiff discovers, or through the exercise of reasonable diligence, should have discovered the facts constituting the financial abuse. (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15657.7.) 

Moving Parties demur to the Fourth Cause of Action on the grounds that it is barred by the statute of limitations. Moving Parties contend the statute of limitations applies here because the TAC alleges Plaintiff invested $50,000.00 in Grosac in 1988, and Plaintiff therefore knew or should have known since 1988 that she had not received Grosac stock certificates. Moving Parties also contend Plaintiff had presumptive knowledge about the stock certificates since she alleges she was an officer and director of Grosac. Moving Parties also point to an allegation that in 2005, Jorge acknowledged in writing that the parties’ father, Graciliano, wanted each of the siblings to have an equal number of shares in Grosac, and that he would gift Plaintiff enough to make her shares equal. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends this argument was not raised in previous demurrers and is therefore prohibited under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (b). Plaintiff also contends the discovery rule tolled the statute of limitations until 2018 because she did not learn that Defendants were withholding her stock certificates until then, and she did not have any reason to suspect her family would not provide her with stock certificates and stock. 

The Court finds Plaintiff correctly contends Moving Parties failed to raise this argument in previous demurrers. The SAC alleged the date of 1988. (SAC, ¶¶ 15, 25.) Moving Parties’ demurrer to the SAC did not raise the statute of limitations as an issue. (Demurrer (4/24/24).) However, failure to object by demurrer for failure to state a cause of action is not a waivable defect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.80.) As such, because the statute of limitations is simply a form of the argument of failure to state a cause of action, the Court may still consider this objection, notwithstanding Moving Parties’ failure. 

Nevertheless, the Court notes that the statute of limitations for an elder abuse claim would not apply here with respect to the alleged agreement made in the 1980’s because Plaintiff was not an elder at that time. (TAC, ¶¶ 16, 62; Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.27.) Moreover, the Court does not find the alleged $50,000.00 investment in 1988 to necessarily trigger any statute of limitations, as at most it only alleges when Plaintiff made the investment, not when Defendants breached an obligation or harmed Plaintiff. (See TAC, ¶ 28.) The TAC otherwise does not allege dates indicating when Defendants withheld Plaintiff’s stock certificates or indicia of ownership. (See TAC, ¶ 29.) To the extent Defendants’ alleged misconduct occurred starting in 2018, that would be within the four-year statute of limitations for financial elder abuse since Plaintiff filed this action on May 21, 2021. (See TAC, ¶¶ 25-26, 29.) Furthermore, as noted above, the Court finds the onus with respect to the transfer of stock really fell on Graciliano in this matter, not Defendants. Thus, the statute of limitations is not an issue here. 

            Nevertheless, for the other reasons set forth above, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action without leave to amend.

 

LEGAL STANDARD – Motion to Strike 

            “Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(2).) “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id., § 436.) 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE – Motion to Strike 

            The Court GRANTS Moving Parties’ request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (c); Belen v. Ryan Seacrest Prods., LLC (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1145, 1160 fn. 2 [Court of Appeal took judicial notice of statement of information filed with the California Secretary of State indicating who the chief executive officer, secretary, and chief financial officer were of business entity].) However, “[t]aking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning.” (Fremont Indem. Co. v. Fremont Gen. Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113.) 

DISCUSSION 

Meet and Confer 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a), Moving Parties were required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference before bringing this motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) The Court finds Moving Parties’ meet-and-confer efforts to be sufficient. (Leight Decl., ¶ 2, subd. (b).) 

            Page 1, lines 27-28 

            “’Close corporation’ means a corporation, including a close social purpose corporation, whose articles contain, in addition to the provisions required by Section 202, a provision that all of the corporation's issued shares of all classes shall be held of record by not more than a specified number of persons, not exceeding 35, and a statement, ‘This corporation is a close corporation.’” (Corp. Code, § 158, subd. (a).) 

            Moving Parties move to strike the language at page 1, lines 27 through 28 of the TAC, which reads: “At all times relevant here, MERCADO and GROSAC were and are closely held… (corporations).” Moving Parties move strike this language on the grounds that the allegation that Mercado and Grosac are close corporations are conclusory and false. Moving Parties contend none of the articles of incorporation for Mercado or Grosac indicate that all issued shares of all classes shall be held by no more than a specific number of persons, not to exceed 35. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends the motion fails to comply with Rule 3.1322, subdivision (a), of the California Rules of Court, which requires the moving party to quote in full the language sought to be stricken, and the motion does not fully quote the language at issue with respect to close corporations. Plaintiff also contends the motion fails because it relies on the Court construing documents attached to the request for judicial notice, which the Court cannot do. Plaintiff also disputes the reliability of the documents provided for judicial notice. 

            The Court agrees with Moving Parties. While the Court generally may not take judicial notice of the truth of the contents of a document, it may do so when there is only one reasonable interpretation of a document. (See Baldwin v. AAA Northern California, Nevada & Utah Ins. Exchange (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 545, 553 [demurrer sustained without leave to amend where there was only one reasonable interpretation of the document]; A. Demurrers, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 7(I)-A, ¶ 7:15.10.) The articles of incorporation Moving Parties submitted contain stamps from the California Secretary of State, which supports their reliability. (Evid. Code, § 1452, subd. (b).) Moreover, there is only one reasonable interpretation here, i.e., that Mercado and Grosac are not closely held corporations. None of them contain language indicating that they are limited to 35 or less shareholders, nor do they contain the express language, “This corporation is a close corporation.” (Requests for Judicial Notice, Exs. 1-3.) 

To the extent Moving Parties did not quote in full the language sought to be stricken per Rule 3.1322, subdivision (a), of the California Rules of Court, the Court agrees Moving Parties did not fully comply with this requirement, but the Court also finds this to be a technicality here where it is clear what Moving Parties seek to strike. The Court therefore exercises its discretion to not deny on that basis. Nevertheless, the Court admonishes Moving Parties to comply with the requirements of the California Rules of Court going forward. 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion to strike as to page 1, lines 27 through 28 of the TAC. 

            Page 11, line 14 

            Moving Parties move to strike the language at page 11, line 14 of the TAC, which reads: “(Against All Defendants)”. Moving Parties reference the Court’s ruling issued on June 10, 2024, in which the Court sustained the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint as to Defendant Mercado without leave to amend, but the TAC alleges that the Fourth Cause of Action is against all Defendants, which would include Mercado. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends that this is a minor defect that does not affect the substantial rights of the parties. Plaintiff acknowledges the Court’s prior ruling sustaining the demurrer without leave to amend to the Fourth Cause of Action as to Mercado and contends therefore that this defect cannot affect the substantial rights of any party. 

            The Court agrees with Plaintiff. This is a minor pleading error; what matters is the substantive allegations, none of which allege Mercado is liable for financial elder abuse. (See TAC, ¶¶ 61-72; see Alfaro v. Cmty. Hous. Improvement Sys. & Plan. Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1371 [“What is necessary to state a cause of action are the facts warranting legal relief, and not whether a plaintiff has provided apt, inapt, or no labels or titles for causes of action. [Citation.]”) 

            Accordingly, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as to page 11, line 14. Moreover, given that the Court sustained the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action without leave to amend, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as to page 11, line 14 moot. 

            Page 13, lines 19-20 

            Attorney fees are recoverable either by law, statute, or contract. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).)           

Moving Parties seek to strike the language at page 13, lines 19 through 20 of the TAC, which reads: “An order that MERCADO and/or GROSAC pay all of ANGIE’s attorneys’ fees and costs under corporate governance laws and rules.” Moving Parties contend there is no contractual or statutory basis for attorney fees here. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends she is entitled to recover attorney fees based on corporate governance laws. 

            The Court agrees with Moving Parties. The TAC does not allege either a statutory or contractual basis to recover attorney fees here. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).) Neither the TAC nor Plaintiff’s opposition identify any statutes regarding corporate governance that provide for recovery of attorney fees in this case. 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion to strike as to page 13, lines 19 through 20 of the TAC. 

CONCLUSION           

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action of the Third Amended Complaint without leave to amend as to Moving Parties and Jorge. The Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the Second Cause of Action. Defendants must file an answer within 20 days of the Court’s order. 

The Court GRANTS the motion to strike as to page 1, lines 27 through 28 of the TAC, and page 13, lines 19 through 20 as to Moving Parties and Jorge. The Court otherwise DENIES the motion. 

Moving Parties are ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days.


Case Number: 22PSCV01124    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Great New World Equity LLC v. Zhou’s Investment & Management, Inc., dba The Noodle 

Plaintiff Great New World Equity LLC’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees in the Amount of $57,790.54 Against Defendant Zhou’s Investment & Management, Inc. 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees in the reduced amount of $47,620.64. 

             Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is an unlawful detainer action. On September 29, 2022, plaintiff Great New World Equity LLC (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendant Zhou’s Investment & Management, Inc., dba The Noodle (Defendant), alleging the sole cause of action for unlawful detainer. On April 29, 2024, following Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment, the Court entered judgment against Defendant and in favor of Plaintiff. 

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff moved for attorney’s fees. On August 21, 2024, Defendant opposed the motion. On August 27, 2024, Plaintiff replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD

“In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).)

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney… involved in the presentation of the case.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. [Citation.] The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work. [Citation.]” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

“The courts repeatedly have stated that the trial court is in the best position to value the services rendered by the attorneys in his or her courtroom [citation], and this includes the determination of the hourly rate that will be used in the lodestar calculus.[Citation.]” (569 E. Cnty. Boulevard LLC v. Backcountry Against the Dump, Inc. (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 426, 436-437.)

DISCUSSION

Summary of Arguments 

Plaintiff contends it is the prevailing party in this action and therefore entitled to attorney fees because it obtained summary judgment and the subject lease agreement provides for recovery of attorney fees to the prevailing party. Plaintiff contends its requested attorney fees are reasonable for this community based on Plaintiff’s counsel having between 20 and over 25 years of litigation experience, plus the application of a 15% discount to the total bill, which reduced Plaintiff’s counsel’s respective hourly rates in this matter to $446.25 for Mr. Robert A. Bailey and $429.25 for Mr. Gene Wu. Plaintiff further addresses work performed for items that ultimately did not materialize, such as the Mandatory Settlement Conference and the two motions to compel discovery responses, mentioned certain entries that were written off, and otherwise summarized the bulk of the work performed. 

In opposition, Defendant contends Plaintiff’s request for attorney fees is unreasonable, and contends various entries on the declaration of John J. Ma, i.e. Plaintiff’s prior counsel are improper because they were block billed, improperly lumped together work performed over multiple days, and that one entry is impermissibly vague. Defendant also contends there were some duplicative entries and some entries improperly charged to this case instead of the other related civil case. 

Analysis 

The subject lease agreement provides for the recovery of attorney fees here. (Wu Decl. (3/11/24), Ex. 2, ¶ 32.4, italics added [“If either Landlord or Tenant should bring suit against the other with respect to this Lease, including for unlawful detainer… then all costs and expenses incurred by the prevailing party therein (including… attorneys’ and other professional fees…), shall be paid by the other party.”].) If a plaintiff obtains the relief requested on the contract claims, the plaintiff is the prevailing party on the contract as a matter of law. (Texas Commerce Bank v. Garamendi (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1234, 1247; Smith v. Krueger (1983) 150 Cal.App.3d 752, 757.) Here, Plaintiff prevailed on its claim for unlawful detainer. Judgment was entered against Defendant and in favor of Plaintiff. (Judgment (4/29/24).) Defendant does not dispute that the subject lease agreement provides for the recovery of attorney fees here, nor does Defendant dispute that Plaintiff is the prevailing party. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410; C. Opposing the Motion—and Rebutting the Opposition, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 9(I)-C, ¶ 9:105.10; see also Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215 [“Contentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority. [Citation.]”]) Thus, Plaintiff is the prevailing party on the subject lease agreement, and is therefore entitled to attorney fees. (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a); see Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10)(A).) 

The Court also finds the rates of Plaintiff’s current and former counsel to be reasonable. Typically, the reasonable hourly rate used, “is that prevailing in the community for similar work.” (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 616.) The burden is on the party seeking attorney fees to prove their reasonableness. (Id., at p. 615.) Plaintiff has met its burden by providing evidence of its counsel’s experience. (Wu Decl., ¶¶ 3, 10, Ex. A to Ex. 1.) Mr. Ma, i.e., Plaintiff’s original counsel, indicates he has practiced law for almost 30 years in civil litigation, and that his hourly rate in this matter is $400.00 per hour. (Wu Decl., Ex. A to Ex. 1, ¶¶ 2-3.) Mr. Wu has practiced law for 20 years, while Mr. Bailey has practiced for over 25 years. (Wu Decl., ¶ 10, subds. (a), (b).) Mr. Wu’s and Mr. Bailey’s effective rates in this action are $429.25 and $446.25, respectively. (Id.) The Court finds Plaintiff’s counsel’s rates are reasonable for comparable work in this community. (569 E. Cnty. Boulevard LLC v. Backcountry Against the Dump, Inc., supra, 6 Cal.App.5th at pp. 436-437.) 

As for the billing entries that Defendant disputes, the Court agrees with Defendant that the Mr. Ma’s fee bill contains some issues. While a verified fee bill is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were reasonable, (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682), “[t]rial courts retain discretion to penalize block billing when the practice prevents them from discerning which tasks are compensable and which are not. [Citation.]”, (Heritage Pac. Fin., LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 1010). Almost every entry on Mr. Ma’s fee bill is spread out over multiple days, even months in some cases. (Wu Decl., Ex. A to Ex. 1.) Each entry describes multiple tasks lumped together. (See Id.) Also, all of the time entries are exactly one hour or a multiple of one hour, and contain no fractions of an hour, which the Court does not find credible. (See Id.) Moreover, Mr. Ma appears to bill $1,600.00 for services related to the unlimited civil action. (Wu Decl., Ex. A to Ex. 1, 9/26/22 -10/5/22 entries of $1,200.00 and $400.00). Thus, the Court reduces Mr. Ma’s bill by $1,600.00. Further, as a result of the block billing, the Court exercises its discretion to further reduce Mr. Ma’s fee bill of $10,400.00 by 50% to $5,200.00. 

            As for the other entries from Plaintiff’s current counsel, the Court agrees there appears to be some overlap between the November 4, 2022 entry and the November 10, 2022 entry regarding Mr. Wu’s review of the prior motion for summary judgment. (Wu Decl., Ex. 2, p. 13 of pdf.) But, this does not necessarily mean the tasks were duplicative. The Court also finds Plaintiff’s counsel has shown where certain matters involved both this unlawful detainer action and the related civil action, Plaintiff’s counsel split the billing 50/50, so there is no improper billing between the two cases. (Wu Decl., ¶ 6.) The Court therefore declines to strike or reduce the other entries from Plaintiff’s current counsel and finds the attorney’s fees of $40,461.39 to be reasonable. 

            In addition, the Court will award 2 hours for the attorney’s preparation of this motion and 1 hour for the paralegal, and another 2 hours for the attorney’s review of the opposition and preparation of the reply. With the 15% discount that comes to $1,959.25. Plaintiff can include the motion filing fees in its memorandum of costs as recoverable costs under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1033.5. 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion for attorney’s fees in the reduced amount of $47,620.64, awarding $5,200.00 to John J. Ma, Esq. and $42,420.64 to Lagerlof, LLP. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees in the reduced amount of $47,620.64. 

             Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.


Case Number: 23PSCV03776    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 6

Plaintiff Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC’s Request for Entry of Default Judgment 

Defendant: DDL Trucking, Inc. 

COURT RULING 

            Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment is GRANTED.           

BACKGROUND

This is a breach of contract case. On December 6, 2023, plaintiff Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendant DDL Trucking Inc. (DDL), Dengdi Lan aka Deng Di Lan (Lan), and Does 1 through 100, alleging causes of action for breach of written agreement, breach of guaranty, account stated, unjust enrichment, and open book account. On February 1, 2024, default was entered against DDL. On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed a default judgment package against DDL. On July 1, 2024, the Court entered summary adjudication as to the second cause of action for breach of guaranty against Lan.           

LEGAL STANDARD 

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Code Civ. Proc., § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.) 

ANALYSIS 

Plaintiff seeks default judgment against Defendant DDL in the total amount of $75,062.14, including $68,990.01 in damages and $6,072.13 in interest. The Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to the default judgment. However, the Court notes that although summary adjudication was granted in Plaintiff’s favor and against Defendant Lan with respect to the second cause of action for breach of guaranty, Plaintiff has not yet dismissed the remaining causes of action against Lan and submitted a proposed judgment against Lan. 

CONCLUSION           

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment is GRANTED.


Case Number: 24PSCV00137    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Manuel Fernandez, trustee of the Pena Family Revocable Trust dated November 9, 2022 v. Shmuel Y. Mahgerefteh, et al. 

1 – Defendant Mahgerefteh’s Motion for Order Establishing Admissions and for Sanctions in the Amount of $2,475.00 Against Plaintiff;

2 – Defendant Mahgerefteh’s Motion to Compel Responses to Interrogatories and for Sanctions in the Amount of $585.00 Against Plaintiff; and

3 – Defendant Mahgerefteh’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents and for Sanctions in the Amount of $585.00 Against Plaintiff 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Mahgerefteh’s motion for order establishing admissions. The Court further GRANTS the request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $585.00 against Plaintiff. Assuming the filing fee gets paid, Plaintiff must provide verified responses, without objection, to Special Interrogatories (Set One), within 30 days of the Court’s order. 

Defendant Mahgerefteh is ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a real property loan dispute. On January 12, 2024, plaintiff Manuel Fernandez, trustee of the Pena Family Revocable Trust dated November 9, 2022 (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants Shmuel Y. Mahgerefteh (Mahgerefteh), Law Offices of Richard G. Witkin APC (collectively, Defendants) and Does 1 through, alleging causes of action for declaratory relief, unfair and unlawful business practice, injunctive relief, cancellation of instruments, rescission based on unconscionability, and accounting. 

On July 2, 2024, Defendant Mahgerefteh moved to deem requests for admissions admitted. On July 25, 2024, Mahgerefteh moved to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents. The motions are unopposed. 

LEGAL STANDARD

When a party fails to serve a timely response to interrogatories, the party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling a response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Id., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) “The court shall impose a monetary sanction… against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Id., § 2030.290, subd. (c).)
 

When a party fails to serve a timely response to an inspection demand, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling a response to the inspection demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) “[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction… against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Id., § 2031.300, subd. (c).)
 

When a party fails to serve a timely response to a request for admission, the party propounding the request for admission may move for an order to deem the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction… on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) 

DISCUSSION 

Meet and Confer 

Although meeting and conferring is not required before bringing motions to compel, (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404), the Court appreciates Mahgerefteh’s efforts to meet and confer, (Ramos Decls., ¶ 9). 

            Analysis 

            Defendant Mahgerefteh served Requests for Admissions (Set One), Request for Production of Documents (Set One), Form Interrogatories (Set One), and Special Interrogatories (Set One) on Plaintiff on March 7, 2024. (Ramos Decls., ¶ 3, Ex. A.) Despite having granted extensions to respond, Plaintiff failed to provide responses. (Ramos Decls., ¶¶ 5-10.) The Court finds Mahgerefteh’s motions to be well taken. However, the Court notes that Mahgerefteh’s motion to compel responses to interrogatories combines both Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories into one motion. Each set of discovery requests requires its own motion and a separate filing fee. (See Govt. Code, § 70617, subds. (a), (f); Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.010 et seq., 2031.010, et seq., 2033.010, et seq.) Therefore, the Court will GRANT the motions as to Requests for Admissions (Set One), Request for Production of Documents (Set One), and Form Interrogatories (Set One). The Court will later grant the motion as to Special Interrogatories (Set One) once Mahgerefteh pays the necessary filing fee; otherwise, the motion will be denied without prejudice as to Special Interrogatories (Set One). 

            With respect to monetary sanctions, the Court GRANTS Mahgerefteh’s request as to Requests for Admission (Set One) in the reduced amount of $900.00, comprised of 1.0 hour preparing the motion and 1.0 hour appearing at the hearing on the motion, for a total or 2.0 hours, multiplied by the hourly rate of $450.00. The Court further GRANTS Mahgerefteh’s requests as to Request for Production of Documents (Set One) and Form Interrogatories (Set One) in the requested amount of $585.00 for each motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Mahgerefteh’s motion for order establishing admissions. The Court hereby deems the truth of the matters specified in Requests for Admission (Set One) admitted and conclusively established as to Plaintiff. The Court further GRANTS Defendant Mahgerefteh’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $900.00 against Plaintiff. Plaintiff must pay said monetary sanctions to counsel for Defendant Mahgerefteh within 20 days of the Court’s order. 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Mahgerefteh’s motion to compel responses to Request for Production of Documents. Plaintiff must serve verified responses, without objections, and all responsive documents on counsel for Defendant Mahgerefteh within 20 days of the Court’s order. The Court further GRANTS Defendant Mahgerefteh’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $585.00 against Plaintiff. Plaintiff must pay said monetary sanctions to counsel for Defendant Mahgerefteh within 20 days of the Court’s order. 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Mahgerefteh’s motion to compel responses to interrogatories, but only as to Form Interrogatories (Set One). Plaintiff must serve verified responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One), without objection, on counsel for Defendant Mahgerefteh within 20 days of the Court’s order. The Court further GRANTS the request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $585.00 against Plaintiff. Plaintiff must pay said monetary sanctions to counsel for Defendant Mahgerefteh within 20 days of the Court’s order. 

The Court will grant the motion as to Special Interrogatories (Set One), but on the condition that Defendant Mahgerefteh pays the additional motion filing fee for the Special Interrogatories within five days of the Court’s order. Otherwise, the motion will be denied without prejudice as to Special Interrogatories (Set One). Assuming the filing fee gets paid, Plaintiff must provide verified responses, without objection, to Special Interrogatories (Set One), within 30 days of the Court’s order. 

Defendant Mahgerefteh is ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days.
