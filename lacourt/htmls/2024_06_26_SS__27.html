DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV40558    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTIONS FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 6/26/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV40558 ASHLEY OELSEN vs KATHERINE UNVERFERTH, M.D. et al.

Moving Parties: Defendants Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center (Regents), Nataly Petrovic (Cohen), and Dr. Katherine Unverferth

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:

 

NATALY PETROVIC’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT¿IS GRANTED

 

DR. KATHERINE UNVERFERTH’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED

 

REGENTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION IS DENIED

 

 

 

Background

On November 3, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present case against several defendants, including Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center (hereinafter referred to as “Regents”), Nataly Petrovic (Cohen), and Dr. Katherine Unverferth, for medical malpractice and false imprisonment. All defendants have since been dismissed from the case except for Regents, Nataly Petrovic (Cohen), and Dr. Katherine Unverferth. Plaintiff alleges in the Complaint that her mental health conditions related to her pregnancy were treated by Dr. Unverferth and Ms. Cohen from July 10, 2020, to July 29, 2020, but she was discharged from their care afterwards.

On August 11, 2020, Plaintiff visited the emergency department at UCLA Medical Center, where she was evaluated by psychiatric resident Dr. Erika Hsu under the consultation of attending psychiatrist Dr. Katherine Marder. Dr. Hsu allegedly placed Plaintiff on a 72-hour hold (a “5150 hold”) without probable cause, during which Plaintiff claims she was strapped to a hospital bed and restrained from seeking help.

Defendants Regents, Nataly Petrovic (Cohen) (hereinafter referred to singularly as “Defendant Cohen”) and Dr. Unverferth (collectively referred to as “Defendants”) filed motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication.  Defendants argue their conduct did not fall below the applicable standard of care.  Defendant Cohen and Dr. Unverferth both also argue that they were not involved in the 5150 hold decision and that, in any event, their care of Plaintiff had ended at the time of the hold.  Regents also claims immunity under the Welfare & Institutions Code.

Judicial Notice

Plaintiff’s request to take judicial notice of Title 22, California Code of Regulations § 71545 taken from Barclay's Official California Code of Regulations is granted. Evidence Code §452(b)

Legal Standard 

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) 

  

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

  

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

  

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

  

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. [Citation.] Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.  If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true. [Citation.] Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

Immunity under Welfare & Institutions Code

Welfare & Institutions Code section 52781 is a provision under the Lanterman-Petris-Short Act (section 5000 et seq.) (the “LPS Act”), which the Legislature enacted in 1967 “to govern the involuntary commitment of mentally disordered persons.” (Julian v. Mission Community Hospital (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 360, 374.) “One of the purposes of the Act is to provide ‘prompt evaluation and treatment of persons with mental health disorders or impaired by chronic alcoholism . . . To achieve this purpose, a number of [the] Act['s] provisions allow a person to be removed from the general population in order to be civilly committed based on a probable cause determination made by a mental health or law enforcement professional, and then to challenge the civil commitment within a reasonable time afterwards.” (Id. at 374-75.) “Section 5150 . . . allows law enforcement officers and various medical professionals to bring an individual to an appropriate facility for assessment, evaluation, and treatment for up to 72 hours where there is ‘probable cause to believe that the person is, as a result of mental disorder, a danger to others, or to himself or herself, or gravely disabled.” (Id. at 375.) “Section 5278 provides immunity to individuals who exercise this authority in accordance with the law. This immunity ‘allows individuals authorized to detain a person for 72-hour treatment and evaluation to make that decision without fear of exposure to criminal or civil liability.” (Id. at 376; see Cruze v. National Psychiatric Services, Inc. (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 48, 56 (section 5278 applies to individuals and entities).)

“The immunity under section 5278 is not absolute. In enacting the statute, the Legislature ‘intended to provide immunity for claims based on conduct that is expressly authorized by the [Act] but would otherwise constitute a civil or criminal wrong.’ [Citation.] Thus, ‘the scope of section 5278 immunity extends to claims based on facts that are inherent in an involuntary detention pursuant to section 5150. If there is probable cause for the detention, the statute therefore provides immunity for the decision to detain as well as for the detention and its inherent attributes, including the fact that the patient must necessarily be evaluated and treated without consent. These are all inherent aspects of the statutory scheme and thus cannot provide the basis for a civil suit.’ [Citation.] The ‘protected conduct,’ however, ‘is confined to the exercise of statutory authority to detain, evaluate and treat against the patient's wishes, and does not extend to the manner in which evaluation and treatment are carried out.’ [Citation.] Thus, immunity under section 5278 does not extend to ‘negligent acts, intentional torts, or criminal wrongs committed during the course of the detention, evaluation, or treatment.” (Julian, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th at 393.)

A probable cause determination is required before determining whether the immunity under section 5278 applies. (Heater v. Southwood Psychiatric Center (1996) 42 Cal. App.4th 1068, 1071, 1081-1058 (affirming trial court dismissal of the plaintiff’s complaint based on findings following evidentiary hearing under Evidence Code section 402 that defendants had probable cause for detention and thus were immune under section 5278 and immunity extended to all causes of action for false imprisonment, assault and battery, and medical malpractice).)

Discussion

1.  Defendant Cohen

Evidence provided by Defendant Cohen showed that she was not involved in the decision to place Plaintiff on the 72-hour hold or in Plaintiff's treatment during the hold. Defendant Cohen's interaction with Plaintiff was limited in scope and time, such as evaluating Plaintiff to determine her eligibility for the Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP) at UCLA. (Chueh Decl. ¶ 10.) Further, expert testimony opined that the medical care that Defendant Cohen provided to Plaintiff did not fall below the applicable standard of care. (Chueh ¶ 13.)

Plaintiff did not provide any countervailing evidence showing Defendant Cohen’s involvement in the decision to place Plaintiff on hold or in Plaintiff’s treatment during the hold. Therefore, no triable issue of fact was raised regarding Plaintiff’s false imprisonment cause of action against Defendant Cohen.

        Moreover, Dr. Bassanelli, Plaintiff’s expert, did not opine that Defendant Cohen’s conduct fell below the standard of care. His opinion is limited to whether there was probable cause for the 72- hour hold (Bassanelli Decl. ¶ 17) and whether Dr. Hsu and Dr. Marder’s conduct fell below the standard of care. (Decl. ¶ 18.) Thus, Defendant Nataly Cohen’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

Dr. Unverferth

Dr. Unverferth filed a separate motion for summary judgment against Plaintiff. Dr. Unverferth demonstrated that she did not make the decision to place Plaintiff on a 72-hour hold nor was she involved in Plaintiff’s treatment during the hold. Additionally, Dr. Unverferth introduced expert opinion that her conduct met the applicable standard of care (Chueh ¶ 21). Dr. Unverferth has met her initial burden.

Plaintiff’s separate statement omitted the portion relating to false imprisonment, and page 4 of the separate statement is missing. Plaintiff also did not provide counter expert testimony regarding whether Dr. Unverferth’s conduct fell below the applicable standard of care. In a medical malpractice case, expert testimony is usually required. (Flowers v. Torrance Memorial Hospital Medical Center (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 992, 1001). The expert testimony cited by Plaintiff in the opposition pertains only to the probable cause for the hold and the conduct of Dr. Hsu and Dr. Marder (Bassanelli Decl. ¶ 17-18).

Plaintiff’s additional undisputed material facts in the separate statement and the evidence presented do not show that Dr. Unverferth recommended or directed Dr. Hsu to place Plaintiff on a 72-hour hold. At the time of the incident, Plaintiff was no longer under Dr. Unverferth’s care. Plaintiff’s evidence showed only that Dr. Hsu consulted with Dr. Unverferth, and Dr. Unverferth provided Dr. Hsu with her findings regarding Plaintiff's condition while under her care. The evidence does not indicate that Defendant Unverferth directed or recommended Dr. Hsu to place Plaintiff on a 72-hour hold. Plaintiff’s own expert stated, “Unverferth was clear in her deposition that she did not expect Resident Hsu to rely on her input in determining whether there was probable cause to issue a 5150.” (Bassanelli Decl. ¶ 8). Furthermore, Plaintiff has not provided any legal arguments showing that providing prior patient findings to another doctor could result in liability for false imprisonment or medical malpractice.

Plaintiff also contends that Dr. Unverferth provided false information but failed to specify what information provided by Defendant was false. This argument is also not raised in the opposition, which appears to be a duplicate of the opposition to Regents' MSJ. However, Dr. Unverferth’s MSJ differs from Regents’ MSJ because Dr. Unverferth did not admit Plaintiff for the psychiatric hold. Furthermore, Plaintiff’s evidence does not support the contention that Dr. Unverferth provided false information. Although there is testimony from Dr. Unverferth indicating she requested certain information be removed from Plaintiff’s 8/11/20 consultation notes (Unverferth Depo 91:25-94:10), this does not substantiate the claim that she provided false information. Additionally, Plaintiff failed to provide expert testimony on whether other aspects of Dr. Unverferth’s treatment fell below the standard of care. Thus, Dr. Unverferth’s motion for summary judgment as to both medical malpractice and false imprisonment is GRANTED.

UCLA Medical Center (Regents)

Regents essentially makes three arguments in support of its motion for summary judgment: 1) Regents' decision to place Plaintiff on hold is entitled to immunity under Welfare and Institutions Code section 5278; 2) the medical care rendered by Regents complied with the applicable standard of care; 3) Regents’ restraint or confinement of Plaintiff was with lawful privilege pursuant to Welfare and Institutions Code.

Based on the evidence presented, including the expert opinion of Dr. Chueh, Regents has met its initial burden in demonstrating probable cause for Plaintiff’s hold. However, establishing probable cause is merely the initial step in asserting immunity under section 5278. “The statute provides immunity for the decision to detain as well as for the detention and its inherent attributes, including the fact that the patient must necessarily be evaluated and treated without consent. These are all inherent aspects of the statutory scheme and thus cannot provide the basis for a civil suit. However, the immunity does not extend to other negligent acts, intentional torts, or criminal wrongs committed during the course of the detention, evaluation, or treatment.” (Jacobs v. Grossmont Hospital, (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 69, 78-79.)

The complaint alleges that Plaintiff was strapped to a hospital bed in an office/lounge setting, restrained in such a manner that she could not move or call for help, while Defendant’s personnel walked by, observing her inappropriately. (Complaint ¶23). Regents appears to have overlooked these allegations in its moving papers and separate statement and has failed to demonstrate that such treatment did not occur, is inherent to the detention, or that such actions were not negligent or intentional torts committed during the course of detention. Thus, Defendant Regents has failed to meet its initial burden in asserting immunity under section 5278 of the Welfare and Institutions Code.

Furthermore, Regents' three arguments that Regents is immune under section 5278, the medical care rendered by Regents complied with the applicable standard of care, and Regents restrained Plaintiff with lawful privilege all depend on whether there was probable cause for the hold. The parties presented expert declarations that reached differing conclusions on the issues of probable cause and medical malpractice. (Compare Chueh ¶ 13 and Bassanelli Decl. ¶ 17-18). Plaintiff’s expert opined that there was no probable cause for the hold (Bassanelli Decl. ¶ 17) and that Dr. Hsu and Dr. Marder, both employees of Regents, engaged in conduct that fell below the standard of care (Bassanelli Decl. ¶ 18). Defendants did not file a reply or raise any evidentiary objections. Thus, Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact as to Regents’ medical malpractice claim and false imprisonment claim and Regent’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL  

Hearing Date: 6/26/2024 at 1:30 p.m.  

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV18845 DAVID SEIF, AN INDIVIDUAL vs JOHN DOE.  

Moving Party: Plaintiff’s Counsel Daniel Moossai  

Responding Party: Unopposed  

Notice: Sufficient  

  

Ruling: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL IS GRANTED. 

  

Background 

 

Attorney Daniel Moossai represents Plaintiff. Moosai moves to be relieved as counsel, citing an irremediable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. No opposition has been filed. 

 

Legal Standard¿ 

¿ 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.) 

¿ 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).) 

¿ 

Analysis and Conclusion¿ 

¿ 

Moosai has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Moosai seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).)  

  

The Court notes that the next hearing is an OSC Re: Dismissal set for 9/19/24, which should be sufficient time for Plaintiff to retain new counsel. Alternatively, if Plaintiff or new counsel needs additional time to prepare for trial, sufficient time exists for Plaintiff to take action to seek a continuance of the trial. Thus, the present motion to be relieved as counsel is GRANTED effective upon service on Plaintiff.

Case Number: 22STCV32356    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 6/25/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV32356 LANI MILLER vs JOANNA DEFIRMIAN et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Judith Defirmian

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED

 

Background

On October 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present complaint. Plaintiff alleges that on July 9, 2022, Defendant Defirmian held a party for Defendant Davis, Defirmian’s daughter, in the common area of her condominium complex. Alcohol was served at the party, and Defendant Davis attacked Plaintiff while Plaintiff was filming the party for alleged violations of HOA policies. The only cause of action against Defendant Defirmian is for premises liability. Defendant now moves the Court for summary judgment, arguing that she was not aware of any prior similar incidents involving Defendant Davis and that the alleged attack at issue was not foreseeable.

Preliminary issues

The Court agrees with Plaintiff’s contention that Defendant’s separate statement should have included more details and been broken down further. As it stands, the allegations in the separate statement read more like legal conclusions than material facts. However, Defendant’s counsel presents facts supporting the motion for summary judgment in his declaration and cites evidence for each fact. (See Sheedy Decl.) Furthermore, the pertinent evidence submitted consists only of the depositions of Defirmian and Schulick, making it not too laborious to identify the supporting evidence. The Court will hear the motion on its merits.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice. Defendant’s objection No. 1-5, and 10 are overruled. The Parties did not make any other objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)

Legal Standard 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) 

        “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

        To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

        The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

Foreseeability

While the occupier of land has a general duty to exercise ordinary care for the safety of persons who come upon the property, he is not an insurer of their safety (Taylor v. Centennial Bowl, Inc. (1966) 65 Cal.2d 114, 121.) "Where ... it is the conduct of a third party on the premises which directly causes the injury, liability may attach only where the possessor of the premises has reasonable cause to anticipate such conduct and the probability of resulting injury, and fails to take affirmative steps to control the wrongful conduct.” (Rogers v. Jones (1976) 56 Cal.App.3d 346, 351.) Foreseeability should be assessed in light of the totality of the circumstances, including such factors as the nature, condition, and location of the premises. (Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal. 4th 666 670.) It is based on whether something is reasonably likely to occur, and that a reasonably thoughtful person would guide his or her conduct by it. (Bigbee v. Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. (1983) 34 Cal. 3d 49, 57.) Defendant is tested by what he/she knows, not what the defendant could have or should have learned. (Margaret W. v. Kelley R. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 141, 158.)

Initial burden

Defendant has met her initial burden. Defendant Defirmian testified that she held birthday parties for her daughter, Defendant Davis, almost every year for more than twenty years without issue. (Def’s Ex. B, Defirmian Depo.16:23-17:1, 82:15-16.) Mr. Schick, former president of the HOA for the condo, testified that he was not aware of not did he witness any altercations involving Defendant Davis prior to the subject incident. (Def’s Ex. C, Schick Depo. 88:4-89:6.) Defendant Defirmian testified that prior to 2022, she was not aware of her daughter having any problems with residents at the complex. She was not aware of incidents of her daughter chasing other residents, yelling at other residents, or any harassment or threats involving Davis and other residents prior to the incident. (Defirmian Depo. 65:21-67:7.) Defirmian is also not aware of any issues her daughter has with alcohol and that her personality does not change when she drinks alcohol. (Defirmian Depo. 71:18-74:7.) Defendant has met her burden.

Triable issue of fact

In raising a triable issue of fact, Plaintiff introduced the declaration of Sheri Magid, Defirmian’s neighbor, who stated that this was not the first incident involving Davis and altercations with other residents. Davis previously had altercations with Magid and another neighbor, Periman, where Davis swore and threatened the neighbors, resulting in a police report being filed. (Plf’s Ex.H.)

Davis herself testified that she had three prior arguments with other residents in the complex. (Davis Depo., p. 45:21-46:2, Plf’s Ex. L.) However, Davis only informed her mother of one altercation with another resident, which related to an incident where a neighbor came to Defirmian’s garage and videotaped the workers remodeling the house while cussing at them and screaming about permits. Davis asked the neighbor to leave. (Davis Depo., p. 50:11-51:15.)

Plaintiff then presented evidence that Defirmian was aware of Davis’ angry dispositions. Mark Strong, Defendant Davis’ stepfather, declared that Davis told him and Defirmian that she is a crack cocaine user. (Strong Decl.¶4, Ex. K.) Strong also declared that in Defirmian’s presence, Davis acted erratically, having fits of anger, and was easily provoked. (Strong Decl. ¶6.)

As for Davis’ alcohol consumption during the incident, Plaintiff Davis testified that she had one sip of wine and nothing else. She only drank tea and coffee prior to that. (Davis Depo., p. 75:1-17, 76:3-10.) Plaintiff’s evidence does not show whether drinking affected Davis’s personality or aggression or if Plaintiff drank more alcohol. Although Plaintiff introduced evidence that Defendant might have used cocaine, no evidence was presented regarding any issues related to alcohol.

Plaintiff presented evidence disputing the frequency of birthday parties hosted in the common area (Strong Decl., ¶8: (Defendant did not throw birthday parties every year)). However, there is no dispute that prior birthday parties were held, and no evidence was presented indicating any issues such as altercations, fights, or arguments at those parties.

Discussion

Plaintiff’s evidence shows that Defendant Defirmian knew of one prior incident where Davis told a neighbor to leave. No evidence was presented showing that Defirmian was aware of Davis shouting, swearing, or fighting with the resident beyond asking the neighbor to leave for that one event. “Foreseeability is tested by what the defendant knows, not what the defendant could have or should have learned.” (Margaret W. v. Kelley R. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 141, 158.) Beyond this one incident, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that Defirmian was aware of any other altercation between Davis and other residents.

Second, Plaintiff presented evidence showing that Defirmian was aware her daughter used crack cocaine and had a "hot head" personality and was prone to anger. However, the connection between being prone to anger and assaulting a resident at the condo’s common areas during her birthday party is tenuous and not reasonably foreseeable. As the court in Williams v. Fremont Corners, Inc. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 654, 671-72, held, knowing there is a general potential for rowdy or troublesome conduct by bar patrons does not make the category of aggressive parking lot assaults reasonably foreseeable. The present case involves similar categories.

Moreover, although Plaintiff disputes whether a birthday party was held every year, she does not dispute that prior parties were held at the same location without incident.

Finally, Plaintiff’s contention that Defendant had alcohol at the common area in violation of HOA policies is immaterial to the assault at issue, as Davis only had one sip of red wine prior to the incident, and Plaintiff did not present evidence to counter Defendant’s claim that Davis does not have any issue with alcohol or that her personality changes with alcohol.

Simply, this case involves a mother holding a birthday party for her daughter at the common area of her condo complex. Plaintiff failed to present evidence of Defirmian’s prior knowledge of Davis’s heated or violent altercations with neighbors or prior incidents or problems related to Defendant's birthday parties hosted at the common area. There is also a lack of any specific connection to the incident in question, except a general awareness of Davis’s hotheadedness, cocaine usage, and easy to anger personality. The Court does not find that Davis’ potential for anger or outbursts made the assault reasonably foreseeable. Thus, summary judgment is granted. Since summary judgment is granted to the only cause of action against Defendant Defirmian for premise liability, summary judgment is also granted for punitive damages.
