DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22PSCV00257    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  City of El Monte v. Lino James Ibarra, et al. 

City of Covina’s Motion for Leave to Intervene 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS City of Covina’s motion for leave to intervene as a matter of right. City of Covina must file the proposed complaint in intervention within five days of the Court’s order and properly serve it thereafter. 

City of Covina is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling and file proof of service of same within five days of the Court’s order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a nuisance action relating to alleged illegal sales of cannabis. On March 16, 2022, plaintiff City of El Monte (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants Lino James Ibarra (Ibarra), Michael Atencio, Manuel Aquino, Joseph Hernandez, Jung Hsiang Chen, Ya Fen Hsu, Tucker Lincoln (Lincoln), Philip Gonzales, Shane Sapp, H.C. Rue Royale Partner LLC (Rue Royale) (collectively, Defendants) and Does 1 through 100. On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint against the same Defendants, alleging causes of action for narcotics abatement (Health and Safety Code section 11570 et seq.), public nuisance (Civil Code section 3479 et seq.), violation of El Monte Municipal Code (EMMC Chapters 5.18 and Ch. 1.19), and violation of MAUCRSA (Business and Professions Code section 26000 et seq.) 

On May 1, 2024, nonparty City of Covina (Covina) moved for leave to intervene. On May 15, 2024, Lincoln opposed the motion on limited grounds. The other Defendants did not oppose the motion. On May 21, 2024, Covina replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

“An intervention takes place when a nonparty, deemed an intervenor, becomes a party to an action or proceeding between other persons by doing any of the following: (1) Joining a plaintiff in claiming what is sought by the complaint. (2) Uniting with a defendant in resisting the claims of a plaintiff….” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (b)(1), (2).) To intervene in an action, “[a] nonparty shall petition the court for leave to intervene by noticed motion or ex parte application.” (Id., § 387, subd. (c).) 

A nonparty must be permitted to intervene if either of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) a provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene; or (2) the person seeking intervention claims an interest related to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person’s ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties. (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(1)(A)-(B).) A court has discretionary authority to allow a nonparty to intervene “if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.” (Id., § 387, subd. (d)(2).) 

DISCUSSION 

Summary of Arguments 

Covina contends it has a right to intervene here because its foundational police power and authority to enjoin unlawful activity at the subject property, i.e., 558 East Rue Royale Street, Covina, CA 91723 (the Property), within its jurisdiction constitutes sufficient interest relating to the property to warrant intervention by right. Covina contends the existing parties do not adequately represent its interest in enforcing the law with respect to nuisance within its own boundaries, and that even if Plaintiff prevails against Rue Royale, Plaintiff may not have a sufficiently compelling interest in enforcing that resolution. Covina also contends its interest are not adverse to Plaintiff’s and are otherwise generally aligned, but not coextensive or equal. Covina then contends its motion is timely because this case is still in the pleading stage. 

Covina alternatively argues that if it is not entitled to intervene as a matter of right, the Court should grant permissive intervention. Covina contends it has a direct and immediate interest in this matter given its authority to address the unlawful maintenance and use of properties within its boundaries and its duty to abate nuisances for the benefit of the public. Covina contends that if intervention is denied, Plaintiff and Rue Royale may arrive at a resolution contrary to Covina’s interests. Covina then contends its intervention will add no new legal issues since Covina only wishes to prosecute the same claims Plaintiff asserts against Rue Royale, and does not seek to allege new claims against any party. Covina anticipates that Lincoln will oppose the motion, but Covina indicates that the proposed complaint in intervention states no claim against Lincoln. Covina further contends it has followed the proper procedures. 

In opposition, Lincoln contends Covina cannot be substituted in for Plaintiff. Lincoln contends Covina has not established the elements for intervention as a matter of right, arguing that Covina does not cite a statute conferring an unconditional right to intervene and that Covina cannot claim an interest relating to the property as Covina does not own or have a legal interest in the Property. Lincoln contends Covina has not explained how the disposition of Plaintiff’s case would impede Covina from protecting any interest because neither of them have an interest in the Property and Covina’s description of any such impairment is inadequately described. Lincoln also contends Covina has not established the timeliness of this intervention, as timeliness is not based on the stage of the pleadings, but instead is measured from the time Covina knew or should have known its interests in the litigation were not being adequately represented. 

With respect to permissive intervention, Lincoln does not oppose a permissive intervention based on Covina’s representation that it does not seek to intervene to join as a plaintiff against Lincoln. Lincoln further requests that the Court order such intervention be limited to Rue Royale and the cause of action set forth in the proposed complaint in intervention, and that any expansion of the causes of action or substitution of Doe defendants only be allowed upon leave of the Court. 

Analysis – Intervention as a Matter of Right 

“[T]o establish a right to mandatory intervention, the nonparty must: (1) show a protectable interest in the subject of the action, (2) demonstrate that the disposition of the action may impair or impede its ability to protect that interest; and (3) demonstrate that its interests are not adequately represented by the existing parties. [Citation.]” (Carlsbad Police Officers Ass'n v. City of Carlsbad (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 135, 148.) 
 The Court finds that Covina is entitled to intervention as a matter of right. The Property is located within the boundaries of the City of Covina, thereby entitling Covina to address nuisances occurring within its boundaries. “If there is reason to believe that a nuisance, as described in Section 11570, is kept, maintained, or exists in any county, the district attorney or county counsel of the county, or the city attorney of any incorporated city or of any city and county, in the name of the people, may, or any citizen of the state resident in the county, in his or her own name, may, maintain an action to abate and prevent the nuisance and to perpetually enjoin the person conducting or maintaining it, and the owner, lessee, or agent of the building or place in or upon which the nuisance exists from directly or indirectly maintaining or permitting the nuisance.” (Health & Safety Code, § 11571; see also Cal. Const., art. XI, § 7 [“A county or city may make and enforce within its limits all local, police, sanitary, and other ordinances and regulations not in conflict with general laws.”]) 

Moreover, the language of Code of Civil Procedure section 387, subdivision (d)(1)(B), provides that a party has a right to intervene when that party claims an interest “relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(1)(B), italics added.) Covina’s proposed complaint in intervention seeks solely to address the alleged nuisance at the Property. (Sanchez Decl., Ex. A, ¶ 3.) The Court finds the language of Code of Civil Procedure section 387, subdivision (d)(1)(B) sufficiently broad to confer upon Covina the right to intervene and address the nuisance at the Property; thus, it is irrelevant whether Covina owns the Property. 

Furthermore, it is clear that Plaintiff’s and Covina’s interests here are not one and the same, as they are different municipalities governing different jurisdictions. While their interests may align to some extent in this particular action, i.e., regulating illegal drug activity, that is not sufficient for Plaintiff to adequately represent and ensure protection of Covina’s interests in this action. (See Carlsbad Police Officers Ass'n v. City of Carlsbad, supra, 49 Cal.App.5th at p. 148.) 

Finally, while the Court agrees with Lincoln that Covina did not provide any indication as to when it first knew its rights were not being adequately represented, the Court exercises its discretion and finds Covina’s motion is timely given this action is still only in the pleading stage and the case is not yet at issue. (See Lofton v. Wells Fargo Home Mortgage (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 1001, 1013; Lohnes v. Astron Computer Products (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1153.) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Covina is entitled to intervention as a matter of right. 

Analysis – Permissive Intervention 

“Permissive intervention is appropriate if: “(1) the proper procedures have been followed; (2) the nonparty has a direct and immediate interest in the action; (3) the intervention will not enlarge the issues in the litigation; and (4) the reasons for the intervention outweigh any opposition by the parties presently in the action. [Citations.]” (Carlsbad Police Officers Ass'n v. City of Carlsbad, supra, 49 Cal.App.5th at p. 148.) 

Even assuming for the sake of argument that Covina was not entitled to intervene as a matter of right, the Court finds Covina would be entitled to permissively intervene. Covina clearly has a direct and immediate interest in regulating the subject nuisance since it is located within the boundaries of the City of Covina, and nuisance regulation falls within Covina’s scope of authority. (SAC, ¶ 2; Sanchez Decl., Ex. A, ¶ 3; Health & Safety Code, § 11571.) Covina’s participation in this action does not appear that it will enlarge the issues in the case since it only alleges one cause of action for nuisance, which is already a cause of action in this case, regarding the same Property, and against an already named defendant, i.e., Rue Royale. (See SAC, ¶¶ 63-76; Sanchez Decl., Ex. A, ¶¶ 12-24.) Lincoln largely does not oppose intervention on permissive grounds, provided the Court imposes some limits on the intervention. (Opp., 4:2-20.) As for proper procedures, Covina followed the proper procedure by requesting permission from the Court to intervene. (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(2).) 

With respect to the Lincoln’s request for conditioning permissive intervention, the Court finds such conditions unnecessary here given the Court’s finding that Covina is entitled as a matter of right to intervene. (See Carlsbad Police Officers Ass’n v. City of Carlsbad, supra, 49 Cal.App.5th at pp. 149-150 [when intervention is as a matter of right, the court generally may not condition that intervention, even if intervention enlarges the issues in the case].) Even if this Court granted Covina permissive intervention, the Court would still find that there is nothing in the proposed complaint in intervention that implicates Lincoln’s concerns, as Covina only seeks to litigate a nuisance claim against Rue Royale, not Lincoln. (See Sanchez Decl., Ex. A, ¶¶ 3-4, 12-24.) The Court therefore declines to condition Covina’s intervention on Lincoln’s requested conditions. 

Based upon the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS City of Covina’s motion for leave to intervene as a matter of right. City of Covina must file the proposed complaint in intervention within five days of the Court’s order and properly serve it thereafter. 

City of Covina is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling and file proof of service of same within five days of the Court’s order.


Case Number: 24PSCV00137    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Manuel Fernandez, trustee of the Pena Family Revocable Trust dated November 9, 2022 v. Shmuel Y. Mahgerefteh, et al. 

Motion of Charles O. Agege to be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court DENIES the motion of Charles O. Agege to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff without prejudice. 

Counsel is ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a real property loan dispute. On January 12, 2024, plaintiff Manuel Fernandez, trustee of the Pena Family Revocable Trust dated November 9, 2022 (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants Shmuel Y. Mahgerefteh, Law Offices of Richard G. Witkin APC (collectively, Defendants) and Does 1 through 50, alleging causes of action for declaratory relief, unfair and unlawful business practice, injunctive relief, cancellation of instruments, rescission based on unconscionability, and accounting. 

On April 19, 2024, Charles O. Agege of Law Offices of Charles O. Agege, counsel for Plaintiff, moved to be relieved as counsel. The motion is unopposed. 

LEGAL STANDARD

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted, provided that there is no prejudice to the client, and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.) 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).) 

DISCUSSION 

Charles O. Agege of Law Offices of Charles O. Agege (Counsel) seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff. Counsel contends Plaintiff breached the fee agreement. (Agege Decl., ¶ 2.) 

The Court does not find Counsel provided sufficient information to determine whether the purported breach of the fee agreement is material and whether Counsel gave Plaintiff a reasonable warning of intent to withdraw unless Plaintiff fulfilled the fee agreement or performed his obligations, as is required under Rule 1.16, subdivision (b)(5), of the California Rules of Professional Conduct. (Cal. Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16, subd. (b)(5).) The Court does not expect Counsel to provide any more detail than is necessary so as to preserve duty of confidentiality owed to Plaintiff, but simply claiming Plaintiff breached the fee agreement is insufficient.  (See Id.; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (c).) 

The Court also notes that Counsel’s supporting declaration and proposed order do not identify the time and location of the next hearings in this matter.  (See Agege Decl., ¶ 4, subd. (b); Proposed Order, ¶ 7, subd. (a).) Moreover, the Court finds Counsel’s declaration does not set forth sufficient information to determine whether Counsel adequately confirmed Plaintiff’s last known address within 30 days of filing of the motion. “Communications between attorney and client” is too vague. (Agege Decl., ¶ 3, subd. (b)(1)(d).) Additionally, the Court notes that the Notice omits part of the name of Plaintiff’s trust, i.e., the words “dated November 9, 2022.” (See Notice; Proposed Order, ¶ 1.) 

Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion without prejudice. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES the motion of Charles O. Agege to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff without prejudice. 

Counsel is ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days.
