DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV17991    Hearing Date: July 5, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Bifurcate Trial Re: Issue of Liability and/or Damages

Moving Parties: Defendant Cal-Quake Construction, Inc. and Salvatore Martinez

Responding Party: Plaintiff Nanette Price

The court considered the moving, and opposition papers.

BACKGROUND

On May 12, 2020, Plaintiff Nanette Price (Plaintiff) initiated the action in this matter. 

On December 20, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint against Defendants Cal-Quake Construction, Inc., Salvatore Martinez, Joseph Goldberger, and John Taferner, alleging a cause of action for negligence. The complaint alleges that on November 22, 2019, Defendant Martinez, while operating a commercial truck in the course and scope of employment for Defendant Cal-Quake, struck the rear of Plaintiff’s vehicle and fled the scene.

On March 20, 2023, Defendants Cal-Quake Construction, Inc. and Martinez filed this motion to bifurcate. On March 20, 2023, Plaintiff filed an opposition. No reply has been filed.

LEGAL STANDARD

CCP § 598 states: 
 
The court may, when the convenience of witnesses or the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order… that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case, except for defenses which may be tried first pursuant to Sections 597 and 597.5. 
 
Next, CCP § 1048(b) provides: 

The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action… or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues… 
 
Bifurcation serves to (1) promote judicial economy by avoiding introduction and consideration of unnecessary evidence; and (2) prevent the determination of liability from being tainted by the consideration of irrelevant, confusing, misleading, and prejudicial evidence related to the issue of damages. (Tulare v. Tradewell Stores, Inc. (1989) Cal.App.3d 522, 524.) 
 
Bifurcating trial may be proper to achieve the purpose of affording a more logical presentation of the evidence and simplifying the issues for the jury. (Foreman & Clark Corp. v. Fallon (1971) 3 Cal.3d 875, 888, n. 8.)  

“It is within the discretion of the court to bifurcate issues or order separate trials of actions …, and to determine the order in which those issues are to be decided.”  (Royal Surplus Lines Ins. Co., Inc. v. Ranger Ins. Co.¿(2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 193, 205.) 

DISCUSSION

Defendants move to bifurcate trial on the issue of liability and damages, arguing that liability is greatly disputed as this is a case of mistaken identity. Defendants further argue that Plaintiff’s damages claims will necessitate the testimony of a dozen retained and non-retained medical experts, witnesses in support of Plaintiff’s claim for wage loss, life care planning experts, general damages witnesses, and Plaintiff herself. Moreover, Defendants also argue that as Plaintiff’s damages claims are extensive, unique and wide-ranging, they will be prejudiced if trial is not bifurcated due to emotional reactions the jury may have to her claims, which will affect their right to an impartial determination.

The Court exercises its discretion and concludes that the issue of liability and damages should be bifurcated at trial.

The damages phase of trial should be deferred until, and only if, liability is established in Plaintiff’s favor. If there is a defense verdict, then there will be no need to have to present a case on the issue of damages. A “separate trial of the liability issue was considered desirable to avoid wasting court time in cases where the plaintiff loses on the liability issue, to promote settlements where the plaintiff wins on the liability issue, and to afford a more logical presentation of the evidence, thus simplifying the issues for the jury.” (Foreman, supra, 3 Cal.3d at 891.)  

CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ Motion to Bifurcate is GRANTED.

The Court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it finds that (1) there is an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record, (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record, (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced absent sealing, (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, and (5) no less restrictive means exists to achieve the overriding interest.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  A motion seeking an order sealing records must be accompanied by a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)

Motion for Summary Judgment

A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle them to judgment as a matter of law. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c). The moving party must make an affirmative showing that they are entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition. Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742- 743.  Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)

The moving party’s “affidavits must cite evidentiary facts, not legal conclusions, or ‘ultimate’ facts” and the courts must construe the evidence in support of the opposing party, resolving any doubts in favor of the opposing party. Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639; Scalf, 128 Cal.App.4th at 1519; Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets its initial burden. Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840. Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p).

Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.

Discussion

Defendant moves for an order granting summary judgment in its favor on the sole cause of action for negligence in Plaintiff’s FAC.  As the moving party, Defendant must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense.  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.

First Cause of Action for Negligence

The elements for negligence are: (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to the plaintiff.  County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.

             Duty

 “The first element, duty, may be imposed by law, be assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship.”  Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1128 (internal quotations omitted).

California Civil Code section 1714, subdivision (a) states, in part: “[e]veryone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care . . . .”  “As a general rule, each person has a duty to use ordinary care and is liable for injuries caused by his failure to exercise reasonable care in the circumstances.”  Romero v. Superior Court (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1080 (citation and quotations omitted.)

“The issue of whether a duty exists is a question of law to be determined by the court . . . .”  Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1620.  A party who moves for summary judgment because it did not owe a duty of care must affirmatively negate the existence of duty.  Eriksson v. Nunnink (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 826, 848-850.

Here, Defendant contends that it is entitled to summary judgment because it did not owe any duty to Plaintiff. Specifically, Defendant argues it had no duty to “control, delegate, hire, entrust, train, protect, and supervise” Plaintiff or anybody else to prevent the subject accident. Defendant’s evidence in support establishes that Plaintiff was neither an employee nor an independent contractor, and that Plaintiff only gained access to Defendant’s platform by using Hernandez’s California driver’s license and social security number to create a delivery account in Hernandez’s name. (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos. 9-18.) Further, Defendant’s evidence establishes that Defendant had no knowledge of Plaintiff since Defendant did not know at the time of the subject accident that Plaintiff had posed as Hernandez to create an account. (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos. 19.) The Court finds the foregoing evidence is sufficient to satisfy Defendant’s initial burden of negating the existence of the element of duty.  The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant owed a duty to Plaintiff.

Plaintiff attempts to raise a triable issue of material fact by arguing that a special relationship existed between Plaintiff and Postmates, thus triggering a duty. Plaintiff argues there is substantial evidence that establishes that Defendant exercises control over its “couriers,” including dictating which customers to deliver to and the locations deliveries were to be made.  (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos. 57, 59, 63.)

As a preliminary matter, the Court finds that Plaintiff does not satisfy his responsive burden because Plaintiff does not address the fact that he was not an authorized user of Defendant’s platform. Thus, even if Defendant owed a duty of ordinary care to “couriers” based on a special relationship, Plaintiff’s special relationship argument fails as Plaintiff was not a driver for Postmates.

Plaintiff then argues that Postmates allows couriers to establish subcontractors, and his account was created with Hernandez’s authorization and consent. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, UMF No. 11.)  The Court finds, however, that Plaintiff misstates the evidence.  The evidence establishes that subcontracting is permitted only between authorized users of the platform and only to persons who meet and accept all the requirements of the Fleet Agreement and complete "the process to separately receive Delivery Opportunities on the Postmates Platform." (Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, UMF No. 9.) The evidence shows that all of the qualifications were not met here. (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos.  9-10, 13, 19.)  It is undisputed that Plaintiff was not an authorized user of Defendant’s platform, that Plaintiff did not meet the qualifications for subcontracting, and that at the time of the subject incident Defendant was unaware of Plaintiff’s unauthorized use of Defendant’s platform.

Even assuming that Plaintiff was a Postmates courier, Plaintiff fails to set forth any competent evidence that Postmates would then have a duty to encourage Defendant to wear reflective clothing while on deliveries.  Rather, the undisputed evidence is that Postmates couriers are independent contractors and that Postmates does not exercise control over how couriers complete their work.  Instead, couriers are defined in the Postmates Agreement as “independent providers of delivery services” and couriers expressly agree they are not employees of Postmates and that they have “the sole right to control the manner in which Deliveries are performed and the means by which those deliveries are completed.”  (Appx. Of Evidence in Support of Defendant’s Reply, Ex. F, p. 1)

While Plaintiff goes through great lengths to detail precisely how Postmates connects couriers with customers, how deliveries are assigned, how finances are handled, and ensuring that minors don’t access alcohol and tobacco through the service, Plaintiff makes no reference whatsoever to Postmates exercising control over how couriers are to actually complete their deliveries.  (Ex. F, p. 2)  The Agreement goes on to further define the relationship with couriers:

Postmates shall have no right to, and shall not, supervise, direct or control Contractor, or control the manner or prescribe the method Contractor uses to perform Deliveries, and the general public and all governmental agencies regulating the activities contemplated by this Agreement shall be so informed. Contractor shall be solely responsible for determining the most effective, efficient, satisfactory, and safe manner to perform Deliveries, including determining the manner of pickup, delivery, and route selection.  (Ex. F, Para. 2B)

 The Agreement goes on to disclaim any authority to control the means of a delivery:

Contractor authorizes Postmates, during the course of a Delivery, to communicate with Contractor, customer, and/or Merchant to assist Contractor, to the extent permitted by Contractor, in facilitating a Delivery. However, under no circumstances shall Postmates be authorized to control the manner or means by which Contractor performs a Delivery.  (Ex. F, Para. 3E)Based on these undisputed facts, Plaintiff failed to provide evidence that would support finding that a duty arose under a special relationship under the facts set forth in Biakanja v. Irving (1958) 49 Cal.2d 647 or Brown v. USC Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204.  These cases and their progeny do not stand for the proposition of exercising control over independent contractors for the safety of the independent contractor.


Based on these undisputed facts, Plaintiff failed to provide evidence that would support finding that a duty arose under a special relationship under the facts set forth in Biakanja v. Irving (1958) 49 Cal.2d 647 or Brown v. USC Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204.  These cases and their progeny do not stand for the proposition of exercising control over independent contractors for the safety of the independent contractor.

There is a long line of cases in the construction context defining the duties of a principal to subcontractors flowing from Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689 which held that an entity that hires an independent contractor to do work generally is not liable for on-the-job injuries to the independent contractor's workers.  The Court is satisfied that it is more appropriate to analyze this case under the rules set forth under the body of law flowing from Privette as it pertains to the relationship between principals and independent contractors.

“When a person or organization hires an independent contractor, the hirer presumptively delegates to the contractor the responsibility to do the work safely. [Citations.] This presumption is grounded in two major principles: first, that independent contractors by definition ordinarily control the manner of their own work; and second, that hirers typically hire independent contractors precisely for their greater ability to perform the contracted work safely and successfully.” Sandoval v. Qualcomm Incorporated (2021) 12 Cal.5th 256, 269.

There are two exceptions to the Privette doctrine:  retained control and concealed hazard.  Retained control applies when: (1) the hirer retains control over the manner in which the contractor performs the work; (2) the hirer actually exercises its retained control by involving itself in the work such that the contractor is not entirely free to do the work in its own manner; and (3) the hirer's exercise of retained control affirmatively contributes to the worker's injury. Sandoval at 276-277.  Here, retained control does not apply because the undisputed evidence is that Postmates completely, expressly and unambiguously disclaimed any right to control or actual control over how deliveries were to be accomplished.  While Plaintiff contends that Postmates dictated the order in which deliveries should be made, this contention is not supported by the evidence.  Rather, Postmates only “suggested” delivering to the closest address first.  (Plaintiff’s Ex. 11:  Collins Depo., 69:1 – 69:10.)

The concealed hazard exception is limited to situations where the hirer is also the owner or occupier of land. Kinsman v. Unocal Corp. (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659, 675.  Here, Postmates was not the owner or occupier of the accident site.  Even if public policy compelled extending the concealed hazard exception beyond landowners/occupiers, it would still not apply here for “a ‘concealed’ hazard means something specific: a hazard that the hirer either knows or reasonably should know exists, and that the contractor does not know exists and could not reasonably discover without the hirer's disclosure.”  Sandoval, supra, at 272. 

Here, the undisputed facts are that the California Motorcycle Handbook recommends that motorcycle riders wear protective clothing with reflective materials.  (Plaintiff’s Additional Undisputed Material Facts 83 – 89.)  It is further undisputed that Plaintiff took the written test based on the California Motorcycle Handbook to obtain his permit to drive a motorcycle.  (Defendant’s Undisputed Material Fact 23.)  It is also undisputed that Defendant rode a motorcycle every day for approximately 5 – 6 years.  (Defendant’s Undisputed Material Fact 27.)  Based on this record and the reasonable inferences that can be drawn from it, there is not a triable issue of fact over whether Defendant had knowledge of a concealed fact that Plaintiff could not have reasonably discovered without Defendant’s disclosure.

Because of the findings above, the Court will not engage in an analysis of the Rowland factors.  Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108.  As Plaintiff concedes, the first step of the Rowland analysis is whether a special relationship or “some other set of circumstances” give rise to an affirmative duty to protect.  Brown, supra, 11 Cal5th at 209.  Here, there is no special relationship nor is there a set of circumstances giving rise to an affirmative duty.  As such, there is no need to conduct a Roland analysis.

Plaintiff’s Sur Reply

On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff timely filed and served its sur-reply. Plaintiff attempts to raise a triable issue of material fact in the sur-reply brief by arguing that Hernandez is Postmates’ employee or agent, who had a contractual duty to not hire or entrust unqualified persons to deliver on Postmates’ behalf.

In the sur-reply, Plaintiff argued that Defendant knew prior to filing the instant motion that Plaintiff’s theories included Hernandez negligently allowing Plaintiff to make deliveries for Defendant; and because Defendant failed to address this, the instant motion should be denied. Further, Defendant’s failure to address Plaintiff’s viable theories of liability deprived Plaintiff of his due process rights. The Court is not convinced by Plaintiff’s arguments because the Court finds that it was sufficient for Defendant to address in its motion the facts before it as alleged in Plaintiff’s FAC. Plaintiff alleged in the FAC that “Defendants owed a duty and breached that duty by, among other things, failing to adequately and properly control, delegate, hire, entrust, train, protect and supervise.”

As to whether there are triable issues of material fact that give rise to a duty, the Court finds that Plaintiff does not satisfy his responsive burden. Plaintiff argues that triable issues of fact exist about whether Hernandez is Postmates’ employee or agent and whether he negligently allowed Plaintiff to make deliveries on Postmates’ behalf. Plaintiff’s argument is based on the doctrine of respondent superior, which provides that an employer may be liable for an employee’s tortious acts committed within the scope of the employment. Plaintiff argues the ABC test, articulated in Dynamex Operations W. v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 903, applies in this case to determine if a person is an employee or independent contractor. The Court disagrees.

Dynamex applies in the context of wage orders. (See, e.g., 4 Cal.5th at 913-14, noting that “[h]ere we must decide what standard applies, under California law, in determining whether workers should be classified as employees or as independent contractors for purposes of California wage orders, which impose obligations relating to the minimum wages, maximum hours, and a limited number of very basic working conditions (such as minimally required meal and rest breaks) of California employees”)).  Plaintiff does not provide any authority holding that the ABC test should apply in the context of common law vicarious liability.  The traditional common law test articulated in Borello v. Dep’t. of Indus. Relations (1989) 48 Cal.3d 341, which has been applied to vicarious liability, is applicable here. Borello holds that “the principal test of an employment relationship is whether the person to whom service is rendered has the right to control the manner and means of accomplishing the result desired.” Id. at 351.

Here, Plaintiff failed to meet his responsive burden to show a triable issue of material fact that gives rise to a duty between Plaintiff and Postmates. The undisputed evidence before the Court is that the Fleet Agreement defines couriers as “independent providers of delivery services” and couriers expressly agree they are not employees of Postmates and that they have “the sole right to control the manner in which Deliveries are performed and the means by which those deliveries are completed.” (Declaration of Adam Feit ISO Sur-Reply ¶5; Ex. 3, Recitals and ¶2A.)

Further, the undisputed facts of this case fail to establish that Hernandez ever had any relationship with Postmates, be it as an employee or independent contractor.  To the contrary, it was Plaintiff that used the Postmates app to create the account for his own use, albeit in Hernandez’s name.  (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos.  UMF Nos. 11 – 12) 

Plaintiff argues that the Court should disregard the terms of the Fleet Agreement expressly establishing couriers as independent contractors because Postmates retains control because the Fleet Agreement provides, for example, that couriers are “required to comply with certain laws and deliver food ‘uncontaminated, free from tampering, and at the temperature or condition intended by the’ restaurant”; “required to make ‘all Deliveries in a timely, efficient, safe, and lawful manner’”; “not allowed to use other delivery platforms while using the Postmates platform when it would interfere with a courier’s ability to ‘timely, efficiently, and safely complete Deliveries’”; “required to submit to and pass background checks”; “required to maintain the minimum star rating”; and “required to cooperate with Postmates in the investigation of or response to any claim.” (Sur-reply, pp. 11-12.)

Notwithstanding the foregoing, Plaintiff fails to show how Postmates exercises control over how couriers are to actually complete deliveries. In addition, as discussed above, the Fleet Agreement disclaims any authority to control the means of delivery and defines the relationship with couriers. 

Plaintiff also argues that triable issues exists about whether Postmates negligently retained control under Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689. However, as also discussed above, “retained control does not apply because the undisputed evidence is that Postmates completely, expressly and unambiguously disclaimed any right to control or actual control over how deliveries were to be accomplished.”

Because the Court finds that Defendant met its initial burden by negating the existence of duty and Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of material fact, Defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED.


Case Number: 21STCV39402    Hearing Date: July 5, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Set Aside Dismissal

Moving Party:                   Plaintiff Heidi Putallaz

Responding Party:          None

Tentative Ruling:             Granted

BACKGROUND

                This case arises out of an automobile collision that occurred on November 2, 2019. Heidi Putallaz (Plaintiff) alleges that her vehicle was hit by Yuan Zhou’s (Defendant) vehicle while traveling on the freeway. (Complaint, ¶GN-1.) Plaintiff filed a Complaint on October 26, 2021 alleging two causes of action: (1) general negligence and (2) motor vehicle negligence.

               On December 14, 2023, the Court dismissed the case without prejudice for Plaintiff’s failure to prosecute, more specifically, for not serving the Defendant after two years pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. §583.420(a)(1). The motion before the Court now is Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal (the Motion), arguing that Plaintiff’s prior attorney was at fault for not locating and serving Defendant. No opposition is made, as it appears that Defendant has still not been located nor served. 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

               Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) provides:  "The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect....”

               Judges must vacate dismissals, default entries, and default judgments “whenever (1) an application is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, (2) the application is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, and (3) the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect in fact caused the dismissal or entry of default.”  (Benedict v. Danner Press  (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 923, 927 (citing CCP §473(b)). Also see Wagner v. Wagner (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 249, 258.)

               Where counsel seek relief alternatively under both the excusable and attorney-fault provisions of Code of Civil Procedure Section 473, and confirms that any act or omission leading to the entry of the default was done without the client's knowing participation, relief is mandatory. (Solv-All v. Sup. Ct. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 1003, 1012. Under Code of Civil Procedure Section 473, "surprise" means “‘“some condition or situation in which a party . . . is unexpectedly placed to his injury, without any default or negligence of his own, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against.”’”  State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. v. Pietak (2001) 90 Cal. App. 4th 600, 611.)

Analysis

               The Court will grant Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal because Plaintiff has demonstrated that the failure to prosecute the matter was not the client’s fault, but the fault of the prior attorney. Plaintiff’s prior counsel was Leora Sedaghati (Sedaghati). For purposes of the instant Motion, Plaintiff retained Gary Kurtz (Kurtz). Plaintiff and Kurtz allege that the reason the case was dismissed is because Sedaghati stopped communicating with Plaintiff shortly after being retained for this action, and made no effort to pursue the case. (Declaration of Heidi Putallaz, ¶3.) Plaintiff attests that she reached out to Sedaghati multiple times requesting updates on the status of the case, but was ignored, and became increasingly concerned about the lack of communication. (Id. at ¶¶3-5.)

               Plaintiff then asked a personal friend, Kurtz, to look into the case. That is when Kurtz found the case had been dismissed. (Id. at ¶4.) Kurtz himself reached out by email to Sedaghati, but Kurtz never received a response. (Declaration of Gary Kurtz, ¶¶4-5.) Kurtz then filed the instant Motion on May 22, 2024, within the six-months required by Code Civ. Proc. §473.

                Without an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, the decision to vacate is not mandatory, but well within the discretion of the trial court. (See Code Civ. Proc. §473(b).) The moving party argues that Plaintiff is in no way at fault, as she trusted her former attorney – Sedaghati – to perform as a diligent and skilled attorney should. The Court notes that the case was dismissed precisely because such diligence was lacking, as service of the summons and complaint failed to occur within two years. (See December 14, 2023 Minute Order.) However, after review of the moving papers, it is clear to the Court that this lack of diligence was no fault of Plaintiff’s but of her former attorney, Sedaghati.

               Plaintiff’s current counsel for the purposes of this Motion – Kurtz – argues that Plaintiff should have the benefit of the discretionary portion of Code Civ. Proc. §473. The Court agrees. The moving papers, which include the Declaration of both Kurtz and Plaintiff, demonstrate that Plaintiff did what a reasonable client would do in her circumstances. The Court sees no reason to punish the client for the fault of her prior attorney.            

CONCLUSION

               Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is GRANTED.

Moving Party: Defendant Neil William Smith 

Responding Party: Plaintiff Annie Marie Burks  

Tentative Ruling: GRANTED.  

BACKGROUND

This case was commenced on March 17, 2022 when Annie Marie Burks (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint alleging that Neil William Smith (Defendant) physically assaulted and battered her. The applicable proof of service was filed with the Court on August 1, 2023. The motion now before the Court is Defendant Neil Smith’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons (the Motion). Plaintiff, who is pro per, filed a “Declaration to Obtain an Order to Vacate” the Motion. As the Motion has not yet been ruled on, no such “order to vacate” could be granted at this stage, therefore, the Court views these documents as opposition papers to the instant Motion. No reply papers were filed.  

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law 

Complainants have the initial burden to evidence valid statutory service of a summons and complaint. (Dill v. Berquist Const. Co., Inc. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1439-40; Floveyor Internat. v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 59 Cal. App. 4th 789, 794.) Court findings as to conflicting proof regarding whether a summons and complaint were properly served are upheld if based upon substantial evidence. (Stafford v. Mach (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182.) 

Pursuant to CCP 418.10(a)(1), a defendant may move to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court. Without proper service, a court cannot maintain jurisdiction over the defendant and any judgment is void. California Code of Civil Procedure Sections 415.10, 415.20, 415.30, and 415.50 describe four methods of service within California: (1) personal delivery of copy of summons and complaint to person to be served; (2) delivery of a copy of summons and complaint to someone else at defendant’s usual residence or place of business; (3) service by mail coupled with acknowledgment of receipt of summons; and (4) service by publication.

ANALYSIS

The Court will grant Defendant Neil William Smith’s Motion because service was not completed properly. As aforementioned, Plaintiff here has the initial burden to evidence valid statutory service of a summons and complaint. The proof of service filed with the Court on August 1, 2023 is invalid for numerous reasons. 

First, Plaintiff asserts that the documents were delivered by personal service in part 5(a), however, no time nor date is written. (Declaration of Andrea Cook, hereinafter, Cook Decl., Exh. F.) Second, Plaintiff asserts that the documents were served by both personal service and substituted service. Substituted service occurs in place of personal service, not in addition to it. (Id.) Third, there are conflicting dates within the document. In part 5, box b(4) is checked, indicating that the summons and complaint were mailed on February 22, 2022, prior to the Complaint ever being filed with the Court. Then, the very next page has the same February 22, 2022 date crossed out, and instead October 24, 2022 is written in, therefore, it is unclear as to which date was intended to be recorded. (Id.) Fourth and finally, assuming the October 24, 2022 address is the address intended to be used, Defendant declares he was no longer residing at 488 E. Ocean Blvd., Unit 1614, Long Beach, CA 90802 by May 31, 2022. (Cook Decl., Exh. E, ¶3.) Therefore, service to that address would have been invalid. 

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is GRANTED.  


Case Number: 22STCV11248    Hearing Date: July 5, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgment

Moving Parties: Defendants Santa Monica Dental Practice and Joseph Sabet, DDS 

The court considered the moving papers. No opposition or reply papers were filed. 

BACKGROUND

On April 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants alleging negligence/medical malpractice.  Plaintiff alleges dental malpractice against Defendants Santa Monica Dental Practice and Joseph Sabet D.D.S.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants performed root canals on two teeth shortly after Plaintiff first visited them in June 2018 about pain in those teeth.  Plaintiff alleges the pain returned in December 2020 and he visited another dentist on January 19, 2021.  Plaintiff discovered on that date that Defendants had improperly performed the root canals.  Plaintiff served Defendants on January 14, 2022 with a notice letter under CCP §364.  

On April 17, 2024, Defendants filed this motion for summary judgment.  As of July 1, 2024, no opposition has been filed.  On June 28, 2024, Defendants filed a Notice of Non-Opposition.  

LEGAL STANDARD

The elements of a medical malpractice cause of action are (1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 468 fn 2.)  

The standard of skill, knowledge and care prevailing in a medical community is ordinarily a matter within the knowledge of experts.  (Jambazian v. Borden (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 836, 844.)  “The standard of care for physicians is the reasonable degree of skill, knowledge and care ordinarily possessed and exercised by members of the medical profession under similar circumstances.  The test for determining familiarity with the standard of care is knowledge of similar conditions.  Geographical location may be a factor considered in making that determination, but, by itself, does not provide a practical basis for measuring similar circumstances.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center, supra, at 159 Cal.App.4th at 470.)

Whether the standard of care in the community has been breached presents a basic issue of fact in a malpractice action which can only be proved by expert opinion testimony unless the medical question is within the common knowledge of laypersons.  (Id. at 470, fn. 1; 1 Witkin, Cal. Evid. (6th ed. 2021), Opinion, §52.)  “[A]n opinion unsupported by reasons or explanations does not establish the absence of a material fact issue for trial, as required for summary judgment.”  (Golden Eagle Refinery Co., Inc. v. Associated Intern. Ins. Co. (2001) 85 Cal.App.4th 1300, 1315 (citing Kelley v. Trunk (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 519, 524).  Accordingly, where a plaintiff provides proper opposing expert testimony via a declaration, a triable issue of material fact is raised which precludes summary judgment.  (Jambazian, supra, 25 Cal.App.4th at 844.)  The absence of opinion evidence on this issue is fatal to the plaintiff’s cause of action.  (Willard v. Hagemeister (1981) 121 Cal.App.3d 406, 412.)  An expert declaration is admissible as long as Defendants’ expert states in detail his qualifications and the factual basis for his opinion.  (Kelley, supra, 66 Cal.App.4th at 524.)  

Expert testimony is also required to establish the element of causation in a medical malpractice action.  “The law is well settled that in a personal injury action causation must be proven within a reasonable medical probability based on competent expert testimony. Mere possibility alone is insufficient to establish a prima facie case. That there is a distinction between a reasonable medical probability and a medical possibility needs little discussion. There can be many possible causes, indeed, an infinite number of circumstances that can produce an injury or disease. A possible cause only becomes probable when, in the absence of other reasonable causal explanations, it becomes more likely than not that the injury was a result of its action. This is the outer limit of inference upon which an issue may be submitted to the jury.”  (Jennings v. Palomar Pomerado Health Systems, Inc. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1118 (trial court properly struck medical expert testimony on issue of causation where expert’s opinion failed to provide a reasoned explanation regarding the etiology connecting plaintiff’s infected subcutaneous tissue to the nidus inside peritoneal cavity where retractor was retained).  

“A party is entitled to summary judgment only if it meets its initial burden of showing there are no triable issues of fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. This is true even if the opposing party fails to file any opposition. The court's assessment of whether the moving party has carried its burden—and therefore caused a shift—occurs before the court's evaluation of the opposing party's papers. Therefore, the burden on the motion does not initially shift as a result of what is, or is not, contained in the opposing papers.”  (Mosley v. Pacific Specialty Insurance Company (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 417, 434–435 (landlord’s failure to address issue of whether they were aware of their tenant’s marijuana growing operation was not grounds to grant summary judgment where moving party failed to satisfy its initial burden as to the issue); Thatcher v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1086-1087 (court cannot grant summary judgment based merely on lack of opposition; court must first determine if the moving party has satisfied its burden).)

In addition, the evidence and affidavits of the moving party are construed strictly, while those of the opponent are liberally read.  (Government Employees Ins. Co. v. Sup. Ct. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 95, 100. “All doubts as to the propriety of granting the motion (whether there is any issue of material fact [Code of Civil Procedure] § 437c) are to be resolved in favor of the party opposing the motion (i.e., a denial of summary judgment).” (Hamburg v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 497, 502.)

DISCUSSION

Defendants move for summary judgment of the entire complaint on grounds that (1) the action is barred based on the statute of limitations under CCP §340.5; (2) Defendants complied with the applicable standard of care at all times; and (3) no act or omission by Defendants caused or contributed to Plaintiffs’ alleged injury.  Plaintiff did not file an opposition.  However, despite the lack of an opposition, Defendants must still satisfy their initial burden in order to obtain summary judgment.

A.  Statute of Limitations

“In an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person's alleged professional negligence, the time for the commencement of action shall be three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5.)

As used in section 340.5, “injury” means a person's physical condition and its negligent cause. (Gutierrez v. Mofid (1985) 39 Cal.3d 892, 896.) The injury is not necessarily the ultimate harm suffered but instead occurs when appreciable harm is first manifested. (Brewer v. Remington (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 14, 24.)  Whether there has been a belated discovery of the cause of action in malpractice cases essentially is a question of fact, and only where reasonable minds can draw but one conclusion from the evidence does the question become a matter of law.  (Drexler v. Petersen (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 1181, 1188-1189.) 

Section 340.5’s discovery rule “sets forth two alternate tests for triggering the limitations period: (1) a subjective test requiring actual suspicion by the plaintiff that the injury was caused by wrongdoing; and (2) an objective test requiring a showing that a reasonable person would have suspected the injury was caused by wrongdoing.  The first to occur under these two tests begins the limitations period.” (Kitzig v. Nordquist (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 1384, 1391.) Under the first test, the plaintiff need only suspect someone has done something wrong, which does not require the plaintiff to know the specific facts (e.g., medical cause, diagnosis) necessary to establish a medical negligence claim. (Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 397–398; Knowles v. Superior Court (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1290, 1295.) A plaintiff has presumptive knowledge of the negligent injury once he has “notice or information of circumstances to put a reasonable person on inquiry, or has the opportunity to obtain knowledge from sources open to his investigation.” (Gutierrez v. Mofid, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 896; accord, Kitzig v. Nordquist, supra, 81 Cal.App.4th at p. 1391 [one-year statute of limitations begins to run when plaintiff suspects or should suspect wrongdoing caused injury].)

Defendant argues that the statute of limitations as to tooth #2 began to run as early as June 29, 2018 and as to tooth #3, it began to run no later than August 4, 2020.  Based on Defendants’ evidence, Plaintiff underwent a root canal on October 2017. (Defendant’s SSUMF No. 3.)  On June 29, 2018, Plaintiff was examined by Defendant Sabet, who advised him to seek an endodontist consult for a possible Apico or re-treatment of tooth #2.  (Defendant’s SSUMF No. 7.)  On August 20, 2020, Dr. Homyak advised Plaintiff to see an endodontist, after noting that the gingiva around tooth #2 and 3 were inflamed.  (Defendant’s SSUMF No. 24.)

The mere fact that Plaintiff was advised to see an endodontist on these dates does not establish as an issue of law that a “reasonable person would have suspected the injury was caused by wrongdoing.”  (Kitzig, supra, 81 Cal.App.4th at 1391 (emphasis added.)  Even if the advice to see an endodontist alerted Plaintiff to an injury, reasonable minds could differ as to whether Plaintiff should have known that his injury was the result of negligence or some wrongdoing or that Plaintiff was placed on inquiry notice as a result of the advice.  As such, the motion for summary judgment based on statute of limitations is denied.

B.  Based on the undisputed evidence, Defendants did not breach any applicable standard of care, nor were Plaintiff’s injuries caused by Defendants’ act or omission

Defendants negate Plaintiff’s allegations of breach of the standard of care and causation with the declaration of Thomas Jovicich, D.M.D.  Jovicich testifies that based on his review of the relevant records, Defendants did not breach any applicable standard of care, nor were Plaintiff’s injuries caused by any act or omission of Defendants.  (Jovicich Dec., ¶¶10-13.)  

The burden therefore shifts to Plaintiff to submit admissible expert evidence raising a triable issue of material fact as to breach and causation. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.  

As such, no triable issues of fact remain as to Plaintiff’s negligence/dental malpractice claim.  Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

CONCLUSION

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.   No triable issues of fact remain as to Plaintiff’s complaint.  Based on the undisputed evidence, Defendants did not breach any applicable standard of care, nor did they cause Plaintiff’s injury.  (Defendants’ SSUMF Nos. 80 and 97.)
