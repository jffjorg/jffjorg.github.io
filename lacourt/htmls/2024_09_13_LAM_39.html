DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV22060A    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 13, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV22060

MOTION

Demurrer to Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Phibro-Tech, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff People of the State of California ex rel. Meredith J. Williams

 

MOTION

Plaintiff People of the State of California ex rel. Meredith J. Williams, Director of the California Department of Toxic Substances Control (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendant Phibro-Tech, Inc. (“Defendant”), contending Defendant failed to comply with the California Hazardous Waste Control Law.  Defendant has demurred to the first, second and fourth cause of actions in Plaintiff’s complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the demurrer.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Plaintiff alleges as follows: Defendant owns and operates a hazardous waste treatment and recycling facility (Complaint, ¶ 29); on September 20, 2017, Plaintiff’s 2015 Permit Modification of the Closure Plan for Pond 1, Permit No. 91-3-TS-002, No. 03, which Plaintiff refers to as the 2016 Closure Plan Modification, became effective, and it required Defendant to begin the process to close Pond 1 at its facility by March 20, 2018 (id., ¶¶ 45-46); Plaintiff inspected Defendant’s facility on October 9, 2018 and found Defendant had not begun the process of closing Pond 1 at its facility (id., ¶ 50);  Plaintiff again inspected Defendant’s facility on September 4 and 5, 2019 and October 17, 2019 and notified Defendant of violations in reports on April 17 and May 12, 2020 and March 22, 2021 (id., ¶ 51).

Plaintiff’s first cause of action is for failure to implement the closure of Pond 1.  Defendant requests judicial notice of the 2016 Closure Plan Modification.  Plaintiff references this Plan Modification in its complaint but did not attach it to the complaint.  As such, the Court takes judicial notice of it.  (Ascherman v. General Reinsurance Corp. (1986) 183 Cal.App.3d 307, 310-311.)  Defendant argues the 2016 Closure Plan Modification does not require Defendant to close Pond 1 within 180 days of the 2016 Closure Plan Modification’s effective date.  Defendant is incorrect.  The 2016 Closure Plan Modification states Defendant “shall complete closure activities . . . within . . . 180 days after approval of the closure plan.”  (Declaration of Robert B. Martin III in Support of Demurrer, Exhibit B, p. 068.) 

Defendant also requests judicial notice of the September 20, 2017 final order on Defendant’s appeal of the 2016 Closure Plan Modification.  The Court likewise grants this request, as Plaintiff references the final order in the complaint, but does not attach that order to the complaint.  In the order, Defendant’s Permit Appeals Officer found Plaintiff’s January 17, 2017 approval of Defendant’s request for a Class 2 permit modification of its permit for its wastewater treatment system relocation and replacement “to revise the effective date of the 2016 permit modification to be subsequent to the processing of the 2015 Class 2 Permit Modification Request.”  (Martin Decl., Exhibit F, p. 14.)  Plaintiff approved the 2015 Class 2 Permit Modification Request on February 17, 2017.  (Id., Exhibit F, p. 13.)  Thus, the effective date of the 2016 Closure Plan Modification was after February 17, 2017.  However, this is immaterial, as Plaintiff does not contend the 2016 Closure Plan Modification was effective until the September 20, 2017 final order.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 45-46.) 

Defendant also argues the statute of limitations bars Plaintiff’s first cause of action.  A five-year statute of limitations applies to that claim.  (Code of Civil Procedure, § 338.1.)  The statute of limitations runs from “the discovery by the agency . . . of the facts constituting the grounds for commencing the action.”  (Ibid.)  The statutes of limitations for this action were tolled from April 6, 2020 to October 1, 2020, which is an additional 178 days, due to the pandemic. (Cal. Rules of Court, emergency rule 9(a).)  Plaintiff filed this action on September 13, 2023.  Thus, Plaintiff’s action is untimely if Plaintiff discovered the facts underlying Plaintiff’s claim before March 19, 2018.  Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff learned Defendant failed to begin the process of closing Pond 1 in its inspection on October 9, 2018. (Complaint, ¶ 50.)  Plaintiff, therefore, alleges facts to show its complaint is timely. 

Defendant argues Plaintiff should have known it could not have timely closed Pond 1 before March 19, 2018, because Plaintiff knew Defendant had failed to take intermediate steps.  (Complaint, ¶ 47.)  However, Plaintiff’s action is not based on Defendant’s failure to take intermediate steps.  The basis for Plaintiff’s action is Defendant’s failure to timely close Pond 1.  Accordingly, the Court cannot conclude Plaintiff’s first cause of action is untimely.

Defendant requests judicial notice of the 2015 Class 2 Permit Modification Request, which states, in pertinent part, “Within 3 days of commencement of operations of Tanks W-7, W-8, J-6, or J-7, the owner or operator shall remove all waste from Tanks W-1, W-2, W-3, and W-4 and begin closure of Tanks W-1, W-2, W3, and W-4 as specified in the approved 2015 Pond 1 Closure Plan.”  (Martin Decl., Exhibit E, p. 350.)  This references the timeline for the closure of wastewater tanks, not the pond.  As such, its relevance is unclear.  For the same reasons, Defendant’s argument Plaintiff’s second cause of action for failure to complete the closure of Pond One is time-barred fails.

Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is for failure to revise closure cost estimate.  Plaintiff alleges, “On or about October 20, 2017, 30 days after DTSC’s Permit Appeal Officer vacated the stay and reinstated the 2015 Closure Plan and the 2016 Closure Plan Modification, [Defendant] was required . . . to submit to DTSC the revised closure cost estimate for the Facility, including, but not limited to, Pond 1.”  (Complaint, ¶ 76.)  Defendant argues it submitted a closure cost estimate in September 2015.  That is irrelevant, however, as Plaintiff contends Defendant failed to revise its closure cost estimate, not that it failed to submit one.

Defendant also argues it was not required to submit a revised closure cost estimate because the increased closure cost resulted from inflation.  In support of this argument, Defendant cites California Code of Regulations, title 22, section 66264.142, which states, in pertinent part, “[d]uring the active life of the facility, the owner or operator shall adjust the closure cost estimate for inflation . . . .”  (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 22, § 66264.142, subd. (b).)  It is, therefore, unclear why Defendant contends it was not required to update its closure cost estimate.  Defendant also argues Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is time-barred, which, as set forth above, is unpersuasive.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to the first, second, and fourth causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint is overruled.  Defendant is to answer the complaint within 10 days. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV22060B    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 13, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV22060

MOTION

Demurrer to Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff People of the State of California ex rel. Meredith J. Williams

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Phibro-Tech, Inc.

 

MOTION

Plaintiff People of the State of California ex rel. Meredith J. Williams, Director of the California Department of Toxic Substances Control (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant Phibro-Tech, Inc. (“Defendant”), contending Defendant failed to comply with the California Hazardous Waste Control Law (“HWCL”).  Defendant cross-complained against Plaintiff.  Plaintiff demurs to the second cause of action in Defendant’s cross-complaint.  Defendant opposes the demurrer.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Defendant’s second cause of action is brought under title 42 United States Code section 1983, which provides, “Every person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State or Territory or the District of Columbia, subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States or other person within the jurisdiction thereof to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured in an action at law, suit in equity, or other proper proceeding for redress.”  (42 U.S.C. § 1983.)  “Only those government actions that amount to a deliberate flouting of the law qualify for relief under section 1983 . . . , not inadvertent errors, honest mistakes, agency confusion, [or] negligence.”  (Galland v. City of Clovis (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1003, 1038, internal quotations omitted.)  In support of this cause of action, Defendant alleges, “In violation of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and article I, section 7(a) of the California Constitution, [Plaintiff] deliberately flouted the law by erroneously applying the HWCL and its implementing regulations to [Defendant] in such a way as to injure [Defendant]’s economic and property interests.”  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 102.) 

Plaintiff argues Defendant does not allege Plaintiff denied Defendant any legally protected interest.  As Plaintiff points out, Defendant alleges Plaintiff’s actions “threaten[] [Defendant]’s ability to operate . . . ,” not that Defendant is unable to operate.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 110.)  As such, Defendant’s allegations reflect its claim is premature.  The Court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend.  Defendant must allege present harms to its economic and property interests.

Plaintiff also argues Defendant’s right to operate a hazardous waste storage facility is “an economic right . . . , heavily regulated by the government, and derivative to [Defendant]’s ability to satisfy numerous requirements.”  (quoting Barri v. Workers' Comp. Appeals Bd. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 428, 460.)  As such, it is not a fundamental interest entitled to substantive due process protection.  However, Defendant alleges Plaintiff violated the HWCL in its treatment of Defendant.  (See, e.g., Cross-Complaint, ¶ 107.)   Accordingly, Defendant may state a claim under title 42 United States Code section 1983 that Plaintiff violated Defendant’s rights under the HWCL.

Likewise, Plaintiff’s argument Defendant’s allegations do not constitute flouting of the HWCL is unpersuasive.  Defendant alleges Plaintiff deliberately treated Defendant unequally and inconsistently.  (See, e.g., Cross-Complaint, ¶ 113.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s demurrer to the second cause of action in Defendant’s cross-complaint is sustained with leave to amend.  Defendant is to file an amended cross-complaint within 10 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 24STCV17879    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 13, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV17879

MOTION

Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Kenji Morinaga

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff 6340 Hollywood Associates, LLC

  

BACKGROUND

Defendant Kenji Morinaga (“Defendant”) moves to quash service of summons.  Plaintiff 6340 Hollywood Associates, LLC (“Plaintiff”) opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Plaintiff filed a proof of service on August 21, 2024, which reflects, on July 27, 2024 at 11:11 a.m., Plaintiff served Defendant at 6338 Hollywood Boulevard in Los Angeles by substituted service on John Doe, a co-tenant, whose gender, approximate age, “Skin,” hair, height, and weight are noted.  (See Proof of Service of Summons, filed August 21, 2024.)  The proof of service also indicates the documents were mailed to Defendant,  first class with postage prepaid, to the 6338 Hollywood Boulevard address. 

 As service was effectuated by a registered process server, Plaintiff’s service is entitled to a presumption of validity. (Evid. Code, § 647.)  Defendant is, therefore “required to produce evidence that [he was] not served.” (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390.)

Defendant has submitted a declaration in which he states, “I am unaware of having been served, or properly served, with any summons directed to me in this case, and I do not believe I have been so served.”  (See Declaration of Kenji Morinaga, ¶ 5.)  Defendant’s declaration is vague and indirect.  For example, he fails to address whether he has a co-tenant who might have accepted substituted service on his behalf on July 27, 2024 and whether he was substitute served by Plaintiff.  Accordingly, the motion is denied.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
