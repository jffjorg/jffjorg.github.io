DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV39845    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

frederick young,      

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

FORD MOTOR COMPANY; et al.,  

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV39845

  Hearing Date: July 3, 2024  

 

      [Tentative] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiff Fredrick Young (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action against Defendant Ford Motor Company (“Ford”) on December 22, 2022.   Plaintiff asserts causes of action under the Song-Beverly Act. 

The Complaint alleges in pertinent part as follows: On July 13, 2020, Plaintiff purchased a Ford Blue Advantage Certified 2019 Ford Mustang (“Vehicle”) manufactured by Ford.   During the warranty period, the vehicle developed defects.

LEGAL STANDARD

Attorney’s fees are mandatory to the prevailing plaintiff under the Song-Beverly Act.   (Kim v.Euromotors West/The Auto Gallery (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 170, 178; Drouin v. Fleetwood Enterprises (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 486, 493. Pursuant to California Civil Code, Section 1780(e) : “[t]he court shall award court costs and attorney’s fees to a prevailing plaintiff in litigation filed pursuant to this section.” A prevailing plaintiff is someone who has obtained a net monetary recovery on a Song-Beverly Act.  (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 150.)

 “The verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”  (Horsford v. Board Of Trustees Of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)  If the motion is supported by evidence, the opposing party must respond with specific evidence showing that the fees are unreasonable.  (Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys. v. California Ins. Guarantee Ass’n (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 560-63.)  The Court has discretion to reduce fees that result from inefficient or duplicative use of time.  (Horsford, supra, 132 Cal.App.4th at 395.) 

In determining a reasonable attorney fee, the trial court begins with the lodestar, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.  (Warren v. Kia Motors America, Inc. (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 24, 36.)  The lodestar may then be adjusted based on factors specific to the case in order to fix the fee at the fair market value of the legal services provided.  (Ibid.)  These facts include (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.  (Ibid.)

ANALYSIS

            Plaintiff moves for an award of costs in the amount of $1,402.41 and an award of attorney fees in the amount of $35,727.50.

            A. Entitlement to Attorney Fees

The parties formally agreed to resolve this action and Ford paid Plaintiff $61,308.00, and Ford stipulated that Plaintiff could seek attorney’s fees and costs by motion.  As such, Plaintiff is the prevailing party in this action, and as prevailing party in this action, Plaintiff is entitled to a reasonable amount of attorney fees. The issue to be addressed is the reasonableness of the requested amount of attorney fees, costs, and expenses. 

B. Reasonableness of Fees

1. Reasonable Hourly Rates

The hourly rates claimed by Plaintiff’s attorneys and paralegals are as follows: (1) Michael Saeedian $695.00 per hour; (2) Adina Ostoia $695.00 per hour; (3) Christopher Urner $525.00 per hour; and (4) Jorge Acosta $250.00 per hour.

“In determining hourly rates, the court must look to the ‘prevailing market rates in the relevant community.’”  (Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 100.)  In making this determination, “[t]he court may rely on its own knowledge and familiarity with the legal market.”  (Ibid.) 

The Court finds that $400.00 per hour is the reasonable hourly rate in this case for Plaintiff’s attorneys and that $150.00 per hour is the reasonable hourly rate in this case for Plaintiff’s paralegals. 

2. Hours Reasonably Expended

The total number of billable hours claimed by Plaintiff’s attorneys and paralegals are as follows:  (1) Michael Saeedian 28.6 hours; (2) Adina Ostoia 5.4 hours; (3) Christopher Urner 9.9 hours; and (4) Jorge Acosta 27.6 hours.

Ford claims that the number of hours billed is unreasonable. 

The Court finds that the reasonable hours spent by Plaintiff’s attorneys on this matter is  32 hours and that the reasonable hours spent by Plaintiff’s paralegals in this matter are 25 hours.

In making this determination, the court found that plaintiff’s counsel inappropriately billed for some clerical tasks and that some of the billing was excessive, especially for attorneys as experienced as plaintiff’s counsel. While plaintiff has the right to have multiple attorneys, the court finds that some of the attorneys’ work was duplicative and redundant. 

C. Multiplier

Plaintiff requested a lodestar multiplier enhancement. 

 The Court finds that an upward adjustment to the lodestar is not warranted in this action. This was a straightforward lemon law case.  There is no evidence that Plaintiff’s counsel was precluded from taking other cases. A downward adjustment to the lodestar is not warranted either as Plaintiff’s counsel took this case as a contingency.

D. Entitlement and Reasonableness of Costs

Plaintiff requests a total of $1,402.41 in costs and expenses.   Ford does not object to these costs.  As such, the court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to an award of $924.20 in costs and expenses.

D. Conclusion

Based on the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees and costs is GRANTED.  The Court awards $16,550.00 in attorney fees and $1,402.41 in costs and expenses.

 


Case Number: 23STCV06592    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

BLAINE VAN PATTEN, an individual and as

Co-Trustee of the Van Patten Family Trust dated September 10, 2009;

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

JOHNNIE MAXINE MATTHEWS, an individual, and DOES 1 THROUGH 50, INCLUSIVE;

 

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV06592

  Hearing Date:  July 03, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion for leave to file FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT AND TO CONTINUE TRIAL BY REASON OF SAME

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 24, 2023, Plaintiff Blaine Van Patten, as an individual and as Co-Trustee of the Van Patten Family Trust, filed this action against Defendant Johnnie Maxine Matthews. The complaint asserts causes of action for breach of a lease contract, breach of implied contract, restitution based on quasi-contract, interference with prospective economic advantage, and common count.

On February 5, 2020, Plaintiff (landlord) entered into a year-long lease agreement with Defendant for a residential apartment unit ending February 4, 2021. Defendant continued to retain possession of the unit after February 4, 2021, becoming a month-to-month tenant. Plaintiff alleges that beginning January 1, 2022, Defendant continuously failed to pay rent until Plaintiff’s repossession of the unit on September 28, 2023. Additionally, Plaintiff seeks damages to the unit as a result of Defendant’s refusal to allow Plaintiff’s agents to enter the unit to conduct urgent repairs.

On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for leave to file a first amended complaint. Plaintiff seeks leave to amend his complaint to: (1) add new facts as to events transpiring after the filing of the original complaint, and (2) correct and increase damages sought as a result of information gained after the filing of the original complaint. Plaintiff further asks the Court to continue the August 6, 2024 jury trial as a bench trial on the continued trial date.

On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed a proof of service of this motion. However, the proof of service contains an error. Plaintiff electronically served “ALBAAJET@GMAIL.COM” instead of “ALABAAJET@GMAIL.COM.”

Defendant has not filed an opposition.

LEGAL STANDARD

The court may, in the furtherance of justice, and on such terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc, §§ 473, subd. (a), 576.) However, a motion for leave to amend a complaint must be accompanied by a declaration that explains: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1324(b).)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff’s motion must be accompanied by a valid proof of service. (Code Civ. Proc., §1005.) Here, the proof of service indicates that this motion was served electronically to “ALBAAJET@GMAIL.COM.” However, this email address is different from the Defendant’s correct email address and, as of this instant motion, is not a valid email address. Further, Defendant has filed no opposition. Therefore, this Court finds Plaintiff failed to properly serve Defendant and give the required notice of this motion.

            Even if service was proper, the Court would still deny this motion for failure to comply with California Rule of Court 3.1324(b). The Declaration of Einar W. Johnson in support of the motion does not directly state when the new facts alleged were discovered, nor why the request for amendment was not made earlier as required by Rule 3.1324(b). While the Court agrees an action generally is a “fluid state of affairs,” Plaintiff failed to inform the Court when the specific facts alleged in the complaint were discovered. (Declaration of Einar W. Johnson, ISO Mot. ¶4.) Further, the most recent exhibit in support of the instant motion is dated October 12, 2023. Plaintiff does not explain the approximately eight-month delay in filing his motion. For these reasons alone, the Court would be inclined to deny Plaintiff’s motion for failure to satisfy the requirements of Rule 3.1324(b).

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a first amended complaint is DENIED. Plaintiff’s request for a trial continuance is DENIED. As jury fees were waived, the trial will continue as a bench trial on August 6, 2024.

 


Case Number: 23STCV09530    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

LEXI BURROWS, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

TOUCHSTONE GOLF, LLC, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV09530

  Hearing Date:  July 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On April 28, 2023, Plaintiffs Lexi Burrows and Brooklynn Lundberg filed this employment action against Defendants Touchstone Golf, LLC, Ashley Walston, Peter Wagner, and the City of Burbank. Plaintiffs assert claims stemming from harassment, discrimination and retaliation, wrongful termination, and wage violations. Plaintiffs allege that they experienced and spoke out against sexual harassment and discrimination, and reported illegal or unsafe working conditions, only to suffer retaliation for doing so.

            On March 11, 2024, the Court denied the motion to compel arbitration filed by Defendants Touchstone Golf, LLC and City of Burbank. Defendants have appealed that decision. On April 24, 2024, the Court granted Defendants’ motion to stay the proceedings pending the appeal but allowed discovery to continue.  

            On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff Burrows filed the instant two motions to compel further responses to her Requests for Production. Defendants filed their oppositions on June 20, 2024. Plaintiff filed her replies on June 24, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).)

MEET AND CONFER

A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Schein Decl.)

DISCUSSION

            The deadline for serving a motion to compel further responses is 45 days after receipt of the responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(c).) The deadline is extended by two court days if responses are served electronically. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).) If the deadline falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline is extended to the next court day. (Id., § 2016.060.) The service deadline is “mandatory and jurisdictional.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)    

            Here, Defendants served their responses by email on April 18, 2024. (Tong Decl., Ex. B.) Counting forty-five days, plus two court days for electronic service, results in a deadline of Tuesday, June 4, 2024. Section 2016.060 only applies if “the last day to perform or complete any act provided for in this title falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or holiday.” The “last day” to perform an action necessarily accounts for applicable extensions. Here, the “last day” to file a motion to compel was Tuesday, June 4, 2024 after accounting for the two-court-day extension for electronic service. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.290(c), 1010.6(a)(3)(B).) Because June 4, 2024 was neither a weekend nor holiday, section 2016.060 was not triggered. Thus, the deadline was June 4, 2024.

            Plaintiff argues that the holiday rule was triggered on Sunday, June 2, 2024, or 45 days from April 18, 2024. This would mean that the deadline was extended to Monday, June 3, 2024. Plaintiff then applies the two-court-day extension for electronic service, making the final deadline Wednesday, June 5, 2024. This calculation overlooks the language of section 2016.060, which applies the holiday rule only if the “last day” to perform an act falls on a weekend or holiday.[1] Sunday, June 2, 2024 was not the “last day” to serve the motion to compel in this case because the Code grants a two-court-day extension for electronic service. As discussed above, the actual “last day” to serve a motion to compel in this case was June 4, 2024 because of that two-day extension. Since June 4, 2024 was neither a weekend nor holiday, section 2016.060 was not triggered. Therefore, the deadline to serve the motions was June 4, 2024.

Plaintiff did not serve her motions until June 5, 2024, rendering them untimely. This “renders the court without authority to rule on [the] motions to compel other than to deny them.” (Sexton, supra, 58 Cal.App.4th at p. 1410.)

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses are DENIED. Sanctions are denied as the parties acted with substantial justification.  

 


[1] California Rules of Court, Rule 1.10(b) applies the holiday rule in the same way, i.e., only if the “last day” for the performance of a specified act lands on a weekend or holiday.


Case Number: 23STCV31794    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

HALEY CHAPMAN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

MARRIOT INTERNATIONAL, INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV03486

  Hearing Date:  July 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion to exclude corrected deposition testimony

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On February 16, 2023, Plaintiff Haley Chapman, individually and as successor-in-interest to Noelle Glotfelty (Decedent), filed this action against various Defendants for (1) wrongful death, (2) survival cause of action, and (3) negligence. The complaint stems from the death of Decedent, who committed suicide inside a Marriott hotel room.

            On December 14, 2023, Plaintiff took the deposition of Defendant Brett Scott. At the end of the deposition, both parties acknowledged that Scott would have 30 days to review and submit changes to his testimony, in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.520. The court reporter released the transcript on December 30, 2023. However, Scott did not issue an errata sheet until March 27, 2024.

            On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to preclude any reference to or use of Scott’s late corrected testimony. Defendant County of Ventura filed its opposition on June 20, 2024. Plaintiff filed her reply on June 26, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

For 30 days following notice by the deposition officer, “unless the attending parties and the deponent agree on the record or otherwise in writing to a longer or shorter time period, the deponent may change the form or the substance of the answer to a question, and may either approve the transcript of the deposition by signing it, or refuse to approve the transcript by not signing it.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.520(b).) “If the deponent fails or refuses to approve the transcript within the allotted period, the deposition shall be given the same effect as though it had been approved, subject to any changes timely made by the deponent.” (Id., § 2025.520(f).)

DISCUSSION

            Here, the court reporter sent the requisite notice on December 30, 2023. (Maxwell Decl., Ex. B.) It is undisputed that Scott did not issue an errata sheet until March 27, 2024, past the 30 days allowed by section 2025.520(b). (Id., Ex. C.) There is no indication that the parties agreed to a longer time period. Thus, Scott’s correction was untimely. Because Scott did not make any timely changes and did not approve the transcript within 30 days, the deposition is treated as having been approved without changes. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.520(f).) The Code contains no exceptions to its mandate that changes be made within the specified time period, even for good faith or substantial justification. 

            However, section 2025.520 also contains no remedy provision addressing a deponent’s untimely correction. Plaintiff argues that the Court should issue an order precluding the use of the corrected transcript. Plaintiff cites Fredianelli v. Jenkins (N.D.Cal. 2013) 931 F.Supp.2d 1001, 1008, where the court sustained the defendant’s objections to the plaintiff’s untimely deposition corrections. However, this was done on summary judgment when the plaintiff attempted to admit his corrected testimony as evidence. The Court agrees with Defendant that an issue does not arise unless and until a party attempts to admit untimely corrections as evidence on a motion or at trial. Plaintiff’s present motion amounts to a premature motion in limine.

            Plaintiff argues that the Court should issue an order to “limit the damage beforehand.” However, it is unclear what damage could be caused before Defendants attempt to use the corrected testimony as evidence. If Defendants do attempt to use the revised transcript as evidence, Plaintiff may object as the defendant did in Fredianelli, or file a motion in limine at the appropriate time.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion to exclude corrected deposition testimony is DENIED.
