Case Number: 19STCV20062    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JACOB REICH

                          

         vs.

 

MANSOUR HASHEM

 

 Case No.:  19STCV20062

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: June 5, 2024

 

            Defendant’s motion to disqualify judge is DENIED.

 

On June 7, 2019, Plaintiff Jacob Reich filed a Complaint against Defendant Mansour Hashem. On September 1, 2020, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (SAC) alleging: (1) breach of written contract; (2) money had and received; (3) common counts; (4) open book account; (5) account stated; (6) failure to honor checks; (7) fraud; (8) negligence; (9) unjust enrichment; (10) judiciary foreclosure; and (11) reformation of contract.

 

            While a motion to disqualify judge was reserved by Defendant, set to be heard 6/5/2024, no corresponding motion could be located in eCourt. Neither an opposition nor a reply could be located as well.

 

            Given the lack of moving papers, the motion must be denied.

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  July    , 2024

                                                                                                                                                          

   Hon. Jon R. Takasugi
   Judge of the Superior Court

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JACOB REICH

                          

         vs.

 

MANSOUR HASHEM

 

 Case No.:  19STCV20062

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: June 5, 2024

 

Defendant’s request for modification of the assignment order is GRANTED IN PART, DENIED IN PART. The assignment of rents for the 14816 Avis Avenue Property is reduced to 20%, and the 10826 Wagner Street Property is removed from the assignment order at this time. Defendant’s motion is otherwise denied. 

 

On June 7, 2019, Plaintiff Jacob Reich filed a Complaint against Defendant Mansour Hashem. On September 1, 2020, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (SAC)  alleging: (1) breach of written contract; (2) money had and received; (3) common counts; (4) open book account; (5) account stated; (6) failure to honor checks; (7) fraud; (8) negligence; (9) unjust enrichment; (10) judiciary foreclosure; and (11) reformation of contract.

 

            Now, Defendant moves for a revocation or modification of the Court’s rent assignment order.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that the assignment order should be modified based on new facts and circumstances since the order was originally entered.

 

            After review, the Court only agrees in part.

 

            Defendant argues that the order should be modified based on the following grounds: (1) 2 tenants have moved out of the 2007 Ocean Park property reducing rent from $7,425 to $2,450; (2) the 40% garnishment for the 14816 Avis Avenue property should be reduced to 20% because Defendant only owns ½ of the property; (3) it is impossible for the rents at 10826 Wagner Street to be assigned because the property is rented through Airbnb, and guests pay for their stay directly through Airbnb; and (4) Defendant is entitled to be paid as a manager of the properties.

 

            As for the first contention, Defendant supports his claim that rents have decreased with three screenshots. The first is a text exchange that says “Now everyone is moving out of the apartment end of November….” and a second that seems to suggest the keys have been dropped off. However, the third screenshot is an incomplete email that says “Accepted $954 from Mike pertaining to security deposit for 2007 Ocean Park Santa Monica CA 90405.” No explanation is provided as to who Mike it, and the accepting of a security deposit on November 9 would suggest that a new tenant is moving in, not moving out. As such, like with Defendant’s other motions, the evidence to support his claims is incomplete and evasive. Defendant has not submitted adequate evidence to support his claims that tenants have vacated the units and that his monthly rents have been reduced.

 

            As for the second contention, the Court agrees. As a 50% owner of the Avis Property, Defendant receives 50% of rents. 40% of Defendant’s 50% share would amount to an assignment of 20% of the overall monthly rent payment. The order should be adjusted accordingly. In opposition, Plaintiff conceded to this request.

 

            As for the third contention, the Court agrees that the Airbnb system poses a hurdle to the rent-assignment process. Clearly, guests cannot be expected to assign a portion of their payment to Plaintiff, as they pay directly to Airbnb. In opposition, Plaintiff did not identify any authority to suggest otherwise, or which indicated how or whether the assignment order could be designed to direct Airbnb itself to direct the payments it receives from guests in a manner consistent with the assignment order. Accordingly, the Court finds that rents from this property are not assignable at this time.

 

            Finally, as for the fourth contention, Defendant argues that he is entitled to payment as a manager of the properties. However, Defendant offers no legal support for this. (“This failure to cite pertinent legal authority is enough reason to reject the argument”); Akins v. State of California (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1, 50, 71 (contention waived by failure to cite legal authority).

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Defendant’s request for modification of the assignment order in part. The assignment of rents for the 14816 Avis Avenue Property is reduced to 20%, and the 10826 Wagner Street Property is removed from the assignment order at this time. Defendant’s motion is otherwise denied. 

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: 23STCV13556    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

BENJAMIN ZUBKOFF

                          

         vs.

 

GENERAL MOTORS, LLC

 

 Case No.: 23STCV13556

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: June 5, 2024

 

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART, DENIED IN PART, consistent with the ruling set forth below.

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED IN PART, DENIED IN PART, consistent with the ruling set forth below.

 

            Accordingly, the Court declines to award sanctions in connection with either motion.

 

            On 6/13/2023, Plaintiff Benjamin Zubkoff (Plaintiff) filed suit against General Motors, LLC, alleging: (1) fraud concealment; (2) negligent misrepresentation; (3) Business and Professions Code section 17200; (4) breach of express warranty; (5) breach of implied warranty; (6) violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(b.)

 

            Now, Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to his Requests for Production (RFPs) and his Special Interrogatories

 

Discussion

 

I.                   RFPs

 

Plaintiff argues that good cause exists to compel further responses to RFP Nos. 28-29, 37-50, 52-64, 67-86.

 

After review, the Court finds Plaintiff’s requests to be overbroad in scope. For example, Plaintiff requests all documents related to Defendant’s policies or procedures regarding recalls and technical service bulletins. Plaintiff requests all documents evidencing, relating, or referring to complaints by owners and lessees of vehicles beyond that of same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle.

 

In addition to all documents concerning the subject vehicle, the Court generally finds the following documents to be discoverable:

 

1.      Defendant shall produce the “Warranty Policy and Procedure Manual” published by Defendant and provided to its authorized repair facilities, within the State of California, for the period of the purchase date to present.

 

2.      Defendant shall produce any internal analysis or investigation concerning the alleged electrical defect in vehicles for the same year, make, and model of the subject vehicle.

 

3.       Defendant shall produce any customer complaints relating to defects that are similar to the alleged defects claimed by plaintiff in vehicles within California for the same year, make, and model of the subject vehicle, for the period of the purchase date to present.

 

4.       Defendant shall produce all documents evidencing policies and procedures used to evaluate customer requests for repurchase pursuant to the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, for the period of the purchase date to present.

 

5.       Technical Service Bulletins and Recall Notices for vehicles purchased in California for the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle. 

 

6.        All other requests for further production are DENIED.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses is denied. Accordingly, the Court declines to award sanctions at this time.

 

II.               Special Interrogatories

 

Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 14, 20, 23, 25, 26, 39, 41, and 43.

 

After review, the Court finds some of Plaintiff’s requests to be overbroad or irrelevant. For example, Plaintiff requests that Defendant identify all persons “responsible for the customer relations department in the district or region having jurisdiction over Plaintiff’s complaints during the relevant period.” (SROG 25.) However, some of Defendant’s objections are unfounded. For example, Defendant directs Plaintiff to review documents already produced in lieu of a direct response, citing CCP section 2030.230. However, section 2030.230 refers to instances where a response would require a compilation of documents, not where it would require communicating information which is also contained in the document production.

 

Accordingly, the Court rules as follows:

 

-         SROG No. 14: Grant

-         SROG No. 20: Grant

-         SROG No. 23: Grant

-         SROG No. 25: Deny

-         SROG No. 26: Grant

-         SROG No. 39: Deny

-         SROG No. 41: Grant

-         SROG No. 43: Grant

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories is granted in part, denied in part, consistent with the ruling set forth above. Accordingly, the Court declines to award sanctions at this time.

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: 24STCV08719    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

FREDRICK WOOLEY

 

         vs.

 

SHERMAN GAY SENIOR et al.

 

 Case No.:  24STCV08719  

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  June 5, 2024

 

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED, WITH 15 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND. Accordingly, the motion to strike is MOOT.

 

            On 4/8/2024, Plaintiff Fredrick Wooley, Dave Gay, Darrell Gay, Phillip Gay and Emma Gay (collectively, Plaintiff) filed suit against Defendant Sherman Gay Senior (Defendant)

 

            On 5/3/2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s Complaint. Defendant also moves to strike portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

Discussion 

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to state a claim and is uncertain.

 

            The Court agrees.

 

            First, Plaintiffs’ Complaint uncertainly alleges a cause of action for “the inheritance of Patsy Ruth Gay” which is not a cause of action recognized in California. Plaintiffs do not allege any facts which could show that this matter is appropriately brought in this Unlimited Jurisdiction Court, as opposed to Probate Court.

 

            Second, Plaintiffs’ Complaint is handwritten in difficult-to-read cursive, leaving the Court unable to determine much of the substance of the Complaint. However, what is clear is that an allegation that “Plaintiffs are "suing for the total amount of $640,000, the inheritance from Patsy Ruth Gay half” is insufficient to state a claim.

 

            Plaintiffs are urged to consult an attorney.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is sustained, with 15 days leave to amend. Accordingly, the motion to strike is moot.

 

It is so ordered.
