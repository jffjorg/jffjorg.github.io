(“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Arthur Glick (“Arthur”), Doris Glick (“Doris”), Laurie Glick (“Laurie”), and Joan Glick Emery (“Joan”) (collectively “Defendants”), alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract, (2) goods and services rendered, (3) account stated, (4) open book account, (5) unjust enrichment, and (6) interference with contractual relations. The complaint arises from an alleged breach of contract to provide home care services for Defendant Laurie’s parents, Defendant Arthur and Defendant Doris.

 

On November 1, 2021, Defendant Laurie filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Priscilla Roque Manzaneres, and DOES 1 through 20. On April 20, 2023, Defendant Laurie filed an amended cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Priscilla Roque Manzaneres, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and (3) unlawful and unfair business practices.

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Protective Order that Defendant’s Demand for a Bill of Particulars Be Quashed (the “Protective Order Motion”). On November 22, 2024, Laurie and Joan filed an Opposition. On December 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Reply.

 

On November 1, 2024, Laurie and Joan filed the instant Motion for Sanctions (the “Sanctions Motion”) and Request for Judicial Notice. On November 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition. On December 3, 2024, Laurie and Joan filed a tardy Reply.

 

II.                 Request for Judicial Notice

 

In support of the Motion for Sanctions, Laurie and Joan request that the Court take judicial notice of two-minute orders in the present action – namely, the November 7, 2023 and November 21, 2023 minute orders.

 

Having reviewed the moving papers, the Court GRANTS Laurie and Joan’s request, pursuant to California Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c).

 

III.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.    Motion for Sanctions

 

Laurie and Joan move for terminating and monetary sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128 on the grounds that Plaintiff’s “remaining claims are not warranted under existing law and have no evidentiary support” since Laurie and Joan tendered payment to Plaintiff; Plaintiff failed to disclose any evidence of attorney’s fees in discovery and disclosures under Code of Civil Procedure section 96; Plaintiff is not entitled to attorneys’ fees as damages under black letter law; and Plaintiff continues to prosecute meritless claims.  (Motion, pp. 6-10.) Plaintiff then requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $7,230.50, reflecting fees and costs incurred by Laurie and Joan “since tendering the full amount claimed by Plaintiff under the Complaint, which should have resulted in Plaintiff’s dismissal of his remaining claims.” (Motion, pp. 11-12.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 states that:

 

“(b) By presenting to the court, whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating, a pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person’s knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, all of the following conditions are met:

 

(1) It is not being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation.

(2) The claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law.

(3) The allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so Identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery.

(4) The denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief.

 

(c) If, after notice and a reasonable opportunity to respond, the court determines that subdivision (b) has been violated, the court may, subject to the conditions stated below, impose an appropriate sanction upon the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation. In determining what sanctions, if any, should be ordered, the court shall consider whether a party seeking sanctions has exercised due diligence.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subds. (b), (c).) In addition, section 128.7 contains a “safe harbor” provision specifying the motion for sanctions may not be filed “unless, within 21 days after service of the motion, ... the challenged paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation, or denial is not withdrawn or appropriately corrected.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (c)(1).)  

 

In Opposition, Plaintiff contends terminating sanctions are excessively harsh and unwarranted, especially as no prior sanctions have been issued, and the Motion is “frivolous, meritless, and harassing” and an attempt to “subvert the court and the jury.” (Opp., pp. 7-8.) Plaintiff contends its disclosures pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 96 were proper and code compliant. (Opp., p. 8, Exh. C.) Further, Plaintiff states it properly pled its claim for attorneys’ fees in the Complaint and, moreover, Laurie and Joan never addressed the issue of attorneys’ fees and other costs of suit during the duration of discovery, or this protracted litigation. (Opp., pp. 9-10.) Finally, Plaintiff highlights that while Laurie and Joan tendered a check in the amount of $17,026.59 on September 11, 2024, Plaintiff did not accept the tender and the offered payment does not moot Plaintiff’s claims. (Opp., p. 11.)

 

In Reply, Laurie and Joan allege that Plaintiff seeks fees not alleged in the operative Complaint. (Reply, p. 2.) Laurie and Joan argue that Plaintiff’s request for attorney fees and insufficient funds charges as damages, rather than just costs, warrants sanctions.  

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Laurie and Joan’s Reply was one day late. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005.) However, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the merits of the untimely brief.


            The Court finds sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 are unwarranted. Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees is reasonable and, at most, a singular instance of improper pleading. The Court need not fully rule on the merits of the attorney fee claim as such an action in isolation does not justify sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7. Indeed, there is no evidence this request was made for an improper purpose, as a frivolous contention, without evidentiary support, or made based on a lack of information or belief. Moreover, Laurie and Joan had ample opportunity to address this issue prior to this Motion or through alternative means and did not, which is indicative of a lack of due diligence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd, (c).)

 

To the extent Laurie and Joan argue Plaintiff’s refusal of their tendered check and continued prosecution of his claims is sanctionable conduct, the Court disagrees. Laurie and Joan made an offer of settlement to Plaintiff, and Plaintiff refused in lieu of proceeding to trial. (Motion, Joan Decl., Exh. C.) Such conduct is Plaintiff’s right – parties are not required to accept settlement offers.

 

For the foregoing reasons, Laurie and Joan’s Motion for Sanctions is DENIED.

 

B.     Motion for Protective Order

 

Plaintiff moves for a protective order quashing Laurie and Joan’s Demand for a Bill of Particulars (“Demand”).

 

“It is not necessary for a party to set forth in a pleading the items of an account therein alleged, but he must deliver to the adverse party, within ten days after a demand thereof in writing, a copy of the account, or be precluded from giving evidence thereof. The court or judge thereof may order a further account when the one delivered is too general, or is defective in any particular.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 454.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that the Demand is improper, fraught with ill-intended gamesmanship, and, to the extent the Complaint contains ambiguities, such specifics can be clarified through modern discovery procedures rather than the Demand. (Motion, pp. 4-5, 6-7.) Plaintiff further contends the Demand is untimely as the “proper time for seeking this clarification is through the discovery process, and not two weeks before the scheduled trial date.” (Motion, p. 6.) Moreover, Plaintiff states the information Laurie and Joan seek through the Demand is largely already in their possession through the Complaint and discovery process. (Motion, p. 6.) Additionally, Plaintiff alleges the Demand is void for vagueness, and Laurie and Joan’s counsel should be subject to sanctions. (Motion, pp. 7-8.) Finally, Plaintiff relies on Augerais v. Naglee (1887) 74 Cal.60 to make its arguments.

 

In Opposition, Laurie and Joan state there is no legal authority providing a basis for the Protective Order Motion, and Plaintiff has no grounds to refuse to respond to the Demand. (Opp., pp. 2, 6.) Laurie and Joan assert that if Plaintiff has already provided the accounting request in the Complaint or discovery, then Plaintiff could just respond to the Demand with those documents. (Opp., pp. 2-3.) If Plaintiff is not required to respond to the Demand, then Laurie and Joan request Plaintiff to be precluded from introducing at trial damages not specified in the Complaint or discovery responses. (Opp., pp. 3, 7.) Laurie and Joan assert Plaintiff should be required to respond to the Demand because Plaintiff told Laurie and Joan that the amount they tendered in an attempt to avoid trial was insufficient. (Opp., pp. 4-5.) Laurie and Joan state Plaintiff’s Protective Order Motion is “entirely made up” and dismiss Plaintiff’s contention that their motivation behind the Demand pertains to the instant Protective Order Motion. (Opp., pp. 5-6.) Finally, Laurie and Joan distinguish the instant matter from Augerais, supra, 74 Cal.60.

 

In Reply, Plaintiff refutes Laurie and Joan’s claim that their motives for bringing the Demand are irrelevant to the Protective Order Motion by pointing to language regarding motive in Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, which is a statute about sanctions. (Reply, pp. 1-2.) Plaintiff states Joan and Laurie mischaracterize Augerais, supra, 74 Cal.60 and contends that Joan and Laurie’s failure to present legal authority in contravention of Plaintiff’s claims indicates Plaintiff’s arguments are meritorious. (Reply, pp. 2-4.)

 

Analysis

 

Here, Plaintiff sets forth no legal authority demonstrating Defendants’ demand for a bill of particulars is untimely or authorizing the Court to grant Plaintiff’s request for a protective order quashing Defendants’ demand for a bill of particulars.

 

Additionally, Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ demand is “void for vagueness” because “defendant” is used instead of “defendants” on the caption of the demand. (Motion, p. 7, emphasis added.) The Court construes this singular instance as a typographical error rather than a consistent error rendering the demand void. (Motion, Exh. A.)

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES the Protective Order Motion.

 

Finally, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 is misplaced. Plaintiff must raise such a claim in a separate motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (c)(1).) Thus, this request is DENIED.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants Joan Glick Emery and Laurie Glick’s Motion for Sanctions is DENIED.  Moving party is ordered to give notice.

 

Plaintiff AAA T.L.C. Healthcare, Inc.’s Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.  Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED. Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On February 16, 2023, Plaintiff Kim Youngblood (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendant Marton Price (“Defendant”) and Does 1-20, alleging Motor Vehicle and General Negligence causes of action. (Compl., pp. 1, 4, 5.) The underlying facts of this dispute involve a motor vehicle collision between the parties at the intersection of Manchester Avenue and Normandie Avenue, Los Angeles, California, 90047. (Compl., p. 5.)

 

On May 6, 2024, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On June 3, 2024, the Court granted the parties’ stipulation to continue the trial date from August 15, 2024 to December 13, 2024 and to continue all discovery and trial-related deadlines. (6/3/24 Order.)

 

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiff acquired new counsel – Raymond Feldman of Law Offices of John Ye. (6/4/24 Notice of Association of Counsel.)

 

On October 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Reclassify Action from Limited to Unlimited Jurisdiction (the “Motion”).

 

On November 22, 2024, Defendant filed an Opposition.

 

On December 3, 2024, the Court, as part of its rulings, per the oral agreement of the parties, continued the jury trial from December 13, 2024 to June 24, 2025. (12/3/24 Order.)

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 403.040 allows a plaintiff to file a motion for reclassification of an action within the time allowed for that party to amend the initial pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (a).) “A party may amend its pleading once without leave of court at any time before an answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 472, subd. (a).) If the motion is made after the time for the plaintiff to amend the pleading, the motion may only be granted if (1) the case is incorrectly classified; and (2) the plaintiff shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier. (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (b).)

 

In Walker v. Superior Court (1991) 53 Cal.3d 257, 262, the California Supreme Court held that a matter may be reclassified from unlimited to limited only if “the absence of jurisdiction is apparent before trial from the complaint, petition, or related documents,” or “during the course of pretrial litigation, it becomes clear that the matter will ‘necessarily’ result in a verdict below the superior court jurisdictional amount, and the court affords the parties an opportunity to contest transfer.” (Walker, supra, 53 Cal.3d at p. 262.) This high standard is appropriate in light of “the circumscribed procedures and recovery available in the limited civil courts.” (Ytuarte v. Superior Court (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 266, 278.)

 

            In Ytuarte, the Second District Court of Appeal examined the principles set forth in Walker and granted a petition for writ of mandate regarding the trial court’s denial of a party’s motion to reclassify her case from limited to unlimited. (Ytuarte, supra, 129 Cal.App.4th 266.) In so concluding, the Court of Appeal held that “the court should reject the plaintiff’s effort to reclassify the action as unlimited only when the lack of jurisdiction as an ‘unlimited’ case is certain and clear.” (Id., at p. 279.) In other words, the trial court should deny reclassification “only where it appeared to a legal certainty [the party’s] damages would necessarily be $25,000 or less.” (Id., at p. 271.) The Court qualified that a “party must present evidence to demonstrate a possibility that the verdict will exceed $25,000,” and the trial court must review the record to determine “whether a judgment in excess of $25,000.00 is obtainable.” (Id., at pp. 277, 279.)

 

III.              Discussion

 

Moving Party Position

 

            Plaintiff seeks to reclassify the action to unlimited jurisdiction on the basis that Plaintiff “claims bilateral knee injury from the subject accident and her orthopedic surgeon recommends surgery costing about $250,000.” (Motion, p. 3; Feldman Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. B.) Plaintiff also states that Defendant has indicated he would pay 80 percent of Plaintiff’s damages. (Motion, p. 3.)

 

Opposition

           

            Defendant opposes reclassification on the grounds that Plaintiff’s Motion is untimely, there is “no good cause articulated by Plaintiff which excuses her from seeking reclassification earlier,” and the action is correctly classified. (Motion, pp. 2, 3.) Indeed, Defendant argues that this matter is correctly classified because “Plaintiff’s medical specials total approximately $13,000 [and] Plaintiff has not been treated for his alleged injuries from the subject accident since August 2021, over three years ago.” (Motion, pp. 3-4; Park Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

                        Reply

           

            Plaintiff highlights Defendant did not dispute that Defendant agreed to pay 80 percent of Plaintiff’s damages. (Reply, p. 2.) Next, Plaintiff notes Defendant does not deny Plaintiff’s claim regarding bilateral knee surgery and future medical expenses; instead, Defendant merely raises the past medical expenses of $13,000. (Reply, p. 2.) Additionally, Plaintiff highlights Defendant misgendered Plaintiff. (Reply, p. 2.) Finally, Plaintiff states that Defendant fails to cite any legal authority for the proposition that Plaintiff’s Motion is untimely, Defendant misconstrues the standard for motions to reclassify, and there would be no prejudice to the parties should the case be reclassified. (Reply, pp. 2-3.)

 

 

            Analysis

 

The instant Motion to Reclassify was filed after the time to amend the Complaint. Thus, Plaintiff must show that the amount in controversy exceeds the jurisdiction of this Court and good cause for the timing of the Motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (b).)

 

As to the amount in controversy, Plaintiff relies on Plaintiff counsel’s declaration and Plaintiff’s medical records to support the claim that the amount in controversy exceeds the jurisdiction of this Court. (Feldman Decl.; Exh. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel states Plaintiff’s orthopedic surgeon recommends bilateral knee surgery from the subject accident costing about $250,000. (Feldman Decl., ¶ 5.) Dated August 12, 2021, Plaintiff’s medical records from Michael d. Smith, M.D., an Orthopaedic Surgeon, state that “based upon the patient’s subjective complaints and objective findings on physical examination, as well as the results of the CT scans, it is within reasonable medical probability [Plaintiff] will require right and/or left total knee replacement surgery in the future.” (Exh. B, p. 7.) Plaintiff’s medical records then list the costs associated with a total knee replacement, which total to be approximately $107,460 to $117,460 per knee replacement. (Exh. B, pp. 7-8.)

 

After reviewing the record, the Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated an Award of over $35,000 is obtainable. (Ytuarte, supra, 129 Cal.App.4th at pp. 277-279.) Plaintiff presents evidence by way of Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration and Plaintiff’s medical records that her projected future medical costs far exceed $35,000.  Plaintiff need only demonstrate the possibility that a $35,000 award is attainable under Ytuarte; in this action, Plaintiff has presented sufficient evidence for the Court to find such an award is possible. (Id., at pp. 277, 279.)

 

The Court reminds the parties that jurisdiction may be defeated only if “it appears to a legal certainty that plaintiff cannot recover the amount [of the] deman[d].” (Maldonado v. Superior Court (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 397, 401, emphasis in original.) Here, it does not appear, to legal certainty, that Plaintiff cannot recover more than $35,000.00. 

 

Plaintiff also has shown good cause for the timing of this Motion. In Plaintiff’s counsel’s meet and confer correspondence with Defendant’s counsel, Plaintiff’s counsel states: “[P]ls advise by tmrw at 5pm if defense will stipulate to reclassify this action to unlimited jurisdiction, or if not, why def so claims[.] [U]nfortunately, the atty of record law office of howard choi appears to have filed this action in limited court looking only at the past meds, not the futures.” (Exh. A, emphasis added; Feldman Decl., ¶ 4; Motion, p. 3.) The Court finds this note and Plaintiff’s change in counsel satisfactorily demonstrates Plaintiff’s delay in raising this issue and is sufficient good cause for the timing of this Motion. (Exh. A.) Moreover, reclassification is appropriate if “during the course of pretrial litigation, it becomes clear that the matter will ‘necessarily’ result in a verdict” above the limited jurisdictional amount, and “the court affords the parties an opportunity to contest transfer.” (Walker, supra, 53 Cal.3d at p. 262, emphasis added.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion is GRANTED.

Plaintiff Kim Youngblood’s Motion to Reclassify Case Under Unlimited Jurisdiction is GRANTED.

 

The Court reclassifies this action from a limited civil jurisdiction action to an unlimited civil jurisdiction action.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to pay the reclassification fee of $140.00 in the next ten (10) days pursuant to CCP § 403.060(a) before this action will be reclassified to an unlimited civil jurisdiction matter. 

 

Hearing on Motion to Compel set for 01/06/2025 and Trial set for 06/24/2025 are advanced to this date and hereby taken off calendar/vacated.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On March 20, 2023, Plaintiff Garth Powelson (“Plaintiff”), proceeding pro per, filed an action against Defendant City of Long Beach (“Defendant”), alleging a single cause of action for Breach of Contract. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

On May 25, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On September 20, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (the “Motion”).

 

On October 28, 2024, the Motion was called for a hearing and only Defendant’s counsel appeared. The Court found Defendant failed to file a meet and confer declaration. Accordingly, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion to allow Defendant to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. (10/28/24 Order.)

 

On November 13, 2024, Defendant filed a supplemental pleading and two supplemental declarations in support of the Motion.

 

To date, no Opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings if either of the following grounds exist: “The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint,” or “[t]he complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438.)

 

The standard for ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings is “ ‘essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer, that is, under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.’ ”  (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321, citing Schabarum v. California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216; Barker v. Hull (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 221, 224; Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. )d).) 

 

“ ‘Matters which are subject to mandatory judicial notice may be treated as part of the complaint and may be considered without notice to the parties. [Citation.] Matters which are subject to permissive judicial notice must be specified in the notice of motion, the supporting points and authorities, or as the court otherwise permits.’ ” (Bezirdjian, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at p. 321.) “ ‘Judgment on the pleadings does not depend upon a resolution of questions of witness credibility or evidentiary conflicts. In fact, judgment on the pleadings must be denied where there are material factual issues that require evidentiary resolution.’ ” (Id., at pp. 321-322.)

 

III.              Discussion

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “the moving party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 439, subd. (a).) The moving party must file and serve with the motion for judgement on the pleadings a declaration stating either “[t]he means by which the moving party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading … and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the claims,” or that “the party who filed the pleading subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the moving party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 439, subd. (a)(3).)

 

The Motion is now accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 439, subdivision (a). (11/13/24 Peters Decl.; 11/13/24 Supp. Peters Decl.) According to the declarations, Defendant’s counsel repeatedly emailed and called Plaintiff, who is proceeding pro se, from October 28, 2024 to November 12, 2024 in attempts to meet and confer via phone. (11/13/24 Peters Decl., ¶¶ 1-18.)

 

On November 13, 2024, after filing Defendant’s Supplemental Pleading in support of the Motion, Defendant’s counsel received a call from Plaintiff on an unknown number. (11/13/24 Supp. Peters Decl., ¶ 1.) The parties discussed the contents of the Motion and the Motion’s continuance to December 9, 2024; however, Plaintiff disagreed with Defendant and “stated he believed the Complaint had pled a cause of action.” (11/13/24 Supp. Peters Decl., ¶ 1.)

 

Thus, the Court finds the meet and confer requirement satisfied.

 

B.     Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

 

Allegations in the Complaint

 

Plaintiff contends he had agreed to purchase a “RAWSON Sailboat, 30’, Sloop Rig” (the “Rawson boat”) from James Devalle (“Devalle”). (Compl., p. 1.) Plaintiff paid $600.00 down and agreed to pay the remaining balance within 30 days or a reasonable time to receive the title. (Compl., p. 1.) On October 4, 2022, Plaintiff paid the balance to Devalle, and title of the Rawson boat was transferred to Plaintiff. (Compl., p. 1, Exh. A.)

 

Prior to Plaintiff’s purchase, the Rawson boat was impounded at Shoreline Marina because Devalle violated a three-hour time limit at the temporary docks. (Compl., p. 1, Exh. B.) However, Devalle declared he paid for this tow and impound fee. (Compl., p. 1, Exh. C.) Plaintiff was unaware of this tow and impound upon purchase. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

After the boat had been towed and on or around September 23, 2022, Plaintiff heard about the prior towing and impound of the Rawson boat. (Compl., p. 1.) Plaintiff continued working on the Rawson boat. However, Plaintiff then heard the Rawson boat had been “Donated” to the Shoreline Marina. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

Plaintiff met with Eric Skelly, Assistant Harbormaster (“Skelly”). (Compl., p. 1.) Plaintiff informed Skelly of his situation with the Rawson boat. (Compl., pp. 1-2.) Skelly then explained to Plaintiff that to retrieve the Rawson boat, Plaintiff needed the title in his name and a mooring/dock to bring the boat to. (Compl., p. 2.)

 

Plaintiff purchased the Rawson boat and reserved a mooring in San Diego Harbor. (Compl., p. 2.) On October 4, 2022, after receiving title to the Rawson boat, Plaintiff returned to Skelly, who produced a “ ‘post-it’ sticker as an invoice for $1,611,” reflecting a $600 fee for the tow, impound, and fees incurred since September 23, 2022. (Compl., pp. 2-3.)

 

Plaintiff contends Skelly did not provide a quote of $1,611 when they met prior to October 2022; rather, Plaintiff states Skelly just told Plaintiff “it would be, ‘a couple hundred dollars. For towing, and storage.’ ” (Compl., p. 3.) Plaintiff argues the charges from September 23, 2022 to October 4, 2022 is “between the Shoreline, the Life Guards who cited then towed this boat, and with Eric Kelly,” and thus have nothing to do with Plaintiff. (Compl., p. 3.) Plaintiff further argues that Skelly double billed the $600 because Devalle already paid $600 for the first tow and impound. (Compl., p. 3.)

 

Plaintiff requests the Court should award him “the boat [he] paid for, worked on, and obtained mooring for in San Diego.” (Compl., p. 3.)

 

Moving Party Position

 

Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings on the grounds Plaintiff failed to plead a prima facie case for breach of contract and the Defendant is immune from liability under Government Code, sections 818.8 and 822.2. (Motion, p. 2.) As to the breach of contract claim, Defendant contends Plaintiff has failed to plead the existence of a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant, or any employee of Defendant. (Motion, p. 4.) Indeed, Defendant states that the only contract pled in the Complaint is one between Plaintiff and Devalle regarding Plaintiff’s purchase of the Rawson boat. (Motion, p. 5.)

 

Contrary to Plaintiff’s portrayal, Defendant alleges Skelly’s statements to Plaintiff did not create a contract because: (1) there was an absence of consideration; (2) Plaintiff fails to allege his performance or excuse for nonperformance under the alleged contract as, even if there was a contract for Plaintiff to pay outstanding fees to Skelly in exchange for the Rawson boat, Plaintiff has not paid the fees and thus failed in performance of his obligations; and (3) Plaintiff fails to allege how Defendant breached the contract since Defendant merely followed the Long Beach Municipal Code – “[s]ince the boat was re-impounded prior to Plaintiff’s purchase, Plaintiff was obligated to pay all costs incurred in moving, keeping, and storing the sailboat, even if such costs were incurred prior to his ownership of the sailboat. Plaintiff cannot cite to any authority to the contrary City merely enforced its Marina rules and regulations. This conduct does not and cannot amount to any actionable contract breach.” (Motion, pp. 4-6.)

 

Additionally, Defendant argues it is immune from liability under Government Code sections 818.8 and 822.2 in the event Plaintiff’s Complaint is construed to plead a misrepresentation, false promise, or fraud claim on behalf of Skelly. (Motion, pp. 6-7.) Further, Defendant contends the Complaint does not sufficiently allege that Skelly acted with corruption or malice so as to avoid Government Code section 822.2 immunity. (Motion, p. 7.)

 

Analysis  

 

A cause of action for breach of contract must plead “(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for non-performance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.” (Acoustics, Inc. v. Trepte Constr. Co. (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 887, 913.) A written contract can be pled by alleging its making and attaching a copy to the complaint or by alleging the making and the substance of the relevant terms. (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.) The terms of an “oral contract may be pleaded generally as to its effect, because it is rarely possible to allege the exact words.” (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 635, 640.) Finally, an implied-in-fact contract can be alleged based on the promisor’s conduct. (Gomez v. Lincare, Inc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 508, 525.)  “ ‘A cause of action for breach of implied contract has the same elements as does a cause of action for breach of contract, except that the promise is not expressed in words but is implied from the promisor's conduct.’ ” (Ibid.)

 

Government Code section 818.8 states that “[a] public entity is not liable for an injury caused by misrepresentation by an employee of the public entity, whether or not such misrepresentation be negligent or intentional.”

 

Government Code section 822.2 states that “[a] public employee acting in the scope of his employment is not liable for an injury caused by his misrepresentation, whether or not such misrepresentation be negligent or intentional, unless he is guilty of actual fraud, corruption or actual malice.”

 

Here, Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings on the grounds the Complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a breach of contract or misrepresentation cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438.) The Court agrees.

 

For the first element of a breach of contract claim – the existence of a contract – the Complaint alleges some kind of agreement between Devalle and Plaintiff; however, no facts are alleged regarding the relevant terms of an agreement or services provided by Defendant. Thus, Plaintiff fails to plead either a written or oral contract between Plaintiff and Defendant. At most, the Complaint could be construed to allege an implied-in-fact contract based on Skelly’s conduct of promising a reduced amount in storage fees in exchange for Plaintiff obtaining title to the Rawson boat and mooring. (Gomez, supra, 173 Cal.App.4th at p. 525.)

 

Nonetheless, Plaintiff also fails to plead breach. Plaintiff does not plead how Defendant breached the alleged agreement – while Plaintiff pleads Skelly told Plaintiff “it would be, ‘a couple hundred dollars. For towing, and storage’ ” (Compl., p. 3) and then later increased the amount of fees, Plaintiff does not plead Skelly indicated the initial estimate would be a fixed cost not subject to increase as days (and storage fees) passed. Plaintiff pleads he did not obtain title until around 11 days after the initial conversation with Skelly, which is additional days of storage of the Rawson boat. (Compl., pp. 1-3.) Thus, Plaintiff fails to plead Defendant breached in this aspect of the agreement as Plaintiff pleads that Defendant satisfied its end of the alleged agreement – Defendant offered to give Plaintiff the Rawson boat in exchange for Plaintiff’s payment of towing and storage fees. As to Plaintiff’s double-billing claim regarding the $600 impound and towing fees, Plaintiff does not plead facts demonstrating Plaintiff’s immunity from costs for the second impound and towing fee separate and apart from the first towing and impound fee paid by Devalle. Thus, Plaintiff fails to plead a breach by Defendant in this regard as well.

 

Finally, to the extent Plaintiff’s Complaint may be construed to allege a claim of misrepresentation as to the towing and storage fees, the Court finds Defendant is immune from such a claim. (Gov. Code, §§ 818.8, 822.2.)

 

Accordingly, the Motion is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the breach of contract cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subds. (h), (i).)  

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

Defendant City of Long Beach’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Plaintiff’ First Amended Complaint is ordered to be filed within 30 days of this Court’s order. 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff Robertson’s Ready Mix, Ltd. (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendants Robert D. O’Neill (“Robert”) and Meghan O’Neill (“Meghan”) (“Defendants”) and Does 1-50 alleging causes of action for Breach of Contract, Quantum Meruit, Common Count – Account Stated, Common Count – Open Book, and To Enforce Mechanic’s Lien. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Pendency of Action, stating an action to foreclose and enforce a mechanic’s lien had commenced. (9/23/24 Notice of Pendency of Action.)

 

On October 17, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens (the “Motion”).

 

On November 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition.

 

On November 26, 2024, Defendant filed a Reply.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Moving Party Position

 

Defendant moves for an order expunging the Notice of Pendency of Action (“lis pendens”) recorded against the real property located at 1330 8th Street, Manhattan Beach, California 90206 (the “Property”). Defendant asserts the lis pendens was improperly filed and is not supported by a valid real property claim. Specifically, Defendant states the lis pendens must be expunged because no valid contract exists between Plaintiff and Defendant – any “work performed was unauthorized, and no agreement was reached for payment.” Defendant contends Plaintiff’s claim relies on an alleged oral agreement involving $1,188.08, which exceeds the threshold for an oral contract under Business and Professions Code section 7159 and is invalid under Civil Code section 1624, which requires certain contracts – including those for real property improvements exceeding $500 – to be in writing.

 

Next, Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to meet the requirements for a mechanic’s lien because Plaintiff failed to “provide a written contract or an invoice for the services means that the mechanics’ lien and the lis pendens filed in connection with it are invalid.”

 

Thus, Defendant argues the lis pendens should be expunged under Code of Civil Procedure sections 405.30 and 405.31. Defendant also requests an award of reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs.

 

Opposition

 

In Opposition, Plaintiff contends the Motion is procedurally defective as it conflates the dates of the recordation of the mechanic’s lien on June 26, 2024 with the recordation of the lis pendens on September 23, 2024. (Opp., p. 4.) Next, Plaintiff argues the mechanic’s lien is a real property claim, and Defendant’s claims regarding Business and Professions Code section 7159 and Civil Code section 1624 are inapplicable. (Opp., pp. 4-7.) In any event, Plaintiff argues the agreement was “in fact memorialized in writing” and the statute of frauds is inapplicable as a defense. (Opp., p. 7.) Regardless, Plaintiff argues the mechanic’s lien claim does not depend on the validity of Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim because the mechanic’s lien claim arises from the California constitution. Thus, Plaintiff “has established a probable validity of prevailing on its claims.” (Opp., p. 7.)

 

Finally, Plaintiff contends Defendant is not entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs because either Plaintiff is the prevailing party or, in the event Defendant prevails, Defendant is not represented by counsel and thus attorneys’ fees are not available to Defendant. (Opp., p. 8.) Instead, Plaintiff requests attorneys’ fees in the amount of $4,375.00. (Opp., pp. 8-9.)

 

Reply

 

            In Reply, Defendant reiterates that Plaintiff’s lis pendens and mechanic’s lien are procedurally and substantively defective. (Reply, p. 1.) Defendant argues the mechanic’s lien was recorded multiple days past the statutory deadline, which invalidates the lien; Plaintiff failed to serve proper notice on all property owners, which renders the lien void; and Defendant paid the lien amount of $1,188.08, which extinguished the lien. (Reply, pp. 1-6.)

 

            Additionally, Defendant contends Plaintiff’s counsel “has made demonstrably false statements in her declaration that mislead the Court and undermine [Defendant]’s credibility.” (Reply, pp. 6-7.) Defendant contends these “misrepresentations are serious violations of her ethical obligations and warrant sanctions under California Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, enhanced damages, and referral for a perjury investigation.” (Reply, p. 7.) Defendant alleges Plaintiff’s counsel mischaracterizes the mechanic’s lien filing date, makes false claims regarding payment status, and misrepresents Plaintiff’s collection efforts. (Reply, pp. 7-8.)

 

Analysis

 

“At any time after notice of pendency of action has been recorded, any party, or any nonparty with an interest in the real property affected thereby, may apply to the court in which the action is pending to expunge the notice.… Evidence or declarations may be filed with the motion to expunge the notice.… The claimant shall have the burden of proof under Sections 405.31 and 405.32.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.30.) The “claimant” is the “party to an action who asserts a real property claim and records a notice of the pendency of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.1.) The court shall order the notice expunged if (1) the court “finds that the claimant has not established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim,” or (2) the Court “finds that the pleading on which the notice is based does not contain a real property claim.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 405.31-405.32.) The Court shall not order an undertaking to be given as a condition of expunging the notice where the Court finds the pleading does not contain a real property claim or the claimant has not established the probable validity of the real property claim. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 405.31-405.32.)

 

“ ‘Real property claim’ means the cause or causes of action in a pleading which would, if meritorious, affect (a) title to, or the right to possession of, specific real property or (b) the use of an easement identified in the pleading, other than an easement obtained pursuant to statute by any regulated public utility.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.4.)

 

Civil Code section 8416 states that “[a] claim of mechanics lien shall be a written statement, signed and verified by the claimant, containing all of the following:

 

(1) A statement of the claimant’s demand after deducting all just credits and offsets.

(2) The name of the owner or reputed owner, if known.

(3) A general statement of the kind of work furnished by the claimant.

(4) The name of the person by whom the claimant was employed or to whom the claimant furnished work.

(5) A description of the site sufficient for identification.

(6) The claimant’s address.

(7) A proof of service affidavit completed and signed by the person serving a copy of the claim of mechanics lien pursuant to subdivision (c). The affidavit shall show the date, place, and manner of service, and facts showing that the service was made in accordance with this section. The affidavit shall show the name and address of the owner or reputed owner upon whom the copy of the claim of mechanics lien was served pursuant to paragraphs (1) or (2) of subdivision (c), and the title or capacity in which the person or entity was served.

(8) [A certain] statement, printed in at least 10-point boldface type. The letters of the last sentence shall be printed in uppercase type, excepting the Internet Web site address of the Contractors’ State License Board, which shall be printed in lowercase type.”

 

            (Civ. Code, § 8416, subd. (a).)

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Defendant appears to mistakenly refer to the mechanic’s lien recorded on June 26, 2024 as the lis pendens filed on September 23, 2024. The Court construes the Motion to pertain to the lis pendens and addresses the merits accordingly.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has established that the Complaint on which the lis pendens is based contains a real property claim of a mechanic’s lien. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 405.31-405.32) As such, the Court need not address the breach of contract claim.

 

Further, the Court also finds Plaintiff has established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the claim regarding the mechanic’s lien.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 405.31-405.32) Plaintiff submits the written statement of the mechanics’ lien which appears in substantial conformance with the statutory requirements. (Civ. Code, § 8416, subd. (a); Opp., Exh. 1.) Defendant’s challenges in the Reply regarding timeliness, notice, and payment of the lien are either unsupported, misconstrued, or contradicted by Plaintiff’s evidence. (Opp., Exh. 1, 4, 8; Lee Decl.)

 

Finally, Defendant’s remaining arguments are not pertinent or applicable to the instant Motion. The Court declines to address these arguments at this time.

 

Additionally, Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff’s in-house legal counsel, Janice Lee, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7. (Reply, p. 8.) However, Defendant fails to raise this claim in a separate motion as is statutorily required. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (c)(1).) Thus, this request is DENIED.

 

Attorneys’ Fees

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 405.38 provides that, “[t]he court shall direct that the party prevailing on [a motion to expunge lis pendens] be awarded the reasonable attorney’s fees and costs of making or opposing the motion unless the court finds that the other party acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of attorney’s fees and costs unjust.”


            Here, Plaintiff is the prevailing party and is thus entitled to reasonable attorney fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.38.) Plaintiff requests attorney fees and costs of $4,375, reflecting fees for two attorneys who bill at different hourly rates. (Brunner Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.) Plaintiff states the attorneys incurred costs of seven hours at $425.00 an hour and four hours at $350.00 an hour to draft the Opposition and supporting declarations, review the Reply brief, and attend the Motion hearing. (Brunner Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.)

 

            Given the brevity of the Reply brief and that Plaintiff erroneously duplicated 1.5 hours in the fees and costs calculation, Plaintiff’s request is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $2,325.

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant Robert O’Neill’s Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens and Request for Attorneys’ Fees and Costs are DENIED.

 

Plaintiff’s request for Attorney’s Fees and Costs is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $2,325. 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
