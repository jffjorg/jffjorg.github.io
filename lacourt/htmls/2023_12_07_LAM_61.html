DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV42444    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 61

 

Defendants Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Desiree Thomas, and Memorial Health Services Motion for Protective Order regarding Deposition of Ikenna Mmeje is GRANTED, and the scope of Mmeje’s deposition is limited to the hernia pilot program, the proctoring issue, Plaintiff’s termination, discrimination, harassment, or retaliation faced by Plaintiff, and conversations with or about Plaintiff. Plaintiff is also directed to meet and confer with Defendants to select an alternative date of deposition that works for Plaintiff, Mmeje, and his counsel.

 

Defendants to give notice.

 

 

I.                   MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1.)

 

Additionally, “[b]efore, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc. 2025.420, subd. (a).) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b).)

Defendant Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Desiree Thomas, and Memorial Health Services (Defendants) move for an order either quashing the deposition subpoena issued to LBMMC’s former Chief Operating Officer Ikenna Mmeje (Mmeje) or else limiting its scope to particular matters and requiring that it take place on a date when Mmeje is available. (Motion at p. 13.)

Defendants claim that a subpoena was served on Mmeje on September 13, 2023, noticing his deposition to take place on September 28, 2023. (Mmeje Decl. ¶ 3.) Mmeje was unavailable on that date, and provided other dates to Plaintiff as alternatives, seeking Plaintiff’s feedback. (Mmeje Decl. ¶ 3; Mino Decl. Exh. I.) Defendants also told Plaintiff that Mmeje was a former apex employee and that Plaintiff therefore had to satisfy a burden that Mmeje had access to relevant evidence that could not elsewhere be obtained before seeking his deposition. (Mino Decl. Exh. 1.) When Plaintiff responded in an email dated September 15, 2023, that Mmeje had knowledge concerning “the proctoring issue,” the “pilot study of robotic inguinal hernia repair,” and direct conversations with and about Plaintiff and his underlying complaints, (Mino Decl. Exh. 1.) Defendants on September 19 offered dates of availability for Mmeje, and further offered to withdraw any objection based on the apex-employee doctrine if Plaintiff agreed to limit the scope of his deposition to the matters of relevance that he had already described. (Mino Decl. Exh. 1.) Plaintiff declined to agree to any change in date or limitation in the scope of the deposition. (Ibid.) The present motion was filed the day before the deposition was to take place.

Defendants invoke the apex-employee doctrine, which was set out in the case Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282. In that case, a plaintiff sued a company and its CEO for various torts and attempted to depose the CEO. (Id. at pp. 1285–86.) But the only connections that the plaintiff could establish between the CEO and the action were plaintiff’s attempts to forward emails explaining his claims to the CEO, but which the CEO had never seen. (Id. at p. 1286.) The CEO thus declared that he lacked any relevant knowledge regarding the action. (Id. at p. 1285–86.) The court held as follows:

[W]hen a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president or other official at the highest level of corporate management, and that official moves for a protective order to prohibit the deposition, the trial court should first determine whether the plaintiff has shown good cause that the official has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information. If not, as will presumably often be the case in the instance of a large national or international corporation, the trial court should issue the protective order and first require the plaintiff to obtain the necessary discovery through less-intrusive methods. These would include interrogatories directed to the high-level official to explore the state of his or her knowledge or involvement in plaintiff's case; the deposition of lower-level employees with appropriate knowledge and involvement in the subject matter of the litigation; and the organizational deposition of the corporation itself, which will require the corporation to produce for deposition the most qualified officer or employee to testify on its behalf as to the specified matters to be raised at the deposition. (§ 2025, subd. (d)(6).) Should these avenues be exhausted, and the plaintiff make a colorable showing of good cause that the high-level official possesses necessary information to the case, the trial court may then lift the protective order and allow the deposition to proceed.

(Id. at p. 1289.) In later cases, it has been explained that this rule is “based upon the recognition that an official's time and the exigencies of his everyday business would be severely impeded if every plaintiff filing a complaint against an agency head, in his official capacity, were allowed to take his oral deposition. Such procedure would be contrary to the public interest, plus the fact that ordinarily the head of an agency has little or no knowledge of the facts of the case.” (Nagle v. Superior Court (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1465, 1468.) “An exception will be made to this rule only when the deposing party makes two showings. First, the deposing party must show that the government official has direct personal factual information pertaining to material issues in the action. Second, the deposing party must also show the information to be gained from the deposition is not available through any other source.” (Contractors’ State License Board v. Superior Court (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 125, 128 [applying Liberty Mutual to a government context].)

Plaintiff here has made a sufficient showing that the deposition of Mmeje should take place, despite his status as a former COO of LBMMC (and current status as CEO of USC Hospital Arcadia). (Mmeje Decl. ¶ 2.) Although Plaintiff articulated a basis for the relevance of Mmeje’s deposition that included his “day-to-day management during the relevant period of this matter,” Plaintiff has also presented evidence suggesting that Mmeje has direct personal factual information pertaining to material issues, which Plaintiff cannot otherwise obtain. Plaintiff alleges that he had a meeting with Mmeje in November 2019 in which he raised his complaints (later memorialized in an email from Mmeje) and that another witness has testified in deposition that Mmeje made comments adverse to Plaintiff that may have resulted in his removal from a panel schedule or contributed to his termination. (Ghobrial Decl. ¶¶ 6–7, Exhs. E, F.) Mmeje is thus likely in a unique position to confirm what complaints Plaintiff made and any actions that were taken in response.

This does not mean, however, that Defendants’ motion for a protective order is without merit. For one, Plaintiff unilaterally noticed the date of Mmeje’s deposition for a day and time in which he was unavailable, and refused to consider repeated offers of alternative dates. Plaintiff articulates no reason why he could not confer with Defendants and Mmeje regarding an available date for deposition. (Opposition at p. 10; see LASC Civility Guidelines, subd. (e)(2).)[1]

Moreover, Plaintiff does not explain why he could not agree to define the scope of his deposition of Mmeje, when Defendants’ proposed scope of deposition, both in informal correspondence and in the present motion, precisely tracks the grounds of relevance that Plaintiff offers in opposition to this motion. Defendants seek an order limiting the scope of deposition to any conversation he has had with or about Plaintiff, the hernia pilot program, the proctoring issue, and matters concerning discrimination, retaliation, or termination. (Motion at p. 13; Reply at p. 10; Mino Decl. Exh. 1.) Plaintiff offers no specific objection to this proposed scope, much as he offered no response to any proposed definition of the scope in the informal correspondence that preceded the motion. Accordingly, a protective order is therefore proper, limiting the scope of the Mmeje deposition to the hernia pilot program, the proctoring issue, Plaintiff’s termination, discrimination, harassment, or retaliation faced by Plaintiff, and conversations with or about Plaintiff.

Plaintiff argues that Defendants should be held in contempt for failing to appear for the September 28, 2023 deposition. (Opposition at pp. 10–11.) No such order is warranted. Plaintiff was on notice that Mmeje was not available for deposition on the date that Plaintiff had scheduled, and was further aware prior to the deposition that Defendants had filed the present motion for a protective order under Code of Civil Procedure  sections 1987.1 and 2025.420. As the arguments advanced in the present motion are meritorious, Plaintiff had no reasonable expectation that the deposition would proceed as scheduled.

The motion for protective order is GRANTED, and the scope of Mmeje’s deposition is limited to the hernia pilot program, the proctoring issue, Plaintiff’s termination, discrimination, harassment, or retaliation faced by Plaintiff, and conversations with or about Plaintiff. Plaintiff is also directed to meet and confer with Defendants to select an alternative date of deposition that works for Plaintiff, Mmeje, and his counsel.

 


[1] Plaintiff argues that he withdrew a prior subpoena to accommodate Mmeje’s “scheduling constraints.” (Opposition at p. 10.) But the evidence shows that Plaintiff withdrew a prior, unilaterally scheduled subpoena because it was not properly served. (Ghobrial Decl. Exh. D, August 16, 2023 Email.) There was no conference regarding the new deposition date.


Case Number: 22STCV02561    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 61

Defendant Armen Ambarachyan’s Motion to Apply Ohio Law is DENIED.

 

Defendant Armen Ambarachyan’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED as to the second and third causes of action.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

I.                   DEMURRER

A demurrer should be sustained only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Pro., §§ 430.30, et seq.) A court should sustain a demurrer if a complaint does not allege facts that are legally sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (See id. § 430.10, subd. (e).) As the Supreme Court held in Blank v. Kirwan (1985) Cal.3d 311: “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. . . . Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Id. at p. 318; see also Hahn. v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”))

 

“In determining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer … if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Such demurrers “are disfavored, and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.” (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Insurance Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848.)

 

A demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if the complaint, liberally construed, can state a cause of action under any theory or if there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured by amendment. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1081.) The demurrer also may be sustained without leave to amend where the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead render it probable plaintiff cannot state a cause of action. (Krawitz v. Rusch (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 957, 967.)

 

Defendant Armen Ambarachyan (Defendant) demurrers to the second and third causes of action for violations of the Homeowner’s Bill of Rights and Quiet Title. Defendant argues that the HBOR provisions identified require that the subject property be owner-occupied, which the property at issue here is not. (Demurrer at pp. 9–11.) Defendant also argues that Plaintiff cannot obtain either an injunction prohibiting a sale or damages for a completed sale, as Defendant has rescinded the notice of default. (Demurrer at pp. 11–13.) Defendant also argues that the third cause of action for quiet title, based on the same notice of default, is moot, and is further filed in the wrong county. (Demurrer at p. 13.) Defendant also brings a motion to dismiss the first cause of action for usury under Ohio law, also based on the contention that Plaintiff is a resident of Ohio, under which law the interest rate underlying the loan is not usurious. (Motion at pp. 9–17.)

 

As an initial matter, no relief is appropriate on either the demurrer or the motion to apply Ohio law to the extent they rely on Plaintiff’s residence. Although Defendant contends that Plaintiff is a resident of Ohio, the FAC (and the Complaint before it) alleged that Plaintiff’s principal residence is in California, and is indeed on the property at issue in this action. (FAC ¶ 1; Complaint ¶ 3.) This allegation is not rebutted by the simultaneous allegation that the property was also ex-Plaintiff Francesca Fuentes’s primary residence. (Complaint ¶ 3.) Nor is it rebutted by any judicially noticeable matters, since allegations made in other complaints filed against Plaintiff are not judicially noticeable. (See RJN Exhs. A–D; see Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 389, 400 [“Courts can take judicial notice of the existence, content and authenticity of public records and other specified documents, but do not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted in those documents”].) Defendant’s motion to apply Ohio law has no cited procedural basis, and because its upshot is the dismissal of the first cause of action (Motion at pp. 17–18), the court is constrained to interpret it as a demurrer or motion for judgment on the pleadings, with the qualification that it is an evidence-based motion that does not admit the allegations of the FAC. Such a motion is defective. (Brinsmead v. Elk Grove Unified School District (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 583, 587 [“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded”].)

 

However, Defendant’s arguments with respect to the second and third causes of action are persuasive. First, Plaintiff may not obtain either injunctive relief or damages for his alleged violations of HBOR, since the notice of default of which he complains has been rescinded, and no trustee’s sale has taken place. An action under Plaintiff’s claimed HBOR violations may lie “to enjoin a material violation” of the relevant Code sections, or to claim damages “[a]fter a trustee’s deed upon sale has been recorded.” (Civ. Code §§ 2924.12, subd. (a), (b); 2924.19, subd. (a), (b).) But after the rescission of the offending notice, there is nothing to enjoin, and in the absence of a sale, there are no damages no award. Defendant recorded a rescission of its notice of default in Riverside County on September 12, 2023. (RJN Exh. E.; see Jenkins v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 497, 537 [taking judicial notice of notice of default], disapproved on other grounds by Yvanova v. New Century Mortgage Corp. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 919 .) Nor has any trustee’s sale taken place, given the FAC’s allegation that Plaintiff is the fee simple owner of the property. (FAC ¶ 1.) Thus there is no live controversy under HBOR for this court to adjudicate.

Plaintiff’s quiet title claim is similarly infirm, resting as it does upon the allegedly void notice of default. (FAC ¶ 54–58.) Plaintiff’s opposition does not address any arguments based on mootness or the rescission of the notice of default.

The demurrer is therefore SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the second and third causes of action. The motion to apply Ohio law is DENIED.

Defendant seeks attorney fees for prevailing on HBOR claims under Civil Code § 2924.12, subd. (h). (Demurrer at p. 15.) Defendant is entitled to no attorney fees under that statute, since that statute provides attorney fees to “a prevailing borrower.” (Civ. Code § 2924.12, subd. (h).Defendant is not the borrower in this action.
