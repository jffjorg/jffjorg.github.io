DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01283    Hearing Date: December 18, 2023    Dept: 107

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with 10 days’ leave to amend. Defendant’s demurrer to the second and fifth causes of action is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.

 

Timeline

 

            Plaintiff filed their complaint on March 22, 2023, alleging the following causes of action against Defendant:

1.      Breach of Contract;

2.      Promissory Fraud;

3.      Accounting;

4.      Unjust Enrichment; and

5.      Elder Abuse.

 

This action arises out of a construction contract entered into between Plaintiff and Defendant on January 2, 2020 pertaining to a remodel of Plaintiff’s condominium (the “Residence”) (the “Agreement”). Plaintiff alleged Defendant breached the agreement and seeks to recover compensatory damages, punitive damages, enhanced damages for elder abuse, interest, attorneys’ fees and costs of suit, as well as accounting, the creation of a trust over property held by Defendant, and an equitable lien over proceeds from Plaintiff’s property.

 

            On August 11, 2023, Defendant filed a demurrer as to the second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute those causes of action. Defendant also moved to strike punitive damages from Plaintiff’s prayer.

 

            Plaintiff opposed the demurrer and motion to strike in papers filed on November 27, 2023. Defendant replied on December 4, 2023.

 

Meet and Confer

           

The meet and confer requirement has been met. (See Ring Decl.)

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law. Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358. The court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded. Ibid. A demurrer accepts as true all well pleaded facts and those facts of which the court can take judicial notice but not deductions, contentions, or conclusions of law or fact. Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078. Doubt in the complaint may be resolved against the plaintiff and facts not alleged are presumed not to exist. Kramer v. Intuit Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 574, 578. “As a general rule in testing a pleading against a demurrer, the Facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal. App. 3d 593, 604.

 

Further, the court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.) “A motion to strike does not lie to attack a complaint for insufficiency of allegations to justify relief; that is a ground for general demurrer.” Pierson v. Sharp Memorial Hospital, Inc. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 340, 342; see also Warren v. Atchison, T. & S. F. Ry. Co. (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 24, 41 (the grounds that the allegations were insufficient as a matter of law to constitute the cause of action were grounds for demurrer and were not proper grounds for a motion to strike). “[A] motion to strike is generally used to reach defects in a pleading which are not subject to demurrer.” Pierson 216 Cal.App.3d at 342.

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. See, e.g., Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768 (“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.

 

Analysis

 

i.                    Promissory Fraud

 

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1059-1060. The falsity of the promise is sufficiently pled “with a general allegation the promise was made without an intention of performance. . . . Hence, the only necessary averment is the general statement that the promise was made without the intention to perform it, or that the defendant did not intend to perform it.” Id, 1060 (emphasis added).

 

Here, Plaintiff sufficiently pled that Defendant made a false representation that he would complete performance under the Agreement if Plaintiff provided an additional progress payment; Plaintiff relied on that promise and provided an additional progress payment; Defendant did not intend to complete performance when he asked for such payment; and Plaintiff was harmed as a result of his reliance on the false representation. (Complaint ¶ 44-53.)

 

In opposition and in reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cause of action for fraud simply repeats his first cause of action for breach of contract. Breach of contract and fraud are separate and distinct causes of action, and Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to establish both.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for promissory fraud is overruled.

 

ii.                  Accounting

 

An accounting is an equitable proceeding which is proper where there is an unascertained amount owing that cannot be determined without an examination of the books to determine what is due and owing. An action for an accounting has two elements: (1) “that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting” and (2) “that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.” Teselle, v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179. Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to allege the existence of such a relationship between the parties. Plaintiff does not argue this point. (Opposition 2:27-3:8.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for accounting is sustained without leave to amend.

 

iii.                Unjust Enrichment

 

Defendant argues that unjust enrichment is not a cause of action but an “effect” or a “general principle” synonymous with restitution (Demurrer 8:14-22, citing Lauriedale Associates, Ltd. v. Wilson (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1439, 1448; Dinosaur Development, Inc. v. White (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1310, 1315; and Melchior v. New Line Productions, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 779, 793. Defendant concludes that “restitution is a remedy and not a freestanding cause of action.” Reid v. City of San Diego (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 343, 362. Plaintiff does not argue this point and asks that the Court construe this cause of action as a remedy request instead. (Opposition 3:13-20.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for unjust enrichment is sustained with leave to amend for the limited purpose of adding unjust enrichment and/or restitution to Plaintiff’s prayer.

 

iv.                Elder Abuse

 

A party engages in financial elder abuse by “misappropriating funds to which an elder is entitled under a contract.” Paslay v. State Farm Gen. Ins. Co., (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 639, 656. Defendant demurs to this cause of action on substantially the same grounds as his demurrer to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for fraud. Defendant seeks to reduce Plaintiff’s pleadings to one cause of action for breach of contract. Defendant denies that Plaintiff made pleadings sufficient to establish fraud. As stated above, this court disagrees because “a general statement that [a] promise was made without the intention to perform it” is sufficient to establish a pleading of scienter. Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1060.

 

Defendant also makes the argument that the additional payment Plaintiff alleges to have made in reliance of Defendant’s false representation was an amount that Plaintiff had already agreed to pay pursuant to the Agreement. However, Plaintiff alleged that when he made the payment, the amount was “as-yet-unearned” (Complaint ¶ 21) and fell outside of the agreed upon payment plan (Exh. A, 2.)

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action for elder abuse is overruled.

 

v.                  Defendant’s Motion to Strike Punitive Damages

 

                        Defendant seeks to strike punitive damages from the pleadings on the grounds that Plaintiff did not state a prima facie claim of fraud. In light of the evidence and pleadings, the Court deems that Plaintiff sufficiently pleaded the causes of action for promissory estoppel and elder abuse. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages from the pleadings is denied.

Tentative Ruling

           

Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with 10 days’ leave to amend. Defendant’s demurrer to the second and fifth causes of action is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.

Case Number: 23VECV01477    Hearing Date: December 18, 2023    Dept: 107

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED with leave to amend.

 

Timeline

 

            Plaintiffs filed their complaint on April 4, 2023, alleging the following causes of action against Park Reseda Condominium Association Inc., LB Property Management Inc., GTO Security Corp. (“GTO” or “Defendant”), Raymundo Reducindo Oliva (“Reducindo”), and Does 1 through 40 (collectively, “Defendants”):

 

1.      Battery;

2.      Assault;

3.      Negligence;

4.      Negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of employee; and

5.      Loss of consortium.

 

This action arises out of an alleged altercation between Plaintiffs and Reducindo in the parking lot of Park Reseda. Plaintiffs allege the following: Plaintiffs are husband and wife. Plaintiffs further allege that Reducindo was a security guard for Park Reseda and an employee of GTO. According to Plaintiffs, on the night of the incident, Plaintiffs observed that Reducindo was inebriated on the job. Plaintiffs called Reducindo’s supervisor to notify him. After overhearing the call, Plaintiffs allege that Reducindo became enraged and attacked Plaintiffs, who incurred medical expenses. Plaintiffs seek relief in the form of general, special, consequential, incidental, exemplary and punitive damages against Defendants as well as interest, attorneys’ fees, costs, and other remedies that may be available under California law.

 

            On November 13, 2023, GTO filed a motion to strike sections of Plaintiffs’ complaint pertaining to punitive damages and GTO’s alleged ratification of Reducindo’s conduct. Plaintiffs filed opposition papers on December 4, 2023. Defendant replied on December 11, 2023.

 

Meet and Confer

           

The meet and confer requirement has been met. See Snyder Decl.

 

Legal Standard

 

A court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. Code Civ. Proc., § 436. The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. Code Civ. Proc., § 437.

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. See, e.g., Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768 (“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.

 

Analysis

 

i.                    Timeliness of the motion

 

Similar to a demurrer, a motion to strike must be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. However, courts may still consider an untimely motion to strike if doing so does not prejudice the non-moving party.

 

Here, Plaintiffs served the complaint on GTO on June 5, 2023. The parties subsequently agreed on an extension to file a responsive pleading until September 25, 2023. Snyder Decl. ¶ 2-3. GTO filed the instant motion on November 13, 2023, 49 days after the agreed upon deadline. Plaintiffs have not taken steps to obtain a default judgment, nor have they demonstrated that the delay prejudiced them. Therefore, the Court will review the motion on the merits because doing so does not affect Plaintiffs’ substantial rights. See Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.

 

ii.                  Whether punitive damages are available for loss of consortium

 

The pleadings allege that Reducindo “battered” Gomez, including “grabbing” and “striking” him. The pleadings do not allege that Reducindo battered Valencia, even though it is alleged that he pepper sprayed both Gomez and Valencia. Complaint ¶ 17. Defendant argues that loss of consortium is a derivative claim that cannot itself warrant punitive damages. As a result, any punitive damage allegations cannot inure to the benefit of Valencia and should be stricken.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs point to a California Supreme Court ruling which expressly states that “a common law action for loss of consortium is a civil action sounding in tort, and therefore punitive damages are available.” Boeken v. Philip Morris USA, Inc. (2010) 48 Cal.4th 788, 796. Although the language cited is not binding precedent but rather dictum, as Defendant points out in its Reply, the Court will abide by it.

 

iii.                Whether punitive damages are warranted by the pleadings

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes punitive damages only where “it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a). In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must allege facts to demonstrate these elements. College Hospital, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.

 

Defendant is a corporate entity. Under California Civil Code section 3294(b),

 

“[a]n employer shall not be liable for [exemplary damages] based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Emphasis added.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs argue that GTO ratified Reducindo’s conduct by “allowing a drunk, unlicensed security guard to engage with the public.” Opposition 6:4-5. However, Plaintiffs failed to allege that GTO knew that Reducindo did not have a security guard license, or that GTO had advance knowledge that Reducindo goes to work in an inebriated state. Plaintiffs also failed to identify any officer, director or managing agent of GTO to whom acts that warrant punitive damages could be imputed. The deficiencies in the pleadings are accentuated by the well-established rule that claims for punitive damages must be pled specifically. See, e.g., Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d. 864.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff have not made sufficient pleadings to allege that punitive damages against Defendant GTO are warranted.

 

iv.                Leave to amend

 

            Notwithstanding the above, the Court notes that Defendant’s motion may not be the proper recourse for calling out Plaintiffs’ inadequate prayer for punitive damages. “A motion to strike does not lie to attack a complaint for insufficiency of allegations to justify relief; that is a ground for general demurrer.” Pierson v. Sharp Memorial Hospital, Inc. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 340, 342; see also Warren v. Atchison, T. & S. F. Ry. Co. (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 24, 41 (the grounds that the allegations were insufficient as a matter of law to constitute the cause of action were grounds for demurrer and were not proper grounds for a motion to strike). “[A] motion to strike is generally used to reach defects in a pleading which are not subject to demurrer.” Pierson 216 Cal.App.3d at 342.

 

            Plaintiffs contend that leave to amend is usually allowed when the defect is correctible. However, the burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318. Plaintiffs have made no such showing in their Opposition. Nevertheless, in light of Defendant’s use of a motion to strike rather than a demurrer to attack the complaint’s failure to justify the relief of punitive damages, the Court grants Plaintiffs leave to amend. 

 

Tentative Ruling

           

            Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED with leave to amend.
