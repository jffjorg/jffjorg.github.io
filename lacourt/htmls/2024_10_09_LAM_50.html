DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV46503    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

curtis r. olson,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

vidala aaronoff, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

19STCV46503

Hearing Date:

October 9, 2024

Hearing Time:

10:00 a.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION OF ORDER JANUARY 24, 2024 [sic]

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Curtis R. Olson (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on December 23, 2019 against a number of defendants, including Vidala Aaronoff.

Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint on November 17, 2020, alleging ten causes of action. On August 29, 2023, Jane Doe filed a Cross-Complaint in this action against, inter alia, Plaintiff. The Cross-Complaint alleges nine causes of action.

On January 24, 2024, the Court issued an Order in this action concerning Plaintiff’s unopposed “motion for statutory appellate attorney’s fees under C.C.P. § 425.16, SUBD (c)(1).” The Court’s January 24, 2024 Order provides, inter alia, that “the Court will require a supplemental declaration from Plaintiff’s counsel more clearly setting forth the fees incurred by Plaintiff in responding to Defendant’s appeal. In addition, the Court will require additional information concerning Mr. Little’s requested hourly rate.” (January 24, 2024 Order at p. 7:23-26.) The January 24, 2024 Order further provides that “the Court continues the hearing on the instant motion to 2/23/24, at 2 pm in Dept. 50. The Court sets the following briefing schedule:  

Plaintiff’s supplemental declaration to be filed and served by 1/31/24, with a courtesy copy delivered to Dept. 50. Defendants’ response, if any, to be filed and served by 2/7/24, with a courtesy copy delivered to Dept. 50. The argument and evidence in the supplemental papers are limited to the requested additional information, as discussed above, and the response thereto. The parties may not include any argument or evidence on any other issue.” (January 24, 2024 Order at p. 8:12-21.)[1]

            Vidala Aaronoff (“Aaronoff”) now moves “pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1008(a) for reconsideration of the order dated January 24, 2024…” Plaintiff opposes.

Discussion

Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a) provides as follows:

 

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”

The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered, and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. ((Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500); (Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342 [“[T]he party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.”]); (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213 [“The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.”].)

Reconsideration cannot be granted based on claims the court misinterpreted the law in its initial ruling; that is not a “new” or “different” matter. ((Gilberd v. AC Transit, supra, at p. 1500.) Moreover, counsel’s mistake based on ignorance of the law is not a proper basis for reconsideration. ((Pazderka v. Caballeros Dimas Alang, Inc. (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 658, 670.)

In the notice of motion, Aaronoff states that she moves “pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1008(a) for reconsideration of the order dated January 24, 2024 in which the Court ruled that Aaronoff’s Request for Continuance of Hearing and Briefing Schedule, so Defendant could file an opposition to Plaintiff’s Attorney’s Fees Motion, which was supported by a Declaration of Vidala Aaronoff, would not be considered by the Court because Defendant did not serve all parties.” (Notice of Mot. at pp. 1:27-2:3.)

As an initial matter, it does not appear that the Court issued any order on January 24, 2024 denying any request by Aaronoff for a “continuance of hearing and briefing schedule.” (Notice of Mot. at p. 2:1.) As discussed above, the Court’s January 24, 2024 Order concerns Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees and sets a supplemental briefing schedule. Aaronoff does not appear to provide evidence of any Court order concerning any purported request by Aaronoff “for Continuance of Hearing and Briefing Schedule, so Defendant could file an opposition to Plaintiff’s Attorney’s Fees Motion…” (Notice of Mot. at p. 2:1-2.) As noted by Plaintiff in the opposition, “there is no order to reconsider.” (Opp’n at p. 2:4-5.)

In her declaration in support of the motion, Aaronoff states that “[r]econsideration of the order dated January 24, 2024 is requested on the grounds that: a. New or different facts or circumstances have been discovered since the date of the order in that Defendant Aaronoff’s process server via attorney service actually did serve all parties in this instant action. However, the proof of service was merely incorrect and an Amended Proof of Service has been filed which shows that all parties in this action were served.” (Aaronoff Decl., ¶ 3.) Aaronoff does not appear to specify in her declaration what the process server allegedly served the parties with. Moreover, the Court does not see how this relates to the Court’s January 24, 2024 Order, or why it demonstrates grounds for the Court to reconsider the January 24, 2024 Order. As discussed, Aaronoff does not provide evidence of any January 24, 2024 Order denying a request by Aaronoff for a continuance.

            Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that Aaronoff has demonstrated grounds for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Aaronoff’s motion is denied.
