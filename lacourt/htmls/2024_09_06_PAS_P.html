DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV01340    Hearing Date: September 6, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

I.        INTRODUCTION

          Andy Meraz purchased a new Honda Accord in 2022. He sues Honda for defects he is alleged to have experienced with the vehicle. The pleading alleges that the vehicle is equipped with a defective driver-assistance safety system. The Honda Sensing system, also called Collision Mitigation Braking System (CMBS), includes radar, cameras, and a computer system. The purpose of the CMBS is to avoid front-end collisions by detecting and computing things like vehicle speed and objects in the road.  

Meraz alleges that the CBMS is capable of applying brakes when it detects a chance of collision. The gist of the complaint is that the system is defective and frequently malfunctions. It is alleged that system can be thrown off by things such as bad weather and changes in the light on the roadway. Meraz alleges that because of these defects, vehicles can be brought to a sudden stop in the roadway, causing vehicles behind them to have to swerve to avoid a collision. Meraz himself experienced several occasions when his Honda braked on its own. He also complained that the speed limit on the vehicle’s screen is often higher than the actual posted speed limit. Meraz alleges he took his vehicle to an authorized repair facility a number of times with these complaints but was told that the technicians were unable to replicate the issue and that the vehicle was safe to drive.  

The complaint alleges two Song-Beverly cause of action for breach of express and implied warranties. It also includes a third cause of action for fraudulent concealment. Meraz alleges that Honda was aware of the Honda Sensing defect and failed to disclose it.

Before the Court is Honda’s motion for protective order, filed on July 25, 2024, asking the Court to quash Plaintiff’s notices of depositions of Honda’s employees Michelle Jongkind (“Jongkind”) and Carol Chin (“Chin”). Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion on August 23, 2024. Honda filed its reply on August 30, 2024.

Because Honda has failed to meet its burden of establishing good cause to quash the deposition notices, the Court DENIES the motion.

         

II.      LEGAL STANDARD 

Before, during, or after a deposition, any party may promptly move for a protective order. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (a).) The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the matter outside of court.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2025.420, subd. (a), 2016.040.) 

“The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (b).) “This protective order may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions: ¶ (1) That the deposition not be taken at all. ¶ (2) That the deposition be taken at a different time.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (b).)

The court shall impose monetary sanctions against whichever party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances render sanctions unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (h).)

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court …, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

“For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it ‘might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.…’ [Citation.] Admissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.” (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)

“To testify, a witness must have personal knowledge of the subject of the testimony, based on the capacity to perceive and recollect.” (People v. Montoya (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1139, 1150.)

         

III.     ANALYSIS

Honda asks the Court to quash the deposition notices of Jongkind and Chin, arguing the following. On July 17, 2024, Plaintiff unilaterally served notices of those depositions, both to occur on July 26, 2024. (Motion, p. 3:15-17; Declaration of Theodore Dorenkamp, ¶¶ 3-4; Exhibits A and B – copies of the deposition notices.) California Evidence Code section 702, subdivision (a), states that a witness must have personal knowledge of a matter to testify on that matter and the knowledge of the witness must be shown before the witness may testify. (Motion, p. 4:15-17.) Here, “Plaintiff has not and cannot show that these individuals have any personal involvement, or personal knowledge of plaintiff, plaintiff’s vehicle, any alleged defect in plaintiff’s vehicle, or of plaintiff’s communications with AHM or any authorized Honda dealer, and as such it would be unreasonable and burdensome to force these individuals to suffer interrogation.” (Motion, p. 5:17-22.) In addition, “the testimony of the persons identified is not relevant to this case.” (Motion, p. 6:12-13.) “Neither has been identified in discovery responses or document production and neither can be shown to have ever been involved in this case and or possessing any personal knowledge of plaintiff’s vehicle or claims.” (Motion, p. 6:13-16.)

To understand the parties’ positions, it is helpful to review the relevant procedural history in this case.

On December 11, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s request to compel Honda’s further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, No. 55 (“RPD No. 55”). (See Plaintiff’s Separate Statement in Support of Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production Set One, filed July 31, 2023, p. 17:7-11 – RPD No. 55 [asking Honda to produce “All DOCUMENTS produced by [the defendant] in discovery in the matter of Kathleen Cadena, et al., v. American Honda Motor Company, Inc., et al, United States District Court, Central District of California Case No. 2:18-cv-04007-MWF-PJW [“Cadena”]”].)

On December 21, 2023, Honda filed a motion for reconsideration of the Court’s order granting the request to compel further response to RPD No. 55, arguing (among other things) that the RPD requested irrelevant information. (Motion for Reconsideration, p. 6:17-19 [arguing that RPD No. 55 “is … targeting entirely irrelevant information regarding other vehicles and other model years”]; Order Denying Defendant’s Motion for Reconsideration, filed February 6, 2024 (“February 6 Order”), p. 2:18-21 [“Before the Court is Honda’s motion for reconsideration …. Honda argues that: … the documents requested … are irrelevant in this action”].)

In its order denying Honda’s motion for reconsideration, the Court found RPD No. 55 sought relevant information. The Court explained that even though the Cadena case did not concern “defects specifically as to Plaintiff Meraz’s vehicle, a 2022 Honda Accord, it does allege that earlier models, the 2018-2019 Honda Accords and 2017-2019 Honda CRVs, had defects with the Honda sensing system. [Citation.] Information as to the same or similar system entitled ‘Honda Sensing’ is potentially part of what is at issue in this case.” (February 6 Order, p. 6:9-13.)

In his opposition to the instant motion for protective order, Plaintiff argues that Honda produced documents in response to RPD No. 55 that show that Jongkind and Chin were involved in reporting and investigating defects in the Honda sensing system. “Specifically, Ms. Chin authored several emails [produced in response to RPD No. 55], and is directly involved in investigations into the Honda Sensing Defect by Defendant to comply with its obligations to report known defects to National Highway Traffic Safety Administration. [Therefore,] Plaintiff seeks to depose Ms. Chin about the documents that she authored that are relevant to the prosecution of Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Act and fraud claims. Additionally, Plaintiff’s [sic] seek [sic] to depose Ms. Chin regarding her involvement in and knowledge of information related to the Honda Sensing Defect.” (Opposition to the Motion for Protective Order (“Opposition”), p. 2:7-13.) “Similarly, Ms. Jongkind authored or was the direct recipient of several emails in this production relating to Honda’s own internal investigation into the Honda Sensing Defect.” (Opposition, p. 2:13-14.) “Any details of Ms. Chin or Ms. Jongkind’s direct involvement in or knowledge of any internal investigations is crucial for both Plaintiff’s Song Beverly Act and Fraudulent Concealment claims. Any communications, investigations, or work product by Ms. Chin or Ms. Jongkind would necessarily show the extent of their, and by extension Honda’s, knowledge of the defect, and would directly support both Plaintiff’s claim for civil penalties and Fraudulent Concealment.” (Opposition, p. 2:19-24.)

“[A] party seeking a protective order to prevent the taking of a deposition in a civil action has the burden of establishing good cause for such an order.” (Department of Health Services v. Superior Court (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 80, 83–84; Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 318.) 

Here, the Court finds that Honda has failed to meet its burden. Indeed, its reply, Honda has not disputed Plaintiff’s contentions that (1) Jongkind and Chin are involved in reporting and investigating defects in the Honda sensing system, and (2) those deponents authored several documents that were produced in response to RPD No. 55. Therefore, Honda’s relevance and personal knowledge arguments are unpersuasive. Honda has also failed to convince this Court that the depositions will cause the defendant “unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense” within the meaning of the protective order statute. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (b).)

          The Court denies the motion.

          The motion for protective is denied.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT

I.        INTRODUCTION

          In this breach of contract action, Plaintiff San Van High Technology Co. (Plaintiff) alleges that it entered a contract with Defendants Tians LLC, Cahm LLC, Cayg LLC, Jack Gu, Jing Chen, Diamond Ridge Corp. and JL Capital Resources Inc. (JL Capital) for the purchase of real property at 14814 Castle Butte Rd., North Edwards, California 93523. San Van alleges that the purchase included the business defendants ran at the property, defendants’ Industrial Hemp Cultivation License and three separate APNs: 23348030005, 23348029003, 23348005003. It is alleged that Defendants retained Plaintiff’s initial deposit of $700,000 after the purchase fell apart.

          The complaint contains ten causes of action: (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) unjust enrichment, (4) conversion, (5) negligence/professional malpractice, (6) breach of fiduciary duty, (7) common count- open book account, (8) common count- money had and received, (9) common count-money paid, laid out and expended, (10) common count-mistaken receipt.

          On June 5, 2024, default was entered against Defendant JL Capital.

          On June 27, 2024, Defendant JL Capital filed the instant motion to set aside the default. The hearing for the motion was initially set for July 22, 2024, but due to the Court’s network and connectivity problems, was continued to its current date on September 6, 2024.

          On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition to the motion to set aside.

          On July 15, 2024, JL Capital filed its reply.

          Because JL Capital has established grounds for relief from the default entered on June 5, 2024, the Court GRANTS the motion.  

         

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) (“Section 473(b)”) provides a means for relief from default that was entered as a result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The statute has a discretionary provision, which states: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) The statute also contains a mandatory provision that states: “Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her own mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in the entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”  

When statutory relief is unavailable, “‘a trial court may ... [also] vacate a default on equitable grounds ….’ [Citation].” (Sakaguchi v. Sakaguchi (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 852, 862.)

Equitable relief may be based on “extrinsic fraud” or “extrinsic mistake.”  (Luxury Asset Lending, LLC v. Philadelphia Television Network, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 894, 911 (“Luxury Asset”).)

Extrinsic fraud “‘usually arises when a party is denied a fair adversary hearing because he has been “deliberately kept in ignorance of the action or proceeding, or in some other way fraudulently prevented from presenting his claim or defense.”’ [Citation.]” (Luxury Asset, supra, 56 Cal.App.5th at pp. 910-911.) 

“‘“‘Extrinsic mistake involves the excusable neglect of a party. [Citation.] When this neglect results in an unjust judgment, without a fair adversary hearing, and the basis for equitable relief is present, this is extrinsic mistake. [Citation.]’”’ [Citation.]” (Luxury Asset, supra, 56 Cal.App.5th at p. 911.)

III.     ANALYSIS

JL Capital moves to set aside the default entered on June 5, 2024, pursuant to Section 473(b)’s discretionary provision or, alternatively, on the ground of extrinsic mistake.  

The Court finds the motion is timely under Section 473(b) because it is brought within six (6) months of the entry of default. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b) [“Application for this relief … shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken”].)

The Court also finds that the defendant has established “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect” within the meaning of Section 473(b), that warrants vacating the default for the following reasons.

“Within the context of section 473(b) neglect is excusable if a reasonably prudent person under similar circumstances might have made the same error.” (Austin v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 918, 929; see also Younessi v. Woolf (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 1137, 1146 [“‘“A party who seeks relief under section 473 on the basis of mistake or inadvertence of counsel must demonstrate that such mistake, inadvertence, or general neglect was excusable.”’ [Citation.] We must ask ‘“whether ‘a reasonably prudent person under the same or similar circumstances’ might have made the same error.[ ]”’ [Citation]”].)

“It is … well established that it is the policy of the law to bring about a trial on the merits whenever possible, so that any doubts which may exist should be resolved in favor of the application, to the end of securing to a litigant his day in court and a trial upon the merits.” (Frank E. Beckett Co. v. Bobbitt (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d Supp. 921, 928.)

Here, JL Capital has submitted the declaration of its CEO Leann Li (“Li”), who testifies that no one at JL Capital, including her, was ever served with the complaint in this action. (Motion, Li Decl., ¶ 7.) Li also testifies as follows. On September 20, 2023 (a few days after this lawsuit was filed), she filed JL Capital’s updated State of Information with the California Secretary of State, listing her house, 514 W. Lemon Ave., Arcadia, CA 91007, as JL Capital’s address (the “Arcadia Address”). (Li Decl., ¶ 8.) However, Li further testifies, she often stays overnight at her brother’s house in Orange County and her tenants in the Arcadia Address are the ones that forward her mail for her. (Li Decl., ¶ 9.) Li states that she never received copies of the complaint or any other document regarding this action at the Arcadia Address before receiving Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment. (Li Decl., ¶ 9.)

The Court finds that through Li’s declaration, JL Capital has established mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect within the meaning of Section 473(b); in other words, it was excusable neglect to have expected Li’s tenants to forward mail regarding JL Capital to her. (Cf. Bernards v. Grey (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 672, 682-686 [finding excusable neglect where a party was personally served a summons and complaint but then “placed them in his briefcase, merely noting that they referred to the present action and through inadvertence, did not call to counsel’s attention the papers he had received and was not at said time aware of their nature”]; Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 526-27 [finding excusable neglect where secretary misunderstood the directions given to her by the president of the company and failed to forward the papers to Defendant’s attorney]; Gorman v. California Transit Co. (1926) 199 Cal. 246, 248-249 [upholding order vacating a default judgment due to failure to answer because after service of the summons and complaint on the secretary of the corporation, the summons and complaint were mistakenly taken by an employee from the desk of one who was to notify the president of the corporation, and filed].)

Plaintiff’s argument that it attempted to serve JL Capital several times at the Arcadia Address to no avail and even obtained an order from this Court allowing it to serve the defendant through the California Secretary of State is unpersuasive in light of Li’s undisputed testimony that JL Capital never received a copy of the summons or complaint in time to respond to the complaint.

For those reasons, the Court GRANTS the motion to set aside the default pursuant to Section 473(b).

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

An injunction is a writ or order requiring a person to refrain from a particular act; it may be granted by the court in which the action is brought, or by a judge thereof; and when granted by a judge, it may be enforced as an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., §525.) An injunction may be more completely defined as a writ or order commanding a person either to perform or to refrain from performing a particular act. (See Comfort v. Comfort (1941) 17 Cal.2d 736, 741; McDowell v. Watson (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1155, 1160.) It is an equitable remedy available generally in the protection or to prevent the invasion of a legal right. (Meridian, Ltd. v. City and County of San Francisco, et al. (1939) 13 Cal.2d 424.)

The purpose of a preliminary injunction is to preserve the status quo pending final resolution upon a trial. (See Scaringe v. J.C.C. Enterprises, Inc. (1988) 205 Cal.App.3d 1536; Grothe v. Cortlandt Corp. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1316; Major v. Miraverde Homeowners Assn. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 618, 623.) The status quo has been defined to mean the last actual peaceable, uncontested status which preceded the pending controversy. (Voorhies v. Greene (1983) 139 Cal.App.3d 989, 995, quoting United Railroads v. Superior Court (1916) 172 Cal. 80, 87; 14859 Moorpark Homeowner’s Assn. v. VRT Corp. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1396. 1402.)

A preliminary injunction is issued after hearing on a noticed motion. The complaint normally must plead injunctive relief. (Code Civ. Proc., §526(a)(1)-(2).) Preliminary injunctive relief requires the use of competent evidence to create a sufficient factual showing on the grounds for relief. (See e.g. Ancora-Citronelle Corp. v. Green (1974) 41 Cal.App.3d 146, 150.) Injunctive relief may be granted based on a verified complaint only if it contains sufficient evidentiary, not ultimate, facts.  See CCP §527(a). For this reason, a pleading alone rarely suffices. (Weil & Brown, California Procedure Before Trial, 9:579, 9(ll)-21 (The Rutter Group 2007).) The burden of proof is on the plaintiff as moving party. (O’Connell v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1481.)

A plaintiff seeking injunctive relief must show the absence of an adequate damages remedy at law. (Code Civ. Proc., §526(4); Thayer Plymouth Center, Inc. v. Chrysler Motors (1967) 255 Cal.App.2d 300, 307; Department of Fish & Game v. Anderson Cottonwood Irrigation Dist (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 1554, 1565.) The concept of “inadequacy of the legal remedy” or “inadequacy of damages” dates from the time of the early courts of chancery, the idea being that an injunction is an unusual or extraordinary equitable remedy which will not be granted if the remedy at law (usually damages) will adequately compensate the injured plaintiff. (Department of Fish & Game v. Anderson-Cottonwood Irrigation Dist. (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 1554, 1565.)

In determining whether to issue a preliminary injunction, the trial court considers two factors: (1) the reasonable probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial, and (2) a balancing of the “irreparable harm” that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction is denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the court grants a preliminary injunction. (Code Civ. Proc., §526(a)(2); 14859 Moorpark Homeowner’s Assn. v. VRT Corp. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1396. 1402; Pillsbury, Madison & Sutro v. Schectman (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1279, 1283; Davenport v. Blue Cross of California (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 435, 446; Abrams v. St. Johns Hospital (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 628, 636. Thus, a preliminary injunction may not issue without some showing of potential entitlement to such relief. (Doe v. Wilson (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 296, 304.) The decision to grant a preliminary injunction generally lies within the sound discretion of the trial court and will not be disturbed on appeal absent an abuse of discretion. (Thornton v. Carlson (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 1249, 1255.)

A preliminary injunction ordinarily cannot take effect unless and until the plaintiff provides an undertaking for damages which the enjoined defendant may sustain by reason of the injunction if the court finally decides that the plaintiff was not entitled to the injunction. (See Code Civ. Proc., §529(a); City of South San Francisco v. Cypress Lawn Cemetery Assn. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 916, 920.)

 

IV.     ANALYSIS

The Court notes that Defendants’ opposition was untimely filed, but given Plaintiffs’ reply and the lack of any showing of prejudice, the Court will consider all the papers filed.

          A.      Plaintiffs Are Likely to Prevail

To state a claim for private nuisance, a plaintiff must plead (1) that the defendant interfered with plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of the plaintiff’s property; (2) that “the invasion of the plaintiff's interest in the use and enjoyment of the land was substantial, i.e., that it cause[d] the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage”; (3) “the interference was unreasonable, i.e., it must be ‘of such a nature, duration or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.” (Chase v. Wizman (2021) 71 Cal. App. 5th 244.)  

Plaintiffs argue they are likely to prevail on their first cause of action for nuisance, which is ongoing and has not been abated. They claim that the excessive and constant noise from Defendants’ multiple large dogs constitutes a nuisance under Civil Code section 3479, which defines a nuisance as “[a]nything which is injurious to health . . . or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property.” They cite Oliver v. AT&T Wireless Servs. (1999) 76 Cal. App. 4th 521, 534 and San Diego Gas & Elec. Co. v. Superior Court (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938, to assert that a private nuisance interferes with the use and enjoyment of land, causing substantial actual damage and constituting an unreasonable interference with property use. Plaintiffs also reference Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 264, to support their argument that offensive noises can constitute a nuisance, including noise from dogs. In Wilms v. Hand (1951) 101 Cal. App. 2d 811, the court restrained defendants from allowing dogs to disturb their neighbors’ peace. Plaintiffs further assert that a nuisance can exist even without a violation of an ordinance, as established in Chase v. Wizmann (2021) 712 Cal. App. 5th 244, 257.

Plaintiffs assert that they can establish these elements, arguing that Defendants knowingly keep four large dogs, which bark, howl, yelp, and whine excessively, disturbing Plaintiffs' peace and quiet. (Decl. Wall ¶¶ 5-6, Exh. A; Decl. Lee ¶ 5; Decl. Dicecca ¶ 4, Exh. 1.) Plaintiffs emphasize that the dogs, all Belgian Malinois, are known for frequent barking due to their hyper personalities, which creates conflict with neighbors. Plaintiffs contend that reasonable persons would find the dog noise from Defendants’ property to be unreasonable in both amount and duration. (Decl. Wall ¶ 6, Exh. A.) The ongoing noise allegedly interferes substantially with Plaintiffs’ property use, preventing rest, sleep, opening windows, outdoor use, and work from home. (Decl. Wall ¶ 7; Decl. Lee ¶ 6.) The nuisance has reportedly caused severe emotional distress, loss of property use, and loss of earnings, and remains unabated despite the lawsuit. (Decl. Wall ¶¶ 7, 10; Decl. Lee ¶¶ 6, 9.)

In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ claims are unsubstantiated because they allegedly fabricated evidence by provoking the dogs to bark, thereby making any evidence presented by Plaintiffs unreliable. Defendants contend that Plaintiffs’ nuisance claim, based on noise from barking dogs, is weakened because the noise originated from two litters born just before Plaintiffs moved in. By mid-November, eleven of the dogs were removed, leaving only four dogs, which substantially reduced the noise. Defendants argue that this demonstrates a significant reduction in noise, making Plaintiffs’ continued claims less credible. They note that Plaintiffs did not inform Defendants that the remaining dogs were still causing a disturbance before filing the complaint. Furthermore, Defendants assert that Mr. Florez has taken steps to mitigate the issue by agreeing to incur the cost and time to have the remaining four dogs professionally trained to stop barking, which would resolve the problem underlying Plaintiffs’ claim.

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that they have presented sufficient evidence to meet the elements required for a nuisance claim, including that Defendants’ dogs create excessive and continuous noise that interferes with Plaintiffs’ peace and comfort. Plaintiffs cite Wilms v. Hand (1951) 101 Cal. App. 2d 811, where the court granted an injunction due to the barking, howling, and whining of dogs, which disturbed neighbors’ rest and sleep. Plaintiffs assert that their case is similar, as Defendants’ dogs also bark incessantly for several hours at all times, causing significant disruption. (Decl. Wall ¶¶ 5-6, Exh. A; Decl. Lee ¶ 5.) Plaintiffs also refute Defendants’ argument that the noise was “substantially abated” after the removal of puppies, pointing out that even the noise from the remaining four mature and untrained dogs still constitutes a nuisance. They argue that Defendants’ claim of being unaware of the ongoing disturbance does not negate the evidence of a persistent noise nuisance. Plaintiffs also emphasize that there is no declaration from Maria Manuela Florez disputing their evidence of incessant barking during the day, and the declarations from Mr. Florez and Mr. Miller are merely argumentative and inadmissible hearsay, failing to counter the evidence of a continuous and unabated nuisance.

In support of their motion, Plaintiffs submitted two USB drives containing fifty-four video clips.  These videos strongly support Plaintiffs’ contentions.  Most of the videos, filmed on different days from September 9, 2023, to July 31, 2024, depict loud, ongoing barking, howling and whining during both day and night.  Two other videos are worth noting.  Video file ending in 3924 depicts a male individual, apparently Defendant Gustavo Florez, spraying water on Plaintiffs’ home then pulling up the front of his shirt and stating “step outside, motherfucker.”  A second video file ending in 0452 also depicts water being sprayed at the front of Plaintiffs’ home followed by a male individual uttering “motherfucker.”

The Court finds that Plaintiffs are likely to prevail on their first cause of action for nuisance. Plaintiffs have shown that the ongoing and excessive noise from Defendants’ dogs constitutes a nuisance under Civil Code section 3479, and they have provided evidence that the noise from Defendants’ multiple large dogs is injurious to health, offensive to the senses, and interferes with the comfortable enjoyment of their property. This meets the legal standard for nuisance. The Court also notes that Plaintiffs have presented declarations showing that the dogs bark, howl, yelp, and whine, disturbing Plaintiffs’ peace and quiet and interfering with their property use. (Decl. Wall ¶¶ 5-6, Exh. A; Decl. Lee ¶ 5.)

In Wilms v. Hand, the court upheld a preliminary injunction against the defendants, restraining them from allowing the dogs at their pet hospital to disturb the plaintiffs and their guests at a nearby motor hotel. (Wilms, supra, 101 Cal.App.2d at 811.) There, the plaintiffs alleged that the noise from 80 to 100 dogs at the defendants’ business disturbed their peace, rest, and sleep, causing guests to leave the hotel, which affected their business adversely. (Id. at 813-814.) To substantiate these claims, the plaintiffs provided numerous affidavits and witness testimonies. One of the plaintiffs, Stephanie S. Shea, testified that she could hear the dogs barking from a distance of 1,000 feet, describing the noise as “absolutely terrific” and akin to “perfect bedlam,” occurring consistently in the morning, afternoon, and sometimes at night. (Id. at 813.) This testimony was further supported by a sound recording made by plaintiff Earl Wilms, which was played in court to demonstrate the noise’s volume and persistence. Despite objections from the defendants, the court admitted the recording, which showed that the noise level was typical of an average day at the defendants’ pet hospital. (Id. at 815.) The plaintiffs also presented additional witness statements describing the noise’s intensity and its impact on their well-being and business. They argued that the noise was so disruptive that it caused substantial discomfort to residents and guests, further leading to financial loss for the motor hotel due to departing guests. (Id. at 811.) The court found this evidence compelling, concluding that the continuous noise constituted a serious nuisance rather than a minor inconvenience. The court stated, that the “evidence in support of plaintiffs’ complaint [wa]s so overwhelming that [it] deem[ed] it unnecessary to detail it further.” (Id. at 813.)

The Court finds Wilms to be similar to this case. Where in Wilms, the plaintiffs provided substantial evidence—including affidavits, witness testimonies, and a sound recording demonstrating the severity and persistence of the noise— here, Plaintiffs have submitted declarations, video recordings, and other documentation to show that Defendants’ dogs bark, howl, yelp, and whine, causing severe emotional distress and significant disruption to Plaintiffs’ use and enjoyment of their property. (Decl. Wall ¶¶ 5-6, Exh. A; Decl. Lee ¶ 5.) The Court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently demonstrated that the noise from Defendants’ dogs is not a minor inconvenience but a substantial interference with their daily lives and property rights. As in Wilms, here, the disturbance is continuous and intense, causing harm beyond mere annoyance, which supports the argument for injunctive relief. Thus, the Court concludes that Plaintiffs have shown a likelihood of success on the merits of their nuisance claim.

          B.       Balance of Harms Weighs in Plaintiffs’ Favor

Plaintiffs argue that the balance of equities favors granting the preliminary injunction because they would suffer significant harm without it, while Defendants would suffer little to no harm. Plaintiffs contend that denying the injunction would result in irreparable harm, including severe emotional distress, loss of property use, and loss of earnings due to the continuous noise from Defendants’ dogs, which interferes with their daily life and work. (Decl. Wall ¶ 7; Decl. Lee ¶ 6.) They assert that Mr. Wall is experiencing physical effects from stress, and Mr. Lee requires medication and has been sleeping separately from his husband to escape the noise, straining their relationship. (Decl. Wall ¶ 7; Decl. Lee ¶ 6.) Plaintiffs also claim they are unable to use their backyard without immediate barking and that both Plaintiffs’ businesses are suffering due to the noise. Mr. Wall has had to decline business opportunities, and Mr. Lee’s ability to host visitors for his art career is hindered. (Decl. Wall ¶ 7; Decl. Lee ¶ 6, 9). Plaintiffs argue there is no adequate legal remedy other than a preliminary injunction to address these injuries. (Decl. Wall ¶ 11; Decl. Lee ¶ 10.)

On the other hand, Plaintiffs claim that granting the injunction would not harm Defendants. They argue that Ms. Florez, who is 82 years old and unable to care for the dogs, would benefit from not having the burden of managing them. (Decl. Dicecca ¶ 4, Exh. 1 [Decl. Gustavo Adolfo Florez ¶¶ 6 & 15].) Mr. Florez, the sole caretaker, who works long hours, would also benefit from the injunction as it would relieve him from the obligation of managing the dogs. (Id.) Furthermore, the injunction would benefit the dogs, who currently live in unsuitable conditions due to their size and breed and lack of care from the Defendants. (Decl. Wall ¶ 8; Decl. Lee ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs cite sources indicating that Belgian Malinois dogs require extensive exercise and engagement, which they are not receiving in their current environment, leading to behavioral issues and unsuitability for absentee owners like Mr. Florez. (See Mot. p. 5, 7-8.)

In opposition, Defendants argue that the balance of interim harms does not favor Plaintiffs and that granting the preliminary injunction would cause more harm than denying it. They contend that denying the injunction would allow for a less intrusive alternative—training the dogs by a professional trainer to prevent barking. Defendants assert that the costs of kenneling the four dogs are exorbitantly high, negating any perceived benefit of removing them. Defendants claim that removing the dogs would inflict significant harm on them, particularly on Mr. Florez, who views the dogs as his children and would suffer severe emotional trauma if they were removed. They argue that Plaintiffs’ suggestion that Mr. Florez would benefit from the dogs’ removal is baseless and desperate. They also maintain that removing the dogs would violate Defendants’ constitutional rights to own property, potentially giving rise to a due process claim. Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have an adequate remedy at law and that injunctive relief is unnecessary. Defendants also highlight that Plaintiffs are already seeking monetary damages and that alternatives, such as professional dog training, are less intrusive.

Additionally, Defendants argue that if the Court considers removing the dogs, Plaintiffs should be required to post a bond sufficient to cover the potential costs associated with housing the dogs during the trial, which could exceed $100,000. They claim that the cost of kenneling four fully grown dogs ranges between $10,000 and $12,000 per month, depending on whether the dogs need to be isolated, which could increase the cost to over $12,000 per month. Given that a trial date has not yet been set, and their responsive pleading is not due until August 2, 2024, Defendants estimate that the total cost of housing the dogs during the pendency of this action could exceed $100,000. Defendants object to Plaintiffs’ request for a minimal bond of only $1,000, arguing that this amount is insufficient given the potential costs involved and noting that Plaintiffs have already incurred significant attorney's fees.

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that the balance of harms favors them, not Defendants, and that they are suffering irreparable harm due to the ongoing noise from Defendants’ dogs. They claim that the Defendants’ opposition does not dispute that the noise will cause continued harm if not stopped. Plaintiffs also point out that the opposition fails to provide any evidence that removing the dogs would harm Maria Manuela Florez and does not counter the argument that she might benefit from such removal. Although Defendants argue that removing the dogs would cause emotional trauma to Mr. Florez, Plaintiffs counter that his contact with the dogs is limited due to his long work hours and that he could still visit the dogs elsewhere, making any emotional harm less significant than the distress Plaintiffs are enduring. (Decl. Wall ¶ 7; Decl. Lee ¶ 6.) Plaintiffs dispute Defendants’ claim that removing the dogs would violate constitutional rights, arguing that the injunction would not prevent Mr. Florez from owning the dogs but only require them to be kept elsewhere during the litigation. Plaintiffs assert that there are no due process issues because the injunctive relief is being sought through a properly noticed motion. (Opp. at 9:6-13.)

Plaintiffs also reject Defendants’ suggestion of dog training as a viable alternative, stating it is unproven, unsubstantiated, and proposed too late. They argue that Defendants could have trained the dogs voluntarily at any time but did not do so and instead escalated matters, as evidenced by violent and threatening behavior from Mr. Florez. (Decl. Wall ISO App for Default Judgment, 04-10-24, ¶ 13, Exh. B.) Furthermore, Defendants present no evidence from a qualified dog trainer to support the feasibility or effectiveness of training at this stage. Plaintiffs maintain that monetary damages are not an adequate remedy because they would not stop the dogs from barking and do not provide a solution for the ongoing nuisance. They argue that the injunction is necessary to prevent irreparable harm, as detailed in their First Amended Complaint, which seeks both preliminary and permanent injunctive relief for the nuisance caused by the barking dogs. (FAC ¶ 21-22.) They contend that Defendants have not shown any viable alternative remedy.

          The Court finds that the balance of harms weighs in favor of Plaintiffs, and they have sufficiently demonstrated that they would suffer irreparable harm absent Court intervention. However, the relief proposed by Plaintiffs—removing the dogs from Defendants’ possession—is a step that this Court is not currently willing to take. The Court finds that there are less intrusive alternatives, including but not limited to (1) dog training; (2) housing the dogs in the garage and sound proofing the garage; and (3) using muzzles or bark collars. These alternatives would arguably address the noise nuisance without causing undue harm or expense to Defendants.  The Court further finds that these solutions would allow Defendants to retain their property rights while providing Plaintiffs with the relief to which they are legally entitled.

Therefore, borrowing the language set forth in Wilms, the Court grants Plaintiffs’ motion for preliminary injunction as follows: the Court orders Defendants restrained from permitting the dogs on their property "from annoying and disturbing the peace and comfort of Plaintiffs” at all times of the day and night.  The Court at present declines to dictate which measure Defendants shall implement to comply with this ruling.  However, the Court intends to set a future hearing date for status re compliance with this order.

          C.      Bond Amount

Defendants claim that the cost of housing the four dogs ranges from $10,000 to $12,000 per month, with total costs potentially exceeding $100,000, depending on whether the dogs need to be isolated based on compatibility assessments at the kennel. In reply, Plaintiffs point out that there is no declaration from a kennel owner or any supporting documentation to substantiate these costs. The only evidence provided consists of declarations from Mr. Florez and Mr. Miller, which Plaintiffs contend are inadmissible hearsay. Given that the Court has declined to order the dogs off the property, the Court finds that the bond amount requested by Defendants is not warranted. The Court sets a bond amount of $1,000.

 

V.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiffs have presented sufficient evidence that they are likely to prevail on the merits of their nuisance claim and that the balance of harm weighs in their favor. The Court therefore grants injunctive relief as set forth above.  There is currently a future court date of October 29, 2024, for case management conference, demurrer and OSC re answer or default.  Given that Defendants’ demurrer was filed on July 8, 2024, and Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint on July 18, 2024, the Court takes the demurrer off calendar.  However, the Court sets a hearing re status of Defendants’ compliance with preliminary injunction for October 29, 2024.

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of this ruling.

I.        INTRODUCTION

This is a PAGA action in which Plaintiff Ramirez alleges that Defendant Merchants Building Maintenance, LLC, and Does 1 through 10, committed multiple violations of California labor laws, specifically wage and hour requirements. The claims are made on behalf of Plaintiff and other current or former non-exempt employees of Defendants in California, referred to as “Aggrieved Employees.” The allegations include:

1.     Failure to maintain accurate records: Defendants did not keep accurate time and payroll records, including records of hours worked and meal periods.

2.     Non-compliant meal periods: Defendants failed to provide compliant meal periods, often providing late, shortened, interrupted, or no meal periods. They also failed to pay an additional hour of pay at the regular rate for each missed meal period.

3.     Non-compliant rest periods: Similar violations occurred with rest periods, with shortened, interrupted, or missed rest periods. Defendants did not consistently pay the required premium for missed rest periods or paid at incorrect rates.

4.     Failure to pay minimum wages: Defendants did not pay the minimum wage for all hours worked, including work performed off the clock due to interrupted meal periods and unlawful time-shaving or rounding practices.

5.     Failure to pay overtime wages: Defendants failed to pay the correct overtime wages, including failing to account for all non-discretionary pay in the regular rate calculations.

6.     Failure to reimburse business expenses: Defendants did not reimburse employees for necessary business expenses, such as using their personal cell phones.

7.     Failure to provide accurate wage statements: Wage statements provided by the defendants were inaccurate, failing to correctly list total hours worked and the correct rates of pay.

8.     Failure to pay all due wages: Defendants did not pay all wages owed each pay period, including minimum wages, overtime, and meal and rest period premiums.

9.     Failure to pay wages upon termination: Defendants did not timely pay all wages due to former employees upon their separation from employment.

The complaint contains one cause of action for Violation of the Private Attorneys General Act (Labor Code sections 2698 et seq.).

Defendant Merchants Building Maintenance, LLC this motion on May 17, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on August 23, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on August 29, 2024.

The motion is granted.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civ. Proc. §1281 provides: “A written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter existing is valid, enforceable, and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” (CCP § 1281.)

Paraphrased, Code Civ. Proc. §1281.2 provides that upon petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate and a party’s refusal to submit to arbitration, the court shall order the parties to arbitrate the controversy if it determines (1) that an agreement exists, (2) unless it determines that the right to arbitrate has been waived, that grounds exist for revocation, or that a party to the agreement is also party to a pending litigation arising out of the same facts and there exists a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of fact or law. (CCP § 1281.2.)

 

IV.     ANALYSIS

A. Labor Code section 2699.8 Applies

Labor Code section 2699.8 exempts from PAGA janitorial employees covered by a valid Collective Bargaining Agreement (“CBA”) that contains a binding grievance and arbitration procedure. The relevant provision exempts a janitorial employee from certain requirements if they are represented by a labor organization that has represented janitors since before January 1, 2021, and is employed by a janitorial contractor who registered as a property service employer in 2020. This exemption applies to work performed under a valid collective bargaining agreement (CBA) effective before July 1, 2028, that specifies wages, hours, and working conditions and meets the following criteria:

1.     The CBA requires the employer to pay non probationary workers at certain worksites a total hourly compensation, including wages, health benefits, pension, training, vacation, holiday, and other fringe benefits, that is at least 30% above the state minimum wage.

2.     The CBA prohibits all labor code violations covered by this section, provides for a grievance and binding arbitration procedure to address those violations, and allows the labor organization to file grievances on behalf of all affected employees.

3.     The CBA explicitly waives the requirements of this part in clear and unambiguous terms.

4.     The CBA authorizes the arbitrator to award any remedies available under the code, except for penalties payable to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency.

(Labor Code § 2699.8(a).) Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff is a janitorial employee of MBM, LLC, a registered janitorial company with the Department of Industrial Relations and MBM is a party to a CBA that provides for arbitration of PAGA claims. (AO Decl. ¶ 2, WR Decl. ¶¶ 2-3, Exhib. 6; LM Decl. ¶ 3, Exhs. 1, 2; WR Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. 6.) However, Plaintiff argues that Labor Code Section 2699.8 does not apply to him because he was not “represented by a labor organization,” as required by the statute. Plaintiff emphasizes that he was not a member of the Service Employees International Union (SEIU) and did not authorize SEIU to act on his behalf or deduct union dues from his wages. (Wallace Decl., ¶ 12.) Plaintiff argues that the statute’s language requires that a janitorial employee be represented by a labor organization that meets specific criteria, such as having represented janitors before January 1, 2021, and being employed by a registered janitorial contractor under a valid CBA. Plaintiff contends that there is no legal authority clarifying what it means to be “represented by a labor organization,” but dictionary definitions suggest it involves legal authorization or acting on behalf of someone. (See https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/represent.)

          Plaintiff further attempts to distinguish legal authorities relied upon by Defendant to support the claim that an employee who is not a member of a local union can still be “represented” by that union. Plaintiff points out that during the meet and confer process, defendant failed to provide any legal authority supporting this proposition. (Pulmanova Decl., Ex. 10.) However, the Court finds that the cases cited by Defendant are sufficient to support the claim that Plaintiff was represented regardless of his lack of union membership. The Court discusses the cases in turn.

In Steele v. Louisville & N.R. Co. (1944) 323 U.S. 192, 200, the U.S. Supreme Court held that a labor organization designated as the exclusive bargaining representative must represent all employees within the craft or class, including those who are not union members, without discrimination. “The labor organization chosen to be the representative of the craft or class of employees is thus chosen to represent all of its members, regardless of their union affiliations or want of them.” (Id.) Plaintiff argues that Steele dealt with whether the Railway Labor Act required a labor organization, acting as the exclusive bargaining representative, to represent all employees in the craft without discrimination based on race, but this case did not establish that a non-union member is “represented” by a union in the context of PAGA claims. (Steele, supra, 323 U.S. at 193, 202.) While it may be in another context, the Court finds that this case nevertheless affirms the broader principle that a union’s duty extends to all employees within a defined group.

Defendant also properly relies on Trs. of S. Cal. IBEW-NECA Pension Trust Fund v. Flores (9th Cir. 2008) 519 F.3d 1045, 1047, where the Ninth Circuit stated that a CBA that defines covered employees by job classification generally includes all employees within those classifications, regardless of union membership. There, the court found that “the collective bargaining agreements cover both union and nonunion employees.” (Id.) While Plaintiff argues that Flores primarily addresses employer obligations regarding trust fund contributions, the case still supports the principle that union representation under a CBA can extend to all employees in a particular job classification, reinforcing Defendant’s position that Plaintiff, as a “waxer” covered by the CBA, was represented by the union.

Additionally, Queen Mary Rests. Corp. v. N.L.R.B. (1977) 560 F.2d 403, 409, supports the argument that a certified union serves as the exclusive representative for all employees in a bargaining unit, including those who did not vote for the union or are not members. Plaintiff claims this case is irrelevant because it focuses on an employer’s duty to bargain, but the Court finds that it still confirms that union representation is comprehensive for all unit employees, regardless of membership status.

Finally, Defendant’s reliance on Florio v. City of Ontario (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1466 further supports its argument by demonstrating that an employee need not affirmatively sign a CBA to be bound by its terms. Plaintiff acknowledges this legal principle but argues it does not apply to his situation. However, the Court finds that Florio supports the broader notion that employees can be bound by the terms of a CBA, even without formal union membership or direct agreement, aligning with Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff is subject to the CBA.

          Defendant argues that the CBA satisfies each of the requirements in Labor Code section 2699.8(a). First, the CBA complies with Section 2699.8(a)(1) by requiring that all non-probationary employees at specified worksites receive total hourly compensation, including wages, health insurance, pension, training, vacation, holiday, and other benefits, that is at least 30% above the state minimum wage. (W.R. Decl. ¶ 3, 10, Exh. 6; Appendix G-2.) Second, the CBA prohibits any violations of the California Labor Code that are redressable under PAGA and establishes a binding grievance and arbitration procedure to resolve such disputes, meeting the requirements of Section 2699.8(a)(2). (Appendix G-2, Section 4(c).) Third, the CBA clearly and unambiguously waives the requirements of PAGA, authorizing the arbitrator to award all remedies available under the Labor Code, except for PAGA penalties payable to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency, which satisfies Section 2699.8(a)(3) and (4) (Appendix G-2). Finally, the CBA mandates that all claims alleging violations of wage and hour laws, meal and rest period laws, and similar claims must be resolved through binding arbitration, and it waives the right to a jury trial and any representative proceeding to the maximum extent permitted by law. (Appendix G-2.)

          Plaintiff does not discuss or dispute these points. Plaintiff also does not oppose arbitration of his individual PAGA claims.

          Thus, the Court finds that pursuant to Labor Code section 2699.8, Plaintiff’s PAGA action must be dismissed and the individual claims arbitrated.

 

V.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant has presented evidence establishing that Labor Code section 2699.8 is applicable to Plaintiff and that his PAGA action should be dismissed and his individual claims arbitrated. Therefore, the Court dismisses the PAGA action and orders Plaintiff to arbitrate his individual claims.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT


INTRODUCTION

           This action is for unlawful detainer following a foreclosure sale held on 03/21/2024 with the Trustee's Deed Upon Sale recorded on 05/15/2024. The foreclosed property is 3924 Park Place, Unit # 3, Montrose, CA 91020 (“Premises”). The notice to quit was served on 5/27/2024, and the Defendants failed to vacate and remain in possession.

           On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff Breckenridge Property Fund 2016 LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed the complaint against Defendants Ji Hong Chung and Ji Hyun Chung.

           On August 13, 2024, Defendant Ji Hong Chung filed the instant motion for summary judgment.

           On August 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition to Defendant’s motion for summary judgment. On the same day, Plaintiff filed its own motion for summary judgment or in the alternative summary adjudication to which Defendant Ji Hong Chung failed to file an opposition.

           As of September 3, 2024, Defendant has not filed a reply for Plaintiff’s opposition.

           

II.       LEGAL STANDARD

           Summary judgment is appropriately granted where it is shown that an action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action. Code Civ. Proc. §437c(a).

           A cause of action has no merit if one of the following exists: (1) one or more necessary element cannot be established; (2) a defendant establishes an affirmative defense. Section §437c(o).

           A motion for summary judgment shall be granted where all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue of material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. Section 437c(c).

           Summary adjudication may be granted where it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim of damages, or an issue of duty. Section 437c(f).

 

III.     ANALYSIS

           A. Summary of Pleading

           On March 21, 2024, Plaintiff became the owner of the real property located at 3924 Park Place, Unit # 3, Montrose, CA 91020, by purchasing said property at a foreclosure sale. Per the recitations within the Deed, said foreclosure sale and all preceding notices and actions were done in accordance with Cal. Civ Code section 2924. Title under this sale has been duly perfected as the Trustee's Deed Upon Sale was recorded on May 15, 2024.

           On May 27, 2024, Plaintiff caused to be served on Defendants a written notice in compliance with the Code of Civil Procedure, Section 1161 (a) & (b), requiring and demanding that Defendants quit and deliver up possession of the premises to Plaintiff. No landlord/tenant relationship exists between the purchaser and the Defendants. The notice expired on its own terms on May 30, 2024. Defendant has failed to vacate and

remains in possession of the premises.    

           B. Judicial Notice

           Defendant, Ji Hong Chung (“Defendant”), requests that the Court take judicial notice pursuant to the provisions of Evidence Code sections 451 through 453 of the following documents:

           Ex. 102 - Certified copy of Deed of Trust, recorded on 05/30/2006, as Instrument No. 06 1172450 of the Official Records of Los Angeles County.

           Ex. 103 - Authenticated copy of Code of Civil Procedure Section 1161(a)

           The Court takes judicial notice of the documents.

           C. Merits

           Defendant Ji Hong Chung requests that the Court grant this motion for summary judgment on the grounds that Plaintiff’s action has no merit because Defendant allegedly did not receive the notice of default. (Mot. at p. 5.) In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s motion is based upon two logical fallacies and therefore fails. (Opp. at p. 3.) Specifically, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not offered sufficient admissible evidence to create a triable issue of fact whether Plaintiff, a third-party purchaser and stranger to pre-sale transactions and occurrences, is a bona fide purchaser for value and without notice. (Opp. at p. 6.) Moreover, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s argument that she did not receive notice of default is not an available defense as a matter of law. (Id. at p. 5.)

           The elements for Plaintiff’s case pursuant to CCP section 1161(a)(b)(3) are: (1) foreclosure in accordance with the CCP and perfection of title; (2) service of a notice to quit; and (3) Defendant’s failure to quit and deliver possession.

           In support of the motion, Defendant provides the Deed of Trust showing the following language: “The notice of acceleration and opportunity to cure given to Borrower pursuant to Section 22 and the notice of acceleration given to Borrower pursuant to Section 18 shall be deemed to satisfy the notice and opportunity to take corrective action provisions of this Section 20.” (Id. at ¶ 2; RJN Ex. 102 at p. 12.) The Deed of Trust also states “22. Acceleration; Remedies. Lender shall give notice to Borrower prior to acceleration following Borrower's breach of any covenant or agreement in this Security Instrument (but not prior to acceleration under Section 18 unless Applicable Law provides otherwise). The notice shall specify: (a) the default; (b) the action required to cure the default; (c) a date, not less than 30 days from the date the notice is given to Borrower, by which the default must be cured; and (d) that failure to cure the default on or before the date specified 1n the notice may result in acceleration of the sums secured by this Security Instrument and sale of the Property. The notice shall further inform Borrower of the right to reinstate after acceleration and the right to bring a court action to assert the non-existence of a default or any other defense of Borrower to acceleration and sale.” (RJN Ex. 102 at p. 13.) (emphasis added.) Defendant attempts to show there are triable issues of material fact by stating that the trustee was contractually obligated to give notice of the default. Defendant further provides nearly identical declarations of her and her daughter, Ji Hyun Chung where they state that they never received notice. (Ji Hyun Chung Decl. ¶¶ 13-14; Ji Hong Chung Decl. ¶¶ 13-14.) Nevertheless, the Court notes Defendant fails to provide any legal authority or sufficient evidence supporting her arguments for the alleged lack of notice.

           A subsequent purchaser from a purchaser at a foreclosure sale may bring an unlawful detainer to evict.  (Evans v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 162, 167-68.)  “Adding the additional requirement that a subsequent purchaser from the foreclosure sale purchaser prove his own acquisition of title from said foreclosure sale purchaser no more destroys the summary nature of the proceedings than does the corresponding requirement that the landlord's successor in estate prove his acquisition of such interest in a proceeding pursuant to [CCP § 1161].”  (Id. at 169.) 

           Essentially, a defendant may challenge a plaintiff’s right to possession under CCP § 1161(a) by demonstrating noncompliance with Civil Code § 2924, but any other sort of challenge to the plaintiff’s title does not constitute a valid defense to an unlawful detainer claim.  (MCA, Inc. v Universal Diversified Enters. Corp. (1972) 27 Cal.App.3d 170, 176-77.)  For example, a defendant may assert that the foreclosure process was fraudulent because a forbearance agreement had been reached before the sale took place.  (Id.)  The defendant would not be challenging the plaintiff’s ownership of the property, but rather the plaintiff’s right to initiate unlawful detainer proceedings based on the foreclosure sale.  (See Id.)   

           Thus, Defendant failed to meet her burden of proof and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff. Therefore, the Court denies Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

          

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

           The Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

           Service of A Notice To Quit        

           Plaintiff has met its burden for this element. The process server who served the notice to quit, Eric Ratliff states “[o]n May 27, 2024, at approximately 3:30 pm, I proceeded to the premises located at 3924 Park Place, Unit # 3, Montrose, CA 91020.” On May 30, 2024, at 12:00 midnight, the Notice to Quit expired by its own terms. (Reyes Decl. ¶ 10.) Additionally, Plaintiff provided the Proofs of Service for the Notice to Quit. (RJN Nos. 9-10.) Mr. Ratliff further states “I took photographs which are attached as Exhibit 14 and they accurately depict the premises at the time they were taken. They show the posing of the notice Ms. Ch[u]ng taking the notice down, the unit number (3) and the front of the premises from a distance showing the street number on the building of 3942.” (Ratliff Decl. at ¶ 10.) Therefore, Plaintiff has met its burden. The burden shifts to Defendant. However, Defendant failed to file an opposition. Thus, there is no triable issue of material fact as to this element.

           Defendants Fail To Quit and Deliver Possession

           Plaintiff has met its burden for this element. The custodian of records, Olivia Reyes states, “[s]ince 03/21/2024, the premises has been occupied, used, and enjoyed without the consent or authorization of Plaintiff. Defendant's title, if any, to said premises, that existed prior to 03/21/2024, was extinguished by said sale.” (Reyes Decl. ¶ 14.) She further states “Defendant remains in possession of the premises as of the date of this declaration. Plaintiff anticipates that Defendant will remain in possession of the premises until removed by court order. If Defendant were to vacate prior to this hearing, Plaintiff will submit a supplemental declaration informing the court of Defendant's vacation.” (Id. at ¶ 15.) Therefore, Plaintiff has met its burden. The burden shifts to Defendant. However, Defendant failed to file an opposition. Thus, there is no triable issue of material fact as to this element.

 

          

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

           The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment.
