DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV37618    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: 30

Calendar # 11

Date: 1/9/2025

Case Name: NAT-PHARM INC., vs SILVERADO SENIOR LIVING, INC., et al.

Case # 20STCV37618

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

 

Silverado Senior Living Holdings, Inc. and Silverado Senior Living Management (Defendants) move for summary judgment against Nat-Pharm Inc. (Plaintiff). Defendants argue that there is no triable issue of material fact concerning their service past the limitations period established in Code of Civil Procedure section 583.210. Plaintiff contends that Defendants waived this defense by participating in litigation, or alternatively, that the doctrine of equitable estoppel tolled the time for service. The Court denies summary judgment.

 

A.    Plaintiff was given sufficient notice.

 

As a threshold matter, Plaintiff contends that its due process rights were violated by Defendants filing the present motion only 75 days in advance of the hearing. “Notice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 81 days before the time appointed for hearing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(2).) However, this statute was recently amended, only taking effect on January 1, 2025. When Defendants initially filed their motion for summary judgment, the required notice period was only 75 days. There is no reason why the new notice requirement should be applied retroactively. Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiff received sufficient notice.  

 

B.     Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a claim or defense and if not to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, a moving defendant “has met that party’s burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing plaintiff “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

C.     Discussion

 

Defendants move for summary judgment on the basis that their service more than three years after Plaintiff filed its complaint bars any claims against them. However, Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of material fact regarding the potential application of the doctrine of equitable estoppel.

 

Plaintiff’s claims against Defendants are not time barred.

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s claims are barred because Plaintiff served Defendants more than three years after Plaintiff filed its initial complaint. “The summons and complaint shall be served upon a defendant within three years after the action is commenced against the defendant. For the purpose of this subdivision, an action is commenced at the time the complaint is filed.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.210, subd. (a).) This time bar does not apply if the defendant enters a stipulation or does another act that constitutes a general appearance in the action. (Id., § 583.220.) A general appearance does not include the entering of a stipulation extending the time for service, a motion to dismiss made pursuant to this chapter, or an extension of time to plead after filing a motion to dismiss. (Ibid.) If service is not made within the time prescribed, the action shall not be further prosecuted, and it shall be dismissed by the Court. (Id., § 583.250.) Because Defendants were served more than three years after Plaintiff filed its complaint, Defendants have met their burden of demonstrating that there is a complete defense to all causes of action. The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact.

 

First, Plaintiff argues that Defendants may not seek dismissal pursuant to section 583.250 because they have already entered a general appearance in the present action. However, Defendants’ appearance in the action occurred after the three-year limitations period had already expired. “To prevent dismissal, any claimed general appearance must have occurred within the mandatory three-year period. An appearance made thereafter does not deprive a defendant of his right to dismissal.” (Brookview Condominium Owners’ Assn. v. Heltzer Enterprizes-Brookview (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 502, 509 (Brookview).) Because Defendants did not participate in the present action until after being served, more than three years after Plaintiff filed its complaint, the exception in section 583.220 does not apply. Plaintiff’s argument regarding waiver fails under Brookview as well. There, the Court found dismissal appropriate even after the defendant participated in litigation for eight months, because the initial service occurred after the three-year period and defendant did not make a general appearance within those three years. (Ibid.) Thus, Plaintiff’s arguments regarding section 583.220 and waiver are without merit.

 

Second, Plaintiff argues that the doctrine of equitable estoppel applies. “[A] defendant may be equitably estopped from asserting the statute of limitations when, as the result of intentional concealment, the plaintiff is unable to discover the defendant’s actual identity.” (Bernson v. Browning-Ferris Industries (1994) 7 Cal.4th 926, 936 (Bernson).) Tolling only occurs “until such time that the plaintiff knows, or through the exercise of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the defendant’s identity.” (Ibid.) Lack of knowledge, by itself, is not sufficient to toll the statute: “a plaintiff may not disregard reasonably available avenues of inquiry which, if vigorously pursued, might yield the desired information.” (Ibid.)  And C.C.P. section 583.140 states that “[n]othing in this chapter abrogates or otherwise affects the principles of waiver and estoppel.”

 

Here, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether the doctrine of equitable estoppel applies, as Plaintiff presents multiple instances of potential concealment by Defendants. In response to an interrogatory from May 2021 seeking the identity of her current and former employers, Mary Giebel failed to mention Defendants. (Bhakta Decl. ¶ 9, Ex. B.) Additionally, special interrogatories propounded by Plaintiff seeking the identity of persons responsible for certain acts failed to identify Defendants, despite them employing some individuals listed in the discovery responses. (Id., Ex. C.) Another party in the present action previously identified employees of Defendants as persons most knowledgeable in response Plaintiff’s deposition notice, without identifying Defendants. (Id., Ex. E.) While tolling may not extend past August 16, 2022, when Defendants were revealed to Plaintiff through the production of an organizational chart (Emdee Decl. ¶ 6), tolling up until this point would defeat Defendants’ defense under section 583.250.

 

Taken together, Plaintiff’s evidence creates a triable issue of material fact regarding Defendants’ defense under section 583.250, in that the doctrine of equitable estoppel may have acted to extend Plaintiff’s window of service into 2025. Thus, Plaintiff has met its burden to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied.

 


Case Number: 21STCV22984    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: 30

Calendar # 8

Date: 1/9/2025

Case Name: MARIA ANGELICA GARCIA, et al. vs ALLSTATE NORTHBROOK INDEMNITY COMPANY, et al.

Case # 21STCV22984

                       

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

 

Meet and Confer:

 

Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “the moving party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 439, subd. (a).)

 

Counsel for Defendant met and conferred with counsel for Plaintiffs regarding this motion via telephone on November 8, 2024, but the parties were unable to reach a resolution. (Bennett Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. B.) The Court deems these efforts sufficient.

 

Analysis

 

Allstate Northbrook Indemnity Company (Defendant) moves for judgment on the pleadings against the first amended complaint (FAC) of Maria Garcia et al. (Plaintiffs). The motion is granted.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).) A motion by a defendant may be made on the grounds that the complaint or cross-complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(B)(ii).) A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer but is made after the time for demurrer has expired. Except as provided by statute, the rules governing demurrers apply. (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.)  Pursuant to Code Civ Proc. Section 438(d) the Court shall consider not only the face of the pleading, but also any matter of which the Court is required to take judicial notice.  

 

Like a general demurrer, “ordinarily, a [motion for judgment on the pleadings] does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action, and if any part of a cause of action is properly pleaded, the [motion] will be overruled.” (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether properly pled factual allegations—assumed to be true and liberally construed—are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Stone Street Capital, LLC v. Cal. State Lottery Com’n (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 109, 116.)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

This action arises out of an automobile collision that occurred on August 26, 2017. (FAC ¶ 1.) Plaintiffs allege causes of action for breach of contract, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200, unjust enrichment, injunctive relief, and declaratory relief. 

 

            Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage claims must be determined by an agreement between the insurer and the insured, or through arbitration. (Ins. Code, § 11580.2, subd. (f).) No cause of action can accrue to an individual availing themselves of UM coverage under Insurance Code section 11580.2 unless, within two years of the date of the accident, the insured: (1) files a suit against the uninsured motorist; (2) reaches an agreement as to the amount due under the policy; or (3) formally institutes arbitration proceedings. (Id., § 11580.2, subd. (i)(1).) Arbitration proceedings instituted pursuant to this section shall conclude within five years from the institution of the proceeding. (Id., § 11580.2, subd. (i)(2)(A).) This limitations period acts similarly to a dismissal for failure to prosecute. (Santangelo v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 804, 813–814.)

 

On October 16, 2024, the Court ruled that Plaintiffs’ UM claims are barred under Insurance Code section 11580.2, subdivision (i)(2)(A), because more than five years have passed since the institution of arbitration proceedings. Defendant now moves for judgment on the pleadings as to all causes of action on the basis that any benefits Plaintiffs were previously entitled to are now time-barred, causing Plaintiffs’ causes of action to fail as a matter of law.

 

            Plaintiffs’ cause of action for breach of contract fails as a matter of law.

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiffs cannot state a claim for breach of contract considering the Court’s previous ruling. The elements for a cause of action for breach of contract are: (1) the existence of a contract; (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom. (Property California SCJLW One Corp. v. Leamy (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 1155, 1162.) Generally, an insured may pursue a breach of contract claim against an insurer by alleging “the insurance contract, the insured’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, the insurer’s breach, and resulting damages.” (Case v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co., Inc. (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 397, 402 (Case).) Because contract-related damages are required, the insured’s cause of action for breach of contract fails if there are no unpaid policy benefits. (Ibid.)

 

Here, Defendant does not contest the existence of a contract between it and Plaintiffs. Rather, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs cannot show that they suffered any damages because Plaintiffs’ UM claims are now time-barred by Insurance Code section 11580.2. Plaintiffs allege damages in the form of “unpaid policy benefits, interest on contract amounts owed, and other out-of-pocket expenses, as well as consequential damages, in a sum exceeding $100,000.00.” (FAC ¶ 52.) Because Plaintiffs’ UM benefits are now time-barred, Defendant no longer owes Plaintiffs any unpaid policy benefits. Thus, Plaintiffs’ cause of action fails under Case, as Plaintiffs cannot prove any damages because they are no longer entitled to UM benefits, having failed to arbitrate them within the limitations period. Accordingly, the Court grants the motion of Defendant on Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

Plaintiffs’ cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing fails as a matter of law.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs are also barred from stating a cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. There are two separate requirements for establishing breach of the implied covenant: “(1) benefits due under the policy must have been withheld; and (2) the reason for withholding benefits must have been unreasonable or without proper cause.” (Love v. Fire Ins. Exchange (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 1136, 1151.) Thus, a bad faith claim may not be maintained where benefits are no longer due. (Id. at p. 1153.) Here, as discussed above, Plaintiffs are not entitled to UM benefits because they failed to arbitrate their claims within the five-year limitations period. Thus, Plaintiffs cannot maintain a cause of action for breach of the implied covenant. Accordingly, the Court grants the motion of Defendant on Plaintiffs’ second cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs’ cause of action under the Unfair Competition Law fails as a matter of law.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs are also barred from stating a cause of action under the Unfair Competition Law (UCL). Unfair competition includes “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A court may issue injunctive relief to “restore to any person in interest any money or property, real or personal, which may have been acquired by means of such unfair competition.” (Id., § 17203.) A party alleging unfair competition under section 17200 must “state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619 (Khoury).) Actions may only be brought by a private individual if that person has “suffered injury in fact and has lost money or property as a result of the unfair competition.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17204.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs cannot show that they have lost money or property as a result of any unfair competition, because they are not owed any UM benefits, as discussed above. Thus, Plaintiffs’ cause of action fails as a matter of law, as they are unable to allege all the required statutory elements of a UCL violation. Accordingly, the Court grants the motion of Defendant as to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs’ cause of action for unjust enrichment fails as a matter of law.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action for unjust enrichment fails as well. The causes of action for unjust enrichment and restitution are interchangeable. One who is enriched at the expense of another is required to make restitution. (Prof. Tax Appeal v. Kennedy-Wilson Holdings, Inc. (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 230, 238 (Kennedy-Wilson).) The elements of a cause of action for unjust enrichment or restitution are “receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that the relevant benefit consists of the insurance premiums they paid Defendant leading up to the accident given Defendant’s subsequent failure to pay Plaintiffs’ claim. (FAC ¶¶ 57–58.) However, Plaintiffs cannot demonstrate that Defendant owed Plaintiffs any benefits under the insurance policy, since these benefits are now barred by Insurance Code section 11580.2, subdivision (i)(2)(A). Thus, Defendant has not been unjustly enriched through Plaintiffs’ payment of premiums under the terms of the contract between the parties, and Plaintiffs have failed to plead a cause of action for unjust enrichment. Accordingly, the Court grants the motion of Defendant as to Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs’ fifth and sixth causes of action for declaratory and injunctive relief are moot.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ causes of action for injunctive and declaratory relief are moot. With respect to injunctive relief, Plaintiffs request that the Court issue a “permanent injunction prohibiting the Defendant from requiring that any aspect of the arbitration be bifurcated, and further that Defendants . . . be estopped from contacting either directly or indirectly any Plaintiffs, for any reason, without the express consent of their attorneys of record.” (FAC ¶ 57.) Plaintiffs’ request concerning the bifurcation of arbitration proceedings is moot given that those proceedings have been dismissed by the Court. And Plaintiffs’ other request concerning the prevention of communication between Defendant and Plaintiffs is moot given the dispositive nature of the present motion.

 

With respect to declaratory relief, Plaintiffs request that the Court “issue a declaratory order including the determination that Defendants have waived any rights to compel arbitration due to their untimely petition for the same.” (Id. ¶ 62.) This request is moot given that the Court has already compelled, and later dismissed, arbitration. Plaintiffs further request that the Court issue a declaratory order determining that the arbitration agreement between Plaintiffs and Defendant is unenforceable. (Id. ¶ 63.) This request is moot for the same reason. Thus, Plaintiffs have not pled facts sufficient to support a cause of action for either injunctive or declaratory relief. Additionally, the Court does not find any reasonable possibility that Plaintiffs’ may cure the defects in their FAC if granted leave to amend, given that their causes of action rely on insurance benefits which are irretrievable given the statutory time bar on their arbitration. Accordingly, the Court grants the motion of Defendant as to Plaintiffs’ fifth and sixth causes of action and does not grant leave to amend as to any cause of action.

 

Conclusion :

 

            The motion is granted, without leave to amend. Counsel for Defendant is to prepare and submit a proposed Judgment.

 


Case Number: 22STCV14421    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: 30

Calendar # 10

Date: 1/9/2025

Case Name: GWENDA PEREZ vs GK MANAGEMENT CO. INC.

Case # 22STCV14421

 

MOTION FOR A PROTECTIVE ORDER

 

            Plaintiff Gwenda Perez (Plaintiff) moves for a protective order limiting the subpoena served by GK Management Co. Inc. and Dolphin Marina Ltd.’s (Defendants) upon third party Orlando Health, Inc. (Orlando). The motion for a protective order is granted. Sanctions are not awarded.

 

A.    Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.060 provides that “[t]he court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b).) The motion “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b)(5).)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020, subd. (a), provides that “[t]he court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”

The court shall restrict the frequency or extent of the use of a discovery method if it determines either of the following: 

1.       The discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.¿ 

2.       The selected method of discovery is unduly burdensome or expensive, taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.¿¿ 

(Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 2019.030, subd. (a).)

“For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it ‘might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.’ [Citation]. Admissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence. [Citation] These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery.” (Gonzales v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

During the deposition of Plaintiff on June 4 and 5, 2024, Defendants learned of her hospitalization in Florida in 2018 where she received treatment for injuries that allegedly occurred because of her residency at Defendants’ apartments. (O’Brien Decl. ¶ 6.) Defendants served a subpoena on that hospital, Orlando, seeking records related to that hospitalization on November 11, 2024. (Id. ¶ 7.) Regarding meet and confer efforts, Plaintiff’s counsel allegedly contacted Defendants’ counsel on November 18, December 4, and December 5, receiving no response. (Spelger Decl. ¶¶ 6–8.) Defendants’ counsel allegedly contacted Plaintiff’s counsel on December 4 and December 9, and the parties were unable to reach a resolution. (O’Brien Decl. ¶ 9, Exs. D–G.) The Court deems such efforts between the parties sufficient, while questioning Plaintiff’s counsel’s assertion that Defendants failed to confer given the proffered evidence of communications between the parties.

 

            Plaintiff moves for a protective order limiting Defendants’ subpoena to Orlando. The present dispute appears to be caused by miscommunication between the parties regarding the scope of Defendants’ subpoena. Defendants’ subpoena requests that Orlando produce: (1) all communications between it and Plaintiff; (2) all medical records related to Plaintiff; (3) all invoices and payment records regarding services performed on behalf of Plaintiff; and (4) all communications with any other medical professionals related to Plaintiff. (Spelger Decl., Ex. 1.) Plaintiff contends that such a production demand encompasses her entire lifetime of medical records. However, given that Plaintiff only received treatment at Orlando for a few days according to her testimony, the only records Orlando likely would have in its possession would relate to those few days.

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ subpoena is overly broad because it seeks Plaintiff’s entire medical and psychological records and is unlimited in scope and time. As discussed above, the Court finds that the subpoena is temporally limited because of Plaintiff’s brief visit to Orlando and limited in scope because Orlando would presumably only possess records from that single visit. The documents sought by Defendants are directly related to Plaintiff’s claims because she contends that the treatment she received at Orlando related to injuries she sustained from Defendants’ conduct. (O’Brien Decl., Ex. C.) Further, Plaintiff has put at issue many different medical issues via her responses to Defendants’ form interrogatories. Thus, the Court does not endorse Plaintiff’s attempt to limit the subpoena to records concerning respiratory illness only.

 

            Effectively, Defendants do not oppose a protective order limiting production of documents by Orlando to those relating to Plaintiff’s visit in 2018, as they contend that the subpoena only covers those documents anyway. Such an order would serve as a clarification if Orlando somehow had possession of the entirety of Plaintiff’s medical records. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for such a protective order is granted. The Court need not address the parties’ other arguments.

 

Both parties request sanctions. “The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2019.030, subd. (c).) The Court is not inclined to award sanctions against Defendants because they explained the limited scope of the subpoena during the meet and confer process in which Plaintiff’s counsel seemingly did not participate. (Spelger Decl. ¶¶ 7–9.) Thus, the Court finds that Defendants acted with substantial justification in opposing the present motion given the subpoena’s context, and sanctions are not warranted.

 

 

Conclusion:

 

            The motion for a protective order limiting the production of documents to those directly concerning Plaintiff’s visit to Orlando Health, Inc. in 2018 is granted. Sanctions are not awarded.

Case Name: MARIA REYES vs LIVING SPACES MANUFACTURING, LLC

Case # 24STCV06051

           

MOTION TO COMPEL DISCOVERY

 

Tentative Ruling:

Local Court Rules: Department 30

 

            Defendant asserts that Plaintiff did not participate in an informal discovery conference prior to moving to compel further responses. Local Court rules dictate “The court will not hear a discovery motion, other than a motion to compel where there has been no response, until the parties participate in an informal discovery conference with the court.” The present motion qualifies under this rule, since Defendant produced a PMQ for deposition and Plaintiff objects to Defendant’s responses at that deposition.

 

            On September 24, 2024, Plaintiff took the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ. (Chami Decl. ¶ 4.) Defendant’s counsel objected to many questions at the deposition and advised the PMQ not to answer questions. (Id. ¶ 5.) The parties met and conferred following the deposition, with Defendant’s counsel agreeing to another day of deposition testimony on October 10. (Chang Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. 3.) Plaintiff did not agree to this proposal and canceled the second day of deposition testimony already set for September 28. (Id. ¶¶ 6–7, Exs. 4–5.) Plaintiff did not request or participate in an informal discovery conference prior to filing the motion. Because the present motion seeks to compel further responses to deposition questions where Defendant has already offered a response, local rules dictate that Plaintiff first participate in an informal discovery conference. Accordingly, the present motion is stayed pending participation in an IDC.

 

            The Court does not impose sanctions as the present motion is stayed

            The Court stays Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to deposition questions pending participation in an informal discovery conference.
