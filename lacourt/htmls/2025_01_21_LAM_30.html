DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 24STCV11457#2    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 30

Calendar # 2

Date: 1/21/2025

Case Name: ANTAMARI WOOLEY, A MINOR, BY AND THROUGH HIS GUARDIAN AD LITEM, LATASHA WILLIAMS vs UNIVERSAL POINTE APTS., INC., et al.

Case # 24STCV11457

 

DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE (ANZA)

 

 

Anza Management Company (Defendant) demurs to Latasha Williams and Antamari Wooley’s (Plaintiffs) fifth, sixth, seventh, and tenth causes of action in their first amended complaint (FAC). Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiffs’ requests for punitive damages, requests for excessive fees under Civil Code section 789.3, and allegations concerning anonymous online reviews. Defendant’s demurrer is sustained as to Plaintiffs’ sixth and tenth causes of action and their motion to strike is granted in part. Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend their complaint with respect to the tenth cause of action.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is sustained where “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., 430.10, subd. (e).) “A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Yalung v. State (2023) 98 Cal.App.5th 71, 80.) In reviewing a complaint’s legal sufficiency, a court will treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. (Esparza v. Kaweah Delta Dist. Hospital (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 547, 552.) It is well settled that a “demurrer lies only for defects appearing on the face of the complaint[.]” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 594, 601.) “We not only treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but also give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Guclimane Co. v. Stewart Tit. Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, the complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded. (Wilner v. Sunset Life Ins. Co. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 952, 958.)

 

When ruling on a demurrer, a court may only consider the complaint’s allegations or matters which may be judicially noticed. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The Court may not consider any other extrinsic evidence or judge the credibility of the allegations pleaded or the difficulty a plaintiff may have in proving his allegations. (Ion Equipment Corporation v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.) A demurrer is properly sustained only when the complaint, liberally construed, fails to state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action. (Kramer v. Intuit Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 574, 578.)

 

The Court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The Court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the Court. (Id., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

As a threshold matter, the declaration of Taryn Jacobson submitted with Plaintiffs’ opposition to the present motion is improper. Extrinsic evidence is not considered when evaluating a demurrer or motion to strike, and thus the Court will not consider the declaration.

 

Separately, Defendant argues in their reply that demurrer is appropriate because Plaintiffs have not provided a copy of the lease agreement. Defendant cites no authority requiring the production of evidence to survive demurrer in a landlord tenant dispute based on a written lease agreement. Indeed, Plaintiffs are not required to meet an evidentiary burden to survive demurrer; only Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint and the allegations therein are considered. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is overruled on this ground.

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ first, fifth, sixth, seventh, and tenth causes of action on the basis that Plaintiffs have failed to plead facts sufficient to constitute such causes of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) Defendant also argues that the causes of action are uncertain. (Id., § 430.10, subd. (f).) Defendant moves to strike Plaintiffs’ requests for punitive damages, excessive fees under Civil Code section 789.3, and allegations concerning anonymous reviews. (Id., § 436, subd. (a).) The Court will address each argument in turn.

 

Plaintiffs have not named Defendant under the first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for breach of breach of contract. However, Defendant is not named under that cause of action, which applies only to Universal Pointe Apartments, Inc. Thus, Defendant’s demurrer as to this cause of action is superfluous. Accordingly, the Court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs have pled sufficient facts to state their fifth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the basis that Plaintiffs do not plead sufficient facts alleging outrageous conduct. A claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress requires a showing of “(1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.” (Huntingdon Life Sciences v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.) A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ [citation] And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’ [citation].” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050–1051.) This conduct must be alleged with specificity to avoid demurrer. (Yau v. Santa Margarita Ford, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 144, 161.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant acted in an outrageous manner by “intentionally ignoring and failing to keep the Property clean, sanitary, and free from accumulations of debris, filth, rubbish, and garbage, and by violating health and safety codes, ordinances, regulations, and other laws.” (FAC ¶ 152.) Plaintiffs additionally allege that Defendant knew of the Property’s untenantable state for years yet refused to remediate the issue in a way that was offensive to common decency. (Id. ¶ 153.) Plaintiffs further allege that their entire unit flooded due to faulty plumbing in 2023 and Defendant failed to fix the damage to the unit after a month of Plaintiffs’ being displaced. (Id. ¶ 78.) Instead, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant offered for Plaintiffs to sleep in the building’s recreational room, directly adjacent to other tenants which Defendant knew to have previously harassed Plaintiffs. (Id. ¶ 71.) Additionally, Plaintiffs allege that they were subjected to racial slurs by Defendant when expressing concerns. (Id. ¶ 36.) Taken together, such alleged conduct by Defendant exceeds that which is usually tolerated by a civilized society. Thus, Plaintiffs have pled specific facts constituting outrageous conduct by Defendants.

 

Plaintiffs allege that Defendant acted with reckless disregard of the probability that their behavior would cause emotional distress (id. ¶ 155), and that Plaintiffs suffered severe emotional distress because of Defendants’ conduct (id. ¶ 156–157). Thus, Plaintiffs have pled facts sufficient to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, and Plaintiffs’ pleading is not uncertain. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is overruled as to the fifth cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs sixth cause of action for private nuisance is duplicative.

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action for private nuisance on the basis that it is uncertain. “Anything which is injurious to health, including, but not limited to, the illegal sale of controlled substances, or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property . . . is a nuisance.” (Civ. Code, § 3479.) Private nuisances are those which do not affect “at the same time an entire community or neighborhood, or any considerable number of persons.” (Id. §§ 3480, 3481.) Importantly, “[t]he abatement of a nuisance does not prejudice the right of any person to recover damages for its past existence.” (Id. § 3484.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that the conditions of the Property were a nuisance which Defendant failed to abate. (FAC ¶¶ 161–162.) Indeed, Plaintiffs’ allegations concerning their apartment’s flooding and Defendants’ failure to fix the issue constitute an obstruction of the free use of property as required by statute. Thus, Plaintiffs have pled facts sufficient to state a cause of action for nuisance.

 

Defendant argues that demurrer is appropriate because Plaintiffs cannot seek injunctive relief given that they vacated the premises months ago. However, Plaintiffs are entitled to seek damages for the past existence of a nuisance under Civil Code section 3484.

 

Defendant additionally argues that Plaintiffs’ private nuisance claim is duplicative of their second, third, and eighth causes of action concerning habitability and negligence. Nuisance causes of action concerning housing conditions are permitted, “[b]ut because of the broad definition of nuisance, whether a cause of action is viable depends on the facts of each case.” (El Escorial Owners’ Assn. v. DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1337, 1348 (El Escorial).) For example, “[w]here negligence and nuisance causes of action rely on the same facts about lack of due care, the nuisance claim is a negligence claim.” (Id. at p. 1349.) Effectively, if a nuisance cause of action is just a clone of a different cause of action with a different label, demurrer is appropriate. (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs’ nuisance cause of action rests on the same habitability defects underpinning Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, third cause of action for violations of Civil Code section 1941, and eighth cause of action for negligence. (FAC ¶¶ 161–162.) Thus, permitting Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action to go forward would risk allowing a traditional tort to be litigated “under the guise of a nuisance action” as prohibited under El Escorial. (City of San Diego v. U.S. Gypsum Co. (1995) 30 Cal.App.4th 575, 587.) Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer as to the sixth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Plaintiffs’ seventh cause of action for unlawful actions by landlord influencing tenant to vacate is not duplicative.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ seventh cause of action alleging violations of Civil Code section 1940.2 is duplicative of other causes of action. Under section 1940.2, it is unlawful to “[u]se, or threaten to use, force, willful threats, or menacing conduct constituting a course of conduct that interferes with the tenant’s quiet enjoyment of the premises” or to commit a significant or intentional violation of section 1954, in an effort to influence a tenant to vacate a dwelling. (Civ. Code, § 1940.2, subds. (a)(3), (a)(4).) Section 1954 limits the circumstances in which a landlord may enter a dwelling unit.

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant repeatedly entered their unit in violation of section 1954. (FAC ¶ 170.) Plaintiffs further allege that Defendant has interfered with Plaintiffs’ quiet enjoyment of the property in a menacing fashion. (Id. ¶¶ 169, 173.) While Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment encapsulates similar conduct, that cause of action does not require a showing of menacing conduct, or that the landlord acted with a purpose to influence the tenant to vacate the premises. Similarly, Plaintiffs’ third and fourth causes of action involve the same conduct, but once again do not require a showing of purpose. Thus, Plaintiffs’ seventh cause of action is distinct, requiring that additional elements be proven. Nor is the cause of action uncertain. Plaintiffs make specific allegations regarding conduct alleged to be intentional and coercive in violation of section 1940.2. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is overruled as to Plaintiffs’ seventh cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs have not pled facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action under the Unfair Competition Law.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs have not pled sufficient facts to support a cause of action for unfair competition. Unfair competition includes “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A court may issue injunctive relief to “restore to any person in interest any money or property, real or personal, which may have been acquired by means of such unfair competition.” (Id., § 17203.) A party alleging unfair competition under section 17200 must “state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619 (Khoury).)

 

A business practice may be unfair in violation of the unfair competition law even if the practice does not violate any law. (Olszewski v. Scripps Health (2003) 30 Cal.4th 798, 827.) “The test of whether a business practice is unfair ‘involves an examination of [its] impact on its alleged victim, balanced against the reasons, justifications and motives of the alleged wrongdoer. In brief, the court must weigh the utility of the defendant’s conduct against the gravity of the harm to the alleged victim.” (Candelore v. Tinder, Inc. (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 1138, 1155.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that all statutory violations committed by Defendant and any breach of the implied warranty of habitability also constitute unfair business practices. (FAC ¶¶ 210–211.) Plaintiffs seek restitution for rent paid pursuant to Business and Professions Code section 17200. (Id. ¶ 214.) In merely alleging that all unlawful actions by Defendant violate section 17200, Plaintiffs have failed to state with any particularity specific business practices supporting the cause of action. Such conclusory allegations are insufficient to survive demurrer. Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is sustained as to Plaintiffs’ tenth cause of action. Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend.

 

C.     Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages and all paragraphs referencing punitive damages in the complaint. A plaintiff may recover punitive damages where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) Malice is defined as conduct intended to injure the plaintiff or despicable conduct by a defendant acting with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights and safety of others. (Id., § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of their rights. (Id., § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) And fraud refers to intentional misrepresentations or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant. (Id., § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

 

Here, Plaintiffs plead facts justifying punitive damages. Plaintiffs allege that Defendant acted maliciously by exhibiting a conscious disregard for the rights and safety of others. Specifically, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant had constructive knowledge of the issues with the property due to multiple government notices and complaints from Plaintiffs and other tenants, and could ascertain the issues with the property upon visual inspection. (FAC ¶ 100.) Indeed, Defendants’ awareness of the issues with the property could justify a finding that Defendant acted with malice under section 3294. Additionally, Plaintiffs allege intentional conduct by Defendant meant to influence them to vacate the premises. (Id. ¶¶ 171–172, 176.) When ruling on a motion to strike, the Court must take the facts pleaded as true, and such alleged conduct could constitute malice or oppression as well. Thus, Plaintiffs have pled specific facts sufficient to justify a request for punitive damages. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike on this ground is denied.

 

Defendant also argues that Plaintiffs’ requests for damages in excess of $100 per violation pursuant to Civil Code section 789.3 should be stricken. That section prohibits a landlord from “willfully [causing], directly or indirectly, the interruption or termination of any utility service furnished the tenant” with the intent to terminate the occupancy under a lease. (Civ. Code, § 789.3, subd. (a).) A landlord who violates this section is liable for any actual damages to the tenant and an additional $100 for each day the landlord remains in violation. (Id., § 789.3, subd. (c)(2).) Each separate cause of action under section 789.3 shall entitle the plaintiff to an award of at least $250. (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant is liable for “special damages of not less than $100 per violation, and not more than $250 for each day the landlord remains in violation [of section 789.3].” (FAC ¶ 140.) In doing so, Plaintiffs misstate the special damages authorized under section 789.3. Under statute, Plaintiffs are entitled to a maximum $100 in special damages per day that utility services were interrupted. However, if the period of interruption is under three days, Plaintiffs are still entitled to a minimum of $250 in special damages. Thus, each non-consecutive period of interruption justifies a special damages award of at least $250, but for periods of interruption longer than two days damages are limited to $100 per day. Plaintiffs’ assertions to the contrary constitute a false matter contained in a pleading, strikable under Code of Civil Procedure section 436, subdivision (a). Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike on this ground is granted.

 

Finally, Defendant argues that the anonymous online reviews alleged in Plaintiffs FAC are improper hearsay. “Hearsay evidence is evidence of a statement that was made other than by a witness while testifying at the hearing and that is offered to prove the truth of the matter stated.” (Evid. Code, § 1200.) Hearsay evidence is inadmissible. (Ibid.) Here, the statements offered by Plaintiffs are not hearsay because they are not offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted, namely the problems with the property. Rather, the statements could be used to demonstrate that Defendant was made aware of the issues with the property. (FAC ¶ 52.) Thus, they do not constitute improper material contained in a pleading. Moreover, a motion to strike is not the proper vehicle for objecting to potential hearsay evidence. Pleadings may contain hearsay, provided that it is relevant, even if such statements are inadmissible at trial. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike on this ground is denied.

 

Conclusion:

 

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained as to the sixth and tenth causes of action. With respect to the tenth cause of action, Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend. Defendants’ motion to strike is granted as to Plaintiffs’ improper characterization of the remedies owed under section 789.3 only. 15 days leave to amend.

DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE (UNIVERSAL)

 

 

Universal Pointe Apts., Inc. (Defendant) demurs to Latasha Williams and Antamari Wooley’s (Plaintiffs) fifth, sixth, seventh, and tenth causes of action in their first amended complaint (FAC). Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiffs’ requests for punitive damages, requests for excessive fees under Civil Code section 789.3, and allegations concerning anonymous online reviews. Defendant’s demurrer is sustained as to Plaintiffs’ sixth and tenth causes of action and their motion to strike is granted in part. Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend their complaint with respect to the tenth cause of action.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is sustained where “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., 430.10, subd. (e).) “A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Yalung v. State (2023) 98 Cal.App.5th 71, 80.) In reviewing a complaint’s legal sufficiency, a court will treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. (Esparza v. Kaweah Delta Dist. Hospital (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 547, 552.) It is well settled that a “demurrer lies only for defects appearing on the face of the complaint[.]” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 594, 601.) “We not only treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but also give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Guclimane Co. v. Stewart Tit. Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, the complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded. (Wilner v. Sunset Life Ins. Co. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 952, 958.)

 

When ruling on a demurrer, a court may only consider the complaint’s allegations or matters which may be judicially noticed. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The Court may not consider any other extrinsic evidence or judge the credibility of the allegations pleaded or the difficulty a plaintiff may have in proving his allegations. (Ion Equipment Corporation v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.) A demurrer is properly sustained only when the complaint, liberally construed, fails to state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action. (Kramer v. Intuit Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 574, 578.)

 

The Court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The Court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the Court. (Id., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)

Code, § 1200.) Hearsay evidence is inadmissible. (Ibid.) Here, the statements offered by Plaintiffs are not hearsay because they are not offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted, namely the problems with the property. Rather, the statements could be used to demonstrate that Defendants were made aware of the issues with the property. (FAC ¶ 52.) Thus, they do not constitute improper material contained in a pleading. Moreover, a motion to strike is not the proper vehicle for objecting to potential hearsay evidence. Pleadings may contain hearsay, provided that it is relevant, even if such statements are inadmissible at trial. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike on this ground is denied.

 

Conclusion:

 

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained as to the sixth and tenth causes of action. With respect to the tenth cause of action, Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend. Defendant’s motion to strike is granted as to Plaintiffs’ improper characterization of the remedies owed under section 789.3 only. 15 days to amend.
