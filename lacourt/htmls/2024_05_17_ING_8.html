DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19TRCV00871    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 8


Tentative Ruling


HEARING DATE: March 17, 2024


CASE NUMBER: 19TRCV00871


CASE NAME: Donald Baxter v. Alvin Pittman, et al.


MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff, Donald Baxter

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendants, Alvin L. Pittman and Law Offices of Alvin L. Pittman

DISPOSED OF DATE: April 12, 2023

MOTION:¿ (1) Motion to Amend Judgment

Tentative Rulings: (1) CONTINUE with supplemental briefing by the parties.

I. BACKGROUND


A. Factual


On April 12, 2023, the jury awarded Plaintiff $10,000,000 with some express and potentially some implied adjustments. That same day, the Court entered judgment on the jury verdict, reducing the total verdict to $6,800,000, deducting the full amount of the past non-economic loss, and the full amount of the future non-economic loss that the jury awarded, and accounting for the comparative fault of Brian D. Witzer at 15%.

Now, Plaintiff moves this Court to award costs and pre-judgment interest based on Plaintiff’s previously tendered and rejected 998 offers. Plaintiff further moves to correct/amend the judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473(d) on the basis that the judgment omits $250,000 worth of past pain/suffering/emotional distress that would have been obtainable against the physician in the underlying case, and therefore, should have also been assessed against Defendants, Alvin Pittman and Law Offices of Alvin Pittman in the present case and was allegedly improperly reduced.

As such, Plaintiffs also seek to amend the Judgement to have this Court include the $250,000 MICRA-capped pain and suffering amount.

B. Procedural

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Amend Judgment. On May 6, 2024, Defendants, Alvin L. Pittman and Law Offices of Alvin L. Pittman filed an opposition brief. On May 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief.

II. ANALYSIS 


A. Legal Standard

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out they name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect. . .The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as my be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., §473, subd. (a)(1).) The court, “regardless of the lapse of time, has inherent power to correct mistakes in its judgments or orders. [citation] This power, however, is limited to mistakes which are not the result of an exercise of judicial discretion.” (In re Sloan’s Estate (1963) 222 Cal.App.2d 283, 292.)

Under Code of Civil Procedure §1033.5, subdivision (b)(1), ... parties may not recover expert witness fees as costs “except when expressly authorized by law.” Such an express authorization exists under section 998. (First Nationwide Bank v. Mountain Cascade, Inc. (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 871, 875-876.) Whether a section 998 offer was reasonable and made in good faith is left to the sound discretion of the trial court. (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 134.) “Similarly, the decision to award expert witness fees, and the determination of whether these fees were reasonably necessary, are issues left to the discretion of the trial court.” (Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1484.) On appeal, the reviewing court “may reverse the denial of statutorily authorized [expert] fees where the trial court failed to exercise its discretion to determine whether the requested fees were reasonable.” (LAOSD Asbestos Cases (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 1116, 1126.)

Code of Civil Procedure Section 998 establishes a procedure for shifting costs upon a party's refusal to settle by “expand[ing] the number and type of recoverable costs and fees” beyond those otherwise available to a prevailing party under Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (b). (Murillo v. Fleetwood Enterprises, Inc. (1998) 17 Cal.4th 985, 1000.) In addition to expert witness fees, a prevailing plaintiff in a personal injury case may obtain post offer interest on the judgment. (Civ. Code, § 3291.) Prejudgment interest may be awarded on personal injury damages whether they are economic and thus readily determinable in amount at the time the Section 998 offer was made, as well as non-economic damages that are not computable or subject to precise calculation until the jury makes its determination at trial. (Steinfeld v. Foote-Goldman Proctologic Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 13, 21.) The proper method of calculating Section 3291 prejudgment interest amount is to base the interest upon the entire judgment, except that future damages must be reduced to present value. (Deocampo v. Ahn (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 758, 775.)

B. Discussion

Here, Plaintiff have requested this Court, under Code of Civil Procedure section 116.725 to correct a computational/clerical error, whereby, when the Court reduced the non-economic damages awarded by the jury of $2 million ($1 million past, and $1 million future as to Defendant, Alvin Pittman), it also omitted the $250,000 in non-economic MICRA damages that would have otherwise been recoverable. Thus, Plaintiff asserts that judgment of $6,800,000 should therefore be amended to $7,050,000, to include the non-economic damages awarded by the jury as against South Bay Cancer Center and Dr. Rickles, and against Defendant, Alvin Pittman.

Plaintiff further contends that he served separate 998 offers to each defendant on August 15, 2023 for $500,000 each in this matter, and that both of which were deemed rejected by Defendants when the offers expired 30 days later. Thus, Plaintiff argues that pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 3291, applying the 998 statutory scheme to personal injury matters, pre-judgment interest began accruing from the date of each offer on August 15, 2023 up to the time of judgment on April 12, 2024. Moreover, Plaintiff notes that the total interest accrued during that 241-day period totals $465,493.15.

Based on this, Plaintiff argues this Court should amend the judgment entered on April 12, 2024 to award the $250,000 in non-economic damages that Plaintiff was entitled to, and award Plaintiff his pre-judgment interest based on his 998 offers, as well as the costs he incurred prosecuting this matter, as outlined in the memorandum of costs.

In opposition, Defendants, Alvin L. Pittman and the Law Offices of Alvin L. Pittman submit a declaration from Alvin L. Pittman which notes that: (1) Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs contain purportedly unreasonable witness fees in the amount of $113,636.76; (2) Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs contains extraordinarily high prejudgment interest in the amount of $465,493.15 and does not make clear how interest was calculated; (3) Plaintiff’s argument that the judgment should be increased to include the $250,000 in non-economic MICRA damages has no legal basis. Defendants note that the jury awarded Plaintiff “Past and/or future noneconomic loss, including physical pain/mental suffering/quality of life, impairment, inconvenience, disability, humiliation, embarrassment, disfigurement in the amount of $1 million, which is four times the $250,000; (4) the jury made an award of $3.6 million to Plaintiff for “past medical expenses,” and the Court should deduct from the verdict the “negotiated rate differential” to ensure Plaintiff only recovers medical sums actually incurred; and (5) there is insufficient evidence to justify the verdict of 85% responsibility to Alvin Pittman and only 15% to Brian Witzer. Alvin Pittman notes that he requested a special verdict which requested that the jury cut-off any responsibility by Attorney Pittman after the date of the substitution of attorney, April 20, 2018, but the Court rejected the request. Nonetheless, Pittman asserts that the evidence showed that Attorney Witzer had the case for almost a year, and he had complete control of the case and admitted he did not believe that Attorney Pittman was negligent. Moreover, Pittman contends the evidence also showed that Witzer was negligent in failing to file the motion to amend in a timely manner and that he did little or nothing to get a better settlement.

Based on the above points in Pittman’s declaration, he has requested this Court: (1) reduce the memorandum of costs for witness fees of $113,646.76 by an amount according to proof od reasonable witness fees; (2) reduce the memorandum of costs for prejudgment interest of $465,493.15 so as to prevent Plaintiff from obtaining “compound interest;” (3) deduct from the Jury’s verdict of $3.6 million in “past medical expenses” the incurred for medical expenses, to which there was almost no testimony to support; (4) deny Plaintiff’s request for an additional $250,000 in non-economic MICRA damages has no legal basis; and (5) increase the responsibility of Attorney Witzer to at least 50% given his admission that Attorney Pittman was not negligent.

This Court notes, as does Plaintiff in his reply brief, that Defendant Pittman’s declaration appears to be akin to that of a Motion to Tax Costs. However, pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1), a motion to strike or tax costs must be served and filed fifteen (15) days after service of the cost memorandum. Here, Plaintiff’s memorandum of cost was filed on April 23, 2024. As such, the last day to bring a motion to tax costs would have been May 8, 2024, or May 10, 2024 if electronically filed. Here the opposition was filed on May 6, 2024, so technically, if interpreted as a memorandum to tax costs, Defendant’s opposition brief would be timely pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 3.1700(b)(1).

POINTS FOR ORAL ARGUMENT AND SUPPLEMENTAL DECLARATIONS OR BRIEFING

The parties need to provide the Court with oral argument and likely supplemental briefing and supplemental declarations to address a number of issues that are only superficially addressed in the briefs currently before the Court. The issues the Court invites the parties to address more fully are listed below.

1. The Interest Calculation

On its face, and regardless of whether Defendants did or did not fail to timely file a motion to tax costs, the Court cannot accept at face value the single sentence in Mr. Melrose’s declaration and threadbare discussion in the motion to amend judgment as to how the claimed interest amount was calculated. Given that the dollar amount of the judgment is the subject of the motion to amend, it is unclear whether the Plaintiff’s proposed Cost Bill interest amount is predicated on the judgment entered by the Court or the proposed judgment after the requested amendments. It is also unclear whether the claimed interest amount is duplicated based on the two separate 998 Offers or not.

2. The Reasonableness and Good Faith of the Section 998 Offers

The Second District recently made it clear that the issues of the reasonableness and good faith of a Section 998 Offer is within the trial court’s discretion, but the record must reflect the basis for the trial court’s exercise of that discretion. (LAOSD Asbestos Cases, supra, 25 Cal.App.5th at p. 1126.) The moving papers on the motion to amend judgment only scratch the surface of this issue, which is one reason the Court believes it may need a supplemental declaration and briefing on this point.

3. The Reasonableness of the Expert Fees and Whether They Were Incurred Pre-Offer or Post-Offer

Similar to the issue of the reasonableness of the statutory settlement offers, the Court believes it needs a fuller record to exercise its discretion of the hourly rates and associated charges of the expert witnesses whose witness fees are included in the Cost Bill. The moving papers on the motion to amend judgment again only briefly touch this issue, which is challenged in the opposition declaration from Mr. Pittman. While the Court is prepared to take oral argument on this issue, it may need a supplemental declaration and briefing on this point as well. Precedents on this point indicate that the trial court must make findings as to whether that offer was reasonable, or whether the requested expert witness fees were reasonably necessary to the litigation. (LAOSD Asbestos Cases, supra, 25 Cal.App.5th at p. 1127.)

4. Handling the MICRA Issue

The Motion to Amend argues that the MICRA $250,000 amount should be added to the jury’s already considerable awards for non-economic damages. The relevant portion of MICRA, in Civil Code section 3333.2, provides: “(a) In any action for injury against a health care provider based on professional negligence, the injured plaintiff shall be entitled to recover noneconomic losses to compensate for pain, suffering, inconvenience, physical impairment, disfigurement and other nonpecuniary damage. [¶] (b) In no action shall the amount of damages for noneconomic losses exceed two hundred fifty thousand dollars ($250,000).” The jury awarded well over $250,000 for non-economic damages for each of past and future non-economic components. Expert testimony from both sides provided notice to the jury that MICRA existed and that MICRA imposed a cap, although there were no jury instructions to help the jury decide whether they should award a gross amount that the Court would then reduce on post-judgment motion, or whether they should ignore the testimony on the point and simply award the amount they collectively determined was appropriate for each of past and future components on non-economic damages. The Court invites argument and potentially supplemental briefing as to whether MICRA applies to both past and future non-economic damages, whether the $1 Million awards by the jury in the legal malpractice verdict questions for each component should be reduced to $250,000 total or $250,000 for each on past and future non-economic damages, and what authority Plaintiff relies on for the assertion that the Court should add another $250,000 to the $2 Million the jury awarded for such damages. Once again the parties’ briefs are minimalist, and not particularly helpful to the Court. The Court notes that the jury awarded the exact same $2 Million total for non-economic damages both in the underlying case special verdict question 3.b (which combined past and future) and in the malpractice verdict question 6.b and 6.c (which separated them). Do the parties agree that the question 3.b $2Million amount should have been reduced by the Court to $250,000, and if so what effect would that have on the jury’s award of the exact same total amount in the legal malpractice section of the verdict form?

5. Handling the Howell Issue

Defendants raise an argument in their Opposition Brief (rather than in a motion for new trial or JNOV) that the Court should reduce the jury’s award of past medical expenses by the negotiated rate differential reflecting a much smaller amount than the face amount of the medical bills based on in-network rates Plaintiff’s medical insurance contracted to pay to Plaintiff’s health care providers and hospital, pursuant to Howell v. Hamilton Meats & Provisions, Inc. (2011) 52 Cal.4th 541, 563. Howell held that an award of past medical expenses is limited to the lesser of (1) the amount paid or incurred and (2) the reasonable value of the services rendered. (Id. at p. 556.) In Pebley v. Santa Clara Organics, LLC (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1266, 1269, the court held that when an insured plaintiff chooses to seek medical care outside of his or her insurance plan, such a plaintiff may be considered “uninsured,” making the incurred but unpaid medical bills potentially relevant to prove past and future medical damages—so long as additional evidence, usually in the form of expert opinion testimony, is also presented on the reasonable value of the services rendered. The Opposition make no reference to any such expert testimony provided in the trial. Moreover, evidence of unpaid medical bills is relevant to prove or disprove the “reasonable value” prong of past medical damages if it can be shown the bill is actually incurred. (Qaadir v. Figueroa (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 790, 798.) The Opposition quotes to no such evidence bearing on how the Court is being requested to make the requested adjustment in the economic damages awarded.

But the Court and the jury were not provided any evidence of the negotiated rate differential, or whether the health care providers were or were not within the Plaintiff’s public or private insurance coverage as approved providers, or whether any of the bills were paid or discounted by the health care providers, or other evidence bearing on the reasonable value of the medical expenses claimed. The Court invites oral argument as to how the parties contend the Court should address this Howell issue given the record before the Court.

6. Addressing the 15% Reduction the Jury Attributed to Mr. Witzer

The jury in its answer to Special Verdict Question 9 attributed 15% responsibility to Mr. Witzer and 85% of Mr. Pittman. The parties in tehri briefing have very different takes on this, Defendant arguing that the Court should substitute its own judgment for the jury’s and reallocate the apportionment of liability differently. The Court will decline to do so for numerous reasons, not the least of which is that such a request for essentially a remittitur or JNOV should have been brought in Defendant’s own post-trial motion, not in an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion. Plaintiff’s Motion to Amend argues that the Court should also ignore the jury’s assessment of apportioned responsibility, which is essentially a comparative fault determination by the trier of fact after being instructed pursuant to CACI 3702 that the Court would calculate the actual reduction of Plaintiff’s claim by its determination of Mr. Witzer’s percentage of responsibility. The Court will take oral argument as to whether the Clerk made a clerical miscalculation of the percentage reduction found by the jury, or whether there was an error of law in applying the 15% reduction to improper elements of damages.

7. Addressing the Absence of a Motion to Tax Costs When the Opposition to This Motion to Amend Was Filed within the Time Limits for a Motion to Tax

Defendants did not file a motion to tax costs. Plaintiff’s Reply contends that the Court should exercise no discretion, simply award every penny sought in the Cost Bill without any analysis, and essentially find that Defendants have waived their right to challenge any of the claimed costs. However, the Opposition to the Motion to Amend was field within the time limits for a motion to tax costs, and the substance of the opposition brief and declaration are to question the reasonableness of several items of claimed costs. Accordingly, the Court could consider or deem the Opposition papers to be deemed a motion to tax costs. The Court requests oral argument as to whether there is any authority supporting the Court’s treatment of an opposition and declaration to a different post-judgment motion as if it were a motion to tax.


Case Number: 23TRCV01881    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 8



Tentative Ruling

HEARING DATE: May 17, 2024


CASE NUMBER: 23TRCV01881


CASE NAME: Deandre Davon Fluckers, et al. v. Extended Stay America, Inc., et al.


MOVING PARTY: (1) Defendant, Extended Stay America, Inc.

RESPONDING PARTY: (1) Plaintiff, Deandre Davon Fluckers and Renzo Ross


TRIAL DATE: Not Set.


MOTION: (1) Defendant, Extended Stay America, Inc.’s Demurrer to the 1st, 5th, 6th, 8th, and 9th causes of action

(2) Defendant, Extended Stay America, Inc.’s Motion to Strike


Tentative Rulings: (1) Defendant, Extended Stay America, Inc.’s Demurrer is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part.

(2) Defendant, Extended Stay America, Inc.’s Motion to Strike is DENIED in part and MOOTED in part.

I. BACKGROUND


A. Factual


On June 12, 2023, Plaintiffs, Deandre Davon Fluckers, Renzo Ross, and Lisa Y. Ross filed a Complaint against Defendants, Extended Stay America, Inc., Orkin, LLC, aka Orkin Pest Control, Housing Authority for the City of Los Angeles, Beach Front Property Management, Inc. aka Beach PPTY MGMT, Seth Neblett, Ati Pauta, Victor Ocampo, and DOES 1 through 100. On August 9, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). On March 13, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability; (3) Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (4) Nuisance; (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (6) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (7) Collection of Rent for Substandard Dwelling; (8) Willful Interruption of Services; and (9) Violation of Los Angeles County Code § 8.52.130, et seq.

On February 23, 2024, this Court sustained, with leave to amend, the previous demurrer to the FAC filed by Extended Stay America (“Extended Stay”).

Now, Extended Stay and Seth Neblett (“Neblett”) file a Demurrer and Motion to Strike portions of the SAC.

B. Procedural

On April 15, 2024, Defendants Extended Stay and Neblett filed a Demurrer and Motion to Strike. On May 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition brief to both motions. On May 10, 2024, Extended Stay and Neblett filed reply briefs.

II. GROUNDS FOR MOTIONS

Extended Stay and Neblett demur to Plaintiffs’ SAC on the grounds that they argue Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for negligence, fifth cause of action for Intentional infliction of emotional distress, sixth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress, eighth cause of action for willful interruption of services and ninth cause of action for violation of Los Angeles County Code section 8.52.130, et seq., fail to state sufficient facts to constitute any cause of action against the demurring defendants. causes of action fail to state any cause of action for which relief may be granted against Extended Stay.

Further, Extended Stay files a Motion to Strike portions of Plaintiffs’ SAC that discuss Plaintiffs’ allegations regarding attorneys’ fees and request for punitive damages.

III. ANALYSIS

A. Demurrer

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿¿¿


A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

Meet and Confer

Although Plaintiffs contend that their last communication with counsel for Defendants here warranted further meeting and conferring, there is nothing in the record to suggest the

parties attempted to meet and confer further prior to the filing of this motion. This Court finds that Defendants’ meet and confer efforts were sufficient.

Negligence

Extended Stay and Neblett demur to the first cause of action for Negligence fails because they argue Extended Stay cannot be vicariously liable for Orkin’s conduct. Extended Stay and Neblett maintain that Okin is an independent contractor, and that Extended Stay did not exercise any control over Orkin’s provision of pest control services at the subject property.

In this Court’s previous minute order on Extended Stay’s demurrer to the FAC, this Court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend because this Court found the FAC to be misleading and contain typos that did not strengthen Plaintiffs’ argument that Extended Stay was responsible for Orkin’s actions. As such, this Court found that Plaintiffs’ FAC was unclear as to whether Plaintiff was alleging Orkin was an employer or independent contractor.

In opposition, Plaintiffs note that if allowed to meet and confer further, Plaintiffs would have stipulated that the negligence cause of actions against Extended Stay is not based on vicarious liability from Orkin. Plaintiffs assert that they have done so by removing the typo identified by this Court in the previous tentative ruling, and also identifying a section of the cause of action to which only discusses Defendant Orkin and DOES 1 through 100. Plaintiffs contend that with respect to Extended Stay and the other non-Orkin Defendants, their liability is based on their own actions (and interactions) that caused the alleged conditions to permeate the property. The Court notes that other instances of negligent conduct, not including the pest control, have been alleged in prior pleadings as well as this one. With respect to this negligence cause of action, the Court does note that there is a delineation between the allegations against Extended Stay and Orkin.

Specifically, Plaintiff argues that Extended Stay and/or their agents owed a duty to Plaintiffs under Civil Code §§ 1940 and 1941.1 to maintain the property in a condition fit for human occupation, and to repair (SAC, ¶ 123.) However, Plaintiffs nonetheless allege that Defendants breached their duty by violating Civil Code §§ 1940, 1940.2, 1941.1, and 1942.2 when they negligently maintained the property in a condition fit for human occupancy and by failing to repair all subsequent substandard and dilapidated conditions. (SAC, ¶ 124.) The dilapidated conditions do not per se speak to Orkin or pests generally. Instead, the SAC also identifies: sewer/drain defects resulting in sewage and waste water backing up into units,; exposure to toxic mol; defective and old pipes; unreliable water supply; recurrent leaks; water-damaged walls and flooring; filthy, unsafe common areas blighted with trash and debris, feces; lack of statutorily required smoke alarms and carbon monoxide monitors within the apartment units; trespassers loitering in common areas and breaking into adjacent apartments; and structural, plumbing, and electrical hazards including, but not limited to live exposed wires coming out of the walls of common areas were tenants and children traverse. (SAC, ¶ 2.) Plaintiffs also allege causation and damages. (SAC, ¶¶ 130-132.)

When removing the portions attributed to Orkin, this Court notes that Plaintiffs have alleged a sufficient cause of action for negligence as to Extended Stay and Neblett. The Court is not persuaded by Demurring Defendants’ argument that this cause of action is duplicative with Plaintiff’s nuisance cause of action because of one similar paragraph.

Moreover, this Court also notes that Demurring Defendants may not raise an argument for the first time in their reply brief. They attempt to argue, in their reply brief, that Plaintiffs’ SAC is now a sham pleading. However, as noted by this Court, the allegations of dilapidation, other than that relating to pests, has already been found in Plaintiffs’ prior pleadings. As such, the Court ignores this argument. However, the Court does note that it may be duplicative of the Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability as this cause of action is based on Defendants’ alleged inability to provide habitable premises.

The parties may argue the issue of duplication during oral argument.

However, the Court’s tentative ruling is to OVERRULE the demurrer.

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Preliminarily, this Court notes that its February 23, 2024, minute order notes that it sustained Extended Stay’s entire demurrer, including the IIED claim, despite Plaintiffs’ contention that it overruled it. Previously, this Court noted in its last tentative ruling that Plaintiffs’ FAC failed to contain any allegations that Defendants intended to cause Plaintiffs with emotional distress. Plaintiffs have failed to amend their pleading in a way to cure the identified defects. Plaintiffs attempted to, with their FAC, to rely on reckless disregard standard versus the intention element. However, as it did not previously persuade the Court to overrule the previous demurrer, it does not do so here.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED on this issue.

Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

This Court notes, as does Demurring Defendants, that Plaintiffs were not granted leave to add new causes of action. For the reasons identified in the moving papers, Plaintiffs were granted leave to amend their FAC as pleaded. As such, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to this issue.

Willful Interruption of Services

Extended Stay and Neblett demur to the eighth cause of action for willful interruption of services.

Civil Code section 789.3 provides that “A landlord shall not with intent to terminate the occupancy under any lease … of property used by a tenant as his residence willfully cause, directly or indirectly, the interruption or termination of any utility service furnished the tenant, including, but not limited to, water, heat, light, electricity, gas, telephone, elevator, or refrigeration, whether or not the utility service is under the control of the landlord.” (CCP §

789.3(a).) To allege constructive eviction, a plaintiff must allege a disturbance of their possession by the lessor, which “has the effect of “depriving the tenant of the beneficial enjoyment of the premises, provided the tenant vacates the premises within a reasonable time.” (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank Nat'l Tr. Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 292 [citations omitted].)

Plaintiffs’ SAC alleges that during their tenancy, the elevators were rarely functioning. (SAC, ¶ 217.) The crux of the issue with Plaintiffs’ cause of action is that they are alleging the elevators were consistently shut down at Defendants’ request, and rarely serviced properly, however, nothing in the SAC suggests that this was with the intent to terminate Plaintiffs’ occupancy. As plead, it appears that all individuals on the subject premises had to endure the state of the elevators. Without this intention element, Plaintiffs may not maintain this cause of action and the demurrer is SUSTAINED.

The parties will discuss the Court’s ability to grant leave to amend on certain causes of action since Plaintiffs have already had three bites at the apple.

B. Extended Stay’s Motion to Strike

Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).)¿The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.¿ (Id., § 436.)¿ The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.¿ (Id., § 437.)¿ “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”¿ (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)

Discussion

Here, Extended Stay seeks to strike all reference to Plaintiffs’ allegations of attorneys’ fees and requests for punitive damages.

Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes punitive damages in non-contract cases “where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”

“Malice [is defined as] conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights and safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” means “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff. In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth. In ruling on a motion to strike, courts do not read allegations in isolation.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.) Something more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages. There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894.)

To plead a “willful and conscious disregard of the rights of others,” a plaintiff need only allege, “that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1211.) However, the definition of malice also requires that the conduct be despicable. “'Despicable conduct' has been described as conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. Such conduct has been described as '[having] the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.”' (American Airlines, Inc. v. Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 1017, 1050.) In cases involving conduct performed without intent to harm, a finding of malice requires proof by clear and convincing evidence that defendant's tortious wrong amounted to despicable conduct and that such despicable conduct was carried on with a ‘willful and conscious disregard’ of the rights or safety of others.” (See College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)

“A breach of a fiduciary duty alone without malice, fraud or oppression does not permit an award of punitive damages. The wrongdoer must act with the intent to vex, injure, or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights. Punitive damages are appropriate if the defendant's acts are reprehensible, fraudulent or in blatant violation of law or policy. The mere carelessness or ignorance of the defendant does not justify the imposition of punitive damages. Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff's rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.” (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.)

Here, Extended Stay argues Plaintiffs’ SAC is insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. As noted above, this Court ruled on numerous causes of action above, predominantly sustaining the demurrer. As such, the Motion to Strike punitive damages as pertaining to the sustained causes of action on demurrer above are mooted. However, as to the mention of punitive damages for the causes of action that were overruled, or not part of this demurrer (fraudulent concealment), the Court DENIES the Motion to strike.

Attorneys’ Fees

Parties cannot recover attorney’s fees unless expressly authorized by a statute or contract. (Hom v. Petrou (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 459, 464.) As for the Motion to Strike as it

relates to attorneys’ fees, the Court analysis is the same. Attorneys’ fees as they pertain to the causes of action sustained on demurrer are mooted. However, reasonable attorneys’ fees awarded by statute, like in the seventh cause of action for collection of rent on untenable dwelling, etc., or for causes of action overruled on demurrer or not subject to this demurrer are DENIED.

IV. CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Extended Stay’s Demurrer is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part. Further, Extended Stay’s Motion to Strike is DENIED in part, and MOOTED in part.

Extended Stay is ordered to give notice.
