DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV32363    Hearing Date: February 26, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Monday, February 26, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   22STCV32363

CASE NAME:                        Boyle Flats Food & Beverage, Inc., et al. v. Alex Koons  

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiffs/ Cross-Defendants Boyle Flats Food & Beverage, Inc., and Tad Yenawine

OPPOSING PARTY:             Cross-Complainant Alex Koons

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer with Motion to Strike Third Amended Cross-Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        7 February 204

REPLY:                                  20 February 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the TACC is overruled. Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike is granted as to Paragraph 9 without leave to amend and denied as to the second cause of action in the TACC.

 

Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants to give notice.

                                                                                                                                   

 

Background

 

This action arises from the formation by Plaintiff Tad Yenawine (“Yenawine”) and Defendant Alex Koons (“Koons”) of a California entity, Plaintiff Boyle Flats Food and Beverage, Inc dba Purgatory Pizza (“BFFB”). Plaintiffs and Defendant operated a restaurant business called “Purgatory Pizza.”

 

The Complaint alleges Defendant took monies from Plaintiff BFFB’s bank accounts for expenses to open his own pizza restaurant in 2020. The Complaint alleges Defendant applied for and received an Emergency Incident Disaster Loan (“EIDL”) in BFFB’s name which was transferred to Defendant’s own account.  Defendant allegedly made no payments for the EIDL afterwards. The Complaint also alleges Defendant abandoned his duties as manager of Purgatory Pizza and continued to pay himself his salary.  

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges four causes of action: (1) breach of contract; (2) conversion; (3) fraud; and (4) breach of fiduciary duty.  

 

On November 29, 2022, Defendant Koons filed a Cross-Complaint (“CC”) against Plaintiffs. On February 09, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the CC with leave to amend. On April 24, 2023, the Defendant filed an Amended Cross-Complaint but withdrew it on May 17, 2023, and was granted leave to file an amended complaint.

 

The Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SACC”) alleges four causes of action: (1) dissolution of corporation; (2) derivative claim for breach of fiduciary duty; (3) individual claim for breach of fiduciary duty against Cross-Defendant Yenawine; and (4) reimbursement of business expenses. 

 

On October 9, 2023, the court overruled Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the first, second, and third causes of action, and sustained the demurrer as to the fourth cause of action with leave to amend. Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants motion to strike was granted as to Paragraph 9 with leave to amend.

 

On October 23, 2023, Defendant/Cross-Complainant filed the operative Third Amended Cross-Complaint (“TACC”) alleging the same cause of action as the SACC.

 

Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants now demurrer to the TACC and have filed a motion to strike portions of the TACC. Defendant/Cross-Complainant Koons opposed the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

Discussion

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

A.        Demurrer 

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP, § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.”¿(C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)¿For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded.¿ (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.)¿A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.”¿(Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿ 

 

B.        Motion to Strike 

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

 

C.        Leave to Amend 

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿¿¿ 

 

II.        Demurrer

 

A.        No Meet and Confer Declaration

 

CCP §§ 430.41 and 435.5 state that a party filing a demurrer or a motion to strike “shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading[.]” Both sections 430.41 and 435.5 mandate that the moving party file and serve a declaration attesting to the moving party’s meet and confer efforts along with the demurrer and motion to strike. (CCP, § 430.41(a)(3), 435.5(a)(3).)

Here, Plaintiff has failed to meet this obligation as no declaration has been filed reflecting Plaintiff’s attempts to meet and confer efforts. The court has the discretion to continue the hearing and order the parties to meet and confer and order Plaintiff to submit a declaration. (See Dumas v. Los Angeles County Bd. of Supervisors (2020) 45 Cal. App. 5th 348, 355.) Instead, the court has decided to address Plaintiff’s demurrer and motion to strike on the merits.

 

            B.        Second Cause of Action – Derivative Breach of Fiduciary Duty          

 

Plaintiff demurs to the second cause of action on the basis that Defendant’s derivative claims fails because he did not comply with Corp. Code § 800(b)(2):

 

(b) No action may be instituted or maintained in right of any domestic or foreign corporation by any holder of shares or of voting trust certificates of the corporation unless both of the following conditions exist:

 

[ . . . ]

 

(2) The plaintiff alleges in the complaint with particularity plaintiff's efforts to secure from the board such action as plaintiff desires, or the reasons for not making such effort, and alleges further that plaintiff has either informed the corporation or the board in writing of the ultimate facts of each cause of action against each defendant or delivered to the corporation or the board a true copy of the complaint which plaintiff proposes to file.

(Corp. Code, § 800(b)(2).)

 

Plaintiffs assert that the TACC fails to allege with the requisite particularity that Defendant presented to the board of directors his request for redress of injuries and that the board refused. The TACC alleges:

 

23. On September 30, 2022, Alex, through his counsel, issued a letter to Tad, informing him, among other things, that his actions in terminating Alex and in usurping all control over Purgatory Pizza were in violation of California law and the company's corporate documents. Alex demanded that Tad (i) correct his fraudulent and false submissions to the Secretary of State records to properly reflect him as an officer and director of Purgatory Pizza; (ii) confirm that Alex is still the active General Manager of Purgatory Pizza; (iii) reverse all other unauthorized actions, including, without limitation, with respect to bank accounts, payroll, and all other company accounts; (iv) restore access to the Company premises; (v) take no further unauthorized and/or unilateral actions; and (vi) take no action to hinder or undermine Alex's efforts in performing his roles as an officer, director, and as the General Manager of Purgatory Pizza.

 

24. However, in response to this correspondence, Tad purported to file a Complaint on behalf of himself and Purgatory Pizza against Alex. As far as Alex is aware, this action was commenced at the sole behest of Tad, which Alex contends is in violation of California law and the company’s corporate documents, as described above. The Complaint alleges wrongdoing as against Alex in connection with funds that at all times were understood by the parties to be loans, which were recorded on the company's books as loans, and which are being repaid as loans from Alex to the company. Based on the trajectory of the parties' dispute leading up to this litigation, as well as the Complaint's meritless allegations, Alex believes that the Complaint was filed solely for Tad's personal purposes, to attempt to exert control over Alex while negotiating a buyout of his ownership interest.

 

25. Alex remains essentially blocked from any and all participation in the business, with Tad continuing to illegally assert himself as the sole decision maker of Purgatory Pizza.

 

(TACC ¶¶ 23-25.)

 

The court finds the allegations above are sufficient to show that Defendant complied with the requirement to present a complaint to the board before filing a derivative action under Corp. Code section 800(b)(2). The TACC alleges that Plaintiff Yenawine and Defendant Koons are equal owners of Purgatory Pizza  and officers and directors of the company until Plaintiff informed Defendant that he was the sole director of the company. (TACC ¶¶ 6, 14.) Based on these allegations, the fact that Defendant, via his counsel, informed Plaintiff about the injuries he sought to be redressed, is sufficient to show that Defendant informed the board before filing this action.

 

On reply, Plaintiff takes the new position that Plaintiff Yenawine and Plaintiff BFFB did respond to Defendant’s September 30, 2022, letter by filing this action and that the response strips Defendant of the right to pursue a derivative action. (Reply at p. 4:15-19.) Plaintiff fails to cite any legal authority to support this proposition regarding standing and fails to point any language in Corp. Code § 800 that supports such a proposition. While Plaintiff cites authority confirming that the business judgment rule creates a rebuttable presumption that the actions of the board are correct, the business judgment rule is an affirmative defense that Plaintiff must plead and prove and is not at issue in this Motion. (Desaigoudar v. Meyercord (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 173, 180.) Whether Defendant mismanaged the business and Plaintiff was entitled to strip Defendant of his responsibilities under the business judgment rule remains a disputed question of fact not subject to demurrer.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

 

            C.        Fourth Cause of Action – Reimbursement 

 

Pursuant to Lab. Code § 2802(a): “[a]n employer shall indemnify his or her employee for all necessary expenditures or losses incurred by the employee in direct consequence of the discharge of his or her duties, or of his or her obedience to the directions of the employer, even though unlawful, unless the employee, at the time of obeying the directions, believed them to be unlawful.”¿¿“The purpose of this statute is ‘to prevent employers from passing their operating expenses on to their employees.’ [Citations.]” (Cochran v. Schwan's Home Service, Inc. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1137, 1144.)

 

The TACC alleges that BFFB “had a routine business practice of authorizing and issuing business credit cards to its employees so that they are able to make purchases necessary for the business” and BFFB would reimburse its employees. (TACC ¶ 47.)

 

As general manager for BFFB, it was Defendant’s duty to purchase necessary supplies and inventory so BFFB issued to Defendant an “American Express Business Card for Boyle Flats Food and Beverage, Inc. dba Purgatory Pizza under his name so that he could make purchases for the business needs of Purgatory Pizza.” (TACC ¶ 47.) BFFB also authorized the Defendant to use the American Business Credit Card for business expenditures. (TACC ¶ 47.) With the credit card issued under his name, Defendant incurred approximately $22,461.20 in expenses that were necessary and reasonable for the business and that were authorized by BFFB due to Defendant’s role as General Manager, but Defendant was never reimbursed for these expenses. (TACC ¶ 48.)

 

Plaintiff argues that because Defendant was the general manager, the one responsible for the business expenses, any failure of reimbursement lies with Defendant. (See TACC ¶¶ 8, 11.) Even accepting that Defendant was an owner and employer, this does not absolve Plaintiffs of any responsibility for failure to pay reimbursement costs because Plaintiff is also an owner and employer and is jointly liable for any Labor Code violations. Plaintiff has failed to cite any legal authority to support the proposition that Labor Code § 2802 violation cannot be brought by a co-employer against another co-employer while the co-employer was discharging his duties. Moreover, Defendant has sufficiently alleged that the purchases were necessary for supplies and inventory. (TACC ¶¶ 47, 48.) Whether Defendant will be able to prove the facts outlined above is irrelevant to ruling on a demurrer. (See Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–61.) If Plaintiffs seek to show that they played no role in deciding who and how much to reimburse employees because the day-to-day operations are overseen by Defendant, Defendant does allege that Plaintiff Yenawine only acquiesced to a few of the reimbursements. (TACC ¶¶ 8, 11(A.) Whether this allegation is true and whether Plaintiffs will be able to prove they were not responsible for reimbursements remains a disputed issue of fact not subject to demurrer.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled.

 

III.      Motion to Strike

 

Plaintiffs move to strike the following from Defendant’s TAC:

 

·       Entire Paragraph 9 from the TACC

·       The entire second cause of action on the basis that Defendant Koons has no legal standing to bring a derivative cause of action.

 

Paragraph 9 relates to the allegation that Plaintiff Yenawine “displayed a gnome garden statue on his front porch that appeared to be hanging by a noose” and that such conduct caused Purgatory Pizza backlash with accusations of racism and decreased sales until Plaintiff Koons posted an online apology on behalf of Purgatory Pizza.  (TACC ¶ 9.) Defendant argues that Paragraph 9 is necessary to show that despite Plaintiff and Defendant being equal owners, they could not agree on the management of the affairs of BFFB, as required for a claim for involuntary dissolution under Corp. Code § 1800(b)(2).

 

Plaintiff Tad argues that the allegation in Paragraph 9 refers to conduct that occurred in Plaintiff Tad’s own home and is not one of six grounds for dissolution outlined in section 1800(b)(2). The court agrees and finds that the TACC alleges other instances to support a claim for involuntary dissolution, such as Plaintiff Tad not observing corporate formalities, not complying with accounting procedures, and failing to maintain proper books and records. (TACC ¶¶ 7, 8.) Disagreements arose with Plaintiff Tad’s attempts to exert greater control over BFFB and his alleged uses of company funds to pay for personal expenses and have his personal accountant take over the services of Purgatory Pizza. (TACC ¶ 11.)

 

Accordingly, the court agrees that Paragraph 9 adds nothing to claims in the TACC other than to seek to embarrass Plaintiff Tad for conduct that occurred in his home which allegedly caused BFFB backlash. However, such conduct is not relevant to the claims in the TACC. Accordingly, the court grants the motion to strike Paragraph 9 without leave to amend.

 

Plaintiffs insist that they can strike an entire cause of action.  This is improper and would defeat the purpose of filing a demurrer and give parties two bites at the apple. Plaintiffs attempt to do that here by arguing that the Defendant lacks standing to bring the second cause of action because he did not present his claim to the board as required by Corp. Code § 800(b)(2).

 

“The motion to strike is widely used to challenge portions of causes of action seeking punitive damages.” (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1683.) “Although it is ‘improper for a court to strike a whole cause of action of a pleading under [section 436],’ [citation], or to eliminate a ‘matter which is essential to a cause of action,’ [citation], it may strike anything else it determines to be irrelevant.” (Los Altos El Granada Investors v. City of Capitola (9th Cir. 2009) 583 F.3d 674, 687; see also Ferraro v. Camarlinghi (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 529 [objection that complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action is ground for a general demurrer, not a motion to strike].)

Because it would be improper for the court to strike an entire cause of action, especially because Plaintiff has already demurred to that cause of action, the motion to strike the second cause of action is denied.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the TACC is overruled. Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike is granted as to Paragraph 9 without leave to amend and denied as to the second cause of action in the TACC.
