[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTIONS TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 24, 2023

 

Scott E. Gizer, counsel of record for Defendants JewnJabi Inc., Michael Farah, and Mandeep Kaur Saggi, seeks to be relieved as counsel.  Counsel’s declaration states that the relationship has broken down and it is unreasonably difficult for him to carry out his representation effectively.  There has also been an irreconcilable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship and Defendants have failed to pay counsel’s fees.  No party opposed the motion.

Absent a showing of resulting prejudice, an attorney’s request for withdrawal should be granted.  (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406.)  Counsel’s motions comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.  This case was dismissed pursuant to a settlement, and no prejudice is shown.

The proposed orders do not include Defendants’ current or last known phone numbers.  Counsel must provide this information at the hearing.

Subject to the provision of Defendants’ phone numbers, the unopposed motions to be relieved are GRANTED and effective upon filing a proof of service showing service of the signed Form MC-053 orders and this order on JewnJabi Inc., Michael Farah, Mandeep Kaur Saggi, and all parties who have appeared.

“[A] corporation cannot represent itself in a court of record either in propria persona or through an officer or agent who is not an attorney.”  (Caressa Camille, Inc. v. Alcoholic Beverage Control Appeals Bd. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101.)  JewnJabi Inc. must retain new counsel before any further appearances.

Dept. 48

Kevin Yang, counsel of record for Defendant Long Spring Freight LLC, seeks to be relieved as counsel.  Counsel’s declaration states that he is unable to continue representing Long Spring because client communication is no longer possible after the death of Defendant Grace Hui, the manager and sole client contact for Long Spring.  Grace Hui’s brother told counsel that Long Spring is no longer operational, and no one is authorized to make litigation decisions on its behalf.

Plaintiff Leo Cordova opposed the motion.  Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections are overruled.  Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is granted.

Plaintiff argues that there is an alternative managing member for Defendant Long Spring.  According to Long Spring’s Statement of Information filed on October 5, 2022, Jerry Wang is a manager and Chief Executive Officer.  (RJN, Ex. A.)

Jerry Wang provided a declaration to Plaintiff’s counsel.  (Opposition, Bressler Decl., Ex. 1 [“Wang Decl.”].)  He declares that before Grace Hui’s death, she asked him to assist her in winding down the operations of Long Spring.  (Wang Decl. ¶ 3.)  Jerry Wang was not aware of any appointment as an officer.  (Wang Decl. ¶ 3.)  Other than assisting Grace Hui with winding down Long Spring, he does not have any knowledge of Long Spring’s past business activities, any documents relating to Plaintiff, or any personal knowledge about Plaintiff’s allegations.  (Wang Decl. ¶ 3.)

Despite this, Jerry Wang is indeed named as a manager of Long Spring in its most recent Statement of Information.  The Court takes judicial notice of the fact that Long Spring is currently listed as Active and in good standing with the Secretary of State.  Long Spring has not been formally dissolved, pending instructions from Grace Hui’s estate.  (Wang Decl. ¶ 4.)  Accordingly, because Long Spring remains active and Jerry Wang is an alternative client contact, the motion to be relieved as counsel is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

If counsel files a future motion to withdraw on a different basis, Long Spring is cautioned that “a corporation cannot represent itself in a court of record either in propria persona or through an officer or agent who is not an attorney.”  (Caressa Camille, Inc. v. Alcoholic Beverage Control Appeals Bd. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101.)

Plaintiff also argues that if the motion is granted, he will be unduly prejudiced by a continuance of the December 11, 2023 trial date because he is “prepared and ready to present his case at trial.”  Although the Court is denying the motion, the trial date still needs to be continued.  This case is not yet at issue.  Defendant A&D Hauling Services has been served, but it has not yet appeared in this action, and Plaintiff has not taken its default.  Plaintiff must also dismiss Grace Hui or seek to substitute her personal representative or successor-in-interest.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND COMPLAINT

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

October 24, 2023

 

On June 14, 2022, Plaintiff Timothy Philips filed this action against Defendant Seizmic, Inc.  Plaintiff has since filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) and a second amended complaint (“SAC”).  On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Amendment to Complaint (Fictitious/Incorrect Name), naming Sal Fateen as Doe 1.

On July 27, 2023, Fateen filed a motion to quash service of summons and complaint.

A.        Plaintiff Properly Named Fateen Under Section 474.

Code of Civil Procedure section 474 permits a plaintiff who is ignorant of the name of a defendant to name a Doe in a complaint, and “when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 474.)  To do so, the plaintiff must have actually been ignorant of the defendant’s identity when he or she filed the complaint.  (Optical Surplus, Inc. v. Superior Court (1991) 228 Cal. App. 3d 776, 783-782.)  “Ignorance of the true name of the defendant should not be feigned.”  (Scherer v. Mark (1976) 64 Cal. App. 3d 834, 840.)  “[T]he trial court is vested with broad discretion when presented with a motion for leave to file an amended pleading and may consider such factors as whether the proffered pleading states facts which could have been pleaded earlier, and whether a party has established a reasonable excuse for the delay.”  (A.N. v. County of Los Angeles (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1067.)  Courts should liberally grant leave to amend where no prejudice is shown.  (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.)

Fateen argues that Plaintiff did not comply with section 474 because he was not ignorant of Fateen’s identity and relationship.  (Motion at p. 4.)  Fateen notes that Plaintiff previously included allegations about him being in the room when Plaintiff made his safety violations complaint.  (Complaint ¶ 27; FAC ¶ 24; SAC ¶ 24.)

“Code of Civil Procedure section 474 states that it applies when ‘the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant.’  That phrase has not been interpreted literally.  Even though a plaintiff knows the true name of a person he is still ‘ignorant of his name’ if he lacks knowledge of facts giving him a cause of action against that person.”  (Breceda v. Gamsby (1968) 267 Cal.App.2d 167, 174.)  “The distinction between a suspicion that some cause could exist and a factual basis to believe a cause exists is critical in the operation of section 474.  The former is one reason attorneys include general charging allegations against fictitiously named defendants; the latter requires substitution of the defendant’s true name.  [The defendant’s] present urging that its ostensible liability was always obvious to plaintiff confuses these two standards of knowledge and is based upon hindsight.”  (Dieckmann v. Superior Court (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 345, 363.)

As Plaintiff asserts, his “retaliation claim arises out of the termination that resulted from his complaints, not the mere communication of the complaints themselves.”  (Opposition at p. 5.)  On February 13, 2023, Defendant Seizmic responded to Plaintiff’s interrogatories.  (Mah Decl. ¶ 3.)  In response to Special Interrogatory 31, which asked Seizmic to “IDENTIFY the person(s) most knowledgeable at SEIZMIC about TERMINATION of PLAINTIFF’S employment with YOU,” Seizmic stated “Sal Fateen.”  (Mah Decl. ¶¶ 3, 5 & Ex. B.)  There is nothing to suggest that Plaintiff knew of Fateen’s involvement in his termination before the interrogatory responses.

Because Fateen has not shown an improper amendment under section 474 and has not shown prejudice, the motion is denied on this ground.

B.        The Service of Summons is Defective.

Fateen argues that the summons did not include the required information.  A summons served on a newly named Doe defendant must include a notice stating in substance: “To the person served: You are hereby served in the within action (or proceedings) as (or on behalf of) the person sued under the fictitious name of (designating it).”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 474.)

On January 28, 2023, Fateen’s counsel received a summons with the SAC and a Form LASC LACIV 105 Amendment to Complaint.  (Green Decl. ¶ 3.)  At the bottom of the summons, no box is checked following “NOTICE TO THE PERSON SERVED: You are served.”  (Green Decl., Ex. 1.)  Because the summons did not indicate that Fateen was being sued under the fictitious name of Doe 1, it does not comply with section 474.

Plaintiff acknowledges that “boxes on the summons were inadvertently left unchecked,” but he argues that “[o]nly substantial—and not perfect—compliance is necessary.”  (Opposition at pp. 6-7.)  Plaintiff’s cited authority does not support his argument in the context of section 474 and facts similar to this case.  Bein v. Brechtel-Jochim Group, Inc. (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1387 considered whether substituted service under section 415.20 was effective service under the circumstances.  The court in Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1436-1439, found no substantial compliance with sections 415.40 or 416.10.  In Cory v. Crocker National Bank (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 665, 669, the “fundamental issue on appeal [was] whether the summons served upon respondent was valid and in compliance with section 412.30.”

A finding of substantial compliance requires three things: (1) some degree of compliance with the offended statutory requirements; (2) a high probability that the objective nature and circumstances of the attempted service imparted the same notice as full compliance; and (3) actual sufficient notice to put the defendant on his defense.  (Carol Gilbert, Inc. v. Haller (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 852, 865-866 (Carol Gilbert, Inc.).)

Here, there was no compliance with the statutory requirements of section 474.  The complete failure to indicate on the summons that Fateen was being sued under a fictitious name forecloses a finding of substantial compliance.  (See MJS Enterprises, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 555, 558 [a “complete failure to comply” with statutory requirements is not substantial compliance]; Mannesmann DeMag, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 1118, 1123 [“not a case of substantial compliance but one of no compliance at all”].)  Service of a copy of the amendment identifying Fateen as Doe 1 is not a substitute for statutory compliance.  (Carol Gilbert, Inc., supra, 179 Cal.App.4th at p. 859.)

The motion is granted on this ground.

C.        Conclusion

The motion to quash is GRANTED due to defective service of summons.
