DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01656    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Jan Weissman et al,

 

 

           Plaintiffs,

 

   v.

Craig Vandagriff Construction et al.

 

          Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

23VECV01656                    107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Plaintiffs Jan Weissman and Jan Weissman as Trustee of the Jan Weissman Revocable Trust Dated July 17, 2022, move for leave to amend their complaint. This court grants Plaintiffs’ motion.

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On April 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Jan Weissman, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jan Weissman Revocable Trust Dated July 17, 2002 (“Plaintiffs”), filed a complaint alleging breach of written contract, breach of implied warranty, breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, negligence, financial elder abuse, and declaratory relief against Defendants Craig Vandagriff Construction, Inc., Craig Vandagriff, and Jody Vandagriff (collectively, “Defendants”), Brytin Construction, AC Interiors, Inc., Vargas Custom Plaster, Inc., American Plumbing Professionals, and Does 1 to 50.

 

On August 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their first motion for leave to amend the complaint. On August 16, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition. On August 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their reply.

 

On August 29, 2024, this court denied without prejudice Plaintiffs’ first motion for leave to amend the complaint because Plaintiffs did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324. (08/29/24 Minute Order.) Specifically, Plaintiffs failed to “include the date of discovery and reasons for any delay besides ‘inadvertence.’” (08/29/24 Minute Order p. 3.)

 

On September 10, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their amended motion for leave to amend the complaint (the “Motion”). On October 2, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition. On October 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their reply.

 

II.             PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for any delay. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324 (a), (b).)

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion includes a copy of the proposed amended complaint. (Ex. 1.) The Motion also specifies the proposed changes to the complaint. (Motion pp. 8-9.) Further, Plaintiffs’ counsel’s declaration specifies the effect of the amendments, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for any delay. (Uray Declaration ¶¶ 5, 8-10, 17.)  Accordingly, Plaintiffs complied with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) and section 576. The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) “Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . .. [citation]. A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

 

“Time and knowledge are important factors to be considered when granting or denying a¿motion¿to¿amend.” (Stockton v. Ortiz (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 183, 194.) “If the¿motion¿to¿amend¿is timely made and the granting of the¿motion¿will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to¿amend¿and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court of Cal. In and For Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION 

 

Plaintiffs request to amend their complaint as they are entitled to treble damages that they inadvertently left out of the complaint. (Motion p. 4; Uyeno Declaration ¶ 5.) Plaintiffs argue they will suffer prejudice if the Motion is not granted because they would lose the right to seek damages they are entitled to under Civil Code section 3345. (Uyeno Declaration ¶ 17.) Plaintiffs state that minimal discovery has been done, the case is not at issue, trial has not been set, there is currently no discovery cut-off date, and they can conduct discovery on Plaintiffs’ treble damages claim. (Uyeno Declaration ¶¶ 14-16.)

 

In support of their Motion, Plaintiffs submit a declaration by their counsel. (Uyeno Declaration.) Plaintiffs’ counsel’s declaration states that in October 2023, Plaintiffs propounded discovery on Defendants. (Uyeno Declaration ¶ 6.) Defendants served their responses in January 2024. (Uyeno Declaration ¶ 7.) In March 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel conducted research on Plaintiffs’ Financial Elder Abuse claim and discovered Plaintiffs had a right to seek treble damages. (Uyeno Declaration ¶¶ 8, 10.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states that he met and conferred with Defendants’ counsel about the issue in April 2024. (Uyeno Declaration ¶ 12.) When the parties reached no informal resolution, Plaintiffs’ counsel raised the issue with this court at a Trial Setting Conference on June 24, 2024. (Uyeno Declaration ¶ 12.) Plaintiffs subsequently filed the Motion.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue there is no reasonable excuse for Plaintiffs’ delay because Plaintiffs knew or should have known about the facts necessary to plead damages under Civil Code section 3345 at the time they filed the complaint. (Opposition p. 6.) Defendants further argue that Plaintiffs’ original complaint shows Plaintiffs knew about the alleged facts that would apply to Civil Code section 3345 and Plaintiffs’ proposed first amended complaint repeats facts plead in the original complaint. (Opposition pp. 6-7.) Finally, Defendants argue that they will suffer prejudice if Plaintiffs are permitted to amend their complaint because a substantial amount of time has passed since Plaintiffs filed the complaint, the case has been at issue since July 10, 2024, and extensive written discovery has been performed to date. (Opposition pp. 7-8.)

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue Plaintiffs submitted a declaration showing “[w]hen the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered” and Defendants have not shown they would be prejudiced by the amendment. (Reply p. 2.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs knew or should have known about the facts necessary to seek treble damages when they filed the complaint.  But Plaintiffs have identified why the claim for treble damages was not included in the original complaint. (Uray Declaration ¶¶ 8, 10.)

 

Further, “it is an abuse of discretion to deny leave to amend where the opposing party [is] not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048.) This court has broad discretion to liberally grant leave to amend so long as such amendment does not prejudice the other party.  Although this case was filed more than a year ago, no trial date has been set. Further, the parties have propounded and responded to some written discovery, but there is no discovery cut-off date set. (Uyeno Declaration ¶ 16.)  Accordingly, this court finds that the amendment will not prejudice Defendants.

 

V.              CONCLUSION

¿ 

Accordingly, this court grants the Motion with ten days to amend.
