DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

REQUESTING ORAL ARGUMENT PER CRC 3.1308

The Court will attempt to post all Tentative Rulings at least the day prior to the hearing by 3:00 p.m.; however, the Court does not post Tentative Rulings for all matters.  

The Court will indicate in the Tentative Ruling whether the Court is requesting oral argument.  For cases where the Court is not requesting argument, then the Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) where the Court requests notice of intent to appear.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, a party seeking argument should notify "all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue."  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is requested.   

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Notice of the ruling must be served as indicated in the tentative.  Remote appearances are permitted for all law and motion unless otherwise indicated by the Court.  

 

Case Number: 23BBCV02029    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: A

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH CENTRAL DISTRICT - BURBANK

DEPARTMENT A

 

TENTATIVE RULING

SEPTEMBER 17, 2024

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23BBCV02029

 

MP:  

Ivanlouis Nicholson (Plaintiff)

RP:  

Ford Motor Company (Defendant)

 

NOTICE:

 

The Court is not requesting oral argument on this matter.  The Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) whereby notice of intent to appear is requested.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, no argument is requested and any party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.  

 

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

 

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Plaintiff Ivanlouis Nicholson (Plaintiff) brings this action against Ford Motor Company (Ford) for Ford’s alleged violation of the Song Beverly Act. Plaintiff alleges that his 2022 Ford Escape VIN 1FMCU0BZXNUA03285 (the “Subject Vehicle”) was manufactured as defective.

 

Plaintiff now moves for an order compelling Ford’s further response to Plaintiff’s RFPD as follows:

 

RFPD Nos. 1, 3, 8, 10, and 13 concerning the Subject Vehicle.

 

RFPD Nos. 15, 16, 19, 20, 27, 28, 28, 47, 48, 49, 50, 59, 60, 65 and 66 requesting documents related to Ford’s Internal Knowledge and Investigation of an engine defect.

 

RFPD Nos. 71, 72, 75, 76 and 77 requesting Ford’s internal documents concerning the engine defect.

 

RFPD Nos. 86, 87, 88, 90, 99, 100, 103, 106, 107, and 108 requesting documents related to Ford’s warranty and vehicle repurchase policies.

 

RFPD Nos. 113, 114, 115, 116, 117, 118, 119, 120, 121, 122, 123 and 124 requesting documents related to Ford’s communications with Governmental Agencies and Suppliers as concerns the engine defect in 2019 Ford Expedition vehicles.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS:

 

Plaintiff requests the Court strike the declaration of Eric Kemnitz in its entirety. Plaintiff argues that Ford impermissibly attempts to enter the declaration while maintaining objections of attorney-client privilege. Plaintiff asks that in the alternative, the attorney client privilege be waived, and Kemnitz be subject to an Evidentiary Hearing in which he may be cross-examined. The Court finds the content of these declarations to have no bearing on its consideration of this motion. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s objection is OVERRULED.

 

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFPD”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See C.C.P. § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) 

 

II.                 MERITS

 

A motion to compel further responses to a request for production must include a meet and confer declaration consistent with C.C.P. § 2016.040. (C.C.P. § 2031.310 (b)(2).) In turn, Section 2016.040 requires that the moving party have made “a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (C.C.P. § 2016.040.)

 

“A single letter, followed by a response which refuses concessions, might in some instances be an adequate attempt at informal resolution, especially when a legitimate discovery objective is demonstrated. The time available before the motion filing deadline, and the extent to which the responding party was complicit in the lapse of available time, can also be relevant. An evaluation of whether, from the perspective of a reasonable person in the position of the discovering party, additional effort appeared likely to bear fruit, should also be considered” (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 432.)

Upon review of the parties submissions, the Court finds the meet and confer efforts of Plaintiff were insufficient. The Court may deny a motion to compel discovery for lack of a reasonable and good faith attempt to meet and confer. (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1436-1439; Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434-435.)

 

As evidence of their meet and confer efforts, Plaintiff submits a May 9, 2024 meet and confer letter sent to Ford. (Smith Decl. Exh. 12.) This meet and confer letter is 36 pages long and appears to be devoid of any specific reference to Ford’s discovery responses. (Id.) Rather than identify the individual requests which require further response, Plaintiff’s letter simply identifies types of information which are typically in dispute in Song Beverly Cases. (Id.) Nor does this letter contain any reference to Ford’s responses/objections, instead it appears to address a number of usual objections regardless of whether Ford asserted them. For example, Section D(1)(a) of Plaintiff’s meet and confer letter addresses Ford’s allegedly improper objection on grounds that Plaintiff’s vehicle did not suffer the claimed defects. Upon review of each of Ford’s responses their appears to be no such objection. Ford’s responses to RFPD Nos. 28-30 merely state:

 

Claims and reports sometimes do not clearly articulate the basis for the complaint or clearly identify any alleged defect or other issue with the vehicle, and the facts and circumstances surrounding other claims and reports vary widely depending on any number of factors, such as owner maintenance, use or environmental conditions

 

(Bieman Exh. B, p. 38.)

 

The above response may be improper on another basis, but it is not the equivalent to Ford refusing to produce documents because they claim Plaintiff’s vehicle does not suffer from an engine or transmission defect. Further, it appears that the nature of the defect in Plaintiff’s vehicle is something that Ford unsuccessfully sought clarification on in the meet and confer process. It appears from June 25, 2024 email between counsel that Ford sought to telephonically meet and confer as to the nature of the engine/transmission defect so that they could provide the appropriate documents. (Biemann Decl. Exh. E.) Ford contends that Plaintiff refused this request and Plaintiff does not dispute this statement in their reply. (Id.)

 

Additionally, it appears that Ford has produced documents which may be responsive to some of the RFPD Plaintiff seeks to further compel. Although Plaintiff’s declaration in reply concedes that Ford has produced documents, it goes on to state that Ford has failed to produce all documents with respect to various categories. The Court notes that neither party explains whether this production was prior to the filing of this motion or after. To the extent that Ford’s production predated the filing of this motion, Plaintiff’s motion makes no reference to what production has occurred. For example, Plaintiff’s motion seeks further responses related to defects in the Subject Vehicle (RFPD Nos. 1, 3,8,10, and 13.) In response to RFPD No. 8 Ford replied as follows:

 

Ford will comply with this Request, in whole. Ford will produce repair and service-related information for the subject vehicle from Ford’s Global System for Analytics and Reporting (“GSAR”) regarding repairs or services reimbursed by Ford to independent Ford dealers pursuant to Ford-issued warranties, service contracts, and Field Service Actions.

 

(Biemann Decl. Exh. B, p. 4.)

 

Despite the fact that Ford apparently produced these documents, Plaintiff presents no reason as to why further production is required to RFDP No. 8. The same can be said of most of the RFPD to which Ford produced documents.

 

Given the foregoing, the Court does not construe the March 9, 2024 and June 10, 2024 letters to be evidence of a good faith effort to meet and confer. While Ford’s objections may be without merit, Plaintiff’s informal efforts did little to specifically address these concerns. Instead, Plaintiff’s counsel tendered a lengthy letter which could have applied just as easily to any Song-Beverly discovery dispute. This may be a good first step toward addressing discovery issues, but it is far from a substantive effort to meet and confer. Plaintiff’s counsel ignoring Ford’s request to meet and confer telephonically also supports this interpretation. The Court simply does not view three letters in which the parties talk past one another to be a good faith attempt at informal resolution.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further production is DENIED without prejudice and the parties are ordered to meet and confer in good faith as mandated by C.C.P. §2023.010(i).  It appears to the Court that Ford is willing to produce further documentation at this juncture which would address at least some of the requests in Plaintiff’s motion.

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Georgina Gomez (Plaintiff) brings this action against Kia America, Inc. (Kia) for Kia’s alleged violation of the Song Beverly Act. Plaintiff alleges that her 2020 Kia Sportage (the Subject Vehicle) was manufactured as defective.

 

Plaintiff now moves for an order compelling Kia’s further response to Request for the Production of Documents (RFPD) as follows:

 

RFPD Nos. 15-22 requesting documents related to Kia’s policies and procedures for handling Song-Beverly cases.

 

RFPD Nos. 23-29 requesting documents related to Kia’s warranty policy and procedure for handling warranty issues.

 

RFPD Nos. 30-31 requesting documents of customer complaints for vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the Subject Vehicle.  

 

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFPD”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See C.C.P. § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) 

 

II.                 MERITS

 

A motion to compel further responses to a request for production must include a meet and confer declaration consistent with C.C.P. § 2016.040. (C.C.P. § 2031.310 (b)(2).) In turn, Section 2016.040 requires that the moving party have made “a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (C.C.P. § 2016.040.)

 

“A single letter, followed by a response which refuses concessions, might in some instances be an adequate attempt at informal resolution, especially when a legitimate discovery objective is demonstrated. The time available before the motion filing deadline, and the extent to which the responding party was complicit in the lapse of available time, can also be relevant. An evaluation of whether, from the perspective of a reasonable person in the position of the discovering party, additional effort appeared likely to bear fruit, should also be considered” (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 432.)

 

Upon review of the parties submissions, the Court finds the meet and confer efforts of Plaintiff were insufficient. The Court may deny a motion to compel discovery for lack of a reasonable and good faith attempt to meet and confer. (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1436-1439; Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434-435.)

 

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff propounded her RFPD upon Kia. (Jung Decl. ¶ 18, Exh. 3.) On May 14, 2024, Kia rendered its responses to the RFPD. (Jung Decl. ¶ 19, Exh. 4.) What proceeded was a series of meet and confer correspondences which appear to have been prematurely interrupted by Plaintiff’s filing of this motion.

 

On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Kia a meet and confer letter detailing the various perceived deficiencies in Kia’s responses. (Jung Decl. Exh. 6) This letter specifically requested Kia respond by June 24, 2024. (Id.) On July 8, 2024, Kia responded to this letter stating that it would supplement its responses to RFPD Nos. 1, 21, 22, 26-28, and 30 and produce more documents, pursuant to Plaintiff’s stipulation to a protective order. (Haroutunian Decl. ¶ 7, Exh. B.) Kia represents that it had received an extension to reply to the June 17, 2024 letter, thus her July 8 response would have still been timely. (Hartounian Decl. ¶ 7.)

 

On August 12, 2024, Kia served its further responses and supplemental production pursuant to the stipulated protective order. (Hartounian Decl. ¶ 8.) This was in accordance with the July 8, 2024 letter which stated that further production would occur by August 12, 2024. (Hartounian Decl. Exh. 7.) The letter also agreed to extend Plaintiff’ deadline to file this motion to August 26, 2024. (Id.)

 

There is no evidence of any response by Plaintiff’s counsel to Kia’s July 8, 2024 letter.  Instead, this motion to compel further responses was filed on July 16, 2024. Plaintiff’s reply makes no reference to the July 8 letter or the August 12 production. Given this omission, the Court can only assume that the letter was received. If this is the case, Plaintiff’s filing of this motion seems to be without evidence of a good faith meet and confer effort. It was clear that Kia was willing to work with Plaintiff’s counsel to address concerns regarding discovery and to produce supplemental documents. Despite Kia’s good faith offer to work toward informal resolution, Plaintiff elected to abandon the meet and confer process and file the instant motion.

 

Accordingly, the Court orders denies the motion to compel without prejudice, and the parties are ordered to meet and confer in good faith as mandated by C.C.P. §2023.010(i).  It appears to the Court that Kia is willing to produce further documentation at this juncture which would address several of the requests in Plaintiff’s motion.
