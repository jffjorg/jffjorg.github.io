DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV00591    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Jane Doe v. Harvey Weinstein, Case No. 23SMCV00591

Hearing date June 27, 2024

Plaintiff Jane Doe’s Motion to Stay Discovery Pending Resolution of Parallel Criminal Proceedings and Motion for Protective Order

 

On December 19, 2022 defendant Weinstein was convicted of raping plaintiff Jane Doe, who sues based on that assault. Plaintiff seeks a stay of discovery pending resolution of the criminal appeal. Plaintiff also seeks a motion for a protective order.

At the hearing on April 25, 2024 the parties discussed that the New York criminal conviction had been overturned, but the Los Angeles criminal appeal remained pending. Plaintiff’s motion to stay was scheduled to be heard that day, but after oral argument, the court continued the April 25 hearings, including the motion to stay, to June 27, 2024. The court temporarily stayed discovery until the continued hearing date and requested supplemental briefs.

Motion to Stay Proceedings

“The decision whether to stay civil proceedings in the face of a parallel criminal proceeding should be made ‘in light of the particular circumstances and competing interests involved in the case.’ [Citation.] This means the decisionmaker should consider ‘the extent to which the defendant's fifth amendment rights are implicated.’ [Citation.] In addition, the decisionmaker should generally consider the following factors: (1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation.” Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885, quoting Keating v. Office of Thrift Supervision (9th Cir. 1995) 45 F.3d 322, 324.

Doe argues the motion should be granted because a stay pending resolution of the criminal proceedings will promote judicial efficiency and will not burden either party. Plaintiff argues she would be prejudiced if the court denies the stay because she would seek to use the criminal conviction to establish liability. Further, if a civil jury finds Weinstein not liable and the convictions are later affirmed, it would be prejudicial to Doe.

Plaintiff contends prior to her stating her intent to seek a stay of discovery, defendant neither noticed any depositions nor propounded written discovery; defendant only did so after plaintiff expressed her intent to seek a stay. Shukry Decl., ¶ 3. Doe refutes the defense argument that she failed to timely respond to discovery and thus waived all objections. Doe timely served objection-only responses and filed a motion for protective order based on the pending request for stay. Shukry Decl., ¶ 6. Doe argues that without a stay, she will be subjected to harassment by Weinstein and counsel for coming forward as a victim, including being subjected to attempts to discover her sexual history in violation of CCP §2017.220, as well as claims Doe fabricated the assault and was with another man the night of the rape. Doe asserts Weinstein failed to demonstrate he would be burdened by the stay. The court does note that, even if discovery proceeds, plaintiff may bring motions to quash/protective orders if she believes defendant seeks discovery that improperly requests sexual history or other privileged information.

Weinstein argues a stay is unjustified because (1) the law does not permit a plaintiff to bring a lawsuit and then seek to stay it for strategic reasons; and (2) discovery on all issues (liability and damages) will have to be conducted regardless of whether the California criminal convictions are affirmed. Thus, a stay will unnecessarily delay proceedings that will need to occur regardless. Moreover, defendant argues his conviction is likely to be overturned, as there are numerous issues on appeal.

Defendant argues the appeal is unlikely to be resolved soon, and, even if the convictions are affirmed, collateral estoppel will not preclude him from defending the case and conducting discovery. Defendant asserts claim preclusion is inapplicable when the criminal and civil case do not involve the same parties or cause of action. DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber (2015) 61 Cal.4th 813, 824. See also Samara v. Matar (2018) 5 Cal. 5th 322, 326-27; Mills v. Facility Solutions Group, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal. App. 5th 1035, 1048.

On April 25, 2024 this court stayed discovery until the motion for protective order hearing on June 27, 2024. Plaintiff argues the outcome on appeal determines her ability to invoke issue preclusion and/or res judicata. Plaintiff also argued neither party would be burdened because neither had taken depositions, and the matter was in the early stages of discovery. Shukry, ¶¶ 2,7.

Now discovery has been propounded. There is no assurance of a timely resolution of the appellate and criminal proceedings. Although plaintiff contends potential prejudice argues in favor of a stay, the Avant factors must be weighed. As defendant seeks discovery to defend himself in a suit plaintiff elected to bring, there is risk of prejudice to defendant in granting an indefinite stay. Plaintiff does not demonstrate judicial economy is served by an indefinite stay.

The Trial Court Delay Reduction Act (Gov. Code §68600, et seq.) governs the way cases move toward trial: lawyers and clients do not control the pace of litigation (e.g., with open-ended extensions of time for answers, continuing hearing dates, etc.). Rather, it is the court's responsibility to “actively manage” each case and “to compel attorneys and litigants to prepare and resolve all litigation without delay.” [Gov. Code §68607]. Judges must engage in “active management and supervision of the pace of litigation from the date of filing to disposition.” [CRC 3.713(c)]. The Avant factors, on balance, do not weigh in favor of a stay. DENIED.

Motion to for Protective Order

Doe requests a stay until the motion for protective order is heard and requests an additional 10 days to respond to discovery if the motion to stay is denied.

Any issue decided in a prior criminal proceeding is conclusively determined as to the parties in a subsequent civil action. Teitelbaum Furs, Inc. v. Dominion Ins. Co., Ltd. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 601. A felony conviction is exempt from the hearsay rule in a civil action, even if the conviction is under appeal. Principal Life Ins. Co. v. Peterson (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 676. A judgment is not final for purposes of collateral estoppel while it is under appeal. Abelson v. National Union Vire Ins. Co. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 776, 787-788.

Defendant Weinstein’s discovery and deposition requests to plaintiff are the subject of Doe’s motion for stay of discovery. Doe argues that ordering discovery is against the public interest and does not promote judicial efficiency. Doe contends denial of the protective order would cause prejudice because she argues Weinstein seeks to discover private information unavailable to him under criminal discovery rules and then impermissibly use such information in the criminal appeal.

Doe cites Pacers, arguing a party to a parallel criminal proceeding should not be able to obtain, through the civil proceedings, information to which it was not entitled under criminal discovery rules. Pacers Inc v. Superior Court (1984) 162 Cal. App. 3d 686, 690. Doe brought this suit prior to resolution of the criminal proceedings. As such, it is not proper for the court to prohibit discovery until the criminal conviction is no longer subject to appeal. Doe cannot bring suit, then seek to prohibit discovery based upon a criminal appeal which is not certain to timely resolve. In accordance with judicial economy and the ends of justice, timely resolution is paramount. The timeline of the criminal appeal cannot dictate the timeline of this civil action. Doe does not cite authority that a civil defendant is estopped from disputing liability while a prior criminal conviction arising out of the same facts is on appeal.

Pacers involved petitioners who asserted their Fifth Amendment rights at depositions in a civil action. “Petitioners here are civil defendants facing possible criminal prosecution involving the same facts as the civil action . . . Accordingly, they had no obligation to disclose to real parties’ information they reasonably believed might be used against them in a criminal proceeding.” Pacers, supra, at 688-89.

Doe is not asserting a Fifth Amendment privilege; he is facing criminal prosecution, yet is amenable to deposition. Plaintiff must also be. Weinstein issued written discovery to plaintiff on January 22, 2024. Doe requested additional time to answer, which defense counsel granted. Plaintiff has yet to respond, and is ordered to respond within 10 days. DENIED.

Case Number: 24SMCV00436    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Mahfoud v. Letizia, et al., Case No. 24SMCV00436 

Hearing Date June 27, 2024

Defendant Wonder Nights, LLC’s Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment

 

Plaintiff, in pro per, sued defendants Letizia and Wonder Nights arising out of a claim that plaintiff loaned defendants money for a business, which was not repaid, and plaintiff was locked out of the business.

 

On April 9, 2024 default was entered against Wonder Nights, which moves to set aside based on counsel’s inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect. Defense counsel stated he did not inquire into whether his client was properly served and took his time to prepare the answer on the assumption that his client was still waiting for proper service. Morris Decl. ¶¶4, 6.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) provides the statutory framework for setting aside a default, including filing a proposed answer. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(b) requires the first page of each paper to specify immediately below the case number “(1) The date, time, and location, if ascertainable, of any scheduled hearing and the name of the hearing judge, if ascertainable[.]”

 

Plaintiff argues the motion fails to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b)’s requirement that a copy of the proposed answer be filed, and Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(b) requirement that a motion provide the hearing date information.

 

No reply was filed. The court has no basis to find that plaintiff was properly served, and the moving papers fail to include information regarding the date, time, location and hearing officer.

 

Failure to serve notice with the hearing date information renders notice defective. Code Civ. Proc. §1010 [“Notices must be in writing, and the notice of a motion, other than for a new trial, must state when and the grounds upon which it will be made, and the papers, if any, upon which it is to be based.”; Bohn v. Bohn (1913) 164 Cal. 532, 536-537 [failure to include notice of date and time of hearing in notice of motion renders the notice fatally defective]; Galleria Plus, Inc. v. Hanmi Bank (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 535, 538 [addressing notice of motion for sanctions]; Cal. Rules of Court 3.1110(b)(1) [the notice of a motion must set forth "the date, time, and location, if ascertainable, of any scheduled hearing. . . ."].

 

The motion may be continued to allow defendant to give proper notice and comply with applicable statutes.


Case Number: 24SMCV01224    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

MMBA Ventura, LLC v. Sypien, Case No. 24SMCV01224

Hearing Date June 27, 2024

Defendant Sypien’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint (UNOPPOSED)

 

Plaintiff MMBA Ventura filed a form complaint for unlawful detainer on March 15, 2024 seeking possession and past due rent. After demurrer, plaintiff filed an amended form complaint.

 

Per the FAC, defendant entered into a written agreement with plaintiff’s agent to rent the premises and also work as an employee, entitling him a rent credit. A copy of the lease is not attached. Code Civ. Proc. §1161(2). The FAC alleged defendant was substitute served a three-day notice on February 20, 2024 to Vivian (Doe). Defendant demurs to the FAC. No opposition is filed.

 

Demurrer

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1170, the defendants to an action for possession of real property may demur to the complaint. A defendant may demur on several grounds, including the ground that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e).

 

Standing

Defendant argues plaintiff does not have standing to sue, but FAC, para. 4 alleges plaintiff is owner of the premises.

 

Defendant attaches deeds as exhibits to show the property is owned by other entities, not MMBA Ventures. However, the court must accept as true all well-pleaded factual allegations of the complaint. Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal. 3d 311, 318. This is a factual question, not suitable for resolution on demurrer.

 

Sufficiency of Three-Day Notice

A three-day notice must state “the amount that is due, the name, telephone number, and address of the person to whom the rent payment shall be made,” and the methods by which the tenants may pay the landlord. Code Civ. Proc. §1161(2). “Due to the summary nature of such an action, a three-day notice is valid only if the landlord strictly complies with the provisions of section 1161, subdivision 2 …” Levitz Furniture Co. v. Wingtip Comms. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1035, 1038.

 

A three-day notice is invalid if it seeks rent in excess of the amount due. Id. This is to ensure a tenant is “given the proper opportunity to cure and prevent an unlawful detainer action.” Bevill v. Zoura (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 694, 698. “[F]airness requires that the landlord demand the precise sum due, so that the tenant will know what he must do to avoid the forfeiture.” Budaef v. Huber (1961) 194 Cal.App.2d 12, 18.

 

Defendant argues the three-day notice overstates the amount of rent due. The notice stated defendant owed $3,425 per month from March 1, 2023 through February 29, 2024. FAC Exh. 2. Paragraph 6(d) of the FAC alleges the rent agreement was changed when defendant’s employment with plaintiff’s agent terminated in February 2023. “Pursuant to the terms of the employment agreement, rent was increased to $3,425 pursuant to Santa Monica Rent Charter Amendment Section 7001.” FAC ¶6(d).

 

Defendant argues plaintiff served the three-day notice “without first having served [defendant] with a notice raising his rent.” Demurrer, p. 6. If plaintiff did not serve a notice of rent increase per Civil Code sec. 827, which requires notices of rent increase be delivered personally or by mail, the increase was unlawful and the three-day notice demands an amount in excess of the lawful rent. Plaintiff failed to oppose. Sustained with leave

 

Sham Pleading

Defendant argues plaintiff’s allegation as to the rent at the beginning of the rental agreement in the FAC contradicts plaintiff’s alleged rent amount in the original complaint. The allegations are contradictory. See Complaint ¶6(a)(2), cf FAC ¶(6)(a)(2). As to the monthly rent beginning on or about July 1, 2020, the original complaint states $660, and the FAC states $1,490. Complaint ¶6(a)(2); FAC ¶(6)(a)(2). The court acknowledges plaintiff’s attempt to cross-out and omit the rental amount in the original agreement from the FAC, without an explanation as to why the amount was changed. Defendant demonstrated the existence of a sham pleading, as the change in the rental amount can be considered a harmful fact in one pleading that was avoided in a subsequent pleading without adequate explanation. Relevant facts which make a pleading defective cannot simply be omitted, particularly when the original pleading is verified. Webb, supra, 23 Cal.App.5th at 256.

 

Plaintiff must explain the reason for the change in the rental amount.

 

SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.
