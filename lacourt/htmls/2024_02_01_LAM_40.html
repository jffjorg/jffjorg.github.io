DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV18155    Hearing Date: February 1, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

MARIA REYES,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

LYNEER STAFFING SOLUTIONS, LLC, a Delaware Limited Liability Corporation; and DOES 1-20, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV18155

 Hearing Date:   2/1/24

 Trial Date:        11/19/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Maria Reyes’s Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, Form Interrogatories General Set One, Form Interrogatories Law Set One, and Production of Documents, and Request for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,675.00.

 

Background

Pleadings

Plaintiff Maria Reyes, on behalf of herself, sues Defendants Lyneer Staffing Solutions, LLC and Does 1-20 pursuant to an August 1, 2023 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA (Government Code § 12940(h)), (2) Discrimination in Violation of FEHA (Government Code § 12940(a)), (3) Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process (Government Code § 12940(n), (4) Failure to Prevent Discrimination (Government Code § 12940(k), (5) Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodation (Government Code § 12940(m), (6) Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy, (7) Failure to Pay Overtime Wages in Violation of Labor Code §§ 510, 1194, 1198, 558, 558.1, and the IWC Wage Orders, (8) Failure to Provide Meal Periods and Rest Breaks in Violation of California Labor Code §§ 226.7 and 512, (9) Failure to Reimburse Necessary Business Expenses in Violation of California Labor Code § 2802, (10) Failure to Pay Minimum Wage in Violation of Labor Code §§ 558, 558.1, 1194, 1197, 1198, and the IWC Wage Orders, (11) Failure to Pay All Wages at Termination in Violation of Labor Code §§ 201-203, (12) Failure to Provide Accurate Wage Statements in Violation of Labor Code § 226(a), and (13) Unlawful Business Practices (Business & Professions Code § 17200 et seq.

Motion Before the Court

On September 8, 2023, Plaintiff Maria Reyes served various discovery requests on Defendant Lyneer Staffing: (1) Special Interrogatories, Set One (SROGs, Set One), (2) Form Interrogatories – General, Set One (FROGS – General, Set One), (3) Form Interrogatories – Employment Law, Set One (FROGs – Employment Law, Set One), and (4) Requests for Production, Set One (RPDs, Set One). The requests were served at Lyneer Staffing’s counsel’s “virtual office” “by hand.”

On October 16, 2023, based on nonresponse from Defendant Lyneer Staffing, Plaintiff Reyes’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to some of Lyneer Staffing’s counsel, giving Lyneer Staffing until October 27, 2023 to provide objection-free discovery responses and responsive documents.

On October 25, 2023, Plaintiff Reyes’s counsel again sent a meet and confer letter to some of Lyneer Staffing’s counsel.

As of November 3, 2023, Defendant Lyneer Staffing had failed to serve responses to the above discovery requests.

That same day, Plaintiff Reyes filed a motion to compel initial responses to SROGs, Set One, FROGs – General, Set One, and FROGs – Employment Law, Set One, compel initial responses and production to RPDs, Set One, and for monetary sanctions of $3,675 against Plaintiff Reyes.

On November 15, 2023, Defendant Lyneer Staffing’s counsel requested proof of confirmation that delivery of the September 8, 2023 discovery was actually effected given that the proof of service attached to the discovery requests only referenced delivery by courier with a service list to be delivered by the courier, Ace Attorney.

On January 19, 2024, Defendant Lyneer Staffing opposed Plaintiff Reyes’s motion on the ground that that same day, Lyneer Staffing served substantially compliant, verified and substantive responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests and produced documents, and argued that it is instead Lyneer Staffing that is entitled to monetary sanctions of $1,925.

On January 25, 2024, Plaintiff Reyes replied to the opposition.

Plaintiff Reyes’s motion is now before the Court.

 

Motion to Compel Initial Responses, Interrogatories and Production

Legal Standard

A motion to compel an initial response can be made on the ground that a party did not serve a timely response to interrogatories or a demand to produce. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a) [interrogatories], 2031.300, subd. (a) [demand to produce]; see Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404 (Sinaiko).) To establish this ground, a movant must show:

(1) Proper service (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.080, subd. (a) [interrogatories], 2031.040 [demand to produce]);

(2) Expiration of the deadline for the initial response 30 days after service or on date agreed to by parties (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.260, subds. (a), (b) [interrogatories], 2031.260, subds. (a), (b) [demand to produce]); and

(3) No timely response (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 [interrogatories], 2031.300 [demand to produce]).

A court must deny a motion to compel initial discovery where the discovery sought is outside the scope of discovery. (See CBS, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 19; see also Code. Civ. Proc., § 2017.010 [scope of discovery].)

Order Compelling Initial Responses: MOOT.

Plaintiff Reyes argues that this motion is proper based on Defendant Lyneer Staffing’s nonresponse to discovery requests, that the discovery is relevant, that Lyneer Staffing has waived objections to these discovery requests by failing to provide timely responses after service of the discovery requests, and that Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions for discovery abuse. (Mot., pp. 3-6.)

In opposition, Defendant Lyneer Staffing argues that this motion should be denied for various reasons. Lyneer Staffing argues that mandatory email service is applicable to this case because Plaintiff Reyes is represented by counsel, for which reason the service “by hand” of the discovery requests was not proper. Lyneer Staffing also argues that there was no effective service of the discovery requests because the requests were personally served on a staff of Lyneer Staffing’s “virtual office,” a physical address for Lyneer Staffing used for receiving and forwarding business mail and for occasional use of the conference rooms.  Lyneer Staffing also argues that Plaintiff’s counsel has not been forthcoming about the discovery requests and that Lyneer Staffing has subsequently served Code-complaint responses to Plaintiff Reyes’s discovery requests. Last, Lyneer Staffing notes that it has filed a motion to relieve it of the waiver of objections resulting from failing to timely respond to the discovery requests and that Lyneer Staffing, and not Plaintiff Reyes, is entitled to monetary sanctions of $1,925. (Opp’n, pp. 7-11; see Opp’n, Ignacio Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. G [discovery responses].)

In reply, Plaintiff Reyes argues that Defendant Lyneer Staffing fails to accurately or substantively address Plaintiff’s legal authority. Plaintiff Reyes also argues that Lyneer Staffing does not have any authority for its argument that mandatory electronic service is required here and that personal service at the “virtual office” was proper. Last, Plaintiff Reyes argues that Lyneer Staffing’s motion to relieve it from objections will fail and that she rather than Lyneer Staffing is entitled to sanctions. (Reply, pp. 4-7.)

The Court finds in favor of Defendant Lyneer Staffing.

The Court first determines that the issue of service is unclear. There is a strong argument to be made from the language of the applicable rule of court that mandatory electronic service was applicable to service of the discovery at issue here. “Except when personal service is otherwise required by statute or rule, a party or other person that is required to file documents electronically in an action must also serve documents and accept service of documents electronically from all other parties or persons, unless (A) the court orders otherwise, or (B) the action includes parties or persons that are not required to file or serve documents electronically, including self-represented parties or other self-represented persons; those parties or other persons are to be served by non-electronic methods unless they affirmatively consent to electronic service.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.251, subd. (c)(3)(A)-(B), emphasis added.)

Here, the Superior Court’s First Amended General Order dated May 3, 2019 mandates that pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.253, subdivision (b), represented litigants are required to electronically file documents with the Court through an approved electronic filing service provider. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, First Amended General Order dated May 3, 2019, ¶ 2(b).) Plaintiff Reyes, as a represented litigant, is thus required to electronically file documents, and thus, to electronically serve documents as well.

No order appears in the record mandating that electronic service need not be made here pursuant to rule 2.251, and the exceptions to rule 2.251 do not apply because those exceptions are limited to self-represented person or persons who need to be served via non-electronic methods. Here, Defendant Lyneer Staffing is a represented party. Thus, electronic service would appear to have been mandatory for service of SROGs, Set One, FROGS – General, Set One, FROGs – Employment Law, Set One, and RPDs, Set One. Nevertheless, service was effected in person on a staff member of the “virtual office.” (Mot., Favarote Decl., Exs. 1-4, Proofs of Service [showing service effected by courier Ace Attorney, to be served at 888 Prospect Street, Suite 200, La Jolla].) Service was thus—within the context of the Los Angeles Superior Court Rules of Court and First Amended General Order—improper insofar as it did not include electronic service as well.

Plaintiff Reyes makes the reasonable argument, however, that the proper interpretation of rule 2.251, subdivision (c)(3) is that if electronic filing is required by a party or person in the superior court at issue, then it is only electronic service or receipt of those electronically filed documents that must also be effected. (Reply, p. 1, fn. 1 and pp. 4-5.) While the text of the rule does not specifically state that litigants who are required to file documents electronically must only electronically serve the documents that they file, there is ambiguity from the placement of this rule within the rules of court. For example, rule 2.251 subdivision (c)(1) states that a court may require parties to serve documents electronically in specified civil actions by local rule or court order, as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6. The General Order does not specifically so order.

In the instant case, the Court need not resolve the dispute, because the original proofs of service filed with the motion do not properly show that the documents were served personally, since they instead state under penalty of perjury that “I directed the sealed envelope(s) to the party(ies) so designated on the service list to be delivered by Ace Attorney Service, Inc., this date.” (See Mot., Favarote Decl., Exs. 1-4, Proof of Service [showing service address at 888 Prospect Street, Suite 200, La Jolla].) Even in the documents submitted in Reply, there is no formal attestation by a process server, only an email printout from “POD@acelegal” showing confirmation of a 12:28 PM delivery on September 8, 2023 “by hand” on “Maria A.” at “Stacey Cooper Law, PC,” “888 Prospect St. #200, La Jolla.” (Reply, Favarote Decl., Ex. 1.)  (Code of Civil Procedure §§ 1011, 2009.)

Because there is no showing that service was properly made, no waiver of objections has occurred. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a), 2031.300, subd. (a).)

Last, the Court determines that the January 19, 2024 discovery responses by Defendant Lyneer Staffing are Code-compliant. A review of those responses shows that Lyneer Staffing either answered “No” or “Yes” to the form interrogatories, and where the responses are “Yes,” these responses provide the additional information requested by the interrogatory requests, without objections. The responses to the production requests contain objections and a statement of compliance based on ongoing discovery or substantive responses to each request. The responses to the special interrogatories contain some objections, but they all provide substantive responses qualified by the objections, where applicable. The Court adopts its above discussion relating to waiver to determine that the objections in Defendant Lyneer Staffing’s responses are properly stated because they were not waived. (Opp’n, Ignacio Decl., ¶ 9, Ex G [responses to three interrogatory and two production requests].)

The Court briefly discusses when Lyneer Staffing did or should have become aware of service of SROGs, Set One, FROGS – General, Set One, FROGs – Employment Law, Set One, and RPDs, Set One. The October 16, 2023 meet and confer declaration attached to the moving papers show that Plaintiff Reyes’s counsel drafted a letter, to be emailed to Stacey Cooper and Manuel Ignacio as Lyneer Staffing’s counsel, which noted the nonresponse to these discovery requests. (Mot., Favarote Decl., Ex. 5 [showing letter, but not copy of any email attaching letter thereto or proof of service whether formal or informal].) The opposition provides evidence to show that an October 16, 2023 email involved discovery in a San Bernardino Superior Court action asserting PAGA claims against Lyneer Staffing. (Opp’n, Cooper Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. E [October 16, 2023 emails regarding San Bernardino Superior Court action].) The opposition also shows that an October 16, 2023 letter was also sent in the LA Case, although Ms. Cooper denies having seen it. (Opp’n, Cooper Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. F.) The active meeting and conferring that occurred regarding the San Bernardino discovery (Opp’n, Ignacio Dec., Exs A-E), tends to support Defendant’s contention that they failed to see, rather than deliberately ignored, the letter pertaining to this case.

In any event, and while the parties may disagree as to that date, it appears undisputed from the evidence before the Court that Lyneer Staffing’s counsel became aware of the discovery requests as of at least November 3, 2023, when this motion was filed. (Opp’n, Cooper Decl., ¶¶ 6-7 [cannot locate October 16, 2023 email, but discovered these requests following November 3, 2023 motion]). Lyneer Staffing also provides evidence to show that counsel attempted to secure a document from Plaintiff Reyes’s counsel showing that hand delivery was in fact effected by Ace Attorney on September 8, 2023, which, as of January 19, 2024, Plaintiff Reyes’s counsel had failed to do. (Opp’n, Ignacio Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. F [attached printout of November 15, 2023 email by Manuel Ignacio]; cf. Reply, Favarote Decl., Ex. 2 [showing service of responses via email on January 20, 2024, with the Court noting the responses were attached to the January 19, 2024 opposition’s Ignacio Decl. as Ex. G].) The reply attaches an email printout from “POD@acelegal” showing confirmation of a 12:28 PM delivery on September 8, 2023 “by hand” on “Maria A.” at “Stacey Cooper Law, PC,” “888 Prospect St. #200, La Jolla.” (Reply, Favarote Decl., Ex. 1.) However, the reply does not address whether, prior to the reply’s filing date of January 25, 2024, Plaintiff Reyes’s counsel had shared that document with Lyneer Staffing. (See Reply, p. 4, Favarote Decl., ¶¶ 1-3.)

Under these circumstances, the Court finds it reasonable for Lyneer Staffing to have waited to serve responses until it received confirmation of service of the discovery requests, and, when it did not, for good measure, attached responses to that discovery to the opposition, which were served on January 19, 2023, as attached to the opposition, or as emailed to Plaintiff Reyes.

The Court also notes that the parties have exchanged email addresses for where service should be made in the future. (Reply, p. 4, Favarote Decl., Ex. 3) Future service must be made at these email addresses pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subd. (b)(4).

For the above stated reasons, Plaintiff Reyes’s motion is MOOT, as made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.290 and 2031.300, though this order does not affect rights pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300 and 2031.310 to 2031.320.

These determinations also make the January 23, 2024 motion for relief by Defendant Lyneer Staffing MOOT, as it appears service was invalid, and even if it was not, Lymeer subsequently served substantially compliant responses, and the failure to serve responses was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

Order Granting Sanctions: DENIED (as to both parties).

The Court must impose monetary sanctions against anyone—party, nonparty, or attorney—who unsuccessfully makes or opposes the motion, unless it finds that the person to be sanctioned acted withs substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of the sanctions unjust. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c) [interrogatories], 2031.300, subd. (c) [demand to produce]; see Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 404 [interrogatories and demand to produce].)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

Based on the above discussions relating to the complexities of the service issue, when Lyneer Staffing became aware of the discovery at issue, and the reasonability of Lyneer Staffing waiting to receive confirmation of service “by hand” through courier, the Court determines that imposition of sanctions against Lyneer Staffing would be unjust. The Court thus exercises its discretion to DENY sanctions here.

On the other side, the Court DENIES sanctions in favor of Defendant Lyneer Staffing. The Court finds that the issue of service was a reasonable dispute, even if Plaintiff apparently did not produce evidence showing Ace Attorney confirmed delivery of the discovery requests at the “virtual office” until the Reply brief.

 

Conclusion

Plaintiff Maria Reyes’s Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, Form Interrogatories General Set One, Form Interrogatories Law Set One, and Production of Documents, and Request for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,675.00 is:

(1) MOOT as to compelling initial responses to these discovery requests, and

(2) DENIED as to monetary sanctions.

Defendant Lyneer Staffing Solutions, LLC’s opposition request for sanction is also DENIED.

The April 9, 2024 Hearing on Motion - Other for Relief from Waiver of Objections to Discovery is advanced to this date and vacated.

The Court strongly urges the parties to engage in Informal Discovery Conferences in order to resolve further discovery disputes.
