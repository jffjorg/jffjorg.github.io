Case Number: 22STCV13106    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 52

Defendant Farmers Group, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production, Set Four

            Defendant Farmers Group, Inc. (FGI) moves to compel plaintiff Maureen Martinez to provide further responses to requests for production Nos. 31-34.  A requesting party may move to compel further responses to requests for production if “[a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete,” “[a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive,” or “[a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).)

Further Responses  

After FGI filed this motion, plaintiff agreed to serve a supplemental response to No. 31.  The court finds the motion is moot as to request No. 31.

In Nos. 32 to 34, FGI requested “The Profit and Loss Statement for Maureen Martinez Insurance Agency, Inc., including, but not limited to, I.R.S. Form 1040 (Schedule C), for the year [32: 2017 / 33: 2019 / 34: 2020].”  Plaintiff responded with many objections and did not agree to produce any documents.  In her opposition, she argued only in support of her objection based on the tax return privilege. 

Plaintiff justifies her objection under the tax return privilege.  There is “a statutory privilege against disclosing tax returns.”  (Weingarten v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 268, 274.)  But “[t]he privilege will not be upheld when (1) the circumstances indicate an intentional waiver of the privilege; (2) the gravamen of the lawsuit is inconsistent with the privilege; or (3) a public policy greater than that of the confidentiality of tax returns is involved.  [Citation.]  This latter exception is narrow and applies only ‘when warranted by a legislatively declared public policy.’ ”  (Ibid.) 

The privilege applies though the requests seek “Profit and Loss Statements” and not necessarily tax returns themselves.  Parties may not “avoid the application of the privilege by indirect means” of discovery, such as asking for “information concerning specific entries in” a tax return.  (Sav-On Drugs, Inc. v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 1, 7.)  IRS Form 1040, Schedule C is titled, “Profit or Loss From Business.”  (Palmer Reply Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. E.)  FGI’s requests for “Profit and Loss Statements” from Maureen Martinez Insurance Agency, Inc. seek the same information that would appear on the tax return forms.

FGI argues “IRS Form 1040 Schedule C is an underlying document not privileged under Revenue Code section 19282, even if used for the preparation of the tax return.”  (Reply, p. 4.)  But Schedule C expressly instructs, “Attach to Form 1040.”  (Palmer Reply Decl., Ex. E.)  Form 1040 is the “U.S. Individual Income Tax Return.”  Even if Schedule C were an underlying document rather than part of the return, the privilege applies to other documents that “constitute an integral part of the return.”  (Brown v. Superior Court (1977) 71 Cal.App.3d 141, 143 [privilege applies to W-2 forms].) 

FGI does not show an intentional waiver, that the gravamen of the lawsuit is inconsistent with the tax return privilege, or that a greater public policy warrants disclosing the tax returns.  FGI contends any privacy interest is minimal because the profit and loss statements are for Maureen Martinez Insurance Agency, Inc., not plaintiff as an individual.  Assuming that is correct, it is not “ ‘a legislatively declared public policy’ ” (Weingarten v. Superior Court, supra, 102 Cal.App.4th at p. 274) that outweighs the interest in confidentiality of tax returns.

Plaintiff’s objections to requests for production Nos. 32-34 based on the tax return privilege are sustained.          

Sanctions

Disposition

Defendant Farmers Group, Inc.’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production, set four is denied.

Case Number: 24STCV02192    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 52

Non-Appearance Case Review Re: Default Judgment

Plaintiff JDBR Investments, LLC requests court judgment by default against defendants Andre Wegner and Drinkwater Investment Group, LLC (Drinkwater).  Plaintiff’s application for default judgment has two defects.

I. Punitive Damages

A. Entitlement to Punitive Damages

            Plaintiff does not allege a cause of action for which it can recover punitive damages.  “The relief allowable by default is limited not only by the prayer but by the substantive allegations of the complaint.  Under the ‘well pleaded’ complaint rule, it is error to enter a default judgment on a complaint that fails to state a cause of action against the defaulting defendant.”  (Ferraro v. Camarlinghi (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 539.)  Punitive damages may only be awarded “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)

Substantively, the complaint alleges, “On 4/10/22, Plaintiff loaned WEGNER, through his real estate development entity, DRINKWATER, the sum of $200,000 plus interest at 10% per year from 4/11/22 pursuant to the written Promissory Note Agreement.  Defendant agreed to repay the $200,000 loan plus interest by 8/11/22.”  (Comp., ¶ 12.)  “On 4/13/22, Plaintiff wired the $200,000 loan funds to Defendant.”  (¶ 13.)  Plaintiff further alleges, “At the time of the agreement, Defendant also agreed to use Plaintiff’s loan for the renovation project and that Defendant would record a Deed of Trust as a secured lien against the property.  However, Defendant failed to use the loan for the renovation project, failed to record the Deed of Trust, and failed to repay the loan by the 8/11/22 due date.”  (¶ 14.)  The gravamen of the case is thus that defendants failed to repay a loan.

            Plaintiff’s complaint alleges three causes of action: (1) breach of contract, (2) common counts, and (3) fraud.  Only the third cause of action is a breach of an obligation not arising from contract.  Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for fraud.  “The elements of promissory fraud (i.e., of fraud or deceit based on a promise made without any intention of performing it) are: (1) a promise made regarding a material fact without any intention of performing it; (2) the existence of the intent not to perform at the time the promise was made; (3) intent to deceive or induce the promisee to enter into a transaction; (4) reasonable reliance by the promisee; (5) nonperformance by the party making the promise; and (6) resulting damage to the promisee.”  (Behnke v. State Farm General Ins. Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1453 (Behnke).)

In its fraud cause of action, plaintiff alleges, “Defendant represented, in writing at the time of the promissory note, and orally multiple times thereafter, that he would pay Plaintiff back the principal loan balance of $200,000 plus interest.  However, Defendant never repaid any of the loan.  Defendants also represented, in writing at the time of the promissory note, that he would use said loan for a real estate construction renovation project, and that Defendant would record a Deed of Trust as a secured lien against said real estate.  However, Defendant did not provide any accounting to prove that the loan funds were actually used for the construction renovation project (which was abandoned), and never recorded the Deed of Trust against the property as a secured lien, as promised.”  (Comp., ¶¶ 26-29.)  Plaintiff further alleges, “Defendant[s] induced Plaintiff to act in reliance on their representations in order to wrongfully obtain Plaintiff’s money without paying it back.  As a direct and proximate result of Defendant’s fraud described herein, Plaintiff has been harmed in the amount of $200,000 for the principal loan balance, interest on the unpaid principal, … and contractual attorney fees plus court costs.”  (¶¶ 34-35.) 

Plaintiff does not allege facts showing “reasonable reliance by the promisee.”  (Behnke, supra, 196 Cal.App.4th at p. 1453.)  Whether a plaintiff’s “ ‘reliance was justified’ ” is evaluated “ ‘in the light of his own knowledge and experience.’ ”  (Alliance Mortgage Co. v. Rothwell (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1226, 1247.)  Plaintiff does not show it reasonably relied on defendants’ promise to repay the loan and later record a deed of trust.  Plaintiff is not merely an unsophisticated individual.  It is an LLC whose name includes “Investments.”  Acquiring a security interest in the real property simultaneously with the promissory note is not only standard procedure by lenders, but also is contemplated by the promissory note itself.  The promissory note provides, “This Note shall be secured by a Deed of Trust to real property commonly known as 312 E 44th Street, Long Beach, California 90807, executed on April 11, 2022.”  (Comp., Ex. 1, § II.)  The parties executed the promissory note on April 11, 2022.  (Id., p. 5.)  Nevertheless, plaintiff transferred the $200,000 to Drinkwater on April 13 (Comp., Ex. 2, p. 2) before acquiring the deed of trust that should have been executed two days earlier.  

The point of acquiring a deed of trust is to avoid depending on a mere promise of repayment.  “A deed of trust to real property act[s] as security for a loan.”  (Yvanova v. New Century Mortgage Corp. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 919, 926.)  Acquiring a deed of trust gives the lender greater remedies, such as “nonjudicial foreclosure,” which “is designed to provide the lender-beneficiary with an inexpensive and efficient remedy against a defaulting borrower.”  (Ibid.)  That plaintiff JDBR Investments, LLC funded defendants’ loan before acquiring the promised deed of trust was manifestly unreasonable. 

Plaintiff also alleges it relied on misrepresentations defendants made after plaintiff funded the loan.  (Comp., ¶¶ 29-33.)  These allegations cannot support a cause of action for fraud because plaintiff does not allege it suffered any damages due to those misrepresentations.  “The fraud plaintiff must … allege his damages were caused by the actions he took in reliance on the defendant’s misrepresentations.”  (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1064.)  “If the defrauded plaintiff would have suffered the alleged damage even in the absence of the fraudulent inducement, causation cannot be alleged and a fraud cause of action cannot be sustained.”  (Ibid.)  A plaintiff is “incapable of alleging damages” when “even in the absence of the defendant’s fraud, the plaintiff would have suffered the same injury.”  (Id. at pp. 1064-1065.)  Plaintiff already paid defendants the $200,000.  Plaintiff does not allege facts showing it did anything in reliance on subsequent misrepresentations or that any such actions caused damages to plaintiff.

B. Amount of Punitive Damages

Assuming plaintiff could recover punitive damages, it does not justify the amount it requests: $200,000.  “[A]n award of punitive damages hinges on three factors: the reprehensibility of the defendant’s conduct; the reasonableness of the relationship between the award and the plaintiff’s harm; and, in view of the defendant’s financial condition, the amount necessary to punish him or her and discourage future wrongful conduct.”  (Kelly v. Haag (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 910, 914 (Kelly).

On the first and second factors, the conduct giving rise to defendants’ liability for punitive damages is promissory fraud in inducing plaintiff to lend them $200,000.  (Comp., ¶¶ 26-35.)  The harm to plaintiff was simply that defendants did not repay the $200,000 loan.  Defendants’ conduct was minimally reprehensible.  It caused no damages beyond those for breach of contract, which would not permit recovering punitive damages.  As discussed above, plaintiff’s conduct in funding defendants’ loan before acquiring the promised deed of trust was its own blunder more than reprehensible conduct by defendants. 

Awarding plaintiff an extra $200,000 in punitive damages simply because it funded the loan before getting the deed of trust first and because it alleged defendants committed promissory fraud in addition to breach of contract would be an unjustified windfall.  Such punitive damages would arise from plaintiff’s artful pleading instead of defendants’ reprehensible conduct worthy of punishment.

Finally, plaintiff does not present sufficient evidence for the third factor.  The plaintiff must show “ ‘evidence of the defendant’s ability to pay the damage award.’ ”  (Green v. Laibco, LLC (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 441, 453.)  “[E]vidence of the defendant’s financial condition is a prerequisite to a punitive damages award.”  (Adams v. Murakami (1991) 54 Cal.3d 105, 119.)  The plaintiff bears the burden of introducing such evidence.  (Ibid.)  Punitive damages generally should not be so great as to risk “ ‘crippling or destroying the defendant.’ ”  (Kelly, supra, 145 Cal.App.4th at p. 915.) 

Plaintiff presents evidence that in April 2022, Drinkwater acquired real property at 312 East 44th Street in Long Beach, California.  (Rocco Decl., ¶ 27, Ex. 6.)  The record includes evidence the property is worth $950,000 and is encumbered by a mortgage of $250,000.  (Id., ¶¶ 7-8.)  But plaintiff does not present adequate evidence showing Drinkwater currently owns the property as required.  (See Kelly, supra, 145 Cal.App.4th at p. 917.)  Plaintiff asserts Drinkwater Investment Group, LLC is “an inactive and dissolved Wyoming limited liability company.”  (Rocco Decl., ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff thus presents evidence undermining any finding that Drinkwater is able to pay $200,000 in punitive damages. 

Plaintiff also presents evidence Wegner owns real property in San Juan Capistrano (id., Ex. 8), which Zillow estimates is worth about $250,000 (id., Ex. 7).  Plaintiff does not, however, present evidence showing that property has no encumbrances.   

Overall, the record does not adequately show defendants’ ability to pay $200,000 in punitive damages—particularly when added to the other components of the proposed judgment, which total over $250,000.  Even assuming defendants have net assets of $950,000, adding $200,000 in punitive damages to the award of $250,000 presents a substantial risk of financially destroying defendants.

II. Attorney Fees

Plaintiff also seeks excessive attorney fees of $17,500.  Attorney fees for a default judgment are generally limited to a specified schedule based on the amount of damages.  (Local Rule 3.207(a), 3.214(a).)  Plaintiffs may recover “a fee greater than listed in the [default] schedule because of extraordinary services.”  (3.214(d).)  Plaintiff fails to show extraordinary services justifying a fee greater than permitted under the schedule.  Plaintiff relies primarily on work done attempting to locate and serve defendant Wegner.  That does not constitute extraordinary legal services.    

Disposition

Plaintiff JDBR Investments, LLC’s request for default judgment is denied without prejudice.

Case Number: 24STCV26398    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: 52

Defendant Public Storage’s Motion to Compel Arbitration

Defendant Public Storage moves to compel arbitration of plaintiff Victoria Castro’s action against it. 

Both the California Arbitration Act and Federal Arbitration Act permit a party to move to enforce an agreement to arbitrate disputes.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; 9. U.S.C. § 2.)  A motion to compel arbitration is “a summary proceeding.”  (Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1057.)  The moving party can meet the “initial burden to show an agreement to arbitrate by attaching a copy of the arbitration agreement purportedly bearing the opposing party’s signature.”  (Id. at p. 1060.) 

Defendant meets its burden.  It presents evidence showing plaintiff signed an agreement to rent a storage unit from defendant in June 2022.  (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. A.)  The agreement provides, “Both you and Public Storage agree to arbitrate any and all disputes or claims arising out of, in connection with, or in any way relating to the relationship between you and us… .”  (Id., ¶ 6.2.)  Plaintiff’s complaint alleges damages arising from the loss of property taken or stolen from her storage units.  (Comp., ¶¶ BC-2, BC-4.)  The arbitration provision applies to her claims.

Plaintiff gives no basis for the court to deny this motion.  The law favors enforcement of valid arbitration agreements.  (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 97.)  “ ‘ “[A] party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” ’ ”  (Kader v. Southern California Medical Center, Inc. (2024) 99 Cal.App.5th 214, 221.)  Plaintiff does not meet her burden.

Disposition

            Defendant Public Storage’s motion to compel arbitration is granted.  The court hereby orders plaintiff Victoria Castro to arbitrate her causes of action against defendant pursuant to the parties’ rental agreement.  The court hereby stays the entire action pending resolution of the arbitration proceeding.
