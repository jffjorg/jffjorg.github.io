DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV47317    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: B

Hearing:          01/21/2025

Re:                   21STCV47317 Robert Rothschild vs Shahram Poorbehmardeyan

 

 

Defendant Sharam Poorbehmardeyan (“Defendant”)’s Motions to Compel Robert Rothschild (“Plaintiff”)’s Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, is granted. Plaintiff is ordered to serve Defendant with verified, objection-free responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, within 20 days of this hearing, on the condition that Defendant pay $120.00 in outstanding filing fees. Additionally, Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, are ordered to pay $260.00 in sanctions to defense counsel within 20 days.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS

 

Interrogatories

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute does not contain a time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

Where there has been no timely response to interrogatories, the demanding party may seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300(b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. (CCP § 2031.300(a).) Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the interrogatories. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.

 

Request for Production of Documents

 

If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production. (CCP § 2025.480(a).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel . . . responses to interrogatories or production demands, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.” (CCP § 2030.290(c).) Similarly, “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (CCP § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Shahram Poorbehmardeyan (“Defendant”) moves to compel Plaintiff Robert Rothschild (“Plaintiff”)’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant also seeks sanctions in the amount of $260.00, comprised of an hourly rate of $220.00 and a $60.00 filing fee. (Stratman Decl. ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s response to discovery because Plaintiff failed to provide any responses. Defendant propounded discovery requests on Plaintiff on May 23, 2024. (Stratman Decl. ¶¶ 3-5; Exhs. A, B, and C [discovery requests].) Plaintiff never provided responses. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7-8.)

 

In the future, Defendant is ordered to obtain separate hearing reservations and pay separate filing fees for each motion to compel. Combining multiple motions under the guise of one motion with one hearing reservation manipulates the Court Reservation System and unfairly jumps ahead of other litigants. Moreover, combining motions to avoid payment of filing fees deprives the Court of filing fees it is otherwise entitled to collect.

 

Be that as it may, since these motions are unopposed, the Court orders the following, effective once Defendant pays an additional $120.00 in filing fees:

 

Defendant’s motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One are granted. Plaintiff is to serve Defendant with verified, objection-free responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, within 20 days of this hearing.

 

Considering Plaintiff’s failure to comply with their discovery obligations, the Court orders Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions in the reduced amount of $260.00, payable to defense counsel within 20 days of this hearing.

 

Moving party to give notice.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff Noah Cvengros (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Sam Tucker (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10 alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence.

On March 4, 2024, Defendant filed three motions to compel further responses against Plaintiff, as to the following discovery documents: (1) Form Interrogatories, Set One; (2) Requests for Admissions, Set One; and (3) Special Interrogatories, Set One (“Discovery Motions”). Plaintiff also requests sanctions in the total amount of $4,065 ($1,355 per motion).

On December 19, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the instant motions. Plaintiff’s counsel’s was recently substituted into the matter on December 2, 2024. (See December 19, 2024, Minute Order.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s counsel represented that they would provide verified, code-compliant, objection-free responses within 20 days.

Consequently, counsel were to submit a joint statement on the status of discovery at least five days prior to the next hearing. Counsel have not done so.  The Court assumes from the failure to file a joint statement that Plaintiff failed to provide discovery responses as represented and issues the following tentative ruling.

The motions remain unopposed.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

Interrogatories

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 provides, in pertinent part, that “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” (CCP § 2030.300(a).) 

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2030.300(c).) 

 

Request for Admissions

CCP § 2033.290 provides, in pertinent part, that “[o]n receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete. (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (CCP § 2033.290(a).)

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2033.290(c).)

Under California law, the objecting party has the burden of justifying its objections when the propounding party requests that the Court order further responses. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.) Further, under CCP § 2023.010, it is a misuse of discovery to make unmerited objections. (CCP § 2023.010(e).) 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer Requirement

A motion to compel further responses shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP §§ 2030.300(b) and 2033.290(b).) Here, defense counsel provides a declaration demonstrating diligent efforts to meet and confer with opposing counsel. (Silva Van Vo Decl. ¶ 13; Exh. E [meet and confer letter].) On February 9, 2024, defense counsel sent a Meet and Confer Letter to opposing counsel, outlining the deficiencies in Plaintiff’s responses and offering fourteen additional days for further response. (Ibid.) Defense counsel did not receive a response nor supplemental discovery. Therefore, the Court finds Defendant’s meet and confer efforts to be sufficient pursuant to CCP § 2030.300(b).)

 

Form Interrogatories, Set One

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One Nos. 2.5-2.6, 6.1-6.7, 8.5, 17.1, 20.1, and 20.3-20.10.

The motion is granted.. Plaintiff generally fails to provide any response to these interrogatories, with or without objection. Each interrogatory is related to discoverable matter. Therefore, Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, objection-free, supplemental responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2.5-2.6, 6.1-6.7, 8.5, 17.1, 20.1, and 20.3-20.10.

 

Requests for Admissions, Set One

Defendant moves to compel the Plaintiff's further responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 4-9 and 12-13.

The motion is granted. Plaintiff’s responses are generally riddled with typing errors and what appear to be incoherent drafting notes. Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, clear, objection-free, supplemental responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 4-9 and 12-13.

 

Special Interrogatories, Set One

Defendant moves to compel the Plaintiff’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2-9 and 11-14.

The motion is granted. Plaintiff’s responses to Nos. 2-4 are generally riddled with typing errors and what appear to be incoherent drafting notes. Plaintiff’s responses to Nos. 5, 9, and 11, are incomplete. Plaintiff fails to provide any responses to Nos. 6-8 and 12-14.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, clear, objection-free, supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2-9 and 11-14.

 

Monetary Sanctions

“The court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct” under CCP § 2023.030(a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

Here, Plaintiff failed to completely and coherently respond to Defendant’s Discovery Motions, constituting a misuse of the discovery process. Therefore, monetary sanctions are appropriate.

Defendant requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,355 per motion, representing a $60 filing fee, four hours preparing each motion and an anticipated three hours for the hearing of each motion at an hourly rate of $185. (Silva Van Vo Decl. ¶ 15.) The Court reduces monetary sanctions to $615 per motion, representing three hours of work per motion at an hourly rate of $185 and a $60 filing fee, as each motion is largely identical and no oppositions were filed. Therefore, the Court awards monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the total amount of $1,845.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s Discovery Motions are GRANTED.

The Court ORDERS that Plaintiff provides verified, complete and code-compliant responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One Nos. 2.5-2.6, 6.1-6.7, 8.5, 17.1, 20.1, and 20.3-20.10, Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2-9 and 11-14, and Requests for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 4-9 and 12-13, within 20 days of this order. Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, are also ordered to pay defense counsel $1,845.00 in monetary sanctions within 20 days of this order.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On October 2, 2024, Plaintiffs Melissa Jean Pero (“Pero”), Kamran Hussain (“Kamran”), and Ludmila Seletskiy (“Seletskiy)(collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Asif “Robert” Hussain (“Robert”), Kelly Gorgone (“Gorgone), and Does 1 to 25 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for wrongful use of civil proceedings.

On December 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) adding a second cause of action for financial elder abuse.

On December 23, 2024, Robert filed the instant anti-SLAPP motion to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ FAC.

On January 7, 2025, Plaintiffs filed their reply.

On January 13, 2025, Robert filed his reply.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

“Litigation of an anti-SLAPP motion involves a two-step process. First, ‘the moving defendant bears the burden of establishing that the challenged allegations or claims ‘aris[e] from’ protected activity in which the defendant has engaged.’ [Citation.] Second, for each claim that does arise from protected activity, the plaintiff must show the claim has “at least ‘minimal merit.’ [Citation.]”  (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1009 [citations omitted].) As to the second step inquiry, a plaintiff seeking to demonstrate the merit of the claim “may not rely solely on its complaint, even if verified; instead, its proof must be made upon competent admissible evidence.” (Sweetwater Union High School District v. Gilbane Building Co. (2019) 6 Cal.5th 931, 940 [citations omitted].)

A party opposing a special motion to strike has the burden to “state [] and substantiate [] a legally sufficient claim.” (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88 & 93.) “‘Put another way, the [party] “must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the defendant is credited.”’ [Citations.]”  (Ibid. at 88-89.)  To that end, the defendant must present competent evidence “that would be admissible at trial.” (HMS Capital, Inc. v. Lawyers Title Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 204, 212.) “[D]eclarations may not be based upon ‘information and belief’ [citation]” and documents submitted without the proper foundation will not be considered. (Ibid.)  The complaint, even if verified, is insufficient to carry the plaintiff’s shifted burden. (Roberts v. Los Angeles County Bar Association (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 604, 614; Karnazes v. Ares (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 344, 354 [“pleadings do not constitute evidence”].)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections

On January 13, 2025, Robert objected to portions of the following persons’ declarations, provided in support of Plaintiffs’ opposition: (1) Ludmilla Seletskiy; (2) Melissa Jean Pero; (3) Kelly Gorgone; and (4) Kamran Hussain.

 

Seletskiy Declaration

Robert’s objections are overruled as to Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. Seletskiy was in a romantic relationship with decedent Mohammed “Mike” Hussain (“Decedent”) since 2001. (Seletskiy Decl. ¶ 2.) Seletskiy and Decedent were married on January 31, 2022. (Ibid. at ¶ 3.) Therefore, Seletskiy sets forth adequate foundation regarding her knowledge of ownership interest of Tires by MAK, Inc., dba Valley Discount Tires (“Valley Discount Tires”). (See ibid. at ¶ 4 [“I know (of the ownership interest) from tax filings for the Tire Business].) Additionally, pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code § 1260, “. . . evidence of any of the following statements made by a declarant who is unavailable as a witness is not made inadmissible by the hearsay rule: (1) That the declarant has or has not made a will or established or amended a revocable trust. (2) That the declarant has or has not revoked his or her will, revocable trust, or an amendment to a revocable trust. (3) That identifies the declarant’s will, revocable trust, or an amendment to a revocable trust.” (Cal. Evid. Code § 1260(a).) Where intent is a material element of a disputed fact, declarations of a deceased made after as well as before an alleged act that indicate the intent with which he performed the act are admissible in evidence as an exception to the hearsay rule. (Whitlow v. Durst (1942) 20 Cal.2d 523, 524.) The doctrine that declarations of a decedent’s intent to do an act are admissible to prove that act thereafter was done is fully accepted in California. (Casey v. Casey (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 875, 881.) Therefore, Seletskiy’s declarations regarding Decedent’s creation or lack of a will, and circumstances thereof, are admissible under § 1260.

 

Pero, Gorgone, and Kamran Declarations

Robert’s objections to the Pero, Gorgone, and Kamran declaration are overruled in their entirety for the same reasoning discussed supra. (See Evid. Code § 1260; Whitlow, supra, (1942) 20 Cal.2d at 524; Casey, supra, (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d at 881.)

 

Prong One: Arising from Protected Activity

“A cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike if the defendant shows that the cause of action arises from an act in furtherance of the defendant’s constitutional right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue and the plaintiff fails to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim. [Citations.]”  (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 883.)

“An ‘act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ is defined by statute to include ‘(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.’[Citation.] If the defendant shows that the cause of action arises from a statement described in clause (1) or (2) of [§ 425.16(e)], the defendant is not required to separately demonstrate that the statement was made in connection with a ‘public issue.’ [Citation.]”  (Ibid.)

“A cause of action is one ‘arising from’ protected activity within the meaning of section 425.16, subdivision (b)(1) only if the defendant’s act on which the cause of action is based was an act in furtherance of the defendant’s constitutional right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue. [Citation.] In deciding whether the ‘arising from’ requirement is satisfied, ‘the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.’ [(CCP §425.16(b)(2).)] Whether the ‘arising from’ requirement is satisfied depends on the ‘gravamen or principal thrust’ of the claim. [(Episcopal Church Cases (2009) 45 Cal.4th 467, 477, quoting Martinez v. Metabolife International, Inc. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 193.)] A cause of action does not arise from protected activity for purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute if the protected activity is merely incidental to the cause of action. [(Martinez, 113 Cal.App.4th at pg. 188.)]” (Digerati Holdings, LLC, 194 Cal.App.4th at pgs. 883-884.) To show that a claim arises from protected activity under §425.16(b)(1), it is not sufficient to show that the claim “was filed after, or because of, protected activity, or when protected activity merely provides evidentiary support or context for the claim.” (Rand Resources, LLC v. City of Carson (2019) 6 Cal.5th 610, 621.)  “Rather, the protected activity must ‘supply elements of the challenged claim.’ [Citation.]”  (Ibid.)

“At the first step, the moving defendant bears the burden of identifying all allegations of protected activity, and the claims for relief supported by them. When relief is sought based on allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the unprotected activity is disregarded at this stage.” (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 396)(emphasis added.) 

Robert brings the instant motion on the grounds that Plaintiffs’ cause of action for wrongful use of civil proceedings arises from Robert’s protected activity, and that Plaintiffs cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on their claim. (Motion p. 2:4-7.) On June 26, 2023, Robert, the son of decedent, filed a Petition for Probate of Will and Authorization to Administer Estate (the “Petition”) in the Los Angeles County Superior Court, Case No. 23STPB05930. (Robert Decl. ¶¶ 3 and 15; Exh. E [Petition].) Robert did so with the belief that all signatures on Decedent’s will were valid. (Ibid. at ¶ 15.) Robert declares that, after filing the petition, he became aware that Kelly Gorgone’s signature was invalid because Gorgone never witnessed decedent sign the will. (Ibid.) Thereafter, on June 5, 2024, at Robert’s instruction, Robert’s counsel filed a request for dismissal of the Petition with prejudice. (Ibid.; Exh. B [request for dismissal].) Robert was informed and believes that the Probate Court rejected that filing on technical grounds, stating that a different format was required. (Ibid. at ¶ 16.) Accordingly, on June 28, 2024, Robert’s counsel filed a notice of intention to abandon the Petition. (Ibid.; Exh. C [notice].) On September 11, 2024, finding that Robert had withdrawn the Petition, the Probate Court denied the Petition without prejudice. (Ibid. at ¶ 17; Exh. D [Probate Court September 11, 2024, order].)

Plaintiffs filed the instant action on October 2, 2024, and a FAC on December 2, 2024. (See FAC.) Plaintiffs’ FAC alleges that Robert, in bad faith, submitted a forged will to probate. (FAC ¶¶ 23, 25, 28-31.) These allegations serve as the grounds for Plaintiffs’ first cause of action against Robert for wrongful use of civil proceedings. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 32-42.)

Filing a petition for probate of will and authorization to administer estate is a statutorily protected activity under CCP § 425.16 because such a petition constitutes a written or oral statement or writing made before a judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law. (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 883.) Plaintiffs’ cause of action against Robert for wrongful use of civil proceedings arises from his filing of the Petition. Therefore, Robert has shown that this cause of action arises from a protected activity pursuant to CCP § 425.16. The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to show a probability of prevailing on their claim for wrongful use of civil proceedings.

 

Second Prong: Plaintiffs’ Probability of Succeeding

A cause of action for wrongful use of civil proceedings requires a showing that the defendant initiated the underlying action with malicious intent, or “motive other than that of bringing a perceived guilty person to justice or the satisfaction in a civil action of some personal or financial purpose. [Citation.]” (Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 292.) Such malice may range anywhere from “open hostility to indifference. [Citations.]” (Ibid.) “Malice may also be inferred from the facts establishing lack of probable cause.” (Grindle v. Lorbeer (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 1461, 1465-1466.)

Here, under their cause of action for wrongful use of civil proceedings, Plaintiffs allege that “[n]o reasonable person in Defendants[‘] . . . circumstances would have believed there was reasonable grounds to bear witness to a false will and bring the Petition for Probate . . . because the Purported Will was fraudulently obtained after Decedent’s death by Defendants . . .” (FAC ¶ 38.) Additionally, Plaintiffs allege that Decedent’s signature was forged by Robert with “intent to take, withhold, and otherwise wrongfully obtain Decedent’s property . . .” (Ibid. at ¶ 25.) To support these claims, Plaintiffs point to Gorgone’s testimony that Robert approached her in approximately May or June 2023 and asked that she sign the will as a witness, and further asked her to back-date her witness attestation to July 5, 2022, with which Gorgone complied. (Ibid. at ¶ 26.) Gorgone states that, after becoming concerned that her signature was given under false pretenses, she asked Robert to tell her the “truth” about what he asked her to do. (Gorgone Decl. ¶ 8.) Pero declares that, prior to asking Gorgone, Robert asked Pero to sign the will as a witness and she refused. (Pero Decl. ¶ 7.) Finally, Plaintiffs provide evidence that Decedent’s purported signature on the will might not actually be his. (See Kahn Decl. ¶¶ 4-7.)

Therefore, Plaintiffs make a prima facie showing of malice on Robert’s behalf and, in turn, make a prima facie showing of facts supporting a judgment in their favor. (Chavez v. Mendoza (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 1087, 1087.) Accordingly, Plaintiffs meet their burden under the second prong of Bonni, supra, 11 Cal.5th, showing that their claim for wrongful use of civil process has “minimal merit.” Accordingly, Robert’s motion is denied.

 

Attorney’s Fees and Costs

Plaintiffs request attorney’s fees pursuant to CCP §§ 425.16(c)(1) and 128.5. However, Robert’s motion had merit as his filing of the Petition was protected activity, and the instant motion was not made “for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.” (See CCP § 128.5(b)(2).) Therefore, the Court declines to award attorney’s fees or costs.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

Defendant Asif “Robert” Hussain’s special motion to strike Plaintiffs’ first cause of action in their first amended complaint is DENIED.
