Hearing Date:                          January 29, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Los Angeles World Airports and City of Los Angeles

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Ramona Murillo Gamboa

Motion for Summary Judgment

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            On July 24, 2019, plaintiff Ramona Murillo Gamboa filed a complaint against defendants Los Angeles World Airports, City of Los Angeles, SAS Services Group, Concesionaria Vuela Compania de Aviacion S.A.P.I. De C.V., and Controladora Vuela Compania de Aviacion, S.A.B. De C.V. for (1) premises liability; (2) dangerous condition of public property; (3) breach of duty of a common carrier; (4) negligence; (5) negligent hiring; and (6) negligence per se.  Plaintiff alleges that she slipped and fell causing her to sustain injuries on June 20, 2018.

On March 22, 2021, plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint. 

On March 23, 2023, the court sustained with leave to amend Buenos Travel Agency (Doe 1)’s demurrer to the FAC and denied defendant’s motion to dismiss under Barrows.

On April 25, 2023, plaintiff filed a SAC against defendants Los Angeles World Airports, City of Los Angeles, SAS Services Group, Concesionaria Vuela Compania de Aviacion S.A.P.I. De C.V., and Controladora Vuela Compania de Aviacion, S.A.B. De C.V., and Buenos Travel Agency for (1) premises liability; (2) dangerous condition of public property; (3) breach of duty of a common carrier; (4) negligence; (5) general negligence; (6) negligent hiring; and (7) negligence per se.

On May 15, 2023, the court granted defendant S.A.S. Services Group, Inc.’s application for determination of good faith settlement.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467; CCP §437c(c).

DISCUSSION

Defendants Los Angeles World Airports (“LAWA”) and the City of Los Angeles request summary judgment as to the causes of action asserted against them in the SAC (1st, 2nd, 4th, 5th, 6th, and 7th causes of action). 

In the SAC, plaintiff alleges that this is a personal injury action arising out of a slip-and-fall accident which occurred on June 20, 2018 due to defendants’ negligence, including defendants’ failure to provide plaintiff with a wheelchair as requested and failure to properly maintain the premises in Terminal 2 at LAX.  SAC, ¶20.  On June 5, 2018, plaintiff booked or caused to be booked on her behalf with Buenos Travel Agency a non-stop flight for June 20, 2018, departing from Zacatecas, Mexico at 7:46 p.m. arriving in LA at 9:07 p.m.  Id., ¶21.  The booking indicated plaintiff requested or caused to be requested a wheelchair to tend to her physical needs and/or disabilities and/or restrictions.  Id., ¶23.  The booking indicated such wheelchair was to be part of the accommodations that defendant Buenos was including in plaintiff’s itinerary and that was to be communicated to defendants City, LAWA, and/or Volaris. Id., 24.  On June 20, 2018, between 9:30 p.m. and 11:00 p.m., plaintiff arrived at Terminal 2 in LAX.  Id., ¶25.  Upon arriving at the airport, plaintiff requested wheelchair assistance, consistent with the request already made and previously indicated to have been included in plaintiff’s travel itinerary.  Id., ¶26.  Plaintiff waited in the airplane for approximately 30 minutes after requesting wheelchair assistance.  Id., ¶27.  Plaintiff was told by a male employee to exit the plane and start walking.  Id., ¶28.  Plaintiff exited the plane with her carry-on luggage and continued toward the baggage claim and terminal exit.  Id., ¶29.  While proceeding to the baggage claim area, plaintiff attempted to use the subject escalator, falling on her first step.  Id., ¶30.  As a direct and proximate result of defendants’ failure to provide plaintiff with the requisite medical device requested, plaintiff tripped and fell causing her to fall on her right arm, resulting in a fracture to her right forearm and pain in her right leg and right arm.  Plaintiff required immediate medical attention and emergency care.  Id., ¶31.

The parties do not dispute that both City defendants are governmental entities.  Defendants’ Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“DUMF”) 1.  The subject escalator was maintained and controlled by defendant Los Angeles World Airports.  DUMF 3.  A record search performed at LAWA Asset Management Team and LAWA Facilities Technical Services with respect to the subject escalator revealed that there were no work orders for the subject escalator on or around the date of the incident.  DUMF 5.  The conditions of the premises where the incident occurred were clean, dry, and well-lit at the time of the incident.  DUMF 7.

Evidentiary Objections 

Plaintiff’s objections to the declarations of Scott Cunningham, Frank Koehler, Phu Tong, and Vivian D. Howell are OVERRULED. 

Separate Statement

In the opposition, plaintiff asserts that defendant’s separate statement does not comply with CCP §437c(3)(b)(1).  The court finds that it is sufficient as defendants have listed material facts and citations to evidence.

Government Claims Act

Gov. Code §945.4 provides that “no suit for money or damages may be brought against a public entity on a cause of action for which a claim is required to be presented in accordance with . . . Section 910 . . . until a written claim therefor has been presented to the public entity and has been acted upon by the board, or has been deemed to have been rejected by the board . . . .”  Gov. Code §910 requires that the claim states the “date, place, and other circumstances of the occurrence or transaction which gave rise to the claim asserted” and provide [a] general description of the . . . injury, damage or loss incurred so fare as it may be known at the time of presentation of the claim.”

The purpose of the Government Claims Act is to “’to provide the public entity sufficient information to enable it to adequately investigate claims and to settle them, if appropriate, without the expense of litigation.’” Stockett v. Association of Cal. Water Agencies Joint Powers Ins. Auth. (2004) 34 Cal. 4th 441, 446 (citation omitted).  “Consequently, a claim need not contain the detail and specificity required of a pleading, but need only ‘fairly describe what the entity is alleged to have done.’”  Id. (citation omitted).  “A complaint’s fuller exposition of the factual basis beyond that given in the claim is not fatal, so long as the complaint is not based on an ‘entirely different set of facts.’  Only where there has been a ‘complete shift in allegations . . .’ have courts generally found the complaint barred.  Where the complaint merely elaborates or adds further detail to a claim, but is predicated on the same fundamental actions or failures to act by the defendants, courts have generally found the claim fairly reflects the facts pled in the complaint.”  Id. at 447 (citations omitted).

In the SAC, plaintiff alleges that plaintiff has complied with the Tort Claims Act by timely filing a Tort Claim with defendants City of LA and Los Angeles World Airports on December 7, 2018.  SAC, ¶2.  Defendants City and LAWA are public entities upon which plaintiff has timely served a written government claim pursuant to Gov. Code §§905 and 910.  Id., ¶34.

Plaintiff’s claim for damages states:  Under “How did Damage or Injury occur?” it states, “Ms. Murillo-Gamboa was travelling from Zacatecas, Mexico to Los Angeles International Airport (LAX).  She waited in the airplane on Terminal 2 for about 30 minutes for the wheelchair she requested in advance.  However, the airport staff did not provide the requested service.  Instead, one of the staff members told her and six other passengers who were waiting for wheelchairs to get off the plane and start walking.  As Ms. Murillo-Gamboa made an effort to comply with the staff’s request, she tripped and fell while riding the escalator, resulting in severe injuries in the form of a fracture to her right forearm.  Two passengers helped her get up, two airport employees arrived at the scene, and asked her about how she fell.  They made her sign a document without explaining the content and without giving her a copy.  She was transported to the hospital.”  Under “What particular ACT or OMISSION do you claim caused the injury or damage?” it states, “LAX staff failed to provide Ms. Murillo-Gamboa with a wheelchair despite her advance request for it.  Instead, LAX asked her to start walking to the escalator, despite the foreseeable risk that she will trip and fall due to LAX’s staff failure to provide Ms. Murillo-Gamboa with a wheelchair.  LAX negligently hired staff, who negligently failed to provide a wheelchair to Ms. Murillo-Gamboa, which caused her to trip and fall.”  Defendants’ compendium of evidence, Exh. 11.

Defendants argue that the causes of action based on the alleged dangerous condition of the escalator (1st, 2nd, and 4th causes of action) are barred because the claims “premised on an unspecified dangerous condition of the subject escalator, is a complete ‘shift in allegations’ from the allegedly negligent failure to provide a wheelchair stated in her government claim.”  Defendants contend that because plaintiff did not allege any dangerous condition of the subject escalator in her government claim, the City defendants had no notice or opportunity to evaluate any potential liability for a dangerous condition until the filing of the complaint. 

In opposition, plaintiff argues that she substantially complied with Gov. Code §910, but even if she did not, she contends, defendants waived any defects by virtue of their general denial.

The court finds that the 1st, 2nd, and 4th causes of action are barred under the Gov. Claims Act as they are based on allegations that the escalator itself was in a defective or dangerous condition.  Plaintiff’s government claim does not attribute the cause of her injuries to the escalator.  The claim does not identify the allegedly dangerous condition at all.  She only states that the escalator is the location where she purportedly tripped and fell.  Thus, the causes of action based on a defective or dangerous condition are not “fairly reflected” in the government claim.

 

1st cause of action for premises liability 

The SAC alleges that on June 20, 2018, defendants City and LAWA owned, maintained, and controlled the subject escalator on which plaintiff sustained injuries.  SAC, ¶36.  On June 20, 2018, defendants, as reasonably prudent owners of the subject escalator should have foreseen that plaintiff would be exposed to an unreasonable risk of harm located at the subject escalator.  AS such, they owed plaintiff a duty of care.  Id., ¶37.  Defendants owed a duty to use reasonable care to design, construct, maintain, own, operate, occupy, possess, manage, and/or control the subject escalator in a reasonably safe condition; to inspect the premises; to discovery any unsafe conditions on the escalator; to repair or remove any unsafe conditions on the escalator; and/or to provide adequate warning of any unsafe conditions on the escalator, which could reasonably be expected to harm others.  Id., ¶38.  Defendants negligently failed to do the above.  The lack of warning signs near the escalator constituted a dangerous and unsafe condition for individuals walking near or on the escalator.  The escalator was inside a heavy populated terminal in a densely populated airport filled with food and retail businesses with significant foot traffic, making it highly foreseeable that pedestrians would use the escalator, including plaintiff.  Defendants’ negligence created a dangerous and unsafe condition that created a substantial risk of the type of injury when the property was used with due care in a manner in which it was reasonably foreseeable that it would be used.  Id., ¶39.  Reasonable diligence would have revealed that the escalator was not being maintained at suitable levels of cleanliness, with suitable warning signs and safety precautions, thereby creating a dangerous condition.  Defendants did not rectify the dangerous condition.  Thus, defendants breached their duty of care by creating a dangerous condition, which exposed lessees and guests, including plaintiff, to an unreasonable risk of harm.  Id., ¶40.  Defendants had actual and constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition posed by the lack of warning signs at the escalator and had notice of it for a long enough time to have protected against it.  Id., ¶41.

Defendants argue that this claim is also barred as a matter of law by the Gov. Claims Act because it is not based on statute. 

Gov. Code §815 provides that “[e]xept as otherwise provided by statute . . . [a] public entity is not liable for an injury, whether such injury arises out of an act or omission of the public entity or a public employee or any other person.”  “[D]irect tort liability of public entities must be based on a specific statute declaring them to be liable, or at least creating some specific duty of care, and not on the general tort provisions of Civil Code section 1714.  Otherwise, the general rule of immunity for public entities would be largely eroded by the routine application of general tort principles.”  Eastburn v. Regional Fire Protection Authority (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1183.  Since the duty of a governmental agency can only be created by statute or enactment, the statute or enactment claimed to establish the duty must at the very least be identified.  Searcy v. Hemet Unified School Dist. (1986) 177 Cal. App. 3d 792, 802.  “Thus, in California, ‘all government tort liability must be based on statute.’”  Hoff v. Vacaville Unified School District (1998) 19 Cal.4th 925, 932 (citation omitted).

The opposition does not address this cause of action.

The court finds that this cause of action is based on common law and not on any statutory authority and is thus barred as a matter of law against the public entity defendants.

 

2nd cause of action for dangerous condition of public property 

The SAC alleges that the subject escalator was in a dangerous condition at the time of the incident, as the condition of the escalator created a substantial risk of injury to members of the general public like plaintiff when used with reasonable care and in a reasonably foreseeable manner.  SAC, ¶49.  The dangerous condition of the escalator, including the lack of warning signs, created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury that occurred as the result of the failure to warn.  Id., ¶50.  Defendants had notice of the dangerous condition of the subject escalator, lack of warning signs, for a long enough time to have protected against it.  Id., ¶51.

Government Code §835 “sets out the exclusive conditions under which a public entity is liable for injuries caused by a dangerous condition of public property.”  Metcalf v. County of San Joaquin (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1121, 1129. 

A public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if: 1) the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury;¿2) the dangerous condition caused the injury;¿3) the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred; and 4) either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the condition, or the entity had actual or constructive notice of the condition in time to have taken measures to protect against it.  Gov. Code § 835.  “‘Dangerous condition’ means a condition of property that creates a substantial (as distinguished from a minor, trivial or insignificant) risk of injury when such property or adjacent property is used with due care in a manner in which it is reasonably foreseeable that it will be used.”  Gov. Code § 830(a). 

“‘[A] claim alleging a dangerous condition may not rely on generalized allegations but must specify in what manner the condition constituted a dangerous condition.’  A plaintiff’s allegations, and ultimately the evidence, must establish a physical deficiency in the property itself.  A dangerous condition exists when public property ‘is physically damaged, deteriorated, or defective in such a way as to foreseeably endanger those using the property itself,’ or possesses physical characteristics in its design, location, features or relationship to its surroundings that endanger users. . . . ‘[P]ublic liability lies under section 835 only when a feature of the public property has ‘increased or intensified’ the danger to users from third party conduct.’”  Cerna v. City of Oakland (2008) 161 Cal. App. 4th 1340, 1347-1348 (citations omitted).  “[T]o constitute a dangerous condition under Government Code section 835, public

property must possess a physical defect which creates a substantial, as distinguished from a minor, trivial or insignificant, risk of injury. (Gov. Code § 830(a).)  Harmful conduct in and of itself cannot form a basis for recovery without a direct causal connection with the physical defect.” Bartell v. Palos Verdes Peninsula Sch. Dist. (1978) 83 Cal. App. 3d 492, 497.  “Property is not ‘dangerous’ within the meaning of the statutory scheme if the property is safe when used with due care and the risk of harm is created only when foreseeable users fail to exercise due care.”  Brenner v. City of El Cajon (2003) 113 Cal. App. 4th 434, 439 (citation omitted).

            The court construes this claim as one for dangerous condition under Gov. Code §835.

            Defendants argue that even if plaintiff had complied with the government claims presentation requirement, plaintiff has not and cannot plead and prove any physical defect with respect to the subject escalator constituting a “dangerous condition,” including that the escalator was “physically damaged, deteriorated, or defective” or that it possessed “physical characteristics in its design, location, features or relationship to its surroundings” that would create a substantial risk of injury when caused while exercising due care in a foreseeable manner. 

In any event, defendants contend, the subject escalator was in a safe and normal condition at the time of plaintiff’s accident.  See DUMF Nos. 5, 7.  Defendants present evidence explaining that Facilities Technical Services (“FTS”) at LAWA is responsible for providing maintenance services for conveyance systems, such as elevators and escalators, at LAX either directly or through its contractors.  James Andrade decl., ¶2.  The FTS is and has been responsible for the operation, maintenance, control, inspection, and supervision of the subject escalator located in the Tom Bradley International Terminal at LAX.  Id., ¶3.  The FTS regularly checks the escalators.  If there is a malfunctioning escalator, a work order will be created once it is brought to FTS’s attention.  FTS or its contractors will send a maintenance technician to inspect and repair a malfunctioning escalator when notified.  Records are kept for any such maintenance.  Id., ¶4.  Declarant searched the records at FTS with respect to the subject escalator for any work orders near the date of the incident.  The search revealed that there were no work orders for the subject escalator.  Therefore, FTS’s records show that the subject escalator was operating properly on the day of the incident.  Id., ¶5.  Also, declarant reviewed the CCTV footage of the incident, which “clearly shows the subject escalator functioning properly with passengers ascending the escalator before Ms. Gamboa steps on the escalator and falls.  Id., ¶6.  The escalator stops after a bystander presses the emergency stop buttons, which is exactly how the escalator is supposed to function.  Id., ¶7.

Defendants also contend that to the extent that plaintiff alleges that a lack of warning signs near the subject escalator constituted a “dangerous condition,” plaintiff does not allege what signage was missing or what dangers the signage should have warned.  Further, defendants argue, a mere lack of additional warning, without some physical damage or unusual characteristic of the property does not alone amount to a “dangerous condition.” 

In opposition, plaintiff contends that there is at least a triable issue of material fact as to whether plaintiff’s unfamiliarity with the existence of escalators renders the escalator, without any warning signs or directions to use, a dangerous condition of public property.  Plaintiff asserts that defendants “know that people, such as plaintiff, would be coming to the United States for the first time through its airport, and it is foreseeable that someone such as plaintiff would be unable to discern how to utilize the escalators provided absent such a warning.”

The court finds that defendants have met their burden that plaintiff cannot establish the elements for dangerous condition of public property, including that defendants had a duty to provide “warning” signs or instructions as to how to use the escalator.  Plaintiff has failed to present substantial evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact.  Rather, plaintiff presents conjecture and speculation.

 

4th cause of action for negligence (Gov. Code §§815.2, 815.4, 820(a), Civil Code §1714)

Gov. Code §815.2 states:  “(a) A public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee or his personal representative.”

The SAC alleges defendants’ employees, agents, and independent contractors, pursuant to Gov. Code §§815.2, 815.4, and 820(a) and Civil Code §1714, owed a duty of care to plaintiff to maintain, operate, patrol, service, facilitate, inspect, and/or control the subject escalator in a reasonable manner.  SAC, ¶69.  Defendants carelessly, negligently, and recklessly maintained, operated, patrolled, serviced, facilitated, inspected, and/or controlled the escalator, which was the proximate cause of plaintiff’s fall and injuries.  Id., ¶¶70-71.   

The SAC also alleges that as to public employee negligence giving rise to vicarious liability, defendants, while acting within the scope of their employment, engaged in wrongful and/or negligent and/or careless and/or reckless and/or unlawful conduct, namely in failing to provide plaintiff with medical devices when requested, thereby causing plaintiff to fall on the subject escalator.  Id., ¶79.  Defendants had a legal duty to maintain, operate, and control the escalator in a reasonably safe manner.  Defendants had a legal duty to ensure the safety of the passengers and others, including plaintiff, using services of defendants.  Id., ¶80.

Defendants argue that this claim fails because none of the statutory authorities plaintiff cites can afford relief.  As defendants argue under the 2nd cause of action, the conditions of the subject escalator is exclusively governed by Gov. Code §835.  Also, Civil Code §1714 is inapplicable because it sets forth a duty of care under ordinarily negligence only.  See Eastburn, supra, at 1183.  Defendants also contend that under Gov. Code §815.2, plaintiff must establish that an employee of the City defendants “engaged in an act or omission giving rise to that employees tort liability.”  Koussaya v. City of Stockton (2020) 54 Cal. App. 5th 909, 944.  Defendants contend that plaintiff cannot meet her burden in establishing that any employee of the City defendants owed her a duty to provide a wheelchair.  Defendants present evidence that LAWA did not and does not provide wheelchair service to passengers at LAX.  All wheelchair services at LAX are provided by the airlines directly or through their contracted wheelchair service agents.  Vivian Howell decl., ¶4.  Thus, defendants argue, no employee of the City defendants owed plaintiff a duty to provide her a wheelchair, and in the absence of any negligence by an employee, the City defendants cannot be held vicariously liable under Gov. Code §815.2 as a matter of law.

In opposition, plaintiff contends that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the involvement of City defendants in the wheelchair servicing process itself, including the extent to which they have control over and otherwise dictate the availability and means of servicing customers.  Plaintiff asserts that she requested a wheelchair as part of her airline travels, “as evidenced by her itinerary when the flight was booked for her.”  Plaintiff contends that City defendants, in their operation of LAX, provide guidelines, the ASPA Safety Module, for the ground handlers at LAX, which City defendants have approved and adopted.  Plaintiff also asserts that City defendants further provide direct services related to ground handlers and their servicing of customers, including those who have requested wheelchairs. 

The court finds that defendants have met their burden to show that plaintiff cannot establish the elements for liability under Gov. Code §815.2.  Plaintiff has not provided substantial evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact, including that an employee of the City defendants was aware of a wheelchair request for plaintiff or that City defendants had a duty to provide a wheelchair.

 

5th cause of action for general negligence 

The SAC alleges that defendants owed plaintiff a duty of care in their dealings with plaintiff.  SAC, ¶87.  On the subject date, defendants negligently and carelessly acted or failed to act when plaintiff requested and never received a wheelchair following her arrival at the subject premises.  Id., ¶88.  Defendants were informed by plaintiff in advance of her trip and fall on the subject escalator that she required wheelchair assistance, to which end no such wheelchair was ever produced and thereafter approximately 30 minutes following her request, plaintiff was demanded by defendant to leave the area she was in despite failing to provide her with the requisite services, namely a wheelchair for ambulation assistance.  Id., ¶90.

Defendants argue the same as under the 1st cause of action that this claim fails as a matter of law because it is barred by the Gov. Claims Act because it is not based on statutory authority.

The opposition does not address.

The court finds that this cause of action is based on common law and not on any statutory authority and is thus barred as a matter of law.

 

6th cause of action for negligent hiring (Gov. Code §§815.2, 815.4, 820(a), Civil Code §1714) 

The SAC alleges that plaintiff was harmed by defendants and that defendants are responsible for that harm because defendants negligently hired, supervised, or retained defendants.  SAC, ¶100.  Defendants negligently hired employees as to cause injuries and damages to plaintiff.  Id., ¶101.  On June 20, 2018, defendants’ staff failed to provide plaintiff with a wheelchair despite her advance request for it.  Defendants’ staff asked plaintiff to start walking to the escalator, despite the foreseeable risk that she would trip and fall due to defendants’ staff’s failure to provide a wheelchair to plaintiff, which did cause her to trip and fall.  Id., ¶102.  Defendants were unfit or incompetent to perform the work for which they were hired, specifically providing adequate service and assistance to plaintiff.  Id., ¶103.  Defendants are vicariously liable for the acts of their employees under Gov. Code §815.2.  Id., ¶106.

Defendants argue that this claim fails as a matter of law because plaintiff cannot show direct or vicarious liability.  As for direct liability, defendants point out that it is not based on statutory authority.  As for vicarious liability, defendants argue that plaintiff cannot show the elements.  As stated above, defendants provide evidence that LAWA did not and does not provide wheelchair service to passengers at LAX.  All wheelchair services at LAX are provided by the airlines directly or through their contracted wheelchair service agents.  Vivian Howell decl., ¶4.  Further, defendants cite to plaintiff’s responses to LAWA’s special interrogatories that she “does not have personal knowledge sufficient to answer” Special Interrogatories Nos. 21, 22, which requests that plaintiff state all facts to support her contentions in paragraphs 89 and 90, and to Demand No. 28, she responded that she did not have any documents to support her contentions in paragraph 90.

The opposition does not address.

 The court finds that defendants have met their burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements for negligent hiring.  “[T]here is no statutory basis for declaring a governmental entity liable for negligence in its hiring and supervision practices.”  de Villers v. County of San Diego (2007) 156 Cal. App. 4th 238, 253.  Plaintiff has presented no evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact as to vicarious liability.

 

7th cause of action for negligence per se

The SAC alleges that there were in force statutes, ordinances, and regulations prohibiting the conduct exhibited by defendants.  SAC, ¶113.  Plaintiff was a member of the class of persons for whose protection said statutes, ordinances, and regulations were enacted or promulgated.  Id., ¶114.  Defendants violated the traffic laws of City of LA and State of California, which constituted negligence per se, including the Dept. of Motor Vehicle’s Code.  Id., ¶115.

Defendants argue the same as under the 1st and 5th causes of action that plaintiff has not alleged any statutory liability. 

The opposition does not address.

 The court finds that this claim is based on common law and not any statutory authority and is thus barred as a matter of law.  Further, it is not a cause of action; rather it is an evidentiary presumption.

Request for continuance

“If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just.  The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.”  CCP §437c(h).

 “ ‘The affidavit or declaration in support of the continuance request must detail the specific facts that would show the existence of controverting evidence.’”  Granadino v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2015) 236 Cal. App. 4th 411, 420.  “[L]ack of diligence may be a ground for denying a request for a continuance of a summary judgment motion hearing.”  Cooksey v. Alexakis (2004) 123 Cal. App. 4th 246, 257.

In the opposition, plaintiff asserts that if the court is inclined to grant the motion for summary judgment, then plaintiff requests a continuance because of defendant City’s “failure to sit for her entire deposition and her counsel’s obstructive conduct” and that plaintiff should be allowed to complete the depositions of the City defendants’ PMK regarding training/safety, wheelchair services, and the subject escalator.

The court finds that plaintiff has not met her burden for a continuance.  There is no declaration from plaintiff’s counsel.  Further, plaintiff has not shown diligence in attempting to obtain any such evidence.

Accordingly, the motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467; CCP §437c(c).

DISCUSSION

Defendant Volaris requests summary judgment in its favor and against plaintiff as to the SAC (3rd, 4th, 5th, 6th, and 7th causes of action) on the ground that there is no triable issue of material fact and that it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

In the SAC, plaintiff alleges that this is a personal injury action arising out of a slip-and-fall accident which occurred on June 20, 2018 due to defendants’ negligence, including defendants’ failure to provide plaintiff with a wheelchair as requested and failure to properly maintain the premises in Terminal 2 at LAX.  SAC, ¶20.  On June 5, 2018, plaintiff booked or caused to be booked on her behalf with Buenos Travel Agency a non-stop flight for June 20, 2018, departing from Zacatecas, Mexico at 7:46 p.m. arriving in LA at 9:07 p.m.  Id., ¶21.  The booking indicated plaintiff requested or caused to be requested a wheelchair to tend to her physical needs and/or disabilities and/or restrictions.  Id., ¶23.  The booking indicated such wheelchair was to be part of the accommodations that defendant Buenos was including in plaintiff’s itinerary and that was to be communicated to defendants City, LAWA, and/or Volaris. Id., 24.  On June 20, 2018, between 9:30 p.m. and 11:00 p.m., plaintiff arrived at Terminal 2 in LAX.  Id., ¶25.  Upon arriving at the airport, plaintiff requested wheelchair assistance, consistent with the request already made and previously indicated to have been included in plaintiff’s travel itinerary.  Id., ¶26.  Plaintiff waited in the airplane for approximately 30 minutes after requesting wheelchair assistance.  Id., ¶27.  Plaintiff was told by a male employee to exit the plane and start walking.  Id., ¶28.  Plaintiff exited the plane with her carry-on luggage and continued toward the baggage claim and terminal exit.  Id., ¶29.  While proceeding to the baggage claim area, plaintiff attempted to use the subject escalator, falling on her first step.  Id., ¶30.  As a direct and proximate result of defendants’ failure to provide plaintiff with the requisite medical device requested, plaintiff tripped and fell causing her to fall on her right arm, resulting in a fracture to her right forearm and pain in her right leg and right arm.  Plaintiff required immediate medical attention and emergency care.  Id., ¶31.

The parties do not dispute that plaintiff fell after safely exiting the aircraft and while she was attempting to board an escalator in the terminal on her way to baggage claim and the terminal exit.  Defendant’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“DUMF”) 1.  Plaintiff attempted to use the subject escalator despite never having used one in her life.  DUMF 2.  The subject escalator was maintained and controlled by defendants City of LA and Los Angeles World Airports.  DUMF 3.

 

3rd cause of action for breach of duty of a common carrier

Civil Code §2100 states:  “A carrier of persons for reward must use the utmost care and diligence for their safe carriage, must provide everything necessary for that purpose, and must exercise to that end a reasonable degree of skill.”

The SAC alleges that Volaris operates as a common carrier.  SAC, ¶58.  Volaris contracts with SAS for the providing of wheelchair services to Volaris’ passengers.  Id., ¶59.  At the time of the incident, plaintiff was a passenger on Volaris.  Id., ¶60.  Volaris, as a common carrier, had a duty at all times to transport plaintiff as a passenger safely and to use the highest care and vigilance of a very cautious person.  Id., ¶61.  Volaris breached their duty to plaintiff in that they did not exercise the highest care and vigilance of a very cautions person when it failed to provide requested medical devices to plaintiff, who was still onboard Volaris’ airplane when the request was made.  Id., ¶63.  Defendants further breached their duty to plaintiff in that they did not use reasonable care under the circumstances to avoid causing harm to plaintiff in that it did not exercise due caution when they told plaintiff to leave the aircraft without assistance, or without the requested wheelchair, despite having knowledge, constructive or actual, of plaintiff’s need for assistance.  Id., ¶64. 

Defendant Volaris argues that this claim fails as a matter of law because it is a “duty of care,” not a cause of action.  Defendant contends that Civil Code §2100 provides a heightened standard of care that arises out of the special relationship between a common carrier and its passengers.  See Ingham v. Luxor Cab Co. (2001) 93 Cal. App. 4th 1045, 1050-51 (“As construed by the courts, Civil Code section 2100 elevates the duty of a common carrier to its passengers to the higher, affirmative duty.”) (citation omitted).  Defendant also argues that the heightened standard of care does not apply because it only applies while the passenger is in transit, and plaintiff was not in transit as she had exited the plane safely.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant has not cited to any authority that this claim is not a cause of action.

In reply, defendant reiterates its arguments.

The court finds that defendant has met its burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements, including a heightened duty, as Civil Code §2100 does not apply as plaintiff was no longer in transit.  As argued by defendant, plaintiff was delivered safely off the plane.  See also DUMF 1, where plaintiff does not dispute that she exited safely from the aircraft.  Plaintiff fails to meet her burden of raising a triable issue of material fact.  Plaintiff presents no evidence to support her allegations that plaintiff was “still onboard” when the request was made for a wheelchair.  Rather the evidence (see below) indicates that plaintiff did not ask for a wheelchair while on board the plane and before she exited.  Further, there is no evidence that a Volaris employee told plaintiff to exit the aircraft without assistance despite having knowledge of plaintiff’s purported need for assistance.

 

4th cause of action for negligence (Gov. Code §§815.2, 815.4, 820(a), Civil Code §1714)

The SAC alleges defendants’ employees, agents, and independent contractors, pursuant to Gov. Code §§815.2, 815.4, and 820(a) and Civil Code §1714, owed a duty of care to plaintiff to maintain, operate, patrol, service, facilitate, inspect, and/or control the subject escalator in a reasonable manner.  SAC, ¶69.  Defendants carelessly, negligently, and recklessly maintained, operated, patrolled, serviced, facilitated, inspected, and/or controlled the escalator, which was the proximate cause of plaintiff’s fall and injuries.  Id., ¶¶70-71.   

The SAC also alleges that as to public employee negligence giving rise to vicarious liability, defendants, while acting within the scope of their employment, engaged in wrongful and/or negligent and/or careless and/or reckless and/or unlawful conduct, namely in failing to provide plaintiff with medical devices when requested, thereby causing plaintiff to fall on the subject escalator.  Id., ¶79.  Defendants had a legal duty to maintain, operate, and control the escalator in a reasonably safe manner.  Defendants had a legal duty to ensure the safety of the passengers and others, including plaintiff, using services of defendants.  Id., ¶80.

Defendant argues that this claim fails because the Government Code does not apply to Volaris.  As for Civil Code §1714, defendant contends, plaintiff cannot prove that Volaris maintained, operated, patrolled, serviced, inspected, and/or controlled the subject escalator.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant has not met its initial burden.

The court finds that defendant has met its burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements, including any duty as to the escalator.  The SAC frames the issue on summary judgment and plaintiff alleges that LAWA and the City managed, supervised, controlled, and/or operated the escalator in Terminal 2.  SAC, ¶¶14-15.  “’A defendant moving for summary judgment may rely on the allegations contained in the plaintiff’s complaint, which constitute judicial admissions.’”  Castillo v. Barrera (2007) 146 Cal. App. 4th 1317, 1324 (citation omitted).  Also, plaintiff does not dispute that the subject escalator was maintained and controlled by defendants City of LA and Los Angeles World Airports.  DUMF 3.  As such, Volaris “’cannot be held liable for the defective or dangerous condition of property which it [does] not own, possess, or control.’”  Moses v. Roger-McKeever (2023) 91 Cal. App. 5th 172, 179 (citation omitted).  Plaintiff has failed to present substantial evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact.

 

5th cause of action for general negligence 

The SAC alleges that defendants owed plaintiff a duty of care in their dealings with plaintiff.  SAC, ¶87.  On the subject date, defendants negligently and carelessly acted or failed to act when plaintiff requested and never received a wheelchair following her arrival at the subject premises.  Id., ¶88.  Defendants were informed by plaintiff in advance of her trip and fall on the subject escalator that she required wheelchair assistance, to which end no such wheelchair was ever produced and thereafter approximately 30 minutes following her request, plaintiff was demanded by defendant to leave the area she was in despite failing to provide her with the requisite services, namely a wheelchair for ambulation assistance.  Id., ¶90.

Defendant argues that plaintiff cannot meet her burden of proving that Volaris owed a duty to provide her with a wheelchair because there was never a wheelchair request made to Volaris for plaintiff.  At her deposition on October 21, 2021, plaintiff testified that she did not book her flight and never requested wheelchair service from Volaris.  Scott Cunningham decl., Exh. 3, at 29.  Defendant also presents deposition testimony of Sonia Castillo (Volaris’ PMQ) who testified that there was no wheelchair request noted in her Passenger Name Record with Volaris and that no passenger requested a wheelchair.  She stated:  “I questioned both agents, and I asked them if the passenger had requested a wheelchair.  I personally went into the passenger’s PNR to verify whether a wheelchair had been requested or not, and it was not.  And both agents confirmed that no passenger requested a wheelchair upon exiting of the aircraft.”  Cunningham decl., Exh. 4, at 73-74.  According to the deposition testimony of defendant SAS (wheelchair service provider at LAX), James Entsminger (SAS’ PMQ) testified that there was no wheelchair request for plaintiff.  He stated, “We didn’t have any interaction with Volaris concerning that passenger because they had no record of her and neither did we.”  Cunningham decl., Exh. 5.  Defendant notes that plaintiff alleges that “defendant Buenos either failed to inform . . . Volaris . . . of the necessity of the subject disability and/or physical restriction accommodation request, or failed to confirm that such a request had been appropriately fulfilled . . . .”  SAC, at ¶¶92-93.  Defendant also presents the deposition testimony of plaintiff where she responded, “no” when asked if she “normally” used a wheelchair or cane.  She also testified that she could walk without any assistance and would walk for exercise and could walk “quite far” before needing to stop or sit down.  Cunningham decl., Exh. 3.

Defendant also argues that plaintiff cannot show that defendant’s purported breach to provide wheelchair services was the proximate cause of plaintiff’s fall because it was not foreseeable that plaintiff, who had no apparent need for a wheelchair and is fully capable of walking would fall on a normally operating escalator and that plaintiff’s decision to exit the aircraft, walk through LAX, and use the subject escalator are intervening events that broke the chain of causation.  See Johnson v. Northwest Airlines (N.D. Cal. May 5, 2010) 2010 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 139808, which determined that, “[a]ssuming for a moment that NWA failed to provide a wheelchair immediately upon plaintiff’s disembarkment, plaintiff was injured only after she waited for no more than twelve minutes before making the decisions (i) not to wait for the wheelchair; (ii) to walk, unassisted; and (iii) to use a moving walkway, a device she was unfamiliar with.  In brief, these decisions, made solely by plaintiff, broke the chain of causation as a matter of law. . . . These decisions . . . are intervening facts that break the causal chain between defendant’s conduct and plaintiff’s injury.  No reasonable juror could conclude, therefore, that plaintiff’s subsequent fall was the direct or proximate result of NWA’s conduct.” 

In opposition, plaintiff contends that plaintiff’s daughter, Azucena, arranged and paid for plaintiff’s flight, and when she booked it, she requested a wheelchair for her mother.  Plaintiff presents evidence that the reservation receipt from the travel agency states, “Incluye Silla de Rueda,” meaning “including a wheelchair.”  See Azucena Camacho decl.  Plaintiff argues that Volaris’ PMQ testimony that there was no wheelchair request on plaintiff’s Passenger Name Record “doesn’t prove that Azucena didn’t request a wheelchair for her mother; only that Volaris failed to record the request.”  Plaintiff also contends that her general [lack of] need for a wheelchair is irrelevant as to whether one was requested for her on this particular trip.  Plaintiff further argues that “it was easily foreseeable that a person for whom a wheelchair was requested, but not provided, might fall walking without one.”  Plaintiff also asserts that the cases upon which Volaris relies are federal cases.

The court finds that defendant has met its burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements of negligence, including as to breach and causation.  Plaintiff has not presented substantial evidence to raise a triable issue that Volaris was aware of the wheelchair request.

 

6th cause of action for negligent hiring (Gov. Code §§815.2, 815.4, 820(a), Civil Code §1714) 

The SAC alleges that plaintiff was harmed by defendants and that defendants are responsible for that harm because defendants negligently hired, supervised, or retained defendants.  SAC, ¶100.  Defendants negligently hired employees as to cause injuries and damages to plaintiff.  Id., ¶101.  On June 20, 2018, defendants’ staff failed to provide plaintiff with a wheelchair despite her advance request for it.  Defendants’ staff asked plaintiff to start walking to the escalator, despite the foreseeable risk that she would trip and fall due to defendants’ staff’s failure to provide a wheelchair to plaintiff, which did cause her to trip and fall.  Id., ¶102.  Defendants were unfit or incompetent to perform the work for which they were hired, specifically providing adequate service and assistance to plaintiff.  Id., ¶103.  Defendants are vicariously liable for the acts of their employees under Gov. Code §815.2.  Id., ¶106.

Defendant contends that in response to defendant’s special interrogatories, she stated, “[c]alls for expert opinion” when asked to state all facts that support her contentions in paragraphs 89 and 90 as to whether Volaris’ supervisory or administrative employee breached his or her duty or whether she was injured as a direct legal cause of Volaris’ negligent hiring.  Cunningham decl., Exh. 6 (Response to Special Interrogatories, Nos. 27, 28).  Defendant also asserts that plaintiff did not produce any documents to support her claim.  Cunningham decl., Exh. 7 (Response to Request for Production, No. 32).  Defendant further points out that during Volaris’ PMK deposition, none of the designated categories involved Volaris’ hiring or retention policies of any of its employees.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant has not met its initial burden.

The court finds that defendant has met its burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements for negligent hiring.  Plaintiff has not met her burden of raising a triable issue of material fact.  Plaintiff does not even identify any Volaris employee that purportedly acted negligently or caused her injury.  She presents no evidence that any Volaris employee was aware of a wheelchair request.

 

7th cause of action for negligence per se

The SAC alleges that there were in force statutes, ordinances, and regulations prohibiting the conduct exhibited by defendants.  SAC, ¶113.  Plaintiff was a member of the class of persons for whose protection said statutes, ordinances, and regulations were enacted or promulgated.  Id., ¶114.  Defendants violated the traffic laws of City of LA and State of California, which constituted negligence per se, including the Dept. of Motor Vehicle’s Code.  Id., ¶115.

Defendant argues that “negligence per se” is an evidentiary presumption and does not state an independent cause of action.  Defendant further argues that plaintiff cannot produce any evidence, admissible or otherwise, of defendant’s violation of “traffic laws” or prove that there is a substantial probability that plaintiff’s injury was caused by Volaris’ violation of these statutes.

In the opposition, plaintiff agrees that this claim is not a separate cause of action.

The court finds that defendant has met its burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements as “negligence per se” is not a cause of action.  See Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Ctr. (2006) 140 Cal. App. 4th 1256, 1285 (“[T]o apply negligence per se is not to state an independent cause of action.  The doctrine does not provide a private right of action for violation of a statute.”) (citation omitted); Turner v. Seterus, Inc. (2018) 27 Cal. App. 5th 516, 534 (“’[T]he doctrine of negligence per se is not a separate cause of action, but creates an evidentiary presumption that affects the standard of care in a cause of action for negligence.’”) (citation omitted).  Further, plaintiff has not presented any evidence, much less substantial, to raise a triable issue of material fact.

Accordingly, the motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

            Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
