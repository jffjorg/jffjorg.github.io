DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV38374    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: 73

10/03/2023

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

SHAHNAZ AMIRTALESH v. ROCHELLE STERLING, et al. (19STCV38374)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Shahnaz Amirtalesh (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Krista Dawkins (Pyka Lenhardt Schnaider Dawkins)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO VACATE RULING ON DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT (filed 09/06/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Ruling on Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

On October 23, 2019, Plaintiff Shahnaz Amirtalesh (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Rochelle H. Sterling (“Sterling”), Beverly Hills Properties (“BHP”), The Sterling Family Trust, Susan Robman and Does 1-50 (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff’s tenancy at 240 N. Crescent Drive, Apt. 107, Beverly Hills, CA 90210 (the “Apartment”).  In her complaint, Plaintiff alleges that the Apartment landlord, BHP, failed to maintain sewage pipes in the building which caused a leak in her shower drain in October 24, 2018; that BHP delayed in granting her request to replace flood-damaged carpeting in her Apartment with laminate floors as a reasonable accommodation for her disability stemming from lung cancer; that BHP did not properly enforce COVID-19 restrictions regarding the use of the Apartment swimming pool; and further, that Apartment manager, Susan Robman, harassed and discriminated against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s religious beliefs.   

 

In the summer of 2019, Plaintiff filed complaints with the County of Los Angeles Department of Public Health (“DPH”) and the City of Beverly Hills regarding her apartment.  On September 5, 2019, the City of Beverly Hills issued a notice of violations to BHP.  On September 10, 2019, DPH issued an Official Inspection Report for violations regarding wall/ceiling floor and faucet fixtures. 

  

On October 23, 2019, Plaintiff filed her complaint against Defendants. Sterling is the owner of BHP and the Apartment and the trustee of the Sterling Family Trust.  On August 7, 2020, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging eight causes of action.  On or about October 20, 2021, the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action in the SAC without leave to amend.  The remaining causes of action are as follows: 

C/A 1: Breach of Warranty of Habitability 
C/A 2: Negligence  
C/A 3: Nuisance  
C/A 4: Violation of Civil Code § 1942.4 
C/A 6: Violation of the California Fair Employment and Housing Act  
C/A 7: Violation of the California Unruh Civil Rights Act 
C/A 8: Breach of Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment 

 

On March 17, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Defendant Susan Robman from the case.

 

On January 24, 2023, the Court granted Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth, sixth and eighth causes of action. The motion was denied as to the first, second and third causes of action.

 

On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Ruling on Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication, arguing:

·       Defendants’ motion for summary judgment was meritless, and Defendants did not include Rochelle Sterling as an individual defendant in the motion for summary judgment

·       Therefore, the Court’s ruling on the motion was defective

 

Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff fails to identify statutory authority for her motion to vacate

·       Plaintiff’s request for relief resembles a Motion for Reconsideration. Plaintiff’s deadline to request reconsideration of 1/24/23 ruling has already lapsed, and Plaintiff has not identified any new facts, circumstances, or law.

·       The Court has also already dispensed with Plaintiff’s argument that Ms. Sterling did not participate in the motion for summary judgment/adjudication. On March 15, 2024, this Court stated that Defendant Rochelle Sterling has participated in this case and in Defendants’ motion for summary judgment/adjudication

 

Plaintiff filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s motion is not a motion for reconsideration.

·       The motions and rulings were based on fraud. The judgment should be set aside based on fraud under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(d)(3).

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves for an order vacating the Court’s January 24, 2023 ruling on Defendants’ motion for summary judgment/adjudication.

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that in her motion, Plaintiff does not identify on what statutory basis she is moving to vacate the Court’s order. In her reply, Plaintiff does not cite to statutory authority that is binding on this Court, instead Plaintiff cites to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(d)(3), which provides for relief from an order based on fraud. Thus, not only does Plaintiff fail to identify applicable statutory authority that is binding on this Court for her request for relief, but Plaintiff fails to establish any alleged fraud.

 

Plaintiff argues the ruling on Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication was defective because Defendants did not include Rochelle Sterling as an individual defendant in the motion. However, the Court already addressed this in its March 15, 2024 order, stating:

 

“As to the pleading issue raised by the court on February 23, 2024, after her demurrers were adjudicated, Defendant Rochelle Sterling answered the second amended complaint on October 26, 2020. A DBA designation is fictitious and not a separate entity. (See Weil & Brown et al., Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) sec. 6:59.1; Pinkerton’s, Inc. v Superior Court (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1349 [“Doing business under another name does not create an entity distinct from the person operating the business.”].) Therefore, the DBA designation on certain pleadings is fictitious in relation to Rochelle Sterling and is disregarded. Defendant Rochelle Sterling has thus participated in this case and in, for example, Defendants’ motion for summary judgment/adjudication filed on June 16, 2022.”

 

(Court’s 3/15/24 Minute Order, p. 2.) Plaintiff fails to establish any basis for the relief she is seeking. Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion to vacate the Court’s order is denied.

 

CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Ruling on Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

ERIC KAUFMAN v. LOCKTON COMPANIES, LLC, et al. (22STCV23460) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff: Steven Brennan (St. John, Wallace, Brennan & Folan LLP); Debra Fischer (Morgan, Lewis & Bockius LLP)

Counsel for Defendants:  Russell Falconer, James Tsouvalas (Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher LLP) 

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO LIFT STAY AND FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 7/18/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Lift Stay and For Leave to File Second Amended Complaint.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Eric Kaufman brings this action for declaratory relief against Defendants Lockton Investment Advisors, LLC ("LIA"), Lockton Investment Securities, LLC, f/k/a Lockton Financial Advisors, LLC ("LIS"), and Lockton Companies, LLC - Pacific Series ("Pacific Series", collectively "Lockton").

 

LIA provides investment advisory and related services, LIS buys and sells securities as a registered broker dealer, and Pacific Series provides insurance and risk management services. (FAC, ¶ 10.) Kaufman became a producer member of LIA in approximately July 2007 and a producer member in LIS and Pacific Series in approximately July 2008. (FAC, ¶ 23.) To become a producer member of LIA, LIS, and Pacific Services, Kaufman paid a $200 capital contribution for a single "Producer Unit" in each entity. (FAC, ¶ 23.) As of May 1, 2016, Plaintiff entered into an “Amended and Restated Member Agreement" with Pacific Series and with LIS and, as of May 1, 2016, a "Second Amended and Restated Member Agreement” with LIA (collectively the "Member Agreements"). (FAC, ¶ 16.)

 

The Member Agreements include nonsolicitation covenants. (FAC, ¶ 20.) The Member Agreements include Missouri choice of law and Missouri forum selection clauses. The Member Agreements require Kaufman to adjudicate disputes in the federal court in Kansas City, Missouri or in the Circuit Court of Jackson County, Missouri. (FAC, ¶ 18.) he Member Agreements for LIA, LIS, and Pacific Series require Plaintiff to provide thirty days written notice of termination. (FAC, ¶ 22.)

 

Kaufman withdrew from and terminated his membership in LIA, LIS, and Pacific Series on July 18, 2022. (FAC, ¶ 27.) Kaufman subsequently accepted employment with Alliant, a California-based competitor of Lockton. (FAC, ¶ 28.) At Alliant, Kaufman will provide products and services that are directly competitive with the products or services of Lockton. (Ibid.) 

In the first amended complaint, Kaufman asserts a single cause of action for declaratory relief, seeking a judicial declaration that the nonsolicitation covenants, Missouri choice of law and forum selection clauses, and notice requirements are void and unenforceable as a violation of Business and Professions Code Sections 16600 and California public policy. Kaufman alleges: Plaintiff desires a judicial declaration that the enforcement of the Missouri Choice of Law and Missouri Forum Section Provisions would violate California's fundamental public policy in favor of workplace mobility and competition. Plaintiff also desires a judicial declaration that the Notice Provision, Member Agreements Non-Competition Covenants and the December 2021 Non-Competition Covenants are facially void under Business and Professions Code Section 16600 and California public policy in favor of workplace mobility and open competition are thereby unenforceable as to Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 40.) 

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Lift Stay and Leave to File Second Amended Complaint, arguing:

·       Plaintiff seeks to have the current stay order lifted, but only so as to allow him to plead additional, but related, causes of action that largely grow out of and directly relate to the same unlawful noncompete covenants and contracts that were the basis of his original Declaratory Relief claim.

·       The Motion Should Be Granted Because Leave to Amend Was Timely Sought and Because Defendants Will Not Be Prejudiced if the Motion Is Granted.

 

Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff provides no good reason to lift the stay

·       Plaintiff’s intentional delay in bringing his additional claims warrants denying leave to amend.

·       Defendants are prejudiced by the proposed amendments.

·       The proposed amendments are futile.

 

Plaintiff filed a reply maintaining that the motion should be granted.

 

II. DISCUSSION

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” 

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.) Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. 

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Plaintiff seeks to lift the stay in this case to file a Second Amended Complaint.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend complies with Rules 3.1324(a) and (b) of the California Rules of Court. Plaintiff included a copy of the proposed Second Amended Complaint, and has identified the proposed amendments to be added. (Folan Decl., Motion, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff also provided a separate declaration specifying the effect of the amendments, why they are necessary and proper, and the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Folan Decl., ¶¶¿3-6.) 

 

In opposition, Defendants argue Plaintiff intentionally delayed in bringing the additional claims.

 

However, Plaintiff asserts that he limited the relief sought in his initial complaints exclusively to declaratory relief so as to be eligible for trial setting preference pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 1062.3. (Folan Decl., ¶ 4.) This court granted such preference and a trial date of February 13, 2024 was set. (Ibid.) On December 13, 2023 the Court issued its Order denying Defendants' Motion to Dismiss and Stay Proceedings ("Order"), ruling that the Los Angeles Superior Court is the appropriate forum in which Plaintiff is to adjudicate his disputes with Defendants. (Ibid.) Plaintiff argues if he had moved to file a Second Amended Complaint prior to the Court's Order, Defendants likely would have sought to rely on the unlawful forum selection clause and improperly filed a motion to dismiss. (Ibid.) Further, the Court subsequently granted Defendants’ motion to continue the trial date, thereby denying Plaintiff the expedited trial that caused him to limit his claims herein to a request for declaratory relief. (Folan Decl., ¶ 5.) In addition, Plaintiff argues the additional claims were triggered by events that occurred after this action was filed. Plaintiff also contends it is seeking to amend because certain claims set forth in the proposed Second Amended Complaint may become time-barred due to the applicable statute of limitations in or around November 2024. The Court finds that Plaintiff has established good cause to bring the claims now and there has not been unreasonable delay.

 

Defendants also argue in opposition that they will be prejudiced by the proposed amendments because the scope of litigation will be expanded and amendment will be futile.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants will not be prejudiced as there is no trial date currently sit. Further, Plaintiff asserts that if a plaintiff wants to expand the scope of a complaint to add additional legitimate legal theories, it is proper to do so if discovery is still open. The Court does not find Defendants arguments that amendment would be prejudicial or futile persuasive.

 

Based on the foregoing and the judicial policy favoring amendment, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Lift Stay and For Leave to File Second Amended Complaint.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Lift Stay and For Leave to File Second Amended Complaint.

MARTINEZ v. FARMERS FINANCIAL SOLUTIONS, LLC, et al. (22STCV40216)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Marcelo Dieguez (Diefer Law Group, PC)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party: Clayton Hix (Hill, Farrer & Burrill LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S motion FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed 08/07/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees incurred on appeal in the amount $18,438.00. The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for costs incurred on appeal in the amount of $412.20.

 

I. BACKGROUND

  

On December 27, 2022, Plaintiff Citlali Lopez Mayte Martinez filed this employment action against Defendants Simplified Labor Staffing Solutions, Inc. (“Simplified Labor”) and William Espinoza (“Espinoza”).  

 

The Complaint asserts the following causes of action: 

1.      Discrimination in Violation of Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12940 et seq. (FEHA); 

2.      Harassment in Violation of the FEHA;

3.      Retaliation in Violation of the FEHA;

4.      Failure to Prevent Harassment, Discrimination, and Retaliation in Violation of Government Code § 12940(K);

5.      Negligent Supervision and Retention;

6.      Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress;

7.      Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy; and

8.      Failure to Permit Inspection of Personnel and Payroll Records.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about January 11, 2022, Plaintiff began working for Defendants as a laborer at Defendants’ warehouse in Lynwood, California. Plaintiff was a full-time, non-exempt employee, and performed all of Plaintiff’s job duties satisfactorily before Defendants wrongfully terminated Plaintiff’s employment on or about April 20, 2022, in retaliation for making protected FEHA complaints about supervisor Espinoza’s sexual harassment of Plaintiff.

 

On July 11, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees stating that Plaintiff had not established that remittitur was issued. (Court’s 07/11/24 Minute Order.)

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Attorney Fees, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves for an order awarding her attorney fees and costs in the amount of $18,850.20 from Defendant and their attorneys, pursuant to the appellate court decision dated May 15, 2024.

·       Defendants filed an unsuccessful motion to compel arbitration. The present sexual harassment case was clearly barred by the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 (“EFASASH”) and they filed their motion anyways.

·       The appellate court denied the appeal and affirmed the trial court ruling.

·       The appellate court granted costs on appeal to Plaintiff.

·       California public policy supports Defendants paying for the delay they caused by covering Plaintiff’s Attorney Fees and costs. SB 365 was later passed in January 2024 to stop attorneys like those from Hill Farrer in this case from strategically bringing frivolous appeals as a delay tactic.

·       Plaintiff’s counsel’s rates and hours billed are reasonable.

 

On June 27, 2024, Defendant Simplified Labor filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Here, Plaintiff contends she is entitled to attorney’s fees as the “prevailing party,” pursuant to Government Code section 12965(b). However, Plaintiff cannot avail herself of that statute because the case is still pending and there has been no adjudication of the merits.

·       Plaintiff has not properly requested reimbursement for costs, and the time to do so has expired. Plaintiff has not filed or served a verified memorandum of costs under rule 3.1700 and more than 40 days has passed since the remittitur was issued on August 6, 2024.

 

Plaintiff did not file a reply.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

“As a general rule, the prevailing party may recover certain statutory costs incurred in the litigation up to and including entry of judgment. [Citations.] These costs may include attorney fees, if authorized by contract, statute . . . or law. [Citation.] . . . attorney fees require a separate noticed motion. [Citations.]” (Lucky United Props. Inv., Inc. v. Lee (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 125, 137.) This motion may be brought: (1) after judgment or dismissal, for fees incurred “up to and including the rendition of judgment in the trial court--including attorney’s fees on an appeal before the rendition of judgment…”; and (2) on an interim basis, upon remittitur of appeal, of only fees incurred on appeal. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rules 3.1702(b)-(c).)

 

Under California Rules of Court, rule 8.278(a)(1), the party prevailing in the Court of Appeal in a civil case (other than a juvenile case) is entitled to costs on appeal.  The prevailing party is the respondent if the Court of Appeal affirms the judgment without modification or dismisses the appeal. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.278(a)(2).) 

 

“Unless the court orders otherwise, an award of costs neither includes attorney’s fees on appeal nor precludes a party from seeking them under rule 3.1702.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.278.) 

 

However, “[w]here attorney’s fees are authorized by statute they are authorized on appeal as well as in the trial court.” (People ex rel. Cooper v. Mitchell Brothers’ Santa Ana Theater (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 378, 387.) 

 

B.      Whether Plaintiff Can Seek Attorneys’ Fees

 

Plaintiff moves for an order awarding her attorney fees incurred on appeal in the amount $18,438.00 from Defendants and their attorneys, pursuant to the appellate court decision dated May 15, 2024.

 

On April 7, 2023, Defendants filed a motion to compel arbitration pursuant to an arbitration agreement purportedly signed by Plaintiff. On July 13, 2023, this Court denied Defendants’ motion to compel Plaintiff’s sex harassment case to arbitration, finding that the case was barred by the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 (“EFASASH”). Defendant appealed the trial court’s order. On May 15, 2024, the appellate court denied the appeal and affirmed the trial court ruling. (Avila Decl., Ex. A.)

 

Rule 3.1702 allows a motion for attorney fees to be brought upon remittitur of appeal of the fees incurred on appeal. On August 6, 2024, the clerk of the Court of Appeal entered on the record, and inserted in the remittitur, a judgment awarding costs to the prevailing party, stating “Respondent is awarded her costs of appeal.” (Avila Decl., Ex. C.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff is not entitled to attorneys fees because the case is still pending and there has been no adjudication of the merits. Defendants cite to cases, including Frog Creek Partners, LLC v. Vance Brown, Inc. (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 515 (Frog Creek), in support of this argument. In Frog Creek, two parties entered into a contract with an arbitration clause and a separate attorneys fees provision. (Id. at 520.) A dispute arose, and defendant petitions to compel arbitration. (Id.) The Court of Appeal concluded that a plaintiff that defeats the petition to compel arbitration is not entitled to recover attorney’s fees because under Civil Code section 1717, “there may only be one prevailing party entitled to attorney fees on a given contract in a given lawsuit.” (Id.) The Frog Creek court considered the holding in Acosta, and rejected the rationale that “specific contract language may justify a separate attorney fee award.” (Id. at 544.) According to the Frog Creek court, the rationale in Acosta “threatens to significantly undermine” the policy behind Civil Code section 1717. (Id. at 544-545.) 

 

However, unlike the plaintiff in Frog Creek, Plaintiff here is not seeking attorney’s fees under Civil Code section 1717, but under Govt. Code section 12940. California’s Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”) (Govt. Code section 12940 et. seq.) provides for attorney’s fees to the prevailing party in suits brought for employment discrimination. (See Chavez v. City of Los Angeles (2010) 47 Cal.4th 970, 976) (“In any action brought under the California Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) (Gov. Code, § 12900 et seq.). Government Code section 12965, subdivision (b), grants the trial court discretion to award attorney fees to a prevailing party. This statute has been interpreted to mean that in a FEHA action a trial court should ordinarily award attorney fees to a prevailing plaintiff unless special circumstances would render a fee award unjust. (Young v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1474; Steele v. Jensen Instrument Co. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 326, 331.)”) Therefore, the Court does not find Defendants’ arguments persuasive. Defendants do not provide authority establishing that Plaintiff is not able to recover attorney fees under the FEHA for the costs incurred on appeal of the Court’s order. Therefore, the Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to seek reasonable attorney fees.

 

C.      Reasonableness of the Attorneys’ Fees

 

In determining what fees are reasonable, California courts apply the “lodestar” approach. (See, e.g., Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.) This inquiry “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) From there, the “[t]he lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id.) Relevant factors include: “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) An attorney’s own billing rates carry a presumption of reasonableness. (Mandel v. Lackner (1979) 92 Cal.App.3d 747, 761)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel asserts her hourly rate of $878 is a reasonable rate and within the range of current market rates in Los Angeles for attorneys of similar experience. (Avila Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court finds that the rate requested by Plaintiff’s counsel is reasonable. Counsel’s declaration sufficiently attests to her experience and the reasonableness of the rates. (Avila Decl., ¶¶ 3, 11.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel also states she expended 21 hours on the appeal in this case. (Avila Decl., ¶ 9.) Here, the Court has reviewed all of the claimed hours and finds the hours reasonable.

 

Defendants have not opposed the reasonableness of the fees sought in the motion.

 

Therefore, the total sum of the attorney’s fees is $18,438.00 (21 x $878/hr.).

 

D.     Costs

 

Plaintiff moves for an order awarding her costs incurred on appeal in the amount $412.20 from Defendant and their attorneys.

 

However, Defendants argue Plaintiff has not properly requested reimbursement for costs.

 

“Within 40 days after issuance of the remittitur, a party claiming costs awarded by a reviewing court must serve and file in the superior court a verified memorandum of costs under rule 3.1700.” (Cal. R. Ct. 8.278(c)(1); see Jud. Coun. Form MC-012 (“Form Adopted for Mandatory Use”).)

 

Here, while Plaintiff attached a memorandum of costs to her motion, Plaintiff has not filed a verified memorandum of costs pursuant to Cal. R. Ct. 8.278(c)(1). Therefore, the Court does not grant Plaintiff’s request for costs on this ground.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees incurred on appeal in the amount $18,438.00. The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for costs incurred on appeal in the amount of $412.20.

ATLAS CAPITAL, INC. v. TEMPLE POTEAT (23STCV20168)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Joshua Shoumer (Shoumer Law Office)

Counsel for Defendant/ opposing party: Temple Poteat (Pro Per)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT (filed on 07/12/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 23, 2023, Plaintiff Atlas Capital, Inc. brought this breach of contract action against Defendant Temple Poteat.

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff and Defendant entered into two loan agreements, which Defendant subsequently defaulted on. (Compl., p. 4.) Defendant had filed for Chapter 13 Bankruptcy, which was ultimately dismissed on August 16, 2023. (Ibid.) Plaintiff is seeking balance due on both loan agreements, interest, late fees and attorney fees. (Compl., p. 3.)

 

Defendant filed an answer on September 29, 2023.

 

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment, arguing:

·       The facts are undisputed, Defendant signed two promissory notes for Loan Numbers 100948 and 100964. In late 2022 Defendant ultimately breached both promissory notes by failing to make the required scheduled payments pursuant to the note.

·       Defendant only merely disputes Plaintiff’s request for damages but has refused to respond to discovery, failing to provide evidentiary support to refute Plaintiff’s claimed damages.

 

Defendant did not file an opposition.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

 “A party may move for summary adjudication of issues, either by itself or as an alternative to summary judgment. If it appears that the proof supports the granting of the motion for summary adjudication as to some but not all the issues involved in the action, or that one or more of the issues raised by a claim is admitted, or that one or more of the issues raised by a defense is conceded, the court shall, by order, specify that those issues are without substantial controversy. Moreover, upon a motion for summary adjudication, the court shall, by written order or oral order recorded verbatim, specify those issues raised by the motion for summary adjudication as to which there exists a material, triable controversy, and shall specifically refer to the evidence which establishes a triable issue of fact regarding each of those issues ....”  (Cal. Code. Civ. Proc. § 437c(f)).   

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment/summary adjudication bears the initial burden of showing that “there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Plaintiff's¿initial¿burden¿of proof in moving for summary judgment, however, d[oes] not include disproving any affirmative defenses asserted by defendants.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co.¿(2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564, citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853 (“Aguilar”) [“summary judgment law in this state no longer requires a¿plaintiff¿moving for summary judgment to disprove any defense asserted by the defendant as well as prove each element of his own cause of action.... All that the¿plaintiff¿need do is to ‘prove[ ] each element of the cause of action’ ”])  Once Plaintiff meets its initial burden of proving each element of the cause of action, “the burden shifts to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action¿or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

 

The California Supreme Court has recently confirmed that the purpose of the 1992 and 1993 amendments to the summary judgment statute was “‘to liberalize the granting of [summary judgment] motions.’” (Perry v. Bakewell Hawthorne, LLC (2017) 2 Cal.5th 536, 542.)  It is no longer called a “disfavored” remedy.  “Summary judgment is now seen as a ‘particularly suitable means to test the sufficiency’ of the plaintiff’s or defendant’s case.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment of the Complaint’s only cause of action for breach of contract.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff made two loans to Defendant. Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a loan agreement identified as Loan No 100948 on 9/28/2021. The loan was secured by a commercial tractor identified as a 2015 Freightliner (VIN ending GW3505). The principal loan amount was $25,000.00. Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a second loan agreement identified as Loan No 100964 on 1/4/2022. The loan was secured by a commercial tractor identified as a 2016 Freightliner (VIN ending GH0616). The principal loan amount was $25,023.00. Defendant defaulted on the loan, causing Plaintiff to repossess the collateral subject to Loan No 100948. Defendant subsequently filed Chapter 13 Bankruptcy, ceasing Plaintiff's collection efforts. During Bankruptcy negotiations between counsel for Plaintiff 'and Defendant, Defendant surrendered possession to the collateral subject to Loan No 100964. Both vehicles were ultimately sold and sale proceeds applied to their respective accounts. The remaining balances on each loan are as follows: (1) Loan No 100948: $8,628.55 as of 8/22/2023 (2) Loan No 100964: $30,055.55 as of 8/22/2023.

 

Plaintiff argues that there exist no disputed issues of material fact regarding any elements of Defendant’s breach. In support, Plaintiff submits copies of the first and second loan agreements signed by Plaintiff and Defendant. (Shoumer Decl., Exs. 1, 3.) The agreements provide in relevant part that “If you fail to pay us what you owe when it is due or when we demand you pay, you will be in default of this Agreement.” (Id., Ex. 1, p. 2; Ex. 3, p. 2.) Plaintiff also submits copies of the Payment Statements for the first and second loans, which show that Defendant failed to make the monthly required payments. (Id., Ex. 2; Ex. 4.) In addition, Plaintiff submits copies of the Sale Credit Statements showing credit that was applied to the outstanding balance on both loans as a result of the sale of the vehicles that were collateral to the loans. (Id., Ex. 5; Ex. 6.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has met its burden. Plaintiff provided a copy of the loan agreements showing the existence of a contract between the parties and Plaintiff provided evidence attesting to Plaintiff’s performance of the agreements and Defendant’s default under the agreements by failing to make required loan payments. The burden shifts to Defendant. Yet, Defendant did not file an opposition and did not provide evidence of a disputed material issue of fact. “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion…is meritorious.”¿(Ibid.) Thus, Defendant fails to carry her burden.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

MINGZHE ZHAO v. MI IN FASHION, INC. dba CES FEMME, et al. (24STCV08148)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Yong Bom Lee (Lee & Associates, Law Office, APC)

Counsel for Defendants:  Pro Per

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO STRIKE DEFENDANTS MI IN FASHION, INC., HYUN S. LEE, AND YOUNG MI LEE’S ANSWERS (filed 5/31/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendants Mi In Fashion, Inc., Hyun S. Lee, and Young Mi Lee’s Answers is GRANTED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff Mingzhe Zhao filed this action against Defendants Mi In Fashion, Inc. dba Ces Femme; Hyun S. Lee aka Tommy Lee; and Young Mi Lee.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Written Agreement

2.      Goods Sold and Delivered

3.      Account Stated

4.      Open Book Account

5.      False Promises

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff is an assignee of a commercial claim of Sinsunghui Fashion Co., LTD. (“Assignor” or “Sinsunghui”’) to collect unpaid balance of clothing products sold to Defendants. Sinsunghui is a garment manufacturing and/or exporting company established and existing under the laws of China, with its principal place of business in China.

 

On May 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to strike Defendants’ Answers, arguing:

·       Defendants’ answers are unverified and contain general denials. Defendants cannot have a general denial to a verified complaint.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer 

 

Here, counsel for Plaintiff submitted a declaration stating they made several attempts to call Defendants and sent Defendants a letter outlining the alleged deficiencies in the Answers, but counsel did not receive a response from Defendants. The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligation. Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

“If the complaint is verified, unless the complaint is subject to Article 2 (commencing with Section 90) of Chapter 5.1 of Title 1 of Part 1, the denial of the allegations shall be made positively or according to the information and belief of the defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.30(d).) 

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading…(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) 

 

“When the complaint is verified, the answer shall be verified.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 446(a).) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 431.30(f) states in pertinent part, “[t]he denials of the allegations controverted may be stated by reference to specific paragraphs or parts of the complaint; or by express admission of certain allegations of the complaint with a general denial of all of the allegations not so admitted; or by denial of certain allegations upon information and belief, or for lack of sufficient information or belief, with a general denial of all allegations not so denied or expressly admitted.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.30(f).) “The defenses shall be separately stated, and the several defenses shall refer to the causes of action which they are intended to answer, in a manner by which they may be intelligibly distinguished.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.30(g).) 

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to strike Defendants Mi In Fashion, Inc., Hyun S. Lee, and Young Mi Lee’s Answers on the grounds that the Answers are unverified and consist of only general denials.

 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a verified Complaint. On May 14, 2024, Defendants Mi In Fashion, Inc., Hyun S. Lee, and Young Mi Lee filed Answers to the Complaint.

 

It appears from the Answers that the defenses are separately stated and refer to the cause of action they are intended to answer. However, the Answers do not provide specific denials to allegations by referencing specific paragraphs or parts of the Complaint. Furthermore, the Answers do not have the required statements of verifications. Defendants did not file an opposition.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendants Mi In Fashion, Inc., Hyun S. Lee, and Young Mi Lee’s Answers is GRANTED with leave to amend.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendants Mi In Fashion, Inc., Hyun S. Lee, and Young Mi Lee’s Answers is GRANTED with 20 days leave to amend.
