DEPARTMENT 55 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV45457    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS:  Motion of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Onni Contracting (California), Inc. and Cross-Defendant RLI Insurance Company for Summary Adjudication. Motion of Defendant/Cross-Complainant ONNI CONTRACTING CALIFORNIA), INC. to Compel Deposition.

BACKGROUND

In this case, PRECISION CONSTRUCTION GROUP, INC. (“Precision”) seeks damages against ONNI CONTRACTING (CALIFORNIA), INC. (“Onni”) based on Onni’s alleged breach of a written subcontract between the parties and Onni’s alleged failure to pay Precision for products it provided to Onni, all in connection with Precision’s work as a subcontractor at the real property located at 6933 Santa Monica Blvd., Los Angeles (the “Property”). Precision, which recorded a mechanics lien against the Property, has also sued RLI INSURACE BOND (“RLI”) to recover from the lien release bond the amount stated in the mechanics lien. Precision alleges the following causes of action against Onni: 1) Breach of Written Contract; 2) Goods, Wares and Merchandise Sold and Delivered; 3) Open Book Account; and 4) Account Stated. Precision alleges a claim against RLI for Enforcement of Mechanics Lien Release Bond.

Onni and RLI (referred to collectively as “Defendants”) seek summary adjudication as to four issues. Onni also moves to compel the deposition attendance of the person most qualified (PMQ) of Precision and monetary sanctions. Precision opposes both motions. The Court addresses both motions in this tentative ruling.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

            Summary Adjudication

Generally, a party may only move for summary adjudication when the motion completely resolves a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty. Code Civ. Proc., §437c(f)(1).  

In moving for summary adjudication, a “defendant . . . has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).) Once the defendant has satisfied that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff “to show, by responsive separate statement and admissible evidence, that triable issues of fact exist.” Ostayan v. Serrano Reconveyance Co. (2000) 77 Cal. App. 4th 1411, 1418, disapproved on other grounds by Black Sky Cap., LLC v. Cobb (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 156, 165; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).

            Deposition

Parties are restricted to one deposition, absent a court order based upon a showing of good cause. Code Civ. Proc., §2025.610(a).  Deponents may attend depositions by remote electronic means. Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.310).

EVIDENTIARY RULINGS

Precision’s evidentiary objection to the Declaration of Jeffrey S. Flashman is overruled.

Defendants’ evidentiary objections are sustained as to numbers 17, 19 and 20, and are otherwise overruled. See, e.g., Gribaldo v. Agrippina Versicherunges A. G. (1970) 3 Cal.3d 434, 443 (parol evidence can be admissible to show contract meaning and intent), Howard Ent., Inc. v. Kudrow (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1102, 1114 (parol evidence can include custom and word usage in the industry), and Benavidez v. San Jose Police Dept. (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 853, 861-862 (ambiguous deposition admissions may be contradicted by declarations). 

ANALYSIS

            Summary Adjudication

                        Procedure

Defendants seek summary adjudication of four issues related to Precision’s claims: (1) Precision cannot recover $603,343,13 in material cost escalation as part of its damages; (2) Precision has no evidentiary support for the alleged material cost escalation it seeks as part of its damages; (3) Precision cannot recover $319,158.35 for concrete pour costs as part of its damages; and (4) Precision’s mechanics lien is void. Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(f)(1) does not generally permit summary adjudication of damages claims unless doing so disposes of an entire cause of action. DeCastro West Chodorow & Burns, Inc.  v.  Superior Court (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 410, 422. “[A] party may present a motion for summary adjudication challenging a separate and distinct wrongful act even though combined with other wrongful acts alleged in the same cause of action.” Edward Fineman Co. v. Sup. Ct. (1998) 66 Cal. App 4th 1110, 1118 (involving separate checks). The determination of whether a complaint contains more than one cause of action for these purposes “depends on whether it alleges invasion of one or more primary rights.” Hindin v. Rust (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1247, 1257.  

There is an exception to the general rule that a summary adjudication motion must dispose of an entire cause of action or claim for damages. “[A] party may move for summary adjudication of a legal issue or a claim for damages other than punitive damages that does not completely dispose of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, or an issue of duty…. This motion may be brought only upon the stipulation of the parties whose claims or defenses are put at issue by the motion and a prior determination and order by the court that the motion will further the interests of judicial economy, by reducing the time to be consumed in trial, or significantly increase the ability of the parties to resolve the case by settlement.” Code Civ. Proc., §437c (t). (Underscoring added.)

Here, the Complaint’s four causes of action seek relief based on Onni’s alleged breach of the subcontract that within it contemplates change orders. Defendants’ motion seeks to adjudicate less than that primary right asserted by Onni by requesting adjudication of separate claimed damages as to the subcontract. But adjudication of these issues will not result in the complete disposal of the claims. Moreover, there is no stipulation and order allowing the statutory exception of adjudicating damages issues or other issues that will not completely dispose of any of Precision’s claims in this case. Thus, the motion is not permitted by statute as interpreted by case law.

Therefore, the motion is denied as a matter of procedure.

                        Discovery Continuance

While the Court denies the motion for procedural reasons, it also finds that, even considering the merits of the motion, summary adjudication is not appropriate. First, Precision argues that substantial discovery is outstanding that would relate to opposing the motion for summary adjudication, including deposing Onni’s former project manager and a subcontractor that worked on the project, and subpoenaing documents from Precision’s materials supplier. Sorrentino Decl., ¶¶ 14-16.

“‘If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication ... that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, then be presented, the court shall deny the motion, or order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had....’” Krantz v. BT Visual Images, L.L.C.  (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 164, 175, fn.7 (quoting Code Civ. Proc., §437c(h)). (Underscoring added.) Precision has adequately shown that this discovery, which it delayed to pursue settlement negotiations, may uncover facts to support Precision’s claims. This is another reason to deny the motion.

                        Merits

Issue 1: Precision’s Claim for Material Cost Increases Is Precluded by Contract.

Defendants contend that the following damage element is not compensable: Precision’s Change Order request number 22 for $603,343.13 in material cost escalation. Defendants contend that two contract provisions (paragraphs 10 and Schedule C) prohibit claims for material cost escalation and waive such claims not made within a year of project completion.

Specific contract provisions control over general ones where they are inconsistent. Brady v. Bayer Corp. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 1156, 1178 (citing Civ. Code, § 3534 (as to contract and statutory interpretation, specific language controls over the general)). Further, where two plausible interpretations of the contract language exist, parol evidence is admissible to assist in interpreting the agreement, which therefore presents a question of fact precluding summary adjudication. Wolf v. Superior Court (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1343, 1359, fn. 26.

Here, Precision contends that the language in paragraphs 10 and Schedule C of the subcontract does not preclude its ability to seek recovery from Onni for the material cost escalation, citing to the language in Clause 52. Precision contends Clause 52 provides the subcontractor with a right to terminate the subcontract and to recover compensation for “any loss sustained from plant and material.” Motion, Ex. A; See Precision’s Response to Sep. Stmt. Nos. 3-5. Parol evidence also exists that the meaning of “any loss sustained from plant and material” in Clause 52 in the construction industry includes costs and any increases in material costs occurring over the course of the subject project. See id. Because the topic of damages after contract termination more specifically applies than other contract provisions against recovering increased material costs and collecting on untimely invoices, the contract is reasonably susceptible to an interpretation that Clause 52 allows increased materials costs are damages.

Therefore, there are triable issues of material fact as to whether the subcontract provides for recovery of material costs increases, necessitating denial as to issue number 1.

Issue 2: Precision’s Claim for Material Cost Increases Lacks Any Evidentiary Support.

Defendants argue that Precision has not produced evidentiary support for material cost increases at the deposition of Precision’s PMQ.

A moving defendant must show that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to prove a cause of action, which is more than simply arguing that there is an absence of evidence. Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891 (refusal to answer and objection at deposition insufficient to shift burden).

Here, this issue inadequately relies upon unsupportive depositions, which did not shift the burden of proof. Nevertheless, Precision provides evidence of damages apart from just the depositions. See Precision’s Add’l Disputed Material Facts No. 1-9.

Thus, the burden did not shift, moreover, other damages evidence could raise triable issues of fact, such that the motion must be denied as to issue number 2.

Issue 3: Precision Cannot Recover on Its Claim for Extra Costs to Pour Concrete on September 11, 2021 Because Precision Concedes it Did Not Perform that Work.

Defendants argue that extra costs to pour concrete are not recoverable. Precision acknowledges that the work for the concrete pour is not part of its damages claim. Precision instead contends that its claims seek contract damages. See Precision’s Response to Sep. Stmt. Nos. 10-12; Add’l Disputed Material Facts No. 8-14. “The requirement of performance may be excused by the other party’s breach.” Loral Corp. v. Moyes (1985) 174 Cal.App.3d 268, 280.

While there is no dispute about not performing work in order to claim extra costs to pour concrete, there remains a dispute regarding the total of contract damages that are related to excuses for the inability to perform that work due to the other party’s failure to perform. Add’l Disputed Material Facts No. 8-14.

Therefore, the motion is denied as to issue number 3.

Issue 4: Precision’s Mechanics Lien Is Void Because it Knowingly Includes Claims on Which Precision Cannot Recover.

Defendants contend that Precision recorded an overstated mechanics lien involving the surety on a release bond, such that the bond claim must be dismissed. “Summary judgment is proper only where there are no triable issues of material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment in its favor as a matter of law.” Assad v. Southern Pacific Transportation Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1609, 1612. Here, whether the mechanics lien was overstated is the subject of dispute as to the totality of sums in support. See Precision’s Response to Sep. Stmt. Nos. 13-19; Add’l Disputed Material Facts No. 8-14. Summary adjudication therefore is not warranted.

            Compelling Deposition

Onni also moves to compel attendance of Precision’s PMQ on categories related to Precision’s claims and damages. Onni contends that Precision’s PMQ Jerry Sorrentino was unprepared to answer questions related to these issues in his two prior depositions. No good cause exists for this third deposition. Defendants adequately demonstrate that Mr. Sorrentino is in poor health such that subjecting him to a third day of deposition amounts to harassment. Moreover, the remaining discovery could be obtained via interrogatories and document requests. Primarily, the reply does not contest that Mr. Sorrentino has health issues but offers a remote deposition. But it is not clear from the filings that a remote deposition would satisfactorily address the health issues.

Therefore, the motion to compel a third deposition is denied, as are the parties’ respective requests for sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION

The motion for summary adjudication is denied.

The motion to compel a deposition is denied.

 


Case Number: 22STCV25895    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: 55

 

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint; Defendants’ Motion to Seal Confidential Records Lodged in Support of Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint.

BACKGROUND

In this case, Plaintiffs CHEN FANG and YU LIN (“Plaintiffs”) seek damages related to a Limited Partnership Agreement (“LPA”) they entered into with Defendant Group 48 LP. Defendant CMB Export and Defendant NK are general partners of the Group 48 limited partnership, which is controlled by Defendant Hogan. Plaintiffs and Defendants entered into this agreement in connection with the development of the Century Plaza Hotel (the “Project”).

On 2/6/24, the Court granted Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleadings with leave to amend only with respect to the three causes of action in the Complaint (rescission-frustration of purpose; breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; and unjust enrichment). Plaintiffs now seek leave to file a proposed First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) to allege a class action lawsuit adding claims for: fraud in the inducement; unfair competition in violation of Business and Professions Code Section 17200; breach of contract; breach of fiduciary duty; negligence; gross negligence; and imposition of a constructive trust. The proposed FAC also adds Noreen Hogan as a defendant. Defendants oppose the motion.

Separately, Defendants move to seal specified exhibits included with the motion to amend, as being trade secrets and other confidential business materials. Plaintiffs do not oppose that motion. The Court addresses both motions in this tentative ruling.

LEGAL STANDARD

A trial court has discretion to allow a party to amend a pleading in the furtherance of justice. CCP § 473(a). “If discovery and investigation develop factual grounds justifying a timely amendment to a pleading, leave to amend must be liberally granted.”  Mabie v. Hyatt (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 581, 596.  “It is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal. App. 4th 1422, 1428.

­
 
 The Court, however, has the discretion to deny leave to amend if it finds the party’s delay in presenting the amendment was unwarranted, or when it determines that the other party will be prejudiced due to needing more discovery or delay of the trial. Miles v. City of Los Angeles (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 728, 739. Even if a party delays in moving to amend, however, it is an abuse of discretion for a court to deny leave to amend a pleading where the opposing party is not prejudiced by the proposed amendment. Kittredge Sports Co. v Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1048.

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a), (b).) 

 

With regards to a motion to seal, courts must find a compelling, overriding interest, prejudice absent sealing, and the lack of less-restrictive means, before ordering filed documents sealed. McGuan v. Endovascular Technologies, Inc. (2010) 182 Cal.App.4th 974, 988 (“the protection of trade secrets is an interest that can support sealing records ….”).

ANALYSIS

Motion to Amend

Plaintiffs contend that, since the filing of the initial Complaint, they have learned additional facts related to Defendants’ alleged misconduct. Plaintiffs learned recently that Defendant Hogan’s statements regarding the reasons for Group 48’s insolvency were rejected by an appellate court in a different case related to the Project. In December 2023 Plaintiffs received from Defendants 1,894 previously withheld documents in discovery in this case. Plaintiffs contend that, based on their review of these documents, Defendants made repeated misrepresentations to Plaintiffs regarding the Project. (Mot., 9: 6-13, 14:3-10). The proposed new allegations and claims are based on Plaintiffs’ broader understanding of Defendants’ alleged misconduct in connection with the Project. Plaintiffs filed this motion on 3/5/24, approximately a month after the Court’s ruling on the MJOP.

Defendants argue that there was inexcusable delay in bringing this motion because Plaintiffs have “known all along about the financial troubles of the project, the state court lawsuits, and the foreclosure sale.” (Opp., 8:14-15). The Court disagrees. Defendants’ arguments are mere speculations. (Opp., 6:23-24; 7:3-24;8:1-23). In December 2023, Defendants produced 1,894 documents that had been previously withheld and marked as confidential. (Kusel Decl. ¶ 5:23-26.)  Thereafter, Plaintiffs’ counsel conducted research revealing that Defendants were involved in two other litigations, filed in New York State and California, related to Group 48 and the foreclosure on the Century Plaza Project. (Id.) These filings revealed that other courts had rejected Defendant Hogan’s statements to Plaintiffs back in 2022. Plaintiffs sought to file the FAC two months after receiving and reviewing the 1,894 documents, and just a month after this Court’s ruling on the MJOP. (Id. at ¶ 9:5-6.) Under these circumstances, any delay by Plaintiffs in bringing this motion does not rise to the level of inexcusable delay that would warrant denial of their motion.

Plaintiffs argue that Defendants will not be prejudiced by the addition of new claims because trial is nine months away and consequently so is the deadline to bring a dispositive motion. (Mot., 14:7-10).  In response, Defendants contend that permitting the amended complaint at this late stage would effectively cause this case to start all over again—unfairly forcing Defendants to re-litigate the entire case through all stages. (Opp., 5:23-24). Defendants also contend that Plaintiffs “present entirely new legal theories and claims as class representatives—which were previously ‘not pleaded at all’—'after [a] long unexplained delay’ and after this Court already deemed their existing claims non-viable . . . [and] [s]uch “lack of diligence” is prejudicial and is itself grounds for denying Plaintiffs’ motion to amend.” (Opp., 6:13-16). Defendants failed to provide any facts showing that they would be prejudiced. Defendants merely speculate that the additional causes of actions may result in additional litigation expenses. That the FAC asserts new legal theories is not relevant to determining whether Plaintiffs may file an amended pleading because the proposed amendments relate to the general set of facts in the Complaint, i.e., Defendants’ duties to Plaintiffs under the LPA with respect to the Project. Kittredge Sports Co., 213 Cal. App. 3d at 1048 (new legal theories are irrelevant to a motion to amend so long as the amended pleading relates to the same general facts as the prior pleading). Therefore, the Court does not find prejudice.

Plaintiffs have satisfied the procedural requirements for filing an amended complaint. The Motion includes a copy of the proposed first amended complaint, specifications by reference to pages and lines of the allegations that are to be added, and a declaration specifying the effect and necessity of the proposed amendment. (Kusel Decl., ¶ 8; Mot. 16:2-10; Exh. 1- Proposed FAC).

Motion to Seal

Defendants’ motion states that an unredacted FAC and exhibits have been lodged under seal. Defendants shared the information and/or documents with Plaintiffs subject to a Confidentiality and Non-Disclosure Agreement and provided the discovery responses pursuant to a stipulated protective order.

The Court finds that the declaration of Senior Vice President and General Counsel of CMB Export LLC evidences a compelling, overriding interest, prejudice absent sealing, and the lack of less-restrictive means, as to business trade secrets and sensitive commercial information.

CONCLUSION

The Court grants the motion for leave to amend. Plaintiffs shall file and serve the FAC attached to the motion within 14 days of this order.

The motion to seal is granted.
