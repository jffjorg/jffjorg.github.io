(CCP § 664.6)

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and Enter Judgment is GRANTED. 

 

The Dismissal entered on April 16, 2024, is hereby set aside and vacated, and Judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant in the Total amount of $4,100.00, calculated as follows: Principal amount of $3,600.00, plus Court Costs in the amount of $500.00.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

            On January 20, 2021, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (Plaintiff) filed an action against Defendant Karen Matranga (Defendant) for subrogation, stemming from an automobile collision between Defendant and an individual insured by Plaintiff’s automobile insurance policy. (Compl. ¶ 6.).  Plaintiff compensated the insured for claimed damages in the amount of $10,853.03 and filed the instant claim against Defendant for allegedly causing the damages.  (Ibid.) 

 

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for Dismissal with Reservation to Vacate and Enter Judgment Upon Breach (Stipulation), signed by both parties, to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $8,390.05.  On November 2, 2023, the Court dismissed the entire case without prejudice pursuant to the Stipulation.

 

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment (Motion). 

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

CCP § 664.6, provides a summary procedure that enables judges to enforce a settlement agreement by entering a judgment pursuant to the terms of the parties’ settlement.  In particular, the statute provides:

 

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.  If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following:

 

(1) The party.

(2) An attorney who represents the party.

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf.

 

CCP § 664.6(a)-(b) (emphasis added).

 

III.              Discussion

 

A. Retention of Jurisdiction

 

“‘[V]oluntary dismissal of an action or special proceeding terminates the court’s jurisdiction over the matter.’  (Conservatorship of Martha P. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 857, 867) [12 Cal.Rptr.3d 142.) ‘If requested by the parties,’ however, ‘the [trial] court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce [a] settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.’ (§ 664.6, italics added.)”  (Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 917.) “‘Because of its summary nature, strict compliance with the requirements of section 664.6 is prerequisite to invoking the power of the court to impose a settlement agreement.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Sully-Miller Contracting Co. v. Gledson/Cashman Construction, Inc. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 30, 37).)

 

“A request for the trial court to retain jurisdiction under section 664.6 ‘must conform to the same three requirements which the Legislature and the courts have deemed necessary for section 664.6 enforcement of the settlement itself: the request must be made (1) during the pendency of the case, not after the case has been dismissed in its entirety, (2) by the parties themselves, and (3) either in a writing signed by the parties or orally before the court.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 440).)  “The ‘request must be express, not implied from other language, and it must be clear and unambiguous.’” (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen, supra, 97 Cal.App.4th at 440).)

 

Here, the parties signed a Stipulation for Dismissal with Reservation to Vacate and Enter Judgment Upon Breach (Stipulation) containing the parties’ agreement for the Court to retain jurisdiction pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §664.6 and to enforce the terms of the stipulation and enter judgment in the event of default.  (Stipulation ¶ 3.)  Prior to the dismissal of this action, the Stipulation was signed by the parties and submitted to the Court.  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  The Court dismissed the entire case without prejudice pursuant to the Stipulation and expressly stated that it “shall retain jurisdiction pursuant to CCP § 664.6.” (04/16/2024 Order.)

 

The Court finds that the Stipulation complies with § 664.6 requirements and the Court has retained jurisdiction to enter judgment pursuant to the parties’ Stipulation in this action.

 

B. Entry of Judgment

 

The Stipulation Agreement filed on March 28, 2024, provides that Plaintiff and Defendant agreed to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $8,390.05 (Stipulation ¶ 2.)  Pursuant to the Stipulation, Defendant’s insurance carrier would make an initial payment of $4790.05, which was made on June 19, 2023. (Ibid.) Subsequently, Defendant would make a payment of $600.00 by April 1, 2024, and then beginning on May 1, 2024, Defendant would make sixty (60) monthly payments of $50.00 until the settlement amount is paid in full. (Ibid.)  

 

The Stipulation also provides that in the event Defendant fails to make timely payments, Plaintiff will mail a letter to Defendant regarding the default.  (Ibid. at ¶ 3.)  If Defendant does not remedy the default within 14 days of receiving notice, “then plaintiff will be entitled to have any dismissal in this action set aside and judgment entered for the settlement amount, minus credit for payments received, plus any costs associated with entering the judgment not to exceed $500.”  (Ibid.)  The declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel shall be sufficient proof for judgment to be entered.  (Ibid.)

 

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for an order to vacate the dismissal and enter judgment against Defendant. According to Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant’s insurance carrier made a one-time payment of $4,790.05; however, Defendant failed to make any payments. (Espinosa Decl. ¶ 4.) On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff mailed Defendant a default letter regarding the missed payments; however, Defendant has not made any payments.  (Id. at ¶¶ 5-6, Exhibit C.)

 

Plaintiff seeks to have the dismissal set aside and judgment entered against Defendant in the amount of $4,100.00 as follows: $8,390.05 (settlement amount), minus $4,790.05 (payment made by Defendant’s insurance carrier), plus $500.00 (court costs).  (Id. at ¶ 7.)  The court costs consist of $370.00 complaint filing fee, $80.00 service of process fee, and $60.00 motion fee.  (Id. at ¶ 8.)

 

The Court finds the Stipulation to be valid and enforceable under Code of Civil Proc. § 664.6.  Plaintiff provides evidence that Defendant defaulted on payments and has not made any additional payments after Plaintiff sent notice.  Thus, a valid and signed stipulation agreement was breached and the Court retained jurisdiction to enter judgment upon breach.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and Enter Judgment is GRANTED.  Dismissal entered on April 16, 2024, is vacated, and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $4,100.00 as follows: principal amount of $3,600.00 plus court costs of $500. 

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and Enter Judgment is GRANTED. 

 

The Dismissal entered on April 16, 2024, is hereby set aside and vacated, and Judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant in the Total amount of $4,100.00, calculated as follows: Principal amount of $3,600.00, plus Court Costs in the amount of $500.00. 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

            This case arises from an alleged breach of an attorney-client retainer agreement between Plaintiff Salisbury Group, Inc. (Plaintiff) and Defendant Natale Mercuri (Defendant). According to the complaint, Defendant was billed for legal services and costs relating to the scope of Plaintiff and Defendant’s attorney-client retainer agreement, and Defendant has failed and refused to pay the legal bills. (Compl., page 3.)

 

            On January 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, and (2) common counts.

 

            On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to deem admitted Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, set one.

 

            On October 17, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition.

 

            On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

            “Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)  “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).)

 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)

 

 

III.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves the Court to deem admitted Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, set one

(RFAs) that was served upon Defendant.

 

            Here, on August 22, 2024, Plaintiff served upon Defendant via email Plaintiff’s RFAs. (Salisbury Decl., ¶ 9; Exhibit A.) Thus, the last day to serve verified responses to the RFAs was September 24, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 10.) Plaintiff did not receive Defendant’s responses and Defendant did not request an extension of time to respond. (Id. at ¶¶ 10-11.) Defendant argues that he was not properly served with Plaintiff’s RFAs because the email address used for electronic service was incorrect, but the email address Defendant suggests is the correct email address is the same email address Plaintiff used for electronic service. (Frey Decl., Exhibit A.)

 

Defendant also provides that the failure to timely respond to Plaintiff’s RFAs was due to Defendant’s counsel’s inadvertence. (Sanders Decl., ¶ 5.) Upon discovering the mistake, Defendant filed the responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs. (Id. at Exhibit B.) Thus, Defendant is in substantial compliance prior to the hearing of the instant motion.

 

Therefore, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to deem admitted its RFAs that were served upon Defendant.

 

IV.              Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c), provides, in part: “It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary . . . on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”

 

While sanctions would mandatory against Defendant because Defendant did not timely serve the responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs, Plaintiff is representing her own law firm and thus would be considered an attorney who litigates in propria persona. With respect to sanctions, pro se litigants may not recover attorney’s fees as a discovery sanction. (Kravitz v. Superior Court (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 1015, 1020.) Thus, Plaintiff is not entitled to discovery sanctions.

 

However, pro se litigants may recover reasonable expenses actually incurred, including computer-assisted legal research, photocopying, transportation, or any other identifiable expense. (Id. at p.1022.) Plaintiff declares that she incurred the following costs in filing the motion:

 

-          Filing fee: $60.00

-          E-filing costs for the motion: $10.50

-          EFM filing fee: $4.50

-          E-service costs for the motion: $1.00

-          E-filing costs for the reply to response to the motion $10.50

-          EFM filing fee for the reply: $4.50

-          E-service costs for the reply: $1.00

 

This totals $92.00. The Court finds this reasonable, and awards Plaintiff the full costs

incurred in bringing this motion.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER:

 

For the reasons stated above,

 

Plaintiff Salisbury Group, Inc.’s Motion to Deem Admitted Its Request for Admissions, Set One is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff’s request for Monetary Sanctions is DENIED. Plaintiff’s Request for Costs incurred is GRANTED in the amount of $92.00, payable to Plaintiff within 15 days of this Court’s order.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

This is breach of contract case. On August 25, 2023, plaintiff American Contractors Indemnity Company (“Plaintiff”) filed the complaint against defendant Rares Crisan (“Defendant”), alleging that Defendant failed to pay money owed to Plaintiff.

 

The complaint alleges that the parties entered into an Indemnity Agreement in 2012, in which Plaintiff provided a bond to Defendant in reliance on Defendant’s promise to save Plaintiff harmless from any resulting loss. (Compl., ¶ 5.) Defendant breached his obligations by refusing to reimburse Plaintiff upon demand, resulting in damages in the amount of $5,200, plus interest, attorney’s fees, and costs. (Compl. ¶¶ 8-9.) In 2021, the parties entered into a reimbursement agreement, in which Defendant agreed to reimburse Plaintiff through monthly payments a total amount of $9,955.12. (Compl., ¶ 11.) However, Plaintiff only received payments under the reimbursement agreement in the amount of $1,800.) (Compl., ¶ 13.)

 

On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for summary judgment, along with supporting declarations, exhibits, and a statement of undisputed facts.

 

Notice of the motion and supporting papers have been served in compliance with the Code of Civil Procedure § 437(a)(2) on August 6, 2024. 

 

No opposition has been filed by Defendant.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.  In analyzing such motions, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.  Thus, summary judgment or summary adjudication is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  CCP § 437c(c); Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741. 

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate or establish an essential element.  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.  A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must be denied where the moving party’s evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition or where the opposition is weak.  See Leyva v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475; Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384, 387. 

 

Once the moving party has met the burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material facts exists as to a cause of action or a defense thereto.  CCP § 437c(o)(2).  When a party cannot establish an essential element or defense, a court must grant a motion for summary adjudication.  CCP § 437c(o)(1)-(2). 

 

III.              Request for Judicial Notice

A motion for summary judgment “shall be supported by affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice shall or may be taken.” (California Code of Civil Procedure, section 437c(b)(1).) Request for judicial notice must be made in a separate document. (California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1350(c)(5).) Judges cannot take judicial notice of hearsay statements asserted in court filings, but can take judicial notice of the existence of such documents. Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 768; Williams v. Wraxall (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 120, 130, fn. 7 (judges may take judicial notice of the existence of court documents, “but cannot take judicial notice of the truth of hearsay statements in decisions or court files, including pleadings, affidavits, testimony, or statements of fact.”). Courts can take judicial notice of the fact that complaints were filed, but not of the truth of the statements contained in those. Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 483.

 

1.      Plaintiff’s complaint filed in this action filed on August 25, 2024. The Court GRANTS the request for judicial notice.

 

2.      Defendant’s Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint filed on October 17, 2023.  The Court GRANTS the request for judicial notice.

 

 

IV.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on the basis that the undisputed evidence demonstrates Defendant has no defense to the breach of contract cause of action alleged in the FAC.

 

 To establish a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must show: “(1) [t]he existence of a contract; (2) plaintiff performed its obligations under the terms of the contract, or plaintiff's performance thereunder was excused; (3) defendant breached the contract; and (4) plaintiff suffered damages as a result of defendant’s breach. (M.G. Chamberlain & Co. v. Simpson (1959) 173 Cal.App.2d 263.)”

 

Plaintiff alleges that a written Indemnity Agreement exists between the parties and Defendant failed to indemnify and reimburse Plaintiff for money paid out. [SSUF ¶¶ 1-3.] Indemnity Agreement entered into between Plaintiff and Defendant identifies the indemnity owed to Plaintiff. [SSUF ¶3] The surety reviewed the claim for potential violations of the Business & Professions Code, and based on Plaintiff’s review of the submitted documentation, it was reasonably clear that Defendant was in violation of Section(s) 7109, and 7113. [SSUF ¶¶4-6]. Notice of the good faith settlement was provided to Defendant dated April 15, 2021. [SSUF ¶¶5-6]. Plaintiff issued payment to Claimant Galleria Kitchens & Baths in the amount of $7,500.00 from the Bond [SSUF ¶6] and reminded R C Custom Cabinets and Defendant of their indemnity obligations to indemnify and to hold Plaintiff harmless and requested reimbursement of the Bond loss. [SSUF ¶¶6-9, 11-12].

 

Defendant was aware of the payment to Claimant since Plaintiff notified Defendant of its determination that liability for Claimant’s claim under the bond was reasonably clear and financial damages incurred by Claimant. [SSUF ¶¶4-6]. Plaintiff further notified Defendant after payment was made. [SSUF ¶7, 12]. Defendant agreed to make payments to Plaintiff and then defaulted after only paying $1,800.00 towards the $7,500.00 loss. [SSUF ¶¶8-9,11-12].

 

ACIC has been damaged in the principal sum of $5,200.00 plus interest at the legal rate from the date ACIC paid the claim and attorneys’ fees and costs. [SSUF ¶13, 14]. The total amount of interest that has accrued on the claim against Defendant’s bond is $2,013.07. [SSUF ¶14].

 

 Defendant, in his responses to Plaintiff’s requests for admission, admitted that he agreed to indemnify and reimburse Plaintiff for any and all sums paid out under the bond and that he did not pay Plaintiff the sum of $5,200 plus attorney’s fees and costs due under the bond. (Exhibit List, Ex. 4.) Furthermore, Plaintiff has submitted as exhibits the indemnity and reimbursement agreements made between the parties and signed by Defendant. (Exhibit List, Ex. 3.) This establishes that a contract existed between the parties, Defendant breached the contract, and Plaintiff suffered damages as a result of Defendant’s breach.

 

Plaintiff has produced admissible evidence on each element of its causes of action for breach of contract. [SSUF ¶¶ 1-36].  Therefore, Plaintiff has carried its initial burden, and the burden now shifts to Defendant to show that there is a triable issue of material fact.

 

Defendant has not filed any opposition to Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment and has not filed any evidence demonstrating a triable issue of material fact. Therefore, Defendant has failed to carry his shifted burden.

 

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

III.             
Legal
Standard



 



California law incorporates many of the basic policy
objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in
favor of arbitrability. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997)
15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72.) The petitioner bears the burden of proving the
existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the
evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a
preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there
should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier
of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great
Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413 (“Rosenthal”).)
The Court is empowered by the Code of Civil Procedure Section 1281.2 to compel
parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so.   



 



            Code of Civil Procedure section
1281.2 states: 



 



“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement
alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and
that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall
order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it
determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it
determines that:





            (a) The right to compel arbitration
has been waived by the petitioner; or



            (b) Grounds exist for the revocation
of the agreement.



(c) A party to the
arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special
proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of
related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a
common issue of law or fact. For purposes of this section, a pending court
action or special proceeding includes an action or proceeding initiated by the
party refusing to arbitrate after the petition to compel arbitration has been
filed, but on or before the date of the hearing on the petition. This
subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to
the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section
1295.” 



 



(Code of Civ. Proc., §§
1281.2(a)-(c).) 



 



            The petition to compel arbitration
functions as a motion and is to be heard in the manner of a motion – namely,
the facts are to be proven by affidavit or declaration and documentary evidence
with oral testimony taken only in the court’s discretion. (Code of Civ.
Proc., § 1290; Rosenthal, supra,
14 Cal.4th at p. 402.) The petition to compel must set forth the provisions
of the written agreement and the arbitration clause verbatim, or such
provisions must be attached and incorporated by reference. (Cal. Rules of
Court, rule 3.1330.)



 



            If
the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until “an
arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such
earlier time as the court specifies.” 
(See Code of Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)



 



IV.             
Discussion



 



Arbitration Agreement



 



Plaintiffs bring the instant Motion seeking to compel Westlake
to submit to arbitration based on the Contract.



 



“Under both the [Federal
Arbitration Act] and California law, arbitration agreements are valid,
irrevocable, and enforceable, except upon such grounds that exist at law or
equity for voiding a contract.” (Winter
v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The
party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written
arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) In
ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, “the court must first determine
whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute,” and “[g]eneral
principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this
determination.” (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire
Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)



 



Here, Plaintiffs present evidence of the Contract, which
includes a section called, “Arbitration Provision.”  (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. 2, p. 6 at “Arbitration Provision.”)
The Contract was first signed by Plaintiffs and Carbros, and then was assigned
to and signed by Westlake. (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. 2.) The Arbitration Provision
provides in pertinent part:



 



“PLEASE READ CAREFULLY! By agreeing to this
Arbitration Provision you are giving up your right to go to court for claims

ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN YOU AND US DECIDED BY ARBITRATION, AND NOT BY A COURT OR BY
JURY TRIAL. YOU GIVE UP ANY RIGHT THAT YOU MAY HAVE TO PARTICIPATE AS A CLASS
REPRESENTATIVE OR CLASS MEMBER IN ANY CLASS ACTION OR CLASS ARBITRATION AGAINST
US IF A DISPUTE IS ARBITRATED. IN ARBITRATION, DISCOVERY AND RIGHTS TO APPEAL
ARE GENERALLY MORE LIMITED THAN IN A JUDICIAL PROCEEDING, AND OTHER RIGHTS THAT
YOU WOULD HAVE IN COURT MAY NOT BE AVAILABLE.

Arbitration Provision by written amendment signed by the parties. If the

(Motion, Sadr

6-7.)



 



No opposition has been filed.



 



The Court finds
that Plaintiffs have met their burden of proving the existence of a valid
arbitration agreement. As noted above, Westlake does not dispute its signatures
appear on the agreement, and Plaintiffs submitted a copy of the Contract and Arbitration
Provision to the Court. The Arbitration Provision states it applies to “any claim, dispute or controversy
between you and us or our employees, agents, successors, assigns or affiliates
arising from or relating to: 1. the credit application; 2. the purchase of the
Property; 3. the condition of the Property; 4. this Contract; 5. any insurance,
maintenance, service or other contracts you purchased in connection with this
Contract; or 6. any related transaction, occurrence or relationship.” (Motion,
Sadr Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. 2,
pp. 6-7.) The Arbitration Provision specifically applies to Plaintiffs’ claims
against Westlake, given that the causes of action arise out of the Contract and
the condition of the vehicle. (Compl.; Motion, Sadr Decl. Exh. 1.)



 



Plaintiffs
demonstrated that the parties have been unable to come to an agreement
regarding arbitration (Motion,
Sadr Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exh.
1) and agreed to arbitrate the dispute through AAA or another arbitration
organization. Indeed, the Arbitration Provision states: “The party electing
arbitration may choose either of the following arbitration organizations and
its applicable rules, provided it is willing and able to handle the
arbitration: American Arbitration Association, 1633 Broadway, Floor 10, New
York, NY 10019 (www.adr.org) or JAMS, 1920 Main Street, Suite 300 Irvine, CA
92614 (www.jamsadr.com), or it may choose any other reputable arbitration
organization and its rules to conduct the arbitration, subject to the other
party's approval.” (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. 2, pp. 6-7.) Plaintiffs state they elect AAA
as the arbitration forum for this matter (Motion, pp. 6-7), and Westlake and Carbros
have not indicated they do not approve of the AAA. In light of these
circumstances, it is necessary for the Court to intervene and compel Westlake,
now the Westlake of the Contract, and Carbros to arbitration before AAA, JAMs,
or another reputable arbitration organization.



 



Thus, the Court finds that an
Arbitration Agreement exists between the parties and there is no defense to its
enforcement. The matter is to be arbitrated before the AAA or, in the
alternative, JAMS or another reputable arbitration organization.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

This is a breach of contract case. On May 15, 2024, plaintiff Pacific Rejuvenation Medical (Plaintiff) filed the complaint against defendant Fouzia Almarou (Defendant), alleging that Defendant failed to pay money owed to Plaintiff.

 

The complaint was served by personal service on May 24, 2024, to the address at 6460 Balboa Blvd., Apt 4, Van Nuys, CA 91406 (the Balboa Address). Defendant did not file a responsive pleading. Default was entered on June 27, 2024, and default judgment was entered on August 12, 2024.

 

On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to set aside/vacate judgment. No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

There are four bases upon which a defendant may seek to set aside a default judgment: (1) relief for “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), (2) the default judgment was void as a matter of law, (3) lack of actual notice under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5, subdivision (a), or (4) equitable relief for “extrinsic fraud or mistake.”

 

“When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).)

 

“A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date¿prescribed by¿subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that¿the¿party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his¿or her¿avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with¿the¿notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (b).)

 

“Upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (c).)

 

III.              Discussion

 

Defendant moves to set aside the judgment on the basis that she was never served. Defendant submits an affidavit and evidence that at the time of service on June 26, 2024, she did not live at the Balboa Address, having moved out on September 28, 2023. Defendant contends that she therefore could not have been personally served at that address, and her failure to respond was therefore not caused by her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. Defendant does not state how she came to learn about the lawsuit. No opposition has been filed.

 

Defendant’s motion was filed On October 7, 2024, 102 days after entry of default on June 27, 2024. This is within the time limit provided by Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5, subdivision (a). Judgment was entered on August 12, 2024. Defendant’s motion falls within the time limit provided by Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5, subdivision (a).

 

However, Defendant has not filed a proof of service of her motion on Plaintiff. Proof of service of moving papers is required under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1300.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to set aside the judgment is CONTINUED to January 6, 2025 at 10:00 a.m. in Department 25 of Spring Street Courthouse, located at 312 North Spring Street, California 90012.

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside the Default Judgment entered on 08/12/2024 is CONTINUED TO JANUARY 6, 2025 at 10:00 a.m. in Department 25 at the SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE, located at 312 North Spring Street Courthouse.
