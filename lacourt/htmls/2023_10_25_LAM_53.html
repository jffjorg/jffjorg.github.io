[Tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion regarding determination of applicable law

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Champion Power Equipment, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Nancy del Socorro Diaz Martinez

Motion Regarding Determination of Applicable Law

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court grants defendant Champion Power Equipment, Inc.’s request for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (c).)

DISCUSSION

Defendant Champion Power Equipment, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order determining that Texas law shall govern and apply to the claims alleged in this action, filed by plaintiff Nancy del Socorro Diaz Martinez, a minor, by and through her guardian ad litem Paloma Denisse Martinez Flores, as an individual and as successor-in-interest to Nancy Lyseth Martinez, Alejandro Arellano, Alejandra Estafania Martinez, and Joshua Nathanael Diaz (“Plaintiff”). 

“‘As the forum state, California will apply its own law “unless a party litigant timely invokes the law of a foreign state.” ’”  (Chen v. Los Angeles Truck Centers, LLC (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 488, 495.)  “In that event, the party invoking a foreign state’s law . . . ‘must demonstrate that the [foreign state’s] rule of decision will further the interest of the foreign state and therefore that it is an appropriate one for the forum to apply to the case before it.’”  (Ibid.) California decisions “have adopted and consistently applied the so-called ‘governmental interest’ analysis as the appropriate general methodology for resolving choice-of-law questions in this state.”  (McCann v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2010) 48 Cal.4th 68, 83.) 

“The governmental interest analysis ‘involv[es] three steps: “First, the court determines whether the relevant law of each of the potentially affected jurisdictions with regard to the particular issue in question is the same or different.  Second, if there is a difference, the court examines each jurisdiction’s interest in the application of its own law under the circumstances of the particular case to determine whether a true conflict exists.  Third, if the court finds that there is a true conflict, it carefully evaluates and compares the nature and strength of the interest of each jurisdiction in the application of its own law ‘to determine which state’s interest would be more impaired if its policy were subordinated to the policy of the other state’ [citation], and then ultimately applies ‘the law of the state whose interest would be the more impaired if its law were not applied.’ ” ’”  (Textron Inc. v. Travelers Casualty & Surety Co. (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 733, 748.)

A.    Difference in Law

The court finds that there is a difference in California law and Texas law with regard to Plaintiff’s claims and requests for damages in this action.  (Textron Inc., supra, 45 Cal.App.5th at p. 748.)

First, Texas and California courts evaluate the effect of a products liability defendant’s compliance with federal standards differently. 

“In a products liability action brought against a product manufacturer or seller [in Texas], there is a rebuttable presumption that the product manufacturer or seller is not liable for any injury to a claimant caused by some aspect of the formulation, labeling, or design of a product if the product manufacturer or seller establishes that the product’s formula, labeling, or design complied with mandatory safety standards or regulations adopted and promulgated by the federal government, or an agency of the federal government, that were applicable to the product at the time of manufacture and that governed the product risk that allegedly caused harm.”  (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code, § 82.008, subd. (a).)

Conversely, in California, “[c]ourts have generally not looked with favor upon the use of statutory compliance as a defense to tort liability.”  (Ramirez v. Plough, Inc. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 539, 547-548.)  However, if “the evidence shows no unusual circumstances, but only the ordinary situation contemplated by the statute or administrative rule, then ‘the minimum standard proscribed by the legislation or regulation may be accepted by the triers of fact, or by the court as a matter of law, as sufficient for the occasion. . . .’”  (Id. at p. 548.)

Thus, the court finds that there is a difference in law regarding the effect of a manufacturer defendant’s compliance with federal regulations and standards because (1) such compliance, in Texas, would entitle that defendant to a rebuttable presumption that it is not liable, but (2) in California, that defendant is not entitled to a rebuttable presumption that it is not liable, and a court “may[,]” but is not required to, accept statutory compliance as sufficient.  (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code, § 82.008, subd. (a); Ramirez, supra, 6 Cal.4th at p. 548 [emphasis added].)

Second, Texas and California law differs as to the limitation on awards of punitive damages.

In Texas, “[e]xemplary damages awarded against a defendant may not exceed an amount equal to the greater of: [¶] (1)(A) two times the amount of economic damages; plus [¶] (B) an amount equal to any noneconomic damages found by the jury, not to exceed $750,000; or [¶] (2) $200,000.”  (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code, § 41.008, subd. (b).)  But in California, there is no such limit, except as to the imposition of grossly excessive or arbitrary awards in violation of due process.  (Roby v. McKesson Corp. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686, 712 [“The due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution places constraints on state court awards of punitive damages”].)

            Thus, the court has determined that there exists differences in the law of the State of California and the State of Texas with regard to the issues presented in this action.

B.    Existence of a True Conflict

The court finds that a true conflict does not exist.  (Textron Inc., supra, 45 Cal.App.5t at p. 748; Chen, supra, 42 Cal.App.5th at p. 496 [on the second step of the choice-of-law inquiry, courts “examine each jurisdiction’s interest ‘in the application of its own law under the circumstances of the particular case’ [citation], to determine whether a true conflict exists”].)

First, the court finds that Texas has a real interest in the application of its own law in this action. (Chen, supra, 42 Cal.App.5th at p. 496 [“The state’s interest must be real rather than hypothetical”].)  The operative complaint alleges that (1) the decedents died, and Plaintiff was injured, at their house in Edinburg, Texas, and (2) the cause of their injury and death was carbon monoxide poisoning after “a dangerously defective portable generator spewed carbon monoxide throughout their house.”  (FAC ¶ 1 [Plaintiff seeks relief based on Plaintiff’s injuries and the wrongful deaths of decedents at their home “on July 27, 2020 in Edinburg, Texas”].)  “Texas maintains an interest ‘in protecting its citizens from, and compensating them for, injuries resulting from defective products.’”  (Ford Motor Co. v. Aguiniga (Tex. Ct. App. 1999) 9 S.W.3d 252, 261.)  Thus, Texas has an interest in adjudicating the wrongful deaths of its residents caused by a defective product.  (Ibid.)

Second, the court finds that California has only a hypothetical interest in the application of its law in this action.

            The court acknowledges that Plaintiff asserts that Defendant was a California corporation at the time that the subject incident occurred and that Plaintiff filed this action.  But Plaintiff has not pointed to any particular interest that California has in this case, where Plaintiff and the decedents were not California residents or injured in California.  (Chen, supra, 42 Cal.App.5th at p. 498 [finding that California had only “a theoretical interest in the application of its strict products liability rules” when the California defendant “placed an allegedly defective product into the stream of commerce, importing it and selling it to a California tour operator”].)  The “underlying basis” for California’s policy behind imposing strict products liability “is the protection of California residents and other persons within its territorial jurisdictions from injury.  California’s interest in imposing that policy becomes hypothetical when the injured persons are not California residents and were not injured in California.”  (Ibid.) 

            Thus, upon evaluating the interests of Texas and California in the application of their own laws under the circumstances in this case, the court finds that there is no true conflict.  “‘When one of two states related to a case has a legitimate interest in the application of its law and policy and the other has none, there is no real problem; clearly, the law of the interested state should be applied.’”  (Hurtado v. Superior Court (1974) 11 Cal.3d 574, 580.)  The court therefore finds that the law of Texas, as the state that has a legitimate interest in the application of its law in this action, should govern this case.  (Ibid.)

C.    Nature and Strength of Interest of Each Jurisdiction

As set forth above, the court has determined that Texas law should apply because there is no true conflict.  However, even if the court were to determine that there is a true conflict, the court would find that Texas’s interest would be more impaired if its policy were subordinated to the policy of California.  (Textron Inc., supra, 45 Cal.App.5th at p. 748.)

In conducting the analysis under this step, courts must “keep in mind that ‘[t]he court does not “ ‘weigh’ ” the conflicting governmental interests in the sense of determining which conflicting law manifested the “better” or the “worthier” social policy on the specific issue . . . .  [Instead, the process] can accurately be described as . . . a problem of allocating domains of law-making power in multi-state context—[by determining the appropriate] limitations on the reach of state policies—as distinguished from evaluating the wisdom of those policies. . . .’”  (McCann, supra, 48 Cal.4th at p. 97.)

The court finds that, under the circumstances, Texas’s interest in protecting and compensating for the injuries suffered by its residents would be impaired by applying California law.  Moreover, “the application of California law [in this case] would not protect California residents or anyone who was injured in California[,]” because, as set forth above, the parties have not shown that Plaintiff or any decedent was a California resident; instead, Plaintiff and the decedents were domiciled in Texas at the time of the subject incident.  (FAC ¶ 1.)  But “a state’s interest in providing compensation to survivors in a wrongful death case ‘extend[s] only to local decedents and local beneficiaries.’”  (Ibid.)  Because “California’s lawmaking power in respect of its products liability policy is appropriately confined to the protection of California residents and persons injured within California’s borders[,]” the court finds that the application of California law in this case would impair Texas’s interests and policies in protecting and compensating its residents for injuries suffered in that state as a result of defective products. 

D.    Conclusion

The court finds, for the reasons set forth above, that (1) there exists a difference in the laws of Texas and California; (2) there is no true conflict in this case because Texas has a real interest in this action while California has only a hypothetical interest; and (3) even if a true conflict existed, the circumstances support applying Texas law.

The court therefore finds that Texas law shall govern this action.  

ORDER

The court grants defendant Champion Power Equipment, Inc.’s motion regarding determination of applicable law.

The court determines that Texas law shall govern this action. 

The court orders defendant Champion Power Equipment, Inc. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Howard Management Group       

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    (1) Plaintiff Sofia Nemzer, and (2) Defendant 1544 9th Street, LLC

Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court grants defendant Howard Management Group’s request for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (c), (d).)  The court notes that, although it takes judicial notice of the existence of the recorded grant deed, the court does not take judicial notice of the factual matters stated therein.  (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375 [“‘[T]he fact that a court may take judicial notice of a recorded deed, or similar document, does not mean it may take judicial notice of factual matters stated therein’”].)

 

 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Howard Management Group (“HMG”) moves the court for an order granting its motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the fourth, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action alleged by plaintiff Sofia Nemzer (“Plaintiff”) in her Second Amended Complaint.

The court denies HMG’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the fourth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 54.1 because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) Civil Code section 54.3 makes liable “[a]ny person or persons, firm or corporation” who denies a person the enjoyment of public facilities specified in section 54.1 or otherwise interferences with the rights of an individual with a disability thereunder, and therefore does not limit the applicability of this subdivision to landlords only, and (2) Plaintiff has alleged facts establishing that (i) she was denied full and equal access to the subject property as a result of the malfunction and non-function of the elevator, and (ii) HMG contributed to the failure to repair the elevator and therefore contributed to the denial of access and interference with enjoyment of the property.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii); Civ. Code, §§ 54.1, 54.3, subd. (a); SAC ¶¶ 59, 15, 22-23.)

The court grants HMG’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the sixth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1942.4 because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) this statute provides that “[a] landlord of a dwelling may not demand rent, collect rent, issue a notice of rent increase, or issue a three-day notice to pay rent or quit” in certain circumstances, but (2) Plaintiff has not alleged that HMG is a landlord or otherwise acted as Plaintiff’s landlord and instead has alleged that HMG “managed the Property for and on behalf of” defendant 1544 9th Street, LLC.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii); Civ. Code, § 1942.4, subd. (a) [emphasis added]; FAC ¶ 5.)

The court notes that, in its opposition, defendant 1544 9th Street, LLC (“9th Street”) argues that its contract with HMG gives HMG the sole and exclusive right to lease and maintain the premises, such that there is contractual privity between Plaintiff and HMG.  However, 9th Street relies on extrinsic material (specifically, the “Property Management Agreement”) which is improper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (d).)

The court denies HMG’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the seventh cause of action for violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) Plaintiff has alleged that she “is entitled to a monetary judgment against Defendants equal to all of the rent and other monies paid to Defendants by Plaintiff during the term of her tenancy[,]” which the court interprets to request restitution, and (2) Plaintiff therefore has standing to allege this cause of action against HMG irrespective of her allegation that she vacated the property on February 18, 2022.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii); FAC ¶¶ 77, 21; Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 928 [concluding that, because the plaintiff was not in possession of the properties, she had no need of or standing to seek injunctive relief].)

The court denies HMG’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the eighth cause of action for constructive eviction because the face of the pleading states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) agents of a landlord “may also be held liable on any properly pleaded tort causes of action[;]” (2) cases have characterized constructive eviction claims both as tort and contract in certain instances; and (3) this cause of action does not state whether it is based in tort or on the rental agreement, and therefore may be based in tort, such that HMG, as an agent, may be held liable for landlord defendant 9th Street.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii); Stoiber, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d at pp. 929-930; Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 873, 901 [stating that cases have acknowledged a tort claim for wrongful constructive or actual eviction]; Hjelm v. Prometheus Real Estate Group, Inc. (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 1155, 1169 [analyzing Civil Code section 1717 and finding that the claim for constructive eviction arose from the lease].)

ORDER

The court grants defendant Howard Management Group’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to plaintiff Sofia Nemzer’s sixth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1942.4.

The court denies defendant Howard Management Group’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to plaintiff Sofia Nemzer’s fourth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

The court grants plaintiff Sofia Nemzer 20 days leave to file a Third Amended Complaint to cure the defects, if possible, set forth in connection with the sixth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1942.4.

The court orders plaintiff Sofia Nemzer to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

(1)   defendants’ demurrer to first amended complaint

(2)   defendants’ motion to strike portions of first amended complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:             Defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Plaintiffs Jing Du, Yu Bai, and Ye Qiu

(1)   Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with the demurrer and motion to strike.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court sustains plaintiffs Jing Du, Yu Bai, and Ye Qiu’s evidentiary objections to the request for judicial notice filed in support of the reply papers by defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., on October 5, 2023, and therefore denies the request for judicial notice.  (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537 [“The general rule of motion practice . . . is that new evidence is not permitted with reply papers”].)

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Jing Du (“Du”), Yu Bai (“Bai”), and Ye Qiu (“Qiu”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed the operative First Amended Complaint in this action on March 24, 2023, against defendants Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a suspended California corporation, Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming corporation, Amaxi Regional center, LLC, USA Milk Company, LLC, the heirs and devisees of Macie Wang, deceased, Andrew Khun, Jonathan C.M. Lau, and Margaret Lau.  Plaintiffs allege four causes of action for (1) fraud and deceit, (2) negligent misrepresentation, (3) breach of contract, and (4) breach of contract.

Defendants Andrew Khun (“Khun”), Margaret Lau (“Lau”), and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation (“Amaxi”) (collectively, “Defendants”) now move the court for an order (1) sustaining their demurrer to each cause of action alleged by Plaintiffs, and (2) striking from the First Amended Complaint Plaintiffs’ prayers for attorney’s fees, and two allegations set forth in paragraphs 13 and 37.

DEMURRER

The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiffs have alleged, with sufficient particularity, that (1) Khun, Lau, and Macie Wang (“Wang”) created the entity defendants (i.e., including Amaxi) to further their plans to defraud Plaintiffs; (2) Wang and, by extension, Defendants solicited each of the Plaintiffs to provide investments for the entities, and successfully obtained $1,545,000; (3) Wang promised Plaintiffs that they would receive either a full return of their investment in Amaxi in five years, one or more parcels of real estate of equal value, or ownership interest in the business, which statements were false; (4) in reliance on those representations, (i) plaintiffs Du and Bai agreed to go forward with the investment and provided to Wang and Defendants $1,045,000, and (ii) plaintiff Qiu signed a subscription agreement with Amaxi Regional and U.S. Milk Co. and paid to Wang and the other defendants $530,000; and (6) Plaintiffs’ money was never returned.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); FAC ¶¶ 21-24, 26-27, 29-30, 38-40, 44; Apollo Capital Fund, LLC v. Roth Capital Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 226, 242 [“‘ “courts should not . . . seek to absolve the defendant from liability on highly technical requirements of form in pleading.  Pleading facts in ordinary and concise language is as permissible in fraud cases as in any others, and liberal construction of the pleading is as much a duty of the court in these as in other cases” ’”].) 

The court notes that, as Defendants argue in their demurrer, Plaintiffs have not alleged that demurring Defendants made any specific misrepresentations to them.  However, Plaintiffs have alleged that Defendants were engaged in a conspiracy with Wang “to induce Plaintiffs to provide funds to defendants so they could use the funds for their own benefit and to the detriment of Plaintiffs.”  (FAC ¶ 16.)  “To support a conspiracy claim, a plaintiff must allege the following elements: ‘(1) the formation and operation of the conspiracy, (2) wrongful conduct in furtherance of the conspiracy, and (3) damages arising from the wrongful conduct.’”  (AREI II Cases (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1022.)  “It is well settled that ‘ “[b]are” allegations and “rank” conjecture do not suffice for civil conspiracy.’”  (Ibid.)  However, “[w]hile a complaint must contain more than a bare allegation the defendants conspired, a complaint is sufficient if it apprises the defendant of the ‘character and type of facts and circumstances upon which she was relying to establish the conspiracy.’”  (Ibid.) 

As set forth above, Plaintiffs have alleged that (1) Defendants each owned, operated, controlled, and were materially aiding personnel in coordinating efforts to further the fraudulent schemes against Plaintiffs, and (2) Khun and Lau, together with Wang and nonmoving defendant Jonathan C.M. Lau, created the entity defendants, including Amaxi, to further the fraudulent scheme.  (FAC ¶¶ 20-21.)  The court finds that these allegations are sufficient to allege, for purposes of demurrer, that Defendants were engaged in a civil conspiracy to commit fraud by alleging facts showing that (1) Defendants, with Wang and Jonathan C.M. Lau, formed an operation to defraud Plaintiffs, (2) Defendants were involved in coordinating efforts to further the schemes, and (3) Plaintiffs were damaged by their actions.

            The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action for negligent misrepresentation because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiffs have alleged, with sufficient particularity, that (1) Defendants engaged in a civil conspiracy to make the misrepresentations of material fact set forth above, (2) they had no reasonable grounds to believe those representations were true, (3) they made the representations with the intent to induce Plaintiffs’ reliance, and (4) Plaintiffs relied on those statements and suffered resultant damages.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); FAC ¶¶ 20-21 [as to conspiracy claim], 26-27, 29-30, 47, 49-50; Apollo Capital Fund, LLC, supra, 158 Cal.App.4th at p. 243; Borman v. Brown (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1048, 1060 [elements of claim for negligent misrepresentation].)

            The court sustains defendants Khun and Lau’s demurrer to plaintiff Du and Bai’s third cause of action for breach of contract because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against those defendants since Plaintiffs allege that the contract that is the subject of this cause of action was entered into by and between plaintiffs Du and Bai, on the one hand, and Wang and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a suspended California corporation, on the other hand, and therefore have not alleged that defendants Khun and Lau were parties to the subject contract.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); FAC ¶ 52; Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402 [elements of breach of contract cause of action].)

The court overrules Amaxi’s demurrer to plaintiff Du and Bai’s third cause of action for breach of contract because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiffs allege that (1) Du and Bai, entered into a contract with, inter alia, Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a suspended California corporation, and (2) Amaxi is the successor-in-interest to Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a suspended California corporation, which was formed to continue the allegedly fraudulent schemes.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); FAC ¶¶ 52, 5, 22; Miles, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at p. 402.) 

The court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to plaintiff Qiu’s fourth cause of action for breach of contract because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiffs allege that the contract that is the subject of this cause of action was entered into by and between plaintiff Qui, on the one hand, and Wang, Amaxi Regional Center, LLC, and USA Milk Company, LLC, on the other hand, and therefore have not alleged that Defendants were parties to the subject contract.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); FAC ¶ 58; Miles, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at p. 402.) 

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendants move the court for an order striking the following from the First Amended Complaint: (1) the prayer for attorney’s fees; (2) the allegation that “Plaintiffs are informed and believe, and based thereon allege, that defendants M. LAU, KHUN, and J. LAU are the successors-in-interest of decedent Macie Wang,” as set forth in paragraph 13, and (3) the allegation that “KHUN also acknowledged Plaintiffs’ investments in AMAXI NUTRITION, AMAXI REGIONAL and US MILK CO., acknowledged that Plaintiffs were entitled to a refund of their investment monies, and KHUN promised to Plaintiffs that he, through his companies, including AMAXI NUTRITION WYO, would repay their investment monies,” as set forth in paragraph 37.

The court grants Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for attorney’s fees because Plaintiffs have not pleaded facts establishing that a statute or other contractual provision entitles them to request such relief.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)

The court denies Defendants’ motion to strike the allegations in paragraphs 13 and 37 as set forth above because Defendants have not shown that those allegations are “irrelevant, false, or improper[,]” or that they are “not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1683 [motions to strike should not be used as “a procedural ‘line item veto’ for the civil defendant”].)

ORDER

            The court overrules defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation’s demurrer to plaintiffs Jing Du, Yu Bai, and Ye Qiu’s first cause of action for fraud and deceit and second cause of action for negligent misrepresentation.

            The court sustains defendants Andrew Khun and Margaret Lau’s demurrer to plaintiffs Jing Du and Yu Bai’s third cause of action for breach of contract.

            The court overrules Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation’s demurrer to plaintiffs Jing Du and Yu Bai’s third cause of action for breach of contract.

            The court sustains defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation’s demurrer to plaintiff Ye Qiu’s fourth cause of action for breach of contract.

            The court grants defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation’s motion to strike plaintiffs Jing Du, Yu Bai, and Ye Qiu’s prayer for attorney’s fees.

            The court denies all other relief requested in defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation’s motion to strike.

            The court grants Plaintiffs Jing Du, Yu Bai, and Ye Qiu 20 days leave to file a Second Amended Complaint that cures the defects set forth above.

            The court orders defendants Andrew Khun, Margaret Lau, and Amaxi Nutrition Products, Inc., a Wyoming Corporation to give notice of this ruling.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.
