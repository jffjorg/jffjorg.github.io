DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV03170    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

JOSE LUIS SANCHEZ, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

WALT DISNEY PARKS AND RESORTS U.S., INC., et al.,

                       

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  20STCV03170

  Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ motion to uphold highly confidential designation

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 24, 2020, Plaintiffs Jose Luis Sanchez (Sanchez) and Manuel Acevedo Fernandez (Acevedo) filed this action against Walt Disney Parks and Resorts U.S., Inc. (WDPR), Disney Worldwide Services, Inc. (DWWS), Rona Kay (Kay), and Does 1 through 10, alleging violations of FEHA, wrongful discharge, defamation, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. The operative First Amended Complaint, filed January 29, 2020, adds Defendants Diana Eid (Eid) and Janae Bueno (Bueno) and asserts additional FEHA claims. Eid and Bueno were subsequently dismissed on summary adjudication, along with certain FEHA claims.

            Plaintiffs worked for Disney from the 1980s until 2019, when they were terminated. Plaintiffs were terminated for watching pornography at work. Eid and Bueno allegedly witnessed the incident and reported it. Plaintiffs deny watching pornography and maintain that Acevedo was merely showing off pictures of his cats to Sanchez. Plaintiffs claim that their terminations were motivated by bias against their sexual orientation, race, age, and disability.

            On April 16, 2024 Defendants filed the instant ex parte application (treated as a motion) to uphold a “Highly Confidential” or “Attorney’s Eyes Only” designation on information disclosed in an April 8, 2024 letter sent from defense counsel to Plaintiffs’ counsel. Plaintiffs’ counsel filed its opposition on April 24, 2024. Defendants filed their reply on April 30, 2024. 

DISCUSSION

            The stipulated protective order in this case defines “Highly Confidential” information as “[e]xtremely sensitive confidential information or items, the disclosure of which to Plaintiffs would create a substantial risk of serious harm that could not be avoided by less restrictive means.” Defendants seek a highly confidential designation on the information disclosed in defense counsel’s April 8 letter.

            However, the Court does not find that there is a “substantial risk of serious harm.” The relevant parties have the means to obtain the subject material, and it appears they were notified of the issue around late March or early April 2024, meaning they have had over one month to obtain the subject material to the extent they deem it necessary. The time that has passed mitigates the claimed urgency in preventing spoliation. The risks associated with Plaintiffs being informed now are not substantial, in light of the opportunity that the relevant parties have had to obtain relevant evidence and conduct their review. There is no good cause to further foreclose Plaintiffs’ counsel from informing Plaintiffs on the status of their case and hinder Plaintiffs’ right to prepare their case in a timely manner.

            United States v. Padilla (2d Cir. 2000) 203 F.3d 156 is distinguishable because the restriction in that case was justified by concerns over jury and witness tampering, and the court found that the defendants had “no Sixth Amendment right to be warned of [issues] implicating the trial's truth-seeking function.” (Id. at p. 160.) No such concerns are present in this case. As discussed above, the relevant parties have had at least one month to obtain the subject material to the extent they deem it necessary. There are no issues of jury or witness tampering, nor other risks to the integrity of the trial in this case, if Plaintiffs are informed of the April 8 letter at this time.  

            Furthermore, the risk of harm may be “avoided by less restrictive means,” namely, Defendants’ proposed alternative relief. Plaintiffs’ counsel does not oppose the alternative relief requested by Defendants’ motion.

CONCLUSION

            Defendants’ motion to uphold highly confidential designation is DENIED. Defendants’ alternative request is GRANTED.

Case Number: 22STCV34203    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

JANE DOE,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

CASA DE LA FAMILIA, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV34203

  Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses (CRS# 8631, 0353, 7732)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 24, 2022, Plaintiff Jane Doe filed this action against Defendants Casa de la Familia (CDLF), Nogales Psychological Counseling, Inc. (Nogales), and Oscar Aquino (Aquino). The complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9; (2) sexual battery in violation of Civil Code section 1708.5; and (3) negligence. Nogales has been dismissed from the lawsuit.

            According to the complaint, Aquino was employed by CDLF as a marriage and family therapist from April 2019 to May 2020. (Compl. ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that on October 5, 2019, she attended a therapy appointment with Aquino wherein Aquino sexually assaulted her. (Id., ¶¶ 16-17.)

            On April 5 and 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant three motions to compel further responses from Defendant Aquino as to Requests for Admission, Requests for Production, and Special Interrogatories. All three motions are advanced and heard on this date (May 3, 2024). 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).)

MEET AND CONFER

A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Seber Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Requests for Admission and Special Interrogatories

The RFAs and SROGs at issue seek to establish that Defendant Aquino exhibited inappropriate behavior or sexually harassed/assaulted certain patients and that Defendant was reported or disciplined for inappropriate behavior. Defendant objected to the RFAs and SROGs by invoking his Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination and the patients’ right of privacy.  

a. Fifth Amendment

                        1. General Principles

“It is a bedrock principle of American (and California) law, embedded in various state and federal constitutional and statutory provisions, that witnesses may not be compelled to incriminate themselves.” (People v. Trujeque (2015) 61 Cal.4th 227, 267.) “To sustain the privilege, it need only be evident from the implications of the question, in the setting in which it is asked, that a responsive answer to the question or an explanation of why it cannot be answered might be dangerous because injurious disclosure could result.” (Ibid.) “In that regard, a witness's answers need not in themselves support a conviction under a criminal statute, but may ‘furnish a link in the chain of evidence’ needed to prosecute the witness for a crime.” (Ibid.) “Ultimately, a trial court may reject an assertion of the privilege only when it appears to the court ‘perfectly clear, from a careful consideration of all the circumstances in the case, that the witness is mistaken, and that the answers cannot possibly have such tendency’ to incriminate.” (Ibid., emphasis in original.)

            A party’s say-so does not establish the privilege; it is up to the trial court to determine whether the privilege applies. (Trujeque, supra, 61 Cal.4th at pp. 267-68.) “[A] trial court must make ‘a particularized inquiry, deciding, in connection with each specific area that the questioning party wishes to explore, whether or not the privilege is well-founded.’ Although the witness may have a valid claim to the privilege with respect to some questions, the scope of that privilege may not extend to all relevant questions. The witness may be totally excused only if the court finds that he could ‘legitimately refuse to answer essentially all relevant questions.’” (Id. at p. 268.) “This has long been the rule in California in both criminal and civil proceedings.” (Ibid.) 

                        2. Application

            Here, Defendant’s answers regarding whether he sexually harassed or assaulted other individuals, or was disciplined for such, fall within the “chain of evidence” that could ultimately result in criminal liability. Plaintiff argues that the actions implicated by the RFAs and SROGs, while inappropriate, do not amount to criminal behavior. However, some of the requests clearly describe sexual battery, which is a crime. (See, e.g., RFA Nos. 2, 3, 21; SROG Nos. 113, 114.) Moreover, the law is clear that “a witness's answers need not in themselves support a conviction under a criminal statute.” (Trujeque, supra, 61 Cal.4th at p. 267.) “The test is whether the statement might tend to incriminate, not whether it might tend to lead to an actual prosecution or, stated slightly differently, whether the statement could, not would, be used against the witness.” (People v. Seijas (2005) 36 Cal.4th 291, 305, emphasis in original.)

            Given the nature of the case and the information sought, “inappropriate behavior” in this context clearly involves sexual assault or sexual battery, which are crimes. Even if some of the questions do not directly implicate criminal conduct, Defendant’s answers regarding whether he was reported or disciplined for sexual misconduct, or made sexually charged comments, may “tend to incriminate” him as part of the “chain of evidence.” Therefore, it does not “clearly appear” to the Court that Defendant’s answers “cannot possibly” have a tendency to incriminate Defendant. (See Seijas, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 305.) The Court finds that Defendant properly asserted his privilege against self-incrimination. 

            In reply, Plaintiff cites to Maldonado v. Superior Court (2012) 53 Cal.4th 1112, 1127-28 for the proposition that a Fifth Amendment violation does not occur until incriminating information is used against a defendant in a criminal case. However, Maldonado does not preclude a trial court in a civil case from restricting discovery based on concerns over self-incrimination. A party may invoke the Fifth Amendment to refuse to answer questions in a noncriminal proceeding so long as the privilege is “specifically claimed on a particular question and the matter [is] submitted to the court for its determination as to the validity of the claim.” (Warford v. Medeiros (1984) 160 Cal.App.3d 1035, 1045.) A “trial court has broad discretion in ruling on discovery matters.” (Perkins v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 761, 765.) For the reasons articulated above, the Court finds that Defendant properly declined to answer the subject RFAs and SROGs based on the Fifth Amendment.

b. Privacy

            The information sought is not otherwise precluded from disclosure by privacy. Defendant cites no authority supporting the proposition that information about his inappropriate conduct constitutes “protected medical information” under HIPAA or CMIA. Nor does the information implicate the psychotherapist-patient privilege, as the alleged sexually harassing comments are not “confidential communications.” (See Evid. Code, § 1014.) The information is directly relevant, and Plaintiff’s need for the information would outweigh the privacy interests. (See Williams v. Sup. Ct. (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) Nonetheless, as discussed above, Defendant’s refusal to answer is justified by the privilege against self-incrimination.  

II. Requests for Production

“A representation of inability to comply . . . shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)

Defendant responded to each of the subject RFPs by repeating the following statement: “Responding is not in possession, custody, or control of any DOCUMENTS responsive to this request.” This response is not compliant because it merely asserts that Defendant is not in possession of responsive documents without providing any of the information required by Section 2031.230. While Defendant cannot be compelled to produce documents that he does not have, his written responses must still comply with the Code. Defendant’s subsequent explanations during meet and confer and in his opposition do not negate the fact that his responses are defective as currently written. Therefore, amendment is necessary.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to Requests for Admission and Special Interrogatories are DENIED. Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED. Defendant shall produce further responses to the subject RFPs within 15 days of this order.


Case Number: 23STCP03627    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

22ND/INDIAN SCHOOL DEVELOPMENT LLC,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

CMS EQUITY, INC.,

                        Defendant.

 

  Case No.:  23STCP03627

  Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

Plaintiff’s motion to amend judgment

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 3, 2023, Plaintiff 22nd/Indian School Development LLC filed an application for entry of judgment on sister-state judgment. The case arises from an underlying judgment in Arizona in favor of Plaintiff against Defendant CMS Equity, Inc. Plaintiff’s application was granted on October 3, 2023. Therefore, Plaintiff now has a judgment against Defendant in Los Angeles Superior Court.

            On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to amend the judgment to add judgment debtors based on alter ego. Plaintiff seeks to add Christopher Smith, sole owner and member of CMS Equity, as well as ten other corporate entities of which Smith or CMS is the sole owner or member. There is no opposition to the motion.  

LEGAL STANDARD

            “When jurisdiction is, by the Constitution or this Code, or by any other statute, conferred on a Court or judicial officer, all the means necessary to carry it into effect are also given; and in the exercise of this jurisdiction, if the course of proceeding be not specifically pointed out by this Code or the statute, any suitable process or mode of proceeding may be adopted which may appear most conformable to the spirit of this Code.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 187.)

“Pursuant to section 187, a trial court has jurisdiction to modify a judgment to add additional judgment debtors.” (McClellan v. Northridge Park Townhome Owners Ass'n (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 746, 752.) “Judgments are often amended to add additional judgment debtors on the grounds that a person or entity is the alter ego of the original judgment debtor.” (NEC Electronics Inc. v. Hurt (1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 772, 778.) “This is an equitable procedure based on the theory that the court is not amending the judgment to add a new defendant but is merely inserting the correct name of the real defendant.” (Ibid.) “Such a procedure is an appropriate and complete method by which to bind new . . . defendants where it can be demonstrated that in their capacity as alter ego of the [judgment debtor] they in fact had control of the previous litigation, and thus were virtually represented in the lawsuit.” (Ibid.)

DISCUSSION

“The alter ego doctrine arises when a plaintiff comes into court claiming that an opposing party is using the corporate form unjustly and in derogation of the plaintiff's interests . . . In certain circumstances the court will disregard the corporate entity and will hold the individual shareholders liable for the actions of the corporation.” (Greenspan v. LADT LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 486, 510.) Establishing alter ego “generally requires the proponent to demonstrate two elements: (1) a unity of interest and ownership such that the separate personalities of the corporation and the individual do not exist; and (2) an inequitable result if the corporate identity is not disregarded.” (JPV I L.P. v. Koetting (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 172, 189.) “In addition, even if all the formal elements necessary to establish alter ego liability are not present, an unnamed party may be included as a judgment debtor if ‘the equities overwhelmingly favor’ the amendment and it is necessary to prevent an injustice.” (Carolina Casualty Ins. Co. v. L.M. Ross Law Group, LLP (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1188-1189.)

                        1. Unity of Interest

Courts have found the following factors determinative in applying alter ego: that the individual defendant dominated and controlled the entity defendant; that a unity of interest and ownership existed between the individual defendant and entity defendant; that the entity defendant was a mere shell and conduit for the individual defendant’s affairs; that the entity defendant was inadequately capitalized; that the entity defendant failed to abide by corporate formalities; and that the individual defendant used the entity defendant’s assets as her own. (See Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 235-36.)

Here, the undisputed evidence shows that Smith wholly owns CMS Equity (Plntf.’s RJN, Ex. E) and has used CMS Equity’s funds for personal expenses (Kintzele Decl., Ex. B.) The evidence further shows that either Smith or CMS solely owns or manages the other entities. (Plntf.’s RJN, Ex. F-M.) Smith freely transfers assets between CMS and his other entities without any of the associated formalities. (See Kintzele Decl., Ex. B.) This evidence sufficiently shows a unity of interest between Smith, CMS, and the other entities.

            2. Inequitable Result

The undisputed inference from the above evidence is that “Smith has shifted and spread his assets among the Proposed Entities which do not have judgments entered against them in an attempt to prevent Plaintiff from collecting on its own judgment against CMS Equity, Inc.” (Mtn. 16:23-25.) CMS’s insolvency renders direct collection against CMS unlikely. Therefore, the Court finds that not treating Smith and the other entities as alter egos would result in injustice by allowing wrongdoers to escape liability. Accordingly, Smith and the other proposed entities should be added as judgment debtors.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion to amend judgment is GRANTED. The following individuals or entities are added as judgment debtors to the judgment of October 3, 2023: Christopher Smith; MRC AZ Rehab Orangewood, LLC; Clarendon 1311, LLC; La Junta Troon LLC; Red Gravity, LLC; McDonald 4141, LLC; M&CS Holdings AZ, LLC; Christopher Smith & Associates LLC; MS Square Construction, LLC; Grace Monroe Legacy Rentals, LLC; and 23rd 4020, LLC.  

     

 


Case Number: 23STCV09930    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

ALEXANDER KHAZAI,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

SAMMY ZABLEN, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV09930

  Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant mckesson corporation’s demurrer to first amended complaint

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 3, 2023, Plaintiff Alexander Khazai filed this action against numerous Defendants, asserting 16 causes of action arising from a business transaction. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to properly compensate him for his services. Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint on January 11, 2024, reducing his claims to (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) fraud, and (4) accounting.  

            On March 13, 2024, Defendant McKesson Corporation filed the instant demurrer to the first and fourth causes of action in the FAC. Plaintiff filed his opposition on April 22, 2024. Defendant filed its reply on April 26, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether a pleading states a cause of action or defense. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the pleading, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action or defense. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Yun Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Breach of Written Contract (First Cause of Action)

            To establish breach of contract, a plaintiff must show: (1) the contract existed, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) A breach of contract can be alleged by simply “plead[ing] the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402.)

            Plaintiff alleges that he arranged business relationships between the various defendants regarding the sale and distribution of personal protective equipment (PPE) during the COVID pandemic. The action arises from Defendants’ alleged failure to pay Plaintiff his commissions. Defendant McKesson’s demurrer argues that the FAC attributes no actions to McKesson, much less that McKesson formed a contract with Plaintiff.

            However, the FAC alleges that Defendants Michael Merchant and Andy Shantz acted as McKesson’s agents and that they made oral and written promises that Plaintiff would be paid a commission of one penny per mask for connecting McKesson with reliable sources of PPE. (FAC ¶¶ 9, 41.) Plaintiff alleges that he fulfilled his end of the bargain by introducing Merchant and Shantz to Defendant Cecilia Tech, which later supplied McKesson with hundreds of millions of masks. (Id., ¶ 42.) Despite millions of dollars worth of masks sold, Plaintiff has not been paid any commission. (Id., ¶ 43.) Plaintiff alleges that McKesson, through Shantz and Merchant, breached its agreement with Plaintiff by failing to compensate Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 44.)      

These facts satisfy the elements for breach of contract. The FAC is sufficiently clear that Plaintiff facilitated transactions between the defendants in exchange for compensation and that Defendants, including McKesson, failed to pay as agreed. Whether Merchant and Shantz were truly authorized to contract with Plaintiff on behalf of McKesson is a factual matter. Whether the parties truly executed a written contract, and which writings constitute that contract, are also factual matters. For pleading purposes, the allegations must be taken as true. The fact that Plaintiff did not attach a written contract to the FAC or plead the contract verbatim is not dispositive, as a breach of contract may be pled by alleging its legal effect, which Plaintiff has done. Therefore, the FAC properly pleads breach of contract.  

II. Accounting

            “A cause of action for an accounting requires a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.” (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179.)

            The alleged arrangement between Plaintiff and McKesson, discussed above, constitutes a relationship involving a balance owed which requires an accounting to determine. Specifically, McKesson allegedly agreed, through Merchant and Shantz, to pay one penny per mask and exchanged millions of masks as a result of Plaintiff’s services. These facts establish that an accounting is necessary to determine the precise amount owed to Plaintiff. Therefore, the FAC properly pleads a claim for accounting.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant McKesson’s demurrer is OVERRULED.


Case Number: 23STCV31794    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 32

 

ANTHONY HAAG,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

MK DIAMOND PRODUCTS, INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV31794

  Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant brian delahaut’s demurrer to complaint

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On December 28, 2023, Plaintiff Anthony Haag filed this action against Defendants MK Diamond Products, Inc., Brian Delahaut, and CNA Financial Corporation. The parties have stipulated to Continental Casualty Company substituting in for CNA Financial Corporation.

The complaint asserts twelve causes of action arising from a settlement agreement reached as part of an employment discrimination action filed by Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges that he entered the settlement agreement with Defendants MK Diamond and Delahaut. Pursuant to the contract, MK Diamond and Delahaut agreed to pay $93,750 to Plaintiff and $143,750 to Plaintiff’s attorneys in exchange for Plaintiff releasing his claims. MK Diamond and Delahaut had an insurance policy with Defendant CNA Financial, which disbursed the funds. MK Diamond and Delahaut thereafter issued a check to Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges that the check bounced and that Defendants failed to reissue a check or otherwise make payment despite giving assurances. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants have so far only paid him $16,000.

On March 26, 2024, Defendant Delahaut filed the instant demurrer to the complaint. Plaintiff filed his opposition on April 22, 2024. Defendant filed his reply on April 26, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether a pleading states a cause of action or defense. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the pleading, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action or defense. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Shaneyfelt Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Delahaut’s Personal Liability

            “Persons are not normally bound by an agreement entered into by a corporation in which they have an interest or are employees.” (Suh v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1504, 1513.) Defendant Delahaut argues that the causes of action premised on the settlement agreement are improperly asserted against him because he was not a party to that agreement in his individual capacity. The contract, attached as Exhibit A to the complaint, shows it was between Plaintiff and MK Diamond, with Delahaut signing as Vice President of MK Diamond. Furthermore, Plaintiff alleges that at all relevant times, Delahaut “was an agent or employee of PROMISOR [MK Diamond], and in doing the things alleged, was acting withing the scope of that agency or employment.” (Compl. ¶ 4.)  

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Delahaut may be held personally liable based on four distinct theories: (1) Delahaut personally benefitted under the agreement; (2) Delahaut intentionally made himself a party to the agreement; (3) Delahaut is liable in restitution; and (4) Delahaut is liable under Civil Code section 2343(3) through his own torts. (Opp. 2:1-25.) 

            a. Personal Benefit

            Plaintiff cites to Cohen v. TNP 2008 Participating Notes Program, LLC (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 840, 861 for the proposition that courts have compelled officers and employees of a corporation to arbitration, despite those individuals being non-signatories to the arbitration agreement, where those individuals personally benefitted from the agreement. Plaintiff argues that Delahaut benefitted from the settlement agreement because Plaintiff agreed to release claims against Delahaut as well. However, the exception discussed in Cohen pertains specifically to arbitration agreements and cannot be read to mean that any non-signatory who benefits from a contract can be held personally liable for breach of contract.  

            b. Assuming Responsibility

            “Where an agent intentionally makes himself a party to a contract, recovery can be had against him as well.” (Warington Lumber Co. v. Fullerton Mortg. & Escrow Co. (1963) 222 Cal.App.2d 706, 708.) In Warington, the facts supported such a theory: “an advance on a loan to a contractor was made by check payable jointly to the contractor and defendant mortgage and escrow company that had previously agreed to withhold funds due plaintiff who had supplied lumber to the contractor, defendant, on receiving the check, became the holder of funds for plaintiff; defendant was not a mere disbursing agent, but was a principal under the agreement with plaintiff.” (Id. at p. 708.) No similar facts are alleged here. There are no allegations suggesting that Delahaut took over the contract and assumed responsibility in his personal capacity. 

            c. Restitution

            To the extent that Delahaut personally received a benefit at Plaintiff’s expense that should be returned to Plaintiff in equity, that still does not demonstrate that Delahaut was a party to the settlement agreement and does not support a breach of contract claim against Delahaut. The complaint also lacks allegations demonstrating that Delahaut personally received or retained money belonging to Plaintiff. Therefore, the complaint generally fails to allege facts supporting restitution against Delahaut, even apart from the contract.

            d. Independent Wrong

            “One who assumes to act as an agent is responsible to third persons as a principal for his acts in the course of his agency . . . [w]hen his acts are wrongful in their nature.” (Civ. Code, § 2343(3).) “‘[A]cts are wrongful in their nature’ for purposes of Civil Code section 2343 when they constitute an independent tort.” (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 693.) “Section 2343 recognizes the elementary rule that everyone is liable for his torts, and an agent or servant no exception merely because such.” (Id. at p. 694.)

Plaintiff argues that Delahaut “breached the settlement agreement (1st Cause of Action) by committing a number of torts and wrongful acts against Plaintiff and can be held liable as a matter of California law.” (Opp. 8:21-22.) However, Plaintiff cites no authority supporting the proposition that a contract can be breached through tort. In fact, “[a]n omission to perform a contract obligation is never a tort, unless that omission is also an omission of a legal duty.” (Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 551.) To the extent Plaintiff was not fully paid the amount due under the settlement agreement, that is simply a breach of contract. And as discussed above, the current allegations reveal that only MK Diamond is liable under the contract. To the extent Delahaut independently committed a tort by personally taking Plaintiff’s money, the complaint lacks such allegations. Therefore, the complaint fails to allege that Delahaut committed an independent wrong supporting personal liability.   

II. Uncertainty

            Delahaut argues that the complaint is uncertain because it asserts the causes of action against “Defendants” without specifying which actions Delahaut conducted in his personal capacity. As discussed above, the complaint as currently pled does not establish that Delahaut has a personal obligation to pay Plaintiff. Plaintiff argues that he is entitled to assert all claims against all defendants, and that the reference to “Defendants” clearly includes Delahaut.

However, the allegations and incorporated documents in this particular case demonstrate that Delahaut acted in a representative capacity for MK Diamond rather than in his personal capacity. (See Compl. ¶ 4, Ex. A.) Under these circumstances, the general reference to “Defendants” fails to raise a reasonable inference that Delahaut is personally liable. The complaint is not sufficiently clear that Delahaut personally took or retained Plaintiff’s money.

In sum, the current allegations fail to establish that Delahaut is personally liable for breach of contract or for the remaining causes of action based on tortious conduct. Therefore, amendment is warranted.          

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Delahaut’s demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.
