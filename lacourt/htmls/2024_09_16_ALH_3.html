I.            INTRODUCTION

Defendant Li Cui, M.D. (“Defendant”) moves to compel further responses to Demand for Production of Documents (Set One) Nos. 1, 2, 5, 8, and 9 plaintiffs Maribel Arreola-Gonzalez, Saul Gonzalez Arreola, and Eva Gonzalez Arreola (collectively, “Plaintiffs”). Defendant argues that further responses are required because Plaintiffs’ responses are not Code-compliant and included objections which were waived. Defendant also claims that Plaintiffs have failed to produce any documents.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A party responding to inspection demands must either state that it will comply with the demand, that it lacks the ability to comply with the demand, or assert an objection. A party who fails to serve a timely response waives any objectins, including those based on privilege or work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) Therefore, a party who fails to serve a timely response has two options in responding and may only provide a statement of compliance or a representation of the party’s inability to comply with the inspection demand; the Code of Civil Procedure sets forth specific requirements for both.

First, a statement of compliance must specify that the inspection will be allowed “either in whole or in part” and that “all documents that are in the party’s possession, custody, or control of that part and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.220.)

Second, “a representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)

III.        DISCUSSION

Demand No. 1 asks Plaintiffs to produce “[a]ny and all written materials, bills, notes, memoranda or documents in support of or which reflect special damages as prayed for in the complaint.”  

Plaintiffs responded that the demand was “not stated with reasonable particularity” and was “uncertain, overbroad, and compound.” Plaintiffs also agreed to produce “responsive records . . . subject to and without waiving Plaintiff’s attorney-client and work product privileges, including those relating to consulting experts.”  Plaintiffs do not dispute Defendant’s claim that their responses were untimely. Therefore, they have impermissibly asserted objections in their responses. Accordingly, a further response is required.

Demand No. 2 requests “[a]ll documents containing facts supporting or relating to the allegations of [the] complaint regarding the negligence and/or other such wrongful conduct on the part of this propounding defendant.” (Motion, Ex. C.) Plaintiffs’ response recite an entirely different inspection demand which requests copies of insurance policies and agree to produce medical and health insurance policies. This wholly unresponsive statement is not Code-compliant.

Demand No. 5 requests “[a]ll documents evidencing the financial assistance [Plaintiffs] received from decedent prior to his death.” Plaintiffs stated they would “produce responsive financial records located after a diligent search subject to an appropriate protective order.” Plaintiffs cannot qualify their production by only agreeing to produce “financial records” and do not have a basis for requiring a protective order prior to producing any documents.

Demand Nos. 8 and 9 seek all documents evidencing loss of earnings prior to the decedent’s death, loss of past or future earnings, or loss of financial support.  Plaintiffs agree to produce responsive records but impermissibly assert the attorney-client and work product privileges. Therefore, a further response is required.

The Court notes that Plaintiffs claim in their opposition brief that documents have already been produced. Plaintiff’s claim misses the point because the motion seeks further verified responses and there is no evidence that further responses have been served; therefore, given the inadequacy of Plaintiffs’ responses, the motion is not moot.

Yet, despite the inadequacy of Plaintiffs’ responses, Defendant should have filed three motions and paid three filing fees because Defendant seeks orders compelling three plaintiffs to each serve further responses. Therefore, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED on the condition that two additional filing fees are paid. Plaintiffs’ verified further responses are due no later than 20 days after Defendant files proof that the two filing fees have been paid.

Monetary sanctions are imposed on Plaintiffs and counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the amount of $585, consisting of 2 hours at defense counsel’s hourly rate of $205 and $180 in filing fees. Like further responses, sanctions are due no later than 20 days after Defendant files proof that the additional filing fees have been paid.
