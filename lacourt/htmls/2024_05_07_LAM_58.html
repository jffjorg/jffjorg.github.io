DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV22721    Hearing Date: May 7, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce Iwasaki 

Department 58 

 

Hearing Date:              May 7, 2024

Case Name:                 Campbell v. BMW North America, LLC, et al.

Case No.:                    20STCV22721                   

Motion:                       Motion to Tax Costs

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Danya Campbell

Responding Party:      Defendant BMW of North America, LLC

 

Tentative Ruling:  The motion to tax costs is granted in part, and $9,335.19 are deducted from the costs award, yielding a total award of $32,251.66.

 

 

This is a lemon law action arising from Plaintiff Danya Campbell’s lease of a 2018 BMW 530e. (Complaint ¶ 6.) This matter came on for trial on March 13, 2023, and the jury rendered its verdict on March 24, 2023, finding in Plaintiff’s favor on the implied warranty claim in the amount of $10,882.47.

On July 12, 2023, this court denied Plaintiff’s motion for new trial. On January 10, 2024, this court denied Plaintiff’s motion to invalidate Defendant’s section 998 offer. Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees was granted on March 11, 2024, in the amount of $37,589.50, with costs in the amount of $3,911.48.

Defendant filed a memorandum of costs seeking a total $41,586.65 on February 20, 2024. Plaintiff filed the present motion to tax costs on March 7, 2024. Defendant filed an opposition on April 24, and Plaintiff filed a reply on April 30, 2024.

Legal Standard

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(a)(4).” (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1700, subd. (b)(1).)

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (b) [], guarantees prevailing parties in civil litigation awards of the costs expended in the litigation: ‘Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.’” (Williams v. Chino Valley Independent Fire Dist. (“Williams”) (2015) 61 Cal.4th 97, 100.).

“If the items on a verified cost bill appear proper charges, they are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred.” (Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Service, Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.) Although individual cost items are ordinarily challenged by a motion to tax costs, no cost-item is effectively put in issue by “mere statements” claiming them to be unreasonable. (Ibid.) However, where “it cannot be determined from the face of the cost bill whether the items are proper,” “the mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a ‘proper objection’ to an item.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131, 132.)

Analysis

Defendant has filed a costs memorandum seeking a total of $41,586.24 in costs. Defendant is entitled to costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 998, having served an offer under that section for $22,736.02 on September 16, 2021, which was greater than Plaintiff’s ultimate recovery amounting to $10,882.47. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 998, subd. (c)(1).) The court determined this offer to be valid in its order of January 10, 2024, denying Plaintiff’s motion to invalidate the offer.

Plaintiff presents a number of objections to these cost items, which are discussed in turn below.

Plaintiff argues that of the $2,026.96 sought in filing fees, that only one item charged by Defendant — a $61.65 fee dated June 5, 2023 for a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict — constitutes a compensable cost under Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5, subd. 9a)(1). Plaintiff argues that the remainder of the charges identified in Defendant’s Attachment 1g are not filing fees. (Motion at pp. 3–4.)

Plaintiff’s argument is unpersuasive, as the fees identified in attachment 1g are compensable as electronic filing fees under Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5, subd. (a)(14), which renders such fees compensable “if a court requires or orders electronic filing or service of documents.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (a)(14).) Such an order is in effect at this court. (See LASC Local Rule 3.4, subd. (a).) Moreover, the two largest charges in the cost memo’s filing-fee section are, as explained by Defendant in opposition, the fees charged by Defendant’s electronic filing service for posting jury fees, which include the amounts of the jury fees themselves. (Opposition at p. 3; Costs Memo Att. 1g invoices; see 3/21/2023 and 3/24/2023 Notices of Posting Trial Fees.) These costs too are compensable under Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1).

Plaintiff argues that none of Defendant’s claimed $14,451.65 in deposition costs should be awarded. (Motion at pp. 4–5.) Plaintiff notes that nine of the eleven depositions cited in the costs memo were noticed and taken by Plaintiff, not Defendant. (Motion at pp. 4–5.) Plaintiff also argues that the section 998 offer, which marked the starting point from which costs may be sought, was served on the eve of Plaintiff’s deposition, without sufficient time for Plaintiff and counsel to confer as to its merits, meaning the $1,982.30 charge for Plaintiff’s deposition ought to be excluded. (Motion at p. 4.) Plaintiff further argues that the $7,267.70 charge for the deposition of Plaintiff’s expert William Charles Zimmer contains excessive, extraneous charges merely for the convenience of Defendant’s counsel. (Motion at pp. 4–5.)

Plaintiff’s arguments as to the deposition costs are unpersuasive. Defendant is expressly permitted to claim costs associated with the depositions noticed by Plaintiff. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (a)(a)(A) [compensable deposition costs include “an original and one copy of those taken by the claimant and one copy of depositions taken by the party against whom costs are allowed”].) That is what Defendant seeks with respect to nine out of the eleven depositions here. And the fact that Defendant’s expert did not rely on these depositions does not mean that Defendant sought its own copies unreasonably.

Moreover, Plaintiff offers no authority for the proposition that her own deposition cannot be considered a “postoffer” costs under section 998, when that deposition occurred the day after the offer was sent. Defendant is entitled to costs incurred after the offer was made, not only to those incurred after a sufficient amount of time has passed for the receiving party to evaluate the offer. (Code Civ. Proc. § 998, subd. (c)(1).)

However, certain costs associated with the Zimmer deposition are properly taxed. The bulk of the cost associated with a that deposition — $5,311.70 — is not improper, but was evidently the result of a deposition divided into separate “A.M.” and “P.M.” sessions. (Costs Memo Att. 4e.) The remaining charges, however, for “rough draft[s]” of both sessions, “litigation services & logistics,” “professional attendance,” and “virtual services,” are unexplained and amount to $1,956.00 of the charges incurred. Thus $1,956.00 of this charge is properly taxed.

Plaintiff next objects to the $2,022.60 sought by Defendant in connection with “models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits.” (Motion at pp. 5–6.) Plaintiff argues that $49.61 of the costs sought were incurred after trial ended. (Motion at p. 5.) Plaintiff also argues that these costs were merely for the convenience of counsel, and not reasonably necessary. (Motion at pp. 5–6.)

There is little basis to conclude that the bulk of these exhibit charges were only for the convenience of Defendant’s counsel, as the preparation of exhibits for trial was a necessary aspect of the defense. Moreover, Defendant in opposition identifies the costs that Plaintiff challenges as post-trial expenses as being costs incurred for delivering exhibits to the court during trial. (Lee Decl. ¶ 16.) Defendant is entitled to the fees for the preparation of these exhibits, although those charges associated with their messenger delivery — amounting to $382.35 — are separate and taxable. (See Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Family Services (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 675, 696.) Thus $382.35 is taxed.

Plaintiff’s objections to the $13,369.46 in court reporter fees are unpersuasive, as they rest on the erroneous assumption that only court-ordered reporter fees are compensable. (Motion at p. 6.) Section 1033.5 authorizes “[c]ourt reporter fees as established by statute.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (a)(11; Gov. Code § 68086, subd. (c).) Plaintiff relies on the provision of Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5 prohibiting cost awards for “[t]ranscripts of court proceedings not ordered by the court.” (Motion at p. 6.; Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (b0(5).) However, most of “[t]hese charges are not for transcripts. They are for court reporter fees, an entirely different expense. The parties have to pay the court reporter regardless of whether anyone orders transcripts.” (Chaaban v. Wet Seal, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 49, 58.) Only one $186 charge for a certified copy of a transcript of the final status conference fits within the ambit of the prohibitory provision cited by Plaintiff. (Costs Memo Att. 12c.) Thus this $186 charge is taxed.

 

Plaintiff objects to various costs associated with electronic filing and service. (Opposition at pp. 6–7.) These include $275 charged for serving courtesy copies upon the court, $141 to electronically serve documents between the parties, $257 to serve a subpoena, and $91.60 to download documents from the court website. Plaintiff is correct that the costs for service are not recoverable, because fees “for the electronic filing or service of documents” is compensable only “if a court requires or orders electronic filing or service of documents.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (a)(14).) Here, this court requires electronic filing, but not electronic service. (See LASC Local Rule 3.4, subd. (a).) Accordingly, $764.60 is taxed from this category.

 

Plaintiff finally objects to various “other” costs sought in the costs memo. “Where costs are not expressly allowed by the statute, the burden is on the party claiming the costs to show that the charges were reasonable and necessary.” (Foothill-De Anza Community College Dist. v. Emerich (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 11, 29.) Plaintiff here objects to Defendant’s claimed costs for mediation, meals, messengers, service of a subpoena, outside counsel fees, an easel for trial, and parking and mileage fees for trial.

The mediation costs ($2,400) and service of subpoena ($210) are justified as proper costs. As Plaintiff notes, even fees for unsuccessful mediations may be compensable when the mediation was court-ordered, as the mediations were here. (Motion at p. 8, citing Gibson v. Bobroff (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1209.) Defendant in opposition justifies the expense to subpoena Plaintiff’s expert based on the belated disclosure by Plaintiff that they did not intend for their expert to be present at trial. (Lee Decl. ¶ 21.)

The remaining “other” costs are properly taxed. The expenses for meals would have been incurred absent this litigation, and the $379.26 sought for their reimbursement are properly taxed. Likewise, “[r]outine expenses for local travel by attorneys or other firm employees are not reasonably necessary to the conduct of litigation.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 775–776.) Thus the $917.08 for parking and mileage related to trial is properly taxed. Defendant also does not justify the $215 in messenger services, save to cite one charge for retrieving materials from the courthouse. (Opposition at pp. 13–14.) Defendant offers no authority for its claiming of $4,484.00 in attorney fees for consulting with corporate counsel related to Plaintiff’s post-trial motions, and these costs are also taxable. Finally, Defendant does not justify the $50.90 charged for the purchase of an easel for use at trial. Accordingly, $6,046.24 is properly taxed from these “other” costs.

In summary, Plaintiff’s motion to tax costs is granted in part, and $9,335.19 in costs are deducted from the costs award, yielding a total cost award payable by Plaintiff to Defendant of $32,251.66.

 

The parties are ordered to meet and confer to reconcile the fee and cost awards the Court has made to each side. 

 

 


Case Number: 22STCV02810    Hearing Date: May 7, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce Iwasaki 

Department 58 

 

Hearing Date:              May 7, 2024

Case Name:                 Lopez v. Fell, et al.

Case No.:                    22STCV02810                   

Motion:                       Motion to Bifurcate

Moving Party:             Defendants Cliff Fell and Califa Catering, LLC

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Robert Lopez

 

Tentative Ruling:  The motion to bifurcate is denied.

 

 

This is an action for employment and disability discrimination and harassment. The First Amended Complaint (FAC) alleges that Plaintiff Robert Lopez (Plaintiff0 worked for Defendants Califa Catering LLC and Cliff Fell, who assigned Plaintiff to a position catering for Defendant Natural Hair Productions. (FAC ¶¶ 25–37.) Plaintiff was terminated days after seeking leave for an MRI appointment related to a hand injury. (FAC ¶¶ 41–45.)

Trial in this matter is set for July 29, 2024. Defendants Califa and Fell filed the present motion to bifurcate on August 15, 2023. Plaintiff filed an opposition on September 19, 2023. Defendants filed a reply on April 30, 2024.

Legal Standard

“The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action . . . or of any separate issue . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (b).) Additionally, “[t]he court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 598.)

“It is within the discretion of the court to bifurcate issues or order separate trials of actions, such as for breach of contract and bad faith, and to determine the order in which those issues are to be decided.” (Royal Surplus Lines Ins. Co., Inc. v. Ranger Ins. Co. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 193, 205.) “The major objective of bifurcated trials is to expedite and simplify the presentation of evidence.” (Foreman & Clark Corp. v. Fallon (1971) 3 Cal.3d 875, 888.)

Defendants Café Califa and Cliff Fell (Defendants) seek to bifurcate trial into a first phase to determine Defendants’ liability — whether they discriminated or retaliated against Plaintiff Robert Lopez (Plaintiff) — and a second phase to determine Plaintiff’s damages. (Motion at p. 1.) Defendants argue that such an order would promote efficiency by potentially eliminating the need for a damages phase. (Motion at p. 2.)

No bifurcation is warranted, as Defendants’ case for efficiency depends entirely upon a verdict in the liability phase of trial in their favor. Without such a verdict, bifurcation is likely to substantially increase the burden of trial upon all parties, the court, and the jury. If liability is found, the jury would be required to hear additional testimony, a second round of closing arguments, additional jury instructions, and a second round of jury deliberations. Thus bifurcation here risks more inefficiency than Defendants propose to reduce.

Defendants argue that Defendant Natural Hair Productions was only added as a defendant with Plaintiff’s filing of the FAC on April 8, 2024, and that because this Defendant has not appeared, ruling on the present motion should be continued to allow input from this new defendant. (Reply at p. 3.) But Defendant does not articulate how the introduction of this new defendant favors bifurcation. Based on the showing made in this motion, no such order is necessary.

The motion to bifurcate the trial is therefore denied.
