[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF PHILIP HOLGUIN-CABAN

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

October 18, 2024

 

Plaintiff Maria De La Luz Santacruz (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Aldi Inc. (“Defendant”) (erroneously named as “Aldi Supermarket Company”) for premises liability and general negligence arising from a slip and fall. On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for an order compelling the deposition of Philip Holguin-Caban and imposing sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record in the amount of $3,150.

Mr. Holguin-Caban was working as the associate/shift manager of Defendant’s store when Plaintiff slipped and fell on January 5, 2021. (Motion, p. 4.) Plaintiff argues that his deposition is critical because there is no video footage of the incident or the period before the incident because the cameras were not operating on the day of the incident. Defendant identified Holguin-Caban as someone who had knowledge of various matter relating to the incident, including the alleged dangerous condition and the incident report. (Motion, Boris Decl., Ex. 1.) Multiple efforts were made to schedule Mr. Holguin-Caban’s deposition from January 4 to March 13, 2024, before defense counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that Mr. Holguin-Caban was not capable of testifying as a percipient witness due to an illness which rendered him incompetent. (Motion, Boris Decl., Ex. 9.)

Evidence Code § 701(a) disqualifies a person as a witness “if he or she is: (1) Incapable of expressing himself or herself concerning the matter so as to be understood, either directly or through interpretation by one who can understand him; or (2) Incapable of understanding the duty of a witness to tell the truth.” (Evid. Code, § 701, subd. (a).) The burden of proving a witness's incompetence lies with the objecting party. (People v. Augustin (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 444, 448.)

Here, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not shown that Mr. Holguin-Caban is incapable of testifying because even if he has some cognitive impairment, a mental status exam, which appears to have been conducted on April 26, 2023, showed that his thought process and thought content were “normal” and his insight and judgment was “good.” (Motion, Ex. 14.) Although the exam results noted that Mr. Holguin-Caban “[d[emonstrated word-finding difficulties”, this cursory statement is insufficient to demonstrate that he is disqualified as a witness for being incapable of expressing himself. (See People v. Augustin, supra, 112 Cal.App.4th at p. 448-449 [“A witness who is merely difficult to understand is not inherently or automatically incompetent under section 701.”])

In opposition, Defendant merely refers to the records included in Plaintiff’s moving papers and reiterates that Mr. Holguin-Caban has been diagnosed with health issues which impair his ability to sit for a deposition. (Opp., p. 3.) Defendant asserts that compelling a deposition would “cause undue hardship and potentially exacerbate his medical condition” but does not submit any recent evidence, such as a doctor’s note, to support this assertion. (Ibid.) Defendant also argues that sanctions should not be imposed because they acted with substantial justification in refusing to produce Mr. Holguin-Caban to be deposed.

It does not appear that Mr. Holguin-Caban remains employed by Defendant because Plaintiff’s notice of motion states that Mr. Holguin-Caban is Defendant’s former employee. Also, Defendant refers to Mr. Holguin-Caban as an “ex-employee” who was employed as a Shift Manager at the time of Plaintiff’s incident. This is significant because the service of a deposition notice is only effective to require any deponent “who is a party to the action or an . . . or employee of a party to attend and to testify. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) “The attendance and testimony of any other deponent, . . . requires the service on the deponent of a deposition subpoena. . . ” (Id., subd. (b).) “The requisite “party-affiliated” relationship must exist at the time of the deposition notice.” (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 600.) Since Plaintiff did not issue a subpoena to Mr. Holguin-Caban the Court cannot compel him to appear and testify at a deposition.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED. No sanctions are imposed.

I.      INTRODUCTION

         On July 5, 2024, plaintiff Anahid Minaskanian (“Plaintiff”), individually and as successor-in-interest of Lida Zohrabians (“Zohrabians”), filed this action against defendants Central Convalescent Hospital of Glendale Inc. dba Chandler Convalescent Hospital (“Facility”) (erroneously sued separately as “Central Convalescent Hospital of Glendale Inc. dba Chandler Convalescent Hospital-Glendale” and “Chestnut Ridge Post Acute LLC dba Chandler Convalescent Hospital-Glendale”) and Renew Health Consulting Services (“Management”). Plaintiff also named Vartan Vartanians and Mary Boudaghians as nominal defendants.

Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on May 8, 2024. Just like the original complaint, the FAC includes causes of action for: (1) elder abuse and neglect (Welfare & Institutions Code section 15600 et seq.), (2) violations of Residents’ Bill of Rights (Health & Safety Code section 1430(b), (3) negligence, and (4) wrongful death.

          On June 7, 2024, Facility and Management (collectively, “Defendants”) filed a demurrer. Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s causes of action for elder abuse and violation of the Residents’ Bill of Rights are insufficiently pled. Defendants also served a motion to strike which was scheduled to be heard at the same time as the demurrer. Defendants move to strike Paragraphs 44, 45, 55, 56, Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages, attorneys’ fees, and costs.

On October 24, 2024, the hearing was continued so that Defendants could file a copy of the motion to strike with the Court. The motion was filed later that same day.

II.     LEGAL STANDARDS

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.’ [Citation]” (Ibid.)

The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:

(a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.

(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437, subd. (a).) Where the motion to strike is based on matter of which the court may take judicial notice pursuant to Section 452 or 453 of the Evidence Code, such matter shall be specified in the notice of motion, or in the supporting points and authorities, except as the court may otherwise permit. (Id., subd. (b).)

III.    DISCUSSION

A.   Allegations of the FAC

On July 13, 2022, after being hospitalized from COVID, Plaintiff was admitted as a resident at Facility to increase her functional mobility and ability to perform activities of daily living due to weakness and difficulty walking. (FAC, ¶ 17.) Upon admission, Zoharabians “was alert but had periods of forgetfulness and short term memory issues.” (FAC, ¶ 18.) Zohrabians was assessed to need “extensive one person physical assist for dressing, personal hygiene, transferring, toilet use, eating, and bed mobility.” (Ibid.) She was also occasionally incontinent. (Ibid.) Defendants allegedly identified that Zohrabians was at a high risk of falls based upon the medications she was taking, poor safety awareness, and problems with her balance and memory. (FAC, ¶ 19.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants knew they needed to ensure that Zohrabians’s call lights were answered promptly and put her on a toileting schedule, among other things. (Ibid.)

After the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the original complaint, Plaintiff amended it to add that the Facility “did not have sufficient staff assigned to work on each shift which inhibited them from executing care plans and providing the care and supervision each resident, including Zohrabians, needed.” (FAC, ¶ 20.) Plaintiff alleges that this understaffing specifically led to the Facility’s failure to implement a toileting schedule and ensure Zohrabians “received the appropriate supervision and assistance in using the restroom.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff adds that Facility, “via their managing agents”, set the schedule and budget for their staff and was aware of the number of residents and their needs. (FAC, ¶ 21.) The Facility also allegedly understood that understaffing meant that the residents would not receive the care and supervision required and that residents would be at risk of sustaining injury. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff alleges that due to understaffing, Zohrabians experienced significant delays when she attempted to use her call light for assistance to use the restroom, which caused significant distress. (FAC, ¶ 23.) Plaintiff also alleges that Zohrabians preferred using the toilet, but she was kept in diapers and not put on a toileting program or schedule. (Ibid.)

On August 4, 2022, at approximately 7:40 a.m. Zohrabians suffered an unwitnessed fall. (FAC., ¶ 24.) Zohrabians “was attributed with stating she got up because she needed to use the restroom.” (FAC, ¶ 25.) Plaintiff alleges that at 7:25 a.m., Zohrabians was seen by a staff member, but the staff member did have time or ability to assist or offer to assist Zohrabians to the restroom. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants had recently moved Zohrabians to a new room and were aware this could confuse or disorient Plaintiff causing her to need closer monitoring and supervision. (FAC, ¶ 26.) Following the fall, Plaintiff had a bump on her head and was in significant pain. (FAC, ¶ 27.) She was transferred to the hospital where she was diagnosed with a hip fracture and underwent surgery, but passed away on August 14, 2022, due to “[t]he trauma of the surgery and her subsequent condition.” (FAC, ¶ 27.)

B.   Demurrer: First Cause of Action for Elder Abuse and Neglect

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for elder abuse and neglect, which is brought on Zohrabians’s behalf pursuant to the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (“EADACPA”) Welfare and Institutions Code section 15600 et. seq.

Abuse of a dependent adult or elder will justify heightened remedies when the cause of action includes facts showing the following: “(a) Physical abuse, neglect, financial abuse, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering[; or] [¶] (b) The deprivation by a care custodian of goods or services that are necessary to avoid physical harm or mental suffering.” (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.07.) Acts of simple or even gross negligence will not justify the additional civil damage remedies. (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 783-785 (“Covenant Care”).) Where the elder or dependent adult has died, the neglect or abuse resulting in pain or mental suffering must amount to recklessness, oppression, fraud or malice, in order to justify the heightened remedies. (Mack v. Soung (2000) 80 Cal.App.4th 966, 972.) The facts constituting neglect and establishing a causal link between the neglect and the injury must be pled with particularity. (See Covenant Care, supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 790; Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 407.)  

Here, Plaintiff alleges that Zohrabians experienced “significant delays” when using her call light for assistance to use the restroom “[d]uring the course of her residency.” A facility’s alleged pattern of knowingly violating staffing regulations in order to cut costs at the expense of patient care, if proven true, may support a factfinder’s conclusion that the facility acted with recklessness. (Fenimore v. Regents of University of California (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 1139, 1350-1351.) In Fenimore, the Second Appellate District overruled a demurrer where a pleading “identified the staffing regulation the [h]ospital knowingly violated” because “[a] jury may see knowingly flouting staffing regulations as part of a pattern and practice to cut costs, thereby endangering the facility’s elderly and dependent patients, as qualitatively different than simple negligence.”

Here, Plaintiff’s allegations remain too conclusory to state a claim for elder abuse. Plaintiff only alleges that Zohrabians experienced “significant delays” when attempting to use her call light, without specifying the length of time which would make such a delay “significant.” Plaintiff also fails to specifically plead the regulations that were knowingly violated in pursuit of profit which led to Zohrabians’ injury. Plaintiff only refers to inadmissible deficiencies issued for a general failure to provide patient care without stating how those deficiencies specifically relate to Zohrabians’ injuries. In the absence of these specifics, Plaintiff essentially alleges that during her 3-week residency, Zohrabians was kept in diapers and “not assisted to the restroom or asked if she needed to use the restroom” by a staff member on one occasion, which consequently led to a fall. These facts do not constitute a claim for neglect rising to the level of elder abuse and do not evidence recklessness, fraud, malice, or oppression. (Worsham v. O’Connor Hospital (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 331 [no allegations of elder abuse where facility was alleged to be “chronically understaffed”]; Carter, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at p. 405 [oppression, fraud, and malice involve intentional, willful, or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature].) Therefore, Defendants’ demurrer to the First Cause of Action is SUSTAINED.  

C.   Demurrer: Second Cause of Action for Violation of Residents’ Bill of Rights

Health & Safety Code section 1430 (b) permits a current or former resident or patient of a skilled nursing facility to bring “a civil action” against the licensee of a facility that “violates any rights” set forth in the Patients Bill of Rights (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 22, § 72527), or “any other right provided for by federal or state law or regulation.” (H&S Code, § 1430, subd. (b).) Its remedy provision states that “[t]he licensee shall be liable for up to five hundred dollars ($500), and for costs and attorney fees, and may be enjoined from permitting the violation to continue.” (Ibid.) The $500 maximum applies “per civil action rather than per violation.” (Jarman v. HCR ManorCare, Inc. (2020) 10 Cal.5th 375, 392; Nevarrez v. San Marino Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre, LLC (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 102, 137.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to allege any facts sufficient to state a cause of action because there are no allegations identifying the specific rights which were violated and the manner in which they were violated. However, Plaintiff adequately identifies Zohrabians’ rights at issue and sufficiently pleads that Facility violated them by alleging the following:

-      Health & Safety Code section 1599.1(a) and California Code of Regulations, tit. 22, section 72329.1(a), (g)(1): the right to provision of sufficient numbers of qualified personnel to appropriately staff the facility and care for her needs. Plaintiff alleges Facility failed to have a sufficient number of staff members working on each shift during Zohrabians’ residency.

-      California Code of Regulations, tit. 22, section 72527(a)(12).) The right to treatment of consideration, respect, and full recognition of dignity in care of personal needs. Plaintiff alleges Facility staff failed to timely respond to call lights and change Zohrabians’ diaper, which “forced Zohrabians to have to soil her self [sic] and sit in her own excrement for extended periods of time.”

-      California Code of Regulations, tit. 22, sections 72311(a)(1)(B0, (a)(1)(C), and (a)2: The right to have a care plan developed, implemented, reviewed, and updated that indicates what care is necessary and which goals the care plan aims to achieve. Plaintiff alleges Facility failed to implement a toileting program and offer assistance with toileting at specific intervals.

-      Code of Federal Regulations, tit. 42, section 483.25(d): The right to adequate supervision and assistance devices to ensure a hazard-free environment. Plaintiff alleges Facility failed to provide Zohrabians with supervision and assistance with using the restroom which cause her to fall and injure herself.

(FAC, ¶ 75.) Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

D.   Motion to Strike

Defendants move to strike Paragraphs 44, 45, 55, 56, Plaintiff’s allegations requesting punitive damages (¶¶ 71, 78), attorneys’ fees (¶¶ 78), and Plaintiff’s prayer for attorneys’ fees, punitive damages, and costs of suit. (p. 22, ¶¶ 5-7.)

As an initial matter, the Court deems the motion to strike Paragraphs 55, 56, and 71 moot because the demurrer to the elder abuse claim is sustained.

Next, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to strike paragraphs 44 and 45 which describe different violations/citations issued by the DPH. In Nevarrez v. San Marino Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre, LLC (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 102, 122-123, the Second Appellate District found that a trial court abused its discretion by allowing a citation issued by a DPH into evidence. Plaintiffs argue that the citations are not to be used as admissions, but as evidence of Defendant’s knowledge of the cited issues. (Opp., p. 8.) This is a distinction without a difference, because concluding that a party knew of certain circumstances requires assuming (or admitting) that those circumstances exist.

In addition, the Court GRANTS the motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages in paragraph 78 and paragraph 6 of the prayer for relief. Plaintiff fails to allege fraud, oppression, or malice with the requisite specificity pursuant to Civil Code section 3294 in connection with the alleged violations of the Residents’ Bill of Rights.

Last, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for costs and attorneys’ fees in paragraph 78 and paragraphs 5 and 7 in the prayer for relief; both are recoverable under Health and Safety Code section 1430 (b).

IV.    CONCLUSION

Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the First Cause of Action and OVERRULED as to the Second Cause of Action. Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED in part and the Court strikes paragraphs 44, 45, and Plaintiff’s requests for punitive damages. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

I.      INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND

Petitioner Mic-Bry8, LLC (“Petitioner”) seeks court approval of a proposed transfer of structured settlement payments. A direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights is not effective unless the transfer has been approved by a court order finding the following:¿

(1)   The transfer is in the best interest of the payee, considering the welfare and support of the payee’s dependents.

 

(2)   The payee has been advised in writing by the transferee to seek independent professional advice regarding the transfer and has either received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing, the opportunity to receive the advice.

 

(3)   The transferee has complied with the notification requirements pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (f), the transferee has provided the payee with a disclosure form that complies with Section 10136, and the transfer agreement complies with Sections 10136 and 10138.

 

(4)   The transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority.

 

(5)   The payee understands the terms of the transfer agreement, including the terms set forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136.

 

(6)   The payee understands and does not wish to exercise the payee’s right to cancel the transfer agreement.

 

 

(Ins. Code, §§ 10137, 10139.5.)¿

As a result of a personal injury settlement, Payee Carlota Estrada  (“Payee”) became entitled to certain structured settlement payments. Payee and Petitioner have entered into a transfer agreement, under which Payee is to sell 240 monthly life-contingent payments of $380 beginning January 15, 2025 through and including December 15, 2044, totaling $91,200 in exchange for a purchase price of $9,087.07 (Petition, Ex. B ) The discounted present value of the amount being sold is  $55,656.33, calculated by applying the 5.2 % discount rate used by the Internal Revenue Service to value annuities in probate proceedings. The purchase price was calculated using a discount rate of 53.32 %. If Payee did not sell the right to receive structured settlement payments but instead borrowed the net amount of $ 9,086.51 and paid that loan back in installments with each of the payments they are now selling, the equivalent interest rate would be 53.32% per year. (Ibid.)

All of this information has been disclosed to Payee by way of the disclosure form in compliance with Insurance Code § 10136, as required by § 10139.5(a)(3). (Petition, Ex. B ) Payee was advised in writing to seek independent professional advice, both legal and financial. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(2).) Payee chose not to seek an independent professional’s advice and understands the terms of the transfer agreement. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(2), (a)(5); Amended Petition, Ex. D (“Estrada Decl.”), ¶ 11.) Payee also understands and does not wish to exercise their right to cancel the transfer agreement. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(6).) In addition, the transfer agreement does not contravene any applicable statute or order of any court or other governmental authority. (Ins. Code § 10139.5(a)(4).)

         Not less than 20 days prior to the scheduled hearing on any petition for approval of a transfer of structured settlement payment rights under this article, the transferee shall file with the court and serve on all interested parties a notice of the proposed transfer and the petition for its authorization, and shall include the following with that notice:

Payee is 63 years old. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5(c)(1); Estrada Decl. ¶ 4). Payee resides at 345 E. Badillo St., Unit B, Covina, CA 91723. (Ibid.) Payee is single and does not have minor children. (Ins. Payee receives $1,000 per month in disability benefits and $650 per month from her annuity. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5(c)(4); Estrada Decl., ¶ 4.) Payee has not previously assigned or attempted to assign a portion of her structured settlement rights in the last five years. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5(c)(6); Estrada Decl., ¶ 8.) In sum, Payee has provided all of the information required by Insurance Code § 10139.5(c).

In addition, in compliance with Insurance Code § 1039.5(b), Payee indicates the purpose of the transfer and their financial and economic situation. (Estrada Decl., ¶ 6.) Payee states that the funds will be used to purchase a used vehicle because their current vehicle needs a new transmission. (Estrada Decl., ¶ 6.) Given the foregoing, the Court finds that the transfer is fair, reasonable and in the best interest of Payee. (Ins. Code § 10139.5(a)(1) & (b)(1)-(15).) Accordingly, the petition for approval of structured settlement payments is GRANTED.
