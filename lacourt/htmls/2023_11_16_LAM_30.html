DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22STCV27080    Hearing Date: November 16, 2023    Dept: 30

Dept. 30

Calendar No.

Lugo, et. al. vs. General Motors LLC, et. al., Case No. 22STCV27080

 

Tentative Ruling re:  Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Deposition of PMK

 

Plaintiffs Gloria Lopez Lugo and Edwin Lugo Ramos (collectively, Plaintiffs) move for an order compelling Defendant General Motors, LLC (Defendant) to produce its PMQ for deposition and to produce documents. The Court grants the motion as to Category Nos. 1-2 and Request Nos. 1-2 and 5-6, and denies the motion as to Category Nos. 3-4 and Request Nos. 3-4.  Defendant is ordered to produce its PMQ and responsive documents within thirty (30) days of today’s date.

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral

deposition of any person, including any party to the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party or party-affiliated deponent to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).)  

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . or employee of a party . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

“The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).) To establish “good cause,” the burden is on the moving party to show both relevance to the subject matter and specific facts justifying discovery. (See Glenfed Develop. Corp. v. Sup.Ct. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117)

Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010 imposes a “reasonably calculated” limitation on the scope of all discovery.  Section 2017.020(a) vests the judge with authority to limit the scope of discovery if the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of the discovery sought “clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  Section 2019.030 directs the judge to consider the needs of the case, amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation, and to consider whether the discovery being sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable by a more convenient or less expensive or less burdensome way, when deciding whether to restrict the frequency or extent of use of an authorized discovery method.

 

On August 24, 2023, Plaintiffs served the Notice of Deposition for Defendant’s PMQ. (Thomas Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.) The Notice listed 4 matters for examination and 6 requests for documents. Defendant served objections to the Notice on September 7, 2023. (Thomas Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. C.)

 

Category Nos. 1 and 2, and Request Nos. 1, 2, 5, and 6 each seek information directly related to Plaintiff’s vehicle. Those read as follows:

Categories

1.      [Defendant’s] pre-litigation analysis as to whether the [subject vehicle] should be repurchased.

2.      All repairs and service performed on the [subject vehicle].

Requests

1.      [Defendant’s] entire pre-litigation file regarding the SUBJECT VEHICLE.

2.      [Defendant’s] pre-litigation communications with Plaintiff(s) regarding the SUBJECT VEHICLE.

5.       All DOCUMENTS evidencing your pre-litigation evaluation of whether the SUBJECT VEHICLE qualified for a repurchase under the Song-Beverly Act.

6.      All DOCUMENTS that YOU reviewed in YOUR pre-litigation evaluation of whether the SUBJECT VEHICLE qualified for a repurchase under the Song-Beverly Act.

 

In Opposition, Defendant argues that these items are irrelevant because Defendant has already produced responsive documents and has agreed to produce a witness regarding certain categories. The Court disagrees. The fact that Defendant has produced other documents in this action or agreed to produce a witness on certain topics does not limit the remaining scope of discovery. These items directly concern Defendant’s decision on whether to repurchase Plaintiff’s vehicle, and are relevant for purposes of discovery.

 

Category No. 3 and Request No. 3 seek information regarding Defendant’s general policies and procedures for evaluating vehicle repurchase or replacement under the Song-Beverly Act.  Category No. 4 and Request No. 4 concern Defendant’s training and training materials for evaluating pre-litigation repurchase requests.

 

Defendant objected to this topic as overbroad, burdensome, vague, ambiguous, irrelevant, seeking trade secret information, and seeking privileged information. The Court agrees with Defendant that these categories and requests are not sufficiently tailored to Plaintiff’s claims, and so denies the motion as to Category Nos. 3-4 and Request Nos. 3-4.

 

Because the motion is denied in part, monetary sanctions are denied.
