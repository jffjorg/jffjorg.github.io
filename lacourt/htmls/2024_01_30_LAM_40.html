DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 22STCV04358    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

KARYNA MYRES, an individual,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

ERNST HACKER, an individual; KAPE PROPERTY MANAGEMENT, INC., a California corporation; and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          22STCV04358

 Hearing Date:   1/30/24

 Trial Date:        7/30/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant Doe 2 Ronen Hacker’s Motion for an Order Compelling Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set No. One, and for Monetary Sanctions.

 

Background

Pleadings

Plaintiff Karyna Myres brings this habitability suit against Defendants (1) Ernst Hacker, (2) Kape Property Management, Inc. (Kape Property Management), (3) Doe 1 Tamir Hacker, as Successor Co-Trustee of the Survivor’s Trust established under The Hacker Family Trust dated March 24, 1988, and as Successor Trustee of Survivor’s Trust established under the Ernst Hacker and Bila Hacker Trust, restated March 16, 2012, (4) Doe 2 Ronen Hacker, as Successor Co-Trustee of the Survivor’s Trust established under The Hacker Family Trust dated March 24, 1988, (5) Doe 3 Adi Samuel Hacker, as Successor Co-Trustee of the Survivor’s Trust established under The Hacker Family Trust dated March 24, 1988, (6) Doe 4 Niza Hacker, as Successor Trustee of the Ernst Hacker Survivor’s Trust, and (7) Does 5-100.

The operative February 3, 2022 Complaint alleges claims of (1) Nuisance, (2) Breach of Contract, (3) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (4) Negligence, (5) Breach of Statutes, (6) Breach of Warranty of Habitability, (7) Breach of the Covenant Quiet Enjoyment, (8) Constructive Eviction, (9) IIED, and (10) NIED.

The claims arise from allegations that, in February 2020, while a tenant of a residential apartment unit located at 718 North Hayworth Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90046 (Subject Premises), seemingly unlicensed contractors performed construction and demolition work on the unit adjacent to Plaintiff Myres’s unit, which caused portions of the ceiling in her unit to collapse, with debris at one point falling on her head, causing Plaintiff injuries and leading to a diagnosis of post-concussion syndrome. The claims also arise from allegations of ongoing harassment by Defendants’ agents—including allegations of unpaid rent by Defendants’ agents and threats to Plaintiff’s person by construction workers—and allegations that the construction and demolition work caused a gas leak at the Subject Premises, leading SoCal Gas to deem the Premises uninhabitable in December 2020, and forcing Plaintiff to move out of her unit.

Motion Before the Court

On or about December 9, 2022, Defendant Ronen Hacker served Special Interrogatories, Set One (SROGs, Set One), on Defendant Kape Property Management.

The statutory deadline for Defendant Kape to respond to Defendant Ronen Hacker’s propounded interrogatories was January 8, 2023, plus 5 days for mailing.

On or about March 10, 2023, having received no response from Kape Property Management, Ronen Hacker’s counsel mailed a letter to Kape Property Management’s counsel, asking for responses to SROGs, Set One.

Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Interrogatories

Legal Standard

A motion to compel an initial response can be made on the ground that a party did not serve a timely response to interrogatories or a demand to produce. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); see Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404 (Sinaiko).) To establish this ground, a movant must show:

(1) Proper service (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.080, subd. (a));

(2) Expiration of the deadline for the initial response 30 days after service or on date agreed to by parties (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subds. (a), (b)); and

(3) No timely response (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290).

A court must deny a motion to compel initial discovery where the discovery sought is outside the scope of discovery. (See CBS, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 19 (CBS); see also Code. Civ. Proc., § 2017.010 [scope of discovery].)

Order Compelling Initial Responses: MOOT.

After review, the Court agrees with Kape Property Management: Ronen Hacker’s motion is moot as to an order compelling initial responses to SROGs, Set One.

Kape Property Management attaches a copy of its August 4, 2023 responses to its opposition. (See Opp’n, Mosley Decl., Ex. E.) A review of these responses shows that that they are objection-free and verified. (See ibid.) This is proper because a failure to respond to SROGs, Set One, within the permissible timeframe resulted in a waiver of objections. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) A review of the responses also shows that they are substantively responsive, to whatever degree, to SROGs, Set One. (See Opp’n, Mosley Decl., Ex. E.) As a result, the proper mechanism to challenge any deficiencies in the August 4th responses (see Reply, pp. 2-4) is a motion to compel further interrogatory responses pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300.

Ronen Hacker’s motion is thus MOOT.

Sanctions: GRANTED.

The Court must impose monetary sanctions against anyone—party, nonparty, or attorney—who unsuccessfully makes or opposes the motion, unless it finds that the person to be sanctioned acted withs substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of the sanctions unjust. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c); see Sinaiko, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 404.)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

Here, despite eventually serving responses to SROGs, Set One, Kape Property Management greatly delayed in doing so, thus failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery, and meriting sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

The Court determines that sanctions of $1,935 are reasonable in light of the reasonable fees and costs sought in connection with this motion: reasonable rate of $375 per hour for five hours expended on drafting this motion, which the Court will hold to include the time to be expended attending this hearing; and costs of $60 in filing this motion. (Mot., Quigg Decl., ¶ 10.)

Sanctions are thus GRANTED in the amount of $1,935.

(1) MOOT as to an order compelling further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set No. One; and

(2) GRANTED as to sanctions in the amount of $1,935.

Defendant Kape Property Management Inc. is ORDERED to remit payment of these monies to Defendant Doe 2 Ronen Hacker within 30 days of this order.

Motion for Attorneys’ Fees Incurred on Anti-SLAPP Appeal

Legal Standard

 “[T]o collect appellate attorney fees, a party must demonstrate the right to do so under either a statute or a contract, independent of a costs statute.” (Butler-Rupp v. Lourdeaux (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 918, 927.)

A prevailing defendant on an anti-SLAPP motion is entitled to recover their attorney’s fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1); Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.) Since section 425.16, subdivision (c) provides for an award of attorney’s fees and costs to a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike and does not preclude recovery of appellate attorney fees by a prevailing defendant-respondent, those fees are also recoverable. (Trapp v. Naiman (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 113, 122; Dove Audio, Inc. v. Rosenfeld, Meyer & Susman (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 777, 785.) A prevailing party may recover fees incurred in defending an appeal to a favorable anti-SLAPP ruling even where the appeal is voluntarily dismissed. (See Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

The Court begins this inquiry “with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) From there, the “lodestar figure may then be adjusted [according to a multiplier enhancement] based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Ibid.) Relevant multiplier factors include “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

No specific findings reflecting the court’s calculations for attorney’s fees are required; the record need only show that the attorney’s fees were awarded according to the “lodestar” or “touchstone” approach. (Rebney v. Wells Fargo Bank (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 1344, 1349, disagreed with on other grounds in In re Marriage of Demblewski (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 232, 236, fn. 7 [disagreement as to statement of decision requirements].) The Court has broad discretion to determine the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award, which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.” (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1393-1394.)

Order Granting Attorneys’ Fees: GRANTED, in part.

The Showtime Defendants move for an award of $155,938 in legal fees as the alleged prevailing party in an appeal filed by Plaintiff Rueda against the Showtime Defendants’ successful 2020 anti-SLAPP challenge to the FAC’s eighth and ninth causes of action.

I. Initial Notes

The Court notes that it does not find sufficient merit to Plaintiff Rueda’s argument that the Court should deny this motion, either in whole or in part, based on the Showtime Defendants’ unclean hands in allegedly suppressing a recording of a conversation between Defendant Davidson and Plaintiff Rueda. Without ruling on whether the facts do, or do not, support such allegations, the Court finds that no unfair surprise is at issue in this motion for attorneys’ fees. (See Opp’n, p. 19.)

The Court also notes that the May 24, 2022 stipulation between the parties does not encompass fees recovered on appeal. (Opp’n, Khan Decl., Ex. 13, pp. 3-4.)

II. Prevailing Party on Appeal

With exceptions not at issue here, “a prevailing defendant on a special [anti-SLAPP] motion to strike shall be entitled to recover his or her attorney’s fees and costs.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).) “[A] party who partially prevails on an anti-SLAPP motion must generally be considered a prevailing party unless the results of the motion were so insignificant that the party did not achieve any practical benefit from bringing the motion.” (Mann v. Quality Old Time Serv. Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 328, 344-345 (Mann), overruled on other grounds in Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 392-396 (Baral) [disapproving Mann in its “refusal to permit anti-SLAPP motions to reach distinct claims within pleaded counts”]; accord. Moran v. Endres (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 952, 956 (Moran) [defendants were not entitled to fees, where their partial success on their anti-SLAPP motion “in every practical sense meant nothing”].) This no-practical-benefit doctrine does not apply where the defendant “successfully narrowed the scope of the lawsuit, limiting discovery, reducing potential recoverable damages, and altering the settlement posture of the case.” (Mann, supra, at p. 340.) The no-practical-benefit doctrine is a “narrow” exception to the general rule mandating a fee award to a partially successful defendant. (Morrow v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1446.) Except with limited types of claims, a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover that defendant’s attorney’s fees and costs.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).) Whether a partially successful defendant achieved sufficient benefit to be a “prevailing party” is left to the discretion of the trial court and reviewed accordingly. (Mann, supra, at p. 340.)

After review, the Court finds that the Showtime Defendants are the prevailing party on appeal.

As noted by the court of appeal, the Showtime Defendants defended against Plaintiff Rueda’s appeal to the November 13, 2020 ruling only insofar as the ruling struck allegations from the FAC relating to conduct by Defendant Keith Davidson. (Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, pp. 16, 17; see 10/18/19 Anti-SLAPP Mot., p. 1, Notice [seeking order altogether striking eighth and ninth causes of action on grounds that claims arose from protected statements by Defendant Davidson and Plaintiff was unable to establish likelihood of success on merits].) The Court of appeal affirmed the appeal on this issue. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, pp. 12, 22.) (The Court notes that Plaintiff Rueda’s appeal focused on the first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis and evidentiary objections in the trial court ruling without contesting the second prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis: likelihood of prevailing on the merits. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, p. 16 [“Rueda has not challenged the trial court’s determination that he has no reasonable probability of prevailing on the claims that arise from Davidson’s conduct …[;] [w]e accordingly need not analyze the issue; we can and do hold there was no error in granting the anti-SLAPP motion as to those claims”].)) The court of appeal only reversed an issue not challenged by the Showtime Defendants: harassment and intimidation by third parties, as alleged in the FAC’s eighth and ninth causes of action, which they reasonably contend was not pled to clearly implicate the Showtime Defendants, and did not mention them by name. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, pp. 17-21.)

However, it should be noted that as discussed above, the Showtime Defendants’ defense on appeal was limited to the trial court striking the allegations regarding Defendant Davidson’s conduct, as it supported the eighth and ninth causes of action. In contrast, Plaintiff Rueda’s appeal also encompassed striking the allegations related to third parties from the eighth and ninth causes of action, which the Showtime Defendants specifically did not contest on appeal. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, pp. 17-18.) Thus, the Showtime Defendants defended the appeal only in part.

The Court also notes that, in the trial court proceedings, while the Showtime Defendants based their anti-SLAPP motion on the argument that these claims arose from allegations of protected activity by Defendant Davidson, the Showtime Defendants did not request that the Court only strike allegations relating to Davidson from the eighth and ninth causes of action and leave allegations relating to third parties, whose connection to the Showtime Defendants as Doe Defendants was unclear. Instead, the Showtime Defendants requested a wholesale striking out of the eighth and ninth causes of action. (See 10/18/19 Anti-SLAPP Mot., p. 1, Notice.)

Returning to the merits, on the portion of the appeal that they defended, the Showtime Defendants undoubtedly prevailed: affirmance as to whether Defendant Davidson’s conduct arose from protected activity for anti-SLAPP purposes as it relates to the eighth and ninth causes of action. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, pp. 12, 22.) This suffices for the anti-SLAPP statute. Indeed, even had the Showtime Defendants argued that the third-party conduct alleged in the FAC arose from protected activity and the trial court had granted the motion on those grounds only for the court of appeal to overturn, the Showtime Defendants would have partially succeeded on their appeal insofar as it related to Defendant Davidson’s alleged conduct. Here, however, the Showtime Defendants did not make such an argument on appeal, showing that the defense of the appeal was only partial—even if entirely successful.

Last, the Court agrees that the Showtime Defendants’ defense on appeal made them the prevailing parties for the purposes of this motion. The affirmance “narrowed the scope of the lawsuit, limiting discovery, reducing potential recoverable damages, and altering the settlement posture of the case” by affirming a removal of Defendant Davidson’s alleged conduct as grounds for the eighth and ninth causes of action, leaving only the conduct of mostly unnamed third parties as the bases for those claims. (Mann, supra, 139 Cal.App.4th at p. 340.)

The Showtime Defendants are thus prevailing parties for anti-SLAPP fee recovery purposes. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).)

The Court notes that although the court of appeal instructed that the parties were to bear their costs on appeal (see Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, p. 22), that direction does not affect the Showtime Defendants’ entitlement to fees as the prevailing party with partial success on appeal. (See Butler-Rupp v. Lourdeaux (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 918, 925-928 [appellate court’s remittitur stating parties are to bear own costs does not preclude trial court from awarding appellate attorney fees on remand].)

III. Indigence of Plaintiff Rueda

In determining whether amount of fees to be awarded is reasonable under statute, trial court must consider the financial circumstances of the losing party and the impact of the award on that party, but the court must not neglect to consider other circumstances in setting the amount of fees. (Garcia v. Santana (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 464, 476-477 (Garcia).)

After review, the Court finds that indigence is a relevant factor in determining whether an amount of fees to be granted is reasonable.

While the Showtime Defendants argue that it makes a difference whether the fees are discretionary or mandatory, this distinction seems to be one without a difference. Under Garcia, indigence affects the reasonability of a fee award, not the mandatory or discretionary nature of the award. (Garcia, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at pp. 476-477.) The Showtime Defendants—who have the burden on this motion (ComputerXpress, Inc. v. Jackson (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 993, 1020)—have failed to cite to any authority, California or otherwise, holding that courts may not consider indigence when determining the reasonability of a request for mandatory fees. Absent such authority, the Court will not restrict Plaintiff Rueda’s indigence argument in relation to a reasonable fee award.

The Court also notes that while Garcia may have been distinguished by subsequent cases,  it has not been overturned by the California Supreme Court and is still good law. The Court accepts Garcia as controlling authority over the determination of reasonable fees as it relates to statutory entitlement to fees, as contrasted with two decisions referenced by the Showtime Defendants that dealt with other issues. (See Reply, p. 6; cf. LAOSD Asbestos Cases (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 1116, 1123-1126 [holding that ability to pay is not a factor in determining allowable costs, as opposed to fees], Walker v. Ticor Title Co. of Cal. (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 363, 374 [contractual fees].) The Court does not find that Garcia indicated that the result would have been different under the SLAPP statute (Reply, p. 6), and even if it had, such a holding would have been dicta since the SLAPP statute was not at issue there.

Next, the Court recognizes the serious policy implication raised by the Showtime Defendants to the effect that allowing indigence to be a consideration in SLAPP fee awards may open the floodgates to indigent litigators filing SLAPP cases with impunity and little financial repercussions. (Reply, p. 6.) Thus, the Court will exercise its discretion, as directed by the court in Garcia, to balance all the circumstances, including the statutory purposes of entitlement to fees on the one hand, with Rueda’s ability to pay on the other.

Last, the Court accepts Plaintiff Rueda’s evidence as to his financial condition, which shows Plaintiff as being financially in dire straits. (Opp’n, Rueda Decl., ¶¶ 3, 8-13, Exs. 2-5.)

 

IV. Reasonable Fee Rates

After review, the Court finds that the fee rates sought by the Showtime Defendants, which were discounted considerably, are eminently reasonable based on the Court’s experience with fee rates in the Los Angeles County area and based on the declarations of counsel supporting those rates. (Mot., Brenner Decl., ¶¶ 49-56, Godino Decl.; Excelsior Union High Sch. Dist. of L.A. Cnty. v. Lautrup (1969) 269 Cal.App.2d 434, 448 (Excelsior).)

V. Reasonable Hours

After review, the Court finds that the hours expended by the Showtime Defendants’ counsel should be reduced in certain respects.

The Court initially notes that while Showtime Defendants did not provide billing records with their motion, but rather, declarations and charts providing the total number of hours expended by the attorneys who worked on the appeal. (Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 15.) While timesheets for that work are not entirely necessary (Sommers v. Erb (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1644, 1651 (Sommers); Dunk v. Ford Motor Co. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1794, 1810 (Dunk)), the moving party has the burden of documenting the appropriate hours expended and hourly rates (City of Colton v. Singletary (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 751, 784 (City of Colton).

Moving to the merits, the Court first determines that the 47 hours spent on preparing a motion to strike and reply in the appeal—for a requested recovery of $23,554—should not be recovered. As noted by the court of appeal, the Showtime Defendants’ motion to strike “[wa]s not before th[e] court [of appeal]” because the request by Rueda that the Showtime Defendants were challenging had been filed in the trial court, not the appellate court. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 12, p. 1.)

The Court next discounts 15% of the recovery sought for hours expended on the legal brief before the court of appeal, i.e., a reduction of $15,139.05. This is because Plaintiff Rueda’s appeal did not focus on the second prong of the Court’s November 13, 2020 order, but rather, limited itself to rulings on evidentiary objections and the first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis. (See Mot., Brenner Decl., Ex. 13, Remittitur, p. 16 [“Rueda has not challenged the trial court’s determination that he has no reasonable probability of prevailing on the claims that arise from Davidson’s conduct”].) The work on the second prong was thus not necessary based on the scope of the appeal and is consequently not recoverable.

As to the remaining request of $117,244.95—comprised of $155,938, minus $23,554 in motion to strike fees, minus $15,139.05 in briefing related to the second prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis—the Court finds that the request is excessive.

“When a voluminous fee application is made the court may make across-the-board percentage cuts either in the number of hours claimed or in the final lodestar figure” (Morris v. Hyundai Motor America (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 40, quoting Warren v. Kia Motors America, Inc. (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 24, 41 (Warren), internal quotation marks and ellipses deleted.) “A trial court is not required to state each charge it finds reasonable or unreasonable. A reduced award might be fully justified by a general observation that an attorney overlitigated a case.” (Karton v. Ari Design & Construction, Inc. (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 734, 744.) “But the court must clearly explain its reasons for choosing the particular negative multiplier that it chose; otherwise, the reviewing court is unable to determine that the court had valid, specific reasons for its across-the-board percentage reduction.” (Warren, supra, at p. 41.)

However, the Court is considered “an expert in the matter of attorney fees” since “[t]he value of attorney’s services is a matter with which a judge must necessarily be familiar.” (Excelsior, supra, 269 Cal.App.2d at p. 448.) Accordingly, “[w]hen the court is informed of the extent and nature of such services, its own experience furnishes it with every element necessary to fix their value.” (Ibid.)

Here, the Court’s experience with fee awards for partially and wholly successful appeals informs its determination that the number of hours for which the Showtime Defendants seek an award is excessive. In this case, the 200 hours expended in preparing the appeal brief in particular are excessive. In the Court’s experience, when taking the Court’s reductions above into account, the compensable attorney fee range for the entirety of the appeal should be somewhere closer to $80,000 to $100,000. Second, while the Court notes that block billing is not prohibited by California law, nor is it mandatory to attach the actual time records, in some cases block billing can make it difficult to establish the appropriateness of all the hours expended. The declarations filed by the Showtime Defendants failed to show the reasonableness as to all the hours requested that were expended on the appeal.

The Court thus makes a 20% across-the-board reduction to the remaining lodestar amount—$117,244.95—for an initial lodestar calculation of $93,795.96.

VI. Fees Conclusion

The Court makes a further reduction of 50% from the initial lodestar calculation based on Plaintiff Rueda’s showing of indigence. (See Section III discussion supra.) The Court recognizes that this is still a heavy burden on Plaintiff, but concludes that the legislative command for mandatory entitlement to fees under the anti-SLAPP statute weighs in favor of issuing a substantial, though significantly reduced, award against him.

The Showtime Defendants’ motion is thus GRANTED, in part, in the amount of $46,897.98.

 

Conclusion

Defendants Showtime Network Inc. and Paramount Global’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees Incurred on Anti-SLAPP Appeal is GRANTED, in part, in the amount of $46,897.98.
