[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 9, 2024

 

On July 9, 2020, Plaintiff Hilda Rodriguez-Guzman filed this action against Defendants The Accelerated Schools (“TAS”) and Jonathan Williams, alleging (1) whistleblower retaliation under the Labor Code; (2) sexual harassment in violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”); (3) disability discrimination in violation of FEHA; (4) discrimination and retaliation based on association with a member of a protected class; (5) failure to prevent harassment and retaliation; (6) retaliation in violation of FEHA; (7) age discrimination; (8) intentional infliction of emotional distress (“IIED”); (9) wrongful termination; (10) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention; and (11) failure to accommodate in violation of FEHA.

On October 25, 2023, Defendants filed a motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice of Dr. Bains’s PERB Hearing Testimony (Exhibit 13) and Mr. Rob French’s PERB Testimony (Exhibit 40) is denied.  Plaintiff relies on the substance of the testimony, not just its existence.  The Court cannot take judicial notice of the truth of these contents.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Defendant’s Reply states that it is filed concurrently with Defendants’ Objections to Plaintiff’s Evidence in Support of Opposition to Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment.

No objections were filed with the Court.

BACKGROUND FACTS

TAS is a nonprofit public benefit corporation that operates three charter schools: an elementary and middle school known as TAS K-8, the Accelerated Charter Elementary School, and the Wallis Annenberg High School.  (Undisputed Material Fact “UMF” 1.)

Plaintiff began working for TAS in 2007 as the Health Office Aide.  (UMF 3.)  Plaintiff became the Health Services Coordinator in 2012, where her job was to, among other things, administer first aid and medication to care for ill, medically fragile and/or injured children.  (UMF 4-5.)  She was also responsible for assisting with new student enrollment by ensuring that all incoming students had a signed health history form from the parent or legal guardian, and that children’s immunizations were up to date, entered into the Student Information System (“SIS”), and then printed onto the California School Immunization Record (“CSIR”) card.  (UMF 6.)  The position also required that Plaintiff have current CPR and first-aid certificates, but did not require a college degree or state license.  (UMF 7.)  Plaintiff has a high school diploma, but no college degree.  (UMF 2.)

Johnathan Williams was the Chief Executive Officer of TAS until he was removed from the position by the Board of Trustees on February 28, 2019, and he thereafter was placed on leave pending reassignment.  (UMF 8.)  Mr. Williams was appointed President of the Office of Development and Government Affairs on March 12, 2019.  (UMF 9.)

Plaintiff made four complaints about Mr. Williams between September and November 2014.  (UMF 22.)  A report concerning those complaints was issued on February 24, 2015.  (UMF 23.)  Defendants hired a third party (Mike Hines) to investigate Plaintiff’s claims in September 2015, but she refused to participate in the investigative process.  (UMF 24.)  A report was subsequently issued.  (UMF 24.)

Plaintiff filed a uniform complaint on October 31, 2016 for discrimination, retaliation, harassment, and intimidation.  (UMF 25.)  Defendants provided her with a response to the uniform complaint on December 8, 2016.  (UMF 26.)

Plaintiff complained about Mr. Williams again on November 27, 2018.  (UMF 27.)  A report of the investigation was provided to Plaintiff on May 16, 2019.  (UMF 28.)

On April 30, 2018, Plaintiff filed an administrative complaint with the Civil Rights Department (“CRD”) alleging disability discrimination and denial of reasonable accommodations.  (UMF 30.)  Plaintiff alleged that she submitted documentation with permanent modified restrictions on August 8, 2017 that included no lifting, pushing, or pulling more than 10 pounds, no repetitive bending, and to alternate between sitting, standing, and walking as needed.  (UMF 31.)  She claimed that it was agreed to by the parties that a file cabinet would be purchased to assist her with filing records, and that the cabinet had not yet been purchased.  (UMF 31.)  The CRD issued the right-to-sue notice on this charge on April 25, 2019.  (UMF 32.)

On June 19, 2018, Plaintiff had a meeting to discuss the cabinet.   (UMF 42.)  After the meeting, the cabinet was purchased, but it ended up being the wrong one.  (UMF 42.)  All Plaintiff wanted was another cabinet so she could put her records away, but her office was also too small to house all the records.  (UMF 43.)  Plaintiff believed that the only reasonable solution was for her to get a larger office.  (UMF 44.)  Plaintiff and TAS had ongoing conversations about her getting a bigger office.  (UMF 45.)

Plaintiff injured herself while assisting a student in October 2018.  (UMF 46-47.)  She further injured her right arm in March 2019 when she was getting onto an ambulance to ride along with an injured student.  (UMF 48.)  Her right arm was in a sling, and she could not move it.  (UMF 49.)

Plaintiff had permanent restrictions of no lifting/pushing/pulling any more than ten pounds, no repetitive bending/limited to once an hour, alternating between sitting/standing/walking as needed to control symptoms, and then five-minute stretch breaks every hour to use a heating pad.  (UMF 50.)  She had an accommodation meeting on March 26, 2019.  (UMF 52.)  As of this date, Plaintiff was struggling to manually write health records, which was an essential function of her position, and there was no one else in the office who could assist due to limited staffing.  (UMF 53.)  Plaintiff was unable to write, and she also could not take temperatures, which was another essential function of her position.  (UMF 54-55.)  As a result, Plaintiff was put on leave in April 2019.  (UMF 56.)  She received temporary disability benefits during this time.  (UMF 57.)

While Plaintiff was out on leave, TAS was going to remodel the South Central Family Health space and move her into that office so that when she came back in July 2019 she would have adequate space.  (UMF 58.)

Plaintiff was contacted in July 2019 and advised that her position was being eliminated so TAS could hire a full-time RN.  (UMF 59, 67.)

In response to March 2019 and April 2019 surveys, approximately 20% (16 out of 84 total survey respondents) of the parents who responded provided unprompted comments that they would like a school nurse.  (UMF 60-62.)  In the subsequent April 2019 surveys, several comments were again made by parents regarding the need to upgrade services in the Front Office, which includes Health Services.  (UMF 63.)  After evaluating parent comments, the needs of the school, and the current healthcare needs of students, TAS ultimately determined that its healthcare services needed to be overseen by a licensed professional.  (UMF 64.)  To come to this decision, TAS put together a working group who between April and May 2019 discussed whether to move forward with eliminating the Health Services Coordinator position in order to establish the Registered Nurse (“RN”) position.  (UMF 65.)  The RN position not only administers students’ day-to-day healthcare needs, but also develops and maintains a broad program of health education and services to protect, maintain, and enhance the health of students by providing higher-level health care service.  (UMF 68.)  This position requires a Bachelor of Science in Nursing (“SBN”) Degree.  (UMF 68.)

On February 6, 2020, Plaintiff filed an administrative complaint alleging age discrimination, disability discrimination, and retaliation.  (UMF 33.)  She alleged that she provided medical documentation verifying her work restrictions in 2018, which included no lifting, carrying, pushing, or pulling more than 15 pounds, and no repetitive movements with her right hand and arm for more than 15 consecutive minutes at a time.  (UMF 34.)  Plaintiff claimed that TAS stopped honoring her work restrictions on April 16, 2019 when she was placed on involuntary disability leave.  (UMF 34.)  She alleged that she was unlawfully terminated on July 11, 2019, and denied reasonable accommodations.  (UMF 34.)  This complaint was dismissed due to Plaintiff’s May 8, 2020 complaint that raised the same issues.  (UMF 35.)

On May 8, 2020, Plaintiff filed an administrative complaint generically alleging harassment, discrimination, and retaliation.  (UMF 37.)  In particular, Plaintiff claimed that she had been discriminated against and harassed based on age, disability, sex, and her association with a member of a protected class.  (UMF 38.)  She also claimed that she was retaliated against for reporting or resisting discrimination and/or harassment, for participating as a witness in a discrimination or harassment complaint, and for requesting reasonable accommodations.  (UMF 38.)  She further claimed that Defendants “created a false reason to terminate her.”  (UMF 38.)  The CRD issued the right-to-sue notice on May 8, 2020.  (UMF 39.)

Plaintiff’s union (on her behalf) filed unfair labor practices charges claiming that TAS interfered with Plaintiff’s protected rights, unilaterally eliminated Plaintiff’s position without bargaining, and retaliated against her by laying her off for engaging in protected activities.  (UMF 69.)  Formal hearings were conducted on July 20, 21, 22, and 23, 2021; November 18 and 30, 2021; December 2, 2021; and January 18, 2022, and post-hearing briefing was filed.  (UMF 70-71.)  In regards to the retaliation claim, the ALJ indicated that PERB considered a charging party’s discrimination or retaliation claim under the same framework, and that once a prima facie case is established the employer may prove that it would have taken the exact same action even absent protected activity.  (UMF 72.)  The ALJ found that the evidence supported TAS’s decision to eliminate Plaintiff’s position and hire an RN.  (See UMF 73-74.)  Consequently, the ALJ concluded TAS had established it would have taken the same course of action (eliminating Plaintiff’s position) even if Plaintiff did not engage in any protected activity.  (UMF 75.)

On March 17, 2023, PERB issued a final decision confirming that TAS did not retaliate against Plaintiff.  (UMF 78.)  Like the ALJ, after weighing the evidence and credibility, PERB concluded that the evidence showed TAS would have reached the same decision even had Plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.  (UMF 79; see UMF 80-81.)  PERB held that TAS based its eventual decision to eliminate Plaintiff’s position on legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons.  (UMF 82; see UMF 83.)

DISCUSSION

For each claim in the complaint, the defendant moving for adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by showing that one or more elements of a cause of action cannot be established or that there is a complete defense to a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520 (Scalf).)  Then the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf, supra, 128 Cal.App.4th at p. 1520.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.”  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162-163.)

A.        The PERB Proceeding Conclusively Established That There Was No Whistleblower Retaliation (First Cause of Action).

Labor Code “section 1102.6, and not McDonnell Douglas, supplies the applicable framework for litigating and adjudicating section 1102.5 whistleblower claims.”  (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 712 (Lawson).)  After a plaintiff demonstrates by a preponderance of the evidence that his protected activity was a contributing factor in the adverse employment action, the employer must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that the adverse employment action would have occurred for legitimate, independent reasons even if the employee did not engage in the protected conduct.  (Lab. Code, § 1102.6.)  Under this standard, “plaintiffs may satisfy their burden of proving unlawful retaliation even when other, legitimate factors also contributed to the adverse action.”  (Lawson, supra, 12 Cal.5th at pp. 713-714.)

Defendants argue that collateral estoppel bars relitigation of whether TAS would have taken the same actions even if she did not engage in protected activities.  (Motion at pp. 19-22.)

“Collateral estoppel precludes relitigation of issues argued and decided in prior proceedings.  [Citation.]  Traditionally, we have applied the doctrine only if several threshold requirements are fulfilled.  First, the issue sought to be precluded from relitigation must be identical to that decided in a former proceeding.  Second, this issue must have been actually litigated in the former proceeding.  Third, it must have been necessarily decided in the former proceeding.  Fourth, the decision in the former proceeding must be final and on the merits.  Finally, the party against whom preclusion is sought must be the same as, or in privity with, the party to the former proceeding.  [Citations.]  The party asserting collateral estoppel bears the burden of establishing these requirements.”  (Lucido v. Superior Court (1990) 51 Cal.3d 335, 341, footnote omitted.)

“The issue, wrongfulness of the discharge, is identical in the administrative proceeding and this suit.  ‘The “identical issue” requirement addresses whether “identical factual allegations” are at stake in the two proceedings . . . .’’”  (Castillo v. City of Los Angeles (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 477, 481 (Castillo).)  Plaintiff’s union claimed, on her behalf, that TAS interfered with Plaintiff’s protected rights, unilaterally eliminated Plaintiff’s position without bargaining, and retaliated against her by laying her off for engaging in protected activities.  (UMF 69.)  Those are the same allegations in this action.  (See, e.g., Complaint ¶¶ 28, 62, 70.)

An issue is actually litigated when it is properly raised, by the pleadings or otherwise, is submitted for determination, and is determined.  (Castillo, supra, 92 Cal.App.4th at p. 482.)  The issue was actually litigated through formal hearings, post-hearing briefs, and reply briefs.  (UMF 70-71.)

An issue is “necessarily decided” when the issue was not “entirely unnecessary” to the judgment in the prior proceeding.  (Castillo, supra, 92 Cal.App.4th at p. 482.)  The propriety of Defendants’ actions was the focal issue in both the prior proceeding and this action.  After weighing the evidence and credibility, PERB concluded that the evidence showed TAS would have reached the same decision even had Plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.  (UMF 79.)  PERB held that TAS based its eventual decision to eliminate Plaintiff’s position on legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons.  (UMF 82.)

The PERB decision was also final and on the merits.  On March 17, 2023, PERB issued a final decision confirming that TAS did not retaliate against Plaintiff.  (UMF 78.)  No party petitioned the court for extraordinary relief after PERB’s final decision.  (UMF 84.)

Defendants have met their initial burden of showing that collateral estoppel applies, and TAS based its decision to eliminate Plaintiff’s position on legitimate, nondiscriminatory and nonretaliatory reasons.  (See Motion at p. 22.)

Plaintiff argues that PERB has no jurisdiction to enforce the Labor Code, Government Code, and other statutes.  (Opposition at pp. 17-21.)  However, Defendants’ argument is not that the claims themselves were previously litigated.  Rather, the factual issue of the reasons for Plaintiff’s termination was litigated.  This “essential element”—not the entire causes of action—was conclusively established.  (See Reply at pp. 5-6.)

Plaintiff has failed to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact regarding collateral estoppel.

Summary adjudication of the first cause of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

B.        Plaintiff Was Not a Qualified Individual For Her Claims of Disability Discrimination or Failure to Accommodate (Third, Eleventh Causes of Action).

An employee’s prima facie claim of discrimination requires “(1) the employee’s membership in a classification protected by the statute; (2) discriminatory animus on the part of the employer toward members of that classification; (3) an action by the employer adverse to the employee’s interests; (4) a causal link between the discriminatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damage to the employee; and (6) a causal link between the adverse action and the damage.”  (Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  If an employee makes a prima facie showing, the burden shifts to the employer to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse action.  (Id. at p. 714.)

“To establish that an employer has discriminated on the basis of a disability in violation of FEHA, the plaintiff employee has the burden of proving he or she could perform ‘the essential functions of the job with or without reasonable accommodation.’”  (Atkins v. City of Los Angeles (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 696, 716 (Atkins).)  “Under FEHA, ‘reasonable accommodation’ means ‘“a modification or adjustment to the workplace that enables the employee to perform the essential functions of the job held or desired.”’ [Citations.]  While the reasonableness of an accommodation is ordinarily a question of fact [citations], FEHA does not require employers to eliminate essential functions of a job to accommodate a disabled employee.”  (Id. at p. 719.)

“The essential elements of a failure to accommodate claim are: (1) the plaintiff has a disability covered by the FEHA; (2) the plaintiff is a qualified individual (i.e., he or she can perform the essential functions of the position); and (3) the employer failed to reasonably accommodate the plaintiff's disability.  [Citation]”  (Cuiellette v. City of Los Angeles (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 757, 766.)

To accommodate Plaintiff’s August 8, 2017 permanent modified restrictions, Defendants attempted to purchase a new filing cabinet.  (UMF 21, 42-45.)  Plaintiff required a larger office for that cabinet.  (UMF 44-45.)  While Plaintiff was out on leave in April 2019, TAS was going to remodel and move her into an office with adequate space.  (UMF 58.)  Defendants have shown that they reasonably accommodated Plaintiff for these restrictions.  (Motion at p. 29.)

As of Plaintiff’s March 26, 2019 accommodation meeting, Plaintiff was struggling to manually write health records, which she admitted was an essential function of her position, and there was no one else in the office who could assist due to limited staffing.  (UMF 52.)  Plaintiff also could not take temperatures, which was another essential function of her position.  (UMF 54-55.)  Plaintiff was therefore put on leave in April 2019 and received temporary disability benefits.  (UMF 56-57.)  Because Plaintiff could not perform the essential functions and “FEHA does not require employers to eliminate essential functions of a job to accommodate a disabled employee,” Defendants have shown that they did not fail to accommodate this disability.  (Atkins, supra, 8 Cal.App.5th at p. 719; Motion at p. 29.)

In sum, because Plaintiff was not a “qualified individual” who could perform the essential functions of the position, Defendants have met their initial burden of showing no disability discrimination and no failure to accommodate.

Plaintiff argues that she was able to do other requirements of the job, but she does not dispute that she could not perform the above-identified essential functions.  (Opposition at pp. 26-27.)

Summary adjudication of the third and eleventh causes of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

C.        Collateral Estoppel Also Bars the Claims For Disability Discrimination, Discrimination/Retaliation Based on Association, FEHA Retaliation, Age Discrimination (Third, Fourth, Sixth, Seventh Causes of Action).

Defendant argues that Plaintiff is estopped from refuting that TAS had legitimate, nondiscriminatory and nonretaliatory reasons for its actions given the conclusions made by PERB.  (Motion at pp. 24-25.)

Plaintiff has failed to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact regarding collateral estoppel for this issue.

Summary adjudication of the third, fourth, sixth, and seventh causes of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

C.        Plaintiff Did Not Exhaust Her Administrative Remedies for the Sexual Harassment Claim (Second Cause of Action).

“Under the FEHA, the employee must exhaust the administrative remedy provided by the statute by filing a complaint with the [DFEH] and must obtain from the Department a notice of right to sue in order to be entitled to file a civil action in court based on violations of the FEHA.  [Citations.]  The timely filing of an administrative complaint is a prerequisite to the bringing of a civil action for damages under the FEHA.”  (Romano v. Rockwell Internat., Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 479, 492; see Gov. Code, §§ 12960, 12965, subd. (b).)  Prior to 2020, an employee was required to file an administrative complaint with DFEH within one year after the alleged unlawful action.  (Govt. Code, § 12960, subd. (d) [effective Jan. 1 2018 to Dec. 31, 2019].)  When Government Code section 12960 was amended as of January 1, 2020, Assembly Bill 9 made it clear that the amendment “shall not be interpreted to revive lapsed claims.”  (2019 Cal. Legis. Serv. Ch. 709 (A.B. 9).)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff did not exhaust her administrative remedies for her claims of sexual harassment.  (Motion at pp. 22-23.)  Plaintiff alleges sexual harassment by Williams between 2011 and May 2017.  (E.g., Complaint ¶¶ 22.)  Williams was removed as CEO on February 28, 2019 and was rarely on campus in his reassigned role.  (UMF 8, 10.)  Plaintiff filed administrative complaints on April 30, 2018 and February 6, 2020, but neither alleged sexual harassment.  (UMF 30, 33.)  The only administrative complaint alleging sexual harassment was filed on May 8, 2020.  (UMF 37.)  This was after the one-year period for Plaintiff to file a complaint based on conduct occurring through May 2017.  Defendants have met their burden of showing that Plaintiff did not administratively exhaust her claim for harassment.

Plaintiff argues that “the continuing violation doctrine provides that Defendants are liable for the entire course of conduct including their acts that predated the statutory period and those that occurred within in the statutory period.”  (Opposition at p. 22.)  She argues that her “complaint of harassment continued until the time she was terminated on July 19, 2019, as there had been no action to rectify the ongoing harassment, she had experienced for past several years.”  (Id. at p. 23.)  However, Plaintiff provides no evidence to create a triable issue of material fact regarding the timing of Williams’s actual harassment.

Summary adjudication of the second cause of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

D.        Because Defendants Prevail on the Underlying Claims, They Cannot Be Liable for Failure to Prevent (Fifth Cause of Action).

Defendants argue that because there was no harassment or retaliation, they also cannot be liable for failing to prevent harassment or retaliation.  (Motion at pp. 25-26.)  “[C]ourts have required a finding of actual discrimination or harassment under FEHA before a plaintiff may prevail under [Government Code] section 12940, subdivision (k).”  (Carter v. California Dept. of Veterans Affairs (2006) 38 Cal.4th 914, 925, fn. 4.)

For the reasons discussed above, summary adjudication of the fifth cause of action is also granted in favor of Defendants.  (See Glynn v. Superior Court (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 47, 56.)

E.        There Was No Extreme and Outrageous Conduct for IIED (Eighth Cause of Action).

“‘[T]o state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress a plaintiff must show: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’  [Citation.]  ‘Conduct, to be ‘outrageous’ must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’  [Citation.]”  (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.)  “While the outrageousness of a defendant’s conduct normally presents an issue of fact to be determined by the trier of fact [citation], the court may determine in the first instance, whether the defendant’s conduct may reasonably be regarded as so extreme and outrageous as to permit recovery.”  (Trerice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883.)  When a plaintiff does not suffer physical injury, the conduct must involve “extreme and outrageous intentional invasions of one’s mental and emotional tranquility.”  (Alcorn v. Anbro Engineering, Inc. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 493, 498.)

Defendants argue that there was no timely, outrageous conduct.  (Motion at pp. 26-27.)

With respect to Williams, any IIED based on his conduct is barred by Plaintiff’s failure to exhaust her administrative remedies, as discussed above.  Williams was removed as CEO on February 28, 2019 and was rarely on campus in his reassigned role.  (UMF 8, 10.)  Plaintiff did not timely file an administrative complaint after this.

With respect to TAS, the only allegedly wrongful conduct was placing Plaintiff on leave and eliminating her position.  These personnel actions are insufficient to establish IIED.  “In order to properly manage its business, every employer must on occasion review, criticize, demote, transfer and discipline employees.  Employers are necessarily aware that their employees will feel distressed by adverse personnel decisions, while employees may consider any such adverse action to be improper and outrageous.  Indeed, it would be unusual for an employee not to suffer emotional distress as a result of an unfavorable decision by his employer.”  (Cole v. Fair Oaks Fire Protection Dist. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 148, 160.)

Defendants have met their initial burden of showing that there was no outrageous conduct for IIED.

Plaintiff argues that Williams’s conduct was extreme and outrageous.  (Opposition a pp. 24-25.)  However, Plaintiff does not dispute that she did not timely exhaust her administrative remedies for this conduct.  (See UMF 8, 10.)

Summary adjudication of the eighth cause of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

F.         Plaintiff Cannot Establish Harm For Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention (Tenth Cause of Action).

Plaintiff alleges that she was harmed by TAS’s negligent hiring, supervision, and retention of Williams.  (E.g., Complaint ¶¶ 357-364.)

As discussed above, the alleged conduct by Williams is time-barred because Plaintiff failed to timely exhaust administrative remedies.  (See UMF 8, 10.)  Plaintiff therefore cannot use this conduct to establish her harm due to TAS’s alleged negligence.  (See Motion at pp. 28-29.)

Summary adjudication of the tenth cause of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

G.        Plaintiff Cannot Assert a Common Law Wrongful Termination Claim Against Defendant TAS (Ninth Cause of Action).

Generally, an employee may bring a tort cause of action when his employer terminates his employment in contravention of public policy.  (Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 167, 177 (Tameny).)

A public entity is not liable for an injury arising from an act or omission of the entity, a public employee, or any other person except as provided by statute.  (Gov. Code, § 815.)  “[S]ection 815 bars Tameny actions against public entities.”  (Miklosy v. Regents of University of California (2008) 44 Cal.4th 876, 900.)  Defendants have met their initial burden.  (Motion at pp. 27-28.)

Plaintiff argues that her wrongful termination claim is derivative of her statutory claims.  (Opposition at p. 27.)  However, for all the reasons discussed above, her statutory claims fail.

Summary adjudication of the ninth cause of action is granted in favor of Defendants.

H.        There Is No Basis For Punitive Damages.

For the reasons set forth above, Plaintiff cannot establish an underlying claim upon which punitive damages may be awarded.  (See Motion at pp. 29-31.)

Additionally, public entity is not liable for punitive damages or other damages imposed primarily for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.  (Gov. Code, § 818.)

Summary adjudication of the issue of punitive damages is granted in favor of Defendants.

CONCLUSION

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  Defendants are ordered to submit a proposed judgment within five days.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AMENDED ANSWER

 

Dept. 48

On April 27, 2023, Plaintiff G.B. filed this action against Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District.  On June 30, 2023, Defendant filed an answer.  On March 18, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for leave to file an amended answer.  Plaintiff did not oppose the motion.

The Court may, in its discretion and after notice to the adverse party, allow an amendment to any pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  A motion to amend a pleading must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments and must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted or added, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the allegations are located.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)  The motion shall also be accompanied by a declaration attesting to the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)

Defendant provides a redlined copy of the proposed amended answer showing all amendments.  (Hicks Decl., Ex. 2 [“Amended Answer”].)  Defendant seeks to add an affirmative defense under Education Code section 44808, which provides immunity for off-campus conduct.  Defendant first learned of this defense on November 2, 2023, when Defendant conducted Plaintiff’s deposition.  (Hicks Decl. ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff testified that the abuse occurred off-campus.  (Hicks Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. 1 [deposition excerpts].)  The amendment is necessary and proper to ensure Defendant’s due process right to allege and try an affirmative defense.  (Hicks Decl. ¶ 4.)

The unopposed motion is GRANTED.  Defendant is ordered to file and serve the amended answer within 5 days.

On June 21, Plaintiff William S. Jameson, Jr. filed this action against Defendants Lucia Lopez and Yoel Lopez.

On February 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.  Defendants did not file any oppositions.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice of Debtor’s Motion for Voluntary Dismissal of Chapter 13 Case filed in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the Central District of California – Riverside Division, In Re Yoel Lopez and Lucia Yaneisis Ruizaday, Debtors, Case No. 6:23-bk-15781-WJ is denied as irrelevant.

LEGAL STANDARD

A plaintiff moving for summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by proving each element of a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)  That includes damages when damages are an element.  (Paramount Petroleum Corp. v. Superior Court (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 226, 241.)  Then the burden shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.”  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162-163.)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff’s motion is primarily based on Defendants’ deemed admissions (“RFAs”) from Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions and the Court’s October 17, 2023 order deeming the admissions admitted by Defendants.

Where a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  “[A] deemed admitted order establishes, by judicial fiat, that a nonresponding party has responded to the requests by admitting the truth of all matters contained therein.”  (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979.)  “Any matter admitted in response to a request for admission is conclusively established against the party making the admission in the pending action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.410, subd. (a).)  “As a general rule an admission is conclusive in the action as to the party making it.  [Citations.]  Absent leave of court to amend or withdraw the admission, no contradictory evidence may be introduced.”  (Murillo v. Superior Court (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 730, 736 (Murillo).)  “‘Sometimes, the admissions obtained will even leave the party making them vulnerable to summary judgment.’  [Citations.]”  (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 775.)

The first cause of action is for breach of an unsecured promissory note, through which Lucia Lopez promised to repay Plaintiff $400,000.00 by April 15, 2021.  The second cause of action is a common count for money had and received under the same promissory note.  The third cause of action alleges that Defendants fraudulently promised to repay the promissory note.  The fourth cause of action is for unjust enrichment and restitution of the amount loaned under the promissory note.

A.        The Motion Is Granted With Respect to Defendant Lucia Lopez.

Through the deemed admissions, Plaintiff has established the existence and terms of the promissory note with Lucia Lopez and Plaintiff’s performance by lending the money.  (Undisputed Material Facts “UMF” 1, 4-6, 10.)  Lucia has made only $5,000.00 in payments.  (UMF 11.)

Plaintiff has proven that Lucia breached the promissory note.  Plaintiff has also proven his damages of $395,000.00, interest of $82,500.71 (see Civ. Code, § 3289, subd. (b)), and sanctions of $3,420.00 (pursuant to the Court’s October 17, 2023 order), for a total judgment of $480,920.71.  (See Motion at pp. 4-5.)

Defendants did not file a written opposition with evidence to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, nor can they submit evidence to contradict their judicial admissions.  (Murillo, supra, 143 Cal.App.4th at p. 736.)

Accordingly, summary judgment is granted in favor of Plaintiff and against Lucia Lopez.

B.        The Motion Is Denied With Respect to Defendant Yoel Lopez.

Despite the parties’ deemed admissions, some of these admissions are not supported by the other evidence.  The trial court has the power “to determine the admissibility and relevance of evidence related to admitted facts. . . .  The court must have discretion to admit evidence to elucidate and explain an admission, because the admission of a fact does not always reflect the party’s reasonable understanding of that fact.”  (Fredericks v. Filbert Co. (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 272, 278.)

Lucia is the only borrower under the promissory note.  (Motion, Ex. A.)  Because Yoel was not a party to the agreement, he cannot be liable for breach of contract (first cause of action).  Summary adjudication of the first cause of action is denied.

For the same reason, Yoel cannot be liable for the common count (second cause of action).  “A cause of action for money had and received is stated if it is alleged the defendant ‘is indebted to the plaintiff in a certain sum “for money had and received by the defendant for the use of the plaintiff.”’  [Citation.]”  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)  Yoel was not indebted to Plaintiff under the promissory note.  Summary adjudication of the second cause of action is denied.

The fraud claim (third cause of action) alleges that both Defendants “told Plaintiff they needed to borrow $400,000 to purchase the Property.  They promised to repay the loan 3 weeks after the money was lent.  Thereafter, they repeatedly promised to make payments on the loan, with a series of untruthful claims as to why they failed to pay.”  The initial promise to repay in three weeks is superseded by the promissory note signed by only Lucia.

For the subsequent, repeated promises to repay, Yoel admitted that “in text messages, Lucia Lopez and Yoel Lopez repeatedly promised to repay the $400,000 lent by Plaintiff,” and he never intended to repay the $400,000 borrowed from Plaintiff.”  (Motion, Ex. D at p. 2 [RFA Nos. 10, 12].)  A fraud cause of action may be based on a false promise if “the promisor did not intend to perform at the time he or she made the promise and . . . it was intended to deceive or induce the promisee to do or not do a particular thing.”  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 159.)  Here, however, Plaintiff was not induced to do anything in reliance on Yoel’s promises.  Plaintiff had already loaned the $400,000.00 to Lucia.  Without detrimental reliance by Plaintiff on Yoel’s misrepresentation, there is no actionable fraud.  (See Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184 [among its elements, fraud requires justifiable reliance by the plaintiff].)  Summary adjudication of the third cause of action is denied.

For unjust enrichment (fourth cause of action), Plaintiff must prove “the ‘receipt of a benefit and [the] unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.’  [Citation.]”  (Peterson v. Cellco Partnership (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1593.)  Unjust enrichment is not a cause of action, but a court may construe a cause of action for unjust enrichment as a cause of action for quasi-contract seeking restitution.  (Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 231.)  “[T]he quasi-contract, or contract ‘implied in law,’ is an obligation created by the law without regard to the intention of the parties and designed to restore the aggrieved party to his former position by the return of the thing delivered or the money expended.”  (Masonite Corp. v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (1976) 65 Cal.App.3d 1, 10-11.)

Plaintiff appears to base this claim on Lucia’s purchase of real property using the $400,000.00 and her subsequent transfer of the property to Yoel.  (See UMF 43-55.)  The promissory note was intended to provide Defendants with a loan to purchase real property.  (UMF 45-46, 48.)  Defendants did use the funds to purchase a house.  (UMF 49.)  On February 10, 2022, Lucia executed an interspousal transfer grant deed, granting the property to Yoel as his sole and separate property.  (UMF 54; Motion, Ex. G.)

Yoel is not “‘indebted to the plaintiff in a certain sum “for money had and received by the defendant for the use of the plaintiff.”’  [Citation.]”  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)  Yoel benefitted from Lucia’s borrowed funds, but he did not receive funds for the use of Plaintiff.  Summary adjudication of the fourth cause of action is denied.

CONCLUSION

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED in favor of Plaintiff and against Lucia Lopez.  Plaintiff is ordered to submit a proposed judgment within ten days.  A Non-Appearance Case Review Re: Submission of Proposed Judgment is scheduled for May 22, 2024 at 9:00 a.m.

The motion for summary judgment is DENIED as to Yoel Lopez.
