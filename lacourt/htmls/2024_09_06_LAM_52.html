Case Number: 22STCV15941    Hearing Date: September 6, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Sara Simms’s 4 Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

(1 & 2) Requests for Production

Plaintiff Sara Simms moves to compel defendants Epione Beverly Hills, Inc. and Beverly Hills Plastic Surgery Associates each to serve further responses to requests for production, set one, Nos. 1 and 2.  A party propounding requests for production may move to compel further responses if “[a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete,” “[a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive,” or “[a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.”  (CCP § 2031.310(a).)

Both these defendants made meritless objections in response to the requests for production.  No. 1 demands “Any and all policies you had in effect from 2018 through May 2020 relating to prevention of sexual harassment.”  No. 2 demands, “Any and all DOCUMENTS … in support of your sixth affirmative defense (Avoidable Consequences Doctrine) of your Answer to First Amended Complaint against Plaintiff’s Sixth and Seventh Causes of action of her First Amended Complaint.” 

To both requests, each defendant responded only with the following objections: “Defendant hereby incorporates the preliminary statement set forth above.  Defendant objects to this request because it violates the Mutual Agreement to Arbitrate Claims between Plaintiff and Defendants.  Defendant objects to this request because it is not reasonably particularized.  Defendant objects to this request because it is overbroad and burdensome.  Defendant objects to this request because it seeks documents that are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence or relevant to the subject matter of this action.”

Defendants do not justify their objections.  For the objection that the requests violate the parties’ arbitration agreement, both requests relate to plaintiff’s causes of action for sexual harassment.  On March 28, the court ruled plaintiff could withdraw her sixth and seventh causes of action from arbitration pursuant to the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act.  These requests for production do not violate the arbitration agreement. 

The remaining objections are meritless.  These requests are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  Defendants Epione Beverly Hills, Inc. and Beverly Hills Plastic Surgery Associates’ objections to requests for production Nos. 1 and 2 are overruled.

(3 & 4) Special Interrogatories

Plaintiff Sara Simms moves to compel defendants Epione Beverly Hills, Inc. and Beverly Hills Plastic Surgery Associates each to serve further responses to special interrogatories, set one, Nos. 1-4.  A party may move to compel further responses to interrogatories when an answer “is evasive or incomplete” (CCP § 2030.300(a)(1)) or an objection “is without merit or too general” (id., subd. (a)(3)).

These defendants made meritless objections in response to special interrogatories Nos. 1-4.  No. 1 asks: “Which of the affirmative defenses of your Answer to First Amended Complaint do you assert against Plaintiff’s Sixth and Seventh Causes of action of her First Amended Complaint?”  Nos. 2 through 4 ask, “If you assert the sixth affirmative defense (Avoidable Consequences Doctrine) of your Answer to First Amended Complaint against Plaintiff’s Sixth and Seventh Causes of action of her First Amended Complaint,” then (2) state all supporting facts, (3) identify all supporting witnesses, and (4) identify all supporting documents. 

To each interrogatory, defendants objected: “Defendant objects to this interrogatory because it violates the Mutual Agreement to Arbitrate Claims between Plaintiff and Defendants.  Defendant objects to this interrogatory because it is vague and ambiguous.  Defendant objects to this interrogatory because it is unduly overbroad and burdensome.  Defendant objects to this interrogatory because it seeks information that is neither relevant to the subject matter of this action nor likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” 

Defendants do not justify any of their objections.  All objections are meritless for the same reasons discussed above as to the requests for production.  Each interrogatory is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

Defendants Epione Beverly Hills, Inc. and Beverly Hills Plastic Surgery Associates’ objections to special interrogatories Nos. 1-4 are overruled.

Sanctions

Plaintiff moves for the following sanctions against each defendant and its counsel of record, Steven M. Case Number: 23STCV00076    Hearing Date: September 6, 2024    Dept: 52

(1) Order to Show Cause Re: Entry of Default Judgment; (2) Order to Show Cause Re: Sanctions

(1) OSC Re: Default Judgment

            Plaintiff Salvador Lopez, Jr. requests court judgment by default against defendant Advance Pipe Bending, a business entity form unknown.  Plaintiff’s application for default judgment is defective.

Plaintiff no longer requests default judgment against defendant Mitchell McCray.  Plaintiff did not, however, request to dismiss McCray from the action.  An application for default judgment requires “[a] dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(7).)

The request for default judgment is also defective because a substantial portion of plaintiff’s damages arise from a physical injury incurred in the scope of employment.  The superior court does not have jurisdiction over claims covered by the Workers’ Compensation Act.  (Hughes v. Western MacArthur Co. (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 951, 957.)  That act “provides the exclusive remedy for an injury sustained by an employee in the course and scope of employment.”  (Shirvanyan v. Los Angeles Community College District (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 82, 105.)  In addition to the physical injury itself, the rule “encompasses injury ‘collateral to or derivative of a compensable workplace injury’ [citation], such as emotional distress stemming from the experience of a physical injury at work.”  (Ibid.)  In a civil action, injured employees cannot recover damages for emotional distress suffered “because” a body part “was injured, or because” their “injury was painful, or because” their “injury prevented [them] from working.”  (Id. at pp. 105-106.)  They can, however, recover damages for emotional distress suffered “on account of the way the [employer] treated [them] because of” their physical “limitations.”  (Id. at p. 106.)

Here, plaintiff seeks recovery for “physical pain” and for emotional distress caused by the injury.  His declaration in support of this application totals his damages, including: “Physical Pain: 244 days x $50 = $12,200.00” (Lopez Decl., ¶ 9.a) and “Physical Impairment: 244 days (soreness) x $50 = $12,200.00” (id., ¶ 9.d).  He further states: “This accident also caused additional stress in my marriage.”  (Id., ¶ 16.)  “I lost sleep because of the pain and was very agitated and was in [a] bad mood.”  (Id., ¶ 17.)  “The lasting effects of the injury… have caused me to experience pain.”  (Id., ¶ 18.)  Workers’ compensation is the exclusive remedy for such injuries.

Aside from the rule of workers’ compensation exclusivity, plaintiff cannot recover such damages because he has not alleged a cause of action for damages based on personal injury.  Plaintiff’s first amended complaint alleges causes of action for FEHA violations, unlawful termination, and whistleblower retaliation.  Such violations did not cause his hand injury or the emotional distress deriving from the injury.

            Plaintiff’s papers purport to address this problem by repeating the same text restating assorted rules of law about workers’ compensation.  (Lopez Decl., ¶¶ 11-14.)  But plaintiff’s papers do not explain any basis for which plaintiff can recover these damages.  For example, plaintiff quotes Labor Code section 3852, which provides, “The claim of an employee … for compensation does not affect his or her claim or right of action for all damages proximately resulting from the injury or death against any person other than the employer.”  (Lopez Decl., ¶ 12.)  This action is not a claim against any person other than the employer.  Plaintiff is suing his employer, Advanced Pipe Bending, for violations of employment law.  Plaintiff presents no grounds for recovering damages for a physical injury suffered at the workplace via this civil action against his employer.

(2) OSC Re: Sanctions

            On July 11, 2024, the court ordered plaintiff to show cause why he should not pay $100 in sanctions under California Rules of Court, rule 3.110(h).  That rule provides, “When a default is entered, the party who requested the entry of default must obtain a default judgment against the defaulting party within 45 days after the default was entered, unless the court has granted an extension of time.  The court may issue an order to show cause why sanctions should not be imposed if that party fails to obtain entry of judgment against a defaulting party or to request an extension of time to apply for a default judgment within that time.”

            The clerk entered the defaults of defendants Advance Pipe Bending and Mitchell McCray on March 19, 2024.  More than 45 days have passed.  Initially, plaintiff did not timely request entry of default judgment.  Now, plaintiff has done so, but he also filed a nearly identical declaration and summary of the case as last time.  Plaintiff changed the dates and removed some references to defendant McCray.  The court previously explained why plaintiff’s application was defective based on the workers’ compensation exclusivity rule.  There was no reason to file the same inadequate supporting papers again.  The court will exercise its discretion to order plaintiff to pay the court $100 in sanctions.

Disposition

            Plaintiff’s request for court judgment by default is denied.  The court hereby continues the order to show cause re: entry of default judgment to January 30, 2025, at 8:30 a.m.  Plaintiff shall file a complete default judgment application including all documents listed in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a), no later than January 20, 2025.

            The court hereby orders plaintiff to pay the court $100 in sanctions under California Rules of Court, rule 3.110(h), within 30 days.
