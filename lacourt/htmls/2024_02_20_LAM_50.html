DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV27973    Hearing Date: February 20, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

           

lydia cincore-templeton,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

beverly smith, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

20STCV27973

Hearing Date:

February 20, 2024

Hearing Time:

10:00 a.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

MOTION BY PLAINTIFF TO FILE HER MARCH 13, 2023 OPPOSITION TO DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT UNDER SEAL

 

Background

Plaintiff Lydia Cincore-Templeton (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on July 24, 2020, against Defendants Beverly Smith, Cheryl Hickmon, and Delta Sigma Theta Sorority, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”).

The operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) was filed on October 13, 2021. The TAC asserts causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) violation of Unfair Competition Law, (4) declaratory relief, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (6) negligence, and (7) intentional interference with prospective economic, reputational, and political relations.

On February 8, 2023, Defendants filed a motion to enforce settlement. On March 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed a “Partial Opposition by Plaintiff Lydia Templeton to Defendants’ Motion to Enforce Settlement.”

Plaintiff now moves “for an order to file her March 13, 2023 Opposition to defendants’ motion to enforce settlement documents under seal.” The motion is unopposed.

Discussion

Generally, court records are presumed to be open unless confidentiality is required by law. ((Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c).) If the presumption of access applies, the court may order that a record be filed under seal “if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subdivision (d).)

In support of the motion, Plaintiff submits the declaration of her counsel. Plaintiff’s counsel indicates that at a September 7, 2022 Mandatory Settlement Conference, the parties settled this matter. (Quiller Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[a]fter not obtaining a settlement agreement executed by both parties, the defendants filed a motion to enforce the settlement, with a hearing date of March 24, 2023. On or about March 13, 2023, I filed a partial opposition to defendants’ motion to enforce settlement…Within that opposition, I identified the terms of the settlement agreement.” (Quiller Decl., ¶ 4.)

Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[s]ubsequently, defense counsel conveyed to me his belief that the terms of the agreement are confidential.” (Quiller Decl., ¶ 5.) In addition, Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[o]ne of the sticking points in finalizing the settlement agreement is for this Court to issue an Order sealing the Opposition.” (Quiller Decl., ¶ 8.)

Plaintiff’s counsel notes that Plaintiff filed a previous motion to seal, which was heard on May 4, 2023 and denied. The Court’s May 4, 2023 Order provides, inter alia, that “the memorandum of points and authorities in support of the…motion does not cite to any law or discuss the application of California Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subdivision (d).” (May 4, 2023 Order at p. 3:3-5.)

            In the instant motion, Plaintiff argues that “filing these records under seal achieves the overriding interest in protecting this confidential information.” (Mot. at p. 6:23-24.) Plaintiff asserts that “[t]he right of the parties to maintain confidential settlement terms and communications that are not available to the public, outweighs the public right to access to court filings and, therefore, supports sealing the record.” (Mot. at p. 6:15-17.) The Court notes that Plaintiff does not cite to any legal authority to support this argument. In addition, Plaintiff’s counsel states that “defense counsel conveyed to me his belief that the terms of the agreement are confidential.” (Quiller Decl., ¶ 5.) It is unclear if Plaintiff also asserts that the settlement agreement is confidential.

            In addition, the Court notes that the motion does not appear to contain any argument or evidence pertaining to whether “[a] substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (d)(3).)

            Plaintiff also argues in the motion that “[t]he proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, as only [sic] relates to those records that contain confidential settlement terms and correspondence. Plaintiff is only seeking to seal one document that contains terms deemed ‘confidential’ by the parties, or which may otherwise contain confidential or sensitive information.” (Mot. at p. 6:19-22.) However, the Court notes that the motion does not appear to contain any argument as to whether “[n]o less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (d)(5).)

            As discussed, “[t]he court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.”

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is denied without prejudice.  

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this ruling.

DEFENDANT SECURCAPITAL CORP.’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

Background

Plaintiff Wave Investment, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on December 30, 2021 against Defendants SecurCapital Corp. (“SecurCapital”) and Stephen J. Russell (“Russell”) (jointly, “Defendants”)

Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on November 15, 2023 alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) declaratory relief, (3) breach of partnership or joint venture agreement, (4) breach of fiduciary duty, (5) conversion, (6) fraudulent concealment, (7) fraudulent misrepresentation, (8) negligent misrepresentation,

(9) unjust enrichment, (10) accounting, and (11) constructive trust.

In the TAC, Plaintiff alleges, inter alia, that “[t]his action arises from Defendants’ theft of business and investment opportunities in violation of the law and the Parties’ agreements…The primary opportunity was the acquisition of Breakout Capital (‘Breakout’), a small business lender.” (TAC, ¶¶ 1-2.) “[Plaintiff] identified SecurCapital and Russell as potential investors. [Plaintiff] discussed the opportunity with Defendants, and the parties decided to move forward.” (TAC, ¶ 2.) Plaintiff alleges that “[t]o protect itself and to ensure it would be fully compensated for its efforts, [Plaintiff] entered into a Non-Circumvention Agreement that prohibited Defendants from acquiring any assets of Breakout or otherwise investing in the company without [Plaintiff’s] prior written approval.” (TAC, ¶ 3.)

Plaintiff alleges that “[a]fter signing the Non-Circumvention Agreement and agreeing to be bound thereby, Russell, individually and on behalf of SecurCapital, went behind [Plaintiff’s] back and acquired the assets of Breakout without notice to, or approval from, [Plaintiff]. In doing so, Defendants lied to [Plaintiff] to conceal their plan and negotiations with Breakout to prevent [Plaintiff] from exercising its contractual right to block the deal.” (TAC, ¶ 5.) Plaintiff “seeks to recover its lost profits and other economic injury from the loss of this opportunity, compensation for its substantial work, disgorgement of the benefits Defendants unjustly received, and all related damages it suffered from Defendants’ wrongful conduct.” (TAC, ¶ 6.)

SecurCapital now moves for an order granting it leave to file a Cross-Complaint. Plaintiff opposes.

Evidentiary Objection

The Court sustains Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection to paragraph 8 of the Declaration of Stacy Y. North.

Discussion

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50, subdivision (a), “[a] party shall file a cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50, subdivision (b), “[a]ny other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50, subdivision (c), “[a] party shall obtain leave of court to file any cross-complaint except one filed within the time specified in subdivision (a) or (b). Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.”

In addition, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 426.50, “[a] party who fails to plead a cause of action subject to the requirements of this article, whether through oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause, may apply to the court for leave to amend his pleading, or to file a cross-complaint, to assert such cause at any time during the course of the action. The court, after notice to the adverse party, shall grant, upon such terms as may be just to the parties, leave to amend the pleading, or to file the cross-complaint, to assert such cause if the party who failed to plead the cause acted in good faith. This subdivision shall be liberally construed to avoid forfeiture of causes of action.”  

“A policy of liberal construction of section 426.50 to avoid forfeiture of causes of action is imposed on the trial court. A motion to file a cross-complaint at any time during the course of the action must be granted unless bad faith of the moving party is demonstrated where forfeiture would otherwise result. Factors such as oversight, inadvertence, neglect, mistake or other cause, are insufficient grounds to deny the motion unless accompanied by bad faith.” ((Silver Orgs. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98-99.)

SecurCapital’s counsel attaches as Exhibit A to her declaration a copy of SecurCapital’s proposed Cross-Complaint. (North Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.) The proposed Cross-Complaint is brought by SecurCapital against Cross-Defendants Wave Investment, LLC, a Delaware limited liability company, Mark Boyce, and Wave Investment, LLC, a South Carolina limited liability company. (Ibid.) The proposed Cross-Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) fraudulent concealment, (2) fraudulent misrepresentation, (3) fraudulent inducement, (4) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and (5) declaratory relief. (Ibid.)

In the proposed Cross-Complaint, SecurCapital alleges, inter alia, that “[a]t the time Russell, on behalf of SecurCapital, executed the Non-Circumvention Agreement, Boyce had not disclosed, among other things, that: (a) Breakout was insolvent; (b) Boyce believed that Breakout had misused his money; (c) Boyce had recently learned that he could not access

Boyce/Plaintiff Wave’s $1.2 million (i.e., it was ‘trapped’ in Breakout); and (d) Boyce was trying to find investors for Breakout so that he could recover his approximately $1.2 million. Further, at the time SecurCapital entered into the Non-Circumvention Agreement, Boyce misrepresented that it intended to be a co-investor in the Breakout transaction.” (North Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A, ¶ 23.)

SecurCapital further alleges that “[b]y late June 2019, all parties had agreed to the essential terms of the Breakout transaction. However, Boyce kept delaying the final close as he sought repayment of Cross-Defendants’ ‘trapped’ money. Russell repeatedly urged Boyce to move forward, explaining that SecurCapital had already invested $400,000 in the deal with the understanding Cross-Defendants would approve of and coinvest in the transaction. Boyce began telling Russell that Cross-Defendants did not have the money to participate in the Breakout transaction unless Cross-Defendants recovered their ‘trapped’ money, or words to that effect.” (North Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A, ¶ 39.)

In addition, SecurCapital alleges in the proposed Cross-Complaint that “[o]n or about September 29, 2023, Plaintiff Wave produced documents in this litigation that were not previously produced. The September 29, 2023 production included bank and investment statements evidencing that Cross-Defendants had money to invest in the Breakout transaction at the time of the closing…” (North Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A, ¶ 47.) SecurCapital further alleges that “[o]n or about November 8, 2023, Breakout’s founder, Carl Fairbank (‘Fairbank’) produced nearly 500 text messages between Boyce and Fairbank (the “Boyce-Fairbank Texts”). Many of the Boyce-Fairbank Texts were sent around the time of the closing of the Breakout transaction. The Boyce-Fairbank Texts evidence that, while SecurCapital was focusing on closing the Breakout transaction, Cross-Defendants were focused on entering into an agreement with Breakout that provided for repayment of the ‘trapped cash’ with seemingly no intent of participating in the acquisition.” (Ibid., ¶ 48.)

SecurCapital alleges in the proposed Cross-Complaint’s first cause of action for fraudulent concealment that “between April 2019 and July 2019, Cross-Defendants fraudulently and maliciously concealed material information, including, among other things: (a) Breakout was insolvent; (b) Boyce believed that Breakout had misused his money; (c) Boyce had recently learned that he could not access Cross-Defendants’ approximately $1.2 million that was ‘trapped’ in Breakout; (d) Boyce was trying to find investors for Breakout so that he could recover Cross-Defendants’ money; and (e) Boyce had no intention of investing his own money in the Breakout transaction.” (North Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A, ¶ 50.) SecurCapital alleges that “Cross-Defendants concealment of this material information caused SecurCapital to detrimentally change its position in reliance on Defendants’ concealments as follows: SecurCapital entered into the Non-Circumvention Agreement and subsequently advanced approximately $400,000 of ‘good faith’ loans to Breakout pending the close of the Breakout transaction. SecurCapital then had to move forward with the closing of the transaction or lose the $400,000 that was already invested in Breakout. SecurCapital also had to invest more money than originally contemplated, creating extreme financial hardship, and SecurCapital was forced to expend significant effort finding additional funding and obtain financing on unfavorable terms.” (Ibid., ¶ 53.)

In the instant motion, SecurCapital first asserts that “leave to file the compulsory cross-complaint against Wave DE[1] must be granted because there in [sic] no bad faith.” (Mot. at p. 6:13-14.)

SecurCapital asserts that because Wave Investment LLC, a Delaware limited liability company is the Plaintiff in this action, and because the proposed cross-claims against it arise “out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause of action which the plaintiff alleges in [its] complaint,” the proposed cross-claims against Wave Investment LLC, a Delaware limited liability company are compulsory. (Mot. at p. 7:9-12.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30, subdivision (a), “[e]xcept as otherwise provided by statute, if a party against whom a complaint has been filed and served fails to allege in a cross-complaint any related cause of action which (at the time of serving his answer to the complaint) he has against the plaintiff, such party may not thereafter in any other action assert against the plaintiff the related cause of action not pleaded.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 426.10, subd. (c), “related cause of action” means “a cause of action which arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause of action which the plaintiff alleges in his complaint.” In Foot’s Transfer & Storage Co. v. Superior Court (1980) 114 Cal.App.3d 897, 901, cited by SecurCapital, the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he granting of leave to file a cross-complaint is particularly of importance in avoiding forfeiture of causes of action because of the principle expressed in section 426.30 of the Code of Civil Procedure, that cross-complaints relating to the subject of the action must be filed therein or are deemed lost.”

 SecurCapital argues that it “seeks to file its Cross-Complaint based on recently obtained discovery regarding the true financial condition of Cross-Defendants at the time they represented an inability to invest, and upon discovery of the Fairbank-Boyce Texts indicating Cross-Defendants lack of intent to invest in Breakout at all…Because SecurCapital seeks in good faith to bring its Cross-Complaint against Wave DE based upon newly discovered evidence, the Court should grant the Motion.” (Mot. at p. 7:20-25.)

In her supporting declaration, SecurCapital’s counsel states that “[o]n September 29, 2023, as part of a supplemental document production, Plaintiff produced bank statements evidencing that Plaintiff had access to sufficient funds in May 2019 and July 2019 to fund Plaintiff’s share of the contemplated $2 million investment in Newco.” (North Decl., ¶ 5.) SecurCapital’s counsel also states that “[o]n or about November 8, 2023, Breakout’s founder, Carl Fairbank (‘Fairbank’) produced nearly 500 text messages between Boyce and Fairbank, none of which had been previously produced in this litigation (the ‘Boyce-Fairbank Texts’).” (North Decl., ¶ 6.) SecurCapital’s counsel asserts that “[t]he Boyce-Fairbank Texts evidence that, while SecurCapital was focusing on closing the Breakout transaction, Boyce and the Wave entities were focused on entering into an agreement with Breakout that provided for repayment of approximately $1.2 million ‘trapped cash’ Boyce had earlier invested in Breakout, with seemingly no intent of participating in the acquisition with SecurCapital.” (North Decl., ¶ 6.)

In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that “SecurCapital’s delay in seeking to bring cross-claims is inexcusable and demonstrates the motion is intended only to prejudice [Plaintiff].” (Opp’n. at p. 11:3-4.)

Plaintiff contends that “SecurCapital’s claim that it only just learned of the alleged facts supporting its proposed cross-claims in late 2023 ignores the weight of contrary evidence in the record. Defendants’ own materials and testimony confirm that SecurCapital knew of the supposed ‘newly learned’ facts giving rise to their claims in 2019.” (Opp’n at p. 11:10-13, emphasis omitted.) Plaintiff submits the Declaration of Mark Boyce, the founder, principal, and sole member of Wave Investment, LLC. (Boyce Decl., ¶ 1.) Mr. Boyce states in his declaration that “[a]ttached as Exhibit 1 herein is a true and correct copy of an e-mail with Russell that I received, produced by Wave…” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. 1.) The September 5, 2019 email attached to Mr. Boyce’s declaration states, inter alia, as follows:

 

“Mark yet again rather than coming through with your commitment to join the preferred series a group of investors by topping up your investment You’re not now bailing on me altogether…I keep going back to the term sheet where are you were in for a half $1 million and then up to 40% of the second $1.5 million…Just clearly illustrates all you were doing with me is leveraging me to get your settlement with Carl and had no good Faith intention of being a partner in this with me despite the term sheet you executed…It’s bitterly disappointing and you left me hanging with the bag having to replace your equity plus haircut money in a company I would have never bought without your commitment…” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. 1.)  

            Mr. Boyce states that “[t]he reference to ‘settlement with Carl’ is a reference to the $1.2 million that Wave had loaned Breakout, which Breakout owed it.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 3.)  

            Plaintiff also argues that “SecurCapital was likewise on notice that [Plaintiff] had sufficient funds to close the Breakout deal before September 2023, when Defendants received Wave’s 2019 bank statements: it explicitly asked Boyce about this issue at his deposition before it received those documents. The fact that Defendants prepared to depose Boyce on this topic demonstrates it had sufficient awareness of the issue well in advance of December 2023 when it filed this Motion.” (Opp’n at p. 11:17-21.) Plaintiff cites to the following testimony from the September 8, 2023 deposition of Mr. Boyce: “Q. At this point in time, did Wave have the approximately 1.5 million available to invest? A. Yes. Q. Okay. Did that include the nearly 1.2 million that was already invested in Breakout? A. No.” (Miller Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. J (Boyce Depo.) at pp. 67:24-68:4.)

SecurCapital counters that in Foot’s Transfer & Storage Co. v. Superior Court, supra, 114 Cal.App.3d at pages 903-904, the Court of Appeal found that “[r]eal party in interest Worth also urges us to uphold the trial court’s denial of petitioner’s request to file a cross-complaint on the theory that petitioner’s bad faith was demonstrated by the fact that it had, despite its assertions to the contrary, all the facts it needed to support the filing of a cross-complaint as early as 1976, and this was before the complaint was filed by Worth. We recognize that the Gherman court relied, in part, for its conception of ‘bad faith,’ on the premise that delay only may constitute the requisite bad faith to preclude the granting of the request to file a cross-complaint when it appears that a delayed cross-complaint, if allowed, would work a substantial injustice to the opposing party and would prejudice that party’s position in some way. But in the case at bench, we fail to perceive any substantial injustice or prejudice to the real party in interest Worth in this litigation by the filing of petitioner Foot’s Transfer & Storage’s cross-complaint, as belated as it may be. The correspondence of counsel establishes that neither side was unaware of the claims of the other. Similarly, the discovery undertaken in 1979 also indicates a lack of any such unawareness. We hold, therefore, that the evidence to support the trial court’s denial of petitioner’s motion to file a cross-complaint was insufficient as a matter of law in view of the well established liberality with which section 426.50 of the Code of Civil Procedure is to be applied. It is preferable that the parties have their day in court.”

Similarly here, Plaintiff asserts that SecurCapital delayed in seeking leave to file the proposed Cross-Complaint, and that “SecurCapital’s claim that it only just learned of the alleged facts supporting its proposed cross-claims in late 2023 ignores the weight of contrary evidence in the record.” (Opp’n at p. 11:10-11.) Plaintiff also asserts that “the motion is intended only to prejudice [Plaintiff].” (Opp’n at p. 11:4). It appears that such asserted prejudice is an increase in Plaintiff’s litigation costs, as Plaintiff argues that “[t]he Court should not allow SecurCapital to take advantage of the lengthy delay occasioned by the Court’s busy docket to force [Plaintiff] to incur significant expense through the addition of new parties and claims.” (Opp’n at p. 15:8-11.) SecurCapital counters that any such prejudice “does not constitute prejudice to Plaintiff’s position.” (Reply at p. 7:8-9, emphasis omitted.)[2]

The Court also does not find that Plaintiff has demonstrated “substantial evidence of bad faith” by SecurCapital. (Silver Orgs. v. Frank, supra, 217 Cal.App.3d at p. 100.) The Silver Orgs Court noted that “[s]ubstantial evidence of bad faith will obviously negate good faith, the latter being the sine qua non to the granting of a section 426.50 motion. It is therefore necessary to examine the amorphous concept of bad faith…‘Bad faith’ is defined as ‘[t]he opposite of ‘good faith,’ generally implying or involving actual or constructive fraud, or a design to mislead or deceive another, or a neglect or refusal to fulfill some duty or some contractual obligation, not prompted by an honest mistake . . ., but by some interested or sinister motive[,] . . . not simply bad judgment or negligence, but rather…the conscious doing of a wrong because of dishonest purpose or moral obliquity; . . . it contemplates a state of mind affirmatively operating with furtive design or ill will.” (Ibid.)  

In the motion, SecurCapital also asserts that “leave to file the permissive cross-claims against Boyce and Wave SC should be granted ‘in the interest of justice’.” (Mot. at p. 8:1-2.) As set forth above, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50, subdivision (c), “[a] party shall obtain leave of court to file any cross-complaint except one filed within the time specified in subdivision (a) or (b). Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.” SecurCapital asserts that “[a]s Boyce and Wave SC are not plaintiffs in this case, SecurCapital’s cross-claims against them are permissive.” (Mot. at p. 9:5-6.)[3] SecurCapital refers the proposed Cross-Defendant Wave Investment, LLC, a South Carolina limited liability company as “Wave SC.” (Mot. at p. 1:5-6.)

SecurCapital argues that “[t]he interests of justice…favor granting leave to file the permissive cross-claims in order that all claims between the parties arising out of the Breakout investment plan may be determined at once, to avoid the unnecessary duplication of time and effort inherent in pursuit of the permissive cross-claims in a separate action.” (Mot. at p. 9:19-22.) SecurCapital also asserts that the Cross-Defendants to the proposed Cross-Complaint will not suffer prejudice as the trial in this action is currently set for December 11, 2024. 

            In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that “[w]ith respect to SecurCapital’s permissive counterclaims, the court has even more discretion to deny leave.” (Opp’n at p. 12:23-24.) SecurCapital cites to Crocker Nat. Bank v. Emerald (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 852, 864, where the Court of Appeal concluded that “[w]e find the trial court did not abuse its discretion in refusing Emerald leave to file the cross-complaint. Emerald sought leave to file his cross-complaint seven years after the onset of litigation, and five years after a demurrer was sustained to Emerald’s fifth amended cross-complaint without leave to amend. Moreover, Emerald’s motion for leave to file a cross-complaint was filed only five months before the trial date. Emerald provides no explanation for his delay in seeking the court’s permission to file his proposed cross-complaint. Emerald knew of the facts upon which he based his first cause of action for gross negligence since at least August 1980, when he filed his first proposed cross-complaint. In addition, the facts upon which he based his second and third causes of action were known to him since 1986. Emerald’s declaration in support of his motion for reconsideration, following the trial court’s order granting Crocker’s motion for extension of a writ of attachment, contains the same allegations as his proposed cross-complaint. Given Emerald’s lack of explanation concerning his failure to seek leave of the court between 1986 and 1988, we find the trial court acted within its discretion in denying Emerald leave to file his cross-complaint.” The Crocker Court also noted that “Emerald’s proposed cross-complaint is a permissive cross-complaint, governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50, subdivision (c)…” ((Ibid.)
            Plaintiff argues that here, “SecurCapital is seeking leave to file permissive cross-claims over two years after this lawsuit was filed. Fact and expert discovery have been completed, motions in limine are fully briefed, trial readiness binders were lodged, exhibit binders were lodged, and the parties appeared for a Final Status Conference on November 17, 2023, fully prepared to try this case on December 6, 2023. The matter did not proceed to trial due to the Court’s docket availability, which resulted in a lengthy continuance to December 11, 2024. Only after all that has SecurCapital sought leave to assert cross-claims that it has sat on for years. It is not in the interests of justice to reward such sandbagging.” (Opp’n at p. 13:2-9.) Plaintiff also asserts that “much like in Crocker, SecurCapital has no credible explanation for its delay. The evidence shows that SecurCapital accused the Wave Parties of this exact wrongdoing over two years before this lawsuit was filed and over four years before SecurCapital supposedly received the evidence that made it aware of these claims.” (Opp’n at p. 13:9-13.)

            In the reply, SecurCapital asserts that Crocker is distinguishable, as the proposed Cross-Complaint is the first one SecurCapital seeks to file in this action. SecurCapital also points to the evidence in support of the motion that (as discussed above), “[o]n September 29, 2023, as part of a supplemental document production, Plaintiff produced bank statements evidencing that Plaintiff had access to sufficient funds in May 2019 and July 2019 to fund Plaintiff’s share of the contemplated $2 million investment in Newco.” (North Decl., ¶ 5.) In addition, SecurCapital points to the evidence that “[o]n or about November 8, 2023, Breakout’s founder…produced nearly 500 text messages between Boyce and Fairbank, none of which had been previously produced in this litigation.” (North Decl., ¶ 6.) As discussed, SecurCapital’s counsel asserts that such texts “evidence that, while SecurCapital was focusing on closing the Breakout transaction, Boyce and the Wave entities were focused on entering into an agreement with Breakout that provided for repayment of approximately $1.2 million ‘trapped cash’ Boyce had earlier invested in Breakout, with seemingly no intent of participating in the acquisition with SecurCapital.” (North Decl., ¶ 6.)

Lastly, Plaintiff argues that “[t]he interests of justice further favor denial of leave for SecurCapital to assert cross-claims because the claims are meritless on their face.” (Opp’n at p. 13:18-19.) SecurCapital counters that any issues as to the proposed cross-claims are more “appropriately raised by demurrer, summary judgment, or other pretrial proceeding after the compulsory cross-complaint has been filed.” (Silver Orgs. v. Frank, supra, 217 Cal.App.3d at p. 102.) The Court agrees.[4]  

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that SecurCapital has demonstrated that it is “in the interest of justice” for the Court to grant SecurCapital leave to file the proposed Cross-Complaint. ((Id., § 428.50, subd. (c).)

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, SecurCapital’s motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is granted. The Court orders SecurCapital to file and serve the proposed Cross-Complaint within 3 days of the date of this Order. 

SecurCapital is ordered to give notice of this Order.
