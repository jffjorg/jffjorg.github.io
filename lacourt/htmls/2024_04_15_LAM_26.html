DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 23LBCV01405    Hearing Date: April 15, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles 

 

 

EDANA JONES, TYKISHA BAIN, and RAYDELL LATRENT PATTERSON, 

  

Plaintiffs, 

vs. 

 

5505 ACKERFIELD APT LLC, AJJ2020 LLC, and MASUDA INVESTMENTS LLC, and DOES 1-50, inclusive, 

  

Defendants. 

 

 Case No.:  23LBCV01405

  

 Hearing Date:  April 15, 2024

  

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: 

DEFENDANT 5505 ACKERFIELD APT, LLC’S MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF COMPLAINT

 

 

 

Background 

 

On July 27, 2023, Plaintiffs Edana Jones, Tykisha Bain, and Raydell Latrent Patterson (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a breach of habitability action against Defendants 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC (“Ackerfield”), AJJ2020 LLC (“AJJ20202”), Masuda Investments LLC (“Masuda”), asserting four causes of action for (1) Failure to Provide Habitable Dwelling, (2) Breach of the Covenant and Right to Quiet Enjoyment and Possession of the Property, (3) Nuisance, and (4) Negligence. 

On November 8, 2023, Defendant Ackerfield filed the instant motion to strike portions of the Complaint.

On November 13, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their opposition.

No reply has been filed by Defendant Ackerfield as of April 11, 2024.

 

Allegations of the Complaint

The Complaint alleges the following.  505 Ackerfield Avenue, Long Beach, CA 90805 (“Subject Property”) “is a multiple family dwelling, which is occupied by tenants and their families, including low-income tenants, minorities and families with children.”  (Complaint ¶ 14.)  

Ackerfield is the current owner of the Subject Property since 2021 and were Plaintiffs’ landlord.  (Id. ¶¶ 5-6.)  AJJ2020 owned the Subject Property from October 2020 to October 2021 and were landlords of the Subject Property.  (Id. ¶¶ 7-8.)  Masuda owned the Subject Property from December 2012 to October 2020 and were landlords of the Subject Property.  (Id. ¶¶ 9-10.)   

Plaintiffs are current and former tenants of Subject Property.  (Id. ¶ 1.)  Plaintiff Edana Jones resided in unit 103 from October 2021 to February 2023.  (Id. ¶ 2, Exh. A.)  Plaintiffs Tykisha Bain and Raydell Latrent Patterson have lived in unit 104 from July 2019 to present.  (Id. ¶ 3, Exh. A.) 

Plaintiffs made numerous complaints about substandard conditions at the Subject Property, which Defendants Ackerfield, AJJ2020, and Masuda refused to repair. (Id. ¶ 18.)   Rather, Defendants Ackerfield, AJJ2020, and Masuda threatened, harassed, and intimidated the Jones Plaintiffs when they continued to complain about the Subject Property’s condition.  (Id. ¶ 18.)   
 

Legal Standard 

 

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)  For the purposes of a motion to strike pursuant to Sections 435 to 437 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the term “pleading” means a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint, and an immaterial allegation or irrelevant matter in a pleading entails (1) an allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense, (2) an allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense, or (3) a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 435, subd. (a)), 431.10, subds. (b)(1)-(3).)  

 

Discussion 

 

            Defendant Ackerfield moves to strike the following portions of the Complaint:

 

1.     Paragraph 18, page 6, lines 24-26: "Defendants refused to repair the Property as requested, and threatened, harassed and intimidated the Plaintiffs when they continued to complain about Property conditions."

 

2.     Paragraph 21, page 7, lines 7-8: "Defendants acted with callous and intentional disregard for the Plaintiffs' health, safety, and well-being."

 

3.     Paragraph 28, page 8, lines 10-11: "Defendants also harassed the Plaintiffs such that the Plaintiffs were deprived of the quiet enjoyment of their home."

 

4.     Paragraph 29, page 8, lines 12-13: "Defendants conscious disregard of the consequences of their actions and failures to act was intentional, malicious, and outrageous."

 

5.     Paragraph 54, page 11, lines 5-7: "Defendants' actions and failures to act as alleged above were knowing, intentional, and malicious. Plaintiffs are therefore entitled to exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial."

 

6.     Paragraph 64, page 12, lines 17-19: "Defendants' actions and failures to act as alleged above were knowing, intentional, and malicious. Plaintiffs are therefore each entitled to exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial."

 

7.     Paragraph 75, page 13, lines 26-28: "Defendants' failures to abate the public and/or private nuisances at the Property were knowing, intentional, and malicious. Plaintiffs are therefore entitled to exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial."

 

8.     Paragraph 3 on the First Cause of Action in the Prayer for Relief, page 15, line 11: "For exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial."

 

9.     Paragraph 2 on the Second Cause of Action in the Prayer for Relief, page 15, line 14: "For exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial."

 

10.  Paragraph 2 on the Third Cause of Action in the Prayer for Relief, page 15, line 17: "For exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial."

 

Defendant Ackerfield argues that the Complaint’s punitive damages allegations/prayers are not supported by pleadings beyond conclusory statements devoid of the specificity required for oppression, fraud, or malice. 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that the Complaint sufficiently pleads grounds for punitive damages.

Defendant Ackerfield has not filed a reply as of April 11, 2024.

Corporate Defendant

“When the defendant is a corporation, ‘[a]n award of punitive damages … must rest on the malice of the corporation’s employees’” specifically, “the oppression, fraud, or malice perpetrated, authorized, or knowingly ratified by an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation,” where a managing agent “include[s] only those corporate employees who exercise substantial independent authority and judgment in their corporate decision making so that their decisions ultimately determine corporate policy.’” (Wilson v. Southern California Edison Co. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 123, 164, citations omitted.)

Here, the pleaded conduct does not specifically identify who, as an agent of Defendant Ackerfield, engaged in the alleged conduct in the Complaint.  Nevertheless, the Complaint alleges that “each agent, employee and representative of any entity Defendant that performed the actions for such entity Defendant, was acting within the course and scope of such agency, service and representation, and that such entity Defendant directed, aided and abetted, authorized or ratified each and every act and conduct hereinafter alleged” and  “Defendants and the agents were present at or inspected the Property and the complaints made by the Plaintiffs.”  (Complaint, ¶¶ 13, 23.)  The Court is therefore satisfied that the Complaint sufficiently alleges, at the pleadings stage, that some director, officer, or managing agent of Defendant Ackerfield ratified the conduct of its unnamed agents pleaded in the Complaint.

 

Specificity

 

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.”¿ (Clauson v. Superior Court¿(1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255; accord Cyrus v. Haveson (1976) 65 Cal.App.3d 306, 316-17.)  Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud, or malice. (Smith v. Superior Court¿(1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 (citations omitted).)  Conclusory characterizations of defendant’s conduct as willful, intentional or fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of the necessary factual grounds for punitive damages.  (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.)     

To establish “malice”, a plaintiff must demonstrate that (1) the defendant intended to cause injury to plaintiff or (2) despicable conduct with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.  (Civ. Code §3294(c)(1).).  “[A]bsent an intent to injure the plaintiff, malice requires more than a willful and conscious disregard of the plaintiffs’ interest.  The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.”  (College Hosp. Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)  To establish “oppression,” Plaintiff must allege “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”  (Id.)  To establish “fraud,” Plaintiffs must allege “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”  (Id.)   

The Court finds that the Complaint fails to plead sufficient ultimate facts of oppression, fraud, or malice as to support its prayer for punitive damages.  The Court agrees with Defendant Ackerfield that Plaintiffs’ allegations that Defendant Ackerfield threatened and harassed Plaintiffs do not contain specific facts to support such claims and that Plaintiffs merely plead statutory phrases.  Plaintiffs argue in opposition that when Defendant Ackerfield owned and managed the Subject Property, the Property was uninhabitable as a result of Defendant Archerfield’s and its agents’ actions and failures to act.  Upon review, however, the Court finds these allegations are insufficient to rise to the level of malice, oppression, or fraud.  Mere negligence, if any, is insufficient to justify an award for punitive damages.  Further, Plaintiffs argue that the Complaint alleges wrongful intent and cites Cyrus v. Haveson (1976) 65 Cal.App.3d 30.  In that forcible entry case, the Court noted that no wrongful motive or purpose was stated in the complaint.  In this case, the Complaint also does not contain allegations of wrongful motive or purpose. 
 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER 

 

Defendant Ackerfield’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED with 30 days leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice and file proof of same.

Legal Standard 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action.  The person deposed may be a natural person, an organization such as a public or private corporation, a partnership, an association, or a governmental agency.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) 

Code of Civil Procedure §¿2025.450(a) provides:¿“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for¿inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”

Code of Civil Procedure §¿2025.450(b) provides:¿“A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:¿ 

 

1.     The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿ 

 

2.     The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. 

 

Code of Civil Procedure §¿2025.450(c) provides, “(1) If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”¿ 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . .¿If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the¿sanction unjust.”¿¿Failing to respond¿or to submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010(d).)¿

K.”)

EDWARD NEWMAN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

PROSEGUR SECURITY USA, INC, et al

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV11894

 

  Hearing Date:  April 16, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS AS TO THE COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Prosegur Security USA, Inc., Joshua Letourneau, Ty Stafford, Dave Schiller, Kelly Mucray, Prosegur Security Monitoring, Inc., Prosegur Security Integration, LLC, and Prosegur EAS USA, LLC,

RESPONDING PARTY:  Plaintiff Edward Newman

The Court considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 25, 2023, plaintiff Edward Newman (“Plaintiff”), commenced this action against defendants Prosegur Security USA, Inc., Joshua Letourneau, Ty Stafford, Dave Schiller, Kelly Mucray, Prosegur Security Monitoring, Inc., Prosegur Security Integration, LLC, Prosegur EAS USA, LLC, and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging (1) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (2) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (3) Hostile Work Environment; (4) Unlawful Retaliation in Violation of Public Policy; and (5) Wrongful Termination.

The complaint in relevant part alleges the following: Plaintiff was employed as a director of business development and sales at Prosegur Security USA, Inc. since February 27, 2017. He was recognized as a top salesman, responsible for substantial revenue generation and consistently exceeding sales quotas.  (Compl. ¶ 14.)  In May 2022, Prosegur underwent significant changes in management that adversely affected Newman. These changes included a new sales strategy and company culture that Plaintiff found to be degrading and intimidating. His sales region and available products were significantly reduced, yet he was still expected to meet and exceed prior sales goals.  (Compl. ¶ 16-18.)  Plaintiff was placed on a Performance Improvement Plan (“PIP”), which he perceived as a guise for pushing him out of his sales role.  This plan was implemented in a manner that he found to be dubious and prolonged, further contributing to his stress and anxiety.  (Compl. ¶ 18-19.) 

The new management team excluded Plaintiff from sales meetings and reduced his involvement in the sales process.  He was offered a lesser role of account manager despite his proven track record in sales, which added to his feelings of marginalization (Compl. ¶ 20-22.)  The changes at Prosegur and the resulting work environment caused Plaintiff significant emotional and physical distress, including depression, anxiety, gastrointestinal issues, and an overall inability to function effectively both personally and professionally.  These symptoms were confirmed by medical diagnosis.  (Compl. ¶ 23-28.)  Plaintiff’s work environment became increasingly hostile, marked by isolation and strategic changes that severely impacted his ability to perform his job duties.  This hostile environment was maintained over several months, worsening Plaintiff’s mental health and exacerbating his physical symptoms.  (Compl. ¶ 29-31.)

On November 28, 2023, Defendants filed the instant motion for judgment on the pleadings.  On April 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition and on April 9, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer but is made after the time for demurrer has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).) Except as provided by statute, the rules governing demurrers apply. (Civic Partners Stockton, LLC v. Youssefi (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1005, 1012.) “Like a demurrer, the grounds for the motion [for judgment on the pleadings] must appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Id. at 1013.) In ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “[a]ll allegations in the complaint and matters upon which judicial notice may be taken are assumed to be true.” (Rippon v. Bowen (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1313.)

Discussion

Meet and Confer

Per CCP section 439(a), before filing a judgment on the pleadings pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings...” (CCP § 439(a).) The moving party shall file and serve with the motion for judgment on the pleadings a declaration concerning the parties meet and confer efforts.  (CCP § 439(a)(3).)

Defendants have not filed a declaration showing that it complied with the meet and confer requirement. Plaintiff does not contest this lack of confirmation, therefore the Court will consider the motion on the merits. 

Procedural Defects

            Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleadings has been improperly brought.  Plaintiff relies on Code Civ. Proc. section 438, which states a motion for JOP may be brought: “If the moving party is a defendant, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the defendant to demur to the complaint has expired.”  Plaintiff argues that Defendants filed their motion before a single Defendant filed an Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.  Also, Plaintiff contends that Defendants never filed an extension to file a demurrer or filed a Demurrer.

In reply, Defendants argue that their motion is procedurally proper because according to Section 438(f)(1), a motion for judgment on the pleadings can be made after the defendant has filed an answer to the complaint and the time for the plaintiff to demur to the answer has expired.  Defendants point out that Section 438(g)(1) states that such a motion can still be made even if the party did not demur to the pleading on the same grounds as the motion.  Defendants highlight that they are utilizing this provision, arguing that they did not need to demur or answer the complaint on the same grounds before filing their motion.

The Court finds Defendants’ arguments to be persuasive as section 438(g)(1) allows a motion for JOP to be made even if a party did not previously demur to the complaint or answer on the same grounds as those being used in the motion for JOP.  Thus, the Court will consider the merits.

Merits

            Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Defendants argue that the alleged actions are typical business decisions and not “extreme and outrageous” as required for this claim.  They contend that such actions are reasonable for business purposes and not excessive beyond what is tolerated in a civilized community. They also point out that there is no evidence these actions were intended to cause distress or that they were done with reckless disregard of the probability of causing such distress. 

In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that Defendants’ actions go beyond mere business decisions, describing a hostile campaign aimed at forcing out the existing sales team.  He argues that the overhaul of the sales process, along with the removal of resources and established clients, created a hostile work environment.  This was coupled with demeaning behavior and threats aimed at undermining the sales team’s confidence and performance.  Plaintiff contends that these actions were intended to distress the sales team and were not ordinary business practices, thus supporting his claim of intentional infliction of emotional distress.

In reply, Defendants reassert the arguments made in their motion.

The Court finds that Plaintiff fails to allege that Defendants’ conduct was intended to cause distress or done with reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress.  The allegations also fail to show that the actions taken by Defendants were so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society. 

Thus, the motion for JOP as to the IIED claim is GRANTED with leave to amend.

            Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

Defendants argue that there is no independent tort for NIED as it is a form of negligence, requiring a duty to the plaintiff.  They contend that Plaintiff has not sufficiently defined any specific duty owed to him, nor has he articulated how any duty was breached.  Defendants describe the actions taken by Prosegur as standard business decisions necessary for its operation, not extreme actions that would traditionally support an NIED claim.  

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that Defendants breached a duty of care by making business changes that foreseeably caused severe emotional distress.  He asserts that the changes in sales processes, removal of resources, and direct demeaning comments were known or should have been known by Defendants to cause distress, arguing that the management’s actions were negligent given their predictable negative impact on his and others’ mental health.

In reply, Defendants reassert the arguments made in their motion.

Here, the Court agrees with Defendants’ arguments in that Plaintiff fails to sufficiently identify a duty that Defendants owed to Plaintiff.

Thus, the motion for JOP as to the NIED claim is GRANTED with leave to amend.

            Hostile Work Environment

Defendants point out that Plaintiff fails to cite any statutory basis for this claim, such as the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) or Title VII, which are typically used to outline a hostile work environment claim based on discrimination.  They argue that being a “salesperson” is not a protected class and that the described workplace changes do not meet the legal definition of harassment that is severe or pervasive enough to alter employment conditions significantly.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ actions created a hostile work environment by making derogatory comments, stripping resources, and altering sales processes that affected all sales personnel, not just him.  He asserts that the environment was not only toxic but also intentionally designed to be difficult, which qualifies as harassment under legal standards by significantly altering the conditions of employment.

In reply, Defendants reassert the arguments made in their motion.

            The Court agrees with Defendants’ arguments that Plaintiff provides no statutory basis for the hostile work environment claim, specifically by failing to identify how he belongs in a protected class.  Further, the Court does not find that allegations to meet the level of harassment that would be severe or pervasive enough to alter employment conditions significantly.

Thus, the motion for JOP as to the hostile work environment claim is GRANTED with leave to amend.

            Retaliation in Violation of Public Policy Claim

Defendants state that Plaintiff has not alleged any actual whistleblowing or reporting of illegal activities, which are necessary elements for a retaliation claim.  They describe the purported retaliatory actions as standard business practices applied universally across the company, not targeted actions against Plaintiff.  This, they argue, negates the basis for a retaliation claim.

In opposition, Plaintiff claims that the changes in his employment conditions were retaliatory, intended to demote him and reduce his income after he resisted the new, harsher employment conditions.  He suggests that his reduction in sales area and consequent loss of commissions were punitive actions for not accepting the new role of account manager, which would have further decreased his earnings potential.

In reply, Defendants reassert the arguments made in their motion.

            Plaintiff has not alleged any report of illegal activity he made nor that he has suffered any retaliatory action as a result thereof, and thus fails to allege facts to state a cause of action under this claim.

Thus, the motion for JOP as to the hostile work environment claim is GRANTED with leave to amend.

            Wrongful Termination

Defendants emphasize that Plaintiff was not actually terminated but resigned, which critically undermines a wrongful termination claim.  They also challenge the lack of identification of any public policy that was allegedly violated, which is required to substantiate a wrongful termination claim under public policy exception criteria.

            In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that he was constructively discharged, a condition where the employer’s actions make the work environment so intolerable that a reasonable person in the employee’s position would feel compelled to resign.  He argues this was effectively a wrongful termination as it forced him out of the company under duress, rather than him voluntarily deciding to leave.

            In reply, Defendants assert that Plaintiff was not actually terminated but resigned, and thus his claim for wrongful termination is invalid.  They argue that even if he was claiming constructive discharge, it alone does not establish wrongful termination without additional evidence of a violation of specific legal rights or public policy, which Plaintiff has not provided.

            Here, Plaintiff does not identify a public policy that was allegedly violated, such that his claim for wrongful termination cannot stand.

Thus, the motion for JOP as to the wrongful termination claim is GRANTED with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            Thus, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

            Plaintiff is given 30 days leave to amend.
