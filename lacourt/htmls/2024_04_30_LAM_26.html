DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 22STCV40554    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

 

TAWANDA HONORS, ET AL.,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

5505 ACKERFIELD APRTS LLC, ET AL.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:  22STCV40554

 

 Hearing Date:  April 30, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

(1) defendant weststar property MANAGEMENT, inc’s demurrer to second amended complaint

(2) DEFENDANTS 5505 ackerfield apts, llc and weststar property MANAGEMENT, inc.’s motion to strike

 

 

 

Background

            Plaintiffs leased residential premises located at 5505 Ackerfield Ave., Long Beach, CA 90805 that were owned, managed and/or operated by Defendants.  Plaintiffs allege Defendants allowed multiple dangerous conditions to exist at the property that rendered the premises uninhabitable and dangerous to the Plaintiff’s health and safety.  Plaintiffs filed a formal complaint with the City of Long Beach Department of Health.  Plaintiffs allege Defendants also collected rent despite these conditions and illegally served 3-Day Notices to Pay Rent or Quit in violation of Civil Code §1942.2.  Plaintiffs also allege Defendants failed to properly account for and return their security deposits. 

            On December 27, 2022, Plaintiffs filed this action against Defendants 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC, AJJ2020 LLC, Masuda Investments LLC and Weststar Property Management, Inc.  The operative complaint is the Second Amended Complaint filed on December 22, 2023 alleging (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied warranty of habitability; (3) nuisance; (4) IIED; (5) negligence; and (6) violation of Civil Code §1942.4.

            On July 27, 2023, three other residents of 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC, Edana Jones, Tykisha Bain and Raydell Latrent Patterson, filed a separate action, Edana Jones, et al. v. 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC, et al., Case No. 23LBCV01405 alleging the same habitability issues.  The complaint in 23LBCV01405 alleges (1) failure to provide habitable dwelling; (2) breach of covenant and right to quiet enjoyment and possession of the property; (3) nuisance; and (4) negligence. 

            On March 19, 2023, the Court ordered consolidation of Edana Jones, et al. v. 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC, et al., Case No. 23LBCV01405 with the instant action, 22STCV40554.  Case No. 22STCV40554 is the lead action.           

Legal Standard

1.  Demurrer Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal 3d 311, 318.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). (Ion Equipment Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.)

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

2.  Motion to Strike Standard

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer (i.e., words, phrases, prayer for damages, etc.).  (CCP §§ 435-437.)  A party may file a motion to strike in whole or in part within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, however, if a party serves and files a motion to strike without demurring to the complaint, the time to answer is extended.  (CCP §§ 435(b)(1), 435(c).)

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  (CCP § 436.)  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleadings or by way of judicial notice.  (CCP § 437.)

            In ruling on a motion to strike, the allegations in the complaint are considered in context and presumed to be true: “[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Sup.Ct. (Pedus Services, Inc. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

Discussion

Defendant Weststar Property Management Inc.’s Demurrer

            Defendant Weststar Property Management Inc. (“Weststar”) demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract, second cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability and sixth cause of action for Civil Code §1942.4.  Plaintiffs did not file an opposition to the demurrer.

            1.  Defendant’s RJN

            Defendant’s RJN of the First and Second Amended Complaints filed by Plaintiffs in this action is granted. 

2.  First and Second Causes of action for Breach of Contract and Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

            Weststar demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract and second cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability, because it is not in privity of contract with Plaintiffs and it is not the landlord of the property.  Weststar is not an alleged party to the Lease Agreement.  (SAC, ¶50.)  Plaintiffs allege breach of a Lease Agreement attached as Exhibit A to the SAC.  (Id.)  Plaintiffs failed to attach Exhibit A to the SAC, but the Lease Agreement was also attached as Exhibit A to the FAC.  Based on Exhibit A to the FAC, the Court finds that Weststar is not a party to the lease agreement. (FAC filed on May 11, 2023, Ex. A.)  The parties to the lease agreement are Plaintiffs and Masuda Investments. (Id.)  Plaintiffs allege that the current and past owners of the premises are Masuda Investments, AJJ202 LLC and 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC. (SAC, ¶¶35-37.) 

Plaintiffs also admit that Weststar is merely the current property manager of the subject property.  (SAC, ¶38.)  An agent is not generally liable on the contract when he or she acts on behalf of a disclosed principal.  (Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 929.)  Since the suit for breach of the implied warranty is essentially a contractual one, Weststar as the current property manager cannot be held liable for breach of the lease agreement or the warranty of habitability implied into the lease agreement. (Id. at 929.)

            Plaintiffs did not oppose the motion.  Plaintiffs therefore fail to establish that the defects raised by Weststar can be cured with leave to amend.  (Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.) 

Weststar’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action for breach of contract and breach of the implied warranty of habitability is sustained without leave to amend. 

3.  Sixth Cause of Action for Violation of Civil Code §1942.4

Civil Code §1942.4(a) states, “[a] landlord of a dwelling may not demand rent, collect rent, issue a notice of a rent increase, or issue a three-day notice to pay rent or quit pursuant to subdivision (2) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure, if all of the following conditions exist prior to the landlord’s demand or notice….”  Civil Code §1942.4(b) provides, “[a] landlord who violates this section is liable to the tenant or lessee for the actual damages sustained by the tenant and lessee and special damages of not less than one hundred dollars and nor more than five thousand dollars…”

Weststar demurs on grounds that it is only the alleged property manager, not the landlord of the property.  Plaintiffs fail to allege that Weststar is the landlord of the property and only allege that Weststar is the property manager.  (SAC, ¶38.)  Based on the express language of Civil Code §1942.4, only a landlord may be held liable thereunder.  Plaintiffs did not file an opposition arguing that this defect is capable of cure. 

Defendant Weststar’s Demurrer to the sixth cause of action for violation of Civil Code §1942.4 is sustained without leave to amend.    

Defendants 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC and Weststar’s Motion to Strike

            1.  Defendants’ RJN

Defendants’ RJN of the First and Second Amended Complaints filed in this action is granted. 

2.  Parties’ Positions

Defendants 5505 Ackerfield Apts LLC (“5505 Ackerfield”) and Weststar move to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations and request for attorney’s fees.  Defendants argue the failure to remedy the alleged conditions at the property was not oppressive, fraudulent or malicious and at most amounts to negligence.  Defendants argue the attorney’s fees request must be stricken as to Weststar in connection with the first and sixth causes of action for breach of contract and violation of Civil Code §1942.4. 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs argue punitive damages are justified in this case. Plaintiffs argue  Defendants had full subjective knowledge of the dangerous conditions and failed to remedy them despite formal complaints to authorities and citations issued by the City.  Plaintiffs argue the allegations are sufficient to satisfy the “despicable conduct” prong of the definition of malice under Civil Code §3294.  Plaintiffs argue they are entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees if they prevail on their breach of contract and CC §1942.4 claims. 

            In reply, Defendants contend Plaintiffs’ allegations are conclusory and do not establish malice.  Defendants argue more than willful or conscious disregard of Plaintiffs’ interests is required.  Defendants do not respond to Plaintiffs’ arguments regarding reasonable attorney’s fees. 

            3.  Defendants’ Motion to Strike Punitive Damages

            Defendants AJJ2020 LLC and Masuda Investments LLC moved to strike punitive damages from the First Amended Complaint, also arguing that malice, fraud or oppression were not alleged based on the exact same allegations of wrongdoing.  Defendants 5505 Ackerfield and Weststar now move to strike punitives from the Second Amended Complaint based on the same argument—the factual allegations do not support a claim for punitives based on malice, fraud or oppression.

            The Court denies Defendants 5505 Ackerfield and Weststar’s Motion to Strike for the same reasons stated in its October 3, 2023 order denying the Motion to Strike of AJJ2020, LLC and Masuda Investments.  Plaintiffs have more than satisfied the pleading threshold to allege punitives based on malice.  “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (CC §3294(c)(1).)  “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.”  (CC §3294(c)(2).)

            Plaintiffs allege a litany of dangerous and hazardous conditions that existed at the property, of which Defendants had full knowledge and for which the property had been cited by the City of Long Beach.  (SAC, ¶¶41-48.)  Despite repeatedly complaining about the conditions and the health issues they were causing Plaintiffs, Defendants did not remedy those conditions.  (Id.)  As landlords, Defendants’ position gave them the power to damage Plaintiff’s interests, as only Defendants could remediate the hazardous condition issues.  (Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 925-926.)  A reasonable trier of fact could find that clear and convincing evidence of these facts qualify as malice under Civil Code §3294. 

            The request to strike the request for attorney’s fees from the first and sixth causes of action as to Weststar is moot in light of the Court’s ruling on Weststar’s demurrer to these causes of action.  Defendants do not challenge Plaintiffs’ right to request attorney’s fees as to 5505 Ackerfield.

            Defendants’ Motion to Strike is therefore denied as to the punitive damages claim and moot as to the attorney’s fees request against Weststar. 

Conclusion and ORDER

            Defendant Weststar’s Demurrer to the first, second and sixth causes of action against it in the Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND. 

            Defendants 5505 Ackerfield and Weststar’s Motion to Strike is DENIED as to Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations and MOOT as to Plaintiffs’ request for attorney’s fees.

            Moving Party to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant rsm properties inc.’s motion for attorney fees pursuant to code of civil procedure § 425.16

 

Procedural Background

On January 23, 2023, Plaintiffs Caroline S. Lee (“Caroline”) and Chung Soonkyo LLC (“CSL”) (jointly, “Plaintiffs”) filed the instant collateral action against Defendants Jong Han Lee (“Jong”), Bow Tie Realty and Investment, Inc. (“Bow Tie”), RSM Properties, Inc. (“RSM”), Michael I. Kim (“Kim”), Unity Escrow, Inc. (“Unity”), Ticor Title Company of California (“Ticor”), and Douglas Chadwick Biggins (“Biggins”) (collectively, “Defendants”).

The Complaint asserts the following six causes of action: (1) Slander of Title against Jong and Bow Tie; (2) Quiet Title against Jong, Bow Tie, RSM, Unity, Ticor, and Biggins; (3) Breach of Contract against RSM, Unity, and Ticor; (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty against Unity; (5) Negligence against Unity and Ticor; and (6) Extortion (Penal Code § 523) against RSM, Kim, Unity, and Ticor.

On August 23, 2023, the Court granted RSM’s special motion to strike as to each cause of action raised against it. (See August 22, 2023 Order.)

On September 6, 2023, RSM filed the instant motion for attorneys’ fees and costs. As of April 25, 2024, no opposition has been filed.   

Legal Standard

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5, subdivision (a)(10)(A), attorney fees when authorized by contract are allowable as costs and may be awarded upon a noticed motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5, subdivision (c)(5). 

In determining what fees are reasonable, California courts apply the “lodestar” approach. (See, e.g., Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.)  This inquiry “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)  From there, the “[t]he lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Ibid.)  Relevant factors include: “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

Discussion

A.    Right to Recover

Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16, subdivision (c)(1), states that “a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover his or her attorney’s fees and costs.” As the prevailing moving party on a special motion to strike, RSM is entitled to recover its attorney fees and costs reasonably incurred in connection with the litigation of their anti-SLAPP motion.

B.    Reasonableness of Attorney’s Fees

RSM initially moved for attorney fees and costs in the amount of $30,682.70, which consisted of $21,750 for preparing its anti-SLAPP motion, $1,372.70 in costs, and $7,560 for the instant fee motion. (Notice of Motion at pg. 2.) Because no opposition has been filed, RSM has reduced the total amount to $29,232.70. (Notice of Motion at pg. 2; Declaration of Counsel re Non-Receipt of Opposition at ¶ 8.)

            The trial court has broad authority to determine the amount of a reasonable fee. (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) The party bears the burden of proof as to “reasonableness” of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5).) The party seeking fees has the burden of documenting the appropriate hours expended and hourly rates. (City of Colton v. Singletary (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 751, 784.) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.)  

            An attorney's testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records or billing statements, and there is no requirement that such records or statements be offered in evidence. (Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, 293.)  Ascertaining the fee amount is left to the trial court’s sound discretion. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)  Moreover, “[t]here is ‘no mathematical rule requiring proportionality between compensatory damages and attorney's fees awards’, [Citation], and courts have awarded attorney's fees where plaintiffs recovered only nominal or minimal damages.”  (Harman v. City and County of San Francisco (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 407, 421.)

            Here, Counsel Dilip Vithlani, the attorney of record for RSM, attests that his hourly rate is $725 per hour and that this rate is below the average charged by attorneys in the area with comparable experience according to the Laffey Matrix for 2022. (Vithlani Decl. ¶ 28.) “The trial court [i]s entitled to rely on [an attorney’s own] declaration[] to determine the reasonable rates for experienced attorneys in Los Angeles County.” (Pasternack v. McCullough (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1050, 1059.) In light of Counsel Vithlani’s years of experience and level of skill, Counsel Vithlani’s hourly rate is within the realm of reasonable hourly rates in the area. (See Vithalni Decl. ¶ 21-27; Nishiki v. Dank Meredith, APC (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 883, 889 [“trial court has its own expertise I the value of legal services performed in a case [Citation] and it may rely on its own familiarity with the local legal market in setting the hourly rate[.]”].)

            Additionally, Counsel Vithlani claims 30 hours of work on the anti-SLAPP motion. (Motion at pg. 8; Vithlani Decl. ¶ 20.) Though this is a significant amount of time spent, the Court acknowledges that anti-SLAPP motions are complex motions that require skill, time, and extensive research in an ever-evolving area of the law. As to the instant motion, Counsel Vithlani states that 8 hours were expended in its preparation. (Vithlani Decl. ¶¶ 29-30; Declaration of Counsel re Non-Receipt of Opposition at ¶ 8.) The Court finds that these amounts are reasonable. It is further noted that no opposition has been submitted to argue the contrary. Lastly, RSM’s claimed costs of $1,372.70 have not been opposed and deemed reasonable.  

            Accordingly, based on the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that $29,232.70 represents the reasonable attorney fees and costs incurred in conjunction with the anti-SLAPP motion and with the filing of the instant motion.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Based on the foregoing, Defendant RSM Properties, Inc.’s motion for attorney fees and costs is GRANTED in the amount of $29,232.70.

Moving party to give notice.
