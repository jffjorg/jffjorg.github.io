DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV23612    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgement

Case Name: Turuyan Cunguslu, et al. v. Joshua Marlon Torres, et al.
Moving Party: Defendants Combined Properties, Inc.; Seventy-Fifth LLC
Responding Party: Defendant Ralphs Grocery Company (“Ralphs”)
Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND 

This case arises from a June 24, 2018 altercation between plaintiff Turuyan Cunguslu and defendant Joshua Torres at a Ralphs location. (UMF NO. 7). After a verbal argument with Mr. Torres inside the store, Mr. Cunguslu approached the exit door and started to walk outside when Mr. Torres attacked him. The altercation ended when Mr. Cunguslu stumbled toward the parking lot in pursuit of Mr. Torres and fell to the floor. (UMF NO. 30)

In 1997, Ralphs and Defendants entered into a written lease agreement (“Agreement”). (UMF NO. 1). 

Section 14.1(a) the Agreement states: 

Tenant shall and does hereby agree to indemnify, hold harmless, defend and protect Landlord from all claims, actions, demands, costs, expenses, loss and liability whatsoever (including reasonable attorneys fees and costs), on account of any such real or claimed damage or liability and from all liens, claims and demands arising out of the construction, use, occupancy or enjoyment of the Market and its facilities, or any repairs, alterations, maintenance, rebuilding or restoration which Tenant may make upon the Market, or occasioned in whole or In part by any act or omission of Tenant, its agents, contractors, servants, employees or invitees. (UMF NO. 5).

Section 14.2(a) of the Agreement provides: 

Landlord shall and does hereby agree to Indemnify, hold harmless, defend and protect Tenant from all claims, actions, demands, costs, expenses, loss and liability whatsoever (including reasonable attorneys fees and costs), on account of any such real or claimed damage or liability and from all liens, claims and demands arising out of the construction, use, occupancy or enjoyment of the Common Area and its facilities, or any repairs, alterations, maintenance, rebuilding or restoration which Landlord may make upon the Common Area, or occasioned In whole or in part by any act or omission of Landlord, its agents, contractors, servants, employees or invitees. Landlord shall not be liable for damage or injury occasioned by the negligence or willful acts of Tenant or Its designated agents, servants or employees. (emphasis added) (UMF NO. 6)

Pursuant to the Agreement in effect at the time, Section 11.1 states that “[t]he Common Area shall be under the management, supervision and control of the Landlord.” Section 11.5 states that the “Landlord shall operate and maintain Common Area” and provide “(h) Reasonable security in the Common Area.” Section 11.1 provides that premises Common Area includes the exterior parking lot and driveway, stating: “(ii) Parking of motor vehicles, and pedestrian and vehicular ingress and egress by Tenant and other occupants of the Shopping Center and agents, employees, customers, and other invitees of any of them, to and from buildings, Common Area and adjacent public street; “(iii) Parking stalls, private streets, sidewalks, walls, ramps, driveways, lanes, curbs, gutters, traffic control areas, signals, traffic islands, landscaped areas, traffic and parking lighting facilities and Pylon Sign with appropriate underground electrical connection and all things incidental thereto;..”

The Agreement defines the following terms as follows:

• “Market” is defined in Section 2.35 as the “building containing a Floor area of the approximate size set forth in the Lease Schedule to be constructed in the Building Area designated "Market" on the Site Plan, including its areas for truck parking and unloading and trash storage, and any supports and appurtenances that extend from the Market into the Common Area.” (UMF NO. 2). 
• “Common Area” is defined in Section 2.8 as “all of the Shopping Center and its improvements, including sidewalks adjacent to the front of a building, but not including Building Area, areas immediately adjacent to a building for truck parking, unloading or trash storage and permanent merchandising (other than sidewalks), and supports and appurtenances that extend from a building.” (UMF NO. 3). 
• "Primary Common Area" is defined in Section 2.45 as “the portion of the Common Area shown as crosshatched on the Site Plan,” which clearly delineates the areas controlled by each party and establishes the subject incident occurred on property controlled by Ralphs. (UMF NO. 4).

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendants seek summary judgment against Ralphs on the Seventh Cause of Action for Express Indemnity of Ralphs’ Cross-complaint filed February 8, 2021. Defendants argue that, under the terms of the Agreement, they do not owe express indemnity to Ralphs because the altercation did not arise out of the use of the Common Areas of the property. (Motion for Summary Judgement, p. 14.)

OPPOSITION

Ralphs argues the Court should deny Defendants’ motion for summary judgement because the evidence establishes that the altercation took place not only on Ralphs’ premises, but also in the Common Area parking lot driveway. (Opposition, p. 5.) Ralphs asserts that whether the fight “arose” from the “use” of the common areas is an issue of material fact for the jury to determine. (Id. at 14.)

REPLY

Defendants argue that whether a physical altercation occurred inside Ralphs is irrelevant — but rather that the evidence that the underlying incident arose from events that occurred inside Ralphs is dispositive and warrants summary judgement under the terms of the Agreement. (Reply, p. 2.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Summary Judgement

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.  Thus, summary judgment is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(c); Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.)¿

Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility.” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)¿ 

Express indemnity refers to an obligation arising “‘by virtue of express contractual language establishing a duty in one party to save another harmless upon the occurrence of specified circumstances.’” (Bay Dev., Ltd. v. Superior Ct., (1990) 50 Cal. 3d 1012, 1029.) Express indemnity generally is not subject to equitable considerations or a joint legal obligation to the injured party; rather, it is enforced in accordance with the terms of the contracting parties' agreement. (Markley v. Beagle (1967) 66 Cal.2d 951, 961.) The obligation to indemnify and hold harmless arises as soon as the indemnitee tenders the defense of a claim that "allege[s] facts that would give rise to a duty of indemnity." (Crawford v. Weather Shield Mfg., Inc. (2008) 44 Cal.4th 541, 558). An express indemnity agreement is subject to the same rules governing the interpretation of any other contract. (Rossmoor Sanitation, Inc. v. Pylon, Inc. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 622, 633; Continental Heller Corp. v. Amtech Mechanical Services, Inc. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 500, 504.) 

A. Defendants’ Initial Burden 

Defendants have not met their initial burden of proof to provide admissible evidence showing Ralphs’ express indemnity cause of action has no merit. In this case, Defendants’ evidence does not give rise to a single indisputable reasonable inference of where and when the altercation arose. Namely, Defendants rely on evidence of the sequence of events leading up to the altercation to assert that they owe no express indemnity to Ralphs because the physical altercation arose from the use of the market rather than the Common Area. (Motion for Summary Judgement, p. 16.) Concluding the altercation did not arise in a Common Area requires assuming  the argument inside the store definitively marks the singular moment the altercation “arose.” However, despite Defendants’ characterization, when and where the altercation “arose” is ambiguous. 

While Defendants rely on evidence of a verbal conflict between Mr. Torres and Mr. Cunguslu in the store, the evidence here also reflects a distinct gap between the argument and the physical altercation. In the intervening time, Mr. Torres left the store but presumably remained in the parking lot.  In the meantime, Mr. Cunguslu returned to his original shopping task by completing the check-out process. The physical altercation then began in earnest when Mr. Cunguslu exited the store when Mr. Torres returned and knocked a bouquet of flowers from Mr. Cunguslu’s hand and run away.  Mr. Cunguslu then appears to chase Mr. Torres and he subsequently falls.  Based on this evidence, a jury may interpret Mr. Torres and Mr. Cunguslu’s initial verbal argument constituted a separate altercation altogether that conclusively resolved in the store, and that a separate physical altercation arose again outside in a Common Area. The evidence is thus deeply ambiguous regarding when and where an altercation “arose” — and whether more than one altercation occurred. While Defendants certainly provide one plausible interpretation of events, their proposed characterization is merely one of several potential interpretations of the evidence.

This ambiguity is fatal to Defendants’ motion. The Court may only decide the issues as a matter of law where inferences are indisputable, otherwise the factual issues must be resolved by trial.¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Here, the Court cannot conclude that there is only one reasonable inference regarding when or where an altercation arose from the evidence provided. These factual issues are determinative of the express indemnity cause of action, and must be resolved by trial.¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Accordingly, the Court finds Defendants have not made a prima facie showing that the altercation did not arise in a Common Area, and thus do not meet their initial burden required for summary judgement. 

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion for summary judgement is DENIED.

Case Number: 21STCV00473    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment

Case Name: Mohamed Akari v. Harry Kram, M.D., et al.
Moving Party: Harry Kram
Responding Party: Mohamed Akari
Tentative Ruling: DENY

ANALYSIS

Background 

On May 26, 2021, Plaintiff served Defendant by serving Defendant’s office assistant, Alejandra Barajas, in addition to mailing a copy to Defendant. (Willoughby Decl. ¶ 13-14; Exh. F.) Plaintiff also sent an additional to Defendant in his capacity as the agent for service of process on behalf of Defendant Legsavers. (Willoughby Decl. ¶ 15-16; Exh. G.) Plaintiff obtained a default judgment in the amount of $485,397.89 against moving Defendant on May 13, 2024. Defendant allegedly learned about this around September 15, 2024, after his real estate agent informed him that a lien had been placed on his residential property. Plaintiff filed his initial Request for Entry of Default on July 23, 2021 and filed his Request for Judgment on February 28, 2024. Judgment was formally entered on May 13, 2024. Defendant alleges he never received and was never given any court documents served on his office and was unaware of the action and subsequent filings.

Moving Party Position

Defendant requests the Court set aside the default and default judgment, arguing default judgment was the result of surprise, excusable inadvertence, excusable neglect and excusable mistake, and is otherwise void as a matter of law. (Motion to Vacate, p. 5-6.)
 
Opposition

Plaintiff argues the Court should deny Defendant’s motion, arguing that default judgment did not arise from mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that Defendant’s claimed ignorance of proceedings do not implicate the mandatory attorney fault provision of section 473(b). Plaintiff moreover argues Defendant was aware of the instant litigation. (Opposition, p. 10.)

Reply

Defendant argues that he has successfully satisfied his burden of establishing surprise, particularly because Defendant had medical liability insurance, and thus had no reason to ignore service of the complaint and subject himself to personal responsibility. (Reply, p. 2.)

Discussion

I. Judicial Notice

Overruled: Nos. 1-2, 5.
 
III. Motion to Vacate Default Judgment

CCP § 473(b) reads as follows: 

The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken…Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.

A “surprise” under Code Civ. Proc. § 473 is defined as “some condition or situation in which a party to a cause is unexpectedly placed to his injury, without any default or negligence of his own, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against.” (Baratti v. Baratti (1952) 109 Cal.App.2d 917, 921.) 
 
CCP § 473.5 states as follows:¿ 

(a) When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i)¿two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii)¿180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. 
(b) A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party’s lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action. 
 
“Mistake is not a ground for relief under section 473, subdivision (b), when ‘the court finds that the “mistake” is simply the result of professional incompetence, general ignorance of the law, or unjustifiable negligence in discovering the law ....’ (8 Witkin, Cal. Procedure (5th ed. 2008) Attack on Judgment in Trial Court, § 155, p. 749.)” (Hearn v. Howard (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1206.) Moreover, for inadvertence or neglect, relief under Section 473 requires that a litigant's neglect must have been “such as might have been the act of a reasonably prudent person under the same circumstances.” (Hearn v. Howard, supra, 177 Cal.App.4th at p. 1206; accord, Henderson v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 215, 229–230.) Wholly inexcusable inadvertence does not justify relief. “Unless in arranging for his defense he shows that he has exercised such reasonable diligence as a man of ordinary prudence usually bestows upon important business his motion for relief under section 473 will be denied. Courts neither act as guardians for incompetent parties nor for those who are grossly careless of their own affairs.... The only occasion for the application of section 473 is where a party is unexpectedly placed in a situation to his injury without fault or negligence of his own and against which ordinary prudence could not have guarded.” (Id.)

Though public policy broadly supports setting aside default judgements in favor of hearing issues on the merits, the Court declines to do so here. Defendant’s motion relies on the contention that he lacked notice, specifically stating Defendant was “never made aware of the filing of the lawsuit or the service of these documents by Ms. Barajas or anyone in his office.” (Motion to Vacate, p. 6.) Defendant here attempts to substantiate his claim that he lacked notice of the action solely by referring to — and claiming not to have received — the initial complaint and summons. By doing so, Defendant glaringly omits reference to or acknowledgement of the many other forms of notice served on Defendant throughout the lifespan of this case. 

It is worthwhile to summarize, in part, the documents and communications directed at Defendant about this case — any of which would have provided Defendant with notice. On June 25, 2021, Plaintiff served written discovery on Defendant by mail. (Willoughby Decl. ¶ 19-20; Exh. I.) On July 23, 2021, Plaintiff sent Defendant a copy of the request for entry of default via mail. (Willoughby Decl. ¶ 21-22; Exh. J.) Plaintiff again sought an entry of default on August 9, 2021, which was mailed to Defendant. (Willoughby Decl. ¶¶ 25-26; Exh. L.) Following this Court’s entry of judgment on June 17, 2024, Notice of Entry of Judgment was again mailed to Defendant. (Willoughby Decl. ¶ 30-31; Exh. N.) Plaintiff also undertook additional efforts to notify Defendant of this action, including sending three separate letters to Defendant specifically to notify him of this litigation, including to Defendant’s personal email address provided to Plaintiff by an individual at Defendant’s office (Poblet Decl. ¶ 14-18; Exh. C & D), Defendant’s office email address (Poblet Decl. ¶ 14-18; Exh. C & D; Willoughby Decl. ¶ 6-7; Exh. B), and via facsimile. (Poblet Decl. ¶ 14-18; Exh. C & D; Willoughby Decl. ¶ 6-7; Exh. B.) Defendant’s alleged non-receipt of the original complaint and summons accordingly comprise a mere fraction of notices sent to Defendant regarding this action. 

Defendant fails to reference or address any of these notices in asserting that he lacked any awareness of this case. Notably, Defendant also has not provided a declaration from his office manager regarding whether she was served and, if so, what became of the service package; doing so would have provided immediate and compelling evidence to clarify Defendant’s potential excusable neglect. However, such immediate evidence is conspicuously absent here. Defendant thus urges the Court to find that it was reasonable to either discard or ignore repeated mailings to Defendant’s business address.  This the Court will not do.  The Court thus finds Defendant did not exercise “such reasonable diligence as a man of ordinary prudence usually bestows upon important business” and has failed to provide an adequate for “surprise” under Section § 473. 

The Court additionally finds Defendant was not reasonably diligent in bringing this motion.  To be reasonably diligent under Section 473, Defendant must provide a satisfactory excuse for the default and diligence in making the motion after discovering the default. (Stafford v. Mach (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1174.) In this case, Defendant fails to explain how or why this motion was not brought earlier given the repeated number of contacts relating to this case. Defendant’s motion exclusively refers to and attempts to account for the service of original summons and complaint, but does not account for or provide any excuse with respect to the many additional contacts that must have placed Defendant on notice of this action and the intent to enter a default judgment. Defendant has not offered any excuse, much less a satisfactory excuse, as to why repeated mailings in September 2020, May 2021, June 2021, July 2021 and August 2021 went unopened and unanswered.  The overwhelming weight of the evidence compels just one conclusion - that Defendant was aware of this matter and simply ignored it.  As a result, the Court finds Defendant was not reasonably diligent, providing additional support to deny the motion to set aside the default judgement.

RULING

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside Default Judgement is DENIED. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
