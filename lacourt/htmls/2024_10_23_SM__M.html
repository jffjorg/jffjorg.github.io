DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV03967    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           1239 23rd St. Homeowners Association, v. Kurtzer

CASE NO.:                23SMCV03967

MOTION:                  Motion for Order (Mootness, Prevailing Party, Att. Fees)

HEARING DATE:   10/23/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

With respect to attorney fees and costs, unless they are specifically provided for by statute (e.g., CCP §§ 1032, et seq.), the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties.¿(CCP § 1021.) The prevailing party on a contract, which specifically provides for attorney fees and costs incurred to enforce the agreement, is entitled to reasonable attorney fees in addition to other costs.¿(Civ. Code § 1717(a); CCP §§ 1032, 1033.5(a)(10)(A).)¿The court, upon notice and motion by a party, shall determine the prevailing party and shall fix, as an element of the costs of suit, the reasonable attorney fees.¿(Civ. Code § 1717(a), (b).)¿Any notice of motion to claim attorney fees as an element of costs under shall be served and filed before or at the same time the memorandum of costs is served and filed; if only attorney fees are claimed as costs, the notice of motion shall be served and filed within the time specified in CRC 3.1700 for filing a memorandum of costs.¿(CRC 3.1702; Gunlock Corp. v. Walk on Water, Inc. (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1301, 1303, fn. 1.)

 

“It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion. [Citation.]” (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623 624.) The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily “begins with the ‘lodestar’ [method], i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 154.) “[A] computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award.” (Margolin v. Reg’l Planning Comm’n (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004.) The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided. (See Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49 [discussing factors relevant to proper attorneys’ fees award].) Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary. (Id. at 48, fn. 23.) The factors considered in determining the modification of the lodestar include “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Mountjoy v. Bank of Am. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 266, 271.)

 

In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence.¿(Premier Medical Management Systems, Inc. v. California Ins. Guaranty Assoc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.)¿General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice. (Ibid.) 

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff 1239 23rd Street Homeowners Association and Cross Defendants 1239 23'd Street Homeowners Association and Camila Arnold (collectively, the "HOA") move for an order:

 

1. Determining that this action is moot because the relief demanded by the Complaint has been obtained through this Court's preliminary injunction and the performance of the termite eradication at the subject property;

2. Determining that plaintiff and cross-defendants be deemed the prevailing party in that plaintiff obtained the relief demanded in the Complaint; and

3. Awarding attorneys fees and costs to plaintiff and cross defendants as the prevailing

parties in the action in the amount of $13,376.23.

 

Civil Code section 5975 provides in pertinent part: “(c) In an action to enforce the governing documents, the prevailing party shall be awarded reasonable attorney's fees and costs.” A prevailing party means a party which “prevailed on a practical level by achieving its main litigation objectives.” (Almanor Lakeside Villas Owners Assn. v. Carson (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774.) A court must “compare the relief awarded on the… claims with the parties’ demands on those same claims and their litigation objectives as disclosed by the pleadings, trial briefs, opening statements, and similar sources.” (Hsu v. Abbarra (1995) 9 Cal.4th 863, 876.)  

 

The HOA sought to enforce its governing documents against Kurtzer in order to perform necessary termite eradication at the Property. (Compl., ¶¶ 5, 11-12.) Despite her obligations under the governing documents, Kurtzer refused to allow any home inspector in her unit or to allow plaintiff the ability to enter her unit or take any other reasonably necessary actions to complete the remediation. (Compl., ¶¶ 7-8, 12.) The complaint prayed for relief under Civil Code sections 4780 and 4785, which provide that an association is responsible for repair and maintenance of the condo project occasioned by the presence of termites, and that the court may cause the "summary removal" of any occupant as may be necessary for prompt, effective treatment of wood-destroying pests or organisms. Further, Plaintiff requested the temporary, summary removal of Kurtzer from her unit for the purpose of "prompt, effective treatment of wood-destroying pests or organisms." (Compl., ¶ 13.)

 

Kurtzer, on the other hand, filed a cross-complaint (CC) against the HOA and Arnold for failing to first make attempts to resolve this dispute in alternative dispute resolution. (CC ¶¶ 9-14.) Further, the CC requested an injunction to prevent certain proposed “heat treatment” for eradication. (¶¶ 16-19) The CC also requested an injunction to move HOA board meetings from Wednesday evenings so that Kurtzer may attend. (¶ 33.) The CC has been voluntarily dismissed.

 

The parties litigated and resolved the central termite eradication issue. On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff applied ex parte pursuant to Civil Code section 4785 for an order compelling Kurtzer to allow the termite eradication. On January 11, 2024, plaintiff filed another ex parte application to enforce the Court's prior Order. The Court found that Kurtzer was willfully undermining the Court's prior order by intimidating and threatening the exterminators during the pre-treatment inspection. The Court restrained Kurtzer from interfering with the termite treatment. On February 7, 2024, Kurtzer filed an ex parte application for an order prohibiting Plaintiff from proceeding with any heat treatment in her condo unit. The Court denied the application. Finally, on February 9, 2024, the termite eradication was completed, albeit without the heat treatment in Kurtzer’s unit. (Arnold Decl., ¶¶10-12.) Instead, a less efficient method was chosen, which caused the exterminator to decline to provide a standard 5-year warranty against termites and only offer a 1-year warranty. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff claims to have prevailed because it obtained injunctive orders that secured the main relief it sought – termite eradication. Notably, Defendant had to be repeatedly restrained from interfering with the eradication team and the eradication eventually went forward. Strictly speaking, this was what the complaint requested. Further, Defendant dismissed her own cross-complaint in response to an anti-SLAPP motion. These facts suggest Plaintiff prevailed.

 

Defendant argues that, practically speaking, this dispute did not concern termite eradication itself but the method of termite eradication. Notably, Arnold explains the three other members of the HOA wanted heat treatment for the extermination, while Kurtzer wanted fumigation only. (Arnold Decl., ¶ 5.) The parties ultimately did not have all the units treated at once with the heat treatment, and instead had to separately fumigate Defendant’s unit. (Id., ¶12.) Defendant reasons that in a practical sense Plaintiff did not achieve its litigation goal against Defendant of obtaining heat treatment for Unit 4.  

 

Plaintiff’s primary litigation goal was not to secure heat treatment for Defendant’s unit, but to secure any termite extermination for Defendant’s unit. Notably, the complaint does not request any specific method of extermination. The injunction likewise did not seek any particular method. Defendant did not oppose any of the injunctions on the grounds that only heat treatment should be considered. Defendant argues that this was her own litigation objective. Indeed, Defendant’s cross-complaint and February 7, 2024, ex parte application requested such relief. However, Defendant’s litigation goal of preventing heat treatment does not mean Plaintiff’s litigation goal was to secure heat treatment. The record only reveals Plaintiff’s more general goal, to secure termite eradication.

 

Since Plaintiff’s primary litigation goal was to secure termite eradication for Defendant’s unit, and Defendant’s unit underwent termite eradication, Plaintiff has achieved its litigation goals on a practical level. Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to attorneys’ fees and costs. Plaintiff demonstrates that it incurred fees of $13,376.23 and costs of $703.90, aside from previously awarded fees. Plaintiff provides verified billing records in support of the fee. (Shapero Decl., ¶¶ 6-8, Ex. C.) Using the lodestar method, and examining the totality of the record, Plaintiff’s fee request is reasonable.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED. Plaintiff is determined to be the prevailing party for recovering non-monetary relief. Plaintiff is awarded fees and costs in the noticed total amount of $14,080.13.

 

The matter is deemed completed by the Court.

CASE NAME:             Darrell, et al., v. Presus LLC, et al. 

CASE NO.:                   24SMCV00485

MOTION:                     Demurrer and Motion to Strike the First Amended Complaint

HEARING DATE:   10/23/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (CCP §§ 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732, internal citations omitted.) 

 

A special demurrer for uncertainty is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him/her. (CCP § 430.10(f); Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Moreover, even if the pleading is somewhat vague, “ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Ibid.)  

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP §§ 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].) 

 

“Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.) It is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where there is any reasonable possibility that plaintiff can state a good cause of action. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on plaintiff to show¿in what manner¿plaintiff can amend the complaint, and¿how¿that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading.¿(Id.)

ANALYSIS 

 

Defendant the County of Los Angeles demurs to the third cause of action and moves to strike the request for punitive damages.  The County argues that the sole cause of action against it for negligence is based on dangerous conditions of public property. The County observes that judicially noticeable documents show the subject location was within the city limits of West Hollywood. The County contends that it does not own the subject sidewalk.

Government Code section 835 provides that “a public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and that either: (a) [a] negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition; or (b) [t]he public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Section 835.2 a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition.” (Govt. Code, § 835.) Section 830 provides that “‘dangerous condition’ means a condition of property that creates a substantial . . . risk of injury when such property or adjacent property is used with due care in a manner in which it is reasonably foreseeable that it will be used.” (Id., § 830(a).) “[A] claim alleging a dangerous condition may not rely on generalized allegations [citation] but must specify in what manner the condition constituted a dangerous condition.” (Brenner v. City of El Cajon (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 434, 439.)  

The FAC alleges that Plaintiffs dined together at Craig’s Restaurant located at 8826 Melrose Ave., West Hollywood, CA 90069. (FAC ¶¶ 15-16.) At about 11:30 p.m., Plaintiffs left the restaurant and waited in the valet area. (Id.) Three male assailants were waiting near the valet area. (¶¶ 16-17.) Two assailants attacked Plaintiffs, stole a necklace and Rolex watch, and held guns to Plaintiffs. (Id.) The restaurant security guard was distracted by other patrons and made no effort to intervene. (¶18.) Around that time, there had been a number of violent armed robberies in West Hollywood within a few miles of Craig’s restaurant. (¶ 19.)

The FAC alleges that the County owned and/or controlled the property where the subject incident occurred and/or were responsible for providing law enforcement for this area. (FAC ¶ 48.) The subject property was in a dangerous condition as there was a) no adequate lighting, b) no publicly maintained security cameras, and c) no police or security patrol or presence during the incident. (¶ 49.) These conditions created a reasonably foreseeable risk of harm of a mugging, such as the subject incident. (¶50.)

The FAC and the judicially noticeable facts do not demonstrate that the County did not, in fact, own operate or control the subject property. Strictly speaking, the fact that Craig’s Restaurant was within West Hollywood (a city within the County) does not imply that the County did not own, operate or control the subject premises. While the Court concurs that the County would not be apparently liable for maintenance of the public streets, the FAC does not allege an issue with the public streets. Likewise, the County had no duty to provide police at that location (Gov. Code § 845; Summerfield v. City of Inglewood (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 983, 998), but the FAC alleges dangerous conditions aside from inadequate police protection. The FAC alleges that the dangerous conditions included inadequate lighting and security measures such as security cameras. In light of the pled ultimate facts, whether or not the County, in fact, owned, operated or controlled the subject premises must be resolved outside the pleading stage.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

The unopposed motion to strike punitive damages is GRANTED. Punitive damages cannot be recovered, as a matter of law, against a public entity. (Gov. Code §§ 818, 825(a).)
