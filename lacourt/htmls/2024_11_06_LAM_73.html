DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV04515    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: 73

11/06/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

CASTRO v. FCA US LLC, et al. (23STCV04515)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Alexander Khoubian (Alpha Law Firm, PC); Sam Azimtash (Azimtash Law Firm PC)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Erin Hanson (Clark Hill LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT GLENN E. THOMAS INC.’S OBJECTION-FREE RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE (filed 08/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc.’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc. is ordered to provide verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, within 20 days of this order.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 1, 2023, Plaintiff Alfredo Marquez Castro filed the instant action against Defendants FC US LLC and Glenn E. Thomas Company, Inc. alleging the following causes of action:

1.     Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code section 1793.2

2.     Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 1793.2

3.     Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code section 1793.2

4.     Breach of Express Warranty

5.     Breach of Implied Warranty

6.     Negligent Repair

Plaintiffs allege this action arises out of the warranty obligations of Defendant in connection with a vehicle purchased by Plaintiffs and for which Defendant issued a written warranty.

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc’s objection free responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One. No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

A responding party has 30 days after service of interrogatories to serve their responses on the propounding party. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) However, if interrogatories are served by electronic service, a responding party has an additional two (2) court days to respond. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(B).)  

 

If the responding party fails to timely respond to the interrogatories, “The party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010).” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)  

  

Furthermore, the party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories. (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).) And “Unlike a motion to compel further responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45-day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)   

 

B.     Discussion

On October 2, 2023, Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories (Set One) on Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc. (Azimtash Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.) The responses were due on or about November 3, 2023. Plaintiff gave Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc. an extension to provide responses by February 2, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 4, Ex. B.) As of the date of this motion, no responses have been served. (Id. at ¶ 8.) No opposition to Plaintiff’s motion has been filed. As such, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion.

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc.’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Glenn E. Thomas Inc. is ordered to provide verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, within 20 days of this order.

CARTER v. NEIGHBORHOOD HOUSING SERVICES OF LOS ANGELES (23STCV16156)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Orlando Cabanday (Cabanday Law Group)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Nicole Sheth (Kaufman Borgeest & Ryan LLP)

 

(1)   DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES – GENERAL, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 05/06/24)

(2)   DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO requests for admission, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 05/06/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

(1)   Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One is GRANTED. (2)   Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Requests for Admission, Set One is GRANTED. I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff Moieshai Carter (“Plaintiff”) filed this employment action against Defendant Neighborhood Housing Services of Los Angeles County, a California Corporation, doing business as NHS LA County (“NHS” or “Defendant”).

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

 

C/A 1: Disability Discrimination

C/A 2: Racial Discrimination

C/A 5: Wrongful Termination in violation of Public Policy

C/A 12: Failure to Furnish Accurate Wage Statements

C/A 13: Waiting Time Penalties

 

The Complaint alleges that in October 2012, NHS hired Plaintiff as a lending specialist. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff worked under the management of its Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, JP Veen. (Ibid.) Plaintiff continuously worked for NHS until November 2022 when she was wrongfully terminated while on disability. (Compl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff alleges multiple violations of California’s Labor Code and FEHA by NHS.

 

On May 6, 2024, Defendant NHS filed the Motions to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One, and Request for Admissions, Set One. No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

 

Here, Defendant’s counsel declares that a meet and confer letter was sent to Plaintiff’s counsel on April 29, 2023 explaining why the discovery responses to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One, and Request for Admissions, Set One were deficient and requesting supplemental responses. (Whang Decl., ¶ 7.) Defendant did not receive a response. (Id., ¶ 8.) The Court finds that counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.     Form Interrogatories, General, Set One

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One.

 

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) 

 

It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

 

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

Furthermore, to the extent there is any doubt in whether these records should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff responded to every Form Interrogatory – General request with the same objection:

 

“Plaintiff objects to this request to the extent that it seeks confidential, trade secret, proprietary, financial, and/or commercially sensitive information that is privileged and/or confidential and protected from disclosure by applicable law. Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground that the information sought is in the possession of, known to, or otherwise equally available to the propounding party. Plaintiff objects to this request on the grounds that it seeks information that is neither relevant to the subject matter of this action nor reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground that it invades the right of privacy guaranteed to whom it seeks information. Plaintiff objects to this request to the extent it seeks information protected from disclosure by the attorney work-product rule. Plaintiff objects on the grounds that the Interrogatory is vague and ambiguous.”

 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s boilerplate objections are too general and unmeritorious because they do not state how or why Plaintiff believes Defendant’s Interrogatories are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence or in what way they are confidential, vague or ambiguous. The Court agrees, and notes that Plaintiff has failed to oppose the instant motion. Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One. 

 

C.     Requests for Admissions, Set One

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Request for Admissions, Set One.

 

A motion to compel further responses may be brought based on responses to requests for admission (“RFA”) that: (1) provide evasive or incomplete answers; or (2) make unmeritorious or overly-generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)  However, a motion to compel further responses cannot compel the admission of matters already denied.  (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 821.) 

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

Here, Plaintiff responded to every Request for Admission with the same objection:

 

“Plaintiff objects to the entirety of this request on the grounds that it is unduly burdensome and oppressive because it is calculated to annoy and harass. Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground that it calls for an impermissible expert opinion. Plaintiff objects to this request to the extent that it seeks confidential, trade secret, proprietary, financial, and/or commercially sensitive information that is privileged and/or confidential and protected from disclosure by applicable law. Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground that the information sought is in the possession of, known to, or otherwise equally available to the propounding party. Plaintiff objects to this request on the grounds that it seeks information that is neither relevant to the subject matter of this action nor reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground that it invades the right of privacy guaranteed to whom it seeks information. Plaintiff objects to this request to the extent it seeks information protected from disclosure by the attorney work-product rule. Plaintiff objects on the grounds that the Request is vague and ambiguous.”

 

As discussed above, the Court finds Plaintiff’s boilerplate objections are too general and unmeritorious because they do not state how or why Plaintiff believes Defendant’s requests are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence or in what way they are confidential, vague or ambiguous. Plaintiff has not opposed Defendant’s motion. Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Requests for Admission, Set One. 

 

D.     Sanctions

 

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.) 

 

Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, subd. (a). ) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, subd. (d).) Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 provides that a court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery even if no opposition was filed.  

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to make good faith efforts to respond to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories – General and Requests for Admission. Defendant requests $2,650 in sanctions for counsel’s work on each motion, including 5 hours spent on each motion and an anticipated 5 additional hours to review the opposition, prepare a reply and appear at the hearing. The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no opposition was filed to either motion, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced total amount of $1,590 for each motion (6 hours of attorney time at the billing rate of $265 an hour.)

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff shall provide verified responses within 30 days. The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $1,590 in connection with Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Requests for Admission, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff shall provide verified responses within 30 days. The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $1,590 in connection with Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Requests for Admission, Set One. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order.

LIANOVA v. ZARFATI, et al. (24STCV17995)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Natalia Lianova (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: David Bartelstone (Cunningham, Treadwell & Bartelstone)

 

(1)   Demurrer to complaint (filed by Defendant Red Dragon Investment on 10/08/2024)

(2)   MOTION FOR ORDER EXPUNGING NOTICE OF PENDENCY OF ACTION OF RED DRAGON INVESTMENT (filed by Defendant Red Dragon Investment on 10/08/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant RDI’s Demurrer as to the Complaint’s First, Third, Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendant RDI’s Motion for an Order Expunging Notice of Pendency of Action is GRANTED. I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff Natalia Lianova filed a Complaint against Defendants Guy Zarfati (“Zarfati”), LA Development Properties LLC (“LADP”), Red Dragon Investment (“RDI”), Lion Servicing (“Lion”), GSA Management Limited Liability Company, Orange Coast Title Company, Lawyers Title Company, Shery Katz, Does 1-10 and all persons known or unknown claiming any legal or equitable right, title, estate, lien, or interest in the properties described in this complaint and adverse to the Plaintiff’s title or any cloud on plaintiff’s title thereto.

 

The Complaint alleges causes of action for:

1.     Quiet Title (Misrepresentation)

2.     Fraudulent Deceit

3.     Cancellation of Instrument (Quitclaim Deed)

4.     Cancellation of Instrument (Deed of Trust)

5.     Cancellation of Instrument (Deed of Trust)

6.     Declaratory Relief

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff is, and at all times mentioned herein is and was, a resident of certain real property commonly known as 3564 Poppy Drive, Calabasas, CA 91302 (the “Subject Property”). Plaintiff obtained title to the Subject Property on or about November 3, 2011, via that certain Grant Deed that recorded on November 9, 2011 as Instrument Number 20111519207 in the Official Records of the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office (the “Lianova Grant Deed”). Plaintiff’s spouse, Andrey Lizikhin (“Andrey”), transferred any interest he may have held in the Subject Property on or about November 4, 2011 to Plaintiff via that certain Interspousal Grant Deed that recorded on November 9, 2011 as Instrument Number 20111519208 in the Official Records of the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office (the “Interspousal Deed”). On or about March 14, 2023, Defendant Zarfati, who was in a romantic relationship with Plaintiff at that time, advised Plaintiff that she needed to transfer the Subject Property into a Limited Liability Company in order to avoid any Judgments against the Subject Property due to the actions of Plaintiff’s spouse, Andrey. Zarfati further advised Plaintiff that she would be identified as and would hold the position of managing member of the Limited Liability Company. On or about March 14, 2023, Plaintiff, under the above-referenced representations from Zarfati, executed that certain Quitclaim Deed that recorded on March 22, 2023 as Instrument Number 20230182541 in the Official Records of the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office (the “Offending Deed”), which Offending Deed transferred Plaintiff’s interest in the Subject Property to Defendant LADP. Plaintiff alleges that Zarfati never did identify Plaintiff as the managing member of LADP. Plaintiff is informed and believes, and based thereon alleges, that on or about March 14, 2023, Defendant LADP, via its manager, Defendant Zarfati, executed that certain Deed of Trust that recorded against the Subject Property on March 22, 2023 as Instrument Number 20230182542 in the Official Records of the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office (the “RDI DOT”). The RDI DOT identifies Defendant RDI as Lender, Defendant LADP as Borrower, and Defendant Lion as Trustee.

 

On October 8, 2024, Defendant RDI filed a Demurrer to the Complaint, arguing:

·       RDI demurs specifically as to the First, Third, Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action, the only causes of action in which RDI has been named.

·       The claims against RDI fail because RDI is a bona fide encumbrancer of the real property which is the subject of this action and holds its interest free and clear of Plaintiff’s off-record, unknown claims of fraudulent misrepresentation against Zarfati.

·       Further, as to the third cause of action, RDI is not a party to the instrument (quitclaim deed) which Plaintiff is attempting to cancel by way of this cause of action and there are no charging allegations against RDI in this cause of action.

 No opposition was filed.

On October 8, 2024, Defendant RDI filed a Motion to Expunge Notice of Pendency of Action, arguing:

·       While Plaintiff’s Complaint may allege a real property claim, she will be unable to demonstrate the probable validity of that real property claim as against RDI, because RDI is a bona fide encumbrancer, as a matter of law, having parted with its loan and acquired its secured interest in the subject real property of the Verified Complaint for valuable consideration and without notice of Plaintiff’s claims of fraudulent misrepresentation alleged against Defendant, Guy ZaffatL

·       This is a fraud in the inducement case and while Plaintiff may have viable claims against Zarfati based on his alleged fraudulent misrepresentations, her quiet title and cancellation of instruments claims against RDI will undoubtedly fail, because RDI is a bona fide encumbrancer, who took its secured lien interest free and clear of Plaintiff's unrecorded and unknown claims of fraudulent misrepresentations.

·       Here, RDI, prior to foreclosing on the Subject Property, held a secured interest in the form of the RDI Deed of Trust. It is the RDI Deed of Trust which Plaintiff seeks to set aside, with her goal to ultimately set aside RDI's subsequent foreclosure of the RDI Deed of Trust against the Subject Property.

·       Plaintiff has not alleged any facts that would give rise to a successful claim that the underlying "Offending Deed" is void, not merely voidable.

·       RDI is a bona fide encumbrancer.

·       RDI should be awarded its attorneys’ fees. RDI request an order compelling Plaintiff to pay its reasonable attorney's fees, in the sum of $2,762.50 and costs in the amount of $60.00, for a total of $2,822.50.

No opposition was filed.

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Demurrer

 

a.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

b.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant RDI submitted a declaration stating he and Kyle Marks, Plaintiff’s counsel at the time, had numerous telephone and email communications concerning the issues raised in the Demurrer. (Bartelstone Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

c.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

RDI filed a request for judicial notice of the following: 

1.     The Grant Deed (in favor of Natalia Lianova) recorded on November 9, 201 1, as Document No. 20111519207 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

2.     Interspousal Grant Deed (in favor of Natalia Lianova) recorded on November 9, 20II, as Document No. 20111519208 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

3.     Quitclaim Deed (in favor of LA Development Properties LLC, a California Limited Liability Company), recorded on March 22,2023, as Document No. 20230182541 in the Offrcial Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

4.     Deed of Trust (in favor of RDI), recorded on March 22,2023, as Document No. 20230182542 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

5.     Notice of Default (re: RDI Deed of Trust) recorded on September 15, 2023, as Document No. 20230618430 and Notice of Trustee's Sale (re: RDI Deed of Trust) recorded on December 20,2023, as Document No. 20230893267.

6.     Trustee's Deed Upon Sale (in favor of RDI), recorded on August 9,2024, as Document No. 20240535475 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

Evidence Code §452(h) provides that a court may take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”  Additionally, “a court may take judicial notice of the fact of a document’s recordation, the date the document was recorded and executed, the parties to the transaction reflected in a recorded document, and the document’s legally operative language, assuming there is no genuine dispute regarding the document’s authenticity.”  (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 265.) Moreover, “[f]rom this, the court may deduce and rely upon the legal effect of the recorded document, when that effect is clear from its face.” (Id.)

 

The Court grants all of RDI’s unopposed requests for judicial notice.

 

d.     Demurrer to First, Third, Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action

 

Defendant RDI argues that all of Plaintiff’s claims asserted against RDI - the first cause of action for Quiet Title, third cause of action for Fraudulent Deceit, fourth cause of action for Cancellation of Instrument (Deed of Trust) and sixth cause of action for Declaratory Relief - fail because RDI is a bona fide encumbrancer.

Two elements establish a party as a bona fide encumbrancer: (1) encumbrance of the property in good faith for value, (2) without knowledge or notice of the asserted rights of another. (First Fidelity Thirty & Loan Ass'n v. Alliance Bank (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1440-1441.) “Bona fide purchasers and mortgagees who are about to invest money on the faith of title, should be held to have satisfied the demands of reasonable diligence, when they find an unincumbered [sic] record.” (Brock v. First South Savings Assn. (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 661, 673 [10 Cal.Rptr.2d 700].

A bona fide encumbrancer takes title free and clear “so long as the reconveyance is voidable and not void.” (Schiavon v. Arnaudo Brothers (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 374, 376.) “A deed is void if the grantor's signature is forged or if the grantor is unaware of the nature of what he or she is signing.” (Schiavon v. Arnaudo Brothers, supra, 84 Cal.App.4th at p. 378.) 

According to the Complaint, Plaintiff seeks to quiet title to the Subject Property against the interests of the Defendants, and each of them, as of November 9, 2011, the date upon which the Lianova Deed was recorded. (Complaint, ¶ 39.) Plaintiff alleges that the basis of her interest in the Subject Property is that the Offending Deed was obtained fraudulently via misrepresentation by Defendant Zarfati. (Complaint, ¶ 37.) The Complaint further alleges that a present controversy exists in that Plaintiff contends that her signature on the Offending Deed was fraudulently obtained, and that Plaintiff never authorized nor consented to the execution of and recording of the RDI DOT and GSA DOT, and that Defendant Zarfati never held the power necessary to execute the Katz DOT, and therefore Plaintiff should be declared the actual owner of the Subject Property and cancel the RDI DOT, GSA DOT, and Katz DOT. (Complaint, ¶ 59.)

 

Here, the Court agrees with RDI that the Complaint does not contain any allegations that RDI was not a bona fide encumbrancer. First, there are no allegations that the RDI DOT is void. Plaintiff does not allege that any signatures on the deeds were forged. Further, the Complaint contains allegation that RDI parted with valuable consideration in the form of the RDI loan. (Complaint, ¶¶ 23, 24.) Additionally, the Complaint alleges that Zarfati misrepresented to RDI the true ownership of the Subject Property. (Complaint, ¶ 44.) Therefore, there are no allegations indicating that RDI had knowledge or notice of Plaintiff’s asserted rights. The Court finds that RDI has sufficiently established that the claims asserted against it fail because RDI was a bona fide encumbrancer.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer to the first, third, fourth and sixth causes of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

B.     Motion to Expunge Lis Pendency

 

a.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

RDI filed a request for judicial notice of the following: 

1.     The Grant Deed (in favor of Natalia Lianova) recorded on November 9, 201 1, as Document No. 20111519207 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

2.     Interspousal Grant Deed (in favor of Natalia Lianova) recorded on November 9, 20II, as Document No. 20111519208 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

3.     Quitclaim Deed (in favor of LA Development Properties LLC, a California Limited Liability Company), recorded on March 22,2023, as Document No. 20230182541 in the Offrcial Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

4.     Deed of Trust (in favor of RDI), recorded on March 22,2023, as Document No. 20230182542 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

5.     Notice of Default (re: RDI Deed of Trust) recorded on September 15, 2023, as Document No. 20230618430 and Notice of Trustee's Sale (re: RDI Deed of Trust) recorded on December 20,2023, as Document No. 20230893267.

6.     Notice of Pendency of Action, recorded on July 26,2024, as Document No. 20240499897 inthe Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

7.     Trustee's Deed Upon Sale (in favor of RDI), recorded on August 9,2024, as Document No. 20240535475 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

Evidence Code §452(h) provides that a court may take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”  Additionally, “a court may take judicial notice of the fact of a document’s recordation, the date the document was recorded and executed, the parties to the transaction reflected in a recorded document, and the document’s legally operative language, assuming there is no genuine dispute regarding the document’s authenticity.”  (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 265.) Moreover, “[f]rom this, the court may deduce and rely upon the legal effect of the recorded document, when that effect is clear from its face.” (Id.)

 

The Court grants all of RDI’s unopposed requests for judicial notice.

 

b.     Legal Standard

 

A notice of pendency of action may be filed when a real property claim is alleged in an action.  (C.C.P., § 405.1, 405.2.)  A real property claim means the causes of action, if proven, would affect either: (a) title to or the right to possession of specific real property, or (b) the use of an easement identified in the pleading.  (C.C.P., § 405.4.)¿ A party seeking to remove the notice has two options, either attack the pleading as failing to state a real property claim¿(C.C.P., § 405.31),¿or attack the real property claim as lacking evidentiary merit¿(C.C.P., § 405.32).¿¿ 

 

In determining whether a real property claim has been asserted, the Court engages in a demurrer-like analysis.  (Park 100 Investment Group II, LLC v. Ryan¿(2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 795, 808.) ¿

 

When questioning the evidentiary merit of a claim, the claimant who filed the lis pendens has the burden of proof.  (C.C.P., § 405.30.)  Thus, that claimant, in opposing the motion to expunge the lis pendens, must demonstrate the following: (1) the action affects title to or right of possession of the real property described in the notice; (2) in so far as the said notice is concerned, the party recording the notice has commenced the action for a proper purpose and in good faith; and (3) the probable validity of the real property claim by a preponderance of the evidence.  (Hunting World, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 67, 70; see also C.C.P., §405.30, et seq.)  Only admissible evidence is permitted on the motion.¿ (Burger v. Superior Court¿(1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 1013, 1019.) 

 

c.      Discussion

 

Defendant RDI moves to expunge the Notice of Pendency of Action, recorded by Plaintiff on July 26, 2024, as Document No. 20240499897 in the Official Records of the Recorder's Office of Los Angeles County, California.

 

As Plaintiff has not filed an opposition to this motion, she has submitted no evidence or argument of any kind. Plaintiff has failed to meet her burden of demonstrating the probable validity of her real property claims. 

 

Therefore, the motion is GRANTED.

 

d.     Sanctions

 

The prevailing party on a motion to expunge is entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs in making or opposing the motion unless the other party acted with substantial justification.  (C.C.P., § 405.38.)¿ 

 

RDI seeks attorney’s fees in the amount of $2,762.50 and costs in the amount of $60. As the motion is being granted, RDI is entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees and costs. 

 

However, since the motion is unopposed, the Court will not grant RDI fees for the time anticipated to review any opposition and preparation of a reply brief. Therefore, the Court awards attorney’s fees and costs in the total amount of $1,522.50 (4.5 hours of work at a rate of $325.00/hour and the $60 filing fee.)

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant RDI’s Demurrer as to the Complaint’s First, Third, Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendant RDI’s Motion for an Order Expunging Notice of Pendency of Action is GRANTED. The Court GRANTS Defendant RDI’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $1,522.50. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Defendant RDI’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.
