DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV04570    Hearing Date: January 3, 2025    Dept: 107

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Northwest District

 

DEPARTMENT 107

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Tag Holdings Corporation,

 

         v.

 

David Benzaken, et al.,

 

 Case No.:  24VECV04570

 

 Motion to Quash Service of Summons

 

 

 

Tentative Ruling:    (1) Deny Motion; (2) Deny Plaintiff’s Request for Sanctions

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND

 

On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff Tag Holdings Corporation (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants David Benzaken, Basad Corporation, Sapir Razla, Raz Razla, and Does 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract; and (2) indemnification.

 

On October 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a proof of service showing Defendant David Benzaken (“Defendant”) was served by substituted service on October 23, 2024, at 471 Blair Road, Avenel, NJ 07001 (the “Location”). (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24.)

 

On December 6, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to quash service of summons (the “Motion”). On December 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On December 26, 2024, Defendant filed a reply. On December 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed a response to Defendant’s evidentiary objections.

 

II.            REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of the proof of service of summons that Plaintiff filed on October 25, 2024. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

The court need not take judicial notice of a document that is part of the case file. Thus, Defendant’s request for judicial notice is denied.

 

III.         PLAINTIFF’S OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff raises five objections to evidence submitted with Defendant’s Motion.

 

This court sustains the following objections because Defendant’s counsel lacks personal knowledge of these matters. (See Evid. Code § 702.)

 

1.    Objection No. 2, Motion to Quash, page 3, lines 10-11, stating “Plaintiff’s process server continued to attempt personal service only at the Location a total of four time.”

 

2.    Objection No. 3, Motion to Quash, page 3, lines 12-14, stating, “Plaintiff never attempted to serve Defendant at his residence or at another address which is Defendant’s usual place of business, so that service of process would be proper under C.C.P. §415.20.”

 

This court also sustains the following objections because they are hearsay. (See Evid. Code § 1200.)

 

1.    Objection No. 1, Motion to Quash, page 3, lines 4-7, stating: “When Plaintiff attempted personal service on Defendant in October 2024 at 471 Blair Road, Avenel, NJ 07001 (“Location”), Plaintiff’s process server was informed repeatedly that the Location was not Defendant’s usual place of business and directed to serve Defendant instead at his residence.”

 

2.    Objection No. 4, Motion to Quash, page 5, lines 5-9, stating: “As discussed more fully infra, Plaintiff’s efforts to serve Defendant fail to comply with C.C.P. §415.20 for substitute service because the Location is not Defendant’s usual place of business, Plaintiff was advised there was a better and Code-compliant address at which to serve Defendant, and the person with whom the summons and complaint were left does not have the type of ‘close connection’ to Defendant required by statute.”

 

3.    Objection No. 5, Motion to Quash, page 5, lines 20-25, stating: “On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff’s process server was informed by a receptionist at the Location that Defendant ‘is rarely in the office.’ (RJN Exhibit A, p. A-4.) On October 14, 2024, Plaintiff’s process server was again informed by a ‘male employee’ at the Location that Defendant ‘is rarely in the office’ and for that reason, Plaintiff should serve Defendant at his residence. (Id.) On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s process server was advised that Defendant was ‘not in the office.’ (Id.)”

 

This court will consider the above statements to the extent they show that the process server was told Defendant was “rarely” at the Location and should be served at Defendant’s residence.  However, this court will not consider the above statements to the extent they are being offered to prove Defendant is rarely at the Location.

This court overrules Defendant’s objections.

 

V.             TIMELINESS

 

“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion. . . [t]o quash service of summons[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10(a)(1).) Generally, a responsive pleading is due within thirty days after service of the summons. (Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).) When a party is served by substitute service, “[s]ervice . . . is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20(a).)

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of service showing Defendant was served by substituted service on October 23, 2024. (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24.) The documents were mailed to Defendant that same day. (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 p. 3.)  Accordingly, service was deemed complete on November 2, 2024, and a responsive pleading was due by December 2, 2024.

 

However, Defendant submits an email from Plaintiff’s counsel stating that “Requests for entry of default will be filed if answers are not filed by December 6, 2024.” (Ex. 1 p. 1.)

 

Accordingly, this court finds that Defendant timely filed the Motion.

 

VI.          LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A defendant, . . . may serve and file a notice of motion…to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿418.10(a)(1).) 

 

When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) The plaintiff must establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence. (Aquila, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 568.)

 

“The filing of a proof of service declaration ordinarily creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper, but only if the service declaration ‘complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.’” (Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1164.) That presumption may be rebutted by contrary evidence submitted by a defendant. (Fernandes v. Singh (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 932, 941, fn. 6.)

¿¿ 

If service of summons is not properly and validly effectuated by one or more of the approved methods, California courts lack personal jurisdiction over the defendants, and any judicial actions taken without such service violates principles of fundamental due process and is therefore void. (County of San Diego v. Gorham (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1226-1227.)¿

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20, subdivision (a), states:

 

In lieu of personal delivery of a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served as specified in Section 416.10, 416.20, 416.30, 416.40, or 416.50, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in his or her office or, if no physical address is known, at his or her usual mailing address, other than a United States Postal Service post office box, with the person who is apparently in charge thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left. When service is effected by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at a mailing address, it shall be left with a person at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.

 

“Two or three attempts to personally serve a defendant at a proper place ordinarily qualifies as ‘reasonable diligence.’” (Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.)

 

VII.       DISCUSSION

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s proof of service is invalid because “Plaintiff’s process server admits he was advised not once, but twice that the Location was not Defendant’s usual place of business” and “Plaintiff’s process server admits he was informed that Defendant could be served at his residence.” (Motion p. 6.)

 

Plaintiff submits a proof of service stating that the process server served “a person at least 18 years of age apparently in charge at the office or usual place of business of the person to be served” by leaving the summons, complaint, and notice of case assignment – unlimited civil case, “with or in the presence of ‘John Doe’, Employee.” (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 p. 1.) The proof of service describes the “John Doe” and states the process server “informed him or her of the general nature of the papers.” (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 p. 1.) Plaintiff also submits a proof of service by mail showing the documents were subsequently mailed to Defendant. (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 pdf p. 3.) And Plaintiff submits an affidavit of due diligence showing the process server made three unsuccessful attempts to serve Defendant before serving Defendant by substituted service at the Location. (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 pdf p. 4.) The proofs of service are signed by the process servers. (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 pdf pp. 2, 5.) Further, although the process server was told Defendant was “rarely” in the office, this case is distinguishable from cases where a process server is told, for example, that a defendant does not reside at an address. (See Lebel v. Mai, supra, 210 Cal.App.4th at p. 1164 [holding a proof of service did not give rise to a presumption service was valid because the process server was told the defendant did not reside at that address and “[n]othing in the record support[ed] a determination that the [address] was defendant’s residence or that it was reasonable to attempt personal service of him there, with knowledge that he resided abroad.”])  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s submitted proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper.

 

The only evidence Defendant submits with the Motion is a declaration by Defendant’s counsel who discusses downloading and reviewing the proof of service and corresponding with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the proof of service and default and an exhibit containing correspondence between Plaintiff’s counsel and Defendant’s counsel. (See Thompson-Mann Declaration; Ex. 1.)  The declaration and exhibit contain no evidence rebutting the presumption that Plaintiff’s proof of service was proper.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s proof of service does not satisfy the statutory requirements because it was not made at Defendant’s “usual place of business.” (Motion pp. 5-6.) To the extent this evidence is offered to prove the Location where Defendant was served is not Defendant’s usual place of business, this court sustained Plaintiff’s objections to what the process server was told.  Defendant submits no evidence showing the Location where Plaintiff served Defendant is not Defendant’s usual place of business. For example, Defendant does not submit a declaration by Defendant stating Defendant is rarely in the office or that it is not Defendant’s usual place of business.

 

Defendant further argues that “There is no indication on the Proof of Service or in any of the attachments thereto that on or before October 23, 2024, Plaintiff attempted to serve Defendant at Defendant’s residence, as advised on October 14, 2024, or at any other address which may comply with the requirements of C.C.P. §415.20 before leaving the documents with ‘John Doe.’” (Motion p. 6.) Citing Evartt v. Superior Court (1979) 89 Cal.App.3d 795, Defendant argues “Plaintiff must show why personal service could not be effected if Defendant was available for personal service.”

 

In Evartt, the real party in interest alleged an assault occurred on Grimes Avenue. (Evartt v. Superior Court, supra, 89 Cal.App.3d at p. 798.) The petitioner also resided on Grimes Avenue, but “there was no effort to serve the summons and complaint until August 23, 1978, three days before the expiration of the three-year period under section 581a.” (Id.) The real party in interest submitted a proof of service showing the process server made four efforts to personally serve the petitioner (three attempts at the petitioner’s Grimes Avenue address and one attempt at the petitioner’s son’s residence) before leaving a copy at the Grimes Avenue address with a member of the petitioner’s household and mailing a copy to the petitioner. (Id.) On appeal, the Court noted that “Petitioner’s declaration in support of his motion to quash establishes that he had lived continuously at 1809 Grimes Avenue, Modesto, for 14 years, during which period his address and telephone number in the telephone directory had remained unchanged. At no time since the incidents alleged in the complaint had the real party in interest made any demand for damages or communicated with him concerning the complaint, nor to his knowledge was any attempt ever made to serve him with the complaint.” (Id.) The declaration also “stated that he had been available for service at his residence during the entire period of time that he had lived there.” (Id.) The Court held that “real party clearly failed to carry her burden, having not presented one scintilla of information or explanation for her failure to attempt personal service for 2 years and 363 days, during all of which time except for very short periods defendant was available for service at his residence close by.” (Id. at 801-802.) The Court also noted that “[g]iven the nearness of their residences and the locale of the alleged assault, it is clearly inferable that real party knew where petitioner lived and of his availability during the entire period and for motives which do not appear she did not effect earlier service on him personally.” (Id. at 802.)

 

Plaintiff submits an affidavit of diligence with the proof of service showing the process server made three unsuccessful attempts to serve Defendant before serving Defendant by substituted service. (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 pdf p. 4.) Unlike the petitioner in Evartt, Defendant here submits no declaration indicating where Defendant lived or that Defendant was available for service at Defendant’s residence. Moreover, unlike Evartt, where the real party in interest did not attempt to serve the petitioner until three years before the period expired, Plaintiff filed the complaint in this case on September 16, 2024, and served Defendant about a month later, on October 23, 2024. (Complaint; Proof of Service filed 10/25/24.) Thus, Evartt is distinguishable from this case. (Plaintiff submits with the opposition an affidavit of due diligence showing Plaintiff attempted to serve Defendant at a different address. (See Ex. 2.) Defendant objects because it is not stamped showing Plaintiff submitted the document for filing with the court. (See Reply p. 2.))

 

Relying on a Judicial Council Comment, Defendant also argues the documents must be delivered to “a person closely connected with [the defendant].” (Motion p. 6.) Defendant argues “[t]here is no indication on the proof of service that the person to whom Plaintiff’s process server delivered the documents on October 23, 2024 was ‘a person closely connected’ to Defendant beyond Plaintiff’s process server’s statement that ‘John Doe’ was Defendant’s employee.” (Motion pp. 6-7.)

 

There is no requirement that the person given the papers must be identified by their true name. In Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 175, 182, the party argued a proof of service was invalid “because the true name of the person given the summons was not used; he was instead identified as ‘John Doe, co-resident.’” The Court of Appeal held that “The evident purpose of the requirement that the proof of service ‘recite or in other manner show the name’ (§ 417.10, subd. (a)) of the recipient is to enable the recipient to be located in the future, should the claim of service be challenged. The statement that ‘John Doe, co-resident’ received service at a specific address satisfies this purpose.” (Id. at p. 184.)

 

The proof of service submitted here states Plaintiff’s process server “left the documents . . . with or in the presence of: ‘John Doe’, Employee (Age: 55; Ethnicity: Caucasian; Gender: Male; Weight: 225; Height: 6’2”; Hair: Black)” who was “a person at least 18 years of age apparently in charge at the office or usual place of business of the person to be served.”  (Proof of Service filed 10/25/24 p. 1.) Like Trackman v. Kenney, the description here adequately describes the recipient such that the recipient can be in located in the future. Defendant submits no evidence to rebut the presumption that service to the “’John Doe’, Employee” was improper.

 

VIII.    SANCTIONS

 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Defendant “pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, subparts (a), (b) and (c) (2), for misrepresenting the facts and law and burdening the plaintiff and the Court with this baseless motion.” (Opposition p. 7.)

 

“A party seeking sanctions under section 128.7 follows a two-step procedure. [Citation] First, the moving party serves notice of the motion for sanctions on the offending party. [Citation] Service of the motion starts a safe harbor period during which the motion cannot be filed with the court. [Citation] If the challenged paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation or denial is not withdrawn or corrected within a prescribed number of days, the second step is to file or present the motion for sanctions to the court.” (Primo Hospitality Group, Inc. v. Haney (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 165, 173-174.) 

 

“[A] party is not entitled to sanctions pursuant to¿section 128.7¿unless the target of the motion has had a full 21 days to withdraw the allegedly offending paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation, or denial.” (Broadcast Music, Inc. v. Structured Asset Sales, LLC (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 596, 599.)  “[A] moving party cannot file a motion for sanctions until the 22nd day after the motion was served.” (Id.) 

 

Plaintiff submits no evidence showing Plaintiff gave Defendant notice of the motion for sanctions or that Defendant had the full 21-day safe-harbor period to withdraw the Motion. Accordingly, this court awards no sanctions.

 

IX.           CONCLUSION


For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion is denied.
