DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV18602    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: 73

09/13/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

AVERY v. BELANGER, et al. (22STCV18602)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Jason Stone (Stone & Sallus, LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  R. Steven Derryberry (Derryberry & Associates LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT ONLY ESCROW, INC.’S RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE; REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed 08/05/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Only Escrow, Inc shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. in the reduced amount of $1,515.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 7, 2022, Plaintiff Veronica Avery filed the instant action against Defendants Catherine Belanger (“Catherine”), Benjamin Belanger, Kannella, Inc. dba Re/Max All-Pro (Re/Max), and Does 1-10.

 

On October 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.     Elder Financial Abuse

2.     Breach of Fiduciary Duty

3.     Fraud

4.     Violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200

5.     Declaratory Relief and Injunctive Relief

The FAC alleges that Plaintiff is an elder with dementia and cognitive decline, and that Plaintiff suffered damages when Defendants took her home against her will and forged her signature to complete the sale of her home to themselves at a discounted price.

 

On August 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendant and/or its counsel in the amount of $2,000.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

If the response to an RFP includes an objection to the demand in part, it must also include a statement of compliance or noncompliance. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240, subd. (a).) Additionally, the response must (1) identify the particular document that falls within the category of the request to which the objection is being made, and (2) expressly set forth the extent of, and specific ground for, the objection. (Id., subd. (b).)¿A propounding party may move for an order compelling further response to a request for production if it decides that “an objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (a).) 

 

If a motion to compel responses is filed, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff served the Request for Production of Documents, Set One on Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. on June 7, 2024. (Carpinelli Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Defendant’s response was due on or before July 9, 2024, however, no response has been served. (Ibid.)  Therefore, because a response has not been served, the motion to compel is granted. 

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions for counsel’s work on the instant motion. Plaintiff requests a total of $2,000.00, representing 3 hours spent on the motion and to draft a Reply, and 1 hour to appear at the hearing, at an hourly rate of $485, plus $60 for the filing fee. (Id. ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Defendant has failed to respond. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court reduces the total award by $485.00 which represents one hour of Plaintiff counsel’s hourly rate. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the total amount of $1,515.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Only Escrow, Inc shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. in the reduced amount of $1,515.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 


Case Number: 23STCV20961    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: 73

09/13/24

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

GHAZARYAN v. GENERAL MOTORS LLC (23STCV20961)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/ moving party:  David Barry (Barry Law Firm)

Counsel for Defendant/ opposing party:  Mary Arens McBride (Erskine Law Group APC)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, NOS. 1-3, 9, 14-16, 31-33 AND 37-72 (filed 03/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Nos. 1-3, 9, 14-16, and 37-72 is GRANTED. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 30 days.

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Nos. 31-33 is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff Hasmik Ghazaryan filed the instant action against Defendant General Motors, LLC.

 

Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on September 12, 2023, alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of lmplied Warranty of Merchantability under The Song-Beverly Act

2.      Breach of Express Warranty under The Song-Beverly Act

3.      Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200

 

The FAC alleges the following. On November 28, 2020, Plaintiff leased a 2021 Chevrolet Silverado 1500, VIN 24 3GCPWCEK3MG 131465 (hereinafter referred to as the "Vehicle"). (FAC, ¶ 5.) Plaintiff has presented the Vehicle to Defendants for repairs of defects, malfunctions, misadjustments, and/or nonconformities related to: (a) the "Check Engine" warning light illuminating on six (6) separate occasions; (b) the illumination of the "Check Gages" warning light; (c) the EV AP system causing poor acceleration; (d) the Vehicle on two (2) separate occasions producing a popping/rattling noise; (e) erratic and 2 surging RPMs; and (f) the transmission slipping. (FAC, ¶ 11.) However, Defendants or their representatives failed to conform the Vehicle to the applicable warranties because said defects, malfunctions, misadjustments, and/or nonconformities continue to exist even after a reasonable number of attempts to repair was given. (FAC, ¶ 13.)

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents Nos. 1-3, 9, 14-16, 31-33, and 37- 72, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Request Nos. 1-3, 9, 14-16, 31-33, and 37- 72.

·       Defendant’s responses are not code compliant

·       The Documents Requested Are Relevant And Discoverable

·       Defendant’s general objections regarding burden of production are not well-taken given Defendant’s obligations under federal law to maintain the requested records

·       Sanctions are warranted.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff did not meet and confer in good faith

·       The Court should sustain Defendant’s objections.

·       Plaintiff Seeks Documents that GM Has Already Produced

·       Plaintiff Does Not Need All Recalls, Technical Service Bulletins, or Special Service Messages

·       Plaintiff Does Not Need Information Concerning GM’s Internal Investigations, and/or Analysis of the “Defects” Alleged in Their Complaint

 

Plaintiff filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff met and conferred in good faith

·       Defendant Fails To Justify Its Objections or Refusal To Provide Code-Compliant Responses

·       The Documents Sought Are Relevant And Discoverable

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a meet-and confer-declaration

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel a further response to a request for production must be noticed within 45 days of the service of the response, or any supplemental response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.  Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel further response to the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c); see also Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409; Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)  The motion must also be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of any issue presented by it. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).) Finally, a motion to compel further responses to inspection demands must be accompanied by a separate document that sets forth each item to which further response is requested and the factual and legal reasons for compelling it. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a).)  

 

B.      Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiff’s counsel states he sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant's counsel regarding deficiencies with the discovery responses at issue. (Kim Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff’s counsel states the parties met and confirmed telephonically on March 6, 2024 and that he sent a follow up email on March 8, 2024. (Kim Decl., ¶¶ 10-11.) However, Plaintiffs’ counsel states Defendant has not responded. (Kim Decl., ¶ 12.)

 

The Court finds the moving party has met its meet and confer obligation. Accordingly, the Court will consider the merits of the motion.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Request Nos. 1-3, 9, 14-16, 31-33, and 37- 72.

 

a.       Request Nos. 1-3, 9, 14-16

 

Request Nos. 1-3 seek the repair orders, documents related to repair procedures, repair manuals, and warranty repairs regarding the Subject Vehicle.

 

Request No. 9 seeks recall documents and special service messages (including TSBs and services bulletins) related to the Subject Vehicle.

 

Request Nos. 14-16 documents related to statements about and communication related to the Subject Vehicle.

 

Plaintiff contends the information sought relate to pre-litigation opportunities to conform the Subject Vehicle to the applicable express warranties that Plaintiff gave Defendant, as well as the communications and information relied upon in any investigation of Plaintiff’s pre-litigation claims. These repair opportunities form the backbone of any Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act matter.

 

Defendant asserts it produced the available responsive documents and directed Plaintiff to specific documents among its production as responsive to these particular requests. Defendant also objects to these Requests to the extent it seeks confidential, proprietary and trade secret information. Yet, Defendant states that “in the spirit of compromise, GM will also agree to supplement its document production pursuant to a protective order and produce its Warranty Policy and Procedure Manual; its policies and procedures used to evaluate lemon law claims and repurchase requests made under the Song-Beverly Warranty Act during the relevant period and as it relates to Plaintiff and the Subject Vehicle; and other customer complaints within GM’s ESI database that are substantially similar to Plaintiff’s complaint(s) concerning the alleged defects, for vehicles purchased in California of the same year, make, and model as the Subject Vehicle.” (Def’s Separate Statement, p. 5.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to his case. To the extent Defendant asserts a privacy privilege, it must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].)

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of document Nos. 1-3, 9and 14-16.

 

b.      Request Nos. 31-33

 

Request Nos. 31-33 concern documents related to Defendant’s technical hotline, given that Defendant was contacted regarding the issues with the Subject Vehicle.

 

Request No. 31 seeks “All DOCUMENTS relating to the Customer Call Center, including but not limited to, all flow charts, processes, and/or scripts.”

 

Request No. 32 seeks “All DOCUMENTS relating to any Customer Loyalty Program or After Warranty Assistance Program that YOU had in effect during the RELEVANT TIME PERIOD.”

 

Request No. 33 seeks “All DOCUMENTS related to the Technical Hotline.”

 

Defendant argues these requests are overbroad. The Court agrees. “Omnibus” descriptions in requests for documents are improper. (Flora Crane Serv., Inc., v. Super. Ct. In & For City & Cnty. of San Francisco (1965) 234 Cal.App.2d 767, 786). “The unlimited characteristics of such a description may impair or destroy exactitude so that the custodian of the records is not reasonably apprised of what he must produce.” (Id. at 787). Here, requesting “all documents” related to Defendant’s call center, programs and technical hotline without sufficiently specifying the subject and time period appear to be overbroad.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of document Nos. 31-33.

 

c.       Request Nos. 37-72

 

These requests concern similar complaints made by other owners of vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the Plaintiff’s vehicle.

 

Defendant argues the information sought is irrelevant. Specifically, that information about other vehicles, concerns and complaints are not relevant and beyond the scope of Plaintiff’s causes of action.

 

Plaintiff argues that this information is relevant and may serve as proof of the existence of a defect or nonconformity, as well as Defendant’s knowledge of widespread warranty problems and Defendant’s failure to act despite this knowledge.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to his case. The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of document Nos. 37-72.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Nos. 1-3, 9, 14-16, and 37-72 is GRANTED. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 30 days.

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Nos. 31-33 is DENIED.
