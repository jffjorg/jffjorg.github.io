Case Number: 23STCV00076    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 52

Order to Show Cause Re: Entry of Default Judgment

            Plaintiff Salvador Lopez, Jr. brings an action against defendants Advance Pipe Bending, a business entity form unknown, and Mitchell McCray.  Both defendants have defaulted.  California Rules of Court, rule 3.110(h) provides, “When a default is entered, the party who requested the entry of default must obtain a default judgment against the defaulting party within 45 days after the default was entered, unless the court has granted an extension of time.  The court may issue an order to show cause why sanctions should not be imposed if that party fails to obtain entry of judgment against a defaulting party or to request an extension of time to apply for a default judgment within that time.”

            The clerk entered the defaults of defendants Advance Pipe Bending and Mitchell McCray on March 19, 2024.  More than 45 days have passed.  Plaintiff did not timely request entry of default judgment against defendants.  The court, however, exercises its discretion not to issue an order to show cause why sanctions should not be imposed.

The court hereby discharges the order to show cause re: why plaintiff should not pay $100 in sanctions under rule 3.110(g). 

The court hereby continues the order to show cause re: entry of default judgment to July 11, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  Plaintiff shall file a complete default judgment application including all documents listed in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a), no later than July 1, 2024. 


Case Number: 23STCV23861    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendants Jason Schoener and Ten Key Films, Inc.’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

Demurrer

            Defendants Jason Schoener and Ten Key Films, Inc. (Ten Key) demur to plaintiffs George Gamble and Nancy Gamble’s sixth and ninth causes of action.

6th Cause of Action: Aiding and Abetting Conversion

Plaintiffs do not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  “[T]here can be no aiding and abetting liability absent the commission of an underlying tort.”  (Nasrawi v. Buck Consultants LLC (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 328, 344, fn. 7.)  Plaintiffs do not allege sufficient facts for conversion, so this cause of action fails.  Conversion of money requires that the defendant “wrongfully exercised dominion over a specifically identifiable pot of money that already belongs to the” plaintiff.  (Voris v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1141, 1152–1153.)  The plaintiff must “allege [he or she] is ‘entitled to immediate possession at the time of conversion.’ ”  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 452.)

Plaintiffs do not allege any defendant converted money to which they had a right to immediate possession.  Plaintiffs allege they “formed a special purpose company to produce the Film (the ‘Company’).”  (FAC, ¶ 20.)  Early in the first amended complaint, they allege, “Plaintiffs owned and possessed certain petty cash funds which they allowed to be used in connection with the production of the Film.”  (¶ 27.)  Plaintiffs allege defendants Turrow and Featurepro, Inc. converted these petty cash funds.  (¶¶ 77-81.)  Later, they allege “Defendants Schoener, Ten Key and Roven had advance, specific and direct knowledge that Turrow and Feature Pro were not authorized to make personal use of the Company’s petty cash assets.”  (¶ 88, italics added.)  They further allege, “Defendants Schoener and Ten Key nonetheless continued to assist Defendants Turrow and Featurepro in their tortious appropriation of the Film’s petty cash assets.”  (¶ 90, italics added.)  The first amended complaint also refers to “the Film’s petty cash assets” in two headings on page 14.  (P. 14, lines 6, 19.)  Finally, plaintiffs allege defendants “agreed to appropriate the Film’s petty cash assets.”  (¶ 134.)

Plaintiffs do not allege facts showing they had any immediate right to possess this petty cash.  They allege they formed a corporation to produce a film and allowed that money to be used to further the production.  “It is fundamental that a corporation is a legal entity that is distinct from its shareholders.”  (Grosset v. Wenaas (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1100, 1108; accord Curci Investments, LLC v. Baldwin (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 214, 221 [the law generally recognizes “separation between an individual and a business entity”].)  Generally, “an incorporator should be precluded from ignoring his own deliberately chosen corporate form.”  (In re Marriage of Imperato (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 432, 439.)  Plaintiffs cannot sue as individuals for conversion of the corporation’s money.  

That Turrow and Featurepro allegedly misappropriated the money does not constitute an injury to plaintiffs directly.  They were not obligated to return that money to plaintiffs.  (See Fischer v. Machado (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1069, 1072 [conversion applies where an agent sells principal’s goods and is therefore “ ‘required to turn over to his principal a definite sum received by him on his principal’s account’ ”].)  They were supposed to use the money to produce the film—not give it back to plaintiffs.  Using the money for the wrong purposes does not constitute conversion of something plaintiffs had the immediate right to possess.

Plaintiffs’ opposition responds to this argument only by relying on Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41(b), which provides, “A party demurring to a pleading that has been amended after a demurrer to an earlier version of the pleading was sustained shall not demur to any portion of the amended complaint … on grounds that could have been raised by demurrer to the earlier version of the complaint.”  Schoener and Ten Key could not have raised this basis for their demurrer to the initial complaint.  They primarily rely on paragraphs 88 and 90, which are unique to the first amended complaint. 

9th Cause of Action: Civil Conspiracy

Plaintiffs do not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action against Schoener and Ten Key.  “There is no separate tort of civil conspiracy and no action for conspiracy to commit a tort unless the underlying tort is committed and damage results therefrom.”  (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1136.) 

This cause of action fails for the same reasons as the sixth cause of action for aiding and abetting conversion.  Plaintiffs allege Schoener and Ten Key are liable for civil conspiracy because they “agreed to a common plan to appropriate the Film’s petty cash assets.”  (FAC, p. 14, line 6.)  “Pursuant to the agreement…, the Film’s petty cash assets were appropriated.”  (Id., line 19.)  The only allegations supporting this cause of action against Schoener and Ten Key concern conspiracy to commit the tort of conversion.  Without that underlying tort, plaintiffs cannot state a cause of action against Schoener and Ten Key.

Motion to Strike

            Defendants Jason Schoener and Ten Key Films, Inc. move to strike four portions of plaintiffs’ first amended complaint.  Schoener and Ten Key Films successfully demurred to both causes of action alleged against them.  Their motion to strike is therefore moot.

Disposition

            Defendants Jason Schoener and Ten Key Films, Inc.’s demurrers to plaintiffs’ sixth and ninth causes of action are sustained with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

Defendants Jason Schoener and Ten Key Films, Inc.’s motion to strike portions of plaintiffs’ first amended complaint is moot.

Defendant Randolf L. Turrow’s Motion to Set Aside Default

            Defendant Randolf L. Turrow moves to set aside his default. 

Evidentiary Objections

            Plaintiffs George Gamble and Nancy Gamble make “general objections” and three objections to portions of the declaration of John V. Tamborelli.  They make “general objections” and two objections to portions of the declaration of Randolf L. Turrow.  All objections are overruled.     

Turrow’s Default

            Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) provides, “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”  “Because the law favors disposing of cases on their merits, ‘any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.’ ”  (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980.)                  

Turrow shows his default resulted from his mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  He was served with the summons and complaint both on October 15, 2023, and October 28, 2023.  (Turrow Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.)  He states that, as a result, he believed he had 30 days after the latter date to respond to the complaint.  (Id., ¶ 4.)  Plaintiff requested entry of Turrow’s default on November 16.  Turrow’s failure to respond before November 16 constitutes mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Believing his 30 days began running from the second service was a reasonable mistake.  Turrow also acted in good faith to timely respond to the complaint based on that belief.  On November 21, Turrow and co-defendant Feature Pro Inc. filed a declaration of demurring party to extend the deadline to demur.  Turrow and Feature Pro Inc. filed a demurrer on January 5, 2024. 

In his reply brief, Turrow also contends his default was automatically vacated when plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint.  Materially amending a complaint vacates defendants’ defaults.  (See Greenup v. Rodman (1986) 42 Cal.3d 822, 830; accord Airs Aromatics, LLC v. CBL Data Recovery Technologies, Inc. (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 1013, 1025.)  Turrow explains he did not make this argument in the moving papers because plaintiffs never served the first amended complaint on him.  (Tamborelli Reply Decl., ¶ 3.) 

Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint materially amended the allegations against Turrow.  Plaintiffs added several paragraphs of new allegations, including substantive allegations about Turrow.  They added new allegations that: “Defendant Turrow arranged for the transfer of Petty Cash Funds to the bank account of his personally owned company, Defendant Featurepro.”  (FAC, ¶ 95); “Defendant Turrow instructed Defendants Schoener and Ten Key to falsify the financial records to conceal and disguise the Petty Cash Funds Defendant Turrow transferred to Defendant Featurepro’s bank account”  (¶ 96); “Defendant Turrow intended to hire production accounts who would be willing to assist him in his plan to appropriate the Petty Cash Funds”  (¶ 125); “Defendant Turrow believed that Defendants Schoener and Ten Key would report to him directly, follow his instructions specifically, and provide assistance to Turrow in his plan to appropriate the Petty Cash Funds.”  (¶ 126).  Filing the first amended complaint therefore vacated Turrow’s default.

In their opposition, plaintiffs argue Turrow’s motion is defective because he did not submit a proposed responsive pleading as required under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).  The court rejects this argument for two reasons.  First, as discussed above, filing the first amended complaint vacated Turrow’s default even without relief under section 473(b).  Second, Turrow already filed a demurrer to the initial complaint.  The court struck Turrow’s demurrer because he had defaulted.  Requiring him to submit the demurrer again by attaching it to this motion would serve no purpose.

Sanctions

            In his reply brief, Turrow seeks monetary sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.  This request for sanctions is procedurally defective.  Section 128.5(f)(1)(A) provides, “A motion for sanctions under this section shall be made separately from other motions or requests.”  It cannot be done in a reply brief.  Turrow also did not follow the required two-step procedure permitting 21 days of safe harbor.  (See Transcon Financial, Inc. v. Reid & Hellyer, APC (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 547, 550-551.) 

Even if this request were procedurally proper, the court would decline to impose sanctions.  Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5 applies to “ ‘actions or tactics’ which are both frivolous and in ‘bad-faith.’ ”  (Summers v. City of Cathedral City (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1047, 1070.)  Such sanctions “requir[e] subjective bad faith” (In re Marriage of Sahafzadeh-Taeb & Taeb (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 124, 128), meaning “committed with an improper motive, such as to harass or manipulate opposing counsel or the court” (id. at p. 141).  Assuming plaintiffs engaged in frivolous actions or tactics, Turrow does not present sufficient evidence of subjective bad faith. 

Disposition

            Defendant Randolf L. Turrow’s motion to set aside default is granted.  The court hereby vacates the default of defendant Randolf L. Turrow.  Turrow may file a pleading responding to plaintiffs’ first amended complaint within 30 days.  If plaintiffs file a second amended complaint, Turrow may file a responsive pleading within 30 days of service of the second amended complaint.


Case Number: 23STCV28309    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Neda Alhilali’s Motion to Quash Subpoena

Plaintiff Neda Alhilali moves to quash a subpoena to Bank of America, N.A.  Upon a party’s motion, a court “may make an order quashing [a] subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.  In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”  (CCP § 1987.1(a).) 

Defendant Clan Co issued a subpoena to Bank of America for two categories of documents.  (Belisle Decl., Ex. A.)  No. 1 demands, “Any and all DOCUMENTS, bank statements, checks, deposits, withdrawals, transactions related to NEDA ALHILALI’s bank account number ending in 33961 between January 1, 2018 until the DATE OF PRODUCTION.”  No. 2 demands, “Any and all DOCUMENTS, bank statements, checks, deposits, withdrawals, transactions related to any and all bank accounts associated with NEDA ALHILALI between January 1, 2018 until the DATE OF PRODUCTION.” 

Plaintiff objects that this violates her privacy rights.  “The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552 (Williams).)  If the objecting party shows all three elements, then the court must balance the need for disclosure against the right to privacy.  (Ibid.)  The court must also consider “the availability of alternatives and protective measures.”  (Id. at p. 556.)

Plaintiff establishes all three elements.  First, the California Constitution provides a right “of informational privacy.”  (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 552.)  Second, it is reasonable to expect that one’s financial records are private.  Suing for damages generally does not mean the plaintiff must completely open her financial records to the defendant.  Third, the intrusion is relatively serious.  Defendants seek the records for all transactions from plaintiff’s bank accounts for over six years.       

When considering the protective measures available, defendants’ need for disclosure outweighs plaintiff’s need for privacy.  Plaintiff claims damages arising from payments she made to others.  As for the rent payments made to defendants, defendants adequately explain why they cannot get this information from any other source.  “Plaintiff Neda Alhilali’s rent checks were deposited into a Bank of America account in the name of Frank Wattles.  Unfortunately, Frank Wattles is currently suffering from dementia and Alzheimer’s disease and is unable to access that bank account.”  (A. Wattles Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.)  Frank Wattles’s family has been unable to access his bank account.  (Id., ¶¶ 4-7.)

As for payments for repairs, plaintiff claims damages arising from payments of $400, $800, $1,000, and $2,500 between 2019 to 2023.  (Belisle Reply Decl., Ex. A, No. 9.1.)  Defendants’ counsel states plaintiff  “has not yet produced any documentary evidence of any repairs she made to the apartment.”  (Montgomery Decl., ¶ 13.)  Whether plaintiff made those payments is directly relevant to her damages.  Acquiring plaintiff’s bank records directly from the bank is the most reliable way to establish whether she made those payments. 

Though this discovery constitutes a relatively serious intrusion, entering a protective order limiting disclosure will adequately mitigate the invasion of plaintiff’s privacy.  The court will also narrow the subpoena to records from January 1, 2019, to the present. 

Sanctions

            Both parties move for sanctions against one another.  “[T]he court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing” a motion to quash a subpoena, “including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (CCP § 1987.2(a).)  The court exercises its discretion not to impose sanctions.  The motion was not made in bad faith.  The subpoena was not oppressive. 

Disposition

Plaintiff Neda Alhilali’s motion to quash subpoena is granted in part.  The court hereby modifies the subpoena as follows: Request No. 1 shall state, “Any and all DOCUMENTS, bank statements, checks, deposits, withdrawals, transactions relatedto NEDA ALHILALI’s bank account number ending in 33961 between Jaunary 1, 2019 until the DATE OF PRODUCTION.”  Request No. 2 shall state, “Any and all  DOCUMENTS, bank statements, checks, deposits, withdrawals, transactions related to any and all bank accounts associated with NEDA ALHILALI between Jaunary 1, 2019 until the DATE OF PRODUCTION.”

The parties are ordered to meet and confer regarding a stipulated protective order within 7 days.    

Defendants apply ex parte to advance the hearing on their motion to reopen discovery and to compel site inspection.  Defendants show good cause to advance the hearing and to shorten the time of notice.  The jury trial is set for June 5, 2024.  There is not enough time for a regularly noticed motion. 

 

                The application is granted.  The court hereby advances the hearing on defendants’ motion to reopen discovery and compel site inspection from June 26, 2024, to May 27, 2024, at 9:00 a.m.  Plaintiff shall file any opposition to the motion no later than May 21, 2024.  Defendants shall not file any reply.



Case Number: 23STCV30876    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendant Propark America West, LLC’s Motion to Compel Arbitration of Individual PAGA Claim and Stay the Representative PAGA Claims

Defendant Propark America West, LLC moves to compel arbitration of plaintiff Nancy Benavides’s individual claims under the Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA) and moves to stay the representative PAGA claims.  (See Viking River Cruises, Inc. v. Moriana (2022) 596 U.S. 639, 662-663 (Viking River); Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104, 1113-1114 (Adolph) [discussing arbitrable “individual” PAGA claims and nonarbitrable representative or “non-individual” claims].)

Plaintiff opposes the motion on two grounds.  First, she argues her first amended complaint (which she filed after defendant filed this motion) alleges only representative or non-individual claims, so there are no individual claims subject to arbitration under Viking River and Adolph.  (Opp., pp. 4-5.)  Second, she argues the parties’ arbitration agreement contains an unenforceable waiver of the right to bring a representative PAGA action and the agreement provides that a court is the exclusive forum for PAGA claims.  (Opp., pp. 5-7.) 

The parties’ agreement delegates to the arbitrator the power to rule on these gateway issues.  “Parties to an arbitration agreement may agree to delegate to the arbitrator, instead of a court, questions regarding the enforceability of the agreement.  [Citation.]  They ‘can agree to arbitrate almost any dispute—even a dispute over whether the underlying dispute is subject to arbitration.’ ”  (Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 241.)  “There are two prerequisites for a delegation clause to be effective.  First, the language of the clause must be clear and unmistakable.  [Citation.]  Second, the delegation must not be revocable under state contract defenses such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability.”  (Id. at p. 242.)

The parties’ agreement includes a clear and unmistakable delegation clause.  It provides, “[A]ny dispute whatsoever relating to the interpretation, validity, or performance of this Agreement shall be submitted to final binding arbitration.  If an arbitrator finds that a dispute is not covered by this Agreement, then the appropriate state or federal court will decide the claim.”  (Prevost Decl., Ex. J, p. 56.)  In plain language, this provision broadly requires the arbitrator to decide gateway issues of arbitrability, including whether a dispute is “covered by this Agreement.” 

Plaintiff does not demonstrate any basis for revoking the above provisions.  The party opposing arbitration “ ‘ “bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” ’ ”  (Kader v. Southern California Medical Center, Inc. (2024) 99 Cal.App.5th 214, 220-221.)  Plaintiff does not argue the delegation clause (or any part of the agreement) is revocable.  Plaintiff’s opposition does not address defendant’s delegation argument.

Disposition

            Defendant Propark America West, LLC’s motion to compel arbitration of plaintiff’s individual PAGA claim and stay representative PAGA claims is granted.  The court hereby orders plaintiff Nancy Benavides to arbitrate her disputes with defendant, including gateway issues of arbitrability and the scope of the arbitration agreement, but not including plaintiff’s representative PAGA claims.  The court hereby stays the entire action pending resolution of the arbitration proceeding.
