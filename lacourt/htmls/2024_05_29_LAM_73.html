DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV17794    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: 73

5/26/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

DAVIDOFF v. OROJENILAK, et al. (21STCV17794) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Armen Shaghzo (Shaghzo and Shaghzo Law Firm, LP) 

Counsel for Defendant Ryan Orojenilak: Mason Yost, Esq.

 

(1)   REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT AGAINST DEFENDANT RYAN OROJENILAK (filed 3/05/2024) 

(2)   APPLICATION TO SERVE METCO GROUP, INC. THROUGH SECRETARY OF STATE (filed 5/10/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment against Defendant Ryan Orojenilak is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s application to serve Defendant Metco Group, Inc. through the Secretary of State is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to serve the summons on the Secretary of State for Defendant Metco Group, Inc. 

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 12, 2021, Plaintiff Claidin Davidoff the instant action against Defendants Ryan Orojenilak and Metco Group, Inc. (collectively “Defendants”). Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Partnership Agreement

2.      Abuse of Legal Process

3.      Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

4.      Fraud

5.      Unjust Enrichment

6.      Promissory Estoppel

 

The FAC alleges that in or around April 2019, Plaintiff and Defendants, entered into an oral agreement to start a partnership or in the alternative a joint venture in which the Parties would obtain and perform construction work together and share the profits. (FAC, ¶ 12.) Both parties agreed that the profit from each construction work would be divided equally amongst themselves. (FAC, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants did not perform according to the Partnership Agreement and Plaintiff did not receive any profit from any of the four projects that he completed with Defendants. (FAC, ¶ 19.) The FAC further alleges that after multiple attempts by Plaintiff to recover his share of the profits from Defendants, Defendants informed Plaintiff that he may take Defendants' tools stored in a storage unit, as a form of payment towards Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 21.) However, Defendants contacted the police and made a false report that Plaintiff was stealing the tools from Defendants and Plaintiff was arrested and charged. (FAC, ¶¶ 25-28.) Plaintiff alleges Metco Group, Inc., is a California Corporation, registered to Orojenilak, who is its sole owner. (FAC, ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

On March 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a request for entry of default judgment against Defendant Orojenilak. Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $73,933.53, consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $51,030.53, $22,288.00 in interest, and $615.00 in costs. 

 

On May 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed an application and order to serve Metco Group, Inc. through the Secretary of State.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Request for Entry of Default Judgment

 

a.       Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

b.      Application

B.      Application and Order to Serve Metco Group, Inc. through the Secretary of State

 

a.       Legal Standard

 

California Corporations Code § 1702(a) provides:

 

(a) If an agent for the purpose of service of process has resigned and has not been replaced or if the agent designated cannot with reasonable diligence be found at the address designated for personally delivering the process, or if no agent has been designated, and it is shown by affidavit to the satisfaction of the court that process against a domestic corporation cannot be served with reasonable diligence upon the designated agent by hand in the manner provided in Section 415.10, subdivision (a) of Section 415.20 or subdivision (a) of Section 415.30 of the Code of Civil Procedure or upon the corporation in the manner provided in subdivision (a), (b), or (c) of Section 416.10 or subdivision (a) of Section 416.20 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the court may make an order that the service be made upon the corporation by delivering by hand to the Secretary of State, or to any person employed in the Secretary of State's office in the capacity of assistant or deputy, one copy of the process for each defendant to be served, together with a copy of the order authorizing such service. I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Eric Kaufman brings this action for declaratory relief against Defendants Lockton Investment Advisors, LLC ("LIA"), Lockton Investment Securities, LLC, f/k/a Lockton Financial Advisors, LLC ("LIS"), and Lockton Companies, LLC - Pacific Series ("Pacific Series", collectively "Lockton").

 

LIA provides investment advisory and related services, LIS buys and sells securities as a registered broker dealer, and Pacific Series provides insurance and risk management services. (FAC, ¶ 10.) Kaufman became a producer member of LIA in approximately July 2007 and a producer member in LIS and Pacific Series in approximately July 2008. (FAC, ¶ 23.) To become a producer member of LIA, LIS, and Pacific Services, Kaufman paid a $200 capital contribution for a single "Producer Unit" in each entity. (FAC, ¶ 23.) As of May 1, 2016, Plaintiff entered into an “Amended and Restated Member Agreement" with Pacific Series and with LIS and, as of May 1, 2016, a "Second Amended and Restated Member Agreement” with LIA (collectively the "Member Agreements"). (FAC, ¶ 16.)

 

The Member Agreements include nonsolicitation covenants. (FAC, ¶ 20.) The Member Agreements include Missouri choice of law and Missouri forum selection clauses. The Member Agreements require Kaufman to adjudicate disputes in the federal court in Kansas City, Missouri or in the Circuit Court of Jackson County, Missouri. (FAC, ¶ 18.) he Member Agreements for LIA, LIS, and Pacific Series require Plaintiff to provide thirty days written notice of termination. (FAC, ¶ 22.)

 

Kaufman withdrew from and terminated his membership in LIA, LIS, and Pacific Series on July 18, 2022. (FAC, ¶ 27.) Kaufman subsequently accepted employment with Alliant, a California-based competitor of Lockton. (FAC, ¶ 28.) At Alliant, Kaufman will provide products and services that are directly competitive with the products or services of Lockton. (Ibid.) 

In the first amended complaint, Kaufman asserts a single cause of action for declaratory relief, seeking a judicial declaration that the nonsolicitation covenants, Missouri choice of law and forum selection clauses, and notice requirements are void and unenforceable as a violation of Business and Professions Code Sections 16600 and California public policy. Kaufman alleges: Plaintiff desires a judicial declaration that the enforcement of the Missouri Choice of Law and Missouri Forum Section Provisions would violate California's fundamental public policy in favor of workplace mobility and competition. Plaintiff also desires a judicial declaration that the Notice Provision, Member Agreements Non-Competition Covenants and the December 2021 Non-Competition Covenants are facially void under Business and Professions Code Section 16600 and California public policy in favor of workplace mobility and open competition are thereby unenforceable as to Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 40.) 

 

Lockton moved to dismiss Kaufman's complaint and/or stay this action on grounds that the Missouri forum selection clause is mandatory. On December 13, 2023, the Court denied Lockton’s motion to dismiss.

 

A.     Lockton’s Motion for Reconsideration

 

On December 29, 2023, Lockton filed the instant Motion for Reconsideration, arguing:

·       On December 18, in an action in federal court in Missouri involving the same parties, the court entered a final judgment that the same forum-selection clause the Court declined to enforce in its order “requir[es] that any dispute between Lockton and Kaufman be litigated in Missouri” (11.14.2023 Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. 1 at p. 4) and is “valid and enforceable” as a matter of federal law (12.28.23 Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. 1 at p. 5). The final judgment in Missouri warrants reconsideration of the Court’s order denying Defendants’ motion to dismiss and dismissal of this action. (Superior Motels, Inc. v. Rinn Motor Hotels, Inc. (1987) 195 Cal.App.3d 1032, 1057 [“Full faith and credit must be given to a final order or judgment of a federal court.”].)

·       In the alternative, Lockton requests the Court revoke the order and a stay this action. On December 13, the California Supreme Court granted Lockton’s petition for review in the writ proceedings stemming from Lockton Companies v. Giblin, L.A. Superior Court Case No. 22STCV39876, which involves the same forum-selection clause and contracts as this case, as well as the same counsel and virtually all of the same arguments for and against dismissal on grounds of forum non conveniens. (12.28.23 Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. 2.) It would conserve judicial and party resources to await the Supreme Court’s decision in Giblin, as the upshot of that decision may be that this case doesn’t belong in California in the first place.

 

In opposition, Kaufman argues:

·       Lockton’s motion is not ripe

o   The Court should not grant Lockton’s motion—or at least delay ruling on it. This Court should not rule on Lockton’s instant motion until the Supreme Court acts on the order Lockton seeks to negate by its instant motion.

·       The Court should exercise its discretion not to dismiss or stay this action even if the Missouri Ruling has preclusive effect.

 

Lockton filed a reply, arguing:

·       The Missouri federal court’s final judgment conclusively establishes, as matter of federal law, that the forum-selection clause Eric Kaufman agreed to is mandatory, exclusive, valid, and enforceable. The Court does not have discretion to refuse full faith and credit.

·       Federal law determines the preclusive effect of the Missouri federal court’s final judgment.

o   All of Kaufman’s arguments in opposition rest on this Court applying California’s res judicata standard (rather than the federal one) to give the Missouri federal court’s judgment less faith and credit than the judgment would receive in Missouri federal court. (Opp. 5-15.) Kaufman is wrong because federal law, not California law, applies here.

 

 

B.     The Parties Motions to Seal

On March 1, 2024, Lockton filed a Motion to Conditionally Seal Excerpts of Paul Moore Deposition Transcript and Reply in Support of Lockton’s Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Dates. No opposition was filed.

On April 18, 2024, Kaufman filed a notice of Non-Opposition to Lockton’s Motion.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Lockton’s Motion for Reconsideration

 

a.     Review by a Different Judge

 

As an initial matter, the Court addresses whether a judge who did not make the initial ruling can consider this motion.

 

Generally, a motion for reconsideration must be made to the same judge.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a); Marriage of Oliverez (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1248.)  “However, there are narrow exceptions to this general rule.”  (Marriage of Oliverez, supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at 1248.)  “‘[W]here the judge who made the initial ruling is unavailable to reconsider the motion, a different judge may entertain the reconsideration motion.’”  (Id. (quoting Ziller Electronics Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1232) (alteration in original).)  “Another exception is when the facts have changed or when the judge has considered further evidence and law.”  (Id.)  “Additionally, a second judge may reverse a prior ruling of another judge if the record shows that it was based on inadvertence, mistake, or fraud.”  (Id. at 1248-49.)  “Mere disagreement . . . with the prior trial judge’s ruling, however, is not enough to overturn that ruling.”  (Id. at 1249.)   

 

Here, Lockton’s motion moves for reconsideration of the December 13, 2024 order made in this Department, Department 73, by Judge Timothy Dillon. However, Judge Dillon is unavailable to hear the instant Motion due to his temporary reassignment to another Department. Therefore, the Court finds that the assigned judge to Department 73, Judge Rolf Treu, may consider this motion.

 

b.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

Lockton requests judicial notice of the following:

1.     The December 18, 2023 order (ECF No. 178) granting plaintiff’s motion for certification of partial summary judgment under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(b) in Lockton Companies, LLC – Pacific Series, et al. v. Kaufman, No. 4:22-cv-00462- SRB in the United States District Court for the Western District of Missouri.

2.     The December 13, 2023 order granting Lockton’s petition for review in Lockton Companies, LLC, et al. v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County, No. S282136 in the Supreme Court of California.

3.     The First Amended Complaint filed on October 27, 2023 in Lockton Companies, LLC – Pacific Series et al. v. Alliant Ins. Servs., Barnes, Canales, Racunas, and Roderick, Case No. 4:23-cv-00705-SRB, filed in the Western District of Missouri.

4.     Lockton’s May 3, 2023 Petition for Peremptory Writ of Mandate, Prohibition, or Other Extraordinary Relief in Lockton Companies, LLC, et al. v. Superior Court of the State of California, County of Los Angeles; Sallie Giblin, No. B328408 filed in the Court of Appeal of California, Second Appellate District.

5.     Lockton’s October 5, 2023 Petition for Review filed in Lockton Companies, LLC, et al. v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County, No. S282136 in the Supreme Court of California.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)

 

The Court grants all of Lockton’s requests.

 

c.      Motion for Reconsideration

 

The moving party must present new facts, circumstances or law in order to grant a motion for reconsideration.  (See CCP § 1008(a); see also Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.)  The party seeking reconsideration of an order shall state by affidavit what application was made before, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts or circumstances are claimed to be shown.  (CCP § 1008(a).)  Further, “…the party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457 [emphasis added].)  The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.) 

 

Lockton moves for reconsideration of the Court’s December 13, 2023, order denying its motion to dismiss because of subsequent legal developments in California and Missouri that occurred after the Court issued its order.

 

On the same day that this Court issued its order denying Lockton’s motion to dismiss, the California Supreme Court issued an order granting Lockton’s petition for review in Lockton Companies v. Superior Court (Sallie Giblin). In Giblin, the California Supreme Court is reviewing the enforceability of the same forum-selection clause in a case involving the same counsel, the same operative Lockton LLC Member Agreements and contractual provisions, and virtually all of the same arguments for and against dismissal on grounds of forum non conveniens. (12.28.23 Request for Judicial Notice, Exs. 2, 4-5.)

 

Further, on December 18, 2023, the federal court in Missouri entered a final judgment that the same forum-selection clause at issue here is “valid and enforceable” as a matter of federal law. (12.28.23 Request for Judicial Notice, Ex 1 at p. 5.) Lockton asserts that the final judgment in Missouri warrants this Court to grant the motion to reconsider and dismiss Kaufman’s complaint on the ground of forum non conveniens. “Full faith and credit must be accorded a judgment of the federal court. . . . Such a judgment has the same effect in the courts of this state as it would in a federal court.” (Smith v. Walter E. Heller & Co. (1978) 82 Cal.App.3d 259 271-272.)

 

While the parties dispute as to whether the Missouri federal court’s final ruling preclude Kaufman from continuing to litigate this action, the Court declines to grant Lockton’s motion for reconsideration and to enter an order granting Lockton’s motion to dismiss this case. Instead, the Court stays the proceedings in this case pending resolution of the writ proceedings in Giblin, since the California Supreme Court will decide the enforceability of the exact same forum-selection clause at issue in Lockton’s motion to dismiss in this case. As Lockton asserts in its motion, this would be in the interests of the parties and the Court which would both be spared the time and resources required to adjudicate issues that the California Supreme Court has already agreed to resolve.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds a stay in the proceedings in this case is warranted pending the resolution of the writ proceedings in Lockton Companies v. Superior Court (Sallie Giblin).

 

B.     Motions to Conditionally Seal

 

“Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.550(c).) However, a party may move to seal records pursuant to California Rules of Court, rules 2.550-2.551. “A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record. The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b).) To grant an order pursuant to California Rules of Court, rules 2.550 and 2.551, the Court must expressly find facts that establish:  

 

(1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; 

(2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; 

(3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; 

(4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and 

(5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest. 

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  

 

On February 27, 2024, Kaufman filed a Motion to Conditionally Seal Documents Submitted in his Opposition to Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Dates.

 

On March 1, 2024, Lockton filed a Motion to Conditionally Seal Excerpts of Paul Moore Deposition Transcript and Reply in Support of Lockton’s Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Dates.

 

On March 8, 2024, Lockton filed a Motion to Seal: (1) Kaufman’s Opposition to Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Dates; (2) Exhibit 1 to Gerber Declaration in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition; and (3) Exhibit 2 to Gerber Declaration in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition. Kaufman filed a notice of Non-Opposition to Lockton’s Motion.

 

The parties request the Court seal these documents pursuant to the April 19, 2023 Protective Order entered by the court in Lockton Cos., LLC, et al. v. Kaufman, Case No. 4:22- cv-00461-SRB (W.D. Mo.). (Supplemental Declaration of Russell H. Falconer, Ex. A). The April 19, 2023 Protective Order provides that “[a] Party seeking to make a Confidential Filing must comply with the provisions of . . . the presiding California Court[] in filing a document under seal.” (Supp. Falconer Decl., Ex. A ¶ 14.) Under Rule 2.551(b)(3), a “party that files or intends to file with the court, for the purposes of adjudication or to use at trial, records produced in discovery that are subject to a confidentiality agreement or protective order, and does not intend to request to have the records sealed, must:

 

(i) Lodge the unredacted records subject to the confidentiality agreement or protective order and any pleadings, memorandums, declarations, and other documents that disclose the contents of the records, in the manner stated in (d) [below];

(ii) File copies of the documents in (i) that are redacted so that they do not disclose the contents of the records that are subject to the confidentiality agreement or protective order; and

(iii) Give written notice to the party that produced the records that the records and the other documents lodged under (i) will be placed in the public court file unless that party files a timely motion or application to seal the records under this rule.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(3).)

 

Subdivision (d) of Rule 2.551 provides in pertinent part that a record that may be filed under seal “must be put in an envelope or other appropriate container, sealed in the envelope or container,” marked “CONDITIONALLY UNDER SEAL” and lodged with the Court. (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.551(d).) Further, the party submitting the lodged record must affix to the envelope or container a cover sheet that contains “all the information required on a caption page under rule 2.111;” and “[s]tates that the enclosed record is subject to a motion or an application to file the record under seal.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Lockton requests the Court conditionally file excerpts of the of Paul Moore Deposition Transcript and Lockton’s Reply in Support of Lockton’s Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Dates, under seal until Plaintiff has had the opportunity to move to seal these materials. The operative Protective Order states that a producing party “may designate any Discovery Material as ‘Highly Confidential-AEO’ … if such Producing Party in good faith and reasonably believes that disclosure of the Discovery Material … is substantially likely to cause injury to the Producing Party.” (Supp. Falconer Decl., Ex. A ¶ 1.) Lockton asserts the Deposition Transcript of Paul Moore (taken February 8, 2024) qualifies as a “Discovery Material” and was designated AEO. (Supp. Falconer Decl., Ex. G, 302:7-14). Defendants’ Reply in Support of Defendants’ Motion to Continue the Trial Date (at p. 10) references excerpts from Mr. Moore’s February 8, 2024 Deposition Transcript.

 

Lockton also moves the Court to Seal: (1) Kaufman’s Opposition to Motion to Continue Trial Date and Related Dates; (2) Exhibit 1 to Gerber Declaration in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition; and (3) Exhibit 2 to Gerber Declaration in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition. Lockton asserts the documents should be sealed because they do not involve records to be used at trial or as a basis of adjudication of substantive issues in this case. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332.) Thus, the Court may seal these records without analyzing the sealing factors under the Rules of Court. Lockton argues that further, the documents should be sealed because they include highly confidential and confidential Lockton information, including client names, client retention rates, profit information, and Lockton’s business processes and strategy upon a producer’s departure from Lockton, the disclosure of which would cause substantial competitive harm to Lockton and no less restrictive means exist to prevent this harm. (Skinner Decl. ¶¶ 4-5.) On February 27, 2024, Kaufman filed an application to conditionally seal the same documents.

 

The parties do not dispute that the documents should be sealed. Based on the foregoing, the Court grants the parties request.

SMITH, et al. v. GENERAL MOTORS, LLC (23STCV07569)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  David Barry (The Barry Law Firm)

Counsel for Defendant/ opposing party: Mary McBride (Erskine Law Group, PC)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER ANSWERS TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES (filed on 04/02/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 14 and 42 is GRANTED. Defendant shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 12, 2021, Plaintiffs Greg Smith and Lorraine Smith brought this action against Defendant General Motors, LLC. On August 23, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 4 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability under The Song-Beverly Act; (2) Breach of Express Warranty under The Song-Beverly Act; (3) Breach of Express Warranty under California Commercial Code§ 2-313; and (4) Violation of The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

 

This action stems from Plaintiffs’ purchase of a 2018 Chevrolet Camaro LT, VIN 1G1FB3DSXJ0116379 (“Subject Vehicle” or Vehicle”). The Vehicle was manufactured and warranted by Defendant. Plaintiffs allege that after persistent manufacturing defects, Plaintiffs brought the vehicle in for repairs to Defendant’s authorized dealership(s) who were unable to repair the defects and Defendant refused to buy back the vehicle. Plaintiffs’ lawsuit alleges breach of express and implied warranties under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, and request that the subject vehicle be repurchased, that Plaintiffs be awarded appropriate damages and attorneys’ fees as allowed by statute. Plaintiffs also seek a statutory civil penalty for Defendant GM’s bad faith failure to repurchase Plaintiffs’ vehicle as required by the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Answers to Special Interrogatories Nos. 14 and 42, arguing:

·       On February 9, 2024, this Court ordered Defendant to provide code-compliant responses to Special Interrogatories 14 and 42. Plaintiffs argue that Defendant still has not provided code-compliant responses to these interrogatories.

·       Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s response to Special Interrogatory 14 is not fully compliant because Defendant does not provide the names of all the service technicians  

·       Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s response to Special Interrogatory 42 is evasive and nonresponsive. No information particularized to the investigation of the Subject Vehicle has been provided in Defendant’s response.

·       Plaintiffs request that the court impose a monetary sanction in the amount of $6,022.50

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs did not meet and confer in good faith

·       Defendant provided code compliant responses

·       Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions should be denied

 

Plaintiffs filed a reply arguing:

·       Plaintiffs made several attempts to meet and confer

·       Defendant must provide further responses

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.) 

 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiffs did not meet and confer in good faith because they “never made an attempt to substantively understand and address [Defendant’s] concerns in this case.” (Oppos., p. 3.)

 

However, the Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been met. Plaintiffs’ counsel, Brian Kim (“Kim”), sets forth meet and confer efforts prior to filing the instant motion. (Kim Decl., ¶¶ 7-11; Ex. 4-5.) Kim states he sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter highlighting deficiencies within Defendant’s discovery responses. (Ibid.) The parties then conferred telephonically on March 8, 2024 and March 14, 2024. (Ibid.) However, Kim states the parties arrived at an impasse and could not come to an agreement regarding the supplementation of Defendant’s responses. (Ibid.) Kim sent Defendant’s another meet and confer email and attempted to telephonically confer again, but did not receive a response. (Ibid.) The Court finds Plaintiffs’ meet and confer attempts are sufficient.

 

B.     Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”¿ CCP § 2030.010(a).¿ “Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.”¿ CCP § 2030.260(a).¿ 
¿ 

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered. (2) An exercise of the party's option to produce writings. (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.”¿ CCP § 2030.210(a).¿ “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”¿ CCP § 2030.220(c).¿ 

¿ 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).¿ 

¿ 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(c).¿“[T]he time limitations set forth in section 2030 and section 2034, subdivision (a) [are] mandatory and [when] not met, the trial court's order is in excess of its jurisdiction.”¿(Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)

 

a.     Special Interrogatory No. 14

 

Plaintiffs argue that a further response to Special Interrogatory No. 14, must be compelled. Special Interrogatory No. 14 requests the following:  

 

“IDENTIFY all PERSONS who performed warranty repairs upon the SUBJECT VEHICLE.”

 

In response, Defendant provided the names and employee ID numbers of Service Advisors and Technicians with M. K. Smith Chevrolet. Defendant also provided the names and employee ID numbers of Service Advisors with Reynolds Buick GMC. However, Defendant did not provide the names of the Technicians. Defendant only provided the employee ID numbers of the Technicians with Reynolds Buick GMC. Defendant contends it does not have knowledge of third-party information for dealerships and any call centers that are not its agents.

 

Plaintiffs assert that Defendant’s response is not complete. The Court agrees. Defendant provided the requested information as it pertains to M. K. Smith Chevrolet, but provided an incomplete response as to Reynolds Buick GMC. Code Civil of Procedure section 2030.220(c) provides: “[i]f the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” Given Defendant’s relationship with its authorized repair facilities, Defendant has not demonstrated that the subject information is “equally available” to Plaintiffs and Defendant has not shown that it could not have obtained the names of the technicians. Further, the technicians are percipient witnesses since they are likely to have information regarding Plaintiffs’ vehicle and the nature and extent of repairs in varying detail. Plaintiffs have a right to know who they are in order to determine if they should be deposed. The Court finds that Plaintiffs have shown good cause to compel further responses.  

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatory No. 14 is GRANTED.

 

a.     Special Interrogatory No. 42

 

Plaintiffs argue that a further response to Special Interrogatory No. 42, must be compelled. Special Interrogatory No. 42 requests the following:  

 

“Explain with particularity all aspects of YOUR investigation into whether the SUBJECT VEHICLE qualified or was eligible for repurchase or replacement pursuant to The Song-Beverly Warranty Act.”

 

Defendant responded by stating “How the individuals who determine whether a vehicle qualifies for a repurchase or replacement under Song-Beverly depends upon information provided by the customer. For customers who contact GM and request a repurchase, the vehicle-specific information in GM’s possession will be analyzed, including the vehicle’s Global Warranty History Report and any Service Request Activity Report(s). Additionally, it may include documents from the customer, including a copy of the vehicle purchase contract and repair orders. Based upon the vehicle specific information in GM’s possession, plus documents that the customer provides, if any, GM will assess whether the vehicle qualifies for a repurchase or replacement under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.”

 

Defendant contends its response is sufficient because it provided Plaintiffs with the process undertaken in every investigation into whether a vehicle qualifies for a repurchase or replacement.

 

However, the Court does not find Defendant’s response is code compliant. Defendant did not provide any information as to what was done to investigate Plaintiff’s particular Subject Vehicle and whether it qualified or was eligible for a replacement or repurchase. The information is discoverable because it goes directly to an issue in the case and Defendant's affirmative defense that its denial to repurchase Plaintiff’s vehicle was in good faith.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatory No. 42 is GRANTED.

 

C.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

The Court notes that Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions against Defendant’s counsel is defective. A request for sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. (Sole Energy Co. v. Hodges (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 199, 207.) Here, the notice of motion does not specifically identify against whom the sanctions are sought. (Notice of Motion, p. 2.) Plaintiff merely requests “for monetary sanctions for Defendant’s failure to abide by the requirements of the discovery act to date.” (Ibid.) Thus, the Court will not impose sanctions. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 14 and 42 is GRANTED. Defendant shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions.
