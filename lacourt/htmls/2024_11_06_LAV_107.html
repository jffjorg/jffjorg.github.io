DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01883    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

THERESA VALENCIA,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

DEVONSHIRE ENTERPRISES, INC., et al.

Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  23VECV01883            107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Motion to Compel Deposition Questions  

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND

This is a premises liability action. On or about May 8, 2021, Plaintiff Theresa Valencia (“Plaintiff”) was visiting a restaurant, House of Galbi (the “Subject Premises”). (Complaint ¶ 7.) Plaintiff sustained injuries after she tripped and fell in the Subject Premises’ parking lot. (Complaint ¶¶ 7-8.) On May 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Devonshire Enterprises, Inc. dba House of Galbi, G.F.C. Encino Associates II, LLC, and Does 1 through 100, alleging causes of action for premises liability and general negligence. (Complaint p. 1.)

 

On September 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel percipient witness Yun Hwan Kim to answer deposition questions (the “Motion”). On October 24, 2024, Defendants Devonshire Enterprises, Inc. dba House of Galbi & G.F.C. Encino Associates, II, LLC (“Defendants”) filed an opposition. Plaintiff filed a reply on October 29, 2024.

 

II.             PROCEDURE

 

a.     Timeliness

“If a deponent fails to answer any question . . . that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480(a).) The “motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480(b).)

 

Plaintiff deposed Percipient Witness Yun Hwan Kim on September 9, 2024. (Eli Declaration ¶ 16.) Plaintiff filed the Motion on September 24, 2024. September 24, 2024, is within sixty days of September 9, 2024. Accordingly, Plaintiff timely filed the Motion.

b.     Meet and Confer

A motion to compel answers “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel submits a declaration stating counsel met and conferred with Defendants’ counsel on September 9, 2024. (Eli Declaration ¶ 18.) Plaintiff’s counsel states “defense counsel remained firm on refusing to permit her client to answer the relevant deposition questions and acknowledged on the record during Witness Kim’s deposition that I will be filing a Motion to Compel the depositions questions at issue.” (Eli Declaration ¶ 19.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff did not adequately meet and confer before filing the Motion. (Opposition p. 4.) Defendants state “Plaintiff’s purported meet and confer on the record lasted less than one minute and consisted of nothing more than asking why defense counsel believed the attorney client privilege was implicated and indicating she disagreed. Plaintiff did not explain why she disagreed”, “[s]he did not provide any authority in support of her positions” and “[s]he did not make any effort to negotiate an informal resolution.” (Opposition p. 5.) Defendants’ counsel submits a declaration stating Defendants’ counsel “reached out to Plaintiff via email asking her to meet and confer over the phone to attempt to resolve the issues informally” but “Plaintiff’s counsel declined, and instead replied that she only wanted to know if we would produce Mr. Kim for deposition once again.” (Guzman Declaration ¶ 14. Ex. D.)

 

There is no “absolute rule requiring that informal resolution must always await the conclusion of a deposition” and the court “leave[s] it to the parties to determine the proper time, manner, and place for such discussion.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1438.)  However, “the statute requires that there be a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution.” (Id.) “Argument is not the same as informal negotiation” and “debate over the appropriateness of an objection, interspersed between rounds of further interrogation, does not . . . constitute an earnest attempt to resolve impasses in discovery.” (Id. at pp. 1437-1438.) “A reasonable and good-faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with deponent’s counsel at a deposition. Rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.”  (Id. at p. 1439.)

 

Plaintiff’s only purported meet and confer efforts occurred during the deposition. (Ex. D; Eli Declaration ¶ 19.) At the deposition, Plaintiff’s counsel asked Defendants’ counsel about the grounds for Defendants’ counsel’s objection to Plaintiff’s questions. (Ex. D p. 10:14-21.) Later, Plaintiff’s counsel asked Defendants’ counsel if they were “still maintaining [the] objection based off of attorney-client privilege.” (Ex. D p. 76:21-23.) Plaintiff’s counsel then stated, as the deposition, “I met and conferred with [Defendants’ counsel] to say that I do not believe that that is attorney-client communications as to when he was retained. You feel differently. So that’s my meet and confer. I do intend to file a motion to compel this deposition question. So pending the Court’s ruling on our motion to compel, I do reserve the right to bring you back to ask that question if the Court approves that.” (Ex. D p. 77:5-13.) Plaintiff’s counsel also stated they intended to file a motion to compel the question relating to when the witness called the attorneys. (Ex. D pp. 77:20-79:2.)

 

This court finds that Plaintiff’s counsel’s inquiry at the deposition as to the basis for Defendants’ counsel’s objection did not constitute a “serious effort at negotiation [or] informal resolution.” (Townsend v. Superior Court, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at p. 1438.) “[T]he law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.”  (Id. at p. 1439.) That did not occur here.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s counsel did not satisfy section 2025.480’s meet and confer requirement. The Motion is denied.

I.                BACKGROUND

 

This is an employment dispute. (Complaint ¶ 1.) On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff Norma Jimenez filed a complaint against A & V Enterprises, Inc. dba Superior Maintenance, Don Arsenault, and Does 1-50, alleging twenty-seven causes of action.

 

On October 10, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel (“Counsel”) filed a motion to be relieved as counsel (the “Motion”). No opposition has been filed.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 284(2).)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.     Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-051));

 

2.     A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-052));

 

3.     Service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on all other parties who have appeared in the case, and

 

4.     The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) “The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.” (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 554, 559.)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

(A) Notice—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)

 

“A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-051).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a).)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

(B) Declaration—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)

 

“The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(c).)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement by explaining on Form MC-052 that “Plaintiff exhibited a consistent lack of effective communication regarding the case, including withholding pertinent information and displaying erratic and inappropriate behavior. Despite discussions addressing these concerns, Plaintiff failed to make the necessary changes. Ultimately, Plaintiff requested that we cease phone communications and transition to email correspondence. We made multiple attempts to explain the MC-050 form and its implications, but Plaintiff did not sign it despite our multiple and continuous efforts to clarify the form.”

 

(C) Service—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d); Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, Rule 4.35

 

“The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service, electronic service, or mail.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).)

 

“If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

1.     The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

 

2.     The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.”

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d)(1).)

 

“As used in this rule, ‘current’ means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned or no electronic delivery failure message was received is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011(b) applies.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).)

 

“When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40.” (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 4.35.)

 

The Motion does not comply with this requirement. Counsel states in the declaration that Counsel “served the client by mail at the client’s last known address with copies of the motion papers served with this declaration” and counsel “confirmed within the last 30 days that the address is current . . . by mail, return receipt requested.” (MC-052 p. 2.) However, the notice of motion, motion, declaration, and proposed order must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in this case. Counsel’s declaration does not indicate the documents were served on any parties other than Plaintiff. Moreover, Counsel submitted no proposed order that would have been served on Plaintiff.

 

(D) Proposed Order—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)

 

“The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

 

This requirement is not satisfied. Counsel filed no proposed order with this court.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies the motion.
