DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV28184    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: B

HEARING DATE:    Fri., July 12, 2024                               JUDGE /DEPT:        Moskowitz/Dept. B

CASE NAME:           Juan Ramon Fuentes Hernandez. v. Manuel DeJesus Puga et al

CASE NUMBER:     19STCV28184

PROCEEDINGS:     MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants, DNELEVETS, INC. and MANUEL DEJESUS PUGA

RESP. PARTY:         None

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND TO FILE FIRST AMENDED ANSWER

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            The Court GRANTS the motion for leave to amend to allow Defendants to file a First Amended Answer. Defendants are ordered to file the First Amended Answer within 20 days of this order.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

            Plaintiff Juan Ramon Fuentes Hernandez filed the instant lawsuit alleging causes of action for negligence and negligence per se on August 9, 2019. On October 24, 2019, Defendants filed their Answer.

             On May 23, 2024, Defendants filed their Motion for Leave to Amend to File First Amended Answer.

 

            On June 7, 2024, the matter was assigned to Judge Karen Moskowitz in Department B of the Van Nuys Courthouse East.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

            “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(a)(1).) “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) Specifically, there is a right to amend “to correct inadvertent misstatements of facts or erroneous allegations of terms.” (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.)

 

            “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)¿ The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)¿

 

            Discussion

           

            Defendants seek to amend their Answer to include Proposition 213, Last Clear Chance, and reasonable and necessary medical expenses affirmative defenses.

 

            As a preliminary matter, the Court notes Defendants failed to include the courthouse address in the Notice of Motion. (Cal. Rul. of Court 3.1110 (b)(1).) Therefore, Plaintiff was not properly noticed. However, the case was reassigned on June 7, 2024, and notice was sent to Plaintiff on June 7, 2024, and June 18, 2024. As of July 10, 2024, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

            Here, Defendants have complied with California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324. (Hughes Decl. ¶¶ 3-8.) Moreover, Defendants provided a copy of the proposed First Amended Answer. (Id. at ¶ 14; Ex. 1.) Defendants would be severely and irreparably harmed if they are not allowed to file their First Amended Answer to assert primary and meritorious affirmative defenses in this matter. (Id.  at ¶ 13.) Notably, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition showing that he would be prejudiced by the amendment. Therefore, the Court grants the motion.

           

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

            The Court grants the Motion for Leave to Amend. Defendants are ordered to file the First Amended Answer within 20 days of this order.

Moving Party:             Defendant Alicia Rivera

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Araceli Sanchez

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 1, 2019, Plaintiff, Araceli Sanchez (“Plaintiff”), filed this action against Defendant, Alicia Rivera (“Defendant”) for damages arising from a motor vehicle accident.

 

Defendant now moves for the Court to impose terminating, evidentiary, issue sanctions, and /or monetary sanctions against Plaintiff on the grounds that Plaintiff has violated  prior court orders concerning her neurological examination, failed to provide documents relating to a subsequent September 4, 2018 accident (presumedly the result of spoliation of evidence), engaged in other forms of discovery abuse, and failed to pay monetary sanctions. Plaintiff opposes.

 

Trial is set for July 23, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

As a preliminary matter, the court finds that the instant motion is untimely. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.020, “any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.” In this instance, trial is set for July 23, 2024. Therefore, any motion concerning discovery had to have been heard on or before July 8, 2024.

 

In her reply, Defendant claims that the instant motion is not a discovery motion. (Reply at pg. 5.) However, this argument is not persuasive because Defendant requests the Court to issue sanctions due to Plaintiff’s purported discovery abuses. Consequently, the statutory deadline set forth under Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.020 applies. Such discovery deadlines are jurisdictional. (See Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.4th 1403, 1409.) Defendant could have either sought to have the instant hearing advanced or moved pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.050 to have this matter heard, but Defendant chose to do neither.

 

Moreover, Defendant insists that the deadline pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.030 should apply because Plaintiff obstructed Defendant’s medical expert from completing the medical examination, rendering the matter an expert witness issue. (Reply at pg. 5.) This argument is also not persuasive because the sanctions that Defendant seeks goes beyond expert discovery, and it seeks to curtail Plaintiff’s ability to admit certain evidence into the record.

 

Accordingly, because the instant motion is untimely, the motion is denied.

ANALYSIS:

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.     Form and Special Interrogatories

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260(a).)  If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).)  Once compelled to respond, the party waives the right to make any objections, including ones based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020(a), 2030.290.)  No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery.  (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

On November 10, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with Form Interrogatories (Set One).  (Buehler Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff did not serve any responses or request an extension.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  Defense counsel attempted to resolve the issue informally by sending Plaintiff’s counsel an email and requesting responses to the discovery on or before February 29, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. B.)  In response, Plaintiff’s counsel’s office requested a 30-day extension, which was granted.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6-8, Exs. C-D.)  Plaintiff did not provide any responses, thus, defense counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel again on March 26 and April 1, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10, Exs. E-F.)  Defense counsel has also followed up regarding the discovery request by phone, but has not received any responses to these requests.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 11-12.)  The Court finds that Defendant has demonstrated that he has propounded form interrogatories on Plaintiff and Plaintiff has not provided any responses.  Although Defendant was not obligated to meet and confer with Plaintiff, defense counsel has attempted to resolve the issue informally.  Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One).

 

Furthermore, on November 10, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with Special Interrogatories (Set One).  (Buehler Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff did not serve any responses or request an extension.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  Defense counsel attempted to resolve the issue informally by sending Plaintiff’s counsel an email and requesting responses to the discovery on or before February 29, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. B.)  In response, Plaintiff’s counsel’s office requested a 30-day extension, which was granted.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6-8, Exs. C-D.)  Plaintiff did not provide any responses, thus, defense counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel again on March 26 and April 1, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10, Exs. E-F.)  Defense counsel has also followed up regarding the discovery request by phone, but has not received any responses to these requests.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 11-12.)  The Court finds that Defendant has demonstrated that he has propounded special interrogatories on Plaintiff and Plaintiff has not provided any responses.  Although Defendant was not obligated to meet and confer with Plaintiff, defense counsel has attempted to resolve the issue informally.  Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One).

 

B.     Requests for Production

 

A party must respond to requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)  If a party to whom requests for production of documents is directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c).)  The party also waives the right to make any objections, including ones based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2024.020(a), 2031.300.)  No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

On November 10, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with Demand for Production of Documents (Set One).  (Buehler Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff did not serve any responses or request an extension.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  Defense counsel attempted to resolve the issue informally by sending Plaintiff’s counsel an email and requesting responses to the discovery on or before February 29, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. B.)  In response, Plaintiff’s counsel’s office requested a 30-day extension, which was granted.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6-8, Exs. C-D.)  Plaintiff did not provide any responses, thus, defense counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel again on March 26 and April 1, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10, Exs. E-F.)  Defense counsel has also followed up regarding the discovery request by phone, but has not received any responses to these requests.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 11-12.)  The Court finds that Defendant has demonstrated that he has propounded a demand for production of documents on Plaintiff and Plaintiff has not provided any responses.  Although Defendant was not obligated to meet and confer with Plaintiff, defense counsel has attempted to resolve the issue informally.  Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents (Set One).

 

 

 

C.     Monetary Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d)).

 

In addition, §§ 2030.290 and 2031.300 authorize the Court to impose monetary sanctions if a party fails to respond to interrogatories and requests for production.

 

Defendant requests monetary sanctions for each Motion, to be paid by Plaintiff and/or his attorneys of record, jointly and severally.

 

Defendant requests sanctions for the Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Form Interrogatories in the amount of $874.95 as follows: 2 hours for preparing the Motion, 1 hour for attending the hearing, at a billing rate of $250 per hour, and $124.95 in filing fees.  (Buehler Decl. ¶ 13.)  Given that the Court grants the Motion, Defendant is entitled to sanctions.  The Court reduces the sanctions as follows: 1 hour for preparing the Motion and 1 hour for appearing at the hearing, at an hourly rate of $250, plus $124.95 in filing fees, for a total amount of $624.95.

 

Defendant requests sanctions for the Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Special Interrogatories in the amount of $874.95 as follows: 2 hours for preparing the Motion, 1 hour for attending the hearing, at a billing rate of $250 per hour, and $124.95 in filing fees.  (Buehler Decl. ¶ 13.)  Given that the Court grants the Motion, Defendant is entitled to sanctions.  The Court reduces the sanctions as follows: 1 hour for preparing the Motion and 1 hour for appearing at the hearing, at an hourly rate of $250, plus $124.95 in filing fees, for a total amount of $624.95.

 

Defendant requests sanctions for the Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents in the amount of $874.95 as follows: 2 hours for preparing the Motion, 1 hour for attending the hearing, at a billing rate of $250 per hour, and $124.95 in filing fees.  (Buehler Decl. ¶ 13.)  Given that the Court grants the Motion, Defendant is entitled to sanctions.  The Court reduces the sanctions as follows: 1 hour for preparing the Motion and 1 hour for appearing at the hearing, at an hourly rate of $250, plus $124.95 in filing fees, for a total amount of $624.95.

 

Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is granted in the amount of $624.95 for each discovery Motion, for a total of $1,874.85, to be paid by Plaintiff and his attorneys of record.

 

The Court also notes that Defendant requests a dismissal of the lawsuit with prejudice due to Plaintiff’s failure to respond to the discovery requests.  The Court does not find it appropriate to dismiss the Complaint – in effect a terminating sanction - based on the instant Motions.

 

 

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Militos Bagaryan’s Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One).  Plaintiff Calixo Lopez Gaspar is ordered to submit verified responses, without objections, within fifteen (15) days of notice of this order.

The Court GRANTS Defendant Militos Bagaryan’s Motion for Order Compelling Plaintiff’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents (Set One).  Plaintiff Calixo Lopez Gaspar is ordered to submit verified responses, without objections, within fifteen (15) days of notice of this order.

 

The Court grants Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions in the amount of $624.95 for each discovery motion, for a total of $1874.85 , to be paid by Plaintiff Calixo Lopez Gaspar and his attorneys of record within thirty (30) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

Case Number: 21STCV38025    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: B

ANGUIANO v. casillas, et al.

 

Motion for Summary judgment or in the alternative summary adjudication

 

Date of Hearing:  July 12, 2024                                   Trial Date:  November 4, 2024

Department:   B                                                          Case No.: 21STCV38025

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Miguel A. Casillas

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Kimberly Anguiano

                                   

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Kimberly Anguiano alleges that she was “attacked, mauled and bitten” by four dogs owned by defendant Pedro Antonio Romero, at real property located at 4865 West 133rd Street in Hawthorne, California, owned by moving defendant Miguel Casillas.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants Casillas and Romero had an agreement whereby Romero rented part of the 4865 West 133rd Street property to plaintiff, and that Defendants were in a joint venture and/or partnership to do so.

 

Plaintiff filed her complaint on October 14, 2021, alleging causes of action for:

(1)   strict liability under Civil Code section 3342;

(2)   strict liability for failure to remove known danger under Civil Code section 3342.5;

(3)   negligence per se;

(4)   negligence (joint venture and/or commercial agreement) based on Casillas’ actual or constructive knowledge of the vicious propensity of defendant Romero’s dogs;

(5)   premises liability based on the same allegation that defendant Casillas had actual or constructive knowledge of the vicious propensity of defendant Romero’s dogs;

(6)   intentional infliction of emotional distress based on the same agreement and alleged actual or constructive knowledge by Casillas of the vicious propensity of defendant Romero’s dogs; 

(7)   common law strict liability; and

(8)   punitive damages.

 

Defendant Casillas moves for summary adjudication on each of the causes of action alleged against him which include the fourth, fifth and sixth causes of action, and for summary judgment.

 

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

GRANT

 

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal. App. 4th 289, 294.)  “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal. App. 4th 884, 891.) The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.)

 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal. App. 4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence.

 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)    

 

Issue 1: Fourth Cause of Action for Negligence and Issue 2: Fifth Cause of Action for Premises Liability

 

Casillas argues that the fourth and fifth causes of action fail because as the landlord, it did not know that tenant and Defendant Romero was keeping any dangerous or vicious dogs, and there was no joint venture, partnership, or agency relationship between them. As to whether Casillas had actual knowledge of the dog’s dangerous and vicious tendencies, the Court finds that Plaintiff provides sufficient facts to raise a triable issue of fact. They are as follows:

 

Although Casillas denies seeing or asking about the breed of the dogs (Exhibit 2: Casillas Depo. pp. 97:5-17), Romero testifies that Casillas met the dogs on multiple occasions. (Exhibit 3: Romero Depo. pp. 19:11-24, 22:11-23:23, 117:14-24). Casillas admits seeing the “Beware of Dog” sign and may have posted it himself. (Exhibit 2: Casillas Depo. pp. 100:9-25, 101:21-102:3.) He acknowledges that a neighbor complained about the dogs. (Exhibit 2: Casillas Depo. pp. 102:4-104:25.) Additionally, Casillas heard the dogs bark loudly during rent collection. (Exhibit 2: Casillas Depo. p. 97:5-17.)

 

Romero informed Casillas that the dogs needed to be put away before anyone, including Casillas, entered the premises. (Exhibit 3: Romero Depo. pp. 70:16-25, 34:23-35:10, 98:6-99:10, 114:13-24.) Romero also warned anyone entering the property, including maintenance men and Casillas, to let him know in advance to put the dogs away. (Declaration of Kimberly Anguiano, ¶ 9.) Romero often boasted about his dog’s viciousness. (Kimberly Anguiano Decl. ¶ 7.) Plaintiff frequently saw dead animals in the yard. (Kimberly Anguiano Decl. ¶ 8.)

 

Casillas claims he allowed two dogs on the premises, one being a service dog for anxiety, which the Lease Agreement does not mention. (Exhibit 5: Lease Agreement ¶ 15; Exhibit 3: Romero Depo. pp. 42:7-43:24.)

 

These facts and contradictions provide sufficient evidence to raise a triable issue of material fact regarding Casillas’ knowledge of the dogs’ dangerous propensities. However, Plaintiff fails to provide any evidence to raise a triable issue of fact as to the existence of a joint venture between Castillas and Romero. Plaintiff points to the following:

 

Casillas claims in his declaration and deposition that he never authorized Romero to sublease the guest house and never shared profits from the rent. The Lease Agreement allows subleasing for an additional $200.00. (Exhibit 5: Lease Agreement ¶ 5.) Plaintiff provided documents showing she paid Romero $200.00 for rent in August 2019. (Exhibit 7: Rent Payments.) Casillas testified that by the second or third month of the lease, Romero was unable to afford rent due to late payments and a bounced check. (Exhibit 2: Casillas Depo. pp. 94:16-95:16.)

 

Casillas insisted on a $3,000 rent amount despite Romero’s initial request to pay less and noted that Romero consistently paid rent late. (Exhibit 2: Casillas Depo. pp. 60:10-61:19.) Romero testified that he informed Casillas about subleasing the guesthouse to afford the rent, and Casillas did not object. (Exhibit 3: Romero Depo. pp. 78:7-18.) Additionally, Romero’s co-worker, Allen Mejia, subleased the guest house with Casillas' permission, paying rent in cash that went to Casillas. (Exhibit 3: Romero Depo. pp. 28:22-29:12, 30:2-33:21, 36:9-37:17, 50:10-23.)

 

Romero also testified that two additional people lived in the main house with Casillas’ knowledge and permission. (Exhibit 3: Romero Depo. pp. 25:15-27:10.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff fails to demonstrate how this raises a triable issue as to the existence of a joint venture. As Castillas states in his reply, allowing subleasing to help Romero pay rent does not create a shared business interest or an agreement to share profits or losses between Romero and Casillas, so essential elements of partnership and joint venture are absent. Additionally, there is no evidence that Casillas authorized Romero to act on his behalf or that Casillas controlled Romero’s actions. There is also no evidence that Casillas caused the plaintiff to believe that Romero was acting as Casillas’ agent or that the plaintiff reasonably relied on such a belief. Consequently, the subletting arrangement was purely a landlord-tenant relationship. The Court agrees and finds that the fourth and fifth causes of action fail as a matter of law.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Castillas’ MSA as to Issues 1 and 2.

 

Issue 3: Sixth Cause of Action for IIED

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s claim for IIED also fails. Here, there is no evidence that Casillas engaged in extreme or outrageous conduct, nor is there evidence that Casillas intended to cause emotional distress or acted with reckless disregard. Plaintiff argues that Casillas knew there were six people living on the premises, both the main house and guest house were sublet to help Romero pay rent, and the guesthouse was made habitable for subleasing. Casillas also knew about the dogs roaming freely, saw or posted the “Beware of Dog” sign, received a neighbor’s complaint about the dogs, and was informed by Romero that the dogs were not friendly and needed to be put away when anyone entered the premises. Despite these points, the evidence does not demonstrate that Casillas’ conduct was so extreme as to exceed all bounds tolerated in a civilized community. Without such a showing, Plaintiff cannot establish the first element of IIED. Therefore, there is no triable issue of fact regarding whether Casillas’ conduct was outrageous, and the sixth cause of action fails as a matter of law.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Castillas’ MSA as to Issue 3. 

 

Therefore, Castillas’ motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            Set Aside Default

 

CCP § 473.5 states:

 

(a) When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.

(b) A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.

(c) Upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.

 

Further, a “default judgment entered against a defendant who was not served with a summons in the manner prescribed by statute is void. Under § 473(d), the court may set aside a default judgment which is valid on its face, but void, as a matter of law, due to improper service.”  (Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 544.)  Where service is challenged, the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the facts requisite to an effective service.  (Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1160.)

The California Supreme Court has held the following:

Section 473 is often applied liberally where the party in default moves promptly to seek relief, and the party opposing the motion will not suffer prejudice if relief is granted. [Citations.] In such situations “very slight evidence will be required to justify a court in setting aside the default.” [Citations.] [¶] Moreover, because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default. [Citations.] Therefore, a trial court order denying relief is scrutinized more carefully than an order permitting trial on the merits. (Elston v. City of Turlock (1985) 38 Cal.3d 227, 233-34.)

Application to Facts

In the instant case, Plaintiff claims service occurred upon Defendant Majanc on August 29, 2023, and that Proof of Service with respect to the same was filed with this court on August 29, 2023. The Los Angeles Superior Court case summary reflects that Proof of Service by Substituted Service was filed by Plaintiff Tzul on August 29, 2023. Subsequently, a responsive pleading was due in September 2023. However, Defendant declares that although the subject accident occurred on November 14, 2019, Defendant Majanc purchased the vehicle from a Mr. Gregorio Gomez (now deceased) during 2016 and was subsequently paying off the vehicle purchase from him. (Majanc Decl., ¶ 2.) As such, the title remained unchanged with the Department of Motor Vehicles until it was updated in April 2021. (Id.) Majanc declares his address for the past twelve years is:

 1723 James M. Woods Boulevard, Apt 116, Los Angeles, California, 90015. (Id.)

Yet, the Proof of (Substituted) Service indicates service was made at:

 2175 Liberty St NE Salem, OR 97301-8353. (Alban Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. 2.)

 Majanc declares he has never lived in Oregon and, thus, suspects that the summons and complaint were improperly served upon subject vehicle’s prior owner, Gregorio Gomez. (Id.) Moreover, Majanc denies any knowledge or notice of this action, the filing thereof or of purported service at any time prior to the March 15, 2024, default. (Majanc Decl., ¶ 2.) Accordingly, Defendant now requests the Court grant Defendant’s motion for relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) section 473.5 because Defendant has received no actual notice of the action in time to defend himself against Plaintiff’s action. (Mot., 6.) Defendant also requests that the Court quash service of the summons due to lack of service and jurisdiction.

Here, the Court finds that Defendant has filed the instant motion within a reasonable time, not exceeding either two years after entry or default on March 18, 2024, or 180 days after Defendant was served with written notice that the default judgement had been entered. Thus, the instant motion is timely in accordance with CCP § 473.5.

Secondly, Defendant Majanc has properly accompanied the instant motion with an affidavit demonstrating his lack of actual notice in time to defend himself from the action and that such lack of actual notice was not due to avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. (See Majanc Declaration.) This is further evidenced by the fact that the substituted service was made in Oregon at an address not associated with Defendant. Moreover, Defendant has declared that his place of residence has been in Los Angeles for the past twelve (12) years and that he has never lived in Oregon. (Majanc Decl., ¶ 2.) Additionally, Defendant has attached a copy of the Answer (Ex.3) and the Complaint (Ex. 1), along with the POS-010 of the substituted service made at “2175 Liberty Ste NE, Salem, OR 97301-8353 (Ex. 1). (Majanc Decl., Exs. 1-3.)

Thus, Defendant has made the necessary showings required under CCP 473.5. Moreover, the Court finds that the law strongly favors a disposition of a trial on the merits. Given Defendant contends prejudice will not result and that Plaintiff Tzul has not opposed the instant motion, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside Default and GRANTS Defendant Leave to defend the instant action.

With respect to Defendant’s request for the Court to Quash Service of Summons, the Court reminds Defendant that pursuant to Government Code § 70617(a)(4), a filing fee and separate motion request is required for each motion application or any other paper or request requiring a hearing. Nevertheless, the Court’s findings on the instant motion effectively void Plaintiff’s proof of service of summons. Thus, Defendant need not request or move the Court to quash service as summons, as the instant ruling effectively does so.

CONCLUSION

            The Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            On May 4, 2022, Plaintiff Yvonne Escobar (“Plaintiff”) filed suit individually and also as an administrator of the estate of her deceased mother, Josephine Reyes (“Josephine”) against Defendants County of Los Angeles (“County”); Los Angeles County Department of Adult Protective Services (“LA CAPS”), Los Angeles County Department of Mental Health (“LACDMH”), Khahlalah Todd, and Kimberly Moser for the following four causes of action: (1) wrongful death (CCP § 377.60); (2) survival action; (3) negligent retention [LA County Adult Protective Services]; and (4) negligent retention [LA County Department of Mental Health].

Plaintiff alleges that her brother, Alexander Escobar, became increasingly aggressive and violent towards their mother, Josephine in the five years preceding her murder. Between October 2015 and the date of Josephine Reyes’ murder on May 5, 2020, the Monterey City Police Department and Adult Protective Services (“APS”) received numerous reports regarding Alexander Escobar’s violent, physical abuse of his mother occurring at their residence. Thus, Plaintiff now claims that as a direct and proximate result of Defendants’ acts and omissions, Alexander Escobar stabbed Josephine Escobar to death on May 5, 2020, thereby depriving Plaintiff of the life-long love, companionship, comfort, society, and care of Josephine Reyes.

On February 12, 2024, Plaintiff subpoenaed the Monterey Park Police Department (“MPPD”) which is not a party to this action. MPPD responded to Plaintiff’s Subpoena Duces Tecum on March 4, 2024, through its legal representative, Assistant City Attorney to the City of Monterey Park, Timothy Campen (“Campen”), by producing redacted versions of some of the requested documents. Specifically, MPPD provided redacted police reports, while also declining to produce others, citing the California Constitution and Welfare and Institutions Code §§ 4514 and 5328. Campen stated that the redactions were statutorily commanded to protect the privacy rights of individuals whose records were being subpoenaed and that reports pertaining to Welfare Institutions Code (“WIC”) § 5150 were being entirely withheld. Campen also advised Plaintiff to seek an Order from the Court specifically directing the City to produce such records and/or unredacted versions of them.

Thus, on May 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion, requesting the Court issue an order to release the documents, along with the Declaration of Richard L. Lara, and a Proposed Order.

There is no opposition.

 

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            California Welfare & Institutions Code § 5328

Section 5328 specifically provides for disclosure of confidential information “‘[t]o the courts, as necessary to the administration of justice.’” (Sorenson, supra, 219 Cal.App.4th at p. 444, 161 Cal.Rptr.3d 794, quoting § 5328, subd. (f).) So court proceedings discussing information obtained in the course of treating a patient or resident are not confidential precisely because section 5328 expressly authorizes release of such information under the circumstances. Here, by contrast, the Center does not argue that any enumerated exemption to section 5328 authorizes disclosure of confidential information obtained in the course of providing services to mentally ill and developmentally disabled patients and residents. (State Dep't of Pub. Health v. Superior Ct. 60 Cal. 4th 940, 955, 342 P.3d 1217, 1225 (2015)).

Application to Facts

            Plaintiff alleges that her late mother, Josephine Reyes, was a protected adult under the California’s Elder Abuse statute. (Motion (“Mot.”), pg. 7.) Plaintiff also alleges that her brother, Alexander Escobar, had a long history of severe mental illness and a pattern of physically and emotionally abusing his mother, Josephine. (Id.)  Further, Plaintiff asserts that Los Angeles County Department of Mental Health (“LACDMH”) and Los Angeles County Adult Protective Services (“LA CAPS”) were both aware that Josephine was a protected adult, as well as Alexander Escobar’s history of severe mental illness and abuse. (Id.) However, despite the fact both LACDMH and LA CAPS were informed and aware of the circumstances surrounding Josephine’s vulnerability and Alexander’s escalating abuse of her, neither institution acted to prevent the abuse from continuing. (Id.)

            Plaintiff contends that Alexander’s abuse of Josephine may be evidenced in the foregoing MPPD records she seeks. Moreover, MPDD may be the only source of such records because they would have records of the prior incidents and calls, as well as the subject incident of the instant suit. As such, Plaintiff argues the records are relevant pursuant to California Evidence. Code § 210, as they would have a tendency to prove a disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action. Accordingly, the subject documents and information therein would be admissible pursuant to Evidence Code § 350. The Court agrees the records Plaintiff seeks are relevant and also that MPPD may be the only source of such records.

Although Timothy Campen asserted that the “redactions of the records that have already been provided are statutorily commanded to protect the privacy rights of individuals whose records are being subpoenaed,” Plaintiff is correct that the administration of justice serves as an express exception to the confidentiality protections of § 5328. The California Supreme Court was clear in State Dep't of Pub. Health, finding that court proceedings discussing information obtained in the course of providing mental health services are not confidential in these circumstances precisely because § 5328 expressly authorizes the release of this information under these very circumstances.

Thus, the Court grants Plaintiff’s Motion and orders the records to be released for the express purpose contained herein. Accordingly, all relevant records sought that fall under the exception of § 5328 shall be made available in unredacted format.

 

CONCLUSION

            The Motion for Court Order for the Release of Documents is GRANTED.

22STCV15404 INTERINSURANCE EXCHANGE OF THE AUTOMOBILE CLUB

REQUEST FOR COURT JUDGMENT AGAINST [DEFAULTING PARTY]:

 

Gilbert Oscar Munoz

 

TENTATIVE

 

Plaintiff Interinsurance Exchange of The Automobile Club (“Plaintiff”) filed this subrogation action against defendants Gilbert Oscar Munoz, Bonnie Maureen Munoz and Does 1 through 10 for damages Plaintiff paid to its insured, arising from a motor vehicle collision. Plaintiff alleges that prior to commencing the action, it had paid to the insured the sum of $38,348.27.

 

Plaintiff has dismissed defendants Bonnie Maureen Munoz and Does 1 to 10. Plaintiff now seeks court default judgment against the sole remaining defendant, Gilbert Oscar Munoz (“Defendant”), the principal amount of $38,348.27 for uninsured motorist benefits paid/property damage, $4,758.34 in interest, and $575 in costs. Plaintiff submits the declarations of its counsel, its insured, and its custodian of records in support thereof.

 

On April 18, 2024, the Court denied default judgment for the following reasons:

 

Plaintiff submits the declaration of Dwight Clifford (“Clifford”) Clifford provides that he is the custodian of records and subrogation examiner for State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“State Farm”), which he refers to as the “Plaintiff” in this action. However, the plaintiff in this action is Interinsurance Exchange of The Automobile Club, not State Farm. There is no explanation provided regarding the relationship between State Farm and Plaintiff, such that Clifford’s declaration attesting to the damages incurred by State Farm would be applicable to Plaintiff.

 

Further, the Court notes the complaint provides that Plaintiff’s insured was George Sansan, who was the owner of the motor vehicle and under which a policy was issued by Plaintiff to insured George Sansan and occupants. (Compl. at ¶ 4.) However, Plaintiff submits the declaration of Jocelyn Perez Sasan, who declares she was driving her vehicle, when Defendant rear-ended her. The declaration does not properly explain the relationship between Jocelyn Perez Sansan, George Sansan alleged in the complaint, and the relevant insurance policy.

 

Plaintiff must resolve the discrepancies before the Court is able to grant the request for default judgment.

 

The Court ordered Plaintiff to submit a new default judgment package correcting these defects no later than June 3, 2024. The Court stated, “[f]ailure to do so may result in the imposition of sanctions, including monetary sanctions and/or dismissal.”

 

Although past the June 3, 2024 deadline, on June 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the new default judgment application.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff has corrected the deficiencies. For instance, Plaintiff filed an amended declaration of the custodian of records, Dwight Clifford where he provides that he is the custodian of records and subrogation examiner for Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club (“Interinsurance”), which he now refers to as the “Plaintiff” in this action.

 

Moreover, Clifford further states “PLAINTIFF issued an automobile insurance policy to George Sasan ("INSURED"), more specifically known as policy number CAA076332069 (the "policy" ). The policy period covered the date of March 28, 2020. Pursuant to the terms of the policy, PLAINTIFF was obligated to indemnify INSURED for damages incurred to INSURED' vehicle and for bodily injuries sustained by Jocelyn Perez Sasan ("JOCELYN").” (Clifford Decl. ¶ 2.)

 

Clifford also states “PLAINTIFF was informed by INSURED that INSURED's vehicle was involved in an accident on March 28, 2020. At the tIme of the accident, INSURED's vehicle was operated by JOCELYN. PLAINTIFF later ascertained that the other party involved in the accident was Defendant Gilbert Oscar Munoz ("DEFENDANT"). PLAINTIFF determined that DEFENDANT was the driver and the owner of the other vehicle, based on information submitted by INSURED and the Traffic Accident Report.” (Id. at ¶ 3.)

 

Nevertheless, the Court notes that Plaintiff failed to file Proof of Service that the new default judgment application was properly served.

 

Accordingly, the default judgment application is denied without prejudice for failure to provide Proof of Service.
