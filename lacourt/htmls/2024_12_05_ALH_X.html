DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 19STCV11399    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: X


PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO STRIKE DEFENDANT’S COST BILL

Plaintiff moves to strike/tax costs from Defendant KAZN AM 1300, Arthur Liu, and Yvonne Liu’s memorandum of costs filed on September 10, 2024, seeking recovery of litigation expenses in the amount of $32,387.02.  (9/10/24 Memorandum of Costs.)  On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Tax Costs, objecting to the memorandum in its entirety.

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendants’ Memorandum of Costs is granted in part and denied in part.  Total costs of $6,614.91 are awarded.

Legal Authority and Analysis

Plaintiff argues that the memorandum lacks supporting documentation and is unreasonable. Plaintiff asserts that without documentation, it is impossible to audit the costs incurred.  The Defendants oppose the motion, asserting that the costs are reasonable, necessary, and recoverable under Code of Civil Procedure section 1032(b).
 
California Rule of Court, Rule 3.1700 requires that “Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum.”  (Cal. Rules Court, rule, 3.1700(b)(1).)  The time for filing is extended for mail or electronic service pursuant to either Code of Civil Procedure sections 1010.6, subdivision (a)(4) or 1013.  Additionally, “[t]he party claiming costs and the party contesting costs may agree to extend the time for serving and filing the cost memorandum and a motion to strike or tax costs.  This agreement must be confirmed in writing, specify the extended date for service, and be filed with the clerk.  In the absence of an agreement, the court may extend the times for serving and filing the cost memorandum or the notice of motion to strike or tax costs for a period not to exceed 30 days.”  (Cal. Rules Court, rule, 3.1700(b)(3).)
 
The Court observes Plaintiff’s motion was filed on September 30, 2024—20 days after service. The motion attests that Plaintiff and Defendants’ counsel agreed Plaintiff had until September 30, 2024 to serve this motion to strike/tax costs, thus the motion is timely.
 
Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 sets forth the costs recoverable by the prevailing party.  To recover a cost, it must be reasonably necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount.  (Perko’s Enterprises, Inc. v. RRNS Enterprises (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 238, 244.)  In reviewing a motion to tax costs, a guiding principle is that all costs must be “reasonably necessary to the litigation” and “reasonable in need and amount.”  (Acosta v. SI Corporation (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1370, 1380.)
 
If the items on their face appear to be proper charges, the verified costs bill is prima facie evidence of their propriety, and the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show they were not reasonable or necessary.  (Ladas v. California State Auto. Ass’n (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774-776.)  On the other hand, items that are properly objected to are put at issue, and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.  (Ibid.)  Whether an item listed on the costs bill was reasonably necessary is a question of fact for the trial court.  (Bender v. County of Los Angeles (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 968, 989.)  The contesting party may meet its burden by showing the entire costs bill is improper rather than by challenging particular items.  (Fennessy v. DeLeuw-Cather Corp. (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 1192).  Courts have discretion to disallow costs that were unreasonably incurred. (Michell v. Olick (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1194, 1201.)  
 
Here, the right to recovery of costs remains unchallenged.  Rather, Plaintiff challenges the reasonableness of the items in the memorandum because of the lack of attachments, documentation, or attestations to the validity and reasonableness of the costs.  The memorandum of costs lists the following items:

1. Filing and motion fees, $4,229.23
2. Jury fees, $375.00
4. Deposition costs, $11,023.56
5. Service of process, $890.02
8. Witness fees, $4,100.00
11. Court reporter fees as established by statute, $11,539.41
14. Fees for electronic filing or service, $223.12
TOTAL COSTS of $32,387.02

Notably, Defendants did not file a MC-011 worksheet to detail and support their memorandum of costs.  

Plaintiff did not proffer any evidence in support of his motion, but Plaintiff’s counsel, Frank Carleo, filed a declaration on December 2, 2024 in response to the opposition.  The Carleo Declaration attests that Plaintiff’s counsel never consented to service via email, as required by Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) 1010.6(a)(i) & (ii).  On September 11, 2024, he informed opposing counsel that he did not consent to the service of a Memorandum of Costs.  A legal assistant named Shin agreed to serve documents by USPS in the future and retracted the service of the Memorandum of Costs.  The only cost document served was the Memorandum of Costs dated September 10, 2024, signed by attorney Gi Nam Lee.

On November 22, 2024, Carleo discovered a 52-page document—the Lee Declaration which supports Defendants’ opposition—that had been served electronically, contrary to their agreement.  Defendants’ attempt to substantiate their claimed costs in defense counsel’s declaration.  (See Lee Decl., Exh. A [filing fees, jury fees, deposition costs, service of process, expert witness fees, court reporter fees, and fees for electronic filing of service].)  The Carleo Declaration asserts that the Lee Declaration does not include a Judicial Counsel Form MC-011: Memorandum of Costs (Worksheet) and is poorly organized.

The Court agrees—the Lee Declaration is not clearly organized.  For example, all the receipts are included in a single Exhibit A of the Lee Declaration, without descriptions of each receipt and which “Item” the receipt falls under in the memorandum of costs.  Form MC-011 is not mandatory but assists the Court in determining the reasonableness of the costs requested.  There having been no worksheet submitted nor descriptive explanations of the receipts in Exhibit A of the Lee Declaration, the Court and Plaintiff cannot studiously evaluate Defendants’ claimed costs.  Accordingly, based on the Court’s review of the documents, the Court will award the following as costs:  Filing and motion fees of $2,614.98; Jury fees of $265.37; and Deposition costs of $3,734.56.  The motion to strike is denied as to these costs and the total allowable costs are $6,614.91.  The motion to strike is granted as to the remaining costs asserted.
 
Conclusion

Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendants’ Memorandum of Costs is granted in part and denied in part.  Total costs of $6,614.91 are awarded. 

Case Number: 22STCV11780    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: X


DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Defendants Daniel Leo Moon, M.D. and Daniel L. Moon M.D., PC move for summary judgment.

This complaint arises out of Plaintiff’s sole cause of action for medical malpractice against Defendants Daniel Moon, M.D. (as well as his professional corporation) and Methodist Hospital.  The complaint alleges on or about May 12, 2012, moving Defendants negligently and carelessly performed a gall bladder removal (cholecystectomy), allowing a bile leak, and failed to properly detect and remedy the leak, which caused sepsis and multiple medical sequalae thereafter. 

Tentative Ruling

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied.

Discussion and Analysis

Legal Standard on Summary Judgment

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “A party may move for summary judgement in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473c subd. (a)(1).)  “The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.”  (Id., at § 437c, subd. (c).) 

As the moving parties, Defendants bear the initial burden of proof to show that Plaintiff cannot establish one or more elements of her cause of action or to show that there is a complete defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  Only after the moving party has carried this burden of proof does the burden of proof shift to the other party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists – and this must be shown via specific facts and not mere allegations.  (Id.) Where the moving party produces competent expert opinion declarations showing that there is no triable issue of fact on an essential element of the opposing party’s claim (e.g. that a medical defendant’s treatment fell within the applicable standard of care), the opposing party’s burden is to produce competent expert opinion declarations to the contrary.  (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 10:205.5, citing Ochoa v. Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1480, 1487.) 

Defendants Have Established the Absence of a Triable Issue as to Medical Negligence

The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.  (Johnson v. Superior Court (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 297, 305 citing Hanson v. Grode (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 601, 606.)

“The standard of care in a medical malpractice case requires that physicians exercise in diagnosis and treatment that reasonable degree of skill, knowledge and care ordinarily possessed and exercised by members of the medical profession under similar circumstances. ‘ “The standard of care against which the acts of a physician are to be measured is a matter peculiarly within the knowledge of experts; it presents the basic issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by their testimony, unless the conduct required by the particular circumstances is within the common knowledge of the layman.” ’ ”  (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 983–984, internal citations omitted.)

California courts have incorporated the expert evidence requirement into their standard for summary judgment in medical malpractice cases.  When a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.  (Hanson, supra 76 Cal.App.4th at p. 607, citing Munro, supra, 215 Cal.App.3d at pp. 984–985.)

The court does not weigh competing expert testimony.  (Mann v. Cracchiolo (1985) 38 Cal.3d 18, 39, overruled on other grounds by Perry v. Bakewell Hawthorne, LLC (2017) 2 Cal.5th 536, 543.)  Essentially, “the court cannot disregard [an expert] opinion solely because other, more qualified experts opine to the contrary.”  (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, supra, 10:272, citing Mann, supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 39.)

Here, Defendants rely on the declaration of their expert Robert E. Cole, M.D., who has been a board-certified surgeon since 1988.  (Cole Decl., ¶ 2.)  Cole has testified Dr. Moon complied with the standard of care during his care and treatment of Plaintiff.  (Cole Decl., ¶ 5.)  Dr. Cole also declares that to a reasonable degree of medical probability, no purported negligent act or omission on the part of Dr. Moon caused or was a substantial factor in causing Ms. Paulas’ injuries.  (Ibid.)  Dr. Cole based his opinions upon his review of the Plaintiff’s medical records, imaging from the hospital, patient charts, and his education, training, and experience.  (Id. ¶ 3.)  Likewise, Dr. Cole states he is familiar with the standard of care for a provider such as Dr. Moon specializing in general surgery, based on Dr. Cole’s background, training, and experience.  (Ibid.)  Dr. Cole further opined that Plaintiff provided informed consent for the laparoscopic cholecystectomy procedure after discussion with Dr. Moon regarding potential risks and complications, including a possible bile leak and need to return to surgery.  (Id. ¶ 6.)

Dr. Cole’s declaration is sufficient to shift the burden to Plaintiff as to the existence of a triable issue of fact regarding standard of care and causation. 

Plaintiff’s Opposition Evidence Creates a Triable Issue

Plaintiff’s opposition relies on the declaration of board-certified surgeon Stephan Zweig, M.D.  Dr. Zweig is familiar with the standard of care applicable to general surgeons and medical staff in the performance of cholecystectomy.  (Zweig Decl., ¶¶ 1-3.)  Dr. Zweig has reviewed all the pertinent records of the Plaintiff, including all the same records Dr. Cole reviewed plus Dr. Cole’s declaration.  (Id. ¶ 4.)  Dr. Zweig opines Dr. Moon should have requested an interventional radiologist to place a drain when various radiographic studies indicated a bile leak, and that Dr. Moon’s failure to do so was beneath the standard of care.  (Id. ¶ 8.)

Plaintiff presented in the emergency department on May 15, 2021 for extreme abdominal pain, and the next day, gastroenterologist Dr. Michael Chu performed an endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (“ERCP”) for a suspected bile leak.  (Id. ¶¶ 7, 9.)  On May 17, 2021, Plaintiff still had right upper quadrant pain and another CT scan was ordered and it showed more bile leakage, so Dr. Moon wanted to perform an exploratory laparoscopy to clean the bile leakage and finally place a drain.  (Id. ¶ 10.)  Due to her presentation, and that Dr. Moon knew there was continued leakage with no drain, Dr. Zweig attests the standard of care required the drain be placed as soon as possible, and not wait another day.  (Ibid.)  On May 18, 2021, the pain was severe and Plaintiff consented to the laparoscopic procedure, where Dr. Moon cleaned the bile, placed a drain, and washed the abdominal cavity.  (Id. ¶¶ 10-11.)  On May 28, 2021, Plaintiff was discharged to home despite a recommendation for a discharge to a rehabilitation facility.  (Id. ¶ 14.)  The same day, she passed out at home (a syncopal episode) and returned to the hospital, where she was diagnosed with low blood pressure.  (Ibid.)

Based on a review of the records and his background, training, and experience, it is Dr. Zweig’s opinion that Dr. Moon failed to comply with the standard of care in the community for general surgeon in multiple respects: he (1) failed to have a drain placed upon learning of the bile leak and (2) delayed placing a drain by a day, when it should have been done as soon as possible.  (Id. ¶ 15.)  In Dr. Zweig’s opinion, because of the possibility of bile leakage, a drain should have been placed during the initial surgery, as it is a known complication of a cholecystectomy.  (Ibid.)  Dr. Zweig asserts Dr. Moon’s expert (Dr. Cole) agrees this is a known complication yet makes no mention of a simple drain placement being a prophylactic solution to the possible complication.  (Ibid.)  Dr. Zweig concludes that, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, the breaches in the standard of care led to Plaintiff suffering all her damages, including pain, diminished mental capacity, the need for a second surgery, and the syncopal episode.  (Id. ¶ 16.)

In reply, Defendant objects to Dr. Zweig’s Declaration as conclusory and speculative.  The defense argues that an expert declaration must provide specific factual breaches of duty, not just assumptions or speculative opinions.  The defense further contends that Dr. Zweig’s contentions lack transparency because Dr. Zweig fails to note that Dr. Moon was not the treating physician on May 15-16, 2021 when the bile leak was discovered.  Instead, the patient was seen by other physicians, including Dr. Manchandia and Dr. Chu, who recommended the ERCP and considered, but did not perform, a laparoscopy with drainage.  Further, the defense argues, Dr. Zweig’s contention that Dr. Moon should have placed a drain prophylactically during the surgery is not sufficient to establish a breach of the standard of care, as Dr. Zweig does not state that this is the standard of care in the community, but rather argues that it should be the standard of care.

Plaintiff has raised a genuine dispute through Dr. Zweig’s declaration, which asserts specific breaches of the standard of care and links those breaches to Plaintiff’s injuries and damages.  While Dr. Zweig has opined about his practice includes placing a drain during surgery, this is not sufficient where it does not reflect the standard within the community.  With that said, Dr. Zweig opines that Dr. Moon’s failure to place a drain upon learning of a bile leak and waiting a day to place such drain were breaches in the standard of care for the community causing Plaintiff’s harm.  (Id. ¶¶ 15, 16.)  Given the conflicting expert declarations regarding these issues and the Court’s inability to weigh or evaluate the credibility of testimony of either expert, a triable issue of fact exists as to whether Dr. Moon breached the standard of care. 

Conclusion

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied.
