Case Number: 23AVCV00204    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Maria Salas (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she purchased a 2017 Honda Pilot with VIN: 5FNYF5H56HB031880 (the “Subject Vehicle”) on August 29, 2017 and entered an express written contract with Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) by which Defendant undertook to preserve or maintain the utility or performance of the Subject Vehicle or provide compensation if there was a failure in such utility or performance. Plaintiff presents that throughout the United States, Defendant manufactured hundreds of thousands of vehicles with defective transmissions, including all 2014-2019 Honda Pilot Vehicles. Plaintiff further presents that, at the time of the sale of the Subject Vehicle, (1) Defendant was aware or should have been aware of the transmission defect in the Subject Vehicle before it sold the Subject Vehicle due to various issued Technical Service Bulletins (“TSBs”) and complaints; and (2) Defendant had a duty to disclose the transmission defect and did not do so. Plaintiff alleges that, after purchase of the Subject Vehicle at a car dealership in Mission Hills, she delivered the Subject Vehicle to Defendant’s Robertson Palmdale Honda approximately eight times. Of the eight times four times related to repeated complaints of the vehicle’s transmission not shifting properly. Plaintiff contends that Defects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves within the applicable express warranty period. The nonconformities substantially impaired the use, value and/or safety of the Subject Vehicle, Defendant was unable to conform the Subject Vehicle to the applicable express warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts, and Defendant did not make restitution in accordance with the Song-Beverly Act.

 

On February 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging two causes of action for (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty, and (2) Fraudulent Inducement-Concealment.

 

On April 14, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

On January 03, 2024, the parties entered a stipulation to allow Plaintiff leave to file her First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed her FAC alleging the same two causes of action.

 

On February 08, 2024, Defendant filed this Demurrer with Motion to Strike.

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Oppositions.

 

On March 25, 2024, Defendant filed its Replies.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a).)¿A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Ibid.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is¿whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified Dec. 23, 2003.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

¿ 

Standard for a Motion to Strike – The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

Meet and Confer Requirement – Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.) The Court notes that this requirement has been met. (See Decl. Leanna L. H. Vault ¶¶ 2-3.)

Discussion

 

Application – Defendant demurs only to the Second Cause of Action (Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment).

 

As a preliminary matter, Defendant’s argument regarding the Supremacy Clause is unavailing. It is well known that fraudulent inducement and/or fraudulent concealment are causes of action. TSBs are routinely cited to in lemon law cases as evidence of a defendant’s exclusive knowledge. Defendant concedes it provided such data to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (“NHTSA”) and, as such, Defendant concedes that it has the knowledge alleged. (See Demurrer 8:26-27.) While Defendant believes that disclosure of a transmission defect would stand as an obstacle to the accomplishment of the full purposes and objectives of Congress and NHTSA, the statement is unsupported.

 

The Court also notes that while Defendant has a section titled “Plaintiff’s Fraudulent Inducement Claim Lacks the Requisite Factual Specificity and is Therefore, Uncertain, Ambiguous, and Unintelligible,” the argument provided does not indicate how the Complaint uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible. Rather, it appears to only indicate that Plaintiff did not plead the Second Cause of Action with the requisite specificity.

 

Defendant argues that for Plaintiff to adequately plead Second Cause of Action, there must be a relationship which could give rise to a duty to disclose and, here, there is none as Plaintiff did not engage in a direct transaction with Defendant but with Galpin Honda, an authorized Honda dealer. Defendant contends that while Galpin Honda is an authorized dealer, it is not an agent. Defendant next argues that Plaintiff alleges public disclosure through NHSTA, contradicting the exclusive knowledge allegation, and that Plaintiff fails to allege active concealment or plead with specificity. Defendant further argues that Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action is barred by statute of limitations as she purchased the Subject Vehicle on or about August 29, 2017, but did not file the FAC until February 21, 2023. Defendant believes that accrual began on the purchase date and that any tolling theory alleged in the FAC fails as the underlying allegations are conclusory. Defendant also presents that Mobayen v. American Honda Motor Co., et al, Los Angeles County Superior Court Case No. 19STCV33467 (“Mobayen”) was never certified and dismissed on July 20, 2023. Defendant contends that as the request for punitive damages which derives from the Second Cause of Action must fail for the same reasons.

 

Plaintiff presents that Defendant had a duty to disclose concealed facts as they knew about the transmission defects through sources not available to consumers including: Defendant’s own design expertise; Defendant’s own records of customers’ complaints, dealership repair records, records from the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), warranty and post-warranty claims, and internal pre-sale durability testing and technical service bulletins. (See Opp. 4:17-20 [citing to FAC ¶¶16, 22.) Plaintiff cites to Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828 (“Dhital”)].). Plaintiff presents that her FAC specifically alleges facts constituting each element of Defendant’s concealment and meets the requisite specificity as it is alleged that as early as 2012, the fleet of vehicles including the 2017 Honda Pilot, experienced the same or similar transmission defect as the Subject Vehicle, Plaintiff presents that she has met the elements for her Second Cause of Action, citing to portions of the FAC. Plaintiff emphasizes that the specificity requirement is relaxed when the allegations indicate that a defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy or when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party. As to tolling, Plaintiff presents (1) she reasonably relied on representations by Defendant indicating that the Subject Vehicle had been repaired, (2) the discovery rule and the doctrines of fraudulent concealment apply to toll Plaintiff’s causes of action, (3) Plaintiff has alleged the precise time and manner of discovery (i.e., repair visit on November 27, 2020), (4) the attempts at service show diligence, (5) the American Pipe [ and Cost. Co. v. Utah (1974) 141 U.S. 538 (“American Pipe”)] rule applies and Mobayen is ongoing, and (6) equitable tolling applies.

 

In Reply, Defendant has provided new evidence in a Request for Judicial Notice. It is unclear why the Request for Judicial Notice was not brought with the moving papers as Defendant’s arguments included the the transmission of aggregate data to NHTSA which allows the public to review such information and a discussion of Mobayen. “The salutary rule is that points raised in a reply brief for the first time will not be considered unless good cause is shown for the failure to present them before.” (Balboa Ins. Co. v. Aguirre (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 1002, 1010.) This applies to new evidence as well – “[t]he general rule of motion practice … is that new evidence is not permitted with reply papers.” (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537.) “This rule is based on the same solid logic applied in the appellate courts, specifically, that ‘[p]oints raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument.’ ” (Id. at 1538 [citing American Drug Stores, Inc. v. Stroh (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1446, 1453; Browne v. County of Tehama (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 704, 720, fn. 10].) As such, the Court does not consider Defendant’s new evidence submitted in the Reply brief. 

 

Defendant’s Reply arguments include the following: (1) Plaintiff has not met her burden to show that the claims are timely as the delayed discovery rule is misapplied as Plaintiff alleged that “on the November 21, 2020 repair visit, Plaintiff brought her vehicle in to an authorized repair facility for repeated transmission concern” (see FAC ¶¶ 54-58) creating an awareness in Plaintiff that there were issues with the transmission and an investigation would have revealed the TSBs; (2) Plaintiff’s argument requests the Court to overlook that for the American Pipe tolling, a class certification must have been denied, not simply filed, Plaintiff’s understanding of China Agritech v. Resh (2018) 138 S.Ct. 1800 is incorrect, and Plaintiff has not addressed whether the class action sufficiently put Defendant on notice of the substance and nature of Plaintiff’s claims in the individual suit; and (3) Plaintiff is not entitled to equitable tolling as Mobayen did not provide Defendant notice of the claims in the case such that it could identify or preserve evidence as the Mobayen case was focused on Plaintiff Khosrow Mobayen’s lease of a specific 2017 Honda Pilot and its repair history, not Plaintiff’s Subject Vehicle. Defendant reiterates its exclusive knowledge and clarity arguments.

 

The Court notes that Dhital is pending review by the California Supreme Court. (See Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2023) 304 Cal.Rptr.3d 82. Plaintiff did not disclose this in her citations to Dhital. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rules 8.1105(e)(1)(B), 8.1115(e)(1).) As such, the Dhital holding may be cited only for persuasive value. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 8.1115(e)(1).) Defendant, too, has failed to include such information in its Reply when discussing Dhital. (See Reply 10:21-11:6.)

 

The Court first addresses statute of limitations.

 

Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action is governed by the three-year statute of limitations under Cal. Civ. Code § 338(d).

 

Plaintiffs purchased the Subject Vehicle on “August 19, 2017” but did not file suit until February 21, 2023. (FAC ¶ 8.)

 

"[W]here class action status has been denied solely because of failure to demonstrate that the class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable, the commencement of the original class suit tolls the running of the statute for all purported members of the class who make timely motions to intervene after the court has found the suit inappropriate for class action status." (American Pipe, supra, 414 U.S. 538, 553.) Under limited circumstances, if class certification is denied, the statute of limitations is tolled from the time of commencement of the suit to the time of denial of certification for all purported members of the class who either make timely motions to intervene in the surviving individual action or who timely file their individual actions. (Falk v. Children's Hospital of Los Angeles (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 1454, 1463 ["Tolling lasts from the day a class claim is asserted until the day the suit is conclusively not a class action—which may be because the judge rules adversely to the plaintiff."])

 

Paragraph 75 alleges that the Mobayen class action is still ongoing. However, Plaintiffs fail to allege that class certification was denied. (See American Pipe, supra, 414 U.S. 538, 552-53; Chardon v. Fumero Soto (1983) 462 U.S. 650, 654; see also Crown, Cork & Seal Co., Inc. v. Parker (1983) 462 U.S. 345, 354; China Agritech, supra, 138 S.Ct. 1800, 1804.) Indeed, the “watchwords of American Pipe are efficiency and economy of litigation,” and extending American Pipe tolling where class certification has not been denied does not serve that purpose. (China Agritech, supra, 138 S.Ct. 1800, 1804.)

 

However, it appears that the delayed discovery rule may apply. Under the section titled “Plaintiff’s Experiences,” Plaintiff alleges facts surrounding several attempts to deliver the Subject Vehicle to authorized repair facilities of Defendant and rectify the situation. While Plaintiff alleges in the FAC that “Plaintiff could not have reasonably discovered facts giving rise to Plaintiffs claims until 23 November 21, 2020, at the earliest” (FAC ¶¶ 70, 75, 84), Plaintiff alleges (1) on or around August 23, 2019, she delivered the Subject Vehicle in with complaints which include that the transmission was inoperative (FAC ¶ 54); (2) on October 04, 2019, she delivered the Subject Vehicle with complaints that the transmission does not shift properly (FAC ¶ 56); (3) on March 05, 2020, she delievered the Subject Vehicle with complaints that the Subject Vehicle was jerking and Defendant’s authorized repair facility performed transmission service on the Subject Vehicle ; and (4) on August 25, 2020 she delivered the Subject Vehicle with complaints that the gears were not shifted properly and that a diagnostic system was used to review transmission operation (FAC ¶ 58). “[U]nder the delayed discovery rule, a cause of action accrues and the statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff has reason to suspect an injury and some wrongful cause, unless the plaintiff pleads and proves that a reasonable investigation at that time would not have revealed a factual basis for that particular cause of action.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 803.) Thus, it appears that the Second Cause of Action may have began to accrue on either March 05, 2020 or August 25, 2020. Three years from the earliest date, March 05, 2020 is March 05, 2020 and Plaintiff filed her action on February 21, 2023, within the three years.

 

The Court turns next to the elements which can be applied to fraudulent concealment and inducement.  

 

“The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: “ ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974 [internal quotation marks omitted].) “[T]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.)

 

The material fact alleged is the transmission defect.

 

The TSBs themselves are not alleged to have been disclosed to the public; however, Plaintiff’s FAC states that Defendant had knowledge from consumer complaints to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration ("NHTSA"). (See FAC ¶ 16.) Thus, Plaintiff could have known or discovered the alleged defects and Defendant did not actively prevent plaintiffs from discovering the facts. Thus, an issue exists as to concealment of the transmission defect and Defendant’s intent.

 

Thus, on its face, there is a deficiency within the FAC as to Second Cause of Action (Fraudulent Inducement-Concealment).

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Strike concerns the striking of punitive damages and is encompassed by the Demurrer as the punitive damages stem from the Second Cause of Action. Accordingly, Defendant’s  Motion to Strike is now MOOT.

 

-----

 

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿ 

 

An amendment to the FAC to rectify the deficiencies would mean discarding the allegations in the current pleading. This subjects any amendment to the sham pleading doctrine. Plaintiff cannot disregard her current factual allegations. Thus, it appears that leave to amend would not lead to a successful amendment.

 

Accordingly, the Court declines to allow leave to amend.

 

-----

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant American Honda’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendant American Honda’s Motion to Strike is MOOT.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Leave to Amend – “A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. A party may amend the pleading after the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike, upon stipulation by the parties. The time for responding to an amended pleading shall be computed from the date of service of the amended pleading.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 472(a).)¿ 

 

Trial courts may, in furtherance of justice and on any proper terms, allow a party to amend any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §473(a)(1); Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Association (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) Trial courts may also, in their discretion and after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §473(a); Branick, supra, 39 Cal.4th at 242.) Leave to amend is generally liberally granted, provided there is no statute of limitations concern. (Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 411.) Additionally, “judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.) Trial courts may deny the plaintiff’s leave to amend if there is prejudice to the opposing party, such as delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation. (Branic, supra, at 242.)¿ 

 

Under California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324, a motion to amend a pleading before trial must (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a).) A separate supporting declaration specifying (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier must accompany the motion. (Id., Rule 3.1324(b).)¿

Discussion

 

Application – Plaintiffs seek leave to amend to add three causes of action to the pleadings: (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (2) Fraudulent Concealment; and (3) Misrepresentation, as well as to expand the allegations in its operative First Amended Complaint (“Complaint”) based on information recently obtained in discovery in this action. These changes include a request for punitive damages in the proposed Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).

 

Plaintiffs present that the changes stem from responses received during discovery. Plaintiffs further present that the motion is brought in good faith, the proposed amendments are not futile and are necessary for Plaintiffs to vindicate the merits of their claims, that there was no delay in bringing this motion, there will be no prejudice, and the amendment involves an evolution in legal theory which would make admissible evidence damaging to the opposing party is not the kind of “prejudice” the court will consider. Plaintiffs describe the nature and basis of the amendments and argue that all causes of action relate back to allegations within the FAC. Filed concurrently with the motion is a declaration from Plaintiffs’ counsel, Kat Kazemi (“Kazemi”) which appears to address the requirements set out in California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324.

 

PRMC argues that while amendments may be liberally allowed, the trial court’s discretion will not be disturbed on appeal unless it has clearly been abused and it is proper for a court to deny leave to amend if a proposed amendment fails to state a cause of action or if the amendment will be futile. PRMC argues that (1) as to the proposed Breach of Fiduciary Care claim, it is written as on behalf of the Estate of Decedent and alleges only damages to Susan; (2) as to the proposed claim for Fraudulent Concealment, the proposed claim fails to allege that PRMC failed to disclose any of the allegations to Decedent, that PRMC intended to defraud Decedent, and that Decedent would not acted as he did if he had known of the above listed alleged concealed or suppressed facts and that proposed pleading once again mentions Susan though it is only on behalf of Decedent’s Estate; and (3) as to the claim for Misrepresentation, the proposed SAC does not allege specific facts (i.e., ow, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered) and that the cause of action is alleged by Susan and does not apply to “Plaintiffs” in the plural. PRMC argues that none of the claims can be rectified by amendment. PRMC also argues that as Plaintiffs fail to state facts sufficient to constitute any of the proposed additional causes of action, Plaintiffs’ prayers for exemplary and punitive damages fail as a matter of law.

 

Plaintiffs argue that PRMC provides no valid arguments as the arguments are more suited for a demurrer than leave to amend the complaint. Plaintiffs further argue that when a family member is incapacitated or passes away, the fiduciary duties that arise from that relationship extend to the family members of the patient under Wohlgemuth v. Meyer (1956) 139 Cal.App.2d 326, and so, PRMC owed a fiduciary duty to Decedent and Susan. Plaintiffs present that PRMC does not dispute that they never submitted the statutory required adverse event report and that they failed to notify the coroner that a death occurred at their facility pursuant to Cal. Health & Saf. Code §102850. Plaintiffs emphasize that PRMC acknowledges “a right to “any penalties or punitive damages the decedent would have been entitled to recover had the decedent lived.” (Opposition at 5).” (Reply 3:12-14.) Plaintiffs also argue that Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 377.34, as amended in 2022, allows Susan to recover all damages for the deceased party. Plaintiff presents that her proposed claim for fraudulent concealment is viable as the information produced in discovery provide a basis for the claim. Plaintiff also that her prayer for punitive damages are derivative and survive as her fraud claims are properly pled.    

 

As to PRMC’s first argument, the Court notes that Susan is in the action individually, as the Administrator of Decedent’s Estate, and as Decedent’s Successor in Interest. It is unclear, based on the substance of the pleading, whether the claim is meant to be on behalf of Susan. The Court notes that this is not the first time this has occurred as the FAC alleges a survivor action written as brought by Susan alone but alleging a survivor cause of action. (See FAC at First Cause of Action Header and ¶ 30; see also Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 377.30.) “The law respects form less than substance.” (See Cal. Civ. Code § 3528.) Thus, it appears in either case that the claim of Fraudulent Concealment may be rectified. The Court inquires.

 

As to PRMC’s second argument, “the elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are:(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3)the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.) The proposed SAC lists various facts that were allegedly not disclosed. The proposed facts do switch between the plural use of “Plaintiffs,” actions directed at Susan, as well as actions directed at Decedent. It is unclear as to which Plaintiff this proposed claim applies to. The damages alleged are as to Susan only. (See Proposed SAC ¶ 116.) However, the claim is written as brought by Decedent’s Estate. (See Proposed SAC Fifth Cause of Action Header.) As the claim is unclear, it appears that this, too, may be rectified by amendment.

 

Finally, as to PRMC’s third argument, there are two forms of misrepresentation. Plaintiff’s proposed sac alleges intentional misrepresentation. (See Proposed SAC ¶¶ 122-24.) “The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: “ ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974; see also Service by Medallion, Inc. v. Clorox Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1807, 1816.) Plaintiff has alleged that PRMC intentionally failed to disclose: (1) Decedent was able to detach the wires on his body for a self-search of the closest bathroom, (2) PRMC staff only first discovered Decedent was alone in the PRMC emergency department bathroom upon hearing an individual breathing laboriously behind the bathroom door but unable to get out, and (3) PRMC’s emergency room staff did not have ready access to the key to the PRMC bathroom door in the emergency department to unlock it, nor the fact that the key was nowhere to be found by the PRMC staff in the emergency department. (Proposed SAC ¶¶ 122-24.) However, it also appears that the gravamen of the claim is that PRMC informed Susan that Decedent was supervised at every minute and that the alleged failures indicate otherwise. Though not written as clearly as possible, the claim for misrepresentation alleges that (1) PRMC falsely represented to Susan that Decedent was being supervised, (2) the allegations in paragraphs 122 to 124 in the proposed SAC show knowledge of the falsity, and (3) intent may exist as “fraudulent intent is an issue for the trier of fact to decide” (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1061; see also Proposed SAC ¶ 128); and (4) damages in the form of Susan’s continued extreme state of unrest, frustration, uncertainty, and emotional distress. Reliance is unclear as paragraph 130, which alleges the reliance, jumps from Decedent’s death to autopsy. As with the other claims this, too, may be rectified by amendment.

 

As the claims may be rectified, there is a potential basis for punitive damages as the punitive damages are derivative of the proposed causes of action.

 

It is the Court’s intent to grant this motion upon the filing of a new proposed SAC that address the current deficiencies in the attached proposed SAC.

Conclusion

 

It is the Court’s intent to grant this motion upon the filing of a new proposed SAC that address the current deficiencies in the attached proposed SAC.

Case Number: 23AVCV01329    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a negligence action. Plaintiff Amarilla Perez Pivara (“Plaintiff”) presents that she is the owner of a quadruplex in Lancaster California and that she contracted with Defendant Good Seed CDC (“Defendant”) to locate a suitable tenant. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant recommended an individual, Gary Chadwell (“Chadwell”), as a suitable renter; Defendant’s representative sponsored Chadwell as a suitable tenant; both parties went through negotiations with the Los Angeles County Housing Authority[1] to have Chadwell’s rent subsidized, and Chadwell moved in on August 15, 2023. Plaintiff contends that upon Chadwell’s move in, Chadwell would (1) yell, threaten, and intimidate other tenants for no apparent reason; (2) throw trash and bottles on the open common areas, endangering those around him; (3) bring homeless people onto the premises who would bring what appeared to be stolen bicycle parts onto the property; and (4) regularly create disturbances to the point the police were called. Plaintiff presents that she found out, after Chadwell had moved, in that Chadwell was facing criminal charges for making terroristic threats, resisting arrest, and brandishing a deadly weapon in a threatening matter and that Chadwell had been removed from Uncle Dave’s Housing, a place for homeless men. Plaintiff emphasizes that these facts were not disclosed to her by Defendant. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant breached a duty of care to do a background check on Chadwell prior to sponsoring him for tenancy to Plaintiff and by failing to disclose any of Chadwell’s foregoing behavior. Plaintiff presents that she would not have rented to Chadwell had this behavior been disclosed and that it was foreseeable to Defendant that Chadwell would be a problem tenant because of his violent tendencies and pending criminal charges. Plaintiff informs the party and the Court that she was forced to file an unlawful detainer (“UD”) action against Chadwell. Plaintiff alleges damages for lost rent, eviction services, danger to other tenants, and emotional distress.

 

On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging one cause of action for General Negligence.

 

On February 07, 2024, Defendant filed this Demurrer.

 

On March 13, 2024, a Case Management Conference (“CMC”) was held.

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition.

 

On March 25, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a).)¿A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Ibid.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is¿whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified Dec. 23, 2003.)¿

 

Meet and Confer Requirement – Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.) It appears that this requirement has not been met. (See Decl. Daniel C. Lapidus ¶¶ 2-4.) “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(4).) The Court addresses the Demurrer on its merits.

  

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – Defendant demurs to the Complaint under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e) failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to state a cause of action for negligence as “no duty exists ‘to control the conduct of another, [or] to warn those endangered by such conduct;’ ” and  “ ‘[a] person who has not created a peril is not liable in tort merely for failure to take affirmative action to assist or protect another unless there is some relationship between them which gives rise to a duty to act.’ ” (Demurrer 5:11-12 [citing Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal.3d 197 and Williams v. State of California (1983) 34 Cal.3d 18, 23].) Defendant concedes that certain relationships are recognized as special which gives rise to an expectation of protection from a defendant such as the relationship between parents and children, colleges and students, employers and employees, common carriers and passengers, and innkeepers and guests. Defendant presents that the Complaint contains no allegations which exist to create any duty owed by Defendant to Plaintiff as, accepting the truth of Plaintiff’s allegations, the only relationship is Defendant assisted Plaintiff in finding a tenant which does not constitute a special relationship, nor can Plaintiff amend her Complaint to allege such a duty.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s negligent referral represented a dangerous condition, citing Garcia v. Holt (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 600, 604 (“Garcia”). Plaintiff emphasizes Cal. Civ. Code § 1714, emphasizing the foreseeability of the harm Plaintiff has allegedly suffered. Plaintiff cites to case law regarding the standard for a demurrer, leave to amend, and demurrers on uncertainty.

 

In Reply, Defendant reiterates its arguments that the relationship between the parties is not one that gives rise to a duty to protect. Defendant provides the following arguments related to Plaintiff’s Opposition: (1) Plaintiff admits that she is a landlord, and (2) Plaintiff has not given any facts that show that she can amend her Complaint to show that Defendant exercised any degree of protection over Plaintiff or her property, let alone a level of superior control" required to create a duty of care. Defendant provides a new argument that, assuming a special relationship existed, no duty of care would exist as the acts of a third-party tenant could not be foreseeable under J.L. v. Children’s Institute, Inc. (2009) Cal.App.4th 388.

 

The salutary rule is that points raised in a reply brief for the first time will not be considered unless good cause is shown for the failure to present them before.” (Balboa Ins. Co. v. Aguirre (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 1002, 1010.) “This rule is based on the same solid logic applied in the appellate courts, specifically, that ‘[p]oints raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument.’ ” (Id. at 1538 [citing American Drug Stores, Inc. v. Stroh (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1446, 1453; Browne v. County of Tehama (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 704, 720, fn. 10].) As such, the Court does not consider Defendant’s new argument concerning foreseeability submitted in the Reply brief.

 

While Defendant does not present that the Complaint is uncertain, the Complaint raises questions as to this Demurrer. Defendants argue under tort law, specifically citing to Cal. Civ. Code § 1714 and Williams v. State of California (1983) 34 Cal.3d 18, 23 [““ ‘[a] person who has not created a peril is not liable in tort merely for failure to take affirmative action to assist or protect another unless there is some relationship between them which gives rise to a duty to act.’ ”]; however, it is not clear that this cause of action is based solely in tort.

 

While Plaintiff has brought a cause of action titled “General Negligence,” Plaintiff clearly alleges a contractual relationship in which Defendant was to provide a suitable tenant and that the tenant was unsuitable. (See Complaint General Negligence Attachment.) Defendant does not dispute this in its moving papers. Defendant concedes that “the only relationship between the parties is one in which Defendant assisted Plaintiff in finding a tenant.” (Demurrer 5:28-6:1.) The Complaint further does not provide where the alleged duties (i.e., duty of care to do a background check on Chadwell prior to sponsoring him for tenancy to Plaintiff and by failing to disclose any of Chadwell’s foregoing behavior) stem from. Rather, the parties provide what they believe to be the basis for these duties in their papers filed for this Demurrer. As such, the Complaint is uncertain. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(f).)

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.) 

 

Here, the Complaint is uncertain and may be rectified by an amendment.

 

Accordingly, leave to amend is allowed.

 

-----

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Good Seed CDC’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.
