MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES FROM POTOMAC RIVER PRODUCTIONS, INC.

Plaintiff Dan Sackheim (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Potomac River Productions, Inc. (“Potomac”) to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, numbers 1-10, 13, 18, 19, 21, 22, 27-29, 31, 33, and 34-42, and (2) awarding sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Potomac in the amount of $5,469.

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to compel Potomac’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, numbers 1-10 because, given the specially defined term “DESCRIBE IN DETAIL,” those interrogatories contain subparts and are compound in violation of Code of Civil Procedure 2030.060, subdivision (f), and therefore Potomac’s objections on the ground that they are compound are not without merit.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(3).)

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to compel Potomac’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, numbers 13, 18, 27-29, 31, and 33-38 because the answers to those interrogatories are not evasive or incomplete.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(1).)

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to compel Potomac’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, number 19 because Potomac’s objections to that interrogatory are not without merit since the interrogatory calls for information that is protected by the work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(3).)

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Potomac’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, numbers 21-22 and 39-42 because the answers to those interrogatories are evasive and incomplete.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(1).)

In light of the mixed results of the court’s ruling, the court finds that the circumstances presented make the imposition of sanctions unjust and therefore denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Potomac.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES FROM SKYDANCE TELEVISION, LLC

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

 

order to show cause re: whether any of plaintiff’s causes of action or defendants’ affirmative defenses in phase Ii of bifurcated trial are to be tried by the jury or the court

 

 

Order to Show Cause re: Whether any of Plaintiff’s Causes of Action or Defendants’ Affirmative Defenses in Phase II of Bifurcated Trial are to be Tried by the Jury or the Court

On June 7, 2019, the court entered, pursuant to the stipulation of plaintiff CEP America-California, d/b/a Vituity (“Plaintiff”) and defendants Heritage Provider Network, Inc., Regal Medical Group, Inc., Lakeside Medical Group, Inc., Oasis Independent Medical Associates, Inc., Desert Medical Group, Inc., Higher Desert Medical Corporation, A Medical Group, Affiliated Doctors of Orange County Medical Group, Bakersfield Family Medical Group, Inc.,[1] Sierra Medical Group, Inc., and Coastal Communities Medical Group, Inc. (“Defendants”), an order bifurcating the trial in this action into two phases.  Phase I was limited to the issue of the reasonable value of the health care services provided by Plaintiff to Defendants.  (June 7, 2019 Order, ¶ 1.)  Upon the ruling in the Phase I Trial, the parties were to report back to the court as to whether they agreed on the damages calculation, whether they agreed to settle the case, and if they were unable to agree to the damages calculation, the method by which the parties wish to resolve the remaining disputes.

On April 10, 2023, the jury returned the Verdict Form to the court, finding that the reasonable value of the services provided by Plaintiff’s doctors on the claims at issue is $452.00 per claim.  Thereafter, on August 22, 2023, the parties submitted a “Joint Report Regarding Phase II,” as to the issues to be decided in Phase II.  Plaintiff asserted that Phase II will address the following: (1) Plaintiff’s third cause of action requesting declaratory relief, and (2) Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for unfair business practices, including its request for injunctive relief, calculation of damages, and prejudgment interest.  (Aug. 22, 2023, Joint Report, p. 3:2-8.)  Defendants asserted that the following factors are relevant to those issues: (1) whether the patient and/or services at issue were Defendants’ financial responsibility; (2) whether the patients had active insurance or were assigned to Defendants when services were rendered; (3) whether the services were rendered as billed; (4) Defendants’ third affirmative defense of unclean hands, and whether that bars recovery on any or all patient claims; (5) Defendants’ entitlement to offsets;  (6) whether Defendants properly paid certain claims; and (7) Defendants’ 46th affirmative defense for illegality, i.e., whether some or all of the patient claims at issue are barred.  (Aug. 22, 2023 Joint Report, p. 2:8-16.) 

The parties dispute whether these issues should be resolved by the court or a jury.

First, the court finds that Defendants’ affirmative defense of unclean hands shall be tried by the court.  (Aug. 29, 2023 Joint Report, p. 3:12-13 [stating that an issue in Phase II is whether Defendants’ third affirmative defense for unclean hands bars recovery on all or some patient claims].)

“As a general matter . . ., the California Constitution affords a right to a jury trial in common law actions at law that were triable by a jury in 1850, but not to suits in equity that were not triable by a jury at that time.”  (LaFace v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 388, 395.)  “In making this evaluation [courts] look to the gist of the action: whether the nature of the rights involved and the facts of the particular case show that it is legal and therefore cognizable at law.”  (Ibid.)  “‘Complications arise when legal and equitable issues (causes of action, requested remedies, or defenses) are asserted in a single lawsuit.’  [Citation.]  In that situation, ‘The lawsuit is rarely treated as a single unit for purposes of determining the right to a jury trial.  [Citations.]  In most instances, separate equitable and legal issues are “kept distinct and separate,” with legal issues triable by a jury and equitable issues triable by the court.’”  (Judicial Council of California v. Jacobs Facilities, Inc. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 882, 915 [internal citation omitted].)

Defendants do not dispute that they are pursuing equitable defenses, and the court agrees that the defense of unclean hands is equitable in nature.  (Opp., p. 2:14; Kendall-Jackson Winery, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 970, 978; Padideh v. Moradi (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 418, 425 [doctrine of unclean hands “ ‘is an equitable rationale for refusing a plaintiff relief where principles of fairness dictate that the plaintiff should not recover, regardless of the merits of [their] claim’”].)  However, Defendants contend that the issues concerning the applicability of its defense of unclean hands should be submitted to a jury because the gist of Plaintiff’s action is for quantum meruit, which is a legal, and not equitable, claim.  (Chodos v. Borman (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 76, 97 [“a claim for quantum meruit is considered an action at law, not in equity”].)

“When equitable defenses are interposed to a legal cause of action, the ‘proper rule’ is for the court to hear and dispose of the equitable defenses first, before submitting the legal claim to a jury.”  (Judicial Council of California, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th at p. 915.)  But “the trial court has discretion whether to submit an equitable defense to the jury[,]” and the exercise of that discretion may be proper if the theories in support of an equitable defense are intertwined with the legal causes of action.   (Unilogic, Inc. v. Burroughs Corp. (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 612, 622, 623.)

The court finds that Defendants have not shown that the issues relating to their equitable defense of unclean hands are so “intertwined with” Plaintiff’s remaining legal causes of action that the court should exercise its discretion to submit those issues to the jury.  (Unilogic, Inc., supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at p. 623.)  Thus, the court finds that it is proper to hear and dispose of Defendants’ affirmative defense of unclean hands first and by a bench trial.  (Judicial Council of California, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th at p. 915.)

Second, the court finds that Defendants’ affirmative defense of illegality shall be tried by the court.  (Aug. 29, 2023 Joint Report, p. 3:15-16 [stating that an issue in Phase II is whether Defendants’ 46th affirmative defense of illegality bars recovery on all or some patient claims].)  

This defense, which is based on an alleged violation of Penal Code section 550, also appears to be equitable in nature.  (State ex rel. Rapier v. Encino Hospital Medical Center (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 811, 837 [the “gist” of a cause of action brought pursuant to Insurance Frauds Prevention Act, which subjects a person who violates of Penal Code section 550 to penalties and permits the granting of other equitable relief “is equitable”]; Ins. Code, § 1871.7, subd. (b); First Amended Answer, p. 10, ¶ 48.)  Defendants did not directly address this defense in their brief, and thus have not shown that the issues to be presented in support of their illegality defense are so intertwined with the issues to be decided in Phase II that it should be submitted to a jury.  The court therefore finds that it is proper to hear and dispose of Defendants’ illegality defense first and by a bench trial.  (Judicial Council of California, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th at p. 915.)

Third, the court finds that Defendants’ affirmative defense of setoff/offset shall be tried by the court because (1) “[t]he affirmative defense of setoff is equitable in nature [citation] and so would not ordinarily be decided by the jury which decides the amount of damages[,]” and    (2) Defendants have not shown that the issues regarding this affirmative defense are so intertwined with the remaining issues to be decided in Phase II that the court should exercise its discretion to allow it to be decided by a jury.  (Aug. 29, 2023 Joint Report, p. 3:13-14 [stating that an issue in Phase II is whether Defendants are entitled to any offsets]; Los Angeles Unified School District v. Torres Construction Corp. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 480, 500 [internal citation omitted].)

Fourth, the court notes that Plaintiff stated, in the parties’ joint report, that Phase II will also address Plaintiff’s third cause of action for declaratory relief and fourth cause of action for violation of unfair business practices in violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200.  The parties did not address these remaining causes of action in their papers.  However, the court has determined that these causes of action are in equity and therefore shall be tried by the court.  (Artus v. Gramercy Towers Condominium Assn. (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 923, 930 [“declaratory relief is an equitable remedy and need not be awarded if the circumstances do not warrant”]; Nationwide Biweekly Administration, Inc. v. Superior Court of Alameda County (2020) 9 Cal.5th 279, 334 [“in causes of action under the UCL . . . seeking injunctive relief and civil penalties, the gist of the actions is equitable, and there is no right to a jury trial in such actions under California law either as a statutory or constitutional matter”].) 

Finally, the remaining issues in dispute regarding Plaintiff’s first cause of action for breach of implied contract/quantum meruit and second cause of action for common counts shall be tried to a jury after the court completes the non-jury trial on the equitable claims and defenses discussed above.  (Chodos, supra, 227 Cal.App.4th at p. 97 [quantum meruit is a legal claim]; Rubinstein v. Fakheri (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 797, 809 [common count claim “is legal in nature”].)

The court orders plaintiff CEP America-California, d/b/a Vituity to give notice of this ruling.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.
