DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV04058    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

BONNIE VERONESE, et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV04058

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

DORYLUS, LP, et al.

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          June 3, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Dorylus, LP and joinder by Lavi & Associates, Inc. and South Bay Management Services

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Bonnie Veronese and Steven Wohn

Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The motion to strike is DENIED as to para. 49 and prayer at 2. as to fifth cause of action and prayer at 5. for attorney’s fees in the complaint.

The motion is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to paras. 24, 32, 36, and 53 and prayer at 2. as to first, second, third, and sixth causes of action.

Defendants are ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

            On December 5, 2023, plaintiffs Bonnie Veronese and Steven Wohn filed a complaint against Dorylus, LP, Lavi & Associates, Inc., and South Bay Management Services for (1) premises liability, (2) gross negligence, (3) personal injury, (4) breach of contract, (5) breach of warranty of habitability, and (6) NIED.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  CCP § 436(a).  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  CCP §436(b).  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  CCP §436.  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Dorylus, LP (and joinder by Lavi & Associates, Inc.) requests that the Court strike paras. 24 (under the first cause of action for premises liability), 32 (under the second cause of action for gross negligence), 36 (under the third cause of action for personal injury), 49 (under the fifth cause of action for breach of warranty of habitability), and 53 (under the sixth cause of action for NIED) and prayer at para. 2 for punitive damages as to the first, second, third, fifth, and sixth causes of action and prayer at para. 5 for reasonable attorney’s fees as allowed by statute.

            The complaint alleges that the property at issue is 204 Palos Verdes Blvd., Unit B, Redondo Beach, 90277.  Complaint, ¶1.  Plaintiffs resided at the property from January 20, 2014 to August 2023.  Id., ¶2.  Defendant Dorylus owned the property during plaintiffs’ residency.  Id., ¶3.  Defendant Lavi & Associates was the leasing agent and the property manager during the first several years of plaintiffs’ residency.  Id., ¶4.  South Bay Management Services was the property manager during the last approximately year and a half of their tenancy.  Id., ¶5.  The subject property had many defects and problems.  Among the defects and problems were the following:  a. plaintiffs made years of complaints regarding the plumbing from the main bathroom to the back bathroom.  The plumbing and defects resulted in continual poorly working plumbing backing up regularly and routinely clogged drains.  b. Mildew collected in the back bathroom because the air vent did not work.  c. There were several leaks in the dining room and kitchen, which were not timely addressed and not repaired properly.  Even after two attempts to repair there were continuing leaks resulting in so much moisture that mushrooms grew from the ceiling.  d. Windowsills were bubbling and cracking due to the infiltration of moisture.  e. Walls constantly cracked with paint coming off in large pieces from moisture in the unit.  All of the above created constant mold and mildew in the unit, which permeated clothing, accessories, furniture, linens, and other personal property.  f. The oven barely worked, and the dishwasher was always broken.  g. Plaintiffs had to install their own screens because none of the windows had screens and the landlord would not install them.  h. There were ants in the summer so severe that no food could be left outside the refrigerator.  Frequent requests that the landlord spray for bugs were ignored.  Id., ¶10.

            The complaint also alleges that throughout their tenancy, plaintiffs suffered from sinus congestion, coughing, sneezing, cramps, tearing eyes, headaches, lack of focus and concentration, signs of neuropathy, and other symptoms for which they did not know the cause.  Id., ¶11.  In the spring of 2023, plaintiffs discovered that Bonnie was pregnant.  During conversations with her doctors, she discovered that the mold and mildew in the apartment was probably causing their symptoms.  She was also advised to move as soon as possible because exposure to toxic mold would be harmful to their unborn child.  Id., ¶12.  They immediately relocated to a relative’s home.  Id., ¶13.  Many of their belongings have had to be stored and are contaminated with mold.  Id., ¶14.  Since leaving the premises, they have consulted with physicians who have confirmed that their symptoms are related to toxic mold exposure over a lengthy period of time.  Id., ¶15.  Defendants repeatedly complained to defendants about the despicable conditions on and in the premises.  Repairs were insufficient, untimely, or never done, which left the premises in a dangerous and unhealthy condition.  Id., ¶17.

            Defendants argue that the allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages in that they do not show that defendants acted with fraud, malice, and oppression. 

            In opposition, plaintiffs argue that the allegations are sufficient.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that the allegations are sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages under the fifth cause of action for breach of habitability (para. 49).  A tenant may state a cause of action in tort against his landlord for damages resulting from a breach of the implied warranty of habitability.”  Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal. App. 3d 903, 918-19; see also Smith v. David (1981) 120 Cal. App. 3d 101, 112 n.3 (a tenant “may seek general and punitive damages for . . . breach of warranty [of habitability]”).  The court in Stoiber found that plaintiff stated sufficient facts to support exemplary damages when she “alleged that defendant had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises including leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions . . . [and] alleged defendants ‘In maintaining said nuisance, . . . acted with full knowledge of the consequences thereof and the damage being caused to plaintiff, and their conduct was willful, oppressive and malicious.’”  Stoiber, supra, at 920 (citation omitted).

Plaintiffs have alleged breach of the warranty of habitability.  The allegations support malice in that defendants had actual knowledge of the defective conditions, defendants failed to act, defendants maintained the defective conditions, and defendants acted with full knowledge of the consequences and the damage (health and safety) being caused to plaintiffs.

As to paras. 24 (under the first cause of action), 32 (under the second cause of action), 36 (under the third cause of action), and 53 (under the sixth cause of action) and prayer at para. 2 for punitive damages as to the first, second, third, and sixth causes of action, the allegations are insufficient.  “Inasmuch as Civil Code section 3294 requires as a prerequisite to the recovery of punitive damages that the defendant ‘has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,’ the cases have uniformly recognized that proof of negligence, even gross negligence, or recklessness is insufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.”  Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87 (citations omitted).

As to prayer at 5. for attorney’s fees, defendants make no argument.

The motion is therefore DENIED as to para. 49 and prayer at 2. as to fifth cause of action and GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to paras. 24, 32, 36, and 53 and prayer at 2. as to first, second, third, and sixth causes of action.  The motion is DENIED as to prayer for attorney’s fees.

ORDER

 The motion is DENIED as to para. 49 and prayer at 2. as to fifth cause of action and prayer at 5. for attorney’s fees.

The motion is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to paras. 24, 32, 36, and 53 and prayer at 2. as to first, second, third, and sixth causes of action.

Defendants are ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of the ruling.
