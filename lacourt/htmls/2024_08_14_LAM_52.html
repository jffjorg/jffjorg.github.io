Case Number: 21STCV37269    Hearing Date: August 14, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Richard Hester’s Motion to Vacate Order Compelling Case to Arbitration

Plaintiff Richard Hester moves to vacate the court’s order compelling arbitration of this action.  Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, subdivision (a)(1) provides that in employment or consumer arbitration, the party who the arbitration agreement commits a material breach if it fails to pay “fees or costs required to continue the arbitration proceeding … within 30 days after the due date.”  Upon such a breach, the employee may unilaterally “[w]ithdraw the claim from arbitration.”  (Id., subd. (b)(1).)

Defendant Antelope Valley Healthcare District opposes this motion in part on the grounds that the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) preempts Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98.  Plaintiff did not respond to defendant’s argument that the FAA applies to the parties’ arbitration agreement.  The court finds the FAA applies. 

The Court of Appeal has issued conflicting opinions on whether the FAA preempts Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.97 and 1281.98.  (Keeton v. Tesla, Inc. (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 26, 35-41 & fn. 5; Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc. (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 222, 242-244 (Hernandez).)  When “appellate decisions are in conflict,” the trial court “can and must make a choice between the conflicting decisions.”  (Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court of Santa Clara County (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 456.)

The court finds Hernandez more persuasive than the decisions holding the FAA does not preempt Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.97 and 1281.98.  There, the court reasoned, “[A] state law that discriminates on its face against arbitration, such as barring arbitration of a particular type of claim, will be preempted by the FAA.”  (Hernandez, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at p. 243.)  The court held, “[S]ection 1281.97 violates the equal-treatment principle because it mandates findings of material breach and waiver for late payment that do not apply generally to all contracts or even to all arbitrations.  Under California contract law, defenses to enforcement of a contract are generally questions for the trier of fact and subject to doctrines such as substantial compliance, but section 1281.97 imposes a stricter requirement, mandating a finding of material breach and waiver as a matter of law in consumer and employment arbitration contracts, and making it harder to enforce arbitration agreements in those matters.”  (Ibid.)  This reasoning applies equally to section 1281.98, which the Court of Appeal has noted is “almost identical” to section 1281.97.  (Keeton v. Tesla, Inc., supra, (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th at p. 35.) 

The court also finds the dissenting opinion in Hohenshelt v. Superior Court (2024) 99 Cal.App.5th 1319 (review granted, No. S284498) persuasive.  Justice Wiley wrote, “This California statute ‘singles out arbitration agreements for disfavored treatment.’  No other contracts are voided on a hair-trigger basis due to tardy performance.  Only arbitration contracts face this firing squad.”  (Id. at p. 481, dis. opn. of Wiley, J.).) 

The court therefore finds the FAA preempts Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98.  Plaintiff may not withdraw the matter from arbitration.

            Plaintiff Richard Hester’s motion to vacate order compelling case to arbitration is denied.


Case Number: 22STCV13106    Hearing Date: August 14, 2024    Dept: 52

Farmers Defendants’ Motion for Protective Order Regarding Plaintiff’s Document Requests

            Defendants Farmers Group, Inc.; Farmers Insurance Exchange; Fire Insurance Exchange; Truck Insurance Exchange; Mid-Century Insurance Company; and Farmers New World Life Insurance (collectively, “Farmers”) move for a protective order relieving them from responding to plaintiff Maureen Martinez’s requests for production of documents and document requests include in deposition notices.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020(a) provides, “The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  Section 2019.030(a) provides, “The court shall restrict the frequency or extent of use of” discovery “if it determines either of the following: (1) The discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.  (2) The selected method of discovery is unduly burdensome or expensive, taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.” 

For requests for production, section 2031.060(b) provides, “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.”  A protective order may direct that “all or some of the items or categories of items in the demand need not be produced or made available at all.”  (Id., subd. (b)(1).)

Farmers shows good cause for the protective order they seek.  Plaintiff has propounded seven sets of requests for production totaling 667 categories of documents to defendant Farmers Group, Inc.  (Magnanimo Decl., Exs. A, B, C, D, E, F, H.)  Plaintiff has propounded hundreds of other requests to other defendants and in notices of depositions.  Farmers’ counsel estimates responding to all requests would require 1,000 hours of work.  (Id., ¶ 21.) 

This vast amount of discovery is unduly burdensome and expensive.  Plaintiff requests numerous categories of documents that, if discoverable at all, concern collateral issues.  For example, plaintiff requests “all Corporate Bank Statements for” Farmers Group Inc., Farmers Insurance Exchange, Truck Insurance Exchange, Farmers New World Life Insurance Company, and Mid-Century Insurance Company from 2016 to 2020.  (RFP Nos. 517-540.)  She further requests “all emails between [CEO] Jeff Dailey and” four other people (RFP Nos. 499-502), “all annual reviews for Jeff Dailey” and 13 other employees over several years (RFP Nos. 541-554). 

The needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake do not justify this much discovery.  In this action, one plaintiff alleges employment and Labor Code claims and other related causes of action against Farmers.  The interrelationship of the various Farmers entities makes this case at most moderately more complex than the typical employment case.  Plaintiff’s discovery requests go beyond what is reasonable in this action.

Plaintiff’s Other Arguments

            Plaintiff argues defendants did not “promptly move for a protective order” as required.  (CCP § 2031.060(a).)  Defendants initially filed this motion on June 11, 2024, 35 days after plaintiff served requests for production, set seven.  In the circumstances, defendants’ motion was prompt enough.

            Plaintiff also argues defendants did not adequately meet and confer before filing this motion.  Defendants sent a meet and confer letter in January 2024 (Maile Reply Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. C), and participated in an informal discovery conference on March 27 (Magnanimo Decl., ¶ 19).  Defendants made a sufficient effort to informally resolve this dispute.  The volume of discovery plaintiff propounded made further efforts impractical and likely futile.      

Sanctions

            Farmers moves for $3,000 in sanctions against plaintiff and her counsel of record, Maryann P. Gallagher and Sherri Nazarian.  Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.030(c) provides, “The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Plaintiff unsuccessfully opposed this motion for a protective order.  Plaintiff did not act with substantial justification.  Sanctions are just under the circumstances.  Farmers reasonably incurred $3,000 in expenses.

Disposition

            Defendants’ Farmers Group, Inc.; Farmers Insurance Exchange; Fire Insurance Exchange; Truck Insurance Exchange; Mid-Century Insurance Company; and Farmers New World Life Insurance’s motion for protective order is granted. 

The court hereby orders that the Farmers defendants need not produce any documents or provide any further responses to the pending requests for production and the documents requested in deposition notices for Bill Matlock, Zoltan Nagy, Armine Ambartsumyan, William Wilson, David Reyes, Margaret Giles, Kirk Parker, and each defendant’s person most qualified on specified matters. 

Plaintiff may serve amended notices of depositions including no more than 10 document requests each.  Plaintiff may propound no more than 100 additional requests for production, aggregated among all six Farmers defendants.  Plaintiff may only propound additional requests for production with leave of court.   

Plaintiff Maureen Martinez and her counsel of record, Maryann P. Gallagher and Sherri Nazarian, are ordered to pay the Farmers defendants $3,000 in sanctions within 30 days.  Plaintiff and her two attorneys of record are jointly and severally liable for the sanctions.

 


Case Number: 22STCV13174    Hearing Date: August 14, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s Motion for a Stay Pending Appeal or in the Alternative to Continue Trial

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD) moves to stay this action pending appellate review of other cases involving an issue present in this action.  In the alternative, LAUSD moves to continue the trial.

LAUSD moved for judgment on the pleadings on plaintiff’s complaint on the basis that AB 218 violated the California Constitution’s prohibition on gifts of public funds.  The court denied the motion.  When LAUSD filed this motion to stay the action, the Court of Appeal had several pending cases on that issue.  None had been decided.  On July 31, 2024, the Court of Appeal issued a published decision holding AB 218 does not violate the prohibition on gifts of public funds.  (West Contra Costa Unified School District v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (July 31, 2024, No. A169314) ___ Cal.App.5th ___, 2024 WL 3593932.)  That opinion is binding authority on this court. 

After considering all relevant factors, the court finds neither staying this action nor continuing the trial would serve the interests of justice or judicial economy.

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s motion to stay the action or to continue the trial is denied.
