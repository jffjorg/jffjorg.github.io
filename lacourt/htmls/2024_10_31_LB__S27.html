DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00569    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: S27

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - SOUTH DISTRICT

ULISES CORTES, ET AL., Plaintiff(s), vs. CITY OF LOS ANGELES, ET AL., Defendant(s). ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 22LBCV00569 [TENTATIVE] ORDER CONDITIONALLY GRANTING PETITION TO APPROVE SETTLEMENT OF MINOR Dept. S27 8:30 a.m. October 31, 2024

Moving Party: Petitioner, Martha Cortes

Opposing Party: None filed

Notice: OK

1. Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Ulises Cortes and Dolce Cortes (a minor by and through her GAL, Martha Cortes) filed this action against Defendants, City of Los Angeles, County of Los Angeles, Jesus Cortes, and Ilda Cortes for damages arising out of a trip and fall. Ulises, while holding Dolce, tripped and fell on a city sidewalk adjacent to Jesus and Ilda’s property. Dolce suffered a concussion, hematoma, and chipped tooth as a result of the fall, but has completely recovered from her injuries.

2. Petition to Approve Settlement of MInor

The Corteses have agreed to settle all claims with Plaintiffs for the total amount of $105,000. If the settlement is approved, $5000 will be apportioned to Dolce and the other $100,000 will be apportioned to Ulises. Because Dolce is a minor, Plaintiffs seek court approval of the settlement.

If the settlement is approved, $1096.30 will be used for medical expenses, $1250 for attorneys’ fees, and $135 for costs of suit, leaving a balance of $2481.30 (this appears to be an error in the petition, and the correct amount should be $2518.70), which will be placed in a blocked account.

Petitioner, in explaining why $100,000 is being apportioned to Ulises and only $5000 to Dolce, states only, “The plaintiffs involved in this matter had differing injuries and duration of injuries from this traumatic event. Thus, the global settlement of $105,000.000 is to be distributed amongst the two Plaintiffs matter (sic) based on past medical specials, future specials, and extent and duration of injury.”

The Court, at the hearing, wishes to hear about Ulises’s injuries so it can determine whether the apportionment is fair and reasonable.

If the Court is satisfied that the apportionment is reasonable, and if the amount is corrected on all paperwork, the Court will approve the settlement.

Pursuant to CRC 7.952, Petitioner and Plaintiff must appear at the hearing unless their appearance is excused. Plaintiff is three years old, and therefore the Court excuses her from appearing at the hearing.

DATED: October 31, 2024 _____________________________________

MARK C. KIM Judge of the Superior Court

Case Number: 22STCV02259    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: S27

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - SOUTH DISTRICT

LEA ENCARNACION, Plaintiff(s), vs. CONAIR CORPORATION, ET AL., Defendant(s). ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 22STCV02259 [TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING HEARING ON MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT Dept. S27 8:30 a.m. October 31, 2024

Moving Party: Cross-Defendant, Transom Symphony OpCo, LLC

Opposing Party: Defendant and Cross-Complainant, Conair Corporation

Notice: OK

Transom filed this motion for summary judgment on 8/19/24, setting it for hearing on 10/31/24. Conair filed timely opposition papers on 10/15/24. Reply papers were due on or before 10/25/24. On 10/25/24, Transom filed two documents: responses to objections, and responses to the separate statement. On 10/28/24, Conair filed an opposition to Transom’s motion to strike and to Transom’s introduction of new evidence with the reply papers.

Notice: OK

Plaintiff filed this lawsuit on 10/03/23. Plaintiff filed proof of service of the summons, complaint, and related matters on Defendants on 12/04/23 and 1/09/23. Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint on 1/16/24, and proof of service of the FAC on 3/19/24.

Plaintiff propounded RFAs on the individual defendant on 6/04/24. On 8/02/24, having not received responses, Plaintiff filed this motion to deem RFAs admitted and for imposition of sanctions.

On 10/18/24, Defendants filed timely opposition to the motion. Defendants contend the motion is substantively moot because they have now served responses in substantial compliance with the Code. They contend their failure to timely respond was due to the fact that they had not yet retained counsel,

which they recently did. They therefore argue they are entitled to relief from their failure to respond and no sanctions should be imposed.

Any reply to the opposition was due on or before 10/24/24. Plaintiff has not filed reply papers.

The Court finds the motion is substantively moot. The only remaining issue is whether or not to impose sanctions. CCP §2033.280(c), unlike the companion sections regarding interrogatories and documents demands, makes imposition of monetary sanctions absolutely mandatory when a party fails to timely respond to requests for admissions.

The Court finds imposition of sanctions is appropriate for three reasons. First, as noted above, imposition of sanctions is absolutely mandatory. Second, Defendants’ contention in opposition to the request is that, per §2033.280(a), they are entitled to relief from their failure to serve timely responses; that code section, however, contemplates an affirmative motion for relief, not an argument made in opposition to a motion to deem the requests admitted. Third, Defendants have offered no explanation for their failure to retain an attorney between the time they were served with the complaint (in December of 2023 and January of 2024), or even the FAC (in March of 2024), and the time they actually answered (in August of 2024).

Plaintiff seeks imposition of sanctions in the amount of $1500 against the individual defendant, Mahir Marwan Waber. Plaintiff’s attorney declares he has billed four hours to prepare the moving papers at $300/hour, and will bill another hour to prepare for and appear at the hearing. The Court awards two hours of attorney time to prepare this basic discovery motion. No reply was filed and time for hearing should be minimal; the Court therefore awards .5 hours in connection with the hearing. The Court awards a total of 2.5 hours at the requested rate of $300/hour, or $750 in sanctions.

Defendant Waber is ordered to pay sanctions to Plaintiff, by and through his attorney of record, in the total amount of $750, within twenty days.

JANE KR DOE, ETC., Plaintiff(s), vs. LONG BEACH UNIFIED SCHOOL DIST., ET AL., Defendant(s). ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 23LBCV02391 [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS Dept. S27 8:30 a.m. October 31, 2024

Moving Party: Defendant, LBUSD

Opposing Party: Plaintiff, Jane KR Doe, a minor, by and through her GAL, Jane TR Doe

Notice: OK

1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Jane KR Doe, a minor, by and through her GAL, Jane TR Doe, filed this action against Defendants, LBUSD, Jill Baker, Rebecca Caverly, and Brianna Flotree for damages arising out of sexual assault perpetrated on Plaintiff by another student at the school. The individual defendants are the superintendent, principal, and teacher allegedly responsible for allowing the assault to occur.

Plaintiff’s operative complaint is her original complaint. It includes causes of action for negligence, negligent hiring, training, and supervision, and dangerous condition of public property. All causes of action are pled against all defendants.

2. Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

a. Parties’ Positions

Defendants move for judgment on the pleadings, contending:

· The individual defendants are immune from liability;

· Plaintiff failed to allege constructive notice that the student who assaulted her was dangerous;

· The complaint lacks specificity;

· No dangerous condition of public property has been pled.

Plaintiff opposes the motion. She argues her claims, made per §340.1, do not require specificity at the pleading stage. She contends the individual defendants are liable for the assault. She contends she has adequately alleged facts showing Defendants “knew or should have known” of the abuser’s propensity to assault. She contends she has adequately alleged a duty to supervise and protect students on campus, and the failure to carry that duty out rendered the property dangerous. She seeks leave to amend in the event the Court grants the motion.

Defendants, in reply, contend the case Plaintiff cites to stand for the position that lower specificity is required actually shows why Plaintiff’s complaint is deficient. They contend Plaintiff has cited only a single out-of-state federal case to support her position that the individual defendants bear liability for the abuse. They contend specificity is required and no constructive notice and/or dangerous condition is pled.

b. Individual Defendants

Defendants contend the individual defendants cannot be held responsible for the subject assault. Specifically, they contend the Coverdell Act provides for immunity to teachers, principals, and other

school professionals in this context. The Court has reviewed the Act and finds it has no application to this case. It provides immunity for “actions of the teacher…carried out…in furtherance of efforts to control, discipline, expel, or suspend a student or maintain order or control in the classroom or school.” Plaintiff has not alleged that Defendants were engaged in discipline or in acts intended to maintain order or control in the school. She alleges Defendants failed to prevent a childhood sexual assault. Defendants cite no authority applying the Act in this context, and the Court knows of none. The motion on this ground is therefore denied.

c. Constructive Knowledge

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges Defendants are liable for the abuse because (a) they knew or should have known of the abuser’s propensity for abuse, and (b) they failed to implement reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of future unlawful sexual conduct by the perpetrator (¶45).

Defendants rely on Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549 to support their position that Plaintiff cannot establish liability by showing Defendants failed to take reasonable preventatives steps or implement reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of future unlawful sexual conduct by the perpetrator. The Rutter Guide on Statutes of Limitations, §4:516.1, explains the following in this regard:

The California Supreme Court construed the knowledge requirement of a prior version of the statute of repose as requiring the victim “to establish that the nonperpetrator defendant had actual knowledge, constructive knowledge (as measured by the reason to know standard), or was otherwise on notice that the perpetrator had engaged in past unlawful sexual conduct with a minor and, possessed of this knowledge or notice, failed to take reasonable preventative steps or implement reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of future unlawful sexual conduct by the perpetrator.” [Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 C4th 531, 549, 67 CR3d 330, 343]

Comment: The version of the statute construed in Doe used the conjunctive “and,” whereas the current statute, including the 2019 amendment, uses the disjunctive term “or.” It is therefore unclear whether the limited statute of repose as now set forth in CCP § 340.1(c) can be overcome in two different ways—by showing actual or constructive knowledge of “any misconduct” by an employee, volunteer, representative or agent creating a “risk” of childhood sexual assault, or by showing the entity, regardless of lack of knowledge, failed to take reasonable steps or to implement safeguards to avoid acts of childhood sexual assault.

The Court finds the legislature would not have changed the word “and” to the word “or” when amending the statute unless it intended the change to have an effect. Defendants did not, in their moving papers, attempt to negate Plaintiff’s allegations concerning lack of reasonable steps to implement safeguards to avoid acts of childhood sexual assault.

Notably, Defendants spend significant time in their opening brief arguing liability against a public entity is statutory in nature, and failure to discharge a duty cannot give rise to liability absent a statutory basis. The statute, however, is §340.1(c), as discussed above. The motion for judgment on the pleadings on this ground is therefore denied.

d. Dangerous Condition of Public Property

Pursuant to Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1135, a public entity is not liable under §835 for a dangerous condition created by a third party, as opposed to a condition inherent in the property itself.

Plaintiff argues she has sufficiently pled a claim for dangerous condition of public property, relying on Jennifer C. v. LAUSD (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1320, 1323 to support her position. Jennifer was a

special needs student who brought suit against LAUSD for negligent supervision and dangerous condition of public property based on the fact that she was a special needs student and was assaulted by another special needs student on school grounds. The trial court granted summary judgment, and the appellate court reversed. With respect to dangerous condition, the appellate court found there were triable issues of material fact concerning whether the alcove where Jennifer was assaulted was dangerous, in that Jennifer raised triable issues concerning whether the area lacked proper lighting and visibility.

Plaintiff, at ¶93 of the complaint, alleges Defendants created a situation where special needs students had to walk in isolated and hidden areas of the building that could not be property supervised. At ¶37, Plaintiff alleges the perpetrator isolated her in a back hallway that was not visible or supervised.

Defendant argues, in reply, that Plaintiff alleges a hallway, whereas Jennifer alleged an alcove. The Court finds these are issues of fact for the fact-finder, not pleading issues. Plaintiff’s complaint is sufficient, under Jennifer, to state a claim for dangerous condition of public property. The motion for judgment on the pleadings is therefore denied.

e. Conclusion

The motion for judgment on the pleadings is denied in its entirety.
