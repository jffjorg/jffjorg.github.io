I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff John Newman (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Jose Alvarado (“Alvarado”), Ricardo Almanza Bernal (“Almanza”), Nissin International Transport U.S.A., Inc. (“Nissin”), and Does 1 to 100 (collectively, “Defendants”) for damages arising out of a motor vehicle accident. The complaint sets forth a single cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. The form complaint alleges all Defendants operated the motor vehicle, employed persons who operated the motor vehicle in the course of their employment, owned the motor vehicle, entrusted the motor vehicle, and that Defendants were the agents and employees of each other. (Compl. p. 4.)  

Nissin now moves for summary judgment against Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that it owed no duty to Plaintiff and that Plaintiff cannot establish causation. Plaintiff opposes the motion, and Nissin filed a reply.  

 

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE 

Nissin requests the Court to take judicial notice of Plaintiff’s complaint filed in this matter. The request is granted. (Evid. Code §452(d).)  

 

III. MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

  1.  Burdens on Summary Judgment 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (f)(2).)    

The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if the party does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; accord Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(c).) 

 

  1. Whether Facts Evince An Employment Relationship 

Here, the following material facts are undisputed. On November 9, 2018, Plaintiff was struck by a van that had backed up into him. The van driver, Alvarado, was a driver hired by Almanza Trucking. Almanza’s business, which has been in operation since 1996. Almanza became associated as an independent with a company called Fast Express in 1996, and Fast Express assigned jobs to Almanza to pick up and deliver loads. Almanza began making deliveries for Nissin through assignments by Fast Express in 1998. Almanza then parted ways with Fast Express in 1999, and began working directly with Nissin. Nissin did not guarantee when Almanza would receive work, nor did Nissin have a set schedule for pickups and deliveries. All jobs for Nissin to be performed by Almanza Trucking related to picking up merchandise in one place and taking it to another place, or taking it to Nissin. Nissin and Almanza Trucking entered into a monthly Agreement in 2018. Almanza Trucking uses other drivers to perform deliveries for Nissin, which includes defendant Alvarado. All vehicles and equipment used by Almanza Trucking were paid for by Almanza.  

Here, Nissin argues it is entitled to summary judgment because it cannot be held liable for the conduct of an independent contractor. Nissin has met its relatively low prima facie burden that Almanza, and by extension Alvarado, were independent contractors. Under common law, a person who hires an independent contractor is generally not liable to third parties for injuries caused by the contractor in performing work. “Central to this rule of nonliability was the recognition that a person who hired an independent contractor had ‘“no right of control as to the mode of doing the work contracted for.’” [Citations].” (Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689, 693.) The burden shifts to Plaintiff to present a triable issue of material fact. 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues there is an issue of material fact as to whether Alvarado was an independent contractor based on Borello & Sons, Inc. v. Department of Industrial Relations (1989) 48 Cal.3d 341. However, before proceeding on the merits, there are procedural issues with Plaintiff’s opposition that must be addressed.  

First, Plaintiff failed to comply with CCP §437c(b)(3), which provides that the separate statement “shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. Failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may constitute a sufficient ground, in the court's discretion, for granting the motion.” Here, Plaintiff’s separate statement disputes some of Nissin’s material facts, but fails to reference any supporting evidence. Rather, the separate statement generally cites Borello & Sons, Inc. v. Department of Industrial Relations (1989) 48 Cal.3d 341, and notably fails to reference the facts and evidence utilized in Plaintiff’s memorandum of points and authorities. “Opposition separate statements must cite to facts and evidence for the evidence to be considered by the court.”" (Champlin/GEI Wind Holdings, LLC v. Avery (2023) 92 Cal. App. 5th 218, 226.) 

Second, Plaintiff did not file any evidence with the Court. (See CCP 437c(b)(2) [“The opposition, where appropriate, shall consist of affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice shall or may be taken.”].) Plaintiff’s memorandum of points and authorities refers to an” Exhibit A, Deposition Transcript of Bernal” at pages 75-76, 77-78, and 97 in support of his contention that Nissin retained all necessary control over the delivery operation, including over the delivery driver Alvarado. However, the Court is unable to locate the referenced exhibit. Even if this Court were to consider only Nissin’s evidence, and on its own initiative cross-compare the page numbers cited by Plaintiff with those provided in Nissin’s exhibits, not all of the pages referenced by Plaintiff were provided in Nissin’s exhibits.  

Although the Court has discretion to grant Nissin’s motion based on the above-mentioned defects, it will not exercise that discretion at this time. “[A]n immediate grant of summary judgment is, in most instances, too harsh a consequence. “[T]he proper response in most instances, if the trial court is not prepared to address the merits of the motion in light of the deficient separate statement, is to give the opposing party an opportunity to file a proper separate statement....” [Citations].” (Collins v. Hertz Corp. (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 64, 74.) 

 

IV. CONCLUSION  

Based on the foregoing, the hearing on Nissin’s motion for summary judgment is CONTINUED to _______________ in Department 31 of Spring Street Courthouse. Plaintiff must submit a proper and responsive separate statement, including any and all evidence relied upon, pursuant to CCP §437c. The opposition curing the above-noted defects must be served and filed not less than 14 days preceding the continued hearing date. The reply shall be served and filed not less than five calendar days preceding the continued hearing date.

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Gigi Bangura (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Albert Dang, State of California, California State University, California State University of Northridge, CSU-Northridge, and Does 1 to 50 for damages arising from an automobile collision. Trial is currently set for May 30, 2024.  

On April 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to strike defendants Board of Trustees of the California State University (erroneously sued as “State of California, California State University, California State University of Northridge, and CSU Northridge”) and Albert Dang’s (collectively, “Defendants”) supplemental expert designation Misty Coffman (“Coffman”) and Taylor Ehrlich (“Ehrlich”), and contends that Defendants refuse to de-designate Dr. Christopher Warren Young Jr. (“Dr. Young”). 

On April 29, 2024, Defendants filed a notice of withdrawal of the expert witness designation of Dr. Young. Further, Defendants’ opposition filed on May 10, 2024, provides that Plaintiff has withdrawn her request to strike Ehrlich, and at issue is only Coffman’s designation. Plaintiff’s reply appears to support this contention that only expert Coffman is currently at issue. Thus, the Court limits its review to the contentions surrounding Coffman’s designation only, and finds the other issues raised in the moving papers moot.   

Preliminary, Plaintiff argues the motion should still be heard notwithstanding the discovery motion cut-off date. CCP § 2024.020 provides that “any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.” This code section does not preclude the Court from exercising its discretion to have a motion concerning discovery to be heard closer to the initial trial date or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set. (CCP § 2024.050.) Further, Defendants notably do not oppose the procedural aspect of the motion’s filing. The Court will therefore consider the motion on the merits.  

 

  1. Moving Argument  

Plaintiff contends that Defendants designated Coffman as a lifecare planner, which is duplicative of their lifecare planner Dr. Timothy Cushing (“Dr. Cushing”). Plaintiff asserts that despite Defendants’ contention during meet and confer efforts that Dr. Cushing is not a lifecare planner, Dr. Cushing had asserted at his deposition that he was hired as a lifecare planner even though he was not initially designated as a lifecare planner. Plaintiff argues Defendants did not seek leave to augment or amend their expert witness list under CCP §2034.620, and that the supplemental expert Coffman, a lifecare planner, is duplicative of Dr. Cushing’s designation. 

  1. Opposing Argument 

Defendant argues Dr. Cushing is a physiatrist who will opine on physical medicine and orthopedics, and that he was designated to opine on Plaintiff’s medical treatment, future treatment, and Plaintiff’s claimed injuries based on his review of Plaintiff’s medical records. Defendant contends that Dr. Cushing’s testimony that he would comment on the life care plan does not make him a life care planner, because he did not generate a life care plan in this matter. Defendant asserts Dr. Cushing’s designation is different from Coffman’s, whose purpose is to comment on life care costs.  

  1. Reply Argument 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants provide no support that the expert designation controls, and not the deposition testimony of their experts. Plaintiff avers Dr. Cushing reaffirmed multiple times throughout the deposition that he was hired to determine a life care plan, and the lifetime health care and other types needed related to the injuries allegedly sustained. 

  

II. MOTION TO EXCLUDE PLAINTIFF’S EXPERT WITNESSES  

  1. Legal Standard 

Parties may arrange a date to exchange expert witness information, and the failure to abide by the requirements for doing so once a demand for exchange is made may result in exclusion of the expert. (CCP §§ 2034.210, 2034.300.)  “Any party may make a demand for an exchange of information concerning expert trial witnesses without leave of court. A party shall make this demand no later than the 10th day after the initial trial date has been set, or 70 days before that trial date, whichever is closer to the trial date.”  (CCP § 2034.220 (emphasis added).)  “The demand shall specify the date for the exchange of lists of expert trial witnesses, expert witness declarations, and any demanded production of writings. The specified date of exchange shall be 50 days before the initial trial date, or 20 days after service of the demand, whichever is closer to the trial date, unless the court, on motion and a showing of good cause, orders an earlier or later date of exchange.”  (CCP § 2034.230(b) (emphasis added).)  Parties may offer supplemental experts not listed in the initial exchange under the following conditions:  

 

Within 20 days after the exchange described in Section 2034.260, any party who engaged in the exchange may submit a supplemental expert witness list containing the name and address of any experts who will express an opinion on a subject to be covered by an expert designated by an adverse party to the exchange, if the party supplementing an expert witness list has not previously retained an expert to testify on that subject. 

(CCP § 2034.280(a).)  

 

CCP § 2034.280 permits a supplemental designation responsive to the opposing party's designation if and only if the supplementing party has not previously retained an expert on the subject(s) in question. There are, however, limitations on the use and, more importantly, abuse of the supplemental expert witness designation provisions of section 2034.280.  The legislatively preferred method of expert witness exchange is simultaneous and mutual. (CCP § 2034.210.) If the parties do not comply with the requirements of a mutual and simultaneous exchange of experts, the Court has the authority to strike any non-conforming expert witness designation in whole or part. (CCP § 2034.300; see Osborne v. Todd Farm Service (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 43, 202 Cal.Rptr.3d 84; Fairfax v. Lords (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1019, 1021, 41 Cal.Rptr.3d 850.)    

 

  1. Discussion 

Here, Defendants served their supplemental expert list on April 18, 2024 (Mot. Exh. B), which is within 20 days after the initial exchange on April 2, 2024. (CCP § 2034.280(a).) Defendants did not previously retain an expert designated as a life care planner in the initial exchange. (Mot. Exh. A.) Dr. Cushing was not listed as a life care planner, nor does his designation delineate him as a life care planner. (Mot. Exh. A – Adams Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.) Regardless of his testimony at deposition, Dr. Cushing will be testifying to the contents of his report. Nowhere in his report does he mention a life care plan. (Opp. Exh. 3.) Assuming Plaintiff has designed her own life care planner, the supplemental designation of Dr. Coffman acts as the rebuttal witness.  

  

III. CONCLUSION  

Plaintiff’s motion to strike Defendant’s supplemental expert designation is DENIED, but the denial is conditioned upon Defendants making Coffman immediately available for deposition within the next three (3) court days; expert discovery shall be reopened for this limited purpose.

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Marbella Villa (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants 200 Pine Avenue, LLC (“200 Pine”), Automac Parking, Inc. (“Automac”), and Does 1 to 50 for damages arising from a slip and fall on water. Plaintiff filed amendments to complaint naming Octopus Japanese Cuisine, Inc. as Doe 1, Octopus Restaurant Long Beach, Inc. (“Octopus Restaurant”) as Doe 2, and Riverrock Real Estate Group, Inc. (“Riverrock”) as Doe 3.  

On November 17, 2022, 200 Pine filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint against Octopus Restaurant, Automac, and Roes 1 to 200 for (1) Breach of Contract – Additional Insurance, (2) express indemnity, (3) partial indemnity on a comparative fault basis, (4) implied indemnity, (5) comparative contribution (6) total equitable indemnity, (7) apportionment, and (8) declaratory relief. 

On March 28, 2024, Octopus Restaurant a First Amended Cross-Complaint against Automac, 200 Pine, Riverrock, and Roes 1 to 20 for (1) express contractual indemnity, (2) equitable indemnity and contribution, (3) breach of contract, and (4) declaratory relief – duty to defend. 

Riverrock now seeks leave to file a cross-complaint against Octopus Restaurant and Automac for (1) express indemnity – duty to defend, (2) partial indemnity on a comparative fault basis, (3) implied indemnity, (4) comparative contribution (5) total equitable indemnity, (6) apportionment, and (7) declaratory relief. Automac opposes the motion, and Riverrock filed a reply. 

 

  1. Moving Argument 

Riverrock argues the proposed cross-complaint will not raise new issues, and makes claims for express and implied indemnity under contracts already in dispute. Riverrock avers that all other parties have agreed to the filing the cross-complaint except for Automac, and that Riverrock had revised the proposed cross-complaint twice in attempt to satisfy Automac’s objections. However, Automac will not stipulate, necessitating this motion. Riverrock avers no parties will be prejudiced by the filing of the cross-complaint because the contracts at issue are already before the court, and have already been the subject of discovery and depositions between the parties.  

  1. Opposing Argument 

Automac contends Riverrock delayed in seeking leave to cross-complaint, because it has been over a year since Riverrock demanded indemnity from Automac. Automac avers it would not stipulate to the cross-complaint because of unsupported language related to express indemnity and breach of contract. Automac asserts that Riverrock is not a party to the contract between 200 Pine and Automac, and that the agreement does not provide indemnification between Automac and any property managers, in which Riverrock is a “property manager.” Automac further contends Riverrock does not have any new information, because the deposition of Automac’s employee on January 12, 2024 did not reveal any information Riverrock did not already have in 2022 and 2023.  

  1. Reply Argument 

Riverrock argues Automac has acknowledged in its opposition Riverrock’s position that it is a party to and beneficiary of the contract. Riverrock avers the contract contemplates it as a beneficiary under the agreement by naming the “Property Manager” as an additional insured under the liability insurance policies.  

 

II. MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS-COMPLAINT  

  1. Legal Standard 

A cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the initial complaint or cross-complaint against the cross-complainant must be filed before or at the same time as the answer to the initial complaint or cross-complaint, which answer must be filed within 30 days of service of the complaint or cross-complaint.  (CCP §§ 412.20(a)(3), 428.50(a), 432.10.)  Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a trial date. (CCP §428.50(b).)   

If a party fails to file a cross-complaint within the time limits described above, he or she must obtain permission from the court to file the cross-complaint.  (CCP §§ 426.50, 428.50(c).)  A motion to file a cross-complaint at any time during the course of the action must be granted unless bad faith of the moving party is demonstrated where forfeiture would otherwise result. (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal. App. 3d 94, 99.) Leave to file a permissive cross-complaint need only be granted in the interest of justice. (CCP §428.50(c).) Further, where the proposed cross-complaint arises out of the same transaction as plaintiff’s claim, the court must grant leave to file the cross-complaint so long as defendant is acting in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50.) Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50 supplements the authority provided generally to amend pleadings.  

  1. Discussion 

Here, it is undisputed that the proposed cross-complaint arises from the same incident, and encompasses issues already in dispute and subject to discovery by the parties. The Court is not persuaded that Riverrock has been unduly dilatory in seeking leave to file its cross-complaint, especially when no actual prejudice has been articulated by Automac. The parties dispute whether Riverrock is fairly encompassed as a “property manager” under the contractual provisions, but this is not an issue to be resolved on a motion for leave to file a cross-complaint. Trial is currently scheduled for April 11, 2025, and there is ample time to conduct any discovery or for any motions to be brought prior to discovery and motion cut-off deadlines. Further, there are no facts supporting an inference of bad faith or any deliberate delay tactic aimed at prejudicing any party for this Court to deny the motion. (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank¿(1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 100 [“‘Bad faith,’ is defined as ‘[t]he opposite of “good faith,” generally implying or involving actual or constructive fraud, or a design to mislead or deceive another, or a neglect or refusal to fulfill some duty or some contractual obligation, not prompted by an honest mistake ..., but by some interested or sinister motive[,] ... not simply bad judgment or negligence, but rather ... the conscious doing of a wrong because of dishonest purpose or moral obliquity; ... it contemplates a state of mind affirmatively operating with furtive design or ill will.”].) The Court finds it in the interest of justice to allow the pleading. 

 

III. CONCLUSION  

The motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is therefore GRANTED. Riverrock is ordered to file a separate copy of its proposed cross-complaint within five (5) days of the date of this Order.

I. BACKGROUND 

Defendant VEJ Pomona 3 (“Defendant”) propounded on plaintiff Anthony Osegueda (“Plaintiff”) (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for production of documents, set one, on October 5, 2023. (Cook Decl. ¶ 2; Exhs. A-C.) After granting Plaintiff extensions due to Plaintiff’s prior counsel seeking to be relieved as counsel, no responses have been served to date. On December 22, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel’s motion to be relieved was granted. Plaintiff has not responded to defense counsel’s inquiries regarding his current representation status and to the outstanding discovery. Defendant therefore seeks an order compelling Plaintiff to respond, without objections, to the outstanding discovery and to pay sanctions.  

The instant motion was served on Plaintiff in pro per. (Proof of Service, April 16, 2024.) Any opposition was due on or before May 10, 2024; none has been filed.   

 

II. MOTIONS TO COMPEL 

  1. Legal Standard 

For a motion to compel initial discovery responses, all a propounding party must show is that it properly served its discovery requests, that the time to respond has expired, and that the party to whom the requests were directed failed to provide a timely response. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 906.) Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290; 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); § 2031.300, subd. (a).)    

  1. Discussion 

The Court notes that both defendants VEJ Pomona 3 and VCT Management brought the instant motion. However, the evidence demonstrates that only VEJ Pomona 3 propounded the subject discovery. The Court will therefore limit its order to the party which had propounded the discovery at issue. 

  1. Conclusion & Order 

Therefore, because the evidence shows Plaintiff was properly served with discovery by VEJ Pomona 3 and failed to timely respond, any objections have been waived, and the unopposed motion is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to serve verified responses to VEJ Pomona 3’s (1) form interrogatories, set one, (2) special interrogatories, set one, and (3) demand for production of documents, set one, without objections, within thirty (30) days. (CCP §§ 2030.290(a),(b), 2031.300(a),(b).) 

 

III. SANCTIONS 

Sanctions are mandatory against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response, unless a court makes certain findings.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).)¿Plaintiff did not file any opposition. However, sanctions may be awarded, even though no opposition was filed, pursuant to CRC 3.1348(a). Defendants seek sanctions in the amount of $1,577.95 for the motion to compel.    

A court has discretion to award sanctions that are “suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks” but they should not be punitive in nature or levied for the purposes of punishing an offending party. (Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545.)         

VEJ Pomona 3 is awarded one hour for the motion to compel, and one hour to appear at the hearing (awarded only once), at the requested rate of $185 per hour for a total of $370 in attorney’s fees. Motion filing fees are ordinarily in the range of $60, and there is no explanation as to how this came out to $97.95 in filing fees. Thus, the Court declines to award VEJ Pomona 3 the request for filing fees as costs.        

Sanctions are imposed against Plaintiff. Plaintiff is ordered to pay sanctions to VEJ Pomona 3, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $370, within thirty (30) days. 

 

IV. ADDITIONAL FILING FEES¿ 

The Court notes that defendants VEJ Pomona 3 and VCT Management filed a single motion for what should have been three separate motions as to the motions to compel form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and request for production of documents. Combining discovery motions allows the moving party to avoid paying the requisite filing fees. Filing fees are jurisdictional and it is mandatory for court clerks to demand and receive them. (See Duran v. St. Luke’s Hospital (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 457, 460.) Thus, VEJ Pomona 3 and/or VCT Management are ordered to pay two additional filing fees. This ruling will be final only upon proof of payment of the filing fees.

I. BACKGROUND 

On February 16, 2024, defendant City of Los Angeles (“Defendant”) propounded supplemental demand for production of documents, on plaintiffs Luis Ramirez (“Plaintiff Ramirez”) and Cole Hershon (“Plaintiff Hershon”). (Mot. McGuire Decls. ¶ 2; Exh. A.) To date, Plaintiff Ramirez and Plaintiff Hershon have not served responses. (Id. at ¶ 4.) Defendant therefore moves the Court for an order compelling them to provide responses to the outstanding discovery and to pay sanctions. 

As of May 10, 2024, no opposition has been filed.  

 

II. MOTION TO COMPEL SUPPLEMENTAL DISCOVERY 

A party may propound a supplemental interrogatory or supplemental demand for production of documents to elicit any later acquired information bearing on all answers previously made by any party.  (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.070, subd. (a), 2031.050, subd. (a).) Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300; Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)  

Therefore, because the evidence shows Plaintiff Ramirez and Plaintiff Hershon were properly served with discovery and failed to respond, Defendant’s unopposed motion is GRANTED. Plaintiff Ramirez and Plaintiff Hershon are ordered to serve verified responses to defendant Los City of Los Angeles’ demand for production of documents, without objections, within fifteen (15) days. (CCP § 2031.300(a),(b).) 

 

III. SANCTIONS 

Sanctions are mandatory against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response, unless a court makes certain findings.¿(Code Civ. Proc. §2031.300(c).)¿ Plaintiff did not file any opposition. However, sanctions may be awarded, even though no opposition was filed, pursuant to CRC 3.1348(a). Defendant seeks $1,050 in monetary sanctions for each motion. 

A court has discretion to award sanctions that are “suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks” but they should not be punitive in nature or levied for the purposes of punishing an offending party. (Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545.) Defendant is awarded $250 per motion as attorney fees.  

Sanctions are sought and imposed against Plaintiffs and Plaintiffs’ attorney of record, jointly and severally, as follows: 

  1. Plaintiff Ramirez and/or his counsel are ordered to pay sanctions to defendant the City of Los Angeles, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $250, within fifteen (15) days.  

  1. Plaintiff Hershon and/or his counsel are ordered to pay sanctions to defendant the City of Los Angeles, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $250, within fifteen (15) days.
