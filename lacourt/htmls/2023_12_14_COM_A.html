DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

Case Number: 22CMCV00144    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: A

22CMCV00144 Amcor Lighting Inc., dba Jiao Guang USA Group v. Parmida, LLC, et al.

Thursday, December 14, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING  MOTION BY INDIVIDUAL DEFENDANTS, HAMID MAHROU AND PEYMAN MAHROU, FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION OF ISSUES

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The complaint filed on May 24, 2022, alleges that Plaintiff contracted to provide custom lighting and fixtures to Defendants, who allegedly failed to pay the balance due of $684,625.77. Plaintiff alleges claims for breach of contract, account stated, common counts, open book account, and quantum meruit.

II.      ARGUMENTS

       Defendants, Hamid Mahrou (“Hamid”) and Peyman Mahrou (“Peyman”), collectively, “Defendants”) move for adjudication of all causes of action as there is an absence of evidence showing that the Defendants are the alter egos of Defendant, Parmida, LLC (“Parmida”).  Plaintiff argues that the facts supporting each element necessary to invoke the alter ego doctrine are controverted.

       In reply, Defendants contend Plaintiff failed to raise any triable issues of material fact. Plaintiff’s evidence is speculation and unsupported by any evidence.

 

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

       A party can move for summary adjudication “as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, [or] that there is no merit to a claim for [punitive] damages.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c subd. (f)(1).) A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c. subd.(f)(1).)

       The court applies the three-step analysis to motions for summary judgment or adjudication: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleading, (2) determine whether the moving party established facts which negate the opponents’ claim, (3) if the moving party meets its threshold burden of persuasion and the burden shifts, determine whether the opposing party has controverted those facts with admissible evidence. (Torres v. Reardon (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 831, 836.)

IV.    DISCUSSION

       The complaint alleges that Defendants are liable for the debt owed to Plaintiff because the Defendants dominated and controlled Parmida, Defendants used Parmida as a conduit for their personal affairs, and as an obligor for the Defendants’ liabilities. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants operated Parmida pursuant to a scheme or plan whereby Parmida’s income and profits were converted by Defendants. (Complaint, ¶¶ 8.a – e.) Plaintiff alleges that adherence to the fiction of the entity as separate from the individuals will sanction inequity and promote injustice. (Id.)

       Liability based on an alter ego theory is not itself a claim for substantive relief, but rather a procedural claim “to disregard the corporate entity as a distinct defendant and to hold the alter ego individuals liable on the obligations of the corporation where the corporate form is being used by the individuals to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, or promote injustice." (Hennessey's Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1359.) Courts will ignore the corporate entity and deem the corporation’s acts to be those of the persons actually controlling the corporation "when the corporate form is used to perpetuate a fraud, circumvent a statute, or accomplish some other wrongful or inequitable purpose.” (Cam-Carson, LLC v. Carson Reclamation Authority (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 535, 549.)

       Application of the doctrine requires Plaintiff to establish two elements: (1) a unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its equitable owner that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist, and (2) “there must be an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.’” (Cam-Carson at 549.)  

       The test for a “unity of interest” encompasses a non-exhaustive list of factors only some of which may be sufficient to invoke the doctrine “under the particular circumstances of each case.” (Morrison Knudsen Corp. v. Hancock, Rothert & Bunshoft (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 223, 249.) These factors include (1) [c]ommingling of funds and other assets, (2) failure to segregate funds of the separate entities, (3) the unauthorized diversion of corporate funds or assets to other than corporate uses, (4) treating corporate assets as the individual’s own assets (5) failure to maintain minutes or adequate corporate records or corporate formalities (6) the identical equitable ownership in the two entities, (7) domination and control over the corporation by the individuals, (8) failure to adequately capitalize the corporation and (9) absence of corporate assets. (Morrison at 249–250.)

 

A.      The motion is procedurally defective.

       Defendants attempt to establish that Plaintiff’s assert only one theory of liability based on the alter ego doctrine. Plaintiff disputes this assertion because of evidence showing that Plaintiff requested a “personal guarantee letter” promising to make scheduled payments to Plaintiff. (Plaintiff’s Ex. 8, page 27). Peyman prepared and signed a “guarantee letter.” (Plaintiff’s Ex. 8, page 26.)

       A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(f)(1).) Therefore, even if Defendants prevailed in challenging the alter ego theory, liability based on a “personal guarantee” theory remains a basis for the claims; none of the claims can be disposed. This procedural defect is fatal and a basis for denying the motion.

       The motion is also denied for an independent reason; triable issues of fact remain on the alter ego theory of liability as further discussed.

 

B.      Defendants’ objections to Plaintiff’s evidence.

       Declaration of Tom C. Tsay, Plaintiff’s counsel

       #1. Exhibit 5. Overruled. The printout of Parmida’s California Secretary of State Information is subject to judicial notice. (Evid. Code, § 452(c) [official acts of the executive, judicial, and legislative departments.].) The court has discretion to take judicial notice of documents absent a party’s formal request and “on its own volition.” (Scott v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 752.) A document signed by Alex Padilla, affixing the state seal, affirms that Parmida is an authorized California Corporation in good standing.  (Plaintiff’s Ex. 1, Bates No. 3.)

       #2. Exhibit 12. Sustained. Declaration of process server stating that “managers” confirmed the parties have an account at the virtual mailbox center” is hearsay.

       #3-4. Exhibit 13, 14. Sustained. Declaration of process server quoting a current resident is hearsay.

 

Declaration of Joel Rosner, defense counsel, dated 9/25/23.

       #5. Exhibit 4. Overruled. Defendant Hamid Mahrou’s Response to Interrogatory #2. Hamid represented to a third person that his company is wholly owned by him and his son, Peyman. It is Hearsay but admitted as an Admission against a Party Opponent (Evid. Code §1220.)

 

C.      Analysis of facts and evidence.

       Where summary judgment is based on the absence of evidence as Defendants argue here, Defendants must make an affirmative showing by way of “direct or circumstantial evidence that the plaintiff not only does not have but cannot reasonably expect to obtain a prima facie case.” (Hagen v. Hickenbottom (1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 168, 186 [A moving defendant may not shift the burden “simply by suggesting the possibility that the plaintiff cannot prove its case.”].)  To that end, Defendants can rely on Plaintiff’s “factually devoid discovery responses” to shift the burden of proof to Plaintiff to identify specific facts to prove the existence of a triable issue of material fact. (Kaney v. Custance (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 201, 212.)

       In Hagen, the plaintiffs' answers to Union Bank's interrogatories “reflected no more than the plaintiffs' conclusionary belief that Union Bank had acted ‘knowingly and fraudulently’; such answers strongly supported an inference that the plaintiffs had no facts to support their theory.” (Hagen at 186.) Here, Plaintiff’s state facts to support the alter ego theory: admissions by Hamid, documents filed with the California Secretary of State and the Mississippi Secretary of State, including annual reports and Defendants’ Mississippi registration. (See, for example, UF[1] 6, 8, 10, 12, and 14.) Therefore, Defendants’ argument is not supported by Plaintiff’s discovery responses.

       Defendants are required to show not only the absence of facts, but also that Plaintiff “cannot reasonably obtain” such facts. That burden is not met because Plaintiff has proffered admissible evidence to support a “unity of interest” between Defendants and Parmida.

       Hamid is identified as a “member of Parmida” in documents filed with the California and Mississippi Secretaries of State; as an officer and director; as a manager, member, and president. (Plaintiff’s Exs. 1 -4.) Hamid is listed as Parmida’s “manager or member” and authorized agent for service of process. (Plaintiff’s Ex. 6.)

       Peyman signed a “personal guarantee” indicating Parmida’s intent to abide with a payment schedule. (Plaintiff’s Ex. 8.) Plaintiff dealt exclusively with Peyman and Hamid with respect to Parmida’s account. (Id.) Hamid has been Parmida’s managing member since its inception. Accordingly, Defendant’s contention that none of the documents produced by Plaintiff provide a basis for alter ego liability is disputed. (DP 27). Plaintiff’s evidence supports an inference of singular control by Defendants.

       Plaintiff proffers additional facts that Parmida failed to adhere to corporate formalities and filing requirements as Parmida has a history of being delinquent, subject to penalties and suspended or subject to suspension. (PAF[2] 101.) Plaintiff requested production of Parmida’s operating agreement (Doc. Req 10), meeting minutes and list of members (Doc. Req. 13-14), Parmida’s financial statements for the past five years (Doc. Req. 19), capital investments made by Parmida’s members (Doc. Req. 17), and Parmida’s lease agreements (Doc. Req. 18) among other documents. (Plaintiff’s Ex. 15). In response, Defendants state Parmida does not maintain a formal list of members, but otherwise objected to the discovery request on grounds of confidentiality. (Plaintiff’s Ex. 16.) Hamid repeatedly represented that he and his son are the only owners of Parmida. (PAF 118.) Parmida’s annual report was due to be filed with the California Secretary of State on December 31, 2021, but the LLC’s history does not indicate that one was ever filed.

       Accordingly, Plaintiff’s evidence disputes Defendants’ contention of a lack of evidence to invoke alter ego liability. Fact 27 remains in dispute.

V.      CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

Case Number: 23CMCV00018    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: A

23CMCV00018 Lathasha James v. City of Los Angeles

Thursday, December 14, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION BY LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY FOR LEAVE TO FILE A CROSS-COMPLAINT AGAINST CITY OF LOS ANGELES

I.        BACKGROUND

      The complaint alleges claims for premises liability and negligence arising from injuries sustained by Plaintiff when she fell because of an allegedly dangerous condition. The City of Los Angeles (“City”) filed a cross-complaint against Roes for indemnity, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief.

      Defendant Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“Metro”) moves for an order granting leave to file a cross-complaint against Defendant City. Although Metro filed its answer on July 13, 2023, Metro did not file a cross-complaint because its investigation had not yet commenced, and it was required to comply with claims presentation requirements under the Government Tort Claims Act. After receiving Plaintiff’s responses to written discovery, Metro obtained additional information to evaluate the incident. Metro will assert claims for indemnity declaratory relief, contribution, and apportionment of fault.

      Metro timely served Defendant City with this motion on November 14, 2023, by electronic transmission. City did not file an opposition by December 1, 2023, (nine court days before the hearing). (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005 subd. (b).)

II.      DISCUSSION

      A party against whom a cause of action has been asserted in a complaint may file a cross-complaint setting forth either a cause of action against any of the parties who filed the complaint, or any cause of action he has against a person alleged to be liable, whether such person is already a party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10 (b)). Leave to file or amend a cross-complaint may be granted so long as the moving party acted in good faith. The statute is liberally construed to avoid forfeiture. (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50).

      Metro acted in good faith in not filing the cross-complaint at the time of its answer as it could not do so prior to complying with the Government Code’s requirements for presenting a claim. (Gov. Code, § 945.6.) Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED. Metro is ordered to file its cross-complaint forthwith.
