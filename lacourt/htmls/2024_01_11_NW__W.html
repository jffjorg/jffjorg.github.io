DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV00449    Hearing Date: January 11, 2024    Dept: W

SANTOS POCASANGRE v. GROSS-RESEDA COMPANY, LLC

 

defendant/cross-complainant gross-reseda company, llc’s motion for protective order

 

Date of Hearing:        January 11, 2024                   Trial Date:       October 7, 2024

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV00449

 

Moving Party:            Defendant/Cross-Complainant Gross-Reseda Company LLC

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Santos Pocasangre  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Saracyan Decl. ¶7.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a premises liability action. Plaintiff Santos Pocasangre alleges Defendant Gross-Reseda Company LLC negligently, carelessly, recklessly and unlawfully controlled, operated, owned and/or maintained the subject property and failed to take reasonable steps to maintain, prevent, warn or to correct a dangerous condition on the subject property. As a result, Plaintiff alleges she slipped and fell causing injury to Plaintiff. On February 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed her complaint for negligence and premises liability.

 

On April 20, 2023, Defendant Gross-Reseda Company LLC filed a cross-complaint against Roes 1-100 for 1. Implied Contractual Indemnity; 2. Total Indemnity; 3. Equitable Indemnity; 4. Express Contractual Indemnity; 5. Breach of Written Contract; 6. Negligence 7. Contribution 8. Declaratory Relief (Duty to Defend) 9. Declaratory Relief (Duty to Indemnify).

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Gross-Reseda Company, LLC’s Motino for Protective Order is DENIED.

 

discusion

 

Plaintiff Santos Pocasangre moves for a protective order striking and/or restricting Plaintiff’s use of discovery, specifically, that Plaintiff's Special Interrogatories, Set Five; Requests for Admission, Set Four; and Form Interrogatories, Set Four, propounded to Defendant need not be answered at all. Defendant makes the motion on the grounds Plaintiff's second set of discovery requests propounded to Gross are duplicative, cumulative, financially burdensome, harassing, expensive, and not likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

A court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.”  (CCP §2030.090(b).)  The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks.  (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  A motion for a protective order “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (CCP §2030.090.)  “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (CCP §2016.040.)   

 

Defendant argues the additional discovery requests are unduly burdensome and harassing as it is voluminous, replete with requests that are duplicative, seek information that necessarily implicates consultant/expert work product and opinion. Defendant contends Plaintiff seems to be dissatisfied with the responses that have been previously provided in written and oral discovery, and attempts to take a second bite at the apple with these additional requests. For example, the definition of "supervision" and "control" and the specific contract language, maintenance, inspection and issues that arise at the subject property were already answered by the PMK for Gross during her deposition on September 6, 2023; however,

 

In opposition[1], Plaintiff argues this action is complex and moreover, expediency is a factor warranting additional requests as not only they entail less financial burden on the parties, but they are the most practical approach to conduct an inquiry, investigation, or search of files or records to supply the information sought. Plaintiff also argues the discovery is not unreasonable cumulative or duplicative and Defendant itself has propounded over 120 interrogatories itself.

 

The court agrees finds Defendant has not adequately shown that Plaintiff’s discovery requests are sufficiently unduly burdensome or harassing. The discovery includes follow up requests to Defendant’s prior responses. For example, Special Interrogatory No. 77 sought Defendant to define the term ‘supervision’ as used in the Addendum to Lease and Special Interrogatory 89 seeks Defendant to identify the dictionary Defendant is relying on to define the term ‘supervision’ after Defendant responded “the definition of ‘supervision’ is most likely the same as it is defined in the dictionary.” The other additional discovery seeks further information such as specific inspection policies, tenant responsibilities, and the duty to inspect for hazardous conditions. Moreover, if the discovery involves expert work product and/or opinion, Defendant can object on those grounds.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.

Case Number: 23VECV00760    Hearing Date: January 11, 2024    Dept: W

LILACH BASSON, ET AL. V. KRYSTAL BURNS, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS KRYSTAL BURNS AND GLORY PROJECT LLC’S DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        January 11, 2024                                           Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        23VECV00760

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Lilach Basson, Yacov Serazi, and Noam Sherazi

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Lilach Basson, Yacov Serazi, and Noam Sherazi, a minor by his Guardian Ad Litem Lilach Basson, filed a complaint against Defendants Krystal Burns, Norma Burns, and Glory Project LLC for assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and interference with tenancy. Plaintiff Yacov leases property from Defendant Glory Project, LLC. Plaintiff Lilach Basson alleges when she was visiting her husband’s office with Noam, their son, Basson was physically and verbally attacked by Defendants Norma and Krystal Burns. Defendant Krystal Burns manages the property.

 

Default was entered against Defendant Norma Burns on April 13, 2023. On April 24, 2023, default was entered against Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC. The court set aside the default entered against Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC on June 22, 2023. On December 11, 2023, the court set aside the default against Norma Burns.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED. Defendants’ Motion to Strike is

DENIED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs request this court take judicial notice of the Declaration of Yacov Serazi filed May 29, 2023.

 

Defendants object to the request for judicial notice on the grounds the declaration and attached documents seek judicial notice of disputed material facts outside the four corners of Plaintiffs’ Complaint. The request is also improper because Plaintiffs failed to provide any legal authority or arguments to identify the ground(s) upon which they seek judicial notice of documents.

 

These objections are overruled.  The court takes judicial notice of the existence of the documents only.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC demur to the complaint of Plaintiffs Lilach Basson, Yacov Serazi, and Noam Sherazi on the grounds the sixth cause of action for interference with tenancy fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.[1]

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action fails to allege any information regarding the underlying lease or its terms involving the former property owner and Joshua & Associates or whether the former property owner approved the sublease. Defendants further contend the complaint does not include the material terms of the alleged lease or sublease. Neither contract is attached to the complaint. Defendants contend the failure to attach the written lease contract and the written sublease contract violates the statue of frauds and Civil Code section 1624.

 

Under the applicable statute of frauds, leases longer than one year or for the sale of real property are invalid unless it is memorialized by a writing signed by the party to be charged.  (Civ. Code § 1624(a)(3).) Because Plaintiff Serazi seeks to enforce rights as a multi-year tenant of the subject Tarzana real property, Defendants contend Plaintiff has failed to satisfy the statute of frauds.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue this is not a case where the enforceability of Yacov Sherazi’s sublease is an issue. Moreover, the statute of frauds is not available to third persons who are not parties to the agreement and even so, the complaint pleaded the existence of a sublease with the legal effect and provided a copy of the sublease attached to Yacov Serazi’s declaration in the default prove-up package.

 

The court finds based on the allegations, Plaintiff has stated facts sufficient to support a claim for constructive eviction.  Plaintiff has alleged the existence of a sublease agreement with which defendants allegedly interfered.  Plaintiff was not required to attach the written agreement because plaintiff set forth the material terms.  (Complaint, paras. 14-16.)   The court need not address the question of whether the statute of frauds applies in this case, as it is plaintiffs’ position that a written agreement does exist. 

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint regarding punitive damages on the grounds the specified portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint constitute irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in the complaint.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.) 

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .” (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)  

 

“‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. § 3294(c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to defendant with the intention on the part of defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).) 

 

Defendants first argue Plaintiff Serazi’s claim for statutory damages under Civil Code section 789.3 is improper because the alleged sub lease concerns commercial office space. Civil Code section 789.3 provides “A landlord shall not with intent to terminate the occupancy under any lease or other tenancy or estate at will, however created, of property used by a tenant as his residence willfully cause, directly or indirectly, the interruption or termination of any utility service furnished the tenant, including, but not limited to, water, heat, light, electricity, gas, telephone, elevator, or refrigeration, whether or not the utility service is under the control of the landlord.”

 

Plaintiffs agree to strike any reference to Civil Code section 789.3.

 

Next, Defendants argue the allegations in Plaintiffs’ Complaint show a mutual altercation at a commercial property. Under these particular facts, oppression, fraud or malice is not demonstrated to support a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294 against Defendants. Moreover, allegations against a corporate employer for punitive damages require detailed factual pleadings which are absent from Plaintiffs’ complaint.

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have alleged sufficient allegations to support a claim for punitive damages against the individual defendants, and against the LLC. Plaintiffs allege Defendant Krystal put her hands around Plaintiff Lila’s neck, in front of Lila’s son (Noam), and at one point, Defendant Norma grabbed Plaintiff Noam around the throat. Plaintiffs allege Defendants did this with the intent to cause Plaintiffs pain. Accordingly, the court finds complaint alleges that Defendant Krystal Burns committed the acts with malice and in conscious disregard of their safety.

 

Because Krystal Burns is alleged to be a manager and member of the LLC, she would be deemed to exercise substantial discretionary authority in that role over the LLC’s business either as a member-owner, or as a manager (Swart Enterprises, Inc. v. Franchise Tax Bd. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 670, 679), making the corporation potentially liable for punitive damages under Section 3294.  (Powerhouse Motorsports Group, Inc. v. Yamaha Motor Corp., U.S.A. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 867.)  These allegations are sufficient to allow the request for punitive damages to stand. 

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED.


 


Case Number: 23VECV01205    Hearing Date: January 11, 2024    Dept: W

LISA DOTSON, et al. vs DALIA TAN, et al.

 

demurrer with motion to strike complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        January 11, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01205

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Anahit Aghazarian and Apgear Properties, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Lisa Dotson and Kyle Dotson

Meet and Confer:      Yes (Bowen Decl.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a habitability case. Plaintiffs allege Defendants failed to adequately fix or remediate habitability issues or remediated them late. On March 17, 2023 filed a complaint against Defendants for 1. Breach of Contract/Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment/Warranty of Habitability 2. Tortious Breach of The Implied Warranty of Habitability 3. Negligence 4. Private Nuisance 5. Violation of California Civil Code Section 1942.4 6. Violation of California Civil Code Section 1950.5 7. Violation of Unfair Business Practices 8. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress 9. Constructive Eviction 10. Tenant Harassment

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Anahit Aghazarian and Apgear Properties, Inc.’s Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED. Defendants Defendants Anahit Aghazarian and Apgear Properties, Inc.’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Anahit Aghazarian and Apgear Properties, Inc.  demur to the eighth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds the claim fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Defendants also seek to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint for punitive/exemplary damages.

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) intention to cause or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) severe emotional suffering; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress. (See Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780; Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009.) To satisfy the element of extreme and outrageous conduct, defendant’s conduct “‘must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’” (Moncada, supra, 221 Cal.App.4th at 780 (quoting Trerice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883).)  

 

The California Supreme Court has held that a defendant’s actions could be characterized as "outrageous" for purposes of tort liability for intentional infliction of emotional distress, if he “(1) abuses a relation or position which gives him power to damage the plaintiff's interest; (2) knows the plaintiff is susceptible to injuries through mental distress; or (3) acts intentionally or unreasonably with the recognition that the acts are likely to result in illness through mental distress.” (Agarwal v. Johnson (1979) 25 Cal.3d 932, 946 [overruled on other grounds].)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ facts lack the necessary specificity and instead, Plaintiffs rely on legal conclusions which fail to meet the requisite pleading standard. Specifically, Defendants contend the alleged wrongful conduct of Apegar does not rise to conduct so extreme and outrageous so as to exceed the bounds of decency and is not regarded as atrocious and utterly intolerable in a civilized community. Allegedly failing to timely remediate habitability issues in 2020 during a global pandemic is not extreme and outrageous conduct.

 

The court disagrees. Plaintiffs alleged Defendants failed to maintain their Property in a habitable condition (and what those inhabitable conditions included), that Plaintiffs complained to Defendants about the unlawful and substandard conditions, and Defendants refused to repair the Subject Property as requested despite knowing that Plaintiffs were suffering and knowing that the Property was uninhabitable and untenantable. Several cases have found intentional or willful failures to remediate the substandard conditions causing Plaintiffs emotional distress sufficient to support a claim for IIED.  Moreover, the complaint alleges Plaintiffs suffered “illness, physical injury, extreme mental and emotional distress, discomfort, annoyance, anxiety, loss in the value of their leasehold, property damage, medical expenses, out of pocket costs[.]” (Compl. ¶47.) (See, e.g., Girard v. Ball (1981) 125 Cal.App.3d 772, 787-88 [emotional distress must be of “such substantial quality or enduring quality that no reasonable [person] in civilized society should be expected to endure it”].)

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants also move to strike portions of the Complaint relating to claims for punitive and exemplary damages on the grounds the cited portions constitute irrelevant, false, or improper matter, conclusions of law, items of non-recoverable damages, and/or are not supported by the facts.

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .” (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)  

 

“‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. § 3294(c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to defendant with the intention on the part of defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).) 

 

Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort. (See Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95.) And, specific facts must be pleaded in support of punitive damages. (See Hillard v. A.H. Robins Co. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-92.)  

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs have failed to state colorable facts upon which to base their claim for punitive damages. Specifically, the complaint fails to contain any factual allegations that demonstrate (i) “despicable conduct” on the part of Apegar; or, (ii) aggravating circumstances, amounting to malice; or, (iii) circumstances and/or actions, conceived in spirit of mischief or with criminal indifference towards obligations owed to others. (Ebaugh v. Rabkin (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 891, 894.) Defendants contend Plaintiffs merely allege boilerplate legal conclusions that Apegar’s conduct was malicious and oppressive, which is not sufficient to support a prayer for punitive damages.

 

As previously noted, although Plaintiff’s complaint is sparse, this factual scenario is sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages at the pleading stage. (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1055.) In summary, the complaint alleges that Plaintiffs informed Defendants of the bedbug infestation as well as the other habitability issues, Defendants failed to correct it, and permitted it with the knowledge of the consequences thereof.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Strike is DENIED.
