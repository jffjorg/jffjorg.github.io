DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV15435    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 37

CASE NUMBER:                   23STCV15435

CASE NAME:                        Infinity Medical Alliance, Inc. v. A&G Management Services, Inc., et al.

MOVING PARTY:                 Cross-Defendants Infinity Medical Alliance, Inc. and Larisa Vinnitskaya

OPPOSING PARTY:             Cross-Complainants A&G Management Service, Inc. and Aram Sharnazyan

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer to Second Amended Cross-Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        11 June 2024

REPLY:                                  17 June 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         The demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled. The demurrer to the second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh cause of action is sustained without leave to amend. Cross-Defendants to provide notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On July 3, 2023, Infinity Medical Alliance, Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “IMA”) filed a Complaint against A&G Management Services, Inc. (“A&G”); Aram Sharnazyan (“Sharnazyan”); and Does 1 to 50.

 

The Complaint alleges four causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Declaratory Relief, (3) Trespass- Monetary and Injunctive Relief; and (4) Conversion.

 

On August 7, 2023, Defendants A&G and Sharnazyan filed a Cross-Complaint (“CC”) against IMA, Larisa Vinnitskaya; Nikita Baranovskiy, Nixpro Inc.; Konstantin Khlevnoy, Johnathan M. Quick, Jonathan Bohun, and Roes 1 to 50.

 

Cross-Defendants IMA and Larisa Vinnitskaya (“Vinnitskaya”) demurred to the Cross-Complaint (“CC”). On December 15, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the Cross-Complaint with leave to amend.

 

On March 21, 2024, Cross-Complainants filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SACC”) alleging eleven causes of action:

 

1)     Breach of Contract – against IMA and Roes 1 to 50;

2)     Intentional Misrepresentation – against all Cross-Defendants;

3)     Negligent Misrepresentation – against all Cross-Defendants;

4)     Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing – against IMA and Roes 1 to 50;

5)     Receipt of Stolen Property (Pen. Code § 496(c)) – against all Cross-Defendants;

6)     Conversion, against all Cross-Defendants;

7)     Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations - against Vinnitskaya, Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun;

8)     Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations – against Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun;

9)     Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Relations- against Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun;

10) Unfair Business Practices (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq.)- against IMA, Vinnitskaya, Baranovskiy, and Nixpro; and

11) Conspiracy – against all Cross-Defendants

 

Cross-Defendants IMA and Vinnitskaya (collectively “Cross-Defendants) now demur to the SACC. Cross-Complainants oppose the demurrer. The matter is now before the court. 

 

 

Discussion

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.¿ (CCP, § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) In evaluating a demurrer, the court accepts the complainant’s properly pled facts as true and ignores contentions, deductions, and conclusory statements. (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1976) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Moreover, the court does not consider whether a plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations or the possible difficulty in making such proof. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿ The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

II.        Demurrer[1]

 

A.        Summary of Allegations in SACC

 

Cross-Defendant Infinity Medical Alliance, Inc. (“IMA”) is the holder of an adult-use and cannabis business license and is the owner of a cannabis business located in Torrence, CA (the “Business”). (SACC, ¶¶ 4,  16.) Larisa Vinnitskaya (“Vinnitskaya”) is the Chief Executive Officer of IMA. (SACC, ¶ 5.) Cross-complainant Aram Sharnazyan (“Sharnazyan”) owns and operates A&G Management Services, Inc. (“A&G”). (SACC, ¶¶ 1, 2, 17.) On or about September 25, 2019, IMA and A&G entered into an agreement (the “Agreement”) wherein A&G would be the exclusive manager and operator of the Business. (SACC, ¶ 18, Ex. 1.)

 

                        i.          Cross-Complainants’ Relationship with IMA

 

Under the Agreement, A&G would pay IMA a fixed monthly fee of $60,000. (SACC, ¶ 18, Ex. 1, § 3.3.) However, IMA asserts that in June 2022, there was a modification to only pay $45,000 for a year in consideration for A&G’s incurred costs for electrical system upgrades made to the business. (SACC, 32.) IMA drafted an amendment to reflect the reduced monthly fee (the “Amendment”), which A&G signed and returned to IMA on or about June 28, 2022. (SACC, ¶ 32, Ex. 2.) Cross-Complainants assert that IMA accepted the reduced monthly fee for nearly a year without dispute, thus accepting, confirming, and ratifying the adoption of the Amendment. (SACC, ¶ 32.)

 

In April 2022, A&G discovered that the Business had various delinquent tax liabilities going back to 2012 when A&G had not yet entered into the Agreement. (SACC, ¶ 31.) A&G asserts it was willing and ready to make payments to the California Department of Tax and Fee Administration (“CDTFA”) but Vinnitskaya, on behalf of IMA, instructed A&G not to pay the tax liabilities until AMA could settle with CDTFA. (SACC, ¶ 31.)

 

                        ii.         Cross-Complainants’ Relationship with Baranovskiy

 

In June 2022, A&G hired Nikita Baranovskiy (“Baranovskiy”) to assist with the Business’ day-to-day operations. (SACC, ¶ 20.) Baranovskiy in turn hired his associates “Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun as personnel to manage the Business’s day-to-day operations.” (SAC, ¶ 21.) In January 2023, Baranovskiy deposited monies from the cash registers into a bank account belonging to him and/or Nixpro Inc. in violation of A&G’s instruction to deposit all monies into IMA’s bank account. (SACC, ¶ 23.) In March 2023, A&G’s accountant discovered Baranovskiy’s scheme to embezzle the Business’s profits. (SACC, ¶¶ 25, 28.) In June 2023, A&G terminated the partnership with Baranovskiy and his associates. (SACC, ¶ 30.)

 

Cross-Complainants assert that the reduced monthly fee and taxes were never an issue between IMA and A&G until A&G terminated Baranovskiy. (SACC, ¶¶ 33.) In June 2023, IMA alleged A&G breached the Agreement due to nonpayment of taxes and the reduced monthly fee. (SACC, ¶ 34.) On or about July 4, 2023, Cross-Complainants vacated the premises of the Business and ceased all activities regarding the Business or IMA.

 

iii.       IMA’s Unfair Business Practices

 

The SACC alleges upon information and belief that IMA received the proceeds from Baranovskiy’s embezzlement and benefitted from his schemes. (SACC, ¶ 36.)

 

IMA instructed Cross-Complainants to not make payments of taxes to CDTFA to fraudulently induce A&G to breach the Agreement. (SACC, ¶ 37.) IMA did not fully execute the Amendment for the reduced monthly fee in order to fraudulently induce A&G to breach the Agreement. (SACC, ¶ 38.)

 

B.        First Cause of Action Breach of Contract

 

Cross-Complainants’ first cause of action is premised on allegations that IMA induced Cross-Complainants to breach the Agreement by instructing it to not pay taxes and not fully executing the Amendment agreeing to a reduced monthly rate. (SACC, ¶¶ 44-46, 48, Ex. 1, 2.) Accordingly, IMA breached Section 5.1(f) of the Agreement by failing to act in good faith and act in the best interest of the parties. (SACC, ¶ 49, Ex. 1.)

 

Cross-Defendants IMA and Vinnitskaya assert that Cross-Complainants’ purported modifications are invalid because they are against the express terms of the Agreement. The relevant provisions of the Agreement state:

 

Section 2.7 Manager Costs. Subject to Section 3.3, Manager shall be responsible for all costs and expense of managing the Project, including, without limitation, all . . .. (iii) common area maintenance, insurance, real property taxes . . . (iv) federal, state, and/or city taxes related to the Project . . .

 

[ . . .]

 

Section 4.1 Fixed Monthly Fee.

            (a) During the Term, Manager shall pay Company a monthly flat fee of Sixty Thousand Dollars ($60,000) (the “Monthly Fee”) in accordance with the terms hereof. . .

 

[. . . ]

 

Section 10.9 Entire Agreement. This Agreement . . . constitutes the sole and entire agreement of the parties of this Agreement with respect to the subject matter contained herein, and supersedes all prior and contemporaneous understandings and agreements, both written and oral, with respect to the subject matter.

 

[ . . . ]

Section 10.12 Amendment and Modification; Waiver. This Agreement may only be amended, modified, or supplemented by an agreement in writing signed by each party hereto. No waiver by either party of any of the provisions hereof shall be effective unless explicitly set forth in writing and signed by the waiving party. Except as otherwise set forth in this Agreement, no failure to exercise or delay in exercising any rights, remedy, power, or privilege arising from this Agreement shall operate or be construed as a waiver thereof; nor shall any single or partial exercise of any right, remedy, power, or privilege hereunder preclude any other or further exercise thereof or the exercise of any other right, remedy, power, or privilege.

 

(SACC, Ex. 1 at p. 16, §§ 2.7, 4.1, 10.9, 10.12 [italics original].)

 

Therefore, Cross-Defendants assert that they could not breach the Agreement based on verbal modifications because the modifications are invalid under the Agreement unless they are in writing and signed by IMA.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants assert that the implied in fact modifications are valid. “There cannot be a valid, express contract and an implied contract, each embracing the same subject matter, existing at the same time.” (Wal-Noon Corp. v. Hill (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 605, 613.) “However, that is not to say the parties may not in that manner thereafter modify their agreement.” (Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Medical Center (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1379, 1388 [italics added].)

 

When one party has, through oral representations and conduct or custom, subsequently behaved in a manner antithetical to one or more terms of an express written contract, he or she has induced the other party to rely on the representations and conduct or custom. In that circumstance, it would be equally inequitable to deny the relying party the benefit of the other party's apparent modification of the written contract.

 

(Ibid.)

“[W]here the subsequent conduct of parties is inconsistent with and clearly contrary to provisions of the written agreement, the parties' modification setting aside the written provisions will be implied.” (Diamond Woodworks, Inc. v. Argonaut Ins. Co. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1020, 1038 overruled on other grounds by Simon v. San Paolo U.S. Holding Co., Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1159; see also Garrison v. Edward Brown & Sons (1944) 25 Cal.2d 473, 479 [“Before a contract modifying a written contract can be implied, the conduct of the parties according to the findings of the trial court must be inconsistent with the written contract so as to warrant the conclusion that the parties intended by modify the written contract.”].)

 

Cross-Defendants argue the cases relied on by Cross-Complainants are unavailing because the cases cited did not have an integration clause or a requirement that modifications be in writing. However, Cross-Defendants fail to cite case law stating an express agreement that precludes oral modifications may never be modified orally.

“A contract in writing may be modified by an oral agreement to the extent that the oral agreement is executed by the parties.” (Civ. Code, § 1698(b).) “The rules provided by subdivisions (b) and (c) [of section 1698] merely describe cases where proof of an oral modification is permitted; these rules do not, however, affect in any way the burden of the party claiming that there was an oral modification to produce sufficient evidence to persuade the trier of fact that the parties actually did make an oral modification of the contract.” (See Law Revision Commission comments on Civ. Code § 1698.)

 

California courts have found subsequent oral modifications to be valid even when the agreement expressly provides that only written modifications are valid. (See LGCY Power, LLC v. Superior Court (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 844, 867–868 [“That Sewell's employment agreement contains a provision that requires all modifications be in writing does not make Civil Code section 1698, subdivision (b), inapplicable”]; Biren v. Equality Emergency Medical Group, Inc. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 125, 141 [The parties may, by their conduct, waive a contractual provision requiring amendments to the contract to be in writing, where the evidence shows that it was their intent to waive the contractual provision.].)“[N]otwithstanding a provision in a written agreement that precludes oral modification, the parties may, by their words or conduct, waive contractual rights.” (Wind Dancer Production Group v. Walt Disney Pictures (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 56, 78.)

 

Accordingly, whether Cross-Defendant IMA agreed orally to modify the Agreement remains a disputed issue of fact. In other words, the parties may by their conduct wave a no oral modification provision where the evidence shows that was their intent, and such an inquiry “is ordinarily a question of fact unless ‘“there are no disputed facts and only one reasonable inference may be drawn.’ ” (Park v. NMSI, Inc. (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 346, 358.)

 

Taking the facts in the SACC as true, IMA breached the Agreement when it instructed A&G to delay the payment of taxes and sued A&G for breaching the Agreement despite IMA orally agreeing to the modifications in violation Section 5.1(f) of the Agreement:

 

Section 5.1 Representation and Warranties of the Parties. Each Party warrants and represents to the other as follows:

 

[ . . . ]

 

(f)        Such Party will act in good faith and in the best interest of the Parties, collectively, in order to give the Project the best chances of being successful.

 

(SACC, ¶ 49, Ex. 1, § 5.1(f).)

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

 

C.        Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action – Receipt of Stolen Property (Penal Code, § 496(c)) and Conversion

 

Penal Code § 496(a) states in relevant part:

 

Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment in a county jail for not more than one year, or imprisonment pursuant to subdivision (h) of Section 1170.

 

(Id. [italics added].)

 

To plead a cause of action for conversion, one must allege (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.) 

 

Cross-Defendants demurrer to the sixth cause of action on the grounds that the monies embezzled belong to Cross-Defendant IMA, not Cross-Complainants. Accordingly, Cross-Complainants do not have a possessory interest in the converted funds. Cross-Defendants point out under IMA’s cannabis license, A&G as the management company is only entitled to manage the Business on IMA’s behalf but cannot sell cannabis itself or accept many for cannabis in its own name. (See LACM Code, §§ 104.21(e), 105.06(a), and Bus. & Prof. Code, §§ 26031.5, 26038.)

 

The SACC alleges that Baranovskiy would use the Business’s point-of-sale software and would mark the transactions as the sale of “accessories” for $0.2 each but “in reality, these transactions were for cannabis products” and Baranovskiy and his associates would retain the cash from the sale for their own gains. (SACC, ¶¶ 26, 98, 111.) Accordingly, because the sale was for cannabis products, IMA as the license holder was the rightful owner of the monies Baranovskiy embezzled.  The SACC further alleges that Baranovskiy and his associates would input a fifty percent discount for a sale but charge the customer a full, undiscounted price. (SACC, ¶¶ 100, 113.) Cross-Complainants fail to allege they are the owners of the monies Baranovskiy pocketed from these fraudulent sales. The SACC alleges that Baranovskiy and his associates deposited monies into an ATM as part of their scheme, but the SACC fails to allege that Cross-Complaintants are the rightful owners of the monies Baranovskiy deposited into the ATM. (SAC, ¶¶ 101, 114.)

 

Without such facts, Cross-Complainants’ allegation that IMA and Vinnitskaya received “Cross-Complainants’ property in the amount of approximately $2,300,000” is unsupported by the allegations. (SACC, ¶¶ 103, 116.) Cross-Complainants specifically fail to allege they were the lawful owners of the monies Baranovskiy and his associates embezzled.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth and sixth causes of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

D.        Second and Third Causes of Action – Negligent and Intentional Misrepresentation

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage. [Citations.] The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true. [Citations.] Each element of a fraud count must be pleaded with particularity so as to apprise the defendant of the specific grounds for the charge and enable the court to determine whether there is any basis for the cause of action, although less specificity is required if the defendant would likely have greater knowledge of the facts than the plaintiff. [Citation.]”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230–231.) Negligent misrepresentation requires that the party owe a duty to disclose. (See Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 841; Levine v. Blue Shield of California (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1117, 1136.)

 

The second and third causes of action are premised on the fact that IMA and Vinnitskaya represented to Cross-Complainants that it would enter a settlement with CDTFA on the outstanding tax liabilities and that Cross-Complainants should not make any tax payments. (SACC, ¶¶ 53, 66.) In June 2022, IMA represented that it would execute the Amendment to reduce the monthly fee under the Agreement. (SACC, ¶¶ 54, 67.)

 

Cross-Defendants assert that the second and third causes of action are barred by the economic loss doctrine. As explained by the California Supreme Court, “the economic loss rule prevents the law of contract and the law of tort from dissolving into the other.” (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal. 4th 979, 989.) “In general, there is no recovery in tort for negligently inflicted ‘purely economic losses,’ meaning financial harm unaccompanied by physical or property damage.” (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 922.)

“[T]he rule functions to bar claims in negligence for pure economic losses in deference to a contract between litigating parties.” (Id. at p. 922.)

 

The court finds that Cross-Complainants’ second and third causes of action are derivative of the first cause of action for breach of contract. Moreover, Cross-Complainants’ opposition fails to explain why the second and third causes of action are not barred by the economic loss doctrine.

 

The demurrer to the second and third causes of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

E.        Seventh, Eighth, and Ninth Causes of Action: Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations; Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations, and Negligent Interference with Economic Relations

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations are ‘(1) the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of that contract; (3) the defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4)actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.’ ” (Redfearn v. Trader Joe's Co. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 989, 997, citing Reeves v. Hanlon(2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1148.) 

 

The tort of intentional interference with prospective economic relations is the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. (See Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 378 [Analyzing the burden of proof and the “the elements of the tort variously known as interference with ‘prospective economic advantage,’ ‘prospective contractual relations,’ or ‘prospective economic relations’[.]”].)  Subsequently, in Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1153 and Ixchel Pharma, LLC v. Biogen, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 1130, 1141, the California Supreme Court reiterated that the correct name of the tort was intentional interference with prospective economic advantage.

 

To state a claim for the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage (IIPEA), the claimant must allege: (1) an economic relationship between the claimant and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the claimant; (2) the defendant or cross-defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant or cross-defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the claimant proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.¿(Marsh v. Anesthesia Services Medical Group, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 480, 504.)

 

The tort of negligent interference with a prospective economic advantage (“NIPEA”) differs in that the defendant's act disrupting the economic relationship does not need to be intentional. (Venhaus v. Shultz (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1078.) “The tort of negligent interference with economic relationship arises only when the defendant owes the plaintiff a duty of care.” (Stolz v. Wong Communications Limited Partnership (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1811, 1825 [italics original].)  For both IIPEA and NIPEA, the interference must be wrongful by some legal measure other than the fact of the interference itself. (Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 378.) “[A]n act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Korea Supply, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 1159.)

 

Cross-Defendants IMA and Vinnitskaya demurrer to the seventh, tenth, and ninth causes of action on the basis that IMA cannot be charged with breaching its own contract. “Because a party to a contract owes no tort duty to refrain from interference with its performance, he or she cannot be bootstrapped into tort liability by the pejorative plea of conspiracy.” (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 514 (Applied Equipment).) “Conduct amounting to a breach of contract becomes tortious only when it also violates an independent duty arising from principles of tort law.” (Id. at p. 515.)

 

Cross-Complainants point out that the seventh, eighth, and ninth causes of action are alleged against Vinnitskaya and Baranovskiy’s associates, not IMA. Therefore, the interference claims are properly pled. Cross-Defendant Vinnitskaya asserts that the agent immunity rule applies because she acted within the scope of her employment when she allegedly represented to Cross-Complainants that there would be a written amendment to reduce the monthly fee and not to pay taxes. (SACC, ¶¶ 124, 134, 144.)

 

The agent's immunity rule emanates from a further holding in [Wise v. Southern Pac. Co. (1963) 223 Cal.App.2d 50] that: ‘Agents and employees of a corporation cannot conspire with their corporate principal or employer where they act in their official capacities on behalf of the corporation and not as individuals for their individual advantage.’ [Citation.] The rule ‘derives from the principle that ordinarily corporate agents and employees acting for or on behalf of the corporation cannot be held liable for inducing a breach of the corporation's contract since being in a confidential relationship to the corporation their action in this respect is privileged.’

 

(Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at p. 513, fn. 4.) “This immunity rule and its exception (when an agent acts for his “own individual advantage”) are based on the proposition that a corporation cannot conspire with itself—because an agent or employee who is acting within the scope of his authority is (in the eyes of the law) one and the same ‘person’ as the corporation.” (Everest Investors 8 v. Whitehall Real Estate Limited Partnership XI (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 1102, 1109.)

 

As the SACC alleges that Vinnitskaya acted as an agent of IMA in inducing A&G to breach the Agreement, the agent’s immunity rule applies and Vinnitskaya cannot be held liable for breaching the contract between A&G and IMA. (SACC, ¶ 13.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the seventh, eighth, and ninth causes of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

F.        Fourth – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith

 

“Every contract contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing providing that no party to the contract will do anything that would deprive another party of the benefits of the contract.¿The implied covenant protects the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties based on their mutual promises.” (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 885.) “The covenant thus cannot ‘be endowed with an existence independent of its contractual underpinnings.’ [Citation].” (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc.(2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349.)   “[A] tortious breach of contract … may be found when (1) the breach is accompanied by a traditional common law tort, such as fraud or conversion; (2) the means used to breach the contract are tortious, involving deceit or undue coercion; or (3) one party intentionally breaches the contract intending or knowing that such a breach will cause severe, unmitigable harm in the form of mental anguish, personal hardship, or substantial consequential damages.” (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 990.)

 

Cross-Defendants allege that the fourth cause of action fails because the second and third causes of action fail. In other words, no tort cause of action exists to support a claim for Breach of the Implied Covenant and Fair Dealing. Cross-Complainants’ opposition attempts to anchor the fourth cause of action to fraudulent conduct in violation of the Unfair Competition Law. However, Cross-Complainants fail to cite case law to show that a claim for Breach of the Implied Covenant and Fair Dealing can be anchored to a claim for breach of the UCL.

 

More importantly, the fourth cause of action is derivative of the first cause of action for breach of contract because it is based on the same breach, breach of Section 5.1(f) of the Agreement, and Vinnitskaya’s representations that the amendment to reduce the monthly fee would be put in written and to not pay taxes until there is a settlement negotiation with CDTFA. (SACC, ¶¶ 83, 81.) “If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated.”(Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.)

 

Based on the above, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

G.        Tenth Causes of Action –Unfair Business Practices

 

Business and Professions Code § 17200 (“UCL”) prohibits “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200; see Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 610.) To plead this statutory claim, the pleadings must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation. (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)

 

“An unlawful business practice or act is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969.) “A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1473.) Lastly, a fraudulent business practice claims under section 17200 “is not based upon proof of the common law tort of deceit or deception, but is instead premised on whether the public is likely to be deceived.” (Pastoria v. Nationwide Ins. (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1490, 1499.)

 

Cross-Defendants argue that the tenth cause of action fails because it is derivative of Cross-Complainants’ other claims. “When a statutory claim fails, a derivative UCL claim also fails.” (Aleksick v. 7-Eleven, Inc. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1176, 1185.) “Thus, when the underlying legal claim fails, so too will a derivative UCL claim.” (AMN Healthcare, Inc. v. Aya Healthcare Services, Inc. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 923, 950.)

 

The SACC alleges that due to Vinnitskaya’s representations about the taxes and putting the amendment to the monthly fee in writing, “Cross-Defendants have committed one or more acts of unfair competition, including but not limited to fraudulent business practice, within the meaning of California Business and Professional Code § 17200, et seq.’ (SACC, ¶ 160.) Cross-Complaints’ opposition merely asserts that “Cross-Defendants acted fraudulently, which violates Business and Profession Code § 17000.” (Opposition, at pp. 7:27-8:1.)

 

Because the demurrer to all causes of action, except for breach of contract, has been sustained, Cross-Complainants’ derivative UCL claim also fails. Moreover, the court previously found that the tenth cause of action was not pled with reasonable particularity and failed to show how Cross-Defendants Vinnitskaya and IMA’s conduct was unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent under the UCL. (Order 12/15/2023.) Here, the tenth cause of action fails to allege how Vinnitskaya’s actions and IMA’s decision to terminate the Agreement was an unlawful practice, was an unfair practice that caused injury to consumers or was fraudulent in a manner that is likely to deceive the public.

 

The demurrer to the tenth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

I.         Eleventh Cause of Action – Conspiracy

 

“[A]lleging the existence of a conspiracy or aiding and abetting activity does not turn a garden-variety contract cause of action [] into a derivative tort cause of action [].” (AMN Healthcare, Inc., supra,  28 Cal.App.5th at p. 949.) “Conspiracy is not a cause of action, but a legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves,  share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration.” (Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at pp. 510–511.)

 

Cross-Defendants demurrer to the eleventh cause of action on the basis that it is not a cause of action but a mechanism to impose vicarious liability premised on a substantive claim. (See Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Sherwood Partners, Inc. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 802, 823.) The court agrees.

 

The demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled. The demurrer to the second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh cause of action is sustained without leave to amend. Cross-Defendants to provide notice.

Background

 

On July 3, 2023, Infinity Medical Alliance, Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “IMA”) filed a Complaint against A&G Management Services, Inc. (“A&G”); Aram Sharnazyan (“Sharnazyan”); and Does 1 to 50.  

 

The Complaint alleges four causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Declaratory Relief, (3) Trespass-Monetary and Injunctive Relief; and (4) Conversion.

Cross-Defendants IMA and Larisa Vinnitskaya (“Vinnitskaya”) demurred to the Cross-Complaint (“CC”). On December 15, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the Cross-Complaint with leave to amend.  

 

On March 21, 2024, Cross-Complainants filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SACC”) alleging eleven causes of action

Discussion

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.¿ (CCP, § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) In evaluating a demurrer, the court accepts the complainant’s properly pled facts as true and ignores contentions, deductions, and conclusory statements. (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1976) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Moreover, the court does not consider whether a plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations or the possible difficulty in making such proof. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿ The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

II.        Demurrer[1]

 

A.        Summary of Allegations in SACC 

 

Cross-Defendant Infinity Medical Alliance, Inc. (“IMA”) is the holder of an adult-use and cannabis business license and is the owner of a cannabis business located in Torrence, CA (the “Business”). (SACC, ¶¶ 4, 16.) Larisa Vinnitskaya (“Vinnitskaya”) is the Chief Executive Officer of IMA. (SACC, ¶ 5.) Cross-complainant Aram Sharnazyan (“Sharnazyan”) owns and operates A&G Management Services, Inc. (“A&G”). (SACC, ¶¶ 1, 2, 17.) On or about September 25, 2019, IMA and A&G entered into an agreement (the “Agreement”) wherein A&G would be the exclusive manager and operator of the Business. (SACC, ¶ 18, Ex. 1.)  

 

i.          Cross-Complainants’ Relationship with IMA  

 

Under the Agreement, A&G would pay IMA a fixed monthly fee of $60,000. (SACC, ¶ 18, Ex. 1, § 3.3.) However, IMA asserts that in June 2022, there was a modification to only pay $45,000 for a year in consideration for A&G’s incurred costs for electrical system upgrades made to the business. (SACC, 32.) IMA drafted an amendment to reflect the reduced monthly fee (the “Amendment”), which A&G signed and returned to IMA on or about June 28, 2022. (SACC, ¶ 32, Ex. 2.) Cross-Complainants assert that IMA accepted the reduced monthly fee for nearly a year without dispute, thus accepting, confirming, and ratifying the adoption of the Amendment. (SACC, ¶ 32.)  

 

In April 2022, A&G discovered that the Business had various delinquent tax liabilities going back to 2012 when A&G had not yet entered into the Agreement. (SACC, ¶ 31.) A&G asserts it was willing and ready to make payments to the California Department of Tax and Fee Administration (“CDTFA”) but Vinnitskaya, on behalf of IMA, instructed A&G not to pay the tax liabilities until AMA could settle with CDTFA. (SACC, ¶ 31.)  

 

ii.         Cross-Complainants’ Relationship with Baranovskiy and the other Cross-Defendants

 

In June 2022, A&G hired Nikita Baranovskiy (“Baranovskiy”) to assist with the Business’ day-to-day operations. (SACC, ¶ 20.) Baranovskiy is the Chief Executive Officer of Nixpro, Inc.. (SACC, ¶ 6.) Baranovskiy in turn hired his associates “Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun as personnel to manage the Business’s day-to-day operations.” (SAC, ¶ 21.) In January 2023, Baranovskiy deposited monies from the cash registers into a bank account belonging to him and/or Nixpro Inc. in violation of A&G’s instruction to deposit all monies into IMA’s bank account. (SACC, ¶ 23.) In March 2023, A&G’s accountant discovered Baranovskiy’s scheme to embezzle the Business’s profits. (SACC, ¶¶ 25, 28.) In June 2023, A&G terminated the partnership with Baranovskiy and his associates. (SACC, ¶ 30.)  

 

Cross-Complainants assert that the reduced monthly fee and taxes were never an issue between IMA and A&G until A&G terminated Baranovskiy. (SACC, ¶¶ 33.) In June 2023, IMA alleged A&G breached the Agreement due to nonpayment of taxes and the reduced monthly fee. (SACC, ¶ 34.) On or about July 4, 2023, Cross-Complainants vacated the premises of the Business and ceased all activities regarding the Business or IMA.  Cross-Complainants asserts that all Cross-Defendants are agents of each other and “conspired to embezzle funds from the Business under A&G’s management for their, own gains” and “conspired to perpetrate a scheme against Cross-Complainants to fraudulently induce a breach of the Agreement.” (SACC, ¶ 39.)

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants now demurrer to the second, third, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action in the SACC.

 

B.        Second and Third Causes of Action – Negligent and Intentional Misrepresentation 

 

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage. [Citations.] The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true. [Citations.] Each element of a fraud count must be pleaded with particularity so as to apprise the defendant of the specific grounds for the charge and enable the court to determine whether there is any basis for the cause of action, although less specificity is required if the defendant would likely have greater knowledge of the facts than the plaintiff. [Citation.]”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230–231.) Negligent misrepresentation requires that the party owe a duty to disclose. (See Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 841; Levine v. Blue Shield of California (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1117, 1136.)  

 

The SACC alleges the Nixpro Cross-Defendants along with the Cross-Defendants “Quick, Tran and Bohun represented to Cross-Complainants that they would operate the Business in a diligent and honest manner on behalf of Cross-Complainants and for Cross-Complainants’ benefit” including depositing the monies into the correct accounts as instructed by Cross-Complainants. (SACC, ¶¶ 55, 68.) Instead, Cross-Defendants conspired to embezzle money from the Business and induce Cross-Complainants to breach the Agreement. (SACC, ¶¶ 56, 69.)

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants demur to the second and third causes of action on the bases that no actional misrepresentation is alleged with the requisite specificity and there are no factual allegations showing reliance or causation. “In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) “Causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud and, therefore, each element must be pleaded with specificity. (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166 disapproved on other grounds by Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905.) Specificity “necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Lazar, at p. 631.)

 

The SACC fails to allege who, when, and by what means the Nixpro Cross-Defendants represented to Cross-Complainants that it would operate the Business in a “diligent and honest manner.” (SACC, ¶¶ 22, 55, 68.) Instead, the SACC alleges in a conclusory manner that such representation was made “at all relevant times.” (SACC, ¶¶ 22, 55, 68.) The SACC also fails to allege specific facts as to how the Nixpro Cross-Defendants induced Cross-Complainants to breach the Agreement since the SACC fails to allege that discrepancies in accounting led to IMA terminating the Agreement.

 

The SACC alleges that Vinnitskaya’s representations about accepting a reduced monthly fee and to not pay taxes were the reasons IMA terminated the Agreement. The SACC does not allege that Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ embezzlement of funds was the reason IMA decided to terminate the Agreement. “If the defrauded plaintiff would have suffered the alleged damage even in the absence of the fraudulent inducement, causation cannot be alleged and a fraud cause of action cannot be sustained.” (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1064.) In other words, the SACC fails to allege that but for the Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ representation, the Agreement would not have been terminated.

 

The demurrer to the second and third causes of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

C.         Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action – Receipt of Stolen Property (Penal Code, § 496(c)) and Conversion

 

Penal Code § 496(a) states in relevant part:  

 

Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment in a county jail for not more than one year, or imprisonment pursuant to subdivision (h) of Section 1170. 

 

(Id. [italics added].) 

 

To plead a cause of action for conversion, one must allege (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)¿ 

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action on the basis that Cross-Complainants lack standing as they had no legal right to the monies allegedly embezzled. The Nixpro Cross-Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action on the bases that Cross-Complainants had no ownership or right of possession and therefore cannot show Cross-Defendants disposed of the monies in a manner inconsistent with Cross-Complainants’ property rights.

 

The SACC alleges that “Cross-Complainants had a right to possess all profits, funds, and monies pursuant” to Section 3.3 of the Agreement, which provides:

 

Section 3.3 Company Activities: [] Manager may, in its sole discretion, perform certain activities at Company’s request, including, without limitation, distribution activities under the Cannabis Licenses (the “Company Activities”). All revenues generated from the sales of goods and/or services in connection with Company Activities shall be excluded from both the Project and the calculation of Gross Revenues under this Agreement. The Company Activities shall not interfere in any manner with Manager’s business activities or any other valid activities on the Leased Premises, which continued uncured interference by Company shall constitute a material breach of this Agreement.

 

(SACC, ¶¶ 94, 95, Ex. 1, § 3.3)

 

While the fifth cause of action alleges that Cross-Complainants have the right of possession, it does not allege that they are the legal owners of the monies, accordingly the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Moreover, the SACC fails to explain how the fact that the revenue generated by the Business is excluded from the Project and Gross-Revenue under section 3.3 of the Agreement legally operates to give Cross-Complainants a possessory right to the revenue. Section 3.3 states that the Manager may perform distribution activities and other activities “at Companies request, without limitation, distribution activities under the Cannabis License [.]” (SACC, ¶¶ 94, 95, Ex. 1, § 3.3 [italics added].)

 

Cross-Complainants do not allege that IMA requested that they take possession of the monies from the cannabis sale or that such an instruction was permitted under the Cannabis License. The Nixpro Cross-Defendants assert that IMA as the holder of the cannabis license is the only entity with ownership and a possessory right to any revenue because legally it is the only entity that can accept ownership or possession of the cash paid by consumers for licensed cannabis products. (Bus. & Prof. Code, §§ 26031.5, 26038; LAMC Code, §§ 105.06(a), 104.21(e).)

 

Cross-Complainants’ opposition does not address the fact that they may not legally own or take possession of the monies generated from the sale of licensed cannabis products. Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

D.        Seventh, Eighth, and Ninth Causes of Action: Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations; Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations, and Negligent Interference with Economic Relations 

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations are ‘(1) the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of that contract; (3) the defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.’ ” (Redfearn v. Trader Joe's Co. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 989, 997, citing Reeves v. Hanlon (2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1148.)¿ 

 

The tort of intentional interference with prospective economic relations is the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. (See Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 378 [Analyzing the burden of proof and the “the elements of the tort variously known as interference with ‘prospective economic advantage,’ ‘prospective contractual relations,’ or ‘prospective economic relations’[.]”].)  Subsequently, in Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1153 and Ixchel Pharma, LLC v. Biogen, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 1130, 1141, the California Supreme Court reiterated that the correct name of the tort was intentional interference with prospective economic advantage.  

 

To state a claim for the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage (IIPEA), the claimant must allege: (1) an economic relationship between the claimant and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the claimant; (2) the defendant or cross-defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant or cross-defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the claimant proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.¿(Marsh v. Anesthesia Services Medical Group, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 480, 504.) 

 

The tort of negligent interference with a prospective economic advantage (“NIPEA”) differs in that the defendant's act disrupting the economic relationship does not need to be intentional. (Venhaus v. Shultz (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1078.) “The tort of negligent interference with economic relationship arises only when the defendant owes the plaintiff a duty of care.” (Stolz v. Wong Communications Limited Partnership (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1811, 1825 [italics original].) ¿For both IIPEA and NIPEA, the interference must be wrongful by some legal measure other than the fact of the interference itself. (Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 378.) “[A]n act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Korea Supply, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 1159.) 

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants demur to the seventh, eighth, and ninth cause of action on the basis that the Cross-Complainants fail to allege facts showing causation.

 

The court finds that the SACC fails to allege that the Nixpro Cross-Defendants' alleged embezzlement scheme caused A&G to terminate the Agreement or how such conduct caused Cross-Complainants to breach the Agreement themselves. As pled, the SACC alleges that Cross-Complaints breached their own contract due to Cross-Defendants’ inducement:

 

Cross-Complainants, upon information and belief, and thereupon allege that Vinnitskaya, Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun conspired to perpetrate a scheme against Cross-Complainants to fraudulently induce a breach of the Agreement. Cross-Complainants, upon information and belief, and thereupon allege that Vinnitskaya, Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun deceived, defrauded, and conspired against A&G to induce an early termination of the Agreement whereby IMA and Baranovskiy would then be free to form a business relationship together.

 

(SACC, ¶¶ 126, 136, 146.) “Because a party to a contract owes no tort duty to refrain from interference with its performance, he or she cannot be bootstrapped into tort liability by the pejorative plea of conspiracy.” (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 514. In other words, the disruption of the contractual or economic relationship must be intentionally caused by the Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ conduct rather than Cross-Complaints. Lastly, the SACC fails to show that Cross-Complainants’ economic relationship with the Business Consumers existed separate and apart from the Agreement such that Cross-Complainants had a separate and distinct economic relationship protected under IIEPA and NIPEA.

 

Based on the above, the demurrer to the seventh, eighth, and ninth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

E.        Tenth Causes of Action –Unfair Business Practices  

 

Business and Professions Code § 17200 (“UCL”) prohibits “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.”¿(Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200;¿see Clark v. Superior Court¿(2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 610.) To plead this statutory claim, the pleadings must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation. (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc.¿(1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.) 

 

“An unlawful business practice or act is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969.) “A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1473.) Lastly, a fraudulent business practice claims under section 17200 “is not based upon proof of the common law tort of deceit or deception, but is instead premised on whether the public is likely to be deceived.” (Pastoria v. Nationwide Ins. (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1490, 1499.) 

 

Cross-Defendants argue that the tenth cause of action fails because it is derivative of Cross-Complainants’ other claims. “When a statutory claim fails, a derivative UCL claim also fails.” (Aleksick v. 7-Eleven, Inc. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1176, 1185.) “Thus, when the underlying legal claim fails, so too will a derivative UCL claim.” (AMN Healthcare, Inc. v. Aya Healthcare Services, Inc. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 923, 950.) 

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ demurrer has been sustained as to all causes of action alleged against them such that the derivative UCL claim also fails. Cross-Complainants’ opposition argues that Cross-Defendants breached the fourth cause of action for breach of the implied duty of good faith and fair dealing, but the fourth cause of action is not alleged only against Cross-Defendant IMA. (Opposition, at p. 8:3-8.) Moreover, the court previously found that the tenth cause of action was not pled with reasonable particularity and failed to show how the Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ conduct was unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent under the UCL. (Order 12/15/2023.) Here, the tenth cause of action fails to allege how the Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ scheme to embezzle monies was an unlawful practice or an unfair practice that caused injury to consumers or was fraudulent in a manner that is likely to deceive the public.  

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the tenth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  

 

F.        Eleventh Cause of Action – Conspiracy 

 

“[A]lleging the existence of a conspiracy or aiding and abetting activity does not turn a garden-variety contract cause of action [] into a derivative tort cause of action [].” (AMN Healthcare, Inc., supra, 28 Cal.App.5th at p. 949.) “Conspiracy is not a cause of action, but a legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves, share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration.” (Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at pp. 510–511.)  

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants demur to the eleventh cause of action on the basis that it is not a cause of action but a mechanism to impose vicarious liability premised on a substantive claim. (See Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Sherwood Partners, Inc. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 802, 823.) The court agrees.

 

The demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  

 

Conclusion

 

The Nixpro Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the SACC is sustained with leave to amend as to the second, third, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, and ninth causes of action. The demurrer is sustained without leave to amend as to the tenth and eleventh cause of action. Plaintiff is granted 30 days leave to amend. A&G asserts it was willing and ready to make payments to the California Department of Tax and Fee Administration (“CDTFA”) but Vinnitskaya, on behalf of IMA, instructed A&G not to pay the tax liabilities until AMA could settle with CDTFA. (SACC, ¶ 31.)¿¿ 

¿ 

ii.         Cross-Complainants’ Relationship with Baranovskiy and the other Cross-Defendants 

¿ 

In June 2022, A&G hired Nikita Baranovskiy (“Baranovskiy”) to assist with the Business’ day-to-day operations. (SACC, ¶ 20.) Baranovskiy is the Chief Executive Officer of Nixpro, Inc.. (SACC, ¶ 6.) Baranovskiy in turn hired his associates “Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun as personnel to manage the Business’s day-to-day operations.” (SAC, ¶ 21.) In January 2023, Baranovskiy deposited monies from the cash registers into a bank account belonging to him and/or Nixpro Inc. in violation of A&G’s instruction to deposit all monies into IMA’s bank account. (SACC, ¶ 23.) In March 2023, A&G’s accountant discovered Baranovskiy’s scheme to embezzle the Business’s profits. (SACC, ¶¶ 25, 28.) In June 2023, A&G terminated the partnership with Baranovskiy and his associates. (SACC, ¶ 30.)¿¿ 

¿ 

Cross-Complainants assert that the reduced monthly fee and taxes were never an issue between IMA and A&G until A&G terminated Baranovskiy. (SACC, ¶¶ 33.) In June 2023, IMA alleged A&G breached the Agreement due to nonpayment of taxes and the reduced monthly fee. (SACC, ¶ 34.) On or about July 4, 2023, Cross-Complainants vacated the premises of the Business and ceased all activities regarding the Business or IMA.¿¿Cross-Complainants asserts that all Cross-Defendants are agents of each other and “conspired to embezzle funds from the Business under A&G’s management for their, own gains” and “conspired to perpetrate a scheme against Cross-Complainants to fraudulently induce a breach of the Agreement.” (SACC, ¶ 39.)  

¿ 

The Cross-Defendants now demurrer to the second, third, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and eleventh causes of action in the SACC.  

 

B.        Second and Third Causes of Action – Negligent and Intentional Misrepresentation¿ 

¿ 

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance,¿(4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage. [Citations.]¿The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true. [Citations.] Each element of a fraud count must be pleaded with particularity so as to apprise the defendant of the specific grounds for the charge and enable the court to determine whether there is any basis for the cause of action, although less specificity is required if the defendant would likely have greater knowledge of the facts than the plaintiff. [Citation.]”¿ (Chapman v. Skype Inc.¿(2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230–231.) Negligent misrepresentation requires that the party owe a duty to disclose. (See Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 841; Levine v. Blue Shield of California (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1117, 1136.)¿¿ 

 

The SACC alleges that Cross-Defendants “Quick, Tran and Bohun represented to Cross-Complainants that they would operate the Business in a diligent and honest manner on behalf of Cross-Complainants and for Cross-Complainants’ benefit” including depositing the monies into the correct accounts as instructed by Cross-Complainants. (SACC, ¶¶ 55, 68.) Instead, Cross-Defendants conspired to embezzle money from the Business and induce Cross-Complainants to breach the Agreement. (SACC, ¶¶ 56, 69.)  

 

The Cross-Defendants demur to the second and third causes of action on the basis that no actional misrepresentation is alleged with the requisite specificity nor are there factual allegations showing reliance or causation. “In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”¿(Lazar v. Superior Court¿(1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)“Causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud and, therefore, each element must be pleaded with specificity. (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc.¿(2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166 disapproved on other grounds by¿Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.¿(2022) 12 Cal.5th 905.) Specificity “necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Lazar, at p. 631.)  

 

The SACC fails to allege who, when, and by what means the Cross-Defendants represented to Cross-Complainants that it would operate the Business in a “diligent and honest manner.” (SACC, ¶¶ 22, 55, 68.) Instead, the SACC alleges in a conclusory manner that such representation was made “at all relevant times.” (SACC, ¶¶ 22, 55, 68.) The SACC also fails to allege specific facts as to how Cross-Defendants induced Cross-Complainants to breach the Agreement since the SACC fails to allege that discrepancies in accounting led to IMA terminating the Agreement.  

 

The SACC alleges that Vinnitskaya’s representations about accepting a reduced monthly fee and to not pay taxes were the reasons IMA terminated the Agreement. The SACC does not allege that Cross-Defendants’ embezzlement of funds was the reason IMA decided to terminate the Agreement. “If the defrauded plaintiff would have suffered the alleged damage even in the absence of the fraudulent inducement, causation cannot be alleged and a fraud cause of action cannot be sustained.” (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1064.) In other words, the SACC fails to allege that but for Cross-Defendants’ representation, the Agreement would not have been terminated.  

 

The demurrer to the second and third causes of action is sustained with leave to amend.  

 

C.        ¿Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action – Receipt of Stolen Property (Penal Code, § 496(c)) and Conversion 

 

Penal Code § 496(a) states in relevant part:¿¿ 

¿ 

Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment in a county jail for not more than one year, or imprisonment pursuant to subdivision (h) of Section 1170.¿ 

¿ 

(Id. [italics added].)¿ 

¿ 

To plead a cause of action for conversion, one must allege (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)¿¿ 

 

The Cross-Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action on the basis that Cross-Complainants have no legal right to the monies allegedly embezzled. Cross-Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action on the bases that Cross-Complainants had no ownership or right of possession and therefore cannot show Cross-Defendants disposed of the monies in a manner inconsistent with Cross-Complainants’ property rights. Moreover, Cross-Defendants assert that the allegation “Baranovskiy, Nixpro, Khlevnoy, Quick, Tran, and Bohun converted approximately $2,300,000 from Cross-Complainants” is insufficient sum to support a conversion claim. (SACC, ¶¶ 29, 103, 116, 117.)

 

The SACC alleges that “Cross-Complainants had a right to possess all profits, funds, and monies pursuant” to Section 3.3 of the Agreement, which provides:  

 

Section 3.3 Company Activities: [] Manager may, in its sole discretion, perform certain activities at Company’s request, including, without limitation, distribution activities under the Cannabis Licenses (the “Company Activities”). All revenues generated from the sales of goods and/or services in connection with Company Activities shall be excluded from both the Project and the calculation of Gross Revenues under this Agreement. The Company Activities shall not interfere in any manner with Manager’s business activities or any other valid activities on the Leased Premises, which continued uncured interference by Company shall constitute a material breach of this Agreement. 

 

(SACC, ¶¶ 94, 95, Ex. 1, § 3.3) 

 

While the fifth cause of action alleges that Cross-Complainants have the right of possession, it does not allege that they are the legal owners of the monies, accordingly the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  

 

Moreover, the SACC fails to explain how the fact that the revenue generated by the Business is excluded from the Project and Gross-Revenue under section 3.3 of the Agreement, legally operates to give Cross-Complainants a possessory right to the revenue. Section 3.3 states that the Manager may perform distribution activities and other activities “at Companies request, without limitation, distribution activities under the Cannabis License [.]” (SACC, ¶¶ 94, 95, Ex. 1, § 3.3 [italics added].) 

 

Cross-Complainants do not allege that IMA requested that they take possession of the monies from the cannabis sale or that such an instruction was permitted under the Cannabis License. The Cross-Defendants assert that IMA as the holder of the cannabis license is the only entity with ownership and a possessory right to any revenue because legally it is the only entity that can accept ownership or possession of the cash paid by consumers for licensed cannabis products. (Bus. & Prof. Code, §§ 26031.5, 26038; LAMC Code, §§ 105.06(a), 104.21(e).)  

 

Cross-Complainants’ opposition does not address the fact that they may not legally own or take possession of the monies generated from the sale of licensed cannabis products. Moreover, “approximately $2,300,000” is not a sum certain nor does the SACC state that the sum converted by Cross-Defendants is a specific identifiable sum. (See SP Investment Fund I LLC v. Cattell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 898, 907.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  

D.        Seventh, Eighth, and Ninth Causes of Action: Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations; Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations, and Negligent Interference with Economic Relations¿ 

¿ 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations are ‘(1) the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of that contract; (3) the defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4)actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.’ ” (Redfearn v. Trader Joe's Co. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 989, 997, citing Reeves v. Hanlon(2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1148.)¿¿ 

¿ 

The tort of intentional interference with prospective economic relations is the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. (See Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 378 [Analyzing the burden of proof and the “the elements of the tort variously known as interference with ‘prospective economic advantage,’ ‘prospective contractual relations,’ or ‘prospective economic relations’[.]”].)¿ Subsequently, in Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1153 and Ixchel Pharma, LLC v. Biogen, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 1130, 1141, the California Supreme Court reiterated that the correct name of the tort was intentional interference with prospective economic advantage.¿¿ 

¿ 

To state a claim for the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage (IIPEA), the claimant must allege: (1) an economic relationship between the claimant and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the claimant; (2) the defendant or cross-defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant or cross-defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the claimant proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.¿(Marsh v. Anesthesia Services Medical Group, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 480, 504.)¿ 

¿ 

The tort of negligent interference with a prospective economic advantage (“NIPEA”) differs in that the defendant's act disrupting the economic relationship does not need to be intentional. (Venhaus v. Shultz (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1078.)“The tort of negligent interference with economic relationship arises only when the defendant owes the plaintiff a duty of care.” (Stolz v. Wong Communications Limited Partnership (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1811, 1825 [italics original].) ¿For both IIPEA and NIPEA, the interference must be wrongful by some legal measure other than the fact of the interference itself. (Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 378.) “[A]n act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Korea Supply, supra,¿ 29 Cal.4th at p. 1159.)¿ 

 

The Cross-Defendants demur to the seventh, eighth, and ninth cause of action on the basis that the Cross-Complainants fail to allege that Cross-Defendants had knowledge of the economic relationship between IMA and A&G. The court finds that the entire SACC is devoid of facts alleging that Cross-Defendants had knowledge of an economic or contractual relationship between Cross-Complainants and any third party.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the seventh, eighth, and ninth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend. 

 

F.        Eleventh Cause of Action – Conspiracy¿ 

¿ 

“[A]lleging the existence of a conspiracy or aiding and abetting activity does not turn a garden-variety contract cause of action [] into a derivative tort cause of action [].” (AMN Healthcare, Inc., supra,¿ 28 Cal.App.5th at p. 949.) “Conspiracy is not a cause of action, but a legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves,¿ share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration.” (Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at pp. 510–511.)¿¿ 

¿ 

The Cross-Defendants demur to the eleventh cause of action on the basis that it is not a cause of action but a mechanism to impose vicarious liability premised on a substantive claim. (See Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Sherwood Partners, Inc. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 802, 823.) The court agrees.  

 

The demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is sustained without leave to amend

 

Conclusion

 

The Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the SACC is sustained with leave to amend as to the second, third, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, and ninth causes of action. The demurrer is sustained without leave to amend as to the eleventh cause of action. Plaintiff is granted 30 days leave to amend. I.         Legal Standard

 

“A preliminary injunction may be granted at any time before judgment upon a verified complaint, or upon affidavits if the complaint in the one case, or the affidavits in the other, show satisfactorily that sufficient grounds exist therefore.” (CCP § 527(a).) “A preliminary injunction is an interim remedy designed to maintain the status quo pending a decision on the merits.” (MaJor v. Miraverde Homeowners Assn. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 618, 622.)

 

(a) An injunction may be granted in the following cases: 

 

(1) When it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually. 

 

(2) When it appears by the complaint or affidavits that the commission or continuance of some act during the litigation would produce waste, or great or irreparable injury, to a party to the action. 

 

(3) When it appears, during the litigation, that a party to the action is doing, or threatens, or is about to do, or is procuring or suffering to be done, some act in violation of the rights of another party to the action respecting the subject of the action, and tending to render the judgment ineffectual. 

 

(4) When pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief. 

 

(5) Where it would be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount of compensation which would afford adequate relief. 

 

(6) Where the restraint is necessary to prevent a multiplicity of judicial proceedings. 

 

(7) Where the obligation arises from a trust.

 

(CCP § 526(a).) 

 

“Before issuing a preliminary injunction, the trial court must ‘carefully weigh the evidence and decide whether the facts require[ ] such relief.’ [Citation.] The court evaluates the credibility of witnesses and makes factual findings on disputed evidence.” (Fleishman v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 350, 356.)

A judge is not precluded from granting a preliminary injunction merely because of conflicts in the evidence. (See National Subscription Television v. Formula International, Inc. (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 308, 314.) Because a hearing on a preliminary injunction is not a “trial of a question of fact” within the meaning of CCP § 632, a judge is not required to issue a statement of decision, even on request. (Oiye v. Fox (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1049.) A plaintiff seeking injunctive relief must show the absence of an adequate damages remedy at law. (CCP § 526(a)(4).) Mandatory preliminary injunctions rarely are granted, and are reserved for extreme cases where the right is established clearly. (Teachers Ins. & Annuity Assn. v. Furlotti (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1493.)

 

A preliminary injunction ordinarily cannot take effect unless and until the plaintiff provides an undertaking for damages which the enjoined defendant may sustain by reason of the injunction if the court finally decides that the plaintiff was not entitled to the injunction. (See CCP § 529(a); CRC, rule 3.1150(f); City of South San Francisco v. Cypress Lawn Cemetery Assn. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 916, 920.)

III.      Discussion

 

A.        Factual Summary

 

Plaintiffs assert that in 2004 Plaintiffs and Defendant Vargo agreed to merge Plaintiff’s Porter Ranch Physical Therapy, Inc. (“Porter Ranch PT”) with Defendant Vargo’s three physical therapy clinics (collectively “Vargo PT”) to form a joint venture (the “2004 Agreement” or “Joint Venture Agreement”). (Feeley Decl. ¶ 2.) Under the 2004 Agreement, Plaintiff would receive $22,000.00 per month draw from the Porter Ranch PT and Defendant Vargo would receive $8,000.00 per month. (Id. ¶ 3.) “If there were profits beyond those amounts, the partners would split them 50/50, with those distributions typically made on a quarterly basis.” (Ibid.)

 

As part of the 2004 Agreement, the parties opened additional clinics (the “Joint Venture Clinics”) wherein ownership of the clinics was based on the capital contributions and labor to each new location. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 5.) Contributions and labor included scouting for locations, purchasing supplies, evaluating potential hires, and handling administrative aspects of office openings. (Id. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff Feeley asserts his capital contributions and ownership interest in the Joint Venture Clinics are as follows:

  • Porter Ranch – Plaintiff’s ownership if 50%
  • Palmdale (2004) – Contribution: $56,114.00; Ownership: 40%
  • Reseda (2005) – Contribution: $120,000.00; Ownership: 30%
  • Burbank (2011) – Contribution: $60,000.00; Ownership: 40%
  • San Fernando (2018) – Contribution: $66,000.00; Ownership: 33%

 

(Feeley Decl. ¶ 8; Haj Decl. Ex. 12.) Plaintiff had access to the bank account for each joint venture clinic and profits were distributed on a quarterly basis. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 10.) Robert Greider (“Greider”) was tasked with the day-to-day management of the clinics. (Feeley Decl. ¶¶ 11-13.) Per the 2004 Agreement, Plaintiff was paid the agreed-upon $22,000 monthly draw and his proportional share of any profit distributions from the clinics. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 14.)

 

Plaintiff Feeley further asserts that the monthly draw and profit distributions were not based on how much Plaintiff worked.  After becoming ill in 2020, Plaintiff continued to receive the agreed draw and profit distributions for the joint venture clinics. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 16; Haj Decl. ¶¶ 7-10, Ex. 6-9, 16; Grieder Decl. ¶ 11.) However, on January 18, 2023, Plaintiff was ousted from the joint venture. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 17.) Despite disputing Vargo’s authority to change the 2004 Agreement, Plaintiff was physically locked out of the clinics, lost access to the bank accounts, and has been unable to participate in the management of the joint venture. (Id. ¶ 18.) Plaintiff Feeley asserts that February 2023 was the last day he was paid profit distributions or a monthly draw. (Ibid.) After attempts at reaching a settlement failed, Plaintiff filed suit on August 4, 2023. (Id. ¶ 21.)

 

In opposition, Defendants do not deny that Plaintiff Feeley participated in a profit-sharing plan, but Grieder asserts that participation in the profit-sharing plan was dependent on Feeley continuing to work for Vargo PT. (Grieder Decl. ¶ 2.) At issue is whether Plaintiff Feeley’s right to participate in the profit-sharing plan was contingent on being able to work.

                                               i.          For the Porter Ranch clinic (defined below): 50% of any distribution;

                                           iii.          For the Reseda clinic (defined below): 30% of any distribution;

 

                                           iv.          For the Burbank clinic (defined below): 40% of any distribution;

6)     Defendants are immediately enjoined from incurring or attributing business expenses to the joint venture clinics to the extent they otherwise would not have done so but for the terms of this order;

 

7)     As used in this order, the “joint venture clinics” refer to the following physical therapy clinics:

 

a)     The Vargo Physical Therapy, Inc. branded physical therapy clinic located at 3425 W. Victory Blvd., Burbank, California 91505 (the “Burbank clinic”);

 

b)     The Vargo Physical Therapy, Inc. branded physical therapy clinic located at 38656 Medical Center Dr., Suite B, Palmdale, California 93551 (the “Palmdale clinic”);

 

c)     The Vargo Physical Therapy, Inc. branded physical therapy clinic located at 18039 Sherman Way, Reseda, California 91335 (the “Reseda clinic”);

e)      The Vargo Physical Therapy, Inc. branded physical therapy clinic located at 11225 Tampa Avenue, Northridge, California 91326 (the “Porter Ranch clinic”).

 

“In deciding whether to issue a preliminary injunction to a plaintiff, a trial court must weigh two interrelated factors: (1) The plaintiff's likelihood of success on the merits at trial, and (2) the harm to the plaintiff if the injunction is not issued against the harm to the defendant if it is.” (Yee v. American National Ins. Co. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 453, 456.) The burden of proof is on the plaintiff as the moving party “to show all elements necessary to support issuance of a preliminary injunction.”  (O'Connell v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1481.)   

1.         Likelihood to Succeed on the Merits  

 

Under California law, an agreement may be express or implied by conduct and may be written or oral. (Civ. Code, §§ 1619, 1621, 1622, 1623.) The parties do not dispute that Plaintiff Feeley and Defendants were part of a profit-sharing agreement wherein Plaintiff received $22,000.00 a month plus money that was classified as either profits by Plaintiffs or as bonuses by Defendants. (Vargo Decl. ¶ 10; Haj Decl. ¶ 15, Ex. 16.) “Between 2008 and 2022, Vargo PT has paid Mr. Feeley, through Porter Ranch PT, a total of over $4.2 million under the Vargo PT profit-sharing plan.”(Grieder Decl. ¶ 8.)

 

Defendant Vargo asserts the $22,000 monthly payment was a monthly management fee that was contingent on Plaintiff Feeley continuing to work for Vargo PT and that the terms of the profit-sharing arrangement were solely determined at the discretion of Defendant Vargo. (Vargo Decl.  ¶ 16; Grieder Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendants maintain that any payments to Plaintiff Feeley were repayment of the loans with interest plus bonus compensation for work performed. (Vargo Decl. ¶¶ 16. 19.) Defendants assert that Defendant Vargo had the sole authority to modify the terms of the 2004 Agreement but provide no evidence of what these terms were or that continuing to work for Vargo PT was a condition of payment under the agreement.

 

“A contract is unenforceable as illusory when one of the parties has the unfettered or arbitrary right to modify or terminate the agreement or assumes no obligations thereunder.” (Harris v. TAP Worldwide, LLC (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 373, 385.) Here, the Declarations of Mr. Grieder and Defendant Vargo suggest that Defendant Vargo had the sole authority to change or terminate the profit-sharing agreement as he saw fit. (Vargo Decl.  ¶ 16; Grieder Decl. ¶ 5.) No evidence is provided by Defendants to support the finding that participation in the profit sharing and monthly draw was contingent on certain terms being met by the participants, including continuing to work for Vargo PT. In contrast, the evidence shows Plaintiff Feeley continued receiving the $22,000 monthly draw plus profits even though he was out sick and not working for Vargo PT, up until Defendants decided to stop all payments in early 2023. (Vargo Decl. ¶ 25; Haj Decl. Ex. 16.)

 

Defendants also do not dispute that Plaintiff made contributions to the opening of four Vargo PT/joint venture clinics but assert that the contributions were loans to Vargo PT and not capital, reflecting an ownership interest. (Vargo Decl. ¶ 16.) Defendants provide no evidence that Plaintiff Feeley’s contributions were loans, as Defendants fail to provide the loan documents with the agreed upon interest to support the findings that the repayment to Plaintiff was “repayment with interest of loans made to Vargo PT.” (Vargo Decl. ¶ 16.)

 

Defendants further assert that Plaintiff Feeley was merely a Clinical Director of the Porter Ranch clinic with responsibilities not different from those of any other Vargo PT Clinical Director. (Vargo Decl. ¶ 21; Grieder Decl. ¶ 9.) However, Defendants failed to explain why Plaintiff had access to the bank accounts of five Vargo PT clinics, specifically the five Plaintiff asserts were part of the Joint Venture Clincs, despite only being the Clinical Director for the Porter Ranch clinic. (Haj Decl. ¶ 7, Ex. 6, 7; Grieder Decl. ¶ 10.) Defendants further admit that Plaintiff Feeley had the authority to initiate the transfers himself. (Grieder Decl. ¶ 10.) Defendants fail to explain why Plaintiff Feeley was given access to the other bank accounts and fail to show it was common for participants who had made loans to Vargo PT to be given access to the bank accounts for each of the clinics to which the loans corresponded. Moreover, if the profit distributions were bonus compensation set by Defendant Vargo, why and how did Mr. Grieder calculate the appropriate payment amounts? (Vargo Decl. ¶ 16, Grider Decl. ¶ 10.)

 

As Defendants failed to rebut Plaintiffs’ evidence as to a continuing right to payment of a $22,000.00 monthly draw plus profit distributions, Plaintiff Feeley has shown he is likely to succeed on the merits.

 

      2.         Irreparable Harm 

 

“[L]osing a job, and the income it entails, amounts to irreparable harm.” (Costa Mesa City Employees' Assn. v. City of Costa Mesa (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 298, 308.) “To obtain a preliminary injunction, a plaintiff ordinarily is required to present evidence of the irreparable injury or interim harm that it will suffer if an injunction is not issued pending an adjudication of the merits.” (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 554; see also CCP § 526(a)(2).)

 

Plaintiff Feeley asserts that the joint venture was his family’s primary source of support and being cut off from those distributions resulted in Plaintiff having to sell property to pay the mortgage on the family home, legal fees, and related costs. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 23.) Plaintiff Feeley asserts he had to rely on the financial support of family members to meet basic expenses for living. (Ibid.)

 

After the May 17, 2024, hearing, the court allowed Defendants to conduct limited discovery on Plaintiffs’ financial condition to assess whether Plaintiff Feeley can meet his financial obligations and show he will suffer irreparable harm for which there is no adequate legal remedy. (CCP § 526.) After considering the parties' supplemental briefs, the court finds that the Plaintiffs cannot show they will suffer irreparable harm and that monetary damages would be an inadequate legal remedy. “Specific performance of a contract will not be compelled when an adequate remedy exists at law, and if monetary damages afford adequate relief and are not extremely difficult to ascertain, an injunction cannot be granted.” (Thayer Plymouth Center, Inc. v. Chrysler Motors Corp. (1967) 255 Cal.App.2d 300, 306 (Thayer).)

 

Plaintiff Feeley asserts that he had to sell the property to pay ordinary expenses such as the mortgage and legal fees. (Feeley Decl. ¶ 23.) “An injunction cannot issue in a vacuum based on the proponents' fears about something that may happen in the future. It must be supported by actual evidence[.]” (Korean Philadelphia Presbyterian Church v. California Presbytery (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1069, 1084.) Here, it is immaterial that Plaintiffs in the past had to sell personal assets and property to sustain themselves financially. What matters is what present and future harm the Plaintiffs will suffer if the injunction is not granted. Absent from Plaintiff Feeley’s declaration are representations that in the future Plaintiff will need to sell personal assets and property to meet his basic financial obligations.

 

Plaintiff Feeley asserts that his present financial hardship includes “delayed payment of my usual contribution to Porter Ranch Physical Therapy, Inc.’s defined benefit plan” for the benefit of Plaintiff Feeley and his wife. (Feeley Supp. Decl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff Feeley also attached documents showing the Feeley family’s monthly expenses for their mortgage, health insurance, and home equity line of credit for May 2024. (Haj Supp. Decl. ¶ 17, Ex. 4.)

 

Defendants provide evidence that Plaintiffs currently have a significant sum in cash assets as of May 2024.  (See Pancer Supp. Decl. Ex. 1, 2, 4, 5.)  The cash assets do not include Plaintiffs’ retirement accounts or other assets. The court considers Plaintiff Feeley’s monthly expenses against the sum he has available to him in cash assets. Excluding Plaintiffs’ legal fees, the court finds that the cash assets are sufficient to meet the Plaintiffs’ financial obligations in the near future, precluding the finding of irreparable harm. (See California Retail Portfolio Fund GMBH & Co. KG v. Hopkins Real Estate Group (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 849, 857 [“In the context of injunctions, insolvency or the inability to otherwise pay money damages is a classic type of irreparable harm”].)

 

Plaintiff Feeley asserts that another financial hardship is the legal fees he will incur in this action. (Haj Decl. ¶¶ 7, 8.) The court is unaware of any case law finding that the inability to afford legal fees equates to irreparable harm and Plaintiffs have cited no legal authority supporting such a proposition.

 

While the court agrees that the loss of income may in some instances constitute irreparable harm, Plaintiffs fail to show that the loss of income will result in the Feeley family lacking “funds necessary to feed, house, and clothe themselves and their families for the duration” of this litigation. (White, supra, 30 Cal.4th at p. 559.) Plaintiffs fail to provide evidence showing they will be unable to cover future costs for basic necessities or that they will have to cash in their retirement and pay penalties to afford essential necessities. “The applicant must demonstrate a real threat of immediate and irreparable injury [citations] due to the inadequacy of legal remedies.” (Triple A Machine Shop, Inc. v. State of California (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 131, 138.)

Consequently, Plaintiffs fail to show that unless this injunction is granted, the cash reserves they have will be insufficient to sustain them financially and they will be forced to sell property and personal assets that are unique and cannot be replaced and compensated by monetary damages. “Generally, where damages afford an adequate remedy by way of compensation for breach of contract, equitable relief will be denied.” (Thayer, supra, 255 Cal.App.2d at p. 306.)

 

The court is also concerned that if the injunction is granted but Plaintiff fails to prevail in this action, Plaintiffs will be unable to repay the money given to them under this injunction. “In failing to consider the effect of its interim relief on the status quo of the litigation, the trial court ignored the foundational legal principle that the general purpose of a preliminary injunction is to preserve the status quo pending a final adjudication of the claims on the merits.” (O'Connell v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1472.)

 

The court finds that the Plaintiffs have failed to meet their burden of showing they will suffer irreparable harm that is not fully compensable by monetary damages. “In general, if the plaintiff may be fully compensated by the payment of damages in the event he prevails, then preliminary injunctive relief should be denied.” (Tahoe Keys Property Owners' Assn. v. State Water Resources Control Bd. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1471.)

 

C.        Plaintiffs’ Request for Access to the Joint Venture Clinics

 

The evidence before the court reflects that Defendant Vargo decided to cut Plaintiff off from the monthly draw and profit sharing because Plaintiff has been unable to return to work due to health reasons. (Vargo Decl. ¶ 24; Haj Decl. ¶ 15, Ex. 16; Compl. ¶¶ 19, 20.) Plaintiff Feeley provides no evidence that his health has sufficiently improved and he can resume work at the Joint Venture Clinics, including the work involved in managing the clinics. Defendants have also provided evidence that Plaintiff Feeley’s efforts to physically access the Joint Venture Clinics has disrupted business operations and patient care. (Wolpert Decl. ¶¶ 4-6; Gradascevic Decl. ¶ 5-8.)

 

Lastly, the operative pleading does not allege that Defendants have mismanaged the assets from the Joint Venture Clinic such that Plaintiffs need access to the clinics’ bank accounts to assess the extent of such damage. Plaintiffs also fail to show that ordinary discovery is inadequate to obtain the information and evidence Plaintiffs need to prove their claims

 

Based on the above, the court denies Plaintiffs’ request for preliminary injunction.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiffs’ request for a preliminary injunction is denied. Defendants to give notice.
