Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Dept. F47

Date: 5/16/24                                                              TRIAL DATE: 8/26/24

Case #21CHCV00965

 

SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Motion filed on 3/18/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Cross-Defendants L.A. Tire Tech, Inc. and Gerard Neglio

RESPONDING PARTY: Cross-Complainant SiteOne Landscape Supply, LLC

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order, pursuant to CCP 425.16, striking the First Amended Cross-Complaint (FAXC) of SiteOne Landscape Supply, LLC.  Alternatively, Cross-Defendants seek an order striking the following portions of the First Amended Cross-Complaint:

 

(1) The purported first cause of action for breach of contract. (FAXC ¶¶ 20-33.)

(2) The purported second cause of action for express indemnity. (FAXC ¶¶ 34-39.)

(3) The purported third cause of action for declaratory relief. (FAXC ¶¶ 40-44.)

(4) Page 4, lines 18 to 23 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provides: “Cross-Defendants’ indemnity obligation under Section 10 of the Agreement has been triggered by the filing of this lawsuit, which arises “out of or occurring in connection with the Services purchased from or provided by Vendor [L.A. Tire], or any act or omission of Vendor [L.A. Tire] (including any Vendor [L.A. Tire] subcontractor, supplier, or other third party) or Vendor’s [L.A. Tire] negligence, gross negligence, willful misconduct or breach of [the] ARI Terms.”

(5) Page 5, lines 26 to 28 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, and page 6, lines 1 to 2 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provide: “Cross-Defendants’ indemnity obligation to SiteOne was triggered by the filing of this lawsuit, which seeks damages ‘arising out of or occurring in connection with the Services purchased from or provided by Vendor [L.A. Tire], or any act or omission of Vendor [L.A. Tire], (including any Vendor [L.A. Tire] subcontractor, supplier, or other third party) or Vendor’s [L.A. Tire] negligence, gross negligence, willful misconduct or breach of [the] ARI Terms.’”

(6) Page 7, lines 2 to 6 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provides: “Despite repeated requests, Cross-Defendants refuse to indemnify SiteOne for the costs it has already incurred in defense of this lawsuit as required by the Agreement. Cross-Defendants also refuse to confirm their agreement to indemnify SiteOne for any other costs or damages incurred in connection with this action as required by the Agreement. As a result, Cross-Defendants further breached the Agreement.”

(7) Page 7, lines 26 to 28 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provides: “The Agreement entitles SiteOne to be indemnified fully with respect to any losses that SiteOne incurs as a consequence of the allegations contained in the FAC, which arise out of L.A. Tire’s breaches of the Agreement set forth above.”

 

RULING: The motion is denied. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant L.A. Tire Tech, Inc. (L.A. Tire Tech) is a fleet maintenance company who allegedly provided maintenance and repair services to Defendant/Cross-Complainant SiteOne Landscape Supply, LLC (SiteOne) pursuant to an oral agreement.  As of 8/21/21, SiteOne notified L.A. Tire Tech that all of its services were no longer needed by SiteOne.  L.A. Tire Tech contends that at that time it had submitted over $180,000.00 in invoices for services performed by L.A. Tire Tech on the fleet of SiteOne as requested by SiteOne.  L.A. Tire Tech contends that, despite repeated demands, SiteOne has failed and refuses to pay the invoices.   

 

On 12/17/21, L.A. Tire Tech filed this action against SiteOne for: (1) Goods and Services Rendered (Common Count), (2) Account Stated (Common Count), (3) Open Book Account (Common Count) and (4) Breach of Oral Contract.  On 3/7/22, L.A. Tire Tech filed its operative First Amended Complaint for: (1) Goods and Services Rendered (Common Count), (2) Open Book Account (Common Count) and (3) Breach of Oral Contract.  On 3/18/22, SiteOne answered the First Amended Complaint. 

 

On 10/16/23, this Court granted SiteOne’s motion for leave to file a cross-complaint against L.A. Tire Tech and Gerard J. Neglio (Neglio) (collectively, Cross-Defendants), the sole owner of L.A. Tire Tech, for: (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Express Indemnity.  After meet and confer efforts failed to resolve the issues Cross-Defendants had with the cross-complaint, on 11/17/23, Cross-Defendants filed and served their demurrer to the entire cross-complaint and motion to strike which sought  to strike paragraph 6 from the cross-complaint.  The demurrer and motion to strike were scheduled for hearing on 1/2/24.

 

On 12/15/23, Cross-Defendants filed and served a Special Motion to Strike SiteOne’s entire cross-complaint which was originally scheduled for hearing on 6/21/24 and then, on 12/18/23, rescheduled by Cross-Defendants to 2/2/24.

 

On 12/18/23, SiteOne filed and served oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike and on 12/22/23, Cross-Defendants filed and served replies to the oppositions.  On 1/2/24, the Court sustained the demurrer with 15 days leave to amend and granted the motion to strike without leave to amend.  (See 1/2/24 Minute Order).  On 1/17/24, pursuant to this Court’s order, SiteOne filed and served its First Amended Cross-Complaint.

 

On 1/22/24, SiteOne filed and served its opposition to the Special Motion to Strike which addressed the original cross-complaint and on 1/26/24, Cross-Defendants filed and served their  reply to that opposition.

 

On 2/2/24, the Court denied Cross-Defendants’ Special Motion to Strike, which addressed the original cross-complaint, as moot due to the filing of the First Amended Cross-Complaint in response to the ruling on the demurrer to the original cross-complaint.  (See 2/2/24 Minute Order). 

 

On 2/21/24, Cross-Defendants filed their answer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint and on 3/18/24, Cross-Defendants filed and served the instant Special Motion to Strike which seeks an order, pursuant to CCP 425.16, striking the First Amended Cross-Complaint (FAXC) of SiteOne.  Alternatively, Cross-Defendants seek an order striking the following portions of the First Amended Cross-Complaint:

 

(1) The purported first cause of action for breach of contract. (FAXC ¶¶ 20-33.)

(2) The purported second cause of action for express indemnity. (FAXC ¶¶ 34-39.)

(3) The purported third cause of action for declaratory relief. (FAXC ¶¶ 40-44.)

(4) Page 4, lines 18 to 23 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provides: “Cross-Defendants’ indemnity obligation under Section 10 of the Agreement has been triggered by the filing of this lawsuit, which arises “out of or occurring in connection with the Services purchased from or provided by Vendor [L.A. Tire], or any act or omission of Vendor [L.A. Tire] (including any Vendor [L.A. Tire] subcontractor, supplier, or other third party) or Vendor’s [L.A. Tire] negligence, gross negligence, willful misconduct or breach of [the] ARI Terms.”

(5) Page 5, lines 26 to 28 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, and page 6, lines 1 to 2 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provide: “Cross-Defendants’ indemnity obligation to SiteOne was triggered by the filing of this lawsuit, which seeks damages ‘arising out of or occurring in connection with the Services purchased from or provided by Vendor [L.A. Tire], or any act or omission of Vendor [L.A. Tire], (including any Vendor [L.A. Tire] subcontractor, supplier, or other third party) or Vendor’s [L.A. Tire] negligence, gross negligence, willful misconduct or breach of [the] ARI Terms.’”

(6) Page 7, lines 2 to 6 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provides: “Despite repeated requests, Cross-Defendants refuse to indemnify SiteOne for the costs it has already incurred in defense of this lawsuit as required by the Agreement. Cross-Defendants also refuse to confirm their agreement to indemnify SiteOne for any other costs or damages incurred in connection with this action as required by the Agreement. As a result, Cross-Defendants further breached the Agreement.”

(7) Page 7, lines 26 to 28 of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, which provides: “The Agreement entitles SiteOne to be indemnified fully with respect to any losses that SiteOne incurs as a consequence of the allegations contained in the FAC, which arise out of L.A. Tire’s breaches of the Agreement set forth above.”

 

SiteOne has opposed the motion and Cross-Defendants have filed a reply to the opposition. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Cross-Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice of the declarations of Gerald Neglio and Ryan C. McKim filed on 12/15/23 in support of Cross-Defendants’ prior Special Motion to Strike directed at the original cross-complaint is granted as to the existence of the declarations in the court file but not as to the truth of any matter asserted in those declarations. 

 

SiteOne’s objections (numbers 1-12) to the declaration of Gerard Neglio are overruled.

 

Cross-Defendants’ objection (number 1) to the declaration of Christopher Foster is overruled.

 

Cross-Defendants’ objections (number 1-7) to the declaration of Frank Dunn are overruled.

CCP 425.16, the anti-SLAPP (Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation) statute, sets forth a procedure for striking complaints/cross-complaints, or portions thereof, which challenge the exercise of constitutionally protected free speech rights.  See Monster Energy Co. (2019) 7 C5th 781, 788 citing Kibler (2006) 39 C4th 192, 196; Varian Medical Systems, Inc. (2005) 35 C4th 180, 192.

 

CCP 425.16(b)(1) provides:

 

“A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.”

 

Under the anti-SLAPP statute, an “‘act in furtherance of a person's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.”  CCP 425.16(e).

 

In ruling on a special motion to strike, the court undertakes a two-step analysis.  First, the court must determine whether the defendant/cross-defendant has met its burden of establishing the challenged claim(s) arise out defendant/cross-defendant’s exercise of a constitutional right of free speech or petition.  Bonni (2021) 11 C5th 995, 1009; Navellier (2002) 29 C4th 82, 88-89; Jarrow Formulas, Inc. (2003) 31 C4th 728, 733.  Second, if the defendant meets this initial burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff/cross-complainant to establish the claim(s) have minimal merit.  Bonni, supra; Golden Gate Land Holdings LLC (2022) 81 CA5th 82, 89.

 

In determining whether a complaint/cross-complaint arises from protected activity, the protected activity itself must be the wrong complained of, and not just evidence of liability or a step leading to a different act for which liability is alleged.  See Golden Gate, supra at 90.  Allegations regarding protected activity that do not form the basis for recovery, but rather  merely provide context, are not covered under the anti-SLAPP statute.  Trilogy Plumbing, Inc. (2020) 50 CA5th 920, 933; Aguilar (2012) 207 CA4th 1152, 1163.

 

Here, SiteOne’s breach of contract cause of action is based on a written contract between L.A. Tire Tech and ARI of which SiteOne claims to be a third-party beneficiary.  (FAXC ¶¶11, 21, Ex.A thereto).  SiteOne alleges that L.A. Tire Tech breached the contract by: (1) failing to submit all invoices for repairs within 30 days of completing the services; (2) failing to maintain complete and accurate records relating to the services it provided to SiteOne; (3) failing to conform to generally accepted practices in L.A. Tire Tech’s field; (4) failing to perform services in a workmanlike and ethical manner; (5) failing to perform services that were of professional quality; and (6) by providing goods that were not free of defects. (FAXC ¶¶25-28; Dunn Decl. ¶¶2-5).   

 

The breach of contract claim is also based, in part, on L.A. Tire Tech’s refusal to indemnify Site One for the costs it has incurred in defense of this lawsuit based on the indemnity provision in the alleged contract.  (FAXC ¶¶18, 29, Ex.A, Section 10).  The express indemnity cause of action is also based on the indemnity provision in the agreement/contract of which SiteOne claims to be a third-party beneficiary.   (FAXC ¶¶18, 34-39 and Ex.A thereto). 

 

The declaratory relief cause of action merely reflects that the parties have a dispute regarding their respective contractual obligations. 

 

While SiteOne alleges that the filing of the underlying lawsuit “triggered” Cross-Defendants’ indemnity obligation, L.A. Tire Tech’s alleged breaches of contract, which SiteOne contends caused it damages including correcting maintenance and repair work that was performed incorrectly or of inferior quality and costs to ensure SiteOne had complete and accurate maintenance and repair records for its vehicles and equipment, are not protected activity.  (See FAXC ¶¶24-28, 33; Dunn Decl. ¶5).

 

An indemnity claim based on the filing of litigation does not necessarily constitute protected activity for purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.  See Wong (2019) 43 CA5th 358, 365, 367.  As noted above, if the filing of litigation is merely a step leading to a different act for which liability is asserted and not the core injury-producing conduct, the application of the anti-SLAPP statute is not triggered.  Id; See also Area 51 Productions, Inc. (2018) 20 CA5th 581, 594; Park (2017) 2 C5th 1057, 1062.   

 

Cross-Defendants have not shown that SiteOne’s cross-claims arise out L.A. Tire Tech’s protected activity of filing the underlying complaint.  Rather, SiteOne’s cross-claims arise out of L.A. Tire Tech’s alleged breaches of the agreement, including breaching the obligation to indemnify SiteOne for expenses incurred in defending against the underlying litigation.  As such, Cross-Defendants have not met their initial burden of establishing that SiteOne’s cross-claims arise out of protected activity.  

 

However, even if the causes of action in the First Amended Cross-Complaint could be said to arise from protected activity, SiteOne has met its burden of establishing its claims have minimal merit.  See Golden Gate Land Holdings LLC, supra at 89.  In making this determination, the court must draw every legitimate favorable interest from SiteOne’s evidence.  See Kinsella (2020) 45 CA5th 442, 453.

 

The elements of a breach of contract cause of action are: (1) the existence of a contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage.  Richman (2014) 224 CA4th 1182, 1186.  Similarly, the elements of an express indemnity claim are: (1) the parties’ contractual relationship, (2) the indemnitee’s performance of that portion of the contract which gives rise to the indemnification claim, (3) the facts showing a loss within the meaning of the parties’ indemnification agreement, and (4) the amount of damages sustained.  Four Star Electric, Inc. (1992) 7 CA4th 1375, 1380. 

 

Through the declaration of Frank Dunn, SiteOne’s Vice President for Logistics, SiteOne has provided sufficient prima facie evidence to support its claims for breach of contract and express indemnity.  (See Dunn Decl. ¶¶2-5).  The evidence makes a sufficient prima facie showing that the agreement applies to the invoices at issue.  (See Foster Decl., Ex.3).  It also cannot be determined as a matter of law that L.A. Tire Tech is solely deemed a “maintenance vendor” such that Section 4.2 of the agreement would not apply.  The evidence indicates that L.A. Tire Tech performed maintenance services as well as repairs, inspections, certifications and other services.  (Dunn Decl.; Neglio Decl.).  Additionally, the evidence indicates that L.A. Tire Tech had access to ARI during the time the majority of the services at issue were allegedly performed and invoiced (i.e., prior to August 2021).  (Neglio Decl., Ex.2).   

 

Further, sufficient prima facie evidence has been submitted to establish that L.A. Tire reviewed, or had the opportunity to review, the terms of the agreement before agreeing to its terms.  (Foster Decl. ¶¶7-11, Ex.1-3).  In ruling on SiteOne’s demurrer to the original cross-complaint, the Court already determined that the indemnity provision does not require L.A. Tire Tech to indemnify SiteOne for SiteOne’s failure to pay for services rendered and properly invoiced by L.A. Tire Tech.  (See 1/2/24 Minute Order).  However, SiteOne is not precluded from seeking recovery for L.A. Tire Tech’s breaches of the agreement and indemnity for such breaches as the indemnity provision is not limited to third-party claims.  (See Dunn Decl. ¶¶2-5; FAXC, Ex.A Section 10); See Wilshire-Doheny Associates, Ltd. (2000) 83 CA4th 1380, 1396; Dream Theater, Inc. (2004) 124 CA4th 547, 556; Zalkind (2011) 194 CA4th 1010, 1022, 1026-1027; Hot Rods, LLC (2015) 242 CA4th 1166, 1181-1182.  As such, the agreement is not procedurally or substantively unconscionable. 

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied.

MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

(Requests for Admissions, Set 1)

 

Motion filed on 12/12/23.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Alpha Beta Company dba Food 4 Less

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Lorena Dominguez

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: A protective order: (1) limiting or restricting the scope of discovery Plaintiff Lorena Dominguez (Plaintiff) may seek in requests for admissions to information/ evidence that directly proves or disproves the elements of the causes of action pled in Plaintiff’s Complaint, pursuant to CCP 2017.020, and/or (2) limiting or restricting the frequency/ extent/number of requests for admissions Plaintiff may utilize, pursuant to CCP 2033.080(a) and CCP 2019.030.  Additionally, by this motion, Food 4 Less challenges Plaintiff’s declaration of necessity used to justify more than 35 requests for admission under CCP 2033.050, pursuant to CCP 2033.080(b)(2), and requests an order or ruling that, contrary to Plaintiff’s declaration, the number of requests for admissions is not warranted. 

 

Additionally, Food 4 Less requests an order imposing sanctions jointly on Plaintiff and her counsel of record, Ruben Vardanyan of Vardanyan Law Firm, in the amount of $1,435.00.

 

RULING: The motion is denied. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff Lorena Dominguez’s (Plaintiff) claim that she was injured when she slipped and fell on lettuce and/or water on the floor of a Defendant Alpha Beta Company dba Food 4 Less (Food 4 Less) grocery store on 9/1/21.  On 8/9/23, Plaintiff filed this action against Food 4 Less and Sergio Pulido, a Food 4 Less employee she interacted with on the date of the incident, for: (1) Negligence and (2) Premises Liability which includes allegations of willful failure to warn.  On 9/7/23, Food 4 Less filed and served its answer to the complaint.

 

On 9/25/23, Plaintiff electronically served a first set of discovery which included 201 Requests for Admissions.  (Dawood Decl. ¶4).  After meet and confer efforts, Plaintiff withdrew her first set of discovery.  (Id. ¶5).  On 11/9/23, Plaintiff served her Amended Requests for Admissions, Set 1, which include 178 requests for admissions along with Form Interrogatory 17.1.  (Id. ¶6, Ex.B, C).

 

On 11/17/23, Food 4 Less sent a meet and confer letter regarding the amended discovery asking Plaintiff to withdraw and/or limit the discovery by 12/1/23.  (Dawood Decl. ¶7, Ex.D).  Despite follow-ups, Food 4 Less never received a response to the meet and confer efforts regarding the Amended Special Interrogatories, Set 1.  (Id. ¶¶8-9).

 

On 12/12/23, Food 4 Less filed and served the instant motion seeking a protective order: (1) limiting or restricting the scope of discovery Plaintiff may seek in requests for admissions to information/ evidence that directly proves or disproves the elements of the causes of action pled in Plaintiff’s Complaint, pursuant to CCP 2017.020, and/or (2) limiting or restricting the frequency/ extent/number of requests for admissions Plaintiff may utilize, pursuant to CCP 2033.080(a) and CCP 2019.030.  Additionally, by this motion, Food 4 Less challenges Plaintiff’s declaration of necessity used to justify more than 35 requests for admission under CCP 2033.050, pursuant to CCP 2033.080(b)(2), and requests an order or ruling that, contrary to Plaintiff’s declaration, the number of requests for admissions is not warranted.  Additionally, Food 4 Less requests an order imposing sanctions jointly on Plaintiff and her counsel of record, Ruben Vardanyan of Vardanyan Law Firm, in the amount of $1,435.00.

 

On 4/8/23, Plaintiff filed and served an “Opposition to Defendant Alpha Beta Company dba Food 4 Less’s Motions for Protective Orders” which lists 5/16/24 as the only hearing date despite the fact that three separate motions for protective orders are on calendar for 5/14/24, 5/15/24 and 5/16/24.  On 5/9/24, Food 4 Less filed and served a reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

The scope of discovery is broad.  CCP 2017.010 provides:

 

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action. Discovery may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition, and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.”

 

However, if the Court determines the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, the Court must limit the scope of discovery.  See CCP 2017.020.

 

The party seeking a protective order has the burden to show “good cause” by presenting specific facts and admissible evidence establishing “undue burden,” etc. for the relief sought.  Fairmont Insurance Company (2000) 22 C4th 245, 255;  Goodman (1967) 253 CA2d 807, 819-820 .

 

With regard to Requests for Admissions, a party is limited to propounding 35 requests for admissions that do not relate to the genuineness of documents.  CCP 2033.030(a).  However, a party may propound more than 35 requests for admissions not relating to the genuineness of documents if the greater number is warranted because of the complexity or the quantity of the existing and potential issues in the particular case.  CCP 2033.040(a); CCP 2033.050.  If the responding party seeks a protective order on the ground that the number of requests for admissions is unwarranted, the propounding party has the burden of justifying the number of requests.  CCP 2033.040(b). 

 

As noted above, Food 4 Less seeks to: (1) limit or restrict the scope of discovery Plaintiff may seek in requests for admissions to information/ evidence that directly proves or disproves the elements of the causes of action pled in Plaintiff’s Complaint, pursuant to CCP 2017.020, and/or (2) limit or restrict the frequency/ extent/number of requests for admissions Plaintiff may utilize, pursuant to CCP 2033.080(a) and CCP 2019.030.  Additionally, by this motion, Food 4 Less challenges Plaintiff’s declaration of necessity used to justify more than 35 requests for admission under CCP 2033.050, pursuant to CCP 2033.080(b)(2), and requests an order or ruling that, contrary to Plaintiff’s declaration, the number of requests for admissions is not warranted. 

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has made a sufficient showing to justify the additional requests for admission in this case that concerns allegations of negligence and failure to warn.  Even if some of the requests seek information that may not itself be admissible, the responses may lead to the discovery of admissible information/evidence.  The Court also finds that the requests are not unreasonably cumulative, duplicative or harassing. 

 

While Food 4 Less may find other methods of discovery more convenient, less burdensome and/or less expensive, it has failed to establish that such other methods of discovery are obtainable for Plaintiff (i.e., it may be too expensive for Plaintiff to depose all of the individuals necessary to obtain the information sought in the requests).  CCP 2019.030(a)(1)  Similarly, Food 4 Less has not shown that it would be unduly burdensome or expensive to respond to the subject requests under the circumstances.  CCP 2019.030(a)(2).

 

Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied.  Plaintiff cites no authority for the request in the opposition.  Additionally, as noted above, Plaintiff filed one opposition in response to three separate motions set for hearings on three separate days with only one hearing date listed.  Also, the declaration submitted in support of the opposition does not allocate how much time was spent with regard to each motion.  (See Vardanyan Decl. ¶5).  As such, the Court cannot determine how much of the $3,000.00 in sanctions requested could properly be allocated to opposing the instant motion.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied.  Food 4 Less is ordered to provide responses to the Requests for Admissions within 30 days.  Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel is advised that in the  future separate oppositions to separate motions are required.  Failure to properly oppose motions in the future may result in opposition arguments not being considered.
