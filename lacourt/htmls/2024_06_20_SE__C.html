DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19NWCV00820    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: C

BLAINE HOLDING & DEVELOPMENT, LLC v. VIVERA PHARMACEUTICALS, INC.

CASE NO.:  19NWCV00820

HEARING:  6/20/24 @ 9:30 A.M.

#9

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor Blaine Holdings & Development, LLC’s Motion to Compel Compliance is GRANTED. The Court awards sanctions in the amount of $2,115.00.

Moving Party to give NOTICE.

 

The motion is unopposed as of June 17, 2024.

 

Plaintiff Blaine Holding & Development LLC sued defendant Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. for breaching a commercial lease agreement and then holding over following the lease’s expiration. On September 15, 2021, this Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff in the amount of $406,657.19. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 3.) Defendant and Judgement Debtor has not paid any of it.  (Decl. Yost, ¶ 5.)

 

On July 1, 2021, Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. CEO Paul Edalat registered Pala Diagnostics with the California Secretary of State. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 6.) On July 23, 2021, Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc., and Dalrada Health Inc. executed a Limited Liability Company Agreement to operate Pala Diagnostics. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 7.) Under the LLC Agreement, Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. is a minority member with a 49 percent interest in Pala Diagnostics. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 8.)

 

In December 2021, Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. sued Pala Diagnostics in Orange County Superior Court seeking judicial dissolution of the joint venture (the “Orange County Action”). (Decl. Yost, ¶ 9.)

 

On January 19, 2023, Dalrada Health Inc. moved to stay the Orange County action and conduct an appraisal to ascertain the value of Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc.’s 49 percent interest in Pala Diagnostics pursuant to Section 17707.03(c) of the Corporations Code. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 10.) That provision provides that judicial dissolution may be avoided “by purchasing for cash the membership interests owned by the members so initiating the proceeding . . . at their fair market value.” (Corp. Code § 17707.03(c)(1).) On March 6, 2023, the Orange County Superior Court granted Dalrada Health Inc.’s motion, stayed the action, and ordered an appraisal of Vivera Pharmaceutical Inc.’s 49 percent interest to be completed no later than July 10, 2023. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 11.) Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. and Dalrada Health Inc. engaged a third party to conduct one of three appraisals of Vivera Pharmaceutical, Inc.’s interest in Pala Diagnostics. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 11.) The appraisal has been delayed multiple times and, to date, no draft or final appraisal has been issued. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 12.)

 

On May 9, 2023, Blaine Holding & Development, LLC moved this Court for an order: (1) charging Vivera Pharmaceutical, Inc.’s 49 percent interest in Pala Diagnostics with the unpaid balance of the judgment pursuant to Section 708.3102 ; (2) assigning Vivera Pharmaceutical, Inc.’s 49 percent interest in Pala Diagnostics to Blaine Holding pursuant to Section 708.510; and (3) restraining Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. from encumbering, assigning, disposing, or spending its 49 percent interest in Pala Diagnostics. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 13.) On June 28, 2023, this Court granted the motion in its entirety. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 14 & Ex. 1.)

 

On June 5, 2024, the Court held a judgment debtor examination of the person most knowledgeable for Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc.

 

Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor moves to compel compliance with a deposition subpoena of business records served on non-party Paul Zimmer and CBIZ Forensic Services Group, LLC. The subpoena requests draft and final valuation reports; documents submitted by the parties in support of the valuation reports; and communications between the appraisers and the parties. Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor also seeks sanctions.

 

Legal Standard

A deposition subpoena for business records directs a nonparty's “custodian of records” to deliver a copy of the requested documents to a “deposition officer” or to make the original documents available to the subpoenaing party for inspection and copying. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2020.410, subd. (c); 2020.430, subd. (a)-(e).)

Meet and Confer

No meeting and conferring are required where the deponent fails to produce the documents described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).) A declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance is sufficient. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)

Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor complied with the above requirements. (Decl. Yost, ¶¶ 27, 30.)

Timeliness

The motion must be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (b).) The deposition record is “complete” as of the “date specified for production.” (Board of Registered Nursing v. Super. Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032-1033.)

Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor issued a deposition subpoena for business records to Paul Zimmer and CBIZ Forensic Services Group, LLC on January 12, 2024. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 18, Ex. 2.) The date for production stated on the subpoena is March 14, 2024. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 18, Ex. 2.) Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor filed the instant motion on April 2, 2024.

 

Thus, Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor is timely.

 

Compel Compliance

 

“‘An order compelling discovery must rest on a showing that the discovery is reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence….’"  (Cadiz Land Co. v. Rail Cycle (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 74, 117.) Further, a motion to compel production of documents described in a deposition notice must be accompanied by a showing of good cause, i.e. declarations containing specific facts justifying inspection of the documents. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).) The burden rests upon the party seeking the discovery to provide evidence from which the court may determine these conditions are met. (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Super. Ct. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 223-224.)

 

Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor advances the declaration of its counsel, Mark J. Yost. He states that Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor became concerned that the repeated delay of the appraisal may be due to an attempt by Vivera Pharmaceuticals, Inc. and Dalrada Health Inc. to improperly influence the valuation of Pala Diagnostics and/or to structure a settlement that improperly evades Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor’s lien. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 15.)  Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor asserts that it seeks the records as part of post-judgment discovery.

 

Because the information will help Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor enforce the judgment, the Court finds that Defendant has provided evidence to show that there is good cause and the requested discovery is reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence.

 

Based on the above, the motion to compel compliance is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

If the prevailing party asks for monetary sanctions, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party unless it finds the losing party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

 

Yost states that he spent 3.2 hours drafting the moving papers. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 32.) He anticipates spending 1.5 hours drafting a reply and another 1.5 hours to prepare and appear for the hearing. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 32.) His rate is $650 an hour. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 33.) He seeks an award of 6.0 hours billed at $450 per hour. (Decl. Yost, ¶ 34.)

 

Because the motion is unopposed, Yost does not need to draft a reply. Thus, the court awards attorney fees in the amount of $2,115.00 for 4.7 hours of work at $450 per hour.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED. Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART.

Edilberto Garcia, a Foklorio dance teacher, is alleged to have sexually assaulted students at Gage Middle School in the mid-1980s. In the operative First Amended Complaint filed on May 18, 2021, Plaintiffs John G.Z. Doe #1 and John R.Z. Doe #2 (“Plaintiffs”) sue Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”) for 2) sexual harassment (Civ. Code, §§ 51.9 and 52); 3) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention; 4) failure to report suspected child abuse; 5) negligent failure to warn, train, or educate; and 6) negligent supervision of a minor.

 

LAUSD seeks summary judgment and, in the alternative, summary adjudication.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiffs request judicial notice of the following: (1) Assem. Floor Analysis, Analysis of Assem. Bill No. 218 (2019-2020 Reg. Sess.) as amended August 30, 2019; (2) Sen. Rules Com., Analysis of Assem. Bill No. 218 (2019-2020 Reg. Sess.) as amended August 30, 2019; (3) Sen. Com. on Appropriations, Analysis of Assem. Bill No. 218 (2019- 2020 Reg. Sess.) as amended August 30, 2019; (4) Sen. Com. on Appropriations, Analysis of Assem. Bill No. 218 (2019- 2020 Reg. Sess.) as amended March 25, 2019; (5) Sen. Judiciary Com., Analysis of Assem. Bill No. 218 (2019-2020 Reg. Sess.) as amended March 25, 2019; (6) Assem. Floor Analysis, Third Reading, Analysis of Assem. Bill. No. 218 (2019-2020 Reg. Sess.) as amended March 25, 2019; (7) Assem. Floor Analysis, Third Reading, Analysis of Assem. Bill. No. 218 (2019-2020 Reg. Sess.) as introduced January 16, 2019; (8) Assem. Com. on Judiciary, Analysis of Assem. Bill No. 218 (2019-2020 Reg. Sess.) as introduced January 16, 2019.

 

The court will take judicial notice of the legislative history of a statute to ascertain the purpose of and meaning of an ambiguous statute. (Quelimane Co., Inc. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 45, fn. 9.)

 

Thus, the Court grants the requests.

 

Separate Statement

 

The supporting papers shall include a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts that the moving party contends are undisputed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(1).) Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(1).) The failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may in the court's discretion constitute sufficient grounds for denying the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(1).)

 

What witnesses said are not “material facts” as that term is used in the summary judgment statute. (Reeves v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 95, 106.) Material facts need to be stated as facts, rather than stating what witnesses said about the events. (Ibid.)

 

Here, many of the facts in the separate statement are stated as what witnesses said about the events. For example, fact number 36 states, “G.Z. testified that no inappropriate interactions with Garcia occurred on campus.” As the Court stated in its May 29, 2024, order, the Court will strike these prefatory words.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

LAUSD submitted evidentiary objections as part of its reply papers.

 

In granting or denying a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, the court needs rule only on those objections to evidence that it deems material to its disposition of the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (q).)

 

After reviewing LAUSD’s evidentiary objections, the Court finds that they are not material to the disposition of the motion and will not rule on them.

 

Legal Standard

 

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing no triable material fact exists. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party meets this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a converse prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) 

 

Discussion

 

As a preliminary matter, a judge may permit a defendant to raise an affirmative defense for the first time in its summary judgment motion absent a showing of prejudice to the plaintiff. (Nieto v. Blue Shield of Cal. Life & Health Ins. Co. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 60, 75.) Here, LAUSD raises the statute of limitations defense; argues Article XVI, Section 6 of California Constitution as a defense; and argues Education Code section 44808 as a defense for the first time in the instant motion. Given that Plaintiffs have opposed the arguments on their merits, the Court finds no showing of prejudice.

 

Thus, the Court permits LAUSD to raise these affirmative defenses.  

 

All Causes of Action – Statute of Limitations

 

LAUSD argues that the statute of limitations bars Plaintiffs’ causes of action. Under the applicable statute of limitations, Plaintiff must be no more than 40 years old when filing a complaint or Plaintiff must file five years following the discovery of a psychological injury, whichever occurs later. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.1, subd (a).) LAUSD argues that both Plaintiffs discovered the psychological injury from the perpetrator’s conduct outside the five-year window permitted to sue. Thus, the entire suit is barred.

 

LAUSD’s argument relies on the incorrect law. According to the current version of the statute, for childhood sexual assault that allegedly occurred on or before December 31, 2023, the law in effect on December 31, 2023, still applies. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.1, subd. (p).) On December 31, 2023, a revival provision was in effect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.1, former subd. (q).) It revived any claim that “has not been litigated to finality and that would otherwise be barred as of January 1, 2020.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.1, former subd. (q).) They may be commenced within three years of January 1, 2020. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.1, former subd. (q).)

 

Because LAUSD applied the incorrect law, it did not meet its burden to show that no triable issue of material fact exists. The burden does not shift to the opposing party.

 

All Causes of Action – Article XVI, Section 6 of California Constitution

 

LAUSD also argues that the retroactive application of AB 218 violates Article XVI, Section 6 of the California Constitution. The provision states, “the legislature has no power to give or lend, or to authorize the giving or lending…in any manner whatever, for the payment of liability of any individual, association, municipal or other corporation whatever; nor shall it have the power to make any gift or authorize the making of any gift, of any public money.” LAUSD argues that AB 218 unlawfully revived claims that had been barred by the statute of limitations; (2) AB 218 unlawfully removed a substantive element of the Plaintiffs’ cause of action, i.e. presentation of government tort claims. LAUSD further argues that the legislature has no power to create a liability against the state for any past act of negligence.

 

In Chapman v. State (1894) 104 Cal. 690, the California Supreme Court addressed the constitutionality of bringing lawsuits based on acts of negligence that were once barred. In Chapman, the plaintiff filed a claim with the board of harbor commissioners, which denied it. (104 Cal. at 692.) Two years later, the state allowed the claims that the board had rejected to be brought against the state in court. (104 Cal. at 693.) As the court reasoned, in passing legislation that allowed the rejected claims to be brought against the state, the legislature did not create any liability or cause of action against the state where none existed before, but rather gave one more remedy for enforcing liability. (Id. at 696.) Similarly, here, in reviving claims that the statute of limitations barred and removing the requirement to present a government tort claim, the legislature did not create liability, but removed a barrier to enforce liability.

 

The Court determines that LAUSD has not met its burden to show that there is no triable issue of material fact with respect to the application of Article XVI, Section 6 of California Constitution. The burden does not shift to the opposing party.

 

All Causes of Action – Education Code section 44808

 

LAUSD argues that Education Code section 44808 precludes liability because all alleged wrongdoing occurred off campus.

 

Education Code section 44808 states, “…[N]o school district…shall be responsible or in any way liable for the conduct or safety of any pupil of the public schools at any time when such pupil is not on school property, unless such district… has undertaken to provide transportation for such pupil to and from the school premises, has undertaken a school-sponsored activity off the premises of such school, has otherwise specifically assumed such responsibility or liability or has failed to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances.”

 

Here, LAUSD submits evidence that the underlying allegations of sexual abuse occurred off campus, and that no inappropriate interactions with Garcia occurred on campus. (UMF Nos. 34-38, 97-102.)  Therefore, LAUSD has made a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact with respect to the application of Education Code section 44808. 

 

The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to raise a triable issue of material fact.

 

Plaintiffs argue that Section 44808 does not immunize LAUSD from liability for off campus abuse because both Plaintiffs were abused by Garcia during school hours at least once, after Gage employees allowed Garcia to take them out of class and to his apartment where the abuse occurred. 

 

Plaintiffs present evidence that R. Z. was first abused by Garcia during his 6th period dance class, after Garcia told Mr. Barcelo and Ms. Cortes that he was going to take R.Z. off the school campus to Garcia’s apartment to “help” Garcia with moving costumes. At Garcia’s apartment, Garcia took R. Z.’s penis out of R.Z.’s pants, put it in his mouth, and began orally copulating R.Z. R.Z. became scared and left Garcia’s apartment. (AF No. 28.)  When G.Z. was in 8th grade during the 1985/1986 school year, Garcia took G. Z. out of his 4th period math class – with permission from G.Z.’s math teacher – and took G.Z. to his apartment. There, while G. Z. was on the phone with his girlfriend, Garcia took G.Z.’s penis out of his pants and began orally copulating him. (AF No. 31.)

 

Plaintiffs also argue that LAUSD was negligent in permitting Garcia to develop inappropriate relationships with Plaintiffs, groom them, and invite them over to his apartment.  Thus, LAUSD’s negligence can be traced back to its failure to properly supervise Plaintiffs and Garcia while they were on campus.  The fact that the sexual abuse culminated off campus does not immunize LAUSD from its negligence while Plaintiffs were on school premises. (Hoyem v. Manhattan Beach City Sch. Dist. (1978) 22 Cal.3d 508, 515-516.) Plaintiffs present evidence that Gage employees knew of should have known that Garcia was developing inappropriate relationships with his students and failed to act:

 

·       No Gage employee ever stopped the kids and asked questions about where they were going with Garcia. (AF No. 15),

·       Cortez (teacher’s assistant) and Barcelo (instructor) knew that Garcia had Folklorico students come over to hang at his apartment and spend the night. (AF No. 16)

·       Cortes was frequently present at Garcia’s apartment when he had students over, and sometimes even walked with students from school to the apartment. (AF No. 17).

·       Barcelo, and other employees, saw students walk with Garcia to his apartment. (AF No. 18)

·       Barcelo also overheard students remarking that they hung out at Garcia’s house. (AF No. 19.)

·       Everyone at school, including Cortes and Barcelo, knew that Bernie was living with Garcia. Cortes knew because she spent time at Garcia’s house with the students, and Barcelo knew because everyone spoke about it in front of him. (AF No. 22.)

·       Garcia frequently came to school and left with Bernie. (AF No. 23.)

·       No one from Gage or the District ever asked Bernie any questions about his living with Garcia. (AF No. 24.)

·       At one point, parents expressed concern that there was inadequate supervision over the Folklorico class. In response, Barcelo met with parents and students to reassure them that he “had everything under control” and that there was “proper supervision.” (AF No. 26.)

 

In reply, LAUSD acknowledges that under Education Code section 44808, LAUSD “could not be liable for injuries off campus and after school hours unless they were a result of the district’s’ negligence occurring on school grounds or were the result of some specific undertaking by the District, which was then performed in a negligent manner.” (Mosley v. San Bernardino City Unified School District (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1264 (italics added).)

 

The Court determines that Plaintiffs have raised a triable issue of material fact regarding LAUSD’s negligence on school grounds and the application of Education Code section 44808 to the circumstances of this case. 

 

Second Cause of Action

 

LAUSD also argues that Civil Code section 51.9 and 52 do not apply to it because Civil Code section 51.9 provides a cause of action against a “person,” and Civil Code section 52, subdivision (b) states, “Whoever denies the right provided by [Civil Code] Section 51.7 or 51.9, or aids, incites, or conspires in that denial, is liable for each and every offense for the actual damages suffered by any person denied that right,” as well as “exemplary damages,” “attorney’s fees as may be determined by the court,” and, for Civil Code section 51.7, a civil penalty.” LAUSD asserts that as a public school district, it is not a “person” under the statute and cites K.M. vs. Grossmont Union High School Dist. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 717, 751-752. Based on the foregoing, LAUSD has met its burden. The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs.

 

Plaintiffs state that they do not dispute LAUSD’s arguments.

 

Thus, the Court grants summary adjudication as to the second cause of action.

 

Third, Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action

 

As to the these causes of action, LAUSD argues that it is immune from liability based upon Education Code section 44808.  For the reasons discussed above, the Court determines that Plaintiffs have raised a triable issue of material fact with respect to the application of Education Code section 44808. 

 

Fourth Cause of Action

 

As to the fourth cause of action, LAUSD argues that the Child Abuse and Neglect Report Act was not in effect at the time of the alleged incidents. But it was originally enacted in 1980 as the Child Abuse Reporting Act, and it required a child care custodian who “has knowledge of or observes a child in his or her professional capacity or within the scope of his or her employment whom he or she reasonably suspects has been the victim of child abuse to report such suspected instance of child abuse to a child protective agency….” (Stats. 1980, ch. 1071, § 4.) “Child care custodian” includes a teacher, administrative officer, certificated pupil personnel employee of a public school headstart teacher, social worker, and probation officer. (Stats. 1980, ch. 1071, § 4.)

 

The Court determines that LAUSD has not met its burden to show that no triable issue of material fact exists. The burden does not shift to the opposing party.

 

Other Arguments

 

LAUSD also argues that there is not a causal nexus nor is it foreseeable that the dance teacher would commit sexual abuse. (Mot., pg. 25.) LAUSD further argues that the alleged misconduct occurred outside the scope of employment. (Mot, pgs. 25-27, 33.) But Plaintiffs do not allege that the District was vicariously liable for the sexual abuse by Garcia. Rather, Plaintiffs allege that the District is vicariously liable for the negligence of its employees, i.e. teachers and administrators, in failing to protect Plaintiff from foreseeable sexual abuse by an employee of the District – Garcia. (FAC, ¶¶ 37, 58.) The pleadings serve as the “outer measure of materiality” in a summary judgment motion, and the motion may not be granted or denied on issues not raised by the pleadings. (Laabs v. City of Victorville (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1258.)

 

Thus, the Court does not adjudicate these issues.

 

Accordingly, LAUSD’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.  LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to the second cause of action is GRANTED.  LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to the third, fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action is DENIED.

Defendant/Cross-Defendant SPAUDLING EQUIPMENT COMPANY, INC.’s unopposed Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED. CCP §877.6.

 

Moving Party to give Notice.

 

No Opposition(s) filed as of June 17, 2024.     

 

In determining the good faith of a settlement, the Court must balance the public policy favoring settlements with that favoring equitable sharing of costs among tortfeasors. To do so, the settlement must be within the reasonable range of the settling tortfeasor’s share of liability for the Plaintiff’s injuries, taking into consideration the facts and circumstances of the particular case. (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.) The Tech-Bilt court set forth the factors to be considered by the court in making the determination, such as: (1) the rough approximation of claimant’s total recovery and the settling parties’ proportional liability; (2) the amount paid in settlement; (3) the allocation of settlement proceeds among claimants; (4) recognition that a settling party should pay less in settlement than he would if he were liable after trial; (5) the settling party’s financial condition; (6) insurance policy limits of settling respondents; and (7) existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interest of non-settling parties. Furthermore, the practical considerations require that the evaluation be made on the basis of information available at the time of the settlement. (Id.) In sum, “a defendant’s settlement future must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant’s liability be.” (Id.) Thus, as long as settlement is not so far “out of the ballpark” in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of CCP §877.6, the settlement shall be determined as being made in good faith. (Id. 499-500.)

 

While a non-settling party bears the ultimate burden of showing that a settlement was not in good faith, the moving party must make a sufficient showing of all Tech-Bilt factors, either in the original moving papers, or in counter-declarations filed after opposition has been filed. (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 92 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1262.)

 

The parties have been apprised of the settlement terms, and the amount to be paid in the settlement (a mutual waiver of costs). In cases where liability is unlikely, like here, a settlement for fees/costs may be in good faith. (Armstrong World Industries, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 951.) Given the unopposed statements made in the Declaration of Matthew A. Gardner regarding the unlikeliness of Spaudling Equipment Company, Inc.’s liability, and the fact that the instant Motion is unopposed, the unopposed Motion for determination of good faith settlement is GRANTED. The legal standards and requirements of CCP §877.6 and of Tech-Bilt v. Woodward-Clyde & Assoc. (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488 are all met.

This partition action concerns real properly commonly known as 12618 Bailey Street, Whittier, California 90601 (“Subject Property”) In the Complaint filed on October 14, 2022, Plaintiff Mary Lou Kramer alleges that she and Defendant Ricardo Orosco Martinez, in their capacities as trustees of their respective trusts, each own a fifty percent undivided interest in the Subject Property. (¶7.) Defendant Marcus Martinez, the son of Defendant Ricardo Martinez, has been occupying the premises exclusively for at least the past twenty years, but has paid Plaintiff substantially less than one-half of the fair market rental value of the Subject Property. (¶8.)

 

On January 7, 2024, Defendant Ricardo Orosco Martinez (“Decedent”) passed way.

 

Doreen M. Esparza, the daughter of the Decedent, and Successor Trustee to the Martinez Family Revocable Living Trust Dated June 18, 2018, filed the instant application to substitute in as successor in interest to Decedent.

 

“A pending action or proceeding does not abate by the death of a party if the cause of action survives.” (CCP §377.21.) A cause of action against a decedent that survives may be asserted against the decedent’s personal representative or, to the extent provided by statute, against the decedent’s successor in interest. (CCP §377.40). After a defendant dies, the court shall allow the pending action to continue upon motion. (CCP §377.41.)

 

CCP §377.41 states: “On motion, the Court shall allow a pending action or proceeding against the decedent that does not abate to be continued against the decedent’s personal representative or, to the extent provided by statute, against the decedent’s successor in interest, except that the court may not permit an action or proceeding to be continued against the personal representative unless proof of compliance with part 4… of Division 7 of the Probate Code governing creditor claims is first made.”

 

“The person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest… shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury… stating all of the following: (1) The decedent’s name. (2) The date and place of the decedent’s death. (3) ‘No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate.’ (4) If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor in interest. (5) Either of the following… (A) ‘The affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest.. and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.’ (B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent’s successor in interest… with respect to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.’ (6) ‘No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.’ (7) ‘The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury… that the foregoing is true and correct.’”(CCP §377.32.)

 

The Court has reviewed the Declaration of Doreen M. Esparza. The Motion is DENIED without prejudice for failure to comply with the mandatory terms of CCP §377.32. Specifically, Ms. Esparza does not state that no proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the Decedent’s estate, and that no other person has a superior right to be substituted for the Decedent in the pending action or proceeding.

“To the extent authorized by this chapter governing any particular discovery method or any other provision of this title, the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose [sanctions] against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process …” (CCP §2023.030.) Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery are both misuses of the discovery process.” (CCP §§2023.010 (d) and (g).) Sanctions which may be imposed for a misuse of the discovery process include “terminating sanctions.” (CCP §2023.030, subd. (d).)

 

Plaintiffs Hae Won Lee and Bora Violet Park (“Plaintiffs”) seek an order to compel Defendant to comply with this Court’s January 24, 2024 Order, or alternatively, terminating sanctions on the basis that Defendant is refusing to participate in the discovery process. Specifically, Defendant has failed to comply with this Court’s June 23, 2023 CMC Order, and this Court’s January 24, 2024 Order ordering compliance with the June 23, 2023 CMC Order.

 

It is a commonly stated axiom that discovery sanctions “should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793.) However, a court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with discovery obligations. The refusal to reveal material evidence is deemed to be an admission that the claim or defense is without merit. (Id. at 794-795.)

 

Where no Opposition to the instant Motion has been filed as of June 18, 2024, it is undisputed that Defendant is unwilling to comply with their discovery obligations.

 

The Court finds that terminating sanctions are warranted here. The information sought by Plaintiff is important to this case. Defendant’s refusal to participate in this litigation supports the conclusion that lesser sanctions would not be appropriate.

 

The motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED as indicated above. Defendant’s Answer is STRICKEN. Further monetary sanctions are DENIED as unnecessarily punitive.  Evidentiary and Issue Sanctions are rendered MOOT.

 


Case Number: 23NWCV00087    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: C

CUELLAR v. YORK ENTERPRISES SOUTH, INC.

CASE NO.:  23NWCV00087

HEARING:  06/20/24

#6

 

Defendant Ford Motor Company’s Motion for Protective Order is GRANTED with the limitations set forth below.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Defendant Ford Motor Company (Defendant) moves for entry of a protective order.

Background

On January 10, 2023, Plaintiffs Mariano Cuellar and Martin Cuellar (Plaintiffs) filed a Complaint against Defendants York Enterprises South, Inc.; and Ford Motor Company for violations of the Song-Beverly Act and Negligent Repair. The parties have met and conferred regarding entering into a protective order and have agreed upon using the Los Angeles Superior Court Model Order (LASC Order) as the template for the protective order.

Legal Standard

“When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (CCP § 2031.060(a).)

“The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may include… the following directions…that a trade secret or other confidential research, development, or commercial information not be disclosed, or be disclosed only to specified persons or only in a specified way.” (CCP § 2031.060(b)(5).)

Discussion

Defendant seeks to make three changes to the LASC Order. Its Proposed Order modifies Paragraph 7(b) to define affiliated attorneys as attorneys of the same firm, require that all personnel at Plaintiffs’ counsel’s office sign the Proposed Order, revise subsection (d) to include videographers and litigation support companies, remove subsection (f) which allows disclose to mock jurors, and revise subsection (g) to include non-attorney experts. The Proposed Order also modifies Paragraph 8 to prohibit posting confidential documents to any website or advertising them for sale. Finally, the Proposed Order modifies Paragraph 21 to require that Plaintiffs’ counsel return or destroy all confidential documents when the case concludes.

With respect to Paragraph 7, Plaintiff argues that defining affiliated attorneys as attorneys of the same firm is too narrow and would prejudice Plaintiffs because their counsel would not be able to share the documents with other attorneys serving as co-counsel or contracted in, such as trial counsel. The Court finds that the Proposed Order should not be read as prohibiting outside counsel from being able to review the confidential material, however, that outside counsel must first sign the Proposed Order prior to being given access. Subject to this limitation, Defendant’s request to define affiliated attorneys as attorneys of the same firm is GRANTED.

Defendant argues that requiring all non-attorney personnel at Plaintiffs’ counsel’s office to sign the Proposed Order is necessary because it would be as easy to sign the Proposed Order as giving the oral affirmation to be bound by it. However, the Court finds that requiring plaintiff’s counsel in lemon law cases such as these to continually distribute protective orders, require all employees sign the orders, collect and retain all signed orders, and ensure compliance with the requests would be an undue burden. Thus, Defendant’s request to require all personnel sign the Proposed Order is DENIED.

Defendant argues that removing subsection (g) which allows disclosure to mock jurors is necessary because Defendant is not able to identify and ensure compliance with the Proposed Order. It asserts that it cannot confirm the contact information of mock jurors. However, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have a greater interest in preparing for trial in a manner most advantageous to their case and Defendant should not be given the means to restrict Plaintiffs’ counsel’s trial preparation. Thus, Defendant’s request to remove subsection (g) is DENIED.

Plaintiff has not opposed the other modifications to Paragraph 7. Thus, Defendant’s request to modify subsections (d) and (g) are GRANTED.

Defendant argues that limiting Paragraph 8 to prohibit posting confidential documents to any website or advertising them for sale is necessary to clarify the existing language in the Paragraph. However, Paragraph 8 of the LASC Order limits the use of confidential information to “the purposes of preparing for, conducting, participating in the conduct of, and/or prosecuting and/or defending the Proceeding, and not for any business or other purpose whatsoever.” Thus, the LASC Order’s language already prohibits the posting of confidential documents to any website or advertising them for sale. Therefore, Defendant’s request to add limitations to Paragraph 8 is DENIED.

Defendant argues that modifying Paragraph 21 to require return or destruction of confidential documents is necessary to ensure the long-term protection of its confidential documents. However, Defendant has not provided a compelling reason to require return or destruction of the documents in this case and prohibiting Plaintiffs’ counsel from retaining a copy for their file. Plaintiffs’ counsel has an interest maintaining a complete file for its cases and those documents would be subject to the continuing protection of the Proposed Order. Thus, Defendant’s request to modify Paragraph 21 is DENIED.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order is GRANTED with the above limitations.

Case Number: 23NWCV04206    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: C

BAEK v. TISHBI

CASE NO.:  23NWCV04206

HEARING:  06/20/24

 

#13

 

Defendant Dietz International Public Adjusters of California’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED with 30 days leave to amend.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

This fraud action was filed by Plaintiff SEUNG JAE BAEK (“Baek”) on December 29, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following relevant facts: “On or about 07/18/2022, BAEK and other lots of persons and entities were sued by ELLIOT... (Case No. 22 STCV 03159), where BAEK was served on or about 01/25/2023. [¶] In said case, ELLIOT… argued that BAEK fraudulently transferred inventories…using false invoices prior to the fire (i.e. Arson). [¶] Thereafter, BAEK had been reasonably and diligently investigating said factual argument of above case, since 03/02/2023, finding ELLIOT’s intent: 1) to cover up his planned fire and 2) to void, excuse and release his duty and/or liability to pay to the US. Truste in the course of Bankruptcy proceeding. [¶]… ELLIOTT and his Co-conspirator JEFF deliberately and fraudulent falsified (faked) material facts of BAEK’s business invoices (i.e., Day Trippin). [¶] On 7/5/2019, ELLIOTT and STEPHEN in recognition of Falsified (faked) Invoices… brought to the Insurance Claim Broker, DIETZ. [¶]…Insurance claim broker (i.e. Dietz International Public Adjusters of California) designed and encouraged this falsification of invoices, so as to maximize claim amount.” (Complaint ¶¶10-15.) “ELLIOT and JEFF changed, altered and modified material facts of BAEK’s invoices, exactly matching to the material terms of Insurance policy Declaration in that: 1) billing address of faked invoices to the same site of fire as insurance policy indicating at 1901 E. 55th St., Vernon CA 90058; and 2) Numbers of Item shipped and their Prices to Increase of Inventories value burned and scored.” (Complaint ¶16.) Thereafter, more than $4,200,000.00 of the claim amount was determined to pay upon Dietz’s brokerage, where $163,936.20 was paid to Dietz International. (Complaint ¶17.) “[I]t was clear that BAEK was a ‘Scapegoat,’ maliciously and deliberately being used by ELLIOT, who wanted to cover up his planned ARSON.” (Complaint ¶19.)


Plaintiff’s Complaint asserts the following causes of action:

 

(1) Fraudulent Misrepresentation;

(2) Falsification of Documents (Negligence Per Se); and

(3) Negligence

 

The Court notes that as of June 14, 2024, this action has not been deemed related to any other pending civil action throughout the Los Angeles Superior Court, and no Notice of Related case has been filed in this action.

 

Defendant Dietz International Public Adjusters of California (“Dietz”) specially and generally demurs to the first and second causes of action.

 

Uncertainty

 

Dietz argues that the first and second causes of action are fatally uncertain. This argument lacks merit because “[a] special demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach the failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed at the uncertainty existing in the allegations actually made.” (Butler v. Sequeira (1950) 100 Cal.App.2d 143, 145-146.) Moreover, demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond, i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif. Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Ibid.) Here, it is clear from Dietz’s other arguments that it understands what the Complaint at least attempts to allege, and there is no true uncertainty. The Demurrer is not sustained on the basis of uncertainty.

 

First Cause of Action – Fraudulent Misrepresentation

 

The elements of a fraudulent misrepresentation cause of action are: (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or “scienter”); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Engalia v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.)

 

“Fraud must be pleaded with specificity… [t]o withstand a demurrer, the facts constituting every element of the fraud must be alleged with particularity, and the claim cannot be salvaged by references to the general policy favoring the liberal construction of pleadings. (Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’” (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.)

 

This lawsuit arises out of an insurance fraud scheme.  Defendant Elliot Tishibi (“Tishibi”) confided in Baek a plan to set fire to their business to collect insurance proceeds.  Baek agreed to keep the scheme a secret, but eventually disclosed the scheme after Tishibi sued Baek for fraudulent transfer.

 

Dietz argues that whatever the dispute between Baek and Tishibi, the Complaint does not state a cause of action against Dietz. 

 

The Complaint alleges that Tishibi and Defendant Stephen Jung brought falsified invoices to Dietz, their insurance claim broker. (¶14.) Dietz “designed and encouraged this falsification of invoices” so as to maximize the claim amount. (¶15.) Dietz was paid $163,936.20 from the proceeds of the claim. (¶17.)

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED with 30 days leave to amend. As alleged, the Complaint does not allege any facts tending to show how or when Dietz “designed and encouraged” false invoices, how Dietz knew the invoices were false, how Baek relied upon the false invoices, and what damages Baek has suffered (if any). The only damages alleged appear to be for the recovery of damages claimed in the other unrelated civil action. (see Complaint, Prayer ¶3.)

 

Second Cause of Action – Falsification of Documents (Negligence Per Se)

 

As argued in the instant Demurrer, negligence per se is not a separate and independent cause of action. (Ev. Code §669; Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256.) Negligence per se is not a separate cause of action, as it is rooted in the underlying theory of negligence. (Quiroz at 1256.) While some cases have allowed negligence per se to be alleged, even where a claim for general negligence is barred, those cases involve unique circumstances not presented here. (Capri v. L.A. Fitness Int’l, LLC (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1078.)

 

In light of the liberality associated with granting leave to amend, and to the extent that Plaintiff is able to do so, leave to amend is granted. The demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED with 30 days leave to amend.
