The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Case Number: 21STCV43583    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: G

Defendant Claremont Unified School District’s Motion for Summary Judgment, and in the Alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication of Issues

Respondent: Plaintiff John SJ Doe

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant Claremont Unified School District’s Motion for Summary Judgment, and in the Alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication of Issues is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from the sexual abuse of a high school student by school staff. Defendant Brandy Wilborn was a teacher’s aide employed by Defendant Claremont Unified School District (CUSD) at Claremont High School. Plaintiff John SJ Doe was a student at Claremont High School. From December 2014 to May 2015, Wilborn sexually assaulted, abused, molested, and harassed Doe. Wilborn was then arrested in 2015 and pled no contest to unlawful sexual intercourse with a minor.

On November 29, 2021, Doe filed a complaint for damages against CUSD, Wilborn, and Does 1 through 50, alleging the following causes of action: (1) intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED) against all defendants; (2) negligence against CUSD and Does 1-50; (3) negligent supervision against CUSD and Does 1-50; (4) negligent retention/hiring against CUSD and Does 1-50; (5) negligent failure to train, warn, or educate against CUSD and Does 1-50; (6) breach of fiduciary duty against all defendants; (7) constructive fraud pursuant to Civil Code, section 1573 against all defendants; (8) sexual harassment pursuant to Civil Code, section 51.9 against all defendants; (9) sexual harassment and abuse in the educational setting pursuant to Education Code, section 220 against CUSD and Does 1-50; (10) sexual battery against Wilborn; (11) gender violence pursuant to Civil Code, section 52.4 against Wilborn; and (12) violation of Penal Code, section 647.6, subdivision (a)(1) against Wilborn. On June 17, 2022, the court sustained CUSD’s demurrer to the Complaint.

On September 6, 2022, Doe filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same defendants alleging the same causes of action. On February 9, 2023, the court sustained CUSD’s demurrer to the FAC.

On March 1, 2023, Doe filed a Second Amended Complaint (SAC) against the same defendants alleging the same causes of action. On July 24, 2023, the court sustained and overruled CUSD’s demurrer to the SAC in part without leave to amend.

On June 11, 2024, CUSD filed the present motion. On October 4, 2024, the court continued the hearing on CUSD’s motion for CUSD to submit a supplemental separate statement.

A hearing on the present motion is set for November 26, 2024, along with a post-mediation status conference/trial setting conference.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Doe’s evidentiary objections are OVERRULED as to Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 22, 23, 24, 25, 26, 27, and 28 and SUSTAINED as to Nos. 8, 11, and 21.

ANALYSIS

CUSD moves for summary judgment or adjudication of Doe’s negligence causes of action on the grounds that Doe cannot establish the essential elements of duty and causation. CUSD also moves for summary judgment or adjudication of Doe’s first cause of action for IIED and ninth cause of action for sexual harassment and abuse in the educational environment.

For the following reasons, the court DENIES CUSD’s motion.

Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

Negligence Causes of Action

CUSD argues Doe’s negligence causes of action fail because Doe cannot establish the essential elements of duty and causation. The court disagrees.

Duty

The existence of a legal duty is an essential requirement for any cause of action based on negligence. (Romero v. Superior Court (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078 [“Absent a legal duty, any injury is an injury without actionable wrong.”].) “And, while questions concerning whether a duty has been breached and whether that breach caused a plaintiff’s injury may be questions of fact for a jury, the existence of the duty in the first place is a question of law for the court.” (Margaret W. v. Kelley R. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.) Because it is a question of law, the issue of duty “is particularly amenable to resolution by summary judgment.” (Parsons v. Crown Disposal Co. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 456, 465.)

Doe’s negligence causes of action are based on the allegation that CUSD owed Doe a “special, trusting, and confidential duty of care” that arose from their special relationship. (SAC, ¶ 7, 52.) The SAC generally alleges CUSD owed Doe a duty to protect minor children in their care through adequate supervision (SAC, ¶ 39); to refrain from violating Doe’s rights to protection from bodily restraint or harm, personal insult, defamation, and injury to personal relations in compliance with Civil Code section 43 (SAC, ¶ 39); to abstain from injuring Doe’s property or person or infringing on Doe’s rights pursuant to Civil Code section 1708 (SAC, ¶ 39); and to disclose Wilborn’s unfitness and misconduct (SAC, ¶ 40, 42). The second cause of action for negligence incorporates these allegations (SAC, ¶ 63) and alleges that CUSD owed Doe a duty to report known or suspected incidents of child assault and sexual abuse pursuant to the Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act or CANRA (SAC, ¶ 39, 66). The third cause of action for negligent supervision alleges CUSD owed Doe a duty to adequately supervise Wilborn. (SAC, ¶ 77.) The fourth cause of action for negligent retention or hiring alleges CUSD owed Doe a duty to not hire or retain Wilborn considering Wilborn’s alleged dangerous and exploitative propensities. (SAC, ¶ 90.) Last, the fifth cause of action for negligent failure to train, warn, or educate alleges CUSD owed Doe a duty to warn, train, or educate Doe about the risks of sexual assault, harassment, and molestation. (SAC, ¶ 101.)

In moving for summary judgment or adjudication, CUSD makes the following arguments. First, CUSD argues that any alleged special relationship or duty owed by CUSD is foreclosed by the immunity provisions of Education Code section 44808. Second, CUSD argues the duty allegations fail because CUSD did not have actual or constructive notice of Wilborn’s unfitness or potential for misconduct. Third, CUSD argues the duty allegations fail because Wilborn’s misconduct was not reasonably foreseeable. And finally, CUSD argues the CANRA duty allegations fail because CUSD did not have actual knowledge of Wilborn’s abuse.

The court addresses each argument is addressed below.

School District Immunity

Education Code section 44808 states as follows:

“Notwithstanding any other provision of this code, no school district, city or county board of education, county superintendent of schools, or any officer or employee of such district or board shall be responsible or in any way liable for the conduct or safety of any pupil of the public schools at any time when such pupil is not on school property, unless such district, board, or person has undertaken to provide transportation for such pupil to and from the school premises, has undertaken a school-sponsored activity off the premises of such school, has otherwise specifically assumed such responsibility or liability or has failed to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances.

In the event of such a specific undertaking, the district, board, or person shall be liable or responsible for the conduct or safety of any pupil only while such pupil is or should be under the immediate and direct supervision of an employee of such district or board.” (Educ. Code, § 44808.)

CUSD argues Education Code section 44808 applies to the present action as Wilborn’s alleged sexual abuse of Doe occurred off school property. (MSJ, p. 19:22-20:8.) But, as demonstrated below, that is not what the undisputed material facts establish. In the SAC, the only acts of sexual abuse or inappropriate conduct alleged to have occurred on school property are that Wilborn flirted with Doe by engaging in conversations outside of class (SAC, ¶ 16) and touched Doe’s thighs and back in the classroom (SAC, ¶ 15, 29(a)). While the SAC also alleges Wilborn kissed Doe (SAC, ¶ 29(a)), touched Doe’s genitals (SAC, ¶ 29(a)), sent Doe photographs of Wilborn’s breasts and vagina (SAC, ¶ 29(a)), and engaged in sexual intercourse with Doe (SAC, ¶ 29(a), these allegations fail to specifically allege where the instances of abuse took place.

CUSD’s separate statement states it is undisputed that Wilborn claims Wilborn did not have any inappropriate interactions with Doe on campus. (Defendant’s Supplemental Separate Statement (DSSS), ¶ 25.) CUSD also states it is undisputed that Wilborn claims Wilborn did not engage in any fondling or touching while in the classroom with Doe. (DSSS, ¶ 29.) But what Wilborn claims is merely evidence and itself is not a material fact. (Reeves v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 95, 105-106 [noting what a party perceived or said in a deposition itself is not a material fact but rather possible evidence of such a fact].) As a result, the court finds these purported material facts are insufficient.

CUSD’s separate statement also describes multiple occasions where Wilborn had sexual contact with Doe off campus. (DSSS, ¶ 12, 15-16, 19, 27, 28.) But it does not include any material facts that establish these were the only times Wilborn had sexual contact with Doe. Absent such material facts, CUSD failed to carry the initial burden of establishing it is undisputed that all acts of alleged sexual abuse occurred off campus. Thus, the court finds CUSD has not demonstrated Doe’s causes of action for negligence are barred by Education Code section 44808.

Existence of Duty

“California law has long imposed on school authorities a duty to supervise at all times the conduct of the children on the school grounds and to enforce those rules and regulations necessary to their protection.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 869 (C.A.), quoting Dailey v. Los Angeles Unified Sch. Dist. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 741, 747.) Courts also consider school employees to have a special relationship with their students which imposes “the duty to use reasonable measures to protect students from foreseeable injury at the hands of third parties acting negligently or intentionally.” (Id., at p. 869-870.) As part of this duty, if school employees responsible for hiring or supervising teachers “knew or should have known of [teacher’s] prior sexual misconduct toward students, and thus, that he posed a reasonably foreseeable risk of harm to students under his supervision . . . , the employees owed a duty to protect the students from such harm.” (Virginia G. v. ABC Unified School Dist. (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1855.) Furthermore, school employees are mandatory reporters who must report if they have knowledge of or reasonably suspect child abuse pursuant to CANRA. (Pen. Code, § 11166, subd. (a).)

CUSD first argues they owed no duty to Doe because they lacked actual or constructive notice of any potential misconduct by Wilborn or Wilborn’s alleged unfitness as an employee. (MSJ, p. 13:3-28, 15:6-16:7.) As to negligent supervision, however, this is not the standard. In M.W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 508 (M.W.), the court explicitly rejected this actual knowledge limitation on a school district’s duty to supervise their students. (Id., at p. 521-522, 524.) Instead, “[t]he existence of a duty of care of a school district toward a student depends, in part, on whether the particular harm to the student is reasonably foreseeable.” (Id., at p. 518.) As to negligent hiring or retention of school staff, however, CUSD’s duty does depend on whether “the individual alleged to be negligent in a hiring or retention decision knew or should have known of the dangerous propensities of the employee who injured the plaintiff.” (C.A., supra, 53 Cal.4th at p. 878.) CUSD’s duty also depends on whether “the employee’s propensities posed a substantial risk of personal injury to the plaintiff or others in the same circumstances.” (Ibid.)

The SAC alleges CUSD knew or should have known that Wilborn had dangerous and exploitative propensities when they hired Wilborn. (SAC, ¶ 90.) While the SAC does not allege which specific facts should have put CUSD on notice, it suggests such facts would have been revealed by conducting a meaningful and adequate investigation of Wilborn’s background. (SAC, ¶ 90.) The SAC also alleges that during Wilborn’s period of employment and prior to Wilborn’s sexual abuse of Doe, other students or employees of CUSD complained of Wilborn’s sexual improprieties. (SAC, ¶ 94.) As a result, the SAC alleges CUSD was put on notice that Wilborn had previously engaged in and continued to engage in unlawful sexual conduct. (SAC, ¶ 95.)

While CUSD’s separate statement provides material facts addressing the second allegation about Wilborn’s tenure at CUSD, the separate statement fails to address the first allegation that CUSD knew or should have known of Wilborn’s dangerous and exploitative propensities when they first hired Wilborn. (DSSS, p. 17:1-24:17.) By failing to address all material allegations, CUSD cannot establish it is undisputed that CUSD lacked actual or constructive notice of Wilborn’s alleged unfitness when they hired Wilborn. (See Teselle v. McLoughlin¿(2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 171 (Teselle) [failure to address a material allegation in the pleadings by a moving party defendant is fatal to a motion for summary judgment or adjudication on that cause of action or issue].)

As to the issue of foreseeability, CUSD argues the foreseeable risk of harm to Doe was nonexistent. (MSJ, p. 14:10-15:5.) “Foreseeability is determined in light of all the circumstances and does not require prior identical events or injuries.” (M.W., supra, 110 Cal.App.4th at p. 519.) It is an objective standard that looks at whether the negligent conduct or absence of safeguards created a foreseeable risk of a particular type of harm. (Ibid; see also Taylor v. Oakland Scavenger Co. (1941) 17 Cal.2d 594, 600.) The SAC alleges Wilborn would openly touch Doe on the shoulder and rub Doe’s back and thighs in the presence of Brenda Tilford, Doe’s assigned math teacher at Claremont High School. (SAC, ¶ 15.) The SAC alleges Wilborn would flirt or converse with Doe and other students in the hallways and quads of Claremont High School when Wilborn’s role consisted of one-on-one contact with one or two special education students and did not permit fraternization or socialization with other students. (SAC, ¶ 16.) The SAC also alleges that Doe and Wilborn left the school campus together in front of teachers, administrators, and campus monitors. (SAC, ¶ 16.) Last, the SAC alleges CUSD received a report from another parent in April 2015 that claimed Wilborn was in a sexual relationship with Doe. (SAC, ¶ 18.)

In addressing the first allegation, CUSD’s separate statement states it is undisputed that no one observed Wilborn rub Doe’s back or thighs in class. (DSSS, ¶ 9, 11.) The court finds, however, that is not what the evidence establishes. The only supporting citations provided by CUSD reference portions of Doe’s deposition transcript where Doe describes how in class Wilborn would touch Doe’s back or rub Doe’s thighs with Wilborn’s feet when they sat across from each other. (MSJ, Ex. B, p. 57:10-58:18, 62:20-24.) Nowhere in the cited portions of the deposition transcript does Doe state that no one observed Wilborn touching Doe. Next, CUSD’s separate statement provides it is undisputed that Wilborn did not have any inappropriate interactions with Doe on campus and did not touch or fondle Doe in the classroom. (DSSS, ¶ 25, 29.) But while these material facts are supported by citations to Wilborn’s deposition testimony, they omit citations to previously cited deposition testimony from Doe where Doe has described Wilborn touching Doe in class.

The fact that Doe and Wilborn present conflicting accounts of what occurred in the classroom is a triable issue of material fact that precludes the grant of summary judgment or adjudication as to the causes of action for negligence and negligent supervision. Furthermore, even if CUSD had established that no one observed Wilborn rubbing Doe’s back and thighs, the failure of CUSD’s staff to notice and report such behavior created a foreseeable risk that Doe would be subject to further sexual abuse by Wilborn.

Because CUSD is not entitled to summary judgment or adjudication on the issue of whether CUSD owed a duty to properly supervise Doe, the court need not address whether CUSD is entitled to summary judgment or adjudication on whether CUSD also violated duties imposed by CANRA as these allegations are part of the same cause of action for negligence. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1) [“A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”].)

Causation

“An essential element of negligence is that the tortfeasor’s breach caused the injury protected against.” (Sutter Health v. Superior Court (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 1546, 1558.) Unlike determinations of duty, causation is generally a question of fact for the jury. (Hernandez v. Jensen (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 1056, 1064.) The court may only decide causation as a matter of law where there is no reasonable dispute. (Constance B. v. State of California (1986) 178 Cal.App.3d 200, 207.)

CUSD argues they are entitled to summary judgment or adjudication because Doe has no facts from which Doe can draw the legitimate inference that CUSD’s alleged negligence was the cause of Doe’s injury. (MSJ, p. 19:5-9.) But as noted in the previous section, a triable issue of fact does exist with regards to whether CUSD’s staff exercised due care in supervising Doe when Wilborn was touching Doe’s back and thigh in class. Because there can be a reasonable dispute over whether CUSD’s alleged failure to properly supervise Doe in class led to Wilborn sexually abusing Doe, the court  finds CUSD is not entitled to summary judgment or adjudication of the issue of causation.

IIED (First Cause of Action)

CUSD contends Doe’s first cause of action for IIED fails because CUSD cannot be held vicariously liable for Wilborn’s conduct and Doe has admitted CUSD lacked notice of Wilborn’s misconduct. (MSJ, p. 17:14-16.) The court disagrees.

As to the first contention, Doe is not seeking to hold CUSD vicariously liable for Wilborn’s sexual abuse of Doe. Rather, Doe is seeking to hold CUSD vicariously liable for their reckless failure to properly supervise Doe and Wilborn. (SAC, ¶ 58-59.) Thus, this contention is without any basis. And, to the extent CUSD claims Doe has admitted CUSD lacked notice of Wilborn’s misconduct, CUSD’s cursory argument fails to include any citation that supports this claim.

Accordingly, CUSD’s motion for summary judgment or adjudication fails as to this cause of action.

Sexual Harassment and Abuse in the Education Setting (Ninth Cause of Action)

CUSD maintains Doe’s ninth cause of action for sexual harassment and abuse in the education setting fails because CUSD did not have actual notice of Wilborn’s misconduct. The court disagrees.

Legal Standard

Education Code, section 220 prohibits “discrimination on the basis of disability, gender, gender identity, gender expression, nationality, race or ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, or any other characteristic that is contained in the definition of hate crimes set forth in Section 422.55 of the Penal Code, including immigration status, in any program or activity conducted by an educational institution that receives, or benefits from, state financial assistance, or enrolls pupils who receive state student financial aid.” A school is liable for violations of Section 220 when the school has actual notice of the violation and liability is not imposed through principles of respondeat superior or constructive notice. (Donovan v. Poway Unified School Dist. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 567, 605 (Donovan).) Actual notice is required to “ensure liability for money damages under section 220 is based on a funding recipient’s own misconduct, determined by its own deliberate indifference to known acts of harassment.” (Ibid.)

Discussion

The SAC alleges CUSD had actual notice based upon the allegations that Wilborn engaged in grooming behaviors in front of CUSD’s administrators and staff. (SAC, ¶ 14, 152.) Specifically, the SAC alleges Wilborn would openly touch Doe on the shoulder and rub Doe’s back and thighs in the presence of Brenda Tilford, Doe’s assigned math teacher at Claremont High School. (SAC, ¶ 15.) The SAC alleges Wilborn would flirt or converse with Doe and other students in the hallways and quads of Claremont High School when Wilborn’s role consisted of one-on-one contact with one or two special education students and did not permit fraternization or socialization with other students. (SAC, ¶ 16.) The SAC also alleges that Doe and Wilborn left the school campus together in front of teachers, administrators, and campus monitors. (SAC, ¶ 16.) Last, the SAC alleges CUSD received a report from another parent in April 2015 that claimed Wilborn was in a sexual relationship with Doe. (SAC, ¶ 18.)

As noted above in the section discussing foreseeability, a triable issue of material facts exists with regard to whether Wilborn was touching Doe’s back and thighs in class. A triable issue of fact also exists with regard to whether Doe’s math teacher observed Wilborn’s inappropriate behavior. Furthermore, while CUSD’s discussion of this issue provides two paragraphs of legal standards and case law, it is completely devoid of any actual analysis that applies the law to the facts of this case.

Accordingly, CUSD’s motion for summary judgment or adjudication fails as to this cause of action.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, CUSD’s motion for summary judgment or adjudication is DENIED.

Case Number: 24PSCV00713    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: G

Plaintiff Yonghong Li’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Yonghong Li’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This is a wrongful death action arising from the death of a tour bus operator. In September 2023, Ziheng Li was operating a tour bus for Defendants Gary Meng and GM Coach Inc. (GM Coach) in the Yellowstone National Park when Ziheng Li suffered a fatal cerebral hemorrhage. Plaintiff Yonghong Li, Ziheng Li’s mother, alleges Meng was overworking Ziheng Li by requiring Ziheng Li to drive a bus from Los Angeles to the Yellowstone National Park while GM Coach’s other drivers were allowed to travel by plane. At the time, Yonghong Li alleges Meng was aware of Ziheng Li’s poor health condition.

On March 11, 2024, Yonghong Li filed a complaint against Meng, GM Coach, and Does 1-50, alleging the following causes of action: (1) negligence, (2) negligence per se, (3) wrongful death, and (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED). On July 29, 2024, the court sustained Meng and GM Coach’s demurrer to the Complaint with leave to amend.

On August 19, 2024, Yonghong Li filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same defendants alleging the same causes of action. On October 14, 2024, the court sustained Meng and GM Coach’s demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend as to the third cause of action and with leave to amend as to the remaining causes of action.

On November 1, 2024, Yonghong Li filed the present motion. A hearing on the present motion is set for November 26, 2024, along with a case management conference on January 14, 2025.

ANALYSIS

Yonghong Li seeks leave to file a Second Amended Complaint (SAC) that alleges additional causes of action for wrongful death and negligent hiring, supervision, and retention. For the following reasons, the court DENIES Yonghong Li’s motion.

Legal Standard

“A court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) The court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendments of the pleadings. (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.) “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)  The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)

If the party seeking the amendment has needlessly delayed, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.  (See Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.  (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)

Discussion

In this case, Yonghong Li’s counsel provides the effect of the proposed SAC is to include two “new causes of action” for wrongful death and negligent hiring, supervision, and retention. (Deng Decl., p. 2:18-21.) But as to the cause of action for wrongful death, this is not a new cause of action. While Yonghong Li previously alleged this cause of action in the FAC, the court sustained a demurrer to this cause of action without leave to amend. (10/14/2024 Ruling, p. 3.) The court’s reason for doing so was the exclusivity of California’s workers compensation scheme. (10/14/2024 Ruling , p. 3.) The court also noted that Yonghong Li failed to include a timely opposition to the demurrer that addressed this issue. (10/14/2024 Ruling , p. 3.) Yonghong Li is now attempting to reassert the identical cause of action which the court has barred.

While the proposed SAC provides additional allegations to support Yonghong Li’s standing, neither the proposed SAC nor the present motion provide any grounds for the court to reverse its prior finding that this cause of action is barred as a matter of law. By failing to address the grounds upon which the court previously denied leave to amend, the court finds that Yonghong Li failed to demonstrate how this is a proper amendment.

As to the new cause of action for negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, the court notes this cause of action contains the same defects noted in the court’s previous ruling on Meng and GM Coach’s demurrer to the FAC. In previously sustaining the demurrer to the negligence causes of action, the court found the FAC failed to allege how Meng and GM Coach owed Yonghong Li a duty. (10/14/2024 Ruling , p. 2-3.) Similarly, here, this new cause of action fails to allege how Meng and GM Coach owed a duty to Yonghong Li with regards to the hiring, supervision, and retention of Ziheng Li. Thus, the court also finds that Yonghong Li failed to demonstrate how this cause of action constitutes a proper amendment.

Accordingly, the court DENIES Yonghong Li’s motion.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Yonghong Li’s motion for leave to file a SAC is DENIED.

Case Number: 24PSCV03869    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: G

Blue Wave Properties, LLC v. Reusch  (24PSCV03869)

November 26, 2024

Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application for a Temporary Restraining Order and OSC re: Preliminary Injunction Preventing Tenant From Using Property For Other Than Its Intended Purpose

RULING:

The court read and considered the moving papers filed by Plaintiff Blue Wave Properties, LLC (Blue Wave), to include the declarations of Ernie Park, Esq., property manager Kara Wolff, and private investigator Ron Echavarria.  The court did not receive an opposition Defendant Eric Reusch (Reusch). 

Plaintiff requests a Temporary Restraining Order and the setting of an OSC re: Preliminary Injunction so that Defendant Reusch operates the business property located at 2043 South Hacienda Blvd., Hacienda Heights, CA, for no other purpose than the stated purpose as a laundromat.  Plaintiff seeks a TRO that prohibits Reusch from allowing acts at the subject premises that would constitute a nuisance to other business owners, including prostitution, illegal drug sales, and illegal gambling.  Plaintiff presented the declarations of Ms. Wolff and Mr. Echavarria as the basis establishing these acts are occurring at the premises unchecked by Reusch.  

Pursuant to Rule 3.1202, subdivision (c) of the California Rules of Court, an application for ex parte relief "must make an affirmative factual showing in a declaration containing competent testimony based on personal knowledge of irreparable harm, immediate danger, or any other statutory basis for granting relief ex parte.”  The court finds that the declarations of Ms. Wolff and Mr. Echavarria submitted by Plaintiff in support of the ex parte application satisfy this requirement.  Specifically, the declarations states that the Property is a “shopping center . . . with sixteen tenants,” that Defendant Reusch assumed the lease obligations of the subject premises, and that Defendant Reusch’s failure to curb illegal activity at the premises has “fostered a hub for criminal activity. . . .”  As such, the court finds exigent circumstances to support Plaintiff’s request for ex parte relief.

Accordingly ex parte application for a Temporary Restraining Order is GRANTED.   

The court sets an OSC re: Preliminary Injunction for December 6, 8:30 a.m., Dept. G.  As for jurisdiction over Defendant Eric Reusch, the court notes that Plaintiff has not filed a proof of service of the Summons and Complaint as to Defendant.  Therefore, Plaintiff is ordered to serve the OSC re: Preliminary Injunction on Defendant not later than November 30, 2024, in a manner compliant with California Rules of Court 3.1150(a).  Failure to do so will result in denial of Plaintiff’s Preliminary Injunction as the court will not have proper jurisdiction over Defendant. 

The court sets the following briefing schedule:

            Opposition to the OSC re: Preliminary Injunction:     December 4, 2024, by 4:00 pm

            Reply by Plaintiff:                                                       December 5, 2024, by 4:00 pm
