DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV03239    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

LUIS CERDA,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03239

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

SOFI STADIUM, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          June 6, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Pincay RE, LLC and Stadco LA, LLC (erroneously sued and served as Sofi Stadium)

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Luis Cerda

Demurrer to Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and fourth causes of action in the complaint.  Moving defendants are ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

            On October 2, 2023, plaintiff Luis Cerda filed a complaint against Sofi Stadium, City of Inglewood, County of Los Angeles, State of California, California Department of Transportation, Pincay RE, LLC, Blue and God Tenant LLC, Stadco LA, LLC, John Doe 1, John Doe 2, and John Doe 3 for (1) battery, (2) negligence, (3) negligent hiring, training, and supervision, and (4) IIED.

            On February 27, 2023, plaintiff filed a requests for dismissal as to California Department of Transportation and State of California.

            On March 21, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to Blue and Gold Tenant LLC.

            On April 2, 2024, plaintiff filed requests for dismissal as to City of Inglewood and County of Los Angeles.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendants Pincay RE, LLC and Stadco LA, LLC demur to the first cause of action for battery and fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the ground that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The complaint alleges that it concerns a security officer, security guard and/or deputy incident which occurred on October 16, 2022, at or near 1001 Stadium Drive, Inglewood.  Plaintiff was attending a function at the subject premises when he was, suddenly and without warning, physically attacked and assaulted by defendants John Doe 1, 2, and 3 with such force that plaintiff suffered injuries and damages.  Complaint, ¶14.  Both prior to and during the time in which he was physically attacked and assaulted plaintiff made no aggressive movements, no furtive gestures, and no physical movements which would suggest to a reasonable security officer, security guard, and/or deputy that he was armed with any kind of weapon, or had the will, or the ability to inflict substantial bodily harm against any individual.  Both prior to and during the time in which he was physically attacked and assaulted, plaintiff did not act in such a manner as to suggest to a reasonable officer that he had the will, or the ability to inflict substantial bodily harm against any individual.  They were not faced with any circumstances which would have led a reasonable security officer, security guard and/or deputy to believe that plaintiff posed the risk of death, or serious bodily injury to any person.  Id., ¶15.  The subject incident was additionally caused by the negligent deployment of defendants’ security officers, security guards, and/or deputies and the failure of defendants to develop a tactically sound plan for addressing plaintiff’s presence at the subject premises.  As a result, defendants’ negligent actions and negligent decisions led to poor decisions regarding plaintiff’s presence after a negligent assessment and reassessment of the nonexistent threat presented by plaintiff.  The accumulation of these individualized decisions led to injuries to plaintiff.  Id., ¶16.  The Doe defendants acted in the course and scope of his/her employment.”  Id., ¶3.  All claims asserted herein against Defendants John Doe 1, 2, and 3 are presented pursuant to defendants’ vicarious liability for acts and omissions undertaken in the course and scope of their employment.  Id., ¶18.

            First cause of action for battery

“Battery is an offensive and intentional touching without the victim’s consent.”  Kaplan v. Mamelak (2008) 162 Cal. App. 4th 637, 645.  “The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are:  (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.”  Yun Hee So v. Sook Ja Shin (2013) 212 Cal. App. 4th 652, 669.  Intent is satisfied if the defendant acted with willful disregard of the plaintiff’s rights.  See Ashcraft v. King (1991) 228 Cal. App. 3d 604, 613.

As a general matter, case law indicates that “the question of whether a tort is committed within the course of employment turns on whether (1) the act performed was either required or instant to the employee's duties or (2) the employee's misconduct could be reasonably foreseen as an outgrowth of the employee's duties.”  Maria D. v. Westec Residential Sec., Inc. (2000) 85 Cal. App. 4th 125, 138 (citation omitted).  Courts have specifically applied this standard to the conduct of private security guards, stating that for “respondeat superior liability to apply, the security guard’s acts must have been engendered by or be an outgrowth of his employment.”  Id. at 146.  “An employer will not be held liable for an employee’s assault or other intentional tort that did not have a causal nexus to the employee’s work.”  Kephart v. Genuity, Inc. (2006) 136 Cal. App. 4th 280, 290.   An employee’s willful or malicious tort may fall within the scope of employment for purposes of respondeat superior, even though the employer has not authorized the employee to commit intentional torts.  Flores v. AutoZone West, Inc. (2008) 161 Cal. App. 4th 373, 379.  “[T]he determination of whether an employee has acted within the scope of employment presents a question of fact; it becomes a question of law, however, when the facts are undisputed and no conflicting inferences are possible.”  Lisa M. v. Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital (1995) 12 Cal.4th 291, 299 (internal quotations omitted).   

Defendants argue that plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts for vicarious liability for the alleged intentional tort.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient.  Plaintiff also asserts that the cases cited by defendants arise from a jury verdict and/or judgment based on the merits of the respective cases.

The Court finds that the allegations are sufficient as to vicarious liability as plaintiff has alleged that the Doe defendants were acting within the scope of their employment as security officers and security guards when they assaulted and attacked plaintiff.

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first cause of action.

 

            Fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress

The tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress is comprised of three elements:  (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffered severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) the plaintiff's injuries were actually and proximately caused by the defendant's outrageous conduct.  McMahon v. Craig (2009) 176 Cal. App. 4th 222, 234.  “In order to meet the first requirement of the tort, the alleged conduct . . . must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”  Id. at 234-35. Whether conduct is sufficiently extreme and outrageous so as to be actionable may be determined as a matter of law.  Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal. App. 4th 488, 494. To avoid demurrer, the plaintiff must allege such acts with great specificity.  Yau v. Santa Margarita Ford, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal. App. 4th 144, 160-61.  Mere indignities, insults, threats, annoyances, petty oppressions and the like are not enough.  Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal. 4th 1035, 1051.  

Defendants argue that plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts for vicarious liability for the alleged intentional tort.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient as to vicarious liability. 

The Court finds that the allegations are sufficient.  See under the first cause of action.

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the fourth cause of action.

 

 

ORDER

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and fourth causes of action in the complaint. 

Moving defendants are ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

On December 26, 2023, plaintiff Geniezip USA LLC filed a complaint against Roadlight Inc. and Greta Grigoryan for (1) conversion, (2) intentional interference with business relations, and (3) negligent interference with contractual relations. 

On January 4, 2024, plaintiffs Geniezip USA, LLC and FNS, Inc. filed a FAC.  Plaintiffs allege that on December 20, 2023, plaintiff FNS received on behalf of its customer LG Electronics U.S.A. Inc. two containers from the Port of Long Beach containing audio products for delivery to LG in Fort Worth, Texas.  FNS transloaded the contents of both containers into trailers for domestic transportation and utilized its affiliated entity, Geniezip, to arrange with Roadlight to transport the trailers from FNS’s warehouse in Torrance, California to LG’s warehouse in Fort Worth.  To memorialize such requests, Geniezip issued to defendants on December 19 and 20, 2023 Rate Confirmations.  On December 20, 2023, defendants took possession of both shipments.  Notwithstanding such commitment, defendants seized such shipments and refused to deliver such shipments to the rightful consignee, demanding payment of charges assessed by a wholly separate unrelated corporation, Jayhawk Motor Freight Inc., for shipments unrelated to those at issue in this case.  Plaintiffs have become liable to their customer LG for the value of such shipments plus additional costs.

On January 23, 2024, defendant Grigoryan (self-represented) filed an answer.

On April 4, 2024, a default was entered against Roadlight Inc.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.

Request for Production of Documents

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of “good cause” is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487.

Request for Admissions

Pursuant to CCP § 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).  “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions.  Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.”  Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶ 8:1370, citing CCP § 2033.280(b).  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  CCP § 2033.280(c).

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs Geniezip USA LLC and FNS, Inc. request that the Court compel defendant Greta Grigoryan to respond without objections to plaintiffs’ initial form and special interrogatories and requests for production of documents and to deem admitted the requests for admission.

The Court notes that the motion is improper to the extent that it combines multiple motions into one.  The Court will still consider the motion.  Plaintiffs are ordered to pay additional filing fees in the amount of $120.

Plaintiffs explain that they served their discovery requests on defendant on March 6, 2024.  Responses were due on April 8, 2024.  When no responses were received, plaintiffs’ counsel sent a letter dated April 19, 2024 to defendant, requesting responses by April 26, 2024 or plaintiffs would file a motion to compel.  To date, plaintiffs’ counsel has not received responses.

The Court finds that plaintiffs properly served discovery requests and defendant failed to respond. 

The motion is GRANTED.

                        Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Plaintiffs request $2029.50 ($495/hr. x 4.1 hrs.) plus an anticipated $2970 in filing a reply and appearing at the hearing.  The Court finds that $1,660 ($400/hr. x 4 hrs., $60 filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against defendant.

RULING

            The motion for change of venue to Merced County is GRANTED.  The case is transferred to the Superior Court of the County of Merced.  Plaintiff is ordered to pay the transfer costs and fees.

The motion for order transferring venue to Fresno County is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On February 8, 2024, Jamal Asif (self-represented) filed a complaint against Guramel Singh, Lovepreet Singh, and Tuffy Trans Inc for breach of contract and misrepresentation.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “Venue, the county in which an action takes place, is statutorily governed by the type or form of the particular action.  [Citation omitted.]  ‘Venue is determined based on the complaint on file at the time the motion to change venue is made.’  [Citations.]  (Brown v. Superior Court (1984) 37 Cal. 3d 477, 482 . . . .).”  Lebastchi v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal. App. 4th 1465, 1469. 

CCP § 395(a) provides, in relevant part, “[e]xcept as otherwise provided by law and subject to the power of the court to transfer actions or proceedings as provided in this title, the superior court in the county where the defendants or some of them reside at the commencement of the action is the proper court for the trial of the action.  If the action is for injury to person or personal property or for death from wrongful act or negligence, the superior court in either the county where the injury occurs or the injury causing death occurs or the county where the defendants, or some of them reside at the commencement of the action, is a proper court for the trial of the action. . . .” 

CCP § 396b(a) provides, in relevant part, “[i]f an action . . . is commenced in a court having jurisdiction of the subject matter thereof, other than the court designated as the proper court for trial thereof, . . . the action may, notwithstanding, be tried in the court where commenced, unless the defendant, at the time he or she answers, demurs, or moves to strike, or at his or her option, without answering, demurring, or moving to strike and within the time otherwise allowed to respond to the complaint, files with the clerk, a notice of motion for an order transferring the action . . . to the proper court. . . .”  See Van Gaalen v. Superior Court (1978) 80 Cal. App. 3d 371, 378 (“the time limit for filing a notice of motion for change of venue prescribed by section 396b is not jurisdictional in the sense that a trial court is without power to entertain an untimely motion.”).

CCP § 395.5 states, “[a] corporation . . . may be sued in the county where the contract is made or is to be performed, or where the obligation or liability arises, or the breach occurs; or in the county where the principal place of business of such corporation is situated, subject to the power of the court to change the place of trial as in other cases.”

CCP § 397 states, in relevant part, that, “[t]he court may, on motion, change the place of trial in the following cases:  (a) When the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court. . . .” 

CCP § 399 states, in part, “If the transfer is sought solely, or is ordered, because the action or proceeding was commenced in a court other than that designated as proper by this title, those costs and fees, including any expenses and attorney's fees awarded to the defendant pursuant to Section 396b, shall be paid by the plaintiff before the transfer is made.”

DISCUSSION

            Motion to Change Venue

Defendant Tuffy Trans requests that the Court transfer venue to Merced County and order plaintiff to pay the fees and costs associated with transfer or, alternatively, to dismiss the herein complaint without prejudice.

            The complaint alleges that plaintiff purchased a “secondhand, defective, repaired truck with 1500 miles sold as a brand new and substantially much above market-price.”  Plaintiff has attempted to have the truck repaired more than five attempts and that it has been under repair for several days.  The copy of the bill of sale attached to the complaint indicates that Tuffy Trans Inc. was the seller and mailing address is 2411 Bailey Ave., Merced, CA 95341, for $200,000 of a 2023 Freightliner Cascadia in an “as is” condition.  The Certificate of Title indicates the registered owner as Tuffy Trans Inc, 2411 Bailey Ave., Merced, CA.

            Defendant Tuffy Trans argues that Los Angeles County is an improper venue because defendant’s principal place of business is in Merced County; the contract that is the gravamen of the case was entered into in Merced County; and that plaintiff’s allegation that Los Angeles County is the proper venue because the matter involves “real property” is “wholly incorrect.”  Defendant further asserts that none of the named defendants are related to or reside in Los Angeles County.  See Bachitar Singh (CEO of Tuffy Trans) declaration and Exh. 2 (Statement of Information).

The Court finds that Los Angeles County is not a proper venue.  None of the defendants resides in Los Angeles County or have their principal place of business in Los Angeles County.  Further, the sale of the truck did not occur in Los Angeles County.  Whether plaintiff resides in Los Angeles County is of no import.  In consideration of the individual defendants’ concurrent motion, the Court finds that both Merced County and Fresno County are proper venues as one or more defendants reside in Merced County or Fresno County and the contract was entered into and performed in Merced County.

The motion is GRANTED as to transferring venue to Merced County in consideration that the contract was entered into in Merced County (vehicle was purchased in Merced County) and that the seller corporate defendant’s principal place of business is located in Merced County.  The Court further orders that the transfer costs and fees shall be paid by plaintiff.

 

            Motion to Transfer Venue

            Defendants Gurmail Singh and Lovepreet Singh request an order transferring the venue of this action to the Superior Court of County of Fresno. 

            The declarations of the individual defendants Gurmail Singh and Lovepreet Singh state that they have resided in Clovis, California for the last five years and that Clovis is located in Fresno County and that they have never resided in Los Angeles.  They also state that they have never had any business dealings with plaintiff in Los Angeles.  Defendants also point out that plaintiff improperly marked the box that jurisdiction is proper in Los Angeles because “real property that is the subject of this action is located here.” 

            In opposition, plaintiff asserts that his business address is located in Torrance, which is in Los Angeles County.

            Although the Court finds that Fresno County is a proper county for venue, the motion is DENIED in consideration of the corporate defendant’s motion to transfer venue to Merced County.

                        Attorney fees

“In its discretion, the court may order the payment to the prevailing party of reasonable expenses and attorney's fees incurred in making or resisting the motion to transfer whether or not that party is otherwise entitled to recover his or her costs of action. In determining whether that order for expenses and fees shall be made, the court shall take into consideration (1) whether an offer to stipulate to change of venue was reasonably made and rejected, and (2) whether the motion or selection of venue was made in good faith given the facts and law the party making the motion or selecting the venue knew or should have known.”

Defendants request $2,400 ($300/hr. x 8 hrs.) in attorney’s fees.  See Nathan Miller decl.  According to counsel Miller’s declaration, he called plaintiff and requested that he stipulate to transfer venue to Fresno County and informed him that the complaint was filed “in the wrong court.”  Plaintiff purportedly stated that he could consider doing so but did not return counsel’s second call and agree to stipulate.

The Court finds that defendants reasonably made an offer to stipulate to change venue and that plaintiff’s selection of venue in Los Angeles County was not made in good faith as indicated by plaintiff marking the box on the complaint regarding “real property” although real property is not at issue.  Further, plaintiff was aware where he purchased the truck and where the seller was located.  Although the Court is inclined to award attorney’s fees, the request is DENIED because defendants are not prevailing parties on the motion as to Fresno County.

ORDER

The motion for change of venue to Merced County is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to pay the transfer costs and fees.

The motion for order transferring venue to Fresno County is DENIED.

Defendant Tuffy Trans is ordered to give notice of ruling.
