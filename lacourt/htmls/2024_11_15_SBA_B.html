DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV00134    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

SUN D. LEE, et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

24TRCV00134

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

CHUN KIL CHONG, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 15, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Chil Kil Chong, ind. and as trustee, and Richard Chong

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Sun D. Lee and Kwan Lee

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

            The Court considered the moving papers and untimely-filed opposition.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action in the FAC.

BACKGROUND

On January 17, 2024, plaintiffs Sun D. Lee and Kwan Lee (self-represented) filed a complaint against Chun Kil Chong, Chun Kil Chong, trustee of Chong Kil Chong Trust, Min Ju Bark, Richard Chong, and Jon Kim for (1) fraudulent misrepresentation and concealment, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) constructive trust, (4) quasi contract, (5) quiet and clear title, (6) financial elder abuse, and (7) IIED.

            On February 6, 2024, John Kim (self-represented) filed a general denial.

            On February 20, 2024, Chun Kil Chong (self-represented) filed an answer.

On March 29, 2024, defaults were entered against Chung Kil Chong, trustee of Chun Kil Chong Trust, Min Ju Bark, and Richard Chong.  A dismissal was entered as to John Kim.

On June 13, 2024, Chun Kil Chong filed a substitution of attorney.

On September 13, 2024, plaintiffs filed a FAC against Chun Kill Chong, individually and as trustee of the Chun Kil Chong Trust, and Richard Chong for

(1) fraudulent misrepresentation and concealment,

(2) constructive trust,

(3) breach of contract, and

(4) financial elder abuse.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendants demur to the first through fourth causes of action in the FAC on the ground that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

This lawsuit involved real property claimed by Plaintiffs to be their home of 26 years.  They claim that they have resided at it for the entire period, but that financial problems in about 2009 and 2010 led them to transfer the property to Plaintiff  Sun Lee’s brother, Defendant Chun Kill Chong in a short sale.  They claim that this was subject to an oral agreement that defendant would transfer the real property back to them.  Defendants direct the Court to the Plaintiffs’ bankruptcy filed a few months AFTER the transfer of the real property, where the Plaintiffs claimed no interest in real property and no executory contracts.

The FAC alleges that plaintiffs had been residing in their home at 6 Merit Park Drive, Gardena.  FAC, ¶1.  Defendants deliberately and fraudulently took title to plaintiffs’ house, which they purchased in 1998 for $129,000.  Id., ¶7.  Plaintiffs had enjoyed their life in their house before they encountered serious medical problems with Kwan Lee who suffered from chronic cardiac problems.  Plaintiffs wanted to protect their house from the medical bills.  Id., ¶10.  At about the same time, plaintiffs were delinquent on their $272,000 note recorded on February 16, 2006.  Lender WMC Mortgage had filed a notice of default and a notice of sale.  The secured note was an adjustable rate note that provided for a significant increase in the interest rate that plaintiffs could not afford to make.

Plaintiff Sun Lee confided in her brother, Chun Kil Chong, about their financial difficulties.  Defendant Chong offered to help and brought a real estate specialist to the house to consult on what could be done.  It was determined that defendant Chong could save the property from foreclosure and qualify for a new loan to allow plaintiffs to continue living there if plaintiff would pay all of the costs of the new purchase loan.  It was understood and agreed that legal title to the property could be returned to plaintiffs in the future so they would not lose any equity in the property.  Id., ¶11.

The FAC also alleges that the foreclosing lender agreed to a lesser amount to pay off its lien of record by means of a sale to a third party, thus allowing a purchase price of $155,000.  Defendant obtained a loan in his name for $124,000.  Plaintiffs paid the down payment, closing, costs, and any fees of $36,000 paid outside of escrow to close the “short sale.”  The deed was recorded on February 19, 2010.

Plaintiffs remained in possession of their residence and paid the new monthly loan payment to defendant Chong’s bank account and the property taxes.  Id., ¶12.  The parties had understood from the real estate agent that there should be a waiting period of about three years after a short sale before the sellers should consider getting back on title.  Sometime in 2013, plaintiffs asked defendant how they could get their property back.  Defendant explained that he could not transfer the property because he was still obligated on the purchase loan, or a new loan obtained by plaintiffs would be needed and legal fees would be incurred.  Plaintiffs accepted this explanation and continued to live on the premises and made the agreed monthly payments to defendant for the next ten years.  Id., ¶13.

The FAC further alleges that on September 13, 2023, defendant caused to be served on plaintiffs a sixty-day notice to quit.  Prior to this notice, plaintiffs had never believed or understood that defendant had considered them tenants.  On November 21, 2023, defendant, as trustee, filed an unlawful detainer alleging that a 2010 written lease agreement existed between the parties.  No written lease was attached because none existed.  On March 7, 2024, the unlawful detainer was voluntarily dismissed.  Id., ¶15.  Plaintiffs discovered that defendant refinanced the property on November 25, 2023 by borrowing a new loan for $155,000 and paying off the balance of the $124,000 loan and had transferred the property to a trust on February 24, 2021.  Id., ¶¶17-18.

The FAC further alleges that after filing this action in January 2024, plaintiffs stopped making the monthly payment to defendant on the advice of their paralegal advisor.  Defendants served a three-day notice to quit and filed a second unlawful detainer action in March 2024.  Plaintiffs did not defend against the second eviction because the fraud case was pending.  This error in legal judgment resulted in a default judgment and a sheriff’s lockout in June 2024.  Id., ¶19.

First cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation and concealment

“’The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of fact.’”  Hambridge v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal. App. 4th 124, 162 (citation omitted).  “One who willfully deceives another with intent to induce him to alter his position to his injury or risk, is liable for any damage which he thereby suffers.”  Civil Code §1709.

“[T]o establish fraud through nondisclosure or concealment of facts, it is necessary to show the defendant ‘was under a legal duty to disclose them.’”  OCM Principal Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 835, 845.  Nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud when:  (1) there is a fiduciary relationship between the parties; (2) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.  Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum Commission v. Insomniac, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal. App. 4th 803, 831.

The FAC alleges that in 2009 or 2010, when defendant Chong agreed to help plaintiffs save their residence from foreclosure, he fraudulently misrepresented his intentions by stating and agreeing that he would use his credit to obtain a new purchase money loan for their residence, that he would buy their property via a “short sale” and be on legal title but that plaintiffs would remain living at the property and retain all the equity in the property as long as they paid all the costs of the short sale transaction, including the down payment, monthly loan payment, and HOA payments, and any other real property expenses.  Defendant further misrepresented by statements and agreements that in the future plaintiffs would be able to recover title to their property and their equity in the property.  FAC, ¶21.  These representations were false.  The true facts are that defendant saw an opportunity to take advantage of his sister’s financial distress to buy plaintiffs’ property cheaply and gain the existing and future equity buildup at a future sale.  He did not disclose the intent to plaintiffs, but his misrepresentations caused plaintiffs to justifiably believe and trust their family member that he would provide the help they needed using his credit without any out-of-pocket expense on his behalf.  Id., ¶22.

About three years after the short sale closed, defendant found himself in need of money and decided that he could refinance plaintiffs’ residence and pull out some equity money because he was the legal owner.  He refinanced and realized $20,000 to $30,000 with plaintiffs paying the increased monthly payment on the higher loan amount.  He then concealed from plaintiffs that he refinanced and pulled out money for himself and that he considered himself the full legal and beneficial owner of the property, considered plaintiffs his tenants, and could evict them and sell the property whenever he wanted.  When plaintiffs inquired about securing legal title for themselves about three years after the short sale, defendant made excuses saying that it would be too expensive or that title could not be transferred while he was still obligated on a loan.  Id., ¶23.

Not disclosed in the Complaint is the Plaintiffs’ bankruptcy.  Defendants ask the Court to take judicial notice of the July 14, 2010 Voluntary Petition for Bankruptcy filed by Plaintiffs, Case No. 2:10-bk-38994-RN as well as the Discharge of Debtor also filed July 14, 2010.  (Defendants erroneously state in their Request for Judicial Notice that the bankruptcy documents are form 2020.)  Defendants argue that plaintiffs are judicially estopped from pursuing this cause of action because five months (July 14, 2010) after they transferred the property, they filed for bankruptcy and stated in their petition that they had no “legal, equitable, or future interest” in any real property nor any executory contacts.  See Request for Judicial Notice, Exh. 1.  At the time of the transfer as alleged, they were transferring the property to avoid medical bills, and they were delinquent on their note and the lender had recorded a default and sale.  They listed the property as sold due to a “short sale” and received a discharge.  Defendants contend that plaintiffs’ allegations that they have equity in the property and an agreement with defendant are wholly inconsistent with their bankruptcy petition, wherein they stated that they had moved from the property in February 2010 and listed a different resident address.

Defendants further argue that the allegations are insufficient as there are no allegations that when the property was transferred to defendant, he did not intend to perform.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that the fraudulent concealment was when defendant refinanced in 2013 and did not tell plaintiffs but had them pay the new monthly mortgage on the increased refinance loan.  Plaintiffs further argue that judicial estoppel does not apply because the “incorrect scheduling of assets” was not “intentional, but the result of mistake, inadvertence, ignorance of legal procedures or lack of adequate legal advice.”  Plaintiffs assert that the “nature and characterization of the trust agreement between the parties was not easily understood, maybe at best a future expectation, if honored by the defendant, and at worst, a constructive trust claim for violation of the arrangement between the parties.”  Plaintiffs request leave to address the judicial estoppel exception of ignorance, fraud, or mistake.

The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient.  Plaintiffs have not alleged any facts that at the time the property was transferred and purported promises made (2010), defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact, that he would evict them thirteen years later, or intended to defraud them by refinancing in 2013 and evicting them in 2023 or his intent not to perform the alleged promises when they were made.  “’It is insufficient to show an unkept but honest promise, or mere subsequent failure of performance.”  Reeder v. Specialized Loan Servicing LLC (2020) 52 Cal. App. 5th 795, 804 (citation omitted).  As to the allegation that defendant refinanced and did not tell plaintiffs, plaintiffs allege that they paid an increased monthly amount, which purportedly would have given them notice that something occurred to increase their payments.  Plaintiffs were at least on inquiry notice since they knew that the payments requested of them increased and had no information justifying it.

Further, based on plaintiffs’ bankruptcy petition, of which the Court takes judicial notice, plaintiffs declared that there they had no interest in any real property and that there were no executory contract in existence at the time that the transfer of title was made in 2010.  Plaintiffs appear to be judicially estopped.  ).  Plaintiffs obtained the benefit of their bankruptcy “disclosures” and were not obligated to address their claimed interest in the real property.  Has it been disclosed as required they may well have seen their interest addressed by the Bankruptcy Court to their disadvantage.  Now they want to “recover” that interest in the real property for their benefit.  “The doctrine applies when ‘(1) the same party has taken two positions; (2) the positions were taken in judicial or quasi-judicial administrative proceedings; (3) the party was successful in asserting the first position (i.e., the tribunal adopted the position or accepted it as true); (4) the two positions are totally inconsistent; and (5) the first position was not taken as a result of ignorance, fraud, or mistake.’”  Miyahara v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2024) 99 Cal. App. 5th 687, 697 (citations omitted.  These elements have been met.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Second cause of action for constructive trust

“The principal circumstances where constructive trusts are imposed are set forth in Civil Code sections 2223 and 2224.  Section 2223 provides that ‘[o]ne who wrongfully detains a thing is an involuntary trustee thereof, for the benefit of the owner.’  Section 2224 states that ‘[o]ne who gains a thing by fraud, accident, mistake, undue influence, the violation of a trust, or other wrongful act, is, unless he or she has some other and better right thereto, an involuntary trustee of the thing gained, for the benefit of the person who would otherwise have had it.’  Under these statutes and the case law applying them, a constructive trust may only be imposed where the following three conditions are satisfied:  (1) the existence of a res (property or some interest in property); (2) the right of a complaining party to that res; and (3) some wrongful acquisition or detention of the res by another party who is not entitled to it.”  Communist Party v. 522 Valencia, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal. App. 4th 980, 990 (citations omitted).

“A constructive trust cannot exist unless there is evidence that property has been wrongfully acquired or detained by a person not entitled to its possession.”  Id. at 991.  “Before a court can impose a constructive trust on the basis of a defendant's wrongful acquisition or retention of property under Civil Code section 2223 or 2224, there must be some other party rightfully entitled to that property.”  Id.

The FAC alleges that the parties agreed to the short sale of plaintiffs’ residence that was in foreclosure in order to save the property for plaintiffs.  Defendant agreed to use his credit worthiness to obtain a loan with plaintiffs paying all costs of sale, the down payment, monthly loan, and HOA payments and taxes.  It was understood and agreed that plaintiffs would still own all the equity and be allowed to remain living at the property as they had since 1998.  FAC, ¶27.  Plaintiffs relied on defendant’s promises to help them avoid foreclosure and remain living at the premises.  They paid the down payment to defendant and made all the monthly expenses by paying defendant as required.  Id., ¶28.  On September 13, 2023, defendant served notice of lease termination demanding that plaintiffs move out and give up possession.  This was the first conduct by defendant to assert that he had complete legal and equitable ownership of plaintiffs’ residence, which was a total contradiction of the agreement between them.  Id., ¶30.  At the time of the short sale when the promises were made, plaintiffs were ignorant of defendant’s secret intention not to perform their agreement and were justified in relying on plaintiff Sun Lee’s brother’s offer to help save the home.  Plaintiffs could not, in the exercise of reasonable diligence, have discovered defendant’s secret intention and therefore acted reasonably in relying on defendant’s promises.  Id., ¶31.  By virtue of this fraudulent acts, defendant holds the subject property as a constructive trustee for plaintiffs’ benefit.  Id., ¶32.

The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient.  It is not clear what the legal basis is for claiming that Plaintiffs are entitled to the real property.  See rulings under the first and third causes of action.  There is no allegation that defendant wrongfully acquired the real property as plaintiffs freely transferred the legal title to defendant and then filed for bankruptcy and received a discharge.  There is also no allegation that defendant has wrongfully detained the real property as he was still liable on the loan.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.   

 

Third cause of action for breach of contract

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.

The FAC alleges that the parties had an oral agreement.  The essential terms of the agreement were that plaintiffs and defendant Chong agreed to the short sale of plaintiffs’ residence that was in foreclosure in order to save the property for plaintiffs.  Defendant agreed to use his credit worthiness to obtain a loan with plaintiffs paying all the costs.  It was understood and agreed that plaintiffs would still own all the equity and be allowed to remain living at the property.  FAC, ¶34.  On September 13, 2023, defendant breached the agreement by serving a 60-day notice to quit and deliver up possession followed by an unlawful detainer action against plaintiffs in November 2023.  Defendant was eventually able to evict plaintiffs and have them removed and locked out of their residence.  Id., ¶35.  Plaintiffs had performed all obligations to defendant up to and including the November 2023 eviction litigation.  Id., ¶36.

The Court rules as follows:  The FAC is based on a purported oral agreement between plaintiffs and defendant that defendant would reconvey plaintiffs their interest in the property and that plaintiffs would retain the equity and remain in possession.  Although not raised by defendants, under Civil Code §1624, the statute of frauds states, “(a) The following contracts are invalid, unless they, or some note or memorandum thereof, are in writing and subscribed by the party to be charged or by the party's agent:  (1) An agreement that by its terms is not to be performed within a year from the making thereof. . . . (3) An agreement for the leasing for a longer period than one year, or for the sale of real property, or of an interest therein; such an agreement, if made by an agent of the party sought to be charged, is invalid, unless the authority of the agent is in writing, subscribed by the party sought to be charged.”  Plaintiffs do not allege that the purported agreement is evidenced by a written instrument signed by defendant.  Plaintiffs allege that they understood from the real estate agent that there should be a waiting period of about three years after a short sale before the sellers should consider getting back on title.  Thus, the agreement to reconvey was not to be performed within a year of the transfer to defendant.  In their opposition, plaintiffs even acknowledge that there was no date, except maybe after the thirty-year loan was paid off.  Thus, the purported agreement appears to violate the statute of frauds.

Further, the essential terms of the contract are ambiguous as to when a re-conveyance was to occur.  Plaintiffs even acknowledge in their opposition that “defendant represented to the plaintiffs that they would or could eventually get their residence of twelve years back at some later time possibly as early as three years . . . . There was no further detail on how this might happen either as a purchase with a new loan or assuming an existing loan or just a simple deed conveyance.”  As to remaining in possession and retaining equity, the terms are too uncertain and indefinite to be enforced.

Further, as stated above, plaintiffs appear to be judicially estopped from asserting a cause of action for breach of oral contract because they attested that there were no executory contracts in their bankruptcy filing after they had transferred the title.  They now desire to obtain the benefit of the alleged oral contract that they failed to disclose when obligated to do so in their bankruptcy.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fourth cause of action for financial elder abuse

Financial elder abuse occurs when a person takes the property of an elder for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud or by undue influence.  See Welf. & Inst. Code §15610.30(a).  A person is deemed to have taken the property when he or she has deprived an elder of any property right.  See id. §15610.30(c).  Although bad faith or intent to defraud is no longer required, wrongful use of property must still be alleged.  Stebley v. Litton Loan Servicing, LLP (2011) 202 Cal. App. 4th 522, 527-28.  “A person . . . shall be deemed to have taken . . . property for a wrongful use if . . . the person . . . takes . . . the property and the person . . . knew or should have known that this conduct is likely to be harmful to the elder . . . .”  Id. §15610.30(b).

The FAC alleges that on September 2023, plaintiffs were elder adults within the meaning of the Welfare and Inst. Code.  FAC, ¶39.  Defendant Chong and his son defendant Richard Chong, by their actions and conduct in evicting plaintiffs from their residence in breach of their agreement, took wrongful advantage of plaintiffs.  Id., ¶40.  As a direct result of the actions and conduct of defendants, plaintiffs have suffered the loss of possession of their home of twenty-five years, emotional distress with being locked out by the authorities, and forced to find shelter.  Id., ¶41.

Defendants argue that plaintiffs fail to plead with particularity and the allegations are insufficient.  There is no allegation that defendant Chong wrongfully took the property.  As for defendant Richard, the only conduct attributed to him was that he “harassed and intimidated” plaintiffs to move and told them they should divorce.  See FAC, ¶16.

The opposition does not address this claim.

The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient, especially considering that the underlying causes of action for fraud and breach of oral contract have been insufficiently pled and appear to be barred. 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

            Statute of limitations

The statute of limitations is three years (CCP §338(d)) for the first cause of action based on fraud, two years (CCP §339) for the third cause of action for breach of an oral contract, and two years (CCP §335.1) for the fourth cause of action for financial elder abuse.  The statute of limitations is two years for the second cause of action for constructive trust as it is based on the underlying purported oral contract.  ¿“Since ‘A constructive trust is not a substantive device but merely a remedy to compel a person not justly entitled to property to transfer it to another who is entitled thereto’, an action seeking to establish a constructive trust is subject to the limitation period of the underlying substantive right.  If that substantive right is barred by the statute of limitations . . . the remedy necessarily fails.”  Davies v. Krasna¿(1975) 14 Cal.3d 502, 515-516 (citations omitted).

Defendants argue that each of the causes of action is barred by the respective statute of limitations because in 2013, plaintiffs sought to recover the property from defendant and defendant refused to transfer title.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that their causes of action do not accrue until plaintiffs discovered defendant’s assertion of ownership and breach of their agreement when they were served with a sixty-day notice to quit on September 13, 2023, followed by an unlawful detainer action.

The Court finds that each of the causes of action are barred by the respective statute of limitations.  The FAC alleges that the purported oral contract was formed in 2010.  When plaintiffs requested—the complaint alleges that plaintiffs “demanded title as promised” and the FAC alleges that plaintiffs “inquired about the possibility of recovering title”—defendant transfer the property in 2013, he refused.  (The complaint alleges that defendant “den[ied] a change of title” and the FAC alleges that defendant “explained that he could not transfer the [property] back to them.”)  The Court reasonably interprets these allegations as establishing discovery of the breach of the oral agreement (which, as stated above, appears to be barred by the statute of frauds).  Thus, the causes of action at issue accrued in 2013, and expired at the latest 2016.

The Court can not tell from the vague allegations whether or not Plaintiffs may be capable of alleging facts which will show that the statute of limitations had not run as to all the claims.  Since leave to amend is being granted, the Court shall have to evaluate this issue should it be again raised by an amended complaint.

 

ORDER

The demurrer to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action in the FAC are SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
