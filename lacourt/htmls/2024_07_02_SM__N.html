DEPARTMENT N LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21SMCV01129    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: N

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant Vervent, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is GRANTED.

Defendant Vervent, Inc. shall prepare, serve, and submit a proposed judgment as per statute.

Defendant Vervent, Inc.’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED as MOOT.

Defendant Vervent, Inc. to give notice.

REASONING

Defendant Vervent, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication
Defendant Vervent, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves the Court for summary judgment or adjudication as to all four causes of action for breach of contract, unjust enrichment, accounting, and declaratory judgment alleged against it by Plaintiffs ThunderRoad Motorcycle Trust 2016-1; Beach Point Select Master Fund, LP; Beach Point Securitized Credit Fund, LP; BPC Opportunities Fund II, LP; ThunderRoad Financial, LLC; and BP THR II Trust. On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff ThunderRoad Motorcycle Trust 2016-1 voluntarily dismissed its claims in this action. Thus, the Court considers this motion only as to Plaintiffs Beach Point Select Master Fund, LP; Beach Point Securitized Credit Fund, LP; BPC Opportunities Fund II, LP; ThunderRoad Financial, LLC; and BP THR II Trust (“Plaintiffs”).

Defendant’s Notice of Motion
In its initial motion, Defendant moved only for summary judgment in its notice of motion and did not move for summary adjudication in the alternative. It is well established that when a notice of motion seeks only summary judgment, the presence of any triable issue required denial of the motion, i.e., the motion must completely dispose of all claims against the moving party. (See Homestead Savings v. Superior Court (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 494, 497-499.) However, on December 22, 2023, Defendant filed a Notice of Errata, stating therein that seeks summary adjudication in the alternative. Where a trial court is asked to allow an amendment to the notice, it is error not to allow the amendment where the other party had a full and fair opportunity to address the summary adjudication issues. (Silva v. See’s Candy Shops, Inc. (2016) 7 Cal.App.5th 235, 256, disapproved on other grounds by Donohue v. AMN Services, LLC (2021) 11 Cal.5th 58, 77.) The Court finds that Plaintiffs have had sufficient opportunity to address the motion as one for summary adjudication, particularly given that the motion was refiled, and the hearing was continued. Thus, the Court considers Defendant’s motion to be on for summary adjudication in the alternative, and the Court will consider each of Defendant’s arguments in that context.

Defendant’s Moving Separate Statement
Plaintiffs take issue with Defendant’s separate statement on the ground it does not comply with rule 3.1350 of the California Rules of Court because it does not identify, as to each issue, only those material facts pertinent to the disposition of such issue, instead listing fact sections and incorporating those facts by reference, thus failing to put Plaintiffs or the Court on notice of the facts relevant to each issue.

Rule 3.1350(d)(1)(A) of the California Rules of Court provides that “[t]he Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts in support of a motion must separately identify . . . [e]ach cause of action, claim for damages, issue of duty, or affirmative defense that is the subject of the motion.” “The separate statement is not merely a technical requirement, it is an indispensable part of the summary judgment or adjudication process.” (Whitehead v. Habig (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 896, 902.) While the moving separate statement is not a model of clarity, i.e., the use of incorporation by reference of all facts does not help the Court or Plaintiffs much, the formatting is common and technically compliant, and Defendant’s memorandum of points and authorities makes clear which facts in the separate statement support each of the identified issues. Thus, Plaintiffs’ objection to Defendant’s separate statement is OVERRULED.

Defendant’s Reply Separate Statement
The Court has not considered Defendant’s “Re-Filed Reply to Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement of Disputed Material Facts and Additional Disputed Facts,” as this document is a reply separate statement not authorized by statute, and Defendant failed to seek leave of court to file such a document. (See Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 252 [“There is no provision in the statute for” including a reply separate statement].)

Legal Standard
The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467 (Avivi).)

“Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “[T]he court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection) . . . in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

Factual Background
Plaintiff ThunderRoad Financial, LLC (“ThunderRoad”) is an indirect third-party finance company that finances consumer powersports vehicles, mostly motorcycle loans. (Def.’s UMF No. 1.) Defendant services loans originated by third parties, and it sends invoices to borrowers, collects loan payments from them, maintains loan records, and resolves delinquencies. (Def.’s UMF Nos. 5, 6.) Defendant was formed in July 2019 following the merger of two other loan servicers, First Associates Loan Servicing, LLC (“First Associates”) and Portfolio Financial Servicing Company (“PFSC”), all of whom serviced two different portfolios of loans originated by ThunderRoad related to financing borrowers’ purchases of motorcycles. (Def.’s UMF Nos. 1, 5, 13-28.)

The formation and servicing of the two loan portfolios are governed by two sets of agreements. The first loan portfolio was created through a securitization so ThunderRoad could receive additional funding, and ThunderRoad, through a depositor, transferred its interest in a pool of loans through an Indenture, entered between ThunderRoad Motorcycle Trust 2016-1 (“TR Trust”) and U.S. Bank National Association (“U.S. Bank”). (Def.’s UMF Nos. 7-10, 12-15.) By purchasing the issued notes, investors acquired rights to be paid according to the terms of the Indenture, while ThunderRoad increased its cash flow from the purchases, and Beach Point Select Master Fund LP, Beach Point Securitized Credit Fund LP, and BPC Opportunities Fund II LP (collectively “Beach Point”) claims to have purchased an interest in the securitized portfolio by purchasing notes. (Def.’s UMF Nos. 11-12.) TR Trust, ThunderRoad, U.S. Bank, and First Associates also entered into a Sale and Servicing Agreement (“SSA”) through which First Associates became the servicer for the first loan portfolio. (Def.’s Ex. B.) For the second portfolio, ThunderRoad sold its interest in a pool of motorcycle loans to BP THR II Trust (“BP Trust”), and, on October 2, 2017, ThunderRoad and BP Trust entered into a Master Servicing Agreement (“MSA”), under which ThunderRoad became the servicer for the loan portfolio, while ThunderRoad and PFSC also entered into a Third Party Sub-Servicer Agreement (“TRPSA”), under which PFSC became the sub-servicer for the loan portfolio. (Def.’s UMF Nos. 4, 12, 22-26; Def.’s Ex. G.) Defendant began servicing this portfolio in 2019 when First Associates and PFSC merged, and in July 2020, ThunderRoad switched to another servicer and terminated Defendant as the sub-servicer. (Def.’s UMF Nos. 27-29.)

Statute of Limitations
Defendant first argues that Plaintiffs’ claims are barred by the applicable statutes of limitations. Defendant argues that Plaintiff have known and complained of inadequate servicing, miscalculated servicing fees, and incomplete or erroneous servicing reports by First Associates and later Defendant with respect to the first loan portfolio from the outset of the Indenture and SSA in April 2016, but it was not until June 28, 2021, that Plaintiffs brought this action. Claims for breach of contract, declaratory judgment, and accounting are subject to a four-year statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 337, subdivision (a), while a claim for unjust enrichment is subject to a two-year statute of limitations based on a quasi-contract theory of liability under Code of Civil Procedure section 339, subdivision (1).

Defendant provides evidence that ThunderRoad’s Chief Financial Officer emailed First Associates in June 2016 to states that ThunderRoad had been communicating concerns about servicing performance (Def.’s UMF Nos. 36-38), and in a September 2016 email, ThunderRoad’s president told employees that investor concerns concerning recovery rates, collection efforts, servicing fees, and charge offs stemmed from issues with First Associates (Def.’s UMF Nos. 39-41). In January 2017, ThunderRoad’s counsel sent First Associates a demand letter expressly identifying a breach of contract claim and reiterating these same concerns (Def.’s UMF Nos. 43-44), and in a May 2020 email to Defendant, a Beach Point executive acknowledged issues that had been raised with First Associates starting in 2016 (Def.’s UMF No. 42). Defendant argues that the conduct described in these communications is the same stated in Plaintiffs’ breach of contract claim, but Plaintiffs waited more than five years to file their complaint. (See Compl. ¶¶ 43-51, 87-91.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue their claims are not time barred because New York provides for a six-year statute of limitations period, and the SSA includes a choice of law provision. (Def.’s Ex. B.) Notably, New York law requires that Plaintiffs satisfy both the six-year statute of limitations for New York and the statute of limitations of the jurisdiction where the claim arose (N.Y. CPLR § 202; Hughes Electronics Corp. v. Citibank Delaware (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 251, 259-261), and Plaintiffs specifically allege in their complaint that Defendant committed breaches of contract and tortious acts within Santa Monica (Compl. ¶¶ 8-14). Thus, the Court is not convinced that Plaintiffs’ claims are timely on these grounds.

Plaintiffs also argue that their claims are timely under the continuous accrual doctrine. It is axiomatic that the continuous accrual doctrine does not apply here where there was no recurring obligation or periodic payments (State of California ex rel. Metz v. CCC Information Services, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 402, 418), and there is no basis to conclude that there was a recurring obligation or periodic payments made here. Insofar as Plaintiffs argue that Defendant tolled the statute of limitations by impairing access to, or fraudulently concealing, information relating to the alleged breaches, this argument is not well taken when the evidence shows that Plaintiffs communicated about the purported breaches as early as 2016. Finally, the Court cannot conclude that the COVID-19 emergency rules tolled the statute of limitations in such a manner that Plaintiffs’ claims may be saved, as the claims were tolled for only six months from April 6, 2020 to October 1, 2020, and Plaintiffs claims were filed more than a year late.

For these reasons, the Court finds that Plaintiffs’ claims, all of which arise out of purported breaches of contracts, are barred by the applicable statutes of limitations. Accordingly, Defendant Vervent, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. Defendant Vervent, Inc. shall prepare, serve, and submit a proposed judgment as per statute.

Evidentiary Objections
Defendant objects to certain statements within the declaration of Donal Hummer. The Court declines to rule on these objections, as they are not material to the Court’s ruling herein.

Defendant Vervent, Inc.’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings
Given the Court’s ruling granting Defendant’s motion for summary judgment, Defendant Vervent, Inc.’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED as MOOT.


Case Number: 21STCV44592    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: N

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant Gilbert Azafrani’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is DENIED.

Defendant Gilbert Azafrani shall file an answer within 15 days of entry of this order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (b).)

Defendant Gilbert Azafrani to give notice. 

REASONING

Request for Judicial Notice
Defendant Gilbert Azafrani (“Defendant”) requests judicial notice of the Court’s standing order re: personal injury procedure and that the proof of service filed on January 23, 2022 does not mention service of the standing order. Defendant’s request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivisions (d) and (h). While Plaintiff Isabell Jansen (“Plaintiff”) states that she did not receive a copy of this request, the Court notes that both documents are readily available to Plaintiff such that there is no harm to Plaintiff in the Court taking judicial notice of the documents.

Analysis
Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10, subdivision (a)(1), provides that a defendant may move to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him. Defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint upon him on the ground that Plaintiff Isabell Jansen (“Plaintiff”) failed to serve Defendant in a code-complaint manner.

The Court is perplexed by Defendant's Motion to Quash filed on February 16, 2024 in that  Defendant Gilbert Azabrani's previously moved this court to Set Aside Invalid and Void Default and Quash Service of Summons and Dismiss Action.  On January 18, 2023 this Court granted Defendant's Motion to Set Aside Invalid and Void Default, but denied Defendant's Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Dismiss the Action.  Defendant was provided time to answer or otherwise respond, but instead filed the instant Motion to Quash.  

Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008 provides, 
(a) When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.
Defendant's Motion to Quash filed on February 16, 2024 is DENIED as previously heard and decided.  If the Court were to treat the Motion to Quash as a Motion for Reconsideration, the Motion is DENIED as untimely.    However, in an abundance of caution the Court provides its ruling on the matter as set forth below:

The proof of service of summons filed on March 8, 2022, indicates that Defendant was served by personal service at 1101 Victoria Avenue in Venice on January 23, 2022, at 2:40 p.m. The proof of service is signed by a registered California process server, which creates a rebuttable presumption of proper service pursuant to Evidence Code section 647.

Defendant’s opposition makes several arguments relating to substituted service, which is inapposite when the proof of service states that Defendant was personally served, so the Court need only determine whether personal service was effected. Defendant also refers to process servers lacking credibility and references a vexatious litigant, which is not relevant to this case or the process server who effected service here.

Defendant provides his sworn statement that he was in bed all afternoon at his residence at 1101A Victoria Avenue, which is a different address than what is listed in the proof of service, and the individual who resides with him found the summons and complaint on her doorstep on January 23, 2022. (Mot., Azafrani Decl. ¶¶ 2-5.) It follows that the Court is required to make a credibility determination as to whether the process server’s account or Defendant’s account is correct. Notably, the process server’s account is supported by Plaintiff’s counsel’s sworn statement that he was in contact with both Plaintiff and the process server on the date of service, and on that date, Plaintiff advised him that the process server was at the subject residence, and counsel was advised by the process server that personal service had been effectuated. (Opp’n, Rochlin Decl. ¶ 4.) Defendant has provided a declaration from a third party residing at the same residence as Defendant, stating the same facts as Defendant’s declaration. (Mot., Inozemtseva Decl. ¶¶ 2-6.) Given that Plaintiff enjoys a presumption of proper service with a declaration from a registered California process server, the Court finds that Defendant has not sufficiently rebutted that presumption. Further, while service of the Court’s standing order is required, Defendant cites no authority to compel a conclusion that the Court lacks personal jurisdiction due to a failure to serve this document with the summons and complaint. Accordingly, Defendant Gilbert Azafrani’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is DENIED. Given that Defendant filed another  Motion to Quash rather than an appropriate responsive pleading pursuant to the Court's January 18, 2024 order Defendant Gilbert Azafrani shall file an answer  within 15 days of entry of this order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (b).)

The Court again notes, as it has in past orders, that defense counsel’s erratic use of font and space formatting in the motion make Defendant’s motion unnecessarily difficult to read, and the Court asks counsel to limit font and spacing changes to only necessary changes.

Case Number: 22SMCV02908    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: N

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendants Jeffrey Tamkin and Tamkin Development Corporation’s Motion to Stay Civil Action Pending Resolution of Criminal Proceedings is DENIED.

Defendants Jeffrey Tamkin and Tamkin Development Corporation to give notice. 

REASONING

Defendants Jeffrey Tamkin and Tamkin Development Corporation (“Defendants”) move the Court for an order staying this civil action pending the resolution of an indictment and attendant criminal proceeding against Defendant Jeffrey Tamkin (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 514932 (People v. Jeffrey Herter Tamkin)). Defendants argue that a stay is necessary to protect Tamkin’s Fifth Amendment rights in connection with the criminal proceeding, which arises from the same underlying facts as this civil action, and because Tamkin is the only officer and manager of Tamkin Development Corporation, the corporation would be prejudiced by an inability to defend itself. Plaintiff Mark Rogo (“Plaintiff”) has not filed an opposition to the motion.

“[T]he Constitution does not ordinarily require a stay of civil proceedings pending the outcome of criminal proceedings,” but “a court may decide in its discretion to stay civil proceedings when the interests of justice seem to require such action.” (Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885, quotation marks, ellipses, and brackets omitted.) To determine “whether to stay civil proceedings in the face of a parallel criminal proceeding,” the court “should consider the extent to which the defendant’s fifth amendment rights are implicated.” (Ibid., quotation marks omitted.) The court should also consider the following five factors: “(1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation.” (Ibid.)

Defendants provide evidence that Tamkin was indicted on October 31, 2023, for 110 counts of grand theft, fraud in the offer or sale of securities, money laundering, and fraudulent securities scheme related to notes issued to approximately 20 individuals. (Mot., Liu Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff is identified as having served as a grand jury witness prior to the indictment. (Ibid.) However, despite Defendants’ representations in their motion, Plaintiff is not identified as a victim in the indictment, nor do any of the counts relate to Tamkin’s alleged failure to repay the loan and any interest due to Plaintiff. Thus, it is not clear from the indictment provided that this civil action and the criminal proceeding concern the same facts except to allege the same conduct by Tamkin as to other individuals. Given that the criminal case does not concern conduct against Plaintiff, the Court is not convinced that all discovery in this case will require Tamkin to choose between testifying here and asserting his Fifth Amendment rights.

Further, the factors identified in Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court, supra, 79 Cal.App.4th at page 876, weigh in favor of denying Defendants’ request for a stay. First, Tamkin Development Corporation does not have a Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination. (Id. at p. 883 [“unlike private individuals, corporations have no privilege against self-incrimination”].) As to Tamkin, Plaintiff’s interest in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it and the potential prejudice to Plaintiff weighs in favor of denying the motion, and requiring Plaintiff to wait for the resolution of the criminal proceeding “would increase the danger of prejudice resulting from the loss of evidence, including the inability of witnesses to recall specific facts, or the possible death of a party.” (Id. at 887.) Third, as to the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on Defendants, it is not clear that Tamkin Development Corporation cannot “appoint an agent who could, without fear of self-incrimination, furnish such requested information as was available to the corporation” (id. at pp. 887-888), which also weighs in favor of denying the motion. Further, Tamkin can assert his Fifth Amendment privilege to certain deposition questions or discovery requests. The third factor, the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, also weighs in favor of denying the motion because “denial of the stay motion promotes the convenience of the court in the management of its cases.” (Id. at p. 888.) The fourth factor, the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation, has no bearing on the present motion, as there is no evidence before the Court that any other individuals besides Tamkin and Plaintiff are concerned with the outcome of this action. Finally, the fifth factor, the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation, weighs in favor of denying the motion because the “public has a significant interest in a system that encourages individuals to come to court for the settlement of their disputes.” (Id. at p. 889.)

For these reasons, Defendants Jeffrey Tamkin and Tamkin Development Corporation’s Motion to Stay Civil Action Pending Resolution of Criminal Proceedings is DENIED.
