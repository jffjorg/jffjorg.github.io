DEPARTMENT SEC LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT SE-C LAW & MOTION PROCEDURES ARE AS FOLLOWS: APPEARANCES: The Court will hear oral arguments on all matters at the scheduled time of hearing. If all counsel intend to submit on the Tentative Order and do not want oral argument, please advise the clerk, in Department “C”, by calling (562-345-3702). If all sides submit on the Tentative Order and the clerk is so advised, the Tentative Order will become the final order of the court and the prevailing party shall give written Notice of Ruling per CRC 3.1312. If the Moving and Responding parties do not agree to submit on the Tentative Order, the motion will be called as calendared for hearing. There is no need to contact Department “C”, as the matter will remain on calendar for hearing. If the Moving party does not call Department “C” to submit on the Tentative Order and there is no appearance by any party, then the motion(s), at the Court’s discretion, may be taken off calendar without ruling on the motion(s). ORDERS: The minute order reflecting the Court’s Order will constitute the final Order. No additional orders should be submitted to the Court for signature unless required by law or by the Court. Prevailing party shall give written Notice of Ruling per CRC 3.1312. Minute orders, which constitute the final Order of the Court, will only be sent to the parties via U.S. mail  for the following: OSC re: sanctions, OSC re: contempt or matters taken under submission after oral arguments or briefing. Counsel or parties may request copies of all other minute orders/final orders either at the clerk’s office or in writing. If a request is in writing, a self-addressed stamped envelope and the appropriate fee for copies shall be submitted.

GRISSOM V. FCA US LLC

CASE NO.: 23NWCV01442

HEARING: 12/11/2024

#4

TENTATIVE ORDER

I. Plaintiff Georgia Grisson’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories and special interrogatories, set one, is DENIED.

II. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

Georgia Grisson (Plaintiff) moves the Court for an order Compelling FCA US LLC (Defendant) to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s form interrogatories and special interrogatories, set one.

Background

This is a lemon law action. On May 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant alleging violations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. On or about February 24, 2029, Plaintiff leased a 2019 Jeep Grand Cherokee vehicle (Subject Vehicle). (Complaint, ¶ 4.) Plaintiff alleges that after taking the vehicle for repairs three times, the Subject Vehicle continues to have defects including service light illumination, dashboard lights shutting off, acceleration issues, and transmission noises. (Complaint, ¶ 12.)

Legal Standard

Where responses to interrogatories have been served but the requesting party believes that they are deficient because the answers are evasive or incomplete, or, because an objection is without merit, that party may move for an order compelling a further response. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (a).) The motion must set forth good cause justifying the discovery sought and be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (b).) The motion must be made within 45 days after service of verified responses in question, or any verified supplemental responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (c).)

“The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both

pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

Meet and Confer

A motion to compel further response to interrogatories must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.300 subd. (b).) The declaration must state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented in the motion.¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)¿

Here, the parties have not adequately met and conferred. Counsel for Plaintiff states she sent one meet and confer letter via email to Defendant and did not get a response. (Hollins Decl., ¶¶ 4, 5.) One email to counsel does not constitute a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal discovery.

Discussion

The Lease required rent to be paid on the first of each month, and if it was not received after five days of that date, there was an added late charge of 10% of the overdue amount. (UMF, ¶ 5.) Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c subd. (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

The moving party’s initial burden of proof, in moving for summary judgment, does not include disproving affirmative defenses, but instead involves proving each element of a cause of action. (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564.)

Once a moving party has satisfied the initial burden of proof, the burden “shifts to the opposing party to show, by responsive separate statement and admissible evidence, that triable issues of fact exist.” (Ostayan v. Serrano Reconveyance Co. (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1411, 1418, disapproved on other grounds by Black Sky Cap., LLC v. Cobb (2019) 7 Cal.5th 156, 165. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

An issue of damages must dispose of an entire cause of action. (DeCastro West Chodorow & Burns, Inc. v. Superior Court (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 410, 422.) Summary adjudication as to punitive damages is proper only if a reasonable jury could not find clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression. (Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 762.)

Discussion

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on its single claim for breach of contract pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(a)(1), or in the alternative, summary adjudication, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 437c(f)(1), (2).

For a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

Here, Plaintiff has established the elements for its cause of action for breach of contract. Plaintiff established the first element because it is undisputed that the parties entered into the Lease. (UMF, ¶ 2.) Plaintiff performed its covenants, conditions, and promises under the Lease. (Poliquin Decl., ¶ 8.) Defendant breached the Lease by failing to pay the base rent for the period between October to December of 2020. (Poliquin Decl., Exh. 2.) Additionally, Defendant has not paid accrued late fees associated with untimely rental payments. (Poliquin Decl., ¶ 9.) Defendant also breached the lease by failing to keep the property in good order, condition, and repair as warranted by Paragraph 7.1(a) of the Lease. (Poliquin Decl., ¶ 12.) Lastly, Plaintiff provides evidence that it suffered damages totaling $220,024,19 consisting of unpair rent, late fees, and repair charges. (Poliquin Decl., ¶ 11; Exh 2.)

The burden therefore shifts to Defendant to show that triable issues of fact exist. Defendant argues there are four triable issues. Defendant argues triable issues of fact exist as to (1) whether rent is unpaid, (2) late fees, (3) whether Defendant damaged the premises, and (4) whether Defendant can prevail on its FACC.

Defendant contends that rent is not unpaid because the Rent Adjustment Addendum did not list rent payment amounts. Paragraph three of the Rent Adjustment Addendum includes the new base rents to be paid each month except for months 62 through 64. (Poliquin Decl., Exh 2.) Defendants argue that given the precedent of the pre-lease negotiations and that the Rent Adjustment Addendum specified that the month of September was “free,” that there was no rent payment charged between months 62 and 64. In response to requests for production of documents set two, requesting documents of any communications between Plaintiff and Defendant regarding the rental payments for October through December, Defendant provided the Lease. (Goldstein Decl., Exh 4.) The Lease states that the base rent of $44,744.40 applies between the dates of September 1, 2015, to December 31, 2020. (Goldstein Decl., Exh 4.) Therefore, there is unpaid rent for the months of October, November, and December of 2020.

Defendant additionally contends that the June 2020 rent is not unpaid. Defendant provides an email in which Plaintiff deducts half of the June rent, the full July rent, and late charges from the balance in the security deposit. (Dhanraj Decl., Exh. 104.) In reply, Plaintiffs argue that the amount requested in unpaid rent subtracts the security deposit and is therefore correct. However, subtracting the security deposit does not adjust for months in which the security deposit was used to cover the rent payments. Accordingly, there is a triable issue of fact as to the amount due for the unpaid rent.

Next, Defendant argues a triable material fact exists regarding late fees. Defendant argues that the liquidated damages cause is unenforceable under Ridgley v. Topa Thrift & Loan Assn. (1998) 17 Cal.4th 970, 977. Defendant argues that the liquidated damages cause did not reflect “a reasonable endeavor by the parties to estimate a fair average compensation” for the late fees. (See Ridgley, supra, 17 Cal.4th at 977.) However, as Plaintiff’s point out in reply, Ridgley is not current law as applied to non-consumer contracts. Moreover, it is undisputed that there was a late fee of 10% of any overdue amount. (UMF, ¶ 5; Poliquin Decl., Exh. 1, ¶ 13.4.)

Defendant also contends late fees were improperly added for the month of July 2020. Defendant includes the wire transfers depicting a full and timely rent payment on July 1, 2020. (Dhanraj Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 103.) Accordingly, there is a triable material fact as to the amount of late fees owed.

Third, Defendant contends there is a triable material fact regarding whether Defendant damaged the premises. Specifically, Defendant argues certain claimed damages are not the fault of Defendant. Defendant provides emails with Plaintiff in which Defendant provides notice of issues with the bathroom in

November of 2016. (Dhanraj Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. 109.) These emails are from one year after the commencement of the lease period. Defendant has not met its burden of providing substantial responsive evidence sufficient to constitute a triable issue of fact. (See Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

Lastly, Defendant contends there is a triable material fact as to whether it can prevail on its FACC. This issue will be discussed below as it is the subject of Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication.

Motion for Summary Adjudication

In the alternative, Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication on three grounds: (1) there is no defense to Plaintiff’s breach of contract action, (2) the FACC’s first cause of action for breach of contract cannot be established, and (3) the FACC’s second cause of action for common counts cannot be established.

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication on the grounds that there is no defense to Plaintiff’s breach of contract action. As set forth above, there is a triable issue of material fact as to the damages in Plaintiff’s breach of contract action. Accordingly, there is a defense. Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to its breach of contract cause of action is DENIED.

For a cause of action for breach of contract, Defendant must establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

Here, Plaintiff has met its initial burden to show all the elements are not met. Plaintiff established the first element because it is undisputed that the parties entered into the Lease. (UMF, ¶ 2.) Plaintiff shows Defendant breached the Lease by failing to pay the base rent for the period between October to December of 2020. (Poliquin Decl., Exh. 2.) The burden thus shifts to Defendant to show a triable issue of fact exists.

As explained in further detail above, Defendant relies on the Lease and Rent Adjustment Addendum to show that there is a triable issue of fact. However, the Lease states that the base rent of $44,744.40 applies between the dates of September 1, 2015, to December 31, 2020. (Goldstein Decl., Exh 4.) Therefore, Defendant did not pay rent for the months of October, November, and December of 2020. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to the FACC’s first cause of action for breach of contract is GRANTED.

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to the FACC’s second cause of action for common counts on the grounds that Defendant cannot establish all the elements.

A cause of action for common counts – money had and received requires that (1) “defendant is indebted to plaintiff in a certain sum,” (2) “for money had and received by the defendant for the use of plaintiff.” (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)

Here, Plaintiff has met its initial burden. Plaintiff established that Defendant breached the Lease by failing to pay rent for the months of October, November, and December of 2020. The burden therefore shifts to Defendant.

Defendant has shown there is a triable issue of fact as to whether Defendants owe an amount under the Lease. Therefore, outside of the rent payments for October, November, and December of 2020, there are triable issues of material fact as to the amount owed by Defendant based on the security deposit. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to the FACC’s second cause of action is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

Defendant Community Integration Project (Defendant) demurs to the first amended complaint’s (FAC) first, second, third, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

Background

On November 27, 2023, Ernest Celaya (Ernest), by and through his parent and guardian ad-litem Maria Romero, and Lisa Celaya (Lisa), by and through her parent and guardian ad-litem, Maria Romero (Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Defendant. On February 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a FAC alleging eight causes of action: (1) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (2) abandonment via Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act – Welf. & Inst. Code §§ 15610.07, 15610.05, (3) neglect via Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act – Welf. & Inst. Code §§ 15610.07, 15610.57, (4) negligence, (5) negligence per se, (6) breach of contract – third party beneficiary. (7) intentional interference with contractual relations – third party beneficiary, and (8) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing – third party beneficiary.

Plaintiffs are dependent adults who have physical and mental limitations that restrict their abilities to carry out normal activities or protect their rights. (FAC, ¶¶ 8, 9.) Plaintiffs, who are under the care and custody of Defendant, have a sister, Melissa Celaya (Melissa) who is employed by Defendant. (FAC, ¶ 10.) Plaintiffs allege that after Melissa reported unlawful drug use by Defendant’s employees, Defendant ceased all services to Plaintiffs. (FAC, ¶ 11.)

Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) In a demurrer proceeding, the

defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, at p. 747.) A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

“Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) A “[p]laintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” (Ibid.)

Meet and Confer

The parties have adequately met and conferred.

Discussion

Defendant demurs to the first, second, third, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action of the FAC pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.10 subd. (e) and (f) on the grounds that Plaintiffs fail to plead facts sufficient to demonstrate the causes of action.

First Cause of Action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing,

emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.) “Only emotional distress of ‘such substantial quantity or enduring quality’ that an individual in civilized society should not be expected to endure it constitutes severe emotional distress. (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

Here, Plaintiffs plead facts sufficient to constitute the first cause of action. Plaintiffs demonstrate reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress. Plaintiffs allege Defendant retaliated against its clients who are under its care for actions of an employee. (FAC, ¶ 76.) The FAC alleges that as a result of this, Ernest experienced significant depression, refused to eat, and repeatedly questioned “why did they kick me out.” (FAC, ¶ 78.) Additionally, while finding a replacement, his well-being was disrupted for two weeks. (FAC, ¶ 20.) The FAC alleges Lisa experienced significant depression. (FAC, ¶ 79.) The FAC alleges both Plaintiffs experienced post-traumatic stress, humiliation, nausea, insomnia, anxiety attacks, and mental fatigue. (FAC, ¶ 81.) Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer as to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

Second and Third Causes of Action – Abandonment and Neglect

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for abandonment in violation of the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act – Welf. & Inst. Code §§15610.07, 15610.05 on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

The Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15600 et seq.) provides for steep penalties against those who abuse an elder or a dependent adult. Dependent adult abuse includes physical abuse, neglect, isolation, deprivation by a care custodian of necessary goods or services, and financial abuse. (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.07, subd. (a).) Abandonment is defined as “the desertion or willful forsaking of an elder or a dependent adult by anyone having care or custody of that person under circumstances in which a reasonable person would continue to provide care and custody.” (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.05.) Neglect includes failure to assist in personal hygiene, failure to provide medical care for physical and mental health needs, and failure to protect from health and safety hazards. (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.57, subd. (b).)

[S]everal factors [] must be present for conduct to constitute neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act and thereby trigger the enhanced remedies available under the Act. The plaintiff must allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) facts establishing that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult’s basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness). The plaintiff must also allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) that the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering. Finally, the facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury “must be pleaded with particularity,” in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.

(Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 406-407, citations omitted.)

Acts which are prohibited under the Elder Abuse Act provisions of the Welfare and Institute Code “do not include acts of simple professional negligence, but refer to forms of abuse or neglect performed with some state of culpability greater than mere negligence.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 781 (Covenant Care).) To constitute abuse and neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act, thereby triggering the enhanced remedies available under the Act, Plaintiff “must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of something more than negligence; he or she must show reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct,” with recklessness referring “to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a “deliberate disregard” of the “high degree of probability” that an injury will occur,” and “[o]ppression, fraud and malice [involving] intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 88-89, quotation marks omitted.)

The FAC does not allege facts sufficient to state causes of action for abandonment and neglect. Here, Plaintiffs were under the care of Defendant for four to eight hours a day between three and five days a week. (FAC, ¶ 99.) Ernest’s care included helping with body exercises, walking with him to retailers, and showing him how to read and write. (FAC, ¶ 99.) Lisa’s care involved schoolwork, helping to clean her room, walking with her to retailers, and taking

her to appointments. (FAC, ¶ 100.) The FAC alleges Defendants were aware of Plaintiffs’ conditions as their caretaker. Specifically, Defendants were aware Ernest has cerebral palsy and is wheelchair bound, and Lisa has autism. (FAC, ¶¶ 56, 57.) The FAC alleges Defendant was contractually bound not to terminate the contract with Plaintiffs without good cause, and if it were to be terminated, Defendant would continue services until proper replacement services were accommodated. (FAC, ¶ 94.) After Defendant terminated Plaintiffs as clients, Ernest was left without a replacement aid for two weeks. (FAC, ¶ 78.) Lisa did not receive replacement services until March 15, 2023. (FAC, ¶ 79.)

Defendant argues Plaintiffs have not alleged facts sufficient to show oppression, fraud, or malice. The Court agrees. In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that they have shown “willful forsaking” of an elder as proscribed by statute. (Cal. Wel & Inst. Code §15610.05) However, Plaintiffs plead oppression, fraud, and malice, which require a heightened pleading that Defendants acted with “knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 406-407, citations omitted.) Here, Plaintiffs allegations do not amount to deprivations of Plaintiffs’ basic needs such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care. Plaintiffs have not pled by clear and convincing evidence that Defendant acted with knowledge that Plaintiffs would suffer injury. Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second and third causes of action is SUSTAINED with LEAVE TO AMEND.

Sixth Cause of Action – Breach of Contract – Third Party Beneficiary

Defendant demurs to the sixth cause of action for breach of contract – third party beneficiary on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

An action for breach of contract action for a third party beneficiary must state (1) the existence of a contract; (2) made expressly for the benefit of a third person; and (3) enforced before the parties rescinded it. (Walters v. Marler (1978) 83 Cal.App.3d 1, 31 (citing, e.g., Civ. Code, § 1559), disapproved on other grounds by Gray v. Don Miller & Associates, Inc. (1984) 35 Cal.3d 498, 507; City of Oakland v. Oakland Raiders (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 458, 472 (to qualify as third-party beneficiary, a party must show (1) one is likely to benefit from the contract, (2) a motivating purpose of contracting parties to benefit third party, and (3) having third party suing for breach is consistent with objectives and contracting parties’ reasonable expectations).) “If the action is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)

Here, the FAC does not plead facts sufficient to state a cause of action for breach of contract. Plaintiffs allege they are the third-party beneficiaries to a written contract between Defendant and Regional Center, a nonprofit community agency which assists with California’s developmentally disabled residents. (FAC, ¶ 78.) The FAC describes terms regarding termination of the contract but does not provide the terms verbatim or attach a copy of the contract. (See Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel, supra, 74 Cal.App.4th at 307.) Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED with LEAVE TO AMEND.

Seventh Cause of Action – Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations

Defendant demurs to the seventh cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations – third party beneficiaries for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

The elements of a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations are “(1) a valid contract between plaintiff and a third party; (2) defendant’s knowledge of this contract; (3) defendant's intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.” (I-CA Enterprises, Inc. v. Palram Americas, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 257, 289.)

Here, the FAC does not state a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations. The FAC describes terms regarding termination of the contract but does not provide the terms verbatim or attach a copy of the contract. (See Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel, supra, 74 Cal.App.4th at 307.) Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the seventh cause of action is SUSTAINED with LEAVE TO AMEND.

Eighth Cause of Action – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

Defendant demurs to the eighth cause of action for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing – third party beneficiary for failure to plead facts sufficient to state a cause of action.

“The prerequisite for any action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is the existence of a contractual relationship between the parties, since the covenant is an implied term in the contract.” (Smith v. City and County of San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49.) “A breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing involves something beyond breach of the contractual duty itself and it has been held that bad faith implies unfair dealing

rather than mistaken judgment.” (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1394.)

Here, the FAC does not state a cause of action for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. As with the foregoing sixth and seventh causes of action, the FAC fails to plead the existence of a contractual relationship in which Plaintiffs are the third party beneficiaries. Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED with LEAVE TO AMEND.

GALICIA, ET AL. V. HAWAIIAN GARDENS CASINO, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00343

HEARING: 12/11/2024

#7

TENTATIVE ORDER

I. Plaintiffs Maria De Galicia, Cynthia Crowell, and the Estate of Anacleto de Galicia’s motion for leave to amend is GRANTED.

II. Plaintiffs are ORDERED to file the amended complaint attached to this motion within 20 days of this order.

Moving party to give notice.

Plaintiffs Maria De Galicia, Cynthia Crowell, and the Estate of Anacleto de Galicia move for leave to file a first amended complaint (FAC).

Background

This is a wrongful death and survival action. On January 31, 2024, Maria De Galicia, Cynthia Crowell, and the Estate of Anacleto de Galicia, by and through its successor-in-interest, Maria De Galicia (Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Hawaiian Gardens Casino and Juanito Guevarra Param (Defendants). Plaintiffs allege causes of action for negligence and premises liability. Plaintiffs allege Defendant Param struck decedent Anacleto Gonzales De Galicia (Decedent) with a motor vehicle in Defendants’ parking lot, resulting in his death.

On June 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Declaration of Cynthia Crowell for Appointment as Decedent’s Successor in Interest following Plaintiff Maria De Galicia’s assignment and release of her rights.

On August 20, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion for leave to amend to reflect the change in Decedent’s successor-in-interest. On December 11, 2024, Defendant Hawaiian Gardens Casino filed a notice of non-opposition.

Legal Standard

California Rules of Court rule 3.1324(a) provides: A motion to amend a pleading before trial must:

(1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

(2) State what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

(3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

California Rules of Court rule 3.1324(b) requires a separate declaration accompany the motion. The separate declaration must include: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court rule 3.1324(b).)

“If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court of California (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)

Discussion

Plaintiffs seek leave to amend to file a FAC to reflect a change in the successor of interest for the Estate of Anacleto Gonzales De Galicia.

Plaintiffs have complied with California Rules of Court rule 3.1324(a). Plaintiffs include a redlined copy of the proposed FAC describing the amendments to the complaint. (Chackel Decl., Exh. A.)

Plaintiffs have included a separate declaration in compliance with California Rules of Court rule 3.1324(b). In the declaration, counsel for Plaintiffs explains the proposed amendment substitutes Plaintiff Cynthia Crowell as successor in interest to the Estate of Anacleto Gonzales De Galicia in place of Maria De Galicia. (Chackel Decl., ¶ 5.) Counsel explains this change was not made earlier because he was not aware of Maria De Galicia’s assignment to her daughter, Cynthia Crowell, to be appointed as successor in interest. (Chackel Decl., ¶ 6.) There is no prejudice against Defendants because trial has not yet been scheduled and there is still time for discovery.

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ unopposed motion for leave to amend is GRANTED. Plaintiffs are ORDERED to file the amended complaint attached to this motion within 20 days of this order.

Case Number: 24NWCV00735    Hearing Date: December 11, 2024    Dept: SEC

NAVARRETE, ET AL. V. SPROUL STREET ONE, LP, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00735

HEARING: 12/11/2024

#3

TENTATIVE ORDER

I. Defendants Sproul Street Once, L.P. and Jervis Property Service, Inc.’s demurrer to the FAC’s fourth cause of action is OVERRULED.

II. Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC’s fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED with LEAVE TO AMEND.

III. Defendants’ motion to strike portions of the FAC is GRANTED IN PART. The following portions are stricken with LEAVE TO AMEND: (1) the entirety of paragraph 54, (2) item 5 in the prayer for relief, and (3) item 6 in the prayer for relief.

IV. Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED as to the remaining portions in the FAC.

Moving party to give notice.

Sproul Street Once, L.P. and Jervis Property Service, Inc. (Defendants) demur to the first amended complaint’s (FAC) fourth and fifth causes of action and move to strike portions of the FAC and punitive damages.

Background

On March 8, 2024, Harold Atilio Navarrete and Brenda Navarette (Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Defendants. This premises liability action concerns the residential property located at 8037 Stewart and Gray Road, Unit #8, Downey, CA 90241 (Property) owned and operated by Defendants. The FAC alleges nine causes of action: (1) negligence, (2) premises liability, (3) constructive eviction, (4) breach of contract, (5) violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, (6) tortious breach of warranty of habitability, (7) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (8) nuisance, and (9) violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq.

Plaintiffs allege they were diagnosed with chronic fatigue as a result of substandard living conditions in the Property. (FAC, ¶ 25.) Specifically, Plaintiffs allege the Property had leaking plumbing, accumulation of moisture, and toxic mold. (FAC, ¶ 17.) These problems persisted despite Defendants’ repair attempts. (FAC, ¶ 19.)

Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41 subd. (a).)

The parties have not adequately met and conferred. On June 27, 2024, counsel for Defendants sent a letter to counsel for Plaintiffs, but did not receive a response. (Smyth Decl., ¶¶ 2, 3)

Legal Standard

Demurrer Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, at p. 747.) A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

“Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) A “[p]laintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” (Ibid.)

Motion to Strike Standard

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer (i.e., words, phrases, prayer for damages, etc.). (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435-437.) A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleadings or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.)

Discussion

Defendants demur to the fourth and fifth causes of action.

Fourth Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

Defendants demur to the FAC’s fourth cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds that it fails to state a cause of action and that it is uncertain.

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)

Here, Plaintiffs have alleged sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for breach of contract. Plaintiffs’ have established the existence of a lease agreement between the parties and have attached a copy to the FAC. (FAC, ¶ 43; Exh. A.) Plaintiffs state they performed their own obligations under the lease. (FAC, ¶ 56.) Plaintiffs allege Defendants’ breached the lease agreement by stating Defendants’ failure to maintain the Property resulted in the toxic mold. (FAC, ¶ 56.) Lastly, Plaintiffs provide facts alleging damages including medical

and moving expenses as a result of toxic mold exposure. (FAC, ¶ 60.) The Court does not agree that the cause of action fails for uncertainty.

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled.

Fifth Cause of Action – Violation of Civil Code § 1942.4

Defendants demur to the FAC’s fifth cause of action for violation of Civil Code § 1942.4 on the grounds that it fails to state a cause of action and that it is uncertain.

Under Civil Code section 1942.4, a residential landlord may not demand or collect rent, issue a notice of rent increase, or serve a three-day notice to pay rent or quit if (1) the dwelling is untenantable as defined under Civil Code section 1941.1, is in violation of Health & Safety Code section 17920.10, or is deemed and declared substandard under Health & Safety Code section 17920.3, (2) a public officer has inspected the premises and has notified the landlord in writing of his obligations to repair the substandard conditions, (3) the conditions exist and have not been abated 35 days after service without good cause, and (4) the conditions were not cause by the tenant.

Plaintiffs have not alleged facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for a violation under Civil Code section 1942.4. Here, Plaintiffs have alleged facts sufficient to deem the Property untenantable under Civil Code section 1941.1. Plaintiffs have alleged plumbing issues, water accumulation, and the spread of toxic mold. (See Civ. Code §§ 1941.1 subd. (a)(2), (a)(6).) Plaintiffs have not pled facts pertaining to the second element. The FAC does not allege any facts pertaining to an officer inspecting the Property.

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

Motion to Strike

Defendants move to strike portions of the FAC seeking punitive damages, including the use of the words “intentionally,” and “willingly.”

Plaintiffs have not sufficiently pled allegations of malice, fraud, or oppression sufficient to warrant punitive damages. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants were aware of the danger of failing to properly repair the issues. (FAC, ¶ 49.) Plaintiffs additionally allege Defendants attempted to abate the mold by repairing the walls, repainting the walls, and stopping water leaks. (FAC, ¶ 20.) In opposition, Plaintiffs correctly state that malice does not require intent to harm, but rather a conscious disregard of a person’s rights. Plaintiffs then argue that Defendants

consciously disregarded their rights. However, the Court finds that the allegations in the FAC alleging Defendants’ repair attempts demonstrate the opposite of a conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights. The fact that the repair attempts were unsuccessful does not transform the situation into one done in malice.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS with leave to amend Defendants’ motion to strike (1) the entirety of paragraph 54, (2) item 5 in the prayer for relief, and (3) item 6 in the prayer for relief.

The Court does not agree that the use of the words “willingly” and “intentionally” reflect irrelevant, false, or improper matter to be stricken. Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike the remaining portions of the FAC.

Lastly, in opposition, Plaintiff’s argue their prayer for attorney’s fees is supported. Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike did not address attorney’s fees.
