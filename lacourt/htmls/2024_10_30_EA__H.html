Case Number: 19STCV21535    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: H

Zhang v. Ebenezer Medical Clinic, et al., Case No. 19STCV21535

 

ORDER ON MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT

Plaintiff Guifeng Zhang’s unopposed Motion for Entry of Judgment to Enforce Settlement is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiff attorneys’ fees and costs in the amount of $960.00.


Background

On or about July 8, 2018, Plaintiff Guifeng Zhang (“Zhang”) underwent a cosmetic surgical facelift procedure performed by Joseph Dinglasan, M.D. (“Dinglasan”). Zhang alleges that the procedure was not properly performed, that the operating facility was not properly sanitized and equipped, and that her stitches were not removed by medical personnel.

On June 20, 2019, Zhang filed a complaint, asserting a negligence cause of action against Defendants Ebenezer Medical Clinic, Dinglasan and Does 1-100.

On July 22, 2019, Eben Ezer DB Inc. (“Eben Ezer”) and Dinglasan filed a cross-complaint against Zhang and Roes 1-100, asserting causes of action for (1) breach of contract and (2) fraud.

On November 21, 2019, the Court sustained Zhang’s demurrer to the cross-complaint, without leave to amend.

On May 22, 2023, a “Joint Stipulation and Request for the Court to Retain Jurisdiction Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6; Order Thereon” was entered.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s unopposed motion for entry of judgment to enforce settlement. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted.

Legal Standard 

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).)

Discussion

Plaintiff moves the court for an order, per Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6, enforcing the settlement and entering judgment in accordance with the terms of the settlement. Plaintiff also requests $960.00 in attorneys’ fees and costs.

In March 2023, Plaintiff and Dinglasan entered into a “Settlement Agreement and Release in Full of All Claims and Rights” (“Agreement”), wherein Dinglasan agreed to pay Plaintiff $35,000.00 payable at $1,000.00/month beginning on April 1, 2023, at no interest, and continuing thereon on the first day of each month, until paid in full; in return, Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the complaint with prejudice, with the Court reserving jurisdiction under section 664.6 to enforce the terms of the Agreement. (Shambaugh Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. B, ¶¶ 1 and 2.) The Agreement includes a provision for attorneys’ fees for the prevailing party to enforce the Agreement. (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. B, ¶ 3(I).)

The Court notes that on May 22, 2023, a “Joint Stipulation and Request for the Court to Retain Jurisdiction Pursuant to California Code of Civil Proc Section 664.6, Order Thereon” was entered on May 22, 2023. (See also Shambaugh Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A).

Dinglasan has paid only $1,000.00 and has failed and refused to pay any further amount as agreed. (Shambaugh Decl., ¶ 5).

The unopposed motion is granted. Further, Plaintiff seeks to recoup $960.00 in attorneys’ fees and costs [calculated as follows: two hours preparing motion at $450.00/hour, plus $60.00 filing fee]. The Court awards attorney’s fees and costs in the amount requested. 

Case Number: 23PSCV01046    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: H

Brenes v. United Therapy Network, Case No. 23PSCV01046

 

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Defendant United Therapy Network’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

I.        Background

This is an action for professional negligence. In January 2022, Plaintiff Evelia Brenes (“Plaintiff”) received physical therapy treatments from Defendant United Therapy Network (“UTN”) for recovery from a recent knee surgery. During Plaintiff’s third therapy session on January 10, 2022, she alleges UTN’s physical therapist failed to use the appropriate standard of care in administering treatment by using a massage gun on Plaintiff’s knee despite her complaints of pain. Plaintiff alleges UTN’s treatment delayed her recovery and forced her to incur costs for additional medical treatment. 

On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against UTN and Does 1-50, alleging a single cause of action for professional negligence. 

Pending before the Court is UTN’s motion for summary judgment. Because the Court finds that UTN is entitled to summary judgment on the issue of causation, UTN’s motion is granted.

 

II.      Legal Standard 

A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)


III.     Discussion

UTN moves for summary judgment on the grounds that UTN did not breach the applicable standard of care and did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries. As discussed below, UTN is entitled to summary judgment on the issue of causation.

To establish a claim for professional negligence, a plaintiff must demonstrate: “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional’s negligence.” (Turpin v. Sortini (1982) 31 Cal.3d 220, 229-230, quoting 4 Witkin, Summary of Cal. Law (8th ed. 1974) Torts, § 488 et seq., p. 2749.)

“In professional malpractice cases, expert opinion testimony is required to prove or disprove that the defendant performed in accordance with the prevailing standard of care,¿except in cases where the negligence is obvious to laymen.” (Kelley v. Trunk (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 519, 523.) And “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.” (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 985, quoting Hutchinson v. United States (9th Cir. 1988) 838 F.2d 390, 392.) Similarly, “[w]here the complexity of the causation issue is beyond common experience,¿expert testimony is required to establish causation.” (Garbell v. Conejo Hardwoods, Inc. (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 1563, 1569.) Plaintiffs must establish “that defendants’ breach of the standard of care was the cause, within a reasonable medical probability, of [their] injury.” (Bushling v. Fremont Medical Center (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 493, 509.) 

The Complaint alleges UTN owed Plaintiff a duty of care as a physical therapist. (Complaint, ¶ 31.) UTN allegedly breached that duty when its staff used a massage gun on Plaintiff’s recovering knee notwithstanding her complaints of pain and medical history. (Complaint, ¶¶ 35-36.) The Complaint further alleges that the massage gun was the actual and proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries, which included months of unnecessary physical and emotional pain, time, and expenses. (Complaint, ¶¶ 37-39.) In arguing that UTN complied with the applicable standard of care and did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries, UTN points to the opinions of two experts: David Powers, PT, DPT, and Lawrence Miller, M.D.

UTN first relies on the expert opinion of David Powers, PT, DPT, a licensed physical therapist. Dr. Powers opines that UTN’s therapists acted within the applicable standard of care when they used electrical stimulation and a massage gun during Plaintiff’s treatment. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶ 40.) Specifically, Dr. Powers states that there is no evidence that the massage gun was applied improperly, as UTN’s therapist appropriately applied the massage gun to the soft tissue around Plaintiff’s knee in a circular motion for approximately five minutes, appropriately increased the massage gun’s volume or rate of oscillation during treatment, and stopped applying the massage gun upon Plaintiff’s request. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶¶ 41-43; see Plaintiff’s Separate Statement (PSS), ¶¶ 21-23.) According to Dr. Powers, the fact that Plaintiff felt pain during the treatment was not indicative of any negligence and was to be expected during therapy. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶¶ 42-43; see PSS, ¶ 22.)

UTN next relies on the expert opinion of Lawrence Miller, M.D., a licensed physician with experience in evaluating and treating neuropathic pain, complex regional pain syndrome, knee pain, orthopedic pain, and generalized pain complaints. (MSJ, Ex. 4, ¶ 3.) To a reasonable degree of medical probability, Dr. Miller states UTN did not cause or contribute to Plaintiff’s injuries or damages. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶ 41.) Dr. Miller opines that Plaintiff’s pain was caused by postoperative peripheral inflammation following her knee arthroscopy—a condition she developed prior to UTN’s treatment, based on her exhibiting “joint effusion, posterior knee tenderness, and pain during her orthopedic visit on December 27, 2021.” (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶¶ 39, 41.) 

In opposition to UTN’s motion, Plaintiff relies on the expert opinion of Philip Lim, DPT, a licensed physical therapist.[FN1] Dr. Lim opines that UTN’s therapists did not act within the applicable standard of care by (1) utilizing a massage gun in close proximity to the area of Plaintiff’s surgery for a period of longer than one or two consecutive minutes and (2) utilizing a massage gun when Dr. Lugo did not specifically request it in Plaintiff’s referral to UTN. (Dr. Lim Decl., ¶ 8, 11.) Dr. Lim further opines that the improper use of a massage gun directly caused Plaintiff to experience pain in an area of the knee that was still healing from surgery and that the type of pain experienced does not normally result from the use of a massage gun. (Dr. Lim Decl., ¶ 10.)

Based on Dr. Lim’s declaration, the Court finds a dispute of material fact exists as to whether UTN’s administration of a massage gun fell within the applicable standard of care. However, Dr. Lim fails to establish that such a breach actually caused Plaintiff’s injuries to a reasonable degree of medical probability. Although Dr. Lim states that the use of a massage gun caused Plaintiff to experience pain, Dr. Lim nowhere addresses Dr. Miller’s opinion that the cause of Plaintiff’s pain was postoperative peripheral inflammation that developed before Plaintiff visited UTN. Moreover, even assuming that the massage gun did cause Plaintiff to feel increased pain in the moment, Dr. Lim fails to connect the use of the massage gun to the broader injuries and damages alleged in the complaint. (See Compl. ¶ 39 [alleging that Plaintiff experienced the following injuries due to the use of the massage gun: physical pain and discomfort, use of cortisone injection, attending numerous doctor appointments, missing hours and days of work, incurring medical bills and costs, and emotion distress].)[FN2] His conclusory opinion that “Plaintiff’s injuries as alleged in her Complaint . . . were caused by” the use of the massage gun is insufficient to establish a causal connection between UTN’s actions and the injuries alleged in the complaint. Because Dr. Miller’s opinion remains unchallenged, Plaintiff has failed to establish the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to causation.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS UTN’s motion for summary judgment.     


[FN1] The Court grants UTN’s request to take judicial notice of the dictionary definition of “diagnosis,” pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h). As to UTN’s objection to Dr. Lim as an expert on medical causation, the Court acknowledges that physical therapists are not licensed physicians who may render medical diagnoses. (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code section 2052(a).) Even so, the Court need not decide whether Dr. Lim is qualified to opine as to medical causation, because, as explained in more detail below, his proffered opinion fails to establish that the massage gun (as opposed to Plaintiff’s underlying medical condition) caused Plaintiff’s injuries. UTN’s evidentiary objections are otherwise overruled.

[FN2] Dr. Lim’s opinion that the use of the massage gun led Plaintiff to experience pain is not in conflict with Dr. Miller’s opinion. Dr. Miller acknowledges that the use of the massage gun may have been painful but posits that the reason for the pain was “the postoperative induced peripheral inflammatory changes in the knee and developing neuropathic pain condition.” (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶ 41.)

Case Number: 23PSCV01080    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: H

Nottoli, et al. v. The Claremont Hacienda, et al., Case No. 23PSCV01080

 

ORDER ON DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

1.       Defendants Claremont Senior Living, LLC dba The Claremont Hacienda and Sunwest Bank’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend as to Claremont Senior Living, LLC dba The Claremont Hacienda. The Court will hear from counsel for Plaintiffs as to whether leave to amend is requested as to Sunwest Bank, and as to which cause(s) of action, and if so, will require an offer of proof. 

2.       Defendant Sunwest Bank’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is DENIED as MOOT.


I.        Background

Plaintiffs Betty Nottoli (“Nottoli”), in and through her Successor-In-Interest, Patricia Chiuppi, and Patricia Chiuppi (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege that while Nottoli was a resident at The Claremont Hacienda from January 2022 through April 6, 2022, she suffered multiple falls, with the last fall resulting in a hip fracture that ultimately led to her death on June 19, 2022. 

On March 25, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an “Amendment to Complaint,” wherein Sunwest Bank (“Sunwest”) was named in lieu of Doe 21. 

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting causes of action against The Claremont Hacienda, Claremont Senior Living, LLC (“Claremont Senior Living”), Sunwest, and Does 1-50 for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) breach of contract, (4) willful misconduct, and (5) wrongful death. 

The Final Status Conference is set for March 11, 2025. Trial is set for March 25, 2025. 

Pending before the Court are (1) Claremont Senior Living and Sunwest’s demurrer to the Operative Complaint and (2) Sunwest’s motion to strike portions of the Operative Complaint. For the reasons stated herein, the demurrer is sustained and the motion to strike is denied as moot. 


II.      Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

III.     Discussion

Claremont Senior Living and Sunwest (together, “Defendants”) demur, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to the first through fifth causes of action in the Operative Complaint, on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and are uncertain.

A.       Request for Judicial Notice

A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court grants Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice as to Exhibit 1 (i.e., Articles of Organization and Statements of Information filed with the California Secretary of State for Claremont Senior Living); Exhibit 2 (i.e., Statement and Designation of Out-of-State Stock Corporation and Statements of Information filed with the California Secretary of State for Sunwest); Exhibit 3 (i.e., Nottoli’s death certificate); and Exhibit 4 (i.e., the Court’s tentative ruling filed May 30, 2024 and adopted as final). With respect to Exhibit 3, however, the Court does not take judicial notice of its contents. “Taking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning.” (Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374; Bohrer v. County of San Diego (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 155, 164-165 [refusing to take judicial notice of the cause of death listed in a death certificate].)

B.       The Demurrer

Plaintiffs have alleged that Claremont Senior Living is a limited liability company that was, at all relevant times, the licensee of the Claremont Hacienda, a residential care facility for the elderly. (FAC, ¶¶ 4-5.) Plaintiffs have further alleged that Sunwest became the owner/sole member of Claremont Senior Living on November 2, 2020 “when it seized the property on a loan default” and was, at all relevant times, the licensee, owner, operator, management company, consulting company manager, and/or alter ego of the Claremont Hacienda. (Id., ¶ 6.)

Sunwest asserts that it cannot be held liable based solely on its status as Claremont Senior Living’s sole member. The Court agrees. A limited liability company is “considered a separate legal entity, distinct from its stockholders, officers and directors, with separate and distinct liabilities and obligations.” (Curci Investments, LLC v. Baldwin (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 214, 220). “While members actively participate in the management and control of the company, they have limited liability for the company’s debts and obligations to the same extent enjoyed by corporate shareholders.” (Kwok v. Transnation Title Ins. Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 1562, 1571.) The “debts, obligations, or other liabilities of a limited liability company, whether arising in contract, tort, or otherwise. . . do not become the debts, obligations, or other liabilities of a member or manager solely by reason of the member acting as a member or manager acting as a manager for the limited liability company.” (Corp. Code § 17703.04, subd. (a).)

Sunwest next argues that the Operative Complaint does not plead sufficient facts to impose liability against it on the basis that it was operating as a joint venture with Claremont Senior Living, Claremont Hacienda, and Does 1-50 to operate Claremont Hacienda. (See FAC, ¶¶ 12 and 52-58.) The Court agrees.

“A joint venture is an undertaking by two or more persons jointly to carry out a single business enterprise for profit.” (Weiner v. Fleischman (1991) 54 Cal.3d 476, 482 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) “There are three basic elements of a joint venture: the members must have joint control over the venture (even though they may delegate it), they must share the profits of the undertaking, and the members must each have an ownership interest in the enterprise.” (Simmons v. Ware (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 1035, 1049 [quotations and citation omitted]). A joint venture is “virtually the same” as a partnership and “courts freely apply partnership law to joint ventures.” (Weiner, supra, 54 Cal.3d at 482.) “A joint venture or partnership may be formed orally, or assumed to have been organized from a reasonable deduction from the acts and declarations of the parties.” (Id. at 482-483).

Plaintiffs fail to allege that Claremont Senior Living and Sunwest entered into any oral or written agreement to form a joint venture. Plaintiffs claim that Claremont Senior Living and Sunwest created a joint venture through their conduct (Opp., 6:17-18); however, they have failed to plead any facts supporting this claim. (Schmid v. City and County of San Francisco (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 470, 481 [“Bare conclusions devoid of any supporting facts . . . are insufficient to withstand demurrer”].) Plaintiffs concede that Claremont Senior Living was governed by an operating agreement that designated Sunwest as its sole member and Robert Willis as its sole manager. (Id., ¶¶ 38 and 55.) These actions, which Plaintiffs assert in their opposition constitute evidence of a joint venture implied by conduct, are actions taken by Sunwest and Willis under the aforesaid operating agreement, which does not create joint venture liability. (See Corp. Code § 17703.04, subd. (c) [“Nothing in this section shall be construed to affect the liability of a member of a limited liability company to third parties for the member’s participation in tortious conduct, or pursuant to the terms of a written guarantee or other contractual obligation entered into by the member, other than an operating agreement”].)

Plaintiffs have failed to set forth facts that Claremont Senior Living and Sunwest had “joint control” over any venture or specified how they each had an “ownership interest in the enterprise” when Sunwest was Claremont Senior Living’s sole member.

Sunwest further contends that Plaintiffs have failed to allege sufficient facts to establish that it is the “alter ego” of Claremont Hacienda, which Claremont Senior Living owns. The Court agrees. “To recover on an alter ego theory, a plaintiff. . . must allege sufficient facts to show a unity of interest and ownership, and an unjust result if the corporation is treated as the sole actor.” (Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.) “Among the factors to be considered in applying the doctrine are commingling of funds and other assets of the two entities, the holding out by one entity that it is liable for the debts of the other, identical equitable ownership in the two entities, use of the same offices and employees, and use of one as a mere shell or conduit for the affairs of the other. Other factors which have been described in the case law include inadequate capitalization, disregard of corporate formalities, lack of segregation of corporate records, and identical directors and officers.” (Id. at 538-539 [quotations and citations omitted].) Plaintiffs have failed to allege any facts showing a unity of interest or that there would be an inequitable result from treating Claremont Senior Living as the sole actor. 

Finally, Sunwest argues that Plaintiffs’ ratification claim against it fails. The Court agrees. “Ratification is essentially a matter of assent. Consequently, a principal is not bound by ratification unless he acts with knowledge of all the material facts involved in the unauthorized transaction, particularly with knowledge of the acts of the person who assumed to act as his agent.” (Bate v. Marsteller (1959) 175 Cal.App.2d 573, 582.) Here, Plaintiffs vaguely allege that “Defendants’ managing agents” ratified “acts and omissions” of unspecified persons. Plaintiffs have not pled facts reflecting that Claremont Senior Living’s employees were “authorized agents” of Sunwest, that Sunwest had knowledge of, and approved, the actions of those agents, or the basis for such ratification.

The Court sustains Sunwest’s demurrer on this basis. The Court further addresses Defendants’ demurrer to the specific causes of action below.

1.       First Cause of Action (Elder Abuse)

The elements of a cause of action for elder abuse and neglect are determined by the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (“the Act”). (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15600 et seq.) The Act affords heightened remedies to elders and dependent adults who can establish “by clear and convincing evidence that a defendant is liable for . . . neglect as defined in Section 15610.57, and that the defendant has been guilty of recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of this abuse.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15657, subd. (a).)

The Act excludes liability for acts of professional negligence; it does not apply to simple or gross negligence by health care providers. (Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 32). “[T]he Act does not apply unless the defendant health care provider had a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship, involving ongoing responsibility for one or more basic needs, with the elder patient.” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 152.) Neglect under the Act “refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations. Thus, the statutory definition of ‘neglect’ speaks not of the undertaking of medical services, but of the failure to provide medical care.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 89 [quotation marks and citation omitted].)

Further, a plaintiff must show defendant was guilty of “‘recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of [physical, neglectful, or financial elder abuse.]’” (Benun v. Superior Court (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 113, 119 [citation omitted].) Recklessness “refers to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a ‘deliberate disregard’ of the ‘high degree of probability’ that an injury will occur.” (Delaney, supra, 20 Cal.4th at 31.) Oppression, fraud and malice involve intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature. (Id.) A plaintiff must also show that an employer “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice” and, “[w]ith respect to a corporate employer, [that the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (b); Welf & Inst. Code, 15657, subd. (c).)

Several factors, then, “must be present for conduct to constitute neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act and thereby trigger the enhanced remedies available under the Act. The plaintiff must allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) facts establishing that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult's basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness). The plaintiff must also allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) that the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering. Finally, the facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury must be pleaded with particularity, in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 406-407 [quotation marks and citations omitted].)

Defendants contend that Plaintiffs failed to plead a cause of action for elder abuse. The Court agrees. Plaintiffs have failed to plead specific facts establishing a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship between Sunwest and Nottoli. Plaintiffs have failed to explain how Sunwest qualifies as a health care provider or had custody over Nottoli as a member of Claremont Senior Living. Plaintiffs have failed to provide any facts indicating any contact between Sunwest and Nottoli, let alone any specific services, procedures, or care provided by Sunwest to Nottoli.

Plaintiffs do not allege any specific acts attributable to Sunwest or Claremont Senior Living and instead only vaguely allege that “Defendants” failed to “exercise the degree of care necessary to prevent” Nottoli from falling in March 2022 and again in April 2022. (FAC, ¶ 70.) Plaintiffs, however, concede that both falls were “unwitnessed” and that “the circumstances surrounding the [second] fall [are] unknown.” (Id. ¶¶ 24-25.) Plaintiffs also assert that Defendants failed to employ adequately trained staff (id., ¶ 45), yet fail to allege how any such failure caused Nottoli’s harm: “Absent specific facts indicating at least recklessness, any failure to provide adequate supervision would constitute professional negligence but not elder abuse.” (Worsham v. O’Connor Hospital (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 331, 338.)

 Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained on this basis.

          2.       Second, Fourth, and Fifth Causes of Action

Defendants next demur to the second, fourth, and fifth causes of action (i.e. negligence, willful misconduct, and wrongful death, respectively).

Turning to the second cause of action, “[t]he following elements must be pleaded to state a cause of action for negligence: (1) a legal duty of care toward the plaintiff; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) legal causation; and (4) damages.” (Century Surety Co. v. Crosby Insurance, Inc. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 116, 127.) Ordinarily, negligence may be alleged in general terms . . .but there are limits to the generality with which a plaintiff is permitted to state his cause of action, and ... the plaintiff must indicate the acts or omissions which are said to have been negligently performed. He may not recover upon the bare statement that the defendant's negligence has caused him injury.” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527.)

As to the fourth cause of action, willful misconduct “is not a separate tort, but simply an aggravated form of negligence, differing in quality rather degree from ordinary lack of care.” (Id. at 526 [quotations and citation omitted].)

Finally, the elements of the fifth cause of action (wrongful death) “are the tort (negligence or other wrongful act), the resulting death, and the damages.” (Holland v. Silverscreen Healthcare, Inc. (2024) 101 Cal.App.5th 1125, 1132.)

Again, Plaintiffs fail to allege facts establishing how Sunwest, as an alleged member of Claremont Senior Living, owed a legal duty of care to Nottoli. Plaintiffs do not allege any facts establishing that Sunwest had any contact with Nottoli. Further, Plaintiffs have failed to plead causation as against Defendants.

Defendants’ demurrer to the second, fourth, and fifth causes of action is sustained on this basis.

3.       Third Cause of Action (Breach of Contract)

“To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a party must plead the existence of a contract, his or her performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, the defendant’s breach and resulting damage.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)

“If the action is based on an alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written instrument must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 459.)

Plaintiffs have alleged that they entered into a contract with undefined “FACILITY DEFENDANTS.” (FAC, ¶ 86.) Plaintiffs have failed to identify whether the contract was written, oral, or implied by conduct, have failed to adequately identify the parties to the contract, have failed to state when the contract was entered into, and have failed to plead the existence of the contract terms.

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained on this basis.

          C.       The Motion to Strike

The Court denies Sunwest’s motion to strike as moot, based upon the ruling made on the demurrer.

Anakwenze v. Villa, et al.,
Case No. 24PSCV00253



ORDER ON DEMURRER 

Defendants Yeira Lisbeth Villa
and Cuberto Munoz’s Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, without
leave to amend.



I.        Background

Plaintiff
Henrietta E. Anakwenze (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she sustained injuries and
damages in a January 20, 2022 motor vehicle accident.

On
August 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (the “Operative
Complaint” or “SAC”), asserting a motor vehicle cause of action against Yeira
Lisbeth Villa (“Villa”), Cuberto Munoz (together, “Defendants”), and Does 1-10.

Pending
before the Court is Defendants’ demurrer to the Operative Complaint. For the
reasons stated herein, the demurrer is sustained, with no leave to amend.

II.      Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading,
inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action
and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).)

“A demurrer tests the
pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it
lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are
judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d
902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only
allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis
for the claim against him. (Semole v.
Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however,
admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the
pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts
impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take
judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co.
v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

  



III.     Discussion 

Defendants
demur, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to
the Operative Complaint on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to
constitute causes of action and is uncertain.

Defendants
assert that the Operative Complaint is barred by the two-year statute of
limitations set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1, because
Plaintiff filed her original complaint on January 24, 2024 and the date of
injury alleged is January 20, 2022. (SAC, ¶ MV-1.) Indeed, “[a] complaint
showing on its face the cause of action is barred by the statute of limitations
is subject to general demurrer.” (Iverson, Yoakum, Papiano & Hatch v.
Berwald (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 990, 995.) 

“Where a complaint does
not reveal on its face that it is barred by the statute of limitations, a
plaintiff has no obligation to plead around the defense.”  (JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. v. Ward
(2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 678, 688.) It follows, then, that a plaintiff is
obligated to plead around the defense when the complaint reveals on its face
that it is time-barred.

Plaintiff has alleged,
in relevant part, that: “[o]n or about 1/20/22, the defendants who upon
information and belief are out of state residents and who upon further
information and belief were not engaged in interstate commerce, operated their
motor vehicle in violation of CA Vehicle Code Section 22350 on 210 Freeway West
Bound Near Big Dalton Wash, thereby resulting [in] the damages alleged herein.”
(SAC, ¶ MV-1.) 

Plaintiff argues that
the statute of limitations was tolled pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure
section 351 (i.e., “[i]f, when the cause of action accrues against a person, he
is out of the State, the action may be commenced within the term herein limited,
after his return to the State, and if, after the cause of action accrues, he
departs from the State, the time of his absence is not part of the time limited
for the commencement of the action”). 

Defendants, in turn,
argue that section 351 is not applicable where a motor vehicle accident is at
issue. The Court agrees. In Bigelow v. Smik (1970) 6 Cal.App.3d 10, the
plaintiffs’ action to recover personal injury damages against a nonresident
motorist was dismissed for failure to bring suit within the then-applicable
one-year statute of limitations. In that case, the parties were involved in a
car accident in Los Angeles on August 20, 1963; the defendant, a resident of
Ohio, left California a few days later and did not return. The plaintiffs then
sued the defendant on October 8, 1964 and thereafter served him with process in
the manner provided for service on a nonresident motorist. The Second District
Court of Appeal affirmed, concluding that “since a nonresident is amenable to
service of process within the state and to the entry of personal judgment
against him, the reason for section 351 is not present, the section does not
apply, and the period of limitation for connecting suit against him does not suspend.”
(Id. at 15.)

Additionally, in Litwin
v. Estate of Formela (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 607, the plaintiffs’ personal
injury action against the estate of a deceased German citizen arising out of a
car accident was dismissed following the sustaining of a demurrer on statute of
limitations grounds. The Third District Court of Appeal affirmed, determining
that section 351’s tolling provision did not apply to claims arising from motor
vehicle accidents involving nonresident motorists.

Plaintiff cites to Dew v. Appleberry
(1979) 23 Cal.3d 630, where the California Supreme Court observed that while substitute
service provisions may reduce the need for section 351, those provisions did
not repeal section 351 such that the tort action at issue would be tolled
during the period of the defendant’s absence from the state. (Id. at
634-635 [“Although [section 351] itself remains unchanged from the date of
enactment, the Legislature is clearly aware of the statute’s broad
ramifications, and has modified the reach of the rule in
appropriate circumstances. Thus when substituted or constructive service
of process upon a defendant resident motorist is available in ‘a cause of
action arising in this state out of his operation of a motor vehicle anywhere
within this state’ (Veh. Code, § 17460), ‘(n)otwithstanding any provisions
of Section 351 of the Code of Civil Procedure to the contrary. . . the
time of his absence from this State is part of the time limited for the
commencement of the action . . . .’ (id., § 17463).”].) However, the Litwin
court expressly reaffirmed that Bigelow “remains good law” and that Dew did not implicitly
overrule Bigelow, explaining that Dew was a premises
liability case, not a motor vehicle case, and that “Bigelow was
decided not on mere amenability to service, but on amenability to service in
the specific context of motor vehicle accidents in a mobile society and the
policy that nonresidents and residents should be on the same footing in the
litigation of accidents growing out of the use of the highways in this state.” (Litwin, supra, 186
Cal.App.4th at 617-618.)

Defendants’
demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

Case Number: 24PSCV01758    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: H

Yao, et al. v. Yang, Case No. 24PSCV01758

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

Defendant Sandra DeLeon’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

Background

Plaintiffs Mingwen Yao (“Yao”) and Worldyao Group (“Group”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

Yao is the majority shareholder of Group. On August 10, 2020, Yao and Qihui Yang (“Yang”) entered into a written agreement, in which Yang agreed to invest $30,000.00 in Group for a four-month period in exchange for $15,000.00 in investment profits. After Yao failed to produce any profits or return Yang’s original investment, Yang sued Yao and Group. On February 23, 2022, Yang and Yao entered into a confidential settlement agreement, which contained a non-disparagement clause. The settlement agreement is usurious. Yang brought a motion to enforce the settlement agreement after Yao defaulted in making payments due thereunder. Yang disparaged Plaintiffs in the community. Yang then sued Plaintiffs in Case No. 22PSCV01615. Yang was able to obtain a default judgment against Plaintiffs in that case because Plaintiffs were defrauded by James Lee (“Lee”) and his associate, Sandra DeLeon (“DeLeon”), who falsely represented to Plaintiffs that they were attorneys and would defend them in the lawsuit.

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC” or the “Operative Complaint”), asserting causes of action against Yang, DeLeon, Lee, and Does 1-50 for (1) breach of contract, (2) usury, (3) equitable relief to set aside default, and (4) fraud.

On September 5, 2024, Yang’s default was entered.

Pending before the Court is DeLeon’s demurrer to the Operative Complaint. For the reasons stated herein, the demurrer is overruled.

 

Legal Standard

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that, inter alia, the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

 

Discussion

DeLeon demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e), to the fourth cause of action in the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

At the outset, the Court declines to consider the information contained in DeLeon’s first accompanying declaration as improper on demurrer.

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)

“Fraud must be pleaded with specificity…[t]o withstand a demurrer, the facts constituting every element of the fraud must be alleged with particularity, and the claim cannot be salvaged by references to the general policy favoring the liberal construction of pleadings.” (Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782 [emphasis in original].) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73 [quotations and citations omitted; emphasis in original].)

The Court determines that Plaintiffs have sufficiently pled their cause of action for fraud against DeLeon. Plaintiffs have alleged that a default judgment was entered against them in Case No. 22PSCV01615 because they “were defrauded by an individual who held himself out to be a licensed attorney, LEE, and his associate DELEON,” that “LEE and DELEON represented to Plaintiffs that they were attorneys and would competently defend YAO and GROUP in the lawsuit,” that this representation occurred “[i]n or around November 2022, when Plaintiffs consulted with LEE and DELEON regarding the lawsuit,” that Lee and DeLeon intended that Plaintiffs rely on their representation so that Plaintiffs would pay them a retainer fee, that Plaintiffs reasonably relied on the representation because Lee and DeLeon were located in a “legitimate law office space” when Plaintiffs consulted with them and had no other reason to believe otherwise, that afterwards, Plaintiffs discovered that these individuals were not attorneys and had tricked him into paying a $2,000.00 retainer without filing any responsive pleading for them” and that Plaintiffs were damaged as a result of the actions of Lee and DeLeon. (FAC, ¶¶ 14 and 28-32.)

The Court will ignore DeLeon’s attempt to interject purported facts that are extraneous to the face of the complaint as improper on demurrer. Additionally, DeLeon’s argument regarding the propriety of punitive damages should have been raised via a separately filed motion to strike, which was not done.

The Court overrules DeLeon’s demurrer.
