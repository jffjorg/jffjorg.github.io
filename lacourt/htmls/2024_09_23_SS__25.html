Case Number: 22NWLC23020    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Monday, September 23, 2024

Case Name:                             CREDITORS ADJUSTMENT BUREAU, INC. v. THE COAL MINER’S SON, LLC aka COAL MINERS SON LLC dba PARALLEL PIZZA dba BUTTERMILK dba BUTTERMILK FRIED CHICKEN, et al.

Case No.:                                22NWLC23020

Motions:                                 Motion for Terminating Sanctions Against James Nunn, Striking Defendants’ Answer and Entering Default; Request for Monetary Sanctions in the Amount of $1,573.75

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc.

Responding Party:                   None

Notice:                                    OK

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Plaintiff’s Motion is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART as follows:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions against Defendant James Nunn (aka James Nunn Jr aka James J Nunn aka James J Nunn Jr aka James Joseph Nunn aka Joseph James Nunn aka Joseph Nunn) is GRANTED. The Answer filed on August 29, 2024, is stricken as to defendant James Nunn only, and a default is entered as against the defendant James Nunn only on this date.

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions against the Defendant is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

           

            On September 28, 2022, Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants (1) The Coal Miner’s Son, LLC aka Coal Miners Son LLC dba Parallel Pizza adba Buttermilk adba Buttermilk Fried Chicken (“COAL”), (2) James Nunn aka James Nunn Jr aka James J Nunn aka James J Nunn Jr aka James Joseph Nunn aka Joseph James Nunn aka Joseph Nunn (“Nunn”), and (3) Does 1 through 10, inclusive, asserting causes of action for (1) open book account, (2) account stated, (3) reasonable value, (4) breach of contract, and (5) personal guaranty.

 

            The Complaint alleges the following, among other things. Four years before the commencement of this action, the defendants became indebted to “Plaintiff’s Assignor” (the American Textile Maintenance Company dba Republic Master Chefs) in the amount of $6,927.53 for goods the Assignor sold and delivered and/or services the Assignor rendered to the defendants. Compl., ¶¶ 1, 2, 13. “Prior to the commencement of this action, the claims herein sued upon were assigned to Plaintiff by Plaintiff’s Assignor for collection purposes ….” Compl., ¶ 4.

 

            On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the motions seeking orders (1) compelling COAL and Nunn (collectively, “Defendants”) to serve their initial responses to Plaintiff’s first set of demands for inspection, and (2) imposing sanctions against Defendants and their counsel of record, Peter C. Freeman, Esq.

 

            On May 31, 2024, Defendants filed a joint opposition to the motions.

 

            On June 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed its reply briefs.

 

            On June 13, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motions to compel the Defendants’ initial responses to Plaintiff’s demands for inspection (the “June 13 Order”). The Court ordered Defendants to (1) serve code-compliant responses to the demands within 30 days of the ruling and (2) pay Plaintiff $672 as sanctions.

 

            On July 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions against Defendant Nunn.

 

            As of September 19, 2024, no opposition to or reply in support of the motion has been filed.

 

            A non-jury trial is set for October 28, 2024.

 

MOVING PARTY’S POSITION

 

Defendant Nunn has not complied with any aspect of the Court’s June 13 Order (i.e., he has neither produced the discovery responses he was ordered to serve nor paid the court-ordered sanctions). The discovery at issue is essential to the prosecution of this action. Therefore, the Court should enter terminating sanctions against Defendant Nunn by striking his Answer and entering default against the defendant. The Court should also enter an order imposing sanctions of $1,573.75 against the defendant.

 

OPPOSITION

 

            Non.

 

REPLY

 

            None.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

 

A.        Legal Standard

If a party engages in the misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions. Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030. “Misuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. ¶ …. ¶ ….  ¶ (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (a)(1).

 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering [1] the totality of the circumstances:  [2] [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; [3] the detriment to the propounding party; and [4] the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390 (“Los Defensores”).

 

“Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 (citation omitted). “Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.” Ibid.

 

“[D]iscovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992 (“Doppes”).

 

“‘Discovery sanctions “should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.”’ [Citations.] If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.” Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.

 

B.        Discussion

 

Plaintiff argues that because Nunn has disobeyed the June 13 Order by failing to serve discovery responses and pay sanctions, the Court should impose terminating and monetary sanctions against Nunn. Plaintiff’s counsel attests to the following facts in support of the motion for terminating sanctions. Plaintiff’s Assignor and COAL entered into an agreement whereby the Assignor would deliver, or cycle clean dirty linens, and COAL would make payments for those services. Motion, Declaration of Tiffanie Brown (“Brown Decl.”), ¶ 2. Defendant Nunn, the purported officer or owner of COAL, personally guaranteed those payments. Brown Decl., ¶ 2. However, the Defendants defaulted on the payments, prompting Plaintiff to file this lawsuit. Brown Decl., ¶ 2.

 

In its June 13 Order, the Court ordered Nunn, within 30 days of the ruling, to (1) serve code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s First Demand for Production and Inspection of Documents and Other Tangible Things and (2) pay sanctions of $672 to Plaintiff. Brown Decl., ¶ 4. “To date, defendant has failed to comply with any aspect of the Court’s order. Defendant has not produced any additional responses to discovery nor have the sanctions been paid, even though the court’s deadline of July 13, 2024, has since expired.” Brown Decl., ¶ 7.

 

            However, “[a] terminating sanction issued solely because of a failure to pay a monetary discovery sanction is never justified.” Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615.

            In addition, the Court cannot impose monetary sanctions against Nunn solely to punish him for disobeying the June 13 Order. See Kwan Software Engineering, Inc. v. Hennings (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 57, 75 (“discovery sanctions may not be imposed as a punishment. ‘Courts have continued to uphold the principle ... that sanctions may not be imposed solely to punish the offending party.’ [Citations]”).

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s request to impose additional sanctions of $1,573.75 against Nunn is denied

 

Notwithstanding the above, the Court finds terminating sanctions proper based on Nunn’s failure to serve court-ordered discovery responses constitutes misuse of the discovery process. Indeed, one of the discovery requests at issue asked Nunn to produce the following documents: “The original and/or a legible copy of any and all WRITINGS or other written documents or records of any sort which YOU intend to utilize as a basis or ground for any defense in this action which are not privileged.” Minute Order dated June 13, 2024, p. 6 (emphasis added). The demand is relevant because Nunn filed an Answer listing 17 affirmative defenses and, therefore, Plaintiff has a right to know if there are any documents that support those affirmative defenses. See Answer, filed on August 29, 2023, pp. 2:8-5:5.

 

It appears from court records that Defendant Nunn is represented by Freeman & Associates ALC, the same counsel for Defendant COAL. Yet, no opposition has been filed (1) explaining the reason Nunn has failed to comply with the June 13 Order or (2) informing the Court that Nunn intends to comply with the Order. Given that trial is next month, and Plaintiff will be prejudiced if trial proceeds without receiving the discovery at issue, the Court is inclined to issue terminating sanctions.

 

Therefore, the request for terminating sanctions against Nunn is granted.

 

II.        CONCLUSION  

 

The Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART as follows:

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Terminating Sanctions against Defendant James Nunn (aka James Nunn Jr aka James J Nunn aka James J Nunn Jr aka James Joseph Nunn aka Joseph James Nunn aka Joseph Nunn) is GRANTED. The Answer filed on August 29, 2024, is stricken as to defendant James Nunn only, and Default is entered against Defendant James Nunn only, on this date.

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions against the defendant is DENIED.

 

The Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On March 1, 2023, Plaintiff Michael Long (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against PACMAD, LLC. (PACMAD); AAN ENTERPRISES, INC.; and Amy Tsao (Tsao) for lack of access to public accommodations when he was unable to sit at outdoor dining tables because they were not wheelchair accessible. A trial date was set for August 28, 2024.

 

On June 14, 2023, Defendant filed an answer to the complaint with thirteen affirmative defenses.

 

On August 27, 2024, PACMAD filed a Motion to Dismiss and for sanctions on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff violated the court’s Third Amended Standing order by refusing to meet and confer on pre-trial matters and (2) Plaintiff failed to appear/answer on the court ordered trial date of August 28, 2024.

 

On August 28, 2024, counsel for Plaintiff made an oral request for a continuance and the counsel for Defendant opposed the motion. The hearing for the Motion to Dismiss was continued to September 23, 2024. The trial was also postponed to October 14, 2024.

 

On September 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Trial Continuance

 

An assigned trial date is firm (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332.) A party seeking a continuance must make the motion or ex parte application as soon as reasonably practicable once the need is discovered (Ibid.) Continuances are available upon an affirmative showing of good cause, including but not limited to the unavailability of a party because of death, illness or other excusable circumstances (Ibid.) “A court must consider all facts and circumstances that are relevant to the determination”, including but not limited to: (1) the proximity of the trial date; (2) any previous extensions or delays; (3) the length of the continuance requested; … (5) prejudice to the parties; (10) whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance; and (11) any other fact or circumstance (Ibid.)

 

The third amended standing order includes specific instructions for stipulating to continuing the trial, including that trial may be postponed if all attorneys of record stipulate in writing to a specific date.

 

PACMAD argues that Plaintiff knew they would be unavailable for trial as early as July 15, 2024 and informed PACMAD’s counsel that they would be unavailable because Plaintiff would be on a cruise (Avila Decl., Exhibit 2.) PACMAD’s counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that they should make a request for continuance as soon as reasonably practical and that simply waiting until the actual trial date to request the continuance was improper (Avila Decl., ¶ 8; Exhibit 2.) PACMAD argues that Plaintiff’s conduct constitutes a willful disregard of the court’s order and the rules of court.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff responds that his vacation had been planned prior to the trial date and that a vacation is within the “excusable circumstances” in Rule 3.1332(c)(2). Plaintiff concedes they spoke with PACMAD about their need for a continuance on July 15, but that they did not seek a continuance with the court (Mehrban Decl., ¶¶ 2, 3.) Plaintiff argues that they did not file for a continuance to decrease attorneys’ fees, fees that would be borne by PACMAD, if Plaintiff prevails (Mehrban Decl., ¶5.) Plaintiff highlights that continuances are frequently granted in limited civil court, and that it is highly likely that the continuance would have been granted had they filed a request. (Mehrban Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Plaintiff claims that they planned the vacation prior to the filing of the action in March of 2023, giving the Plaintiff more than a year to file for a continuance (Long Decl., ¶ 1.)  Plaintiff’s arguments that the vacation was planned prior to the filing of the action, that no previous continuances had been requested, the short time frame needed for the continuance, and the lack of prejudice to the defendants provide a reasonable circumstance for the court to grant a continuance.

 

Meet and Confer

           

PACMAD requests dismissal and sanctions for Plaintiff’s failure to meet and confer before the set trial date. Under Code Civ. Proc. § 581, failure to meet and confer is not a proper basis for dismissal.

 

PACMAD reached out to counsel for Plaintiff to schedule the required 20-day meet and confer (Avila Decl., ¶¶ 8, 9.) Plaintiff’s counsel refused to participate, PACMAD alleges that in response, Plaintiff’s counsel responded, “if you are going to make a motion to dismiss, then I’m not going to waste my time preparing a joint witness list or exhibit list” (Avila Decl., ¶ 8.) PACMAD attempted to reach out to Plaintiff about the meet and confer several times after that communication to either have Plaintiff file for a continuance or participate in the meet and confer (Avila Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

Plaintiff argues that since they had notified PACMAD that they intended to request a continuance that the meet and confer was premature, and therefore they did not have to meet the requirement.

 

Dismissal

           

            Generally, California law favors tying cases on their merits, it outlines a few instances in which dismissal is appropriate in Code Civ. Proc. § 581. The Court may dismiss an action “when either party fails to appear on the trial and the other party appears and asks for dismissal” (Code Civ. Proc. § 581(b)(5).) Additionally, the court shall dismiss the complaint with prejudice if the plaintiff abandons it (Code Civ. Proc. § 581(d).) Court’s may dismiss a case as a sanction, but dismissal is “to be employed on in the rarest of circumstances” (Guardianship of A.H. (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 155, 160-61.)

 

In civil cases, “personal appearance by a party is not essential; appearance by an attorney is sufficient and equally effective” (Cohen v. Hugest Markets, Inc. (1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 1693, 1700.)

 

PACMAD argues that the motion to dismiss should be granted because the plaintiff abandoned the litigation, the party failed to appear at trial, or under CCP § 581(m) which allows dismissal for other reasons. The motion was filed before the trial date. Plaintiff’s attorney did appear, as they indicated to PACMAD, to request a continuance. Since Plaintiff’s attorney did appear at trial to request a continuance and personal appearance by a party is not required, there is no basis for the motion to dismiss. In this case, the motion to dismiss based on alternative circumstances would be counter to the Court’s preference to hear cases on the merits.

 

Sanctions

           

Sanctions may be awarded for failure to comply with the rules of court without good cause (Cal Rules of Court, rule 2.30(b).) “Rule 2.30 does not authorize full compensation of all attorney fees incurred as a result of a rules violation, but only authorizes the court to award reasonable attorney fees incurred in connection with the proceedings in which the aggrieved parry seeks sanctions” (Sino Century Development Limited v. Farley (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 688, 691.)

           

            Defendant requests sanctions in response to Plaintiffs: (1) scheduling of a cruise for the date set for trial; (2) failure to comply with the meet and confer requirement; (3) failure to file for a continuance.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff alleges that the cruise was scheduled before the complaint was filed (Long Decl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff also alleges that they had good cause for failing to comply with the meet and confer and failure to file for the continuance. Plaintiff’s attorney argues that they did not file for a continuance to decrease the total attorneys’ fees awarded in the case that Plaintiff prevails. Additionally, Plaintiff argues that meeting and conferring about exhibit and witness lists would have been premature or a waste of resources.

 

            The Court finds that sanctions are not appropriate under the circumstances presented. Plaintiff’s counsel is reminded to follow the rules of the Cal. Rules of Court for trial continuances, as well as meet and confer requirements under the Third Amended General Order.  Since Plaintiff’s counsel notified Defense counsel of his intention to request a continuance on the trial date, request for sanctions is DENIED.   

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

            For the foregoing reasons, Defendant PACMAD, LLC.’s Motion to Dismiss Plaintiff’s Complaint is DENIED.   Defendant’s Request for Sanctions is DENIED.  

 

The Moving Party is ordered to give notice.
