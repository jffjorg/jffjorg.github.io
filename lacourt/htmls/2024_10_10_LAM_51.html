DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 21STCV37652    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:   October 10, 2024                                           

 

CASE NAME:           Arleen M. Asadoorian v. General Motors, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                21STCV37652

 

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DISMISSAL UNDER CCP § 473(b)

 

MOVING PARTY:  Plaintiff Arleen M. Asadoorian

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant General Motors LLC

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      An Order setting aside the June 17, 2024 dismissal.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Motion to Set Aside the June 17, 2024 dismissal is GRANTED;

2.      The June 17, 2024 order dismissing the matter is VACATED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On October 13, 2021, Plaintiff Arleen M. Asadoorian (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Defendant General Motors, LLC with six causes of action for: (1) violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(D); (2) violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(B); (3) violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(A)(3); (4) breach of express written warranty; (5) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability; and (6) fraud by omission.

 

According to the Complaint, Plaintiff purchased a Chevrolet Cruze in February 2011 which contained or developed defects. The Subject Vehicle required multiple repairs, but Defendant failed to replace the vehicle.

 

On November 17, 2021, Defendant filed a demurrer with motion to strike.

 

On February 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC).

 

On March 10, 2022, Defendant filed a demurrer to the FAC with motion to strike which the court SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

On July 25, 2022, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On May 17, 2023, the parties stipulated to continue trial and all related dates.

 

On June 12, 2024, the court held a Final Status Conference. Plaintiff did not appear. The court continued the Final Status Conference to June 17, 2024.

 

On June 17, 2024, the court held the continued Final Status Conference. Plaintiff appeared telephonically but did not respond when the court called the matter. The court called the matter for Trial at 10:28 a.m. Plaintiff did not respond. On the court’s own motion, the court dismissed the matter without prejudice pursuant to CCP § 581(b)(5).

 

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff moved ex parte for an Order setting aside the dismissal which the court DENIED.

 

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion. On September 26, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. Replies were due on or before October 3, 2024. As of October 7, 2024, the court has not received a reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect…¿ [The application]¿shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿ (CCP¿§¿473(b).) Courts must grant relief when the attorney’s inexcusable neglect results in the dismissal. (Ibid.) Otherwise, relief under this section is discretionary. (Ibid.) This section also applies to orders of dismissal resulting from voluntary settlement agreements. (Zamora v. Clayborn Contracting Group, Inc. (2002) 28 Cal.4th 249, 255 (Zamora) [internal citations omitted].)  

¿ 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff contends that relief is warranted because counsel mistakenly believed their request for remote appearance had been granted, that they had enough time to appear after their 8:30 a.m. hearing, and ultimately failed to appear because they could not hear the court when they logged in remotely for the 10:00 a.m. Jury Trial hearing. Defendant argues relief is not warranted because Plaintiff counsel was not prepared for trial, not mistakenly appeared telephonically.

 

CCP § 473(b) provides that when an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of dismissal and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, the court shall set aside a dismissal entered against the attorney’s client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) “The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney's affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties.”¿ 

¿ 

Where an “attorney¿affidavit of fault” is filed, there is no requirement that the¿attorney's¿mistake, inadvertence, or neglect be excusable. Relief¿must¿be granted even where the default or dismissal resulted from¿inexcusable¿neglect by defendant's¿attorney. (Robert E. Weil, et al., California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial ¶ 5:495 (2018).)¿ The mandatory relief provision applies to “those dismissals that are procedurally equivalent to a default.” (Jackson v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals, Inc. (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 166, 174 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

 

Here, mandatory relief is warranted. First, Plaintiff’s request to set aside is timely because it was made within six months after entry of dismissal. Second, the request has three attorney affidavits indicating their own mistake, namely, miscalendaring the Jury Trial as a hearing allowing remote appearance.[1] (Avelino Decl. ¶¶ 3, 7, 8.) Indeed, the hearing was a Final Status Conference until Plaintiff kept failing to respond when the court repeatedly called the matter. (June 17, 2024 Minute Order.) Ultimately, counsel’s mistake resulted in the dismissal.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to set aside the June 17, 2024 dismissal.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

1.      Motion to Set Aside the June 17, 2024 dismissal is GRANTED;

2.      The June 17, 2024 order dismissing the matter is VACATED.

If Plaintiff is a prevailing party, Plaintiff may not recover costs related to dismissal and motion to vacate since they are not reasonably related to prosecution of claim. Trial setting conference to follow hearing.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.
