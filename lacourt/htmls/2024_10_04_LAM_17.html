DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV01605    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 17

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENATIVE RULING

 

SKECHERS USA, INC.

 

         vs.

 

CONTINENTAL CASUALTY COMPANY, et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV01605

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 4, 2024

           

 

Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend is GRANTED.

 

On 1/25/2023, Plaintiff Skechers USA, Inc. (Plaintiff) filed suit against Continental Casualty Company, Scottsdale Insurance Company (Continental), Columbia Casualty Company (Columbia), Lexington Insurance Company, Great American Assurance company, and National Fire & Marine Insurance Company, alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) tortious breach of contract; and (3) unfair competition.

 

            On 7/17/2024, Plaintiff moved for leave to amend its prior discovery admissions.

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

Discussion

           

Plaintiff seeks leave to amend its discovery responses provided in response to Great American’s Request for Admission No. 20 and Scottsdale’s Request For Admission Nos. 2-5, 10-14, and 22-26.

 

Plaintiff contends that the responses it provided were inaccurate and the result of an honest mistake. Plaintiff further argues:

 

Notably, there was nothing improper about Skechers’ service of amended discovery responses to clarify certain case facts relating to which insurer(s) have indemnity liability for the settlement of the underlying Converse action. Moreover, there was no strategic advantage to Skechers in doing so. Indeed, Skechers has no reason to prefer payment by one insurer over another; i.e., money is fungible regardless of which insurer must pay. Skechers amended its discovery responses solely for purposes of factual accuracy because the truth matters.

 

            (Motion, 1: 10-14.)

 

            Plaintiff’s motion sets forth a detailed explanation of how the mistake was made. To summarize, in or about October 2023, Skechers’ E-Commerce Merchandising Manager Timothy Lakin performed a search of Skechers’ legacy administrative system called Skechers Web Admin (the Legacy System) to ascertain the dates when specific Skechers footware products were first added to Sketchers.com. To conduct the search, Mr. Lakin searched the style numbers of each of the foregoing Accused Products in the Legacy System. When he typically runs a search on the Legacy System, the search results indicate when certain Skechers products went “live” on Skechers’ domestic e-commerce website Skechers.com in the “Date Available” field. (Id.) Here, his search yielded results indicating the date that each of the Accused Products was first “available” was as set forth above. (Id.)

 

            However, during trial preparation efforts in late May 2024, Skechers checked the dates on which each of the Accused Products was (1) first imported into the United States and (2) first sold on Skechers’ domestic e-commerce website Skechers.com. This search yielded different results. They discovered that “[w]hile a search results on the Legacy System for more recent Skechers products indicate when certain Skechers products went “live” on Skechers’ domestic e-commerce website Skechers.com, here because the Accused Products are more than ten (10) years old the search results have a different meaning.” (Motion, 4: 19-22.)

 

A party may amend an admission in response to a request for admission with leave of court. (CCP § 2033.300 (a).) Amendment should be permitted where: (1) “the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect”; and (2) “the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining [its] . . . defense on the merits.” (CCP § 2033.300(b).) Where a party learns of new facts contradicting a prior admission, leave to amend should be granted so long as the amendment would not substantially prejudice the party who obtained the admission. (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2008) 168 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1418.) The policy favoring leave to amend is so strong that it is an abuse of discretion to deny an amendment unless the adverse party can show meaningful prejudice. (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.)

 

Here, after review, the Court is persuaded that the initial incorrect admissions were the results of reasonable mistake, and that meaningful prejudice will not result if Plaintiff is granted leave to amend its responses. (New Albertsons, supra, at p. 1218.) Great American was dismissed from this case on January 5, 2024 and thus would not be prejudiced by any amendment. Scottsdale has not relied on Skechers’ mistaken admissions in connection with its pending motion for summary judgment/adjudication. The Court has continued trial to February 2, 2025. Accordingly, defendants have ample time to conduct further discovery relating to the subject matter of Skechers’ admissions to the extent any such discovery is warranted.

 

As a final note, Scottsdale and Lexington filed notices of conditional non-opposition. As for Lexington, Lexington lacks standing to oppose the Motion given that it is not the “party who obtained the admission” under Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.300(b) (instead, Great American and Scottsdale obtained the admissions). Moreover, Lexington is already pursuing the requested discovery discussed as a condition of its non-opposition, and Skechers has stipulated to shorten time for hearing on an amended motion for summary judgment by Lexington. As a result, the stated condition is moot.

 

As for Scottsdale, it asks for leave to amend to allow Scottsdale to amend its pending motion for summary judgment (the MSJ), on the grounds that “[i]f the Court grants Skechers leave to amend its prior admissions to admit that its first publication/advertisement of the accused products was not earlier than November 22, 2012, meaning more than two and half months after the end of the SIC policy period, it will be conclusively established by Skechers’ amended admission that there was no occurrence of an offense during the SIC policy period.” (See Limited Opp.)

 

However, discovery first learned in the course of this action is not relevant to whether or not an earlier coverage denial was proper. As noted by Plaintiff in reply, “[h]ere, Scottsdale does not contend that it knew of any facts conclusively negating its duty to defend based upon the PPE at the time it denied coverage back in 2015. In fact, at the time Scottsdale denied coverage, it did not do so based upon the supposed applicability of the PPE and never raised or mentioned the PPE in wrongfully denying its duty to defend. Accordingly, Skechers’ discovery responses in this action and all other subsequently developed “evidence” cited by Scottsdale in its pending MSJ have no bearing on its duty to defend and do not merit an amended MSJ on that issue.” (Reply, 2: 13-19). The Court agrees and thus finds no need for Scottsdale to amend its MSJ at this time.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend is granted.

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: 24STCV11209    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENATIVE RULING

 

CALEX ENGINEERING CO.

 

         vs.

 

WENDELL BENIGA, et al.

 

 Case No.:  24STCV11209

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 4, 2024

 

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED, WITH 15 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

On 5/30/2024, Plaintiff Calex Engineering Co. filed suit against Wendell Beniga and Ironmark Earthworks, Inc. alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) breach of fiduciary duty; (3) aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duty; (5) inducing breach of contract; (6) misappropriation of trade secrets; (7) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200; (8) intentional interference with economic relations; (9) negligent interference with economic relations; and (10) violations of Comprehensive Computer Data Access and Fraud Act.

 

            On 6/25/2024, Defendant Ironmark Earthworks, Inc. (Defendant) demurred to Plaintiff’s tenth cause of action.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to state a claim for Violation of Comprehensive Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (Cal. Penal Code § 502), and arguing that’s the claim is vague and ambiguous.

 

The Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (CDAFA) is set forth in Cal. Penal Code § 502, and “prohibits unauthorized access to customers, computer systems, and computer networks, and provides for a civil remedy in the form of compensatory damages, injunctive relief, and other equitable relief.” (Satmodo, LLC v. Whenever Commc’ns, LLC, No. 17-cv0192-AJB-NLS, 2017 WL 1365839, at *6 (S.D. Cal. Apr. 14, 2017). Section 502 is considered an “anti-hacking statute intended to prohibit the unauthorized use of any computer system for improper or illegitimate purpose.” (Id.) Further, Section 502, subdivision (h), has two subparts that, under certain conditions, prohibit punishing an employee who misuses an employer’s computer. The first subpart applies to an employee’s computer misuse which occurs within the scope of his employment. Penal Code section 502 states:

 

(h)(1) Subdivision (c) does not apply to punish any acts which are committed by a person within the scope of his or her lawful employment. For purposes of this section, a person acts within the scope of his or her employment when he or she performs acts which are reasonably necessary to the performance of his or her work assignment.

 

            Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Beniga, who was Plaintiff’s former employee, left Plaintiff to join Ironmark to help it “unfairly compete” with Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶¶ 20-23.) Before leaving Plaintiff’s employ but while still employed, Plaintiff alleges that Beniga took “trade secrets and confidential and proprietary information” of Plaintiff and then allegedly used this information to help Ironmark compete with Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 22.) Plaintiff also alleges that Beniga signed a confidentiality agreement precluding disclosure of “information relating to the business of Company and any of its activities, projects, or clients as confidential, and not divulge any of this information to outside parties ...during, and subsequent to, employment with Company" and breached said agreement by disclosing alleged confidential information that he took to Ironmark.” (Id., ¶¶ 38-39.)

 

As such, the basis for Ironmark’s liability for Penal Code section 502 is that Ironmark allegedly “aided and abetted” Beniga taking information and has since benefitted from using this information to compete with Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶ 125.)

 

However, Plaintiff has not alleged facts which could show how Ironmark allegedly aided or abetted Beniga. While Plaintiff attempts to clarify in opposition, new facts outside the four corners of the Complaint cannot be properly considered here.

 

Moreover, as noted by Defendant there are 14 possible provisions under which a violation of Penal Code section 502 can be alleged. Moreover, if employees were authorized to have access to and use the information that was copied and downloaded while employed, then there is no violation of the Penal Code section 502 as a matter of law. (See Oracle USA, Inc. v. Rimini Street, Inc. (9th Cir. 2018) 879 F.3d 948, 962 (“[Plaintiff] obviously disapproved of the method — automated downloading — by which [defendant] took [plaintiff's] proprietary information,” but defendant is only liable if it was not authorized to take and use the information that it downloaded); see also, Ticketmaster L.L.C. v. Prestige Entertainment, Inc. (C.D. Cal. 2018) 306 F.Supp.3d 1164, 1176.)

 

Here, the Court agrees that Plaintiff must clarify the nature of its section 502 claim against Plaintiff, and allege facts which could show aiding and abetting beyond the conclusory allegations that Defendant has done so.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is sustained, with 15 days leave to amend.

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: 24STCV12187    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ARNO PATRICK KUIGOUA,

 

         vs.

 

THE MATTHEW L. TUCK & ASSOCIATES, et al.

 

 Case No.:  24STCV12187

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 4, 2024

 

            Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED, WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

On 5/13/2024, in propria persona Plaintiff Arno Patrick Kuigoua (Plaintiff) filed suit against the Matthew L. Tuck & Associates, PLC dba Appellate Law Firm, Aaron Myers, and Matthew L. Tuck.

 

On 8/19/2024, Defendants demurred to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges improper claims that are barred and cannot be asserted in this action pursuant to the release in a contractually binding Memorandum of Understanding (“MOU”) Plaintiff agreed to and signed on February 20, 2024.

 

            In support, Defendants submitted evidence that:

 

-         On February 20, 2024, the parties attended a virtual mediation with the Honorable Linda Quinn (Ret.). Plaintiff and his attorney as well a representative for Defendants and Defendants attorney of record attended. At the end of the day, the Parties reached a settlement. To memorialize the essential terms of the agreement, the Parties circulated and signed a Memorandum of Understanding (“MOU”). The MOU first sentence states “[t]he parties understand that formal documents remain to be drafted and signed, but consider the following to be binding and admissible to prove its contents as to the settlement of the individual pre-litigation action, Arno Kuigoua v. Matthew Tuck & Associates PC and Aaron Myers.”

 

-         Plaintiff’s counsel advised Plaintiff did not have printer/scanner and would instead go to a UPS store to sign and return a fully executed MOU. In the meantime, Plaintiff included a separate sheet of paper attached as the third page to the MOU in Plaintiff’s handwriting that states “I, Arno Kuigoua, hereby agree to the memorandum of understanding prepared today in the course of mediation with Judge Linda Quinn.” It is signed by Plaintiff and dated February 20, 2024. Therefore, Plaintiff has agreed to be bound by the terms of the MOU which include a release of all claims, known and unknown, against Defendants.

 

(Grasu Decl.)

 

After review, the Court finds Plaintiff’s claim here to be barred.

 

When parties intend that an agreement be binding, the fact that a more formal agreement must be prepared and executed does not alter the validity of the agreement. (J.B.B. Inv. Partners Ltd. v. Fair, 37 CA5th 1, 12 (2019); Blix St. Records, Inc. v. Cassidy, 191 CA4th 39, 48 (2010). If the parties definitely agree on all of the essential terms of an agreement in an informal writing, that agreement may constitute an enforceable contract, even though the parties intend to execute a formal writing later. (CSAA Ins. Exch. v. Hodroj, 72 CA5th 272, 277 (2021); Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel, 74 CA4th 299, 306–9 (1999).

 

Here, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts which could show that material terms were missing from the MOU or that could sow he did not intend to agree to be bound by the MOU terms. Rather, Plaintiff’s Complaint amounts to a re-litigation of the very same issues mediated, and resolved, by the MOU.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is sustained, without leave to amend.

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: BC648246    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JOY SLAGEL

                          

         vs.

 

LIBERTY MUTUAL INSURANCE COMPANY, ARIAM ALEMSEGHED, and LEANN LO

                                        

 Case No.:  BC648246

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 4, 2024

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to quash is DENIED.

 

On January 26, 2017, Plaintiff Joy Slagel (Plaintiff) filed suit against Liberty Mutual Insurance Company (LMIC), Ariam Alemseghed, and Leann Lo, alleging: (1) age discrimination; (2) age harassment; (3) retaliation in violation of FEHA; (3) discrimination on the basis of taking disability leave in violation of FEHA; (5) retaliation for taking disability leave in violation of FEHA; (6) failure to provide reasonable accommodation in violation of FEHA; (7) failure to engage in the interactive process in violation of FEHA; (8) breach of express oral contract not to terminate employment without good cause; (9) breach of implied in fact contract not to terminate employment without good cause; (1)) wrongful termination of employment in violation of public policy;  (11) violation of Labor Code section 1102.5; and (12) intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED).

 

            Now, Plaintiff moves to quash Defendant’s subpoena served by Defendant Liberty Mutual Insurance Company on (1) York Risk Services Group, Inc. and (2) CopperPoint Insurance Company.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s subpoenas are improper because, among other things: (1) they were served after the close of discovery; (2) Defendant has no compelling reason for seeking private employment records for an employer subsequent to Slagel’s employment with Defendant; (3) they are overbroad, and (4) the subpoena seeks impermissible character evidence.

 

            In opposition, Defendant argues that the Trial Subpoenas are narrowly tailored and “any evidence concerning Plaintiff’s representations on her employment applications or resumes regarding when or how her employment with Liberty Mutual ended is needed and critical to Liberty Mutual’s ability to establish its key defenses regarding Plaintiff’s mitigation efforts in securing subsequent employment and the impact of such efforts on Plaintiff’s loss of wages claims.”  (Opp., 3: 16-19.)

 

            After review, the Court agrees with Defendant.

 

            First, the Court disagrees with Plaintiff that the subpoenas are discovery subpoenas, rather than trial subpoenas. Here, Defendant seeks a narrowly tailored selection of documents which are known to exist. As such, it does not involve “discovery” but rather the procurement of known documents. (Boal v. Price Waterhouse & Co. (1985) 165 Cal. App. 3d 806, 810.)  

 

            Second, the Court finds that Liberty Mutual’s Trial Subpoenas do not seek character evidence, nor do they seek any evidence regarding “collateral matters.” The Trial Subpoenas request records regarding Plaintiff’s employment with her subsequent employers. The Trial Subpoenas clearly identify all the information Liberty Mutual seeks, such as Plaintiff’s employment application, resume, and pay records. For example, evidence concerning Plaintiff’s representations on her applications or resume regarding when or why her employment with Liberty Mutual ended is directly relevant to her damages claim, particularly to her mitigation efforts in securing subsequent employment, and for use as potential impeachment evidence. Additionally, Plaintiff’s pay records from her subsequent employers are directly relevant and critical to Liberty Mutual’s defense regarding Plaintiff’s lost earnings claims. They are, for instance, relevant to Plaintiff’s testimony regarding how much income and benefits she received following her employment separation from Liberty Mutual.

 

Additionally, pursuant to Evidence Code section 780, the jury may consider the evidence Liberty Mutual specifically requested though its Trial Subpoenas in evaluating the truthfulness of Plaintiff’s testimony at trial regarding these matters. Section 780 provides:

 

Except as otherwise provided by statute, the court or jury may consider in determining the credibility of a witness any matter that has any tendency in reason to prove or disprove the truthfulness of his testimony at the hearing, including but not limited to any of the following:

 

(a) His demeanor while testifying and the manner in which he testifies.

 

(b) The character of his testimony.

 

(c) The extent of his capacity to perceive, to recollect, or to communicate any matter about which he testifies.

 

(d) The extent of his opportunity to perceive any matter about which he testifies.

 

(e) His character for honesty or veracity or their opposites.

 

(f) The existence or nonexistence of a bias, interest, or other motive.

 

(g) A statement previously made by him that is consistent with his testimony at the hearing.

 

(h) A statement made by him that is inconsistent with any part of his testimony at the hearing.

 

(i) The existence or nonexistence of any fact testified to by him.

 

(j) His attitude toward the action in which he testifies or toward the giving of testimony.

 

(k) His admission of untruthfulness.

 

            The Court agrees with Defendant that evidence concerning Plaintiff’s representations to subsequent employers about when or why her employment with Liberty Mutual ended, either on her employment application or other records, is directly relevant and admissible to the extent she presents testimony at trial that is inconsistent with those records.

 

            Third, the Court does not find that the admission of evidence concerning Plaintiff’s subsequent employment would rise to the level of “substantial danger” or prejudice necessitating its exclusion from trial here. Defendant has a competing, compelling need warranting the production of the information requested. (Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Ass'n (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 39-40.)

 

            Finally, Plaintiff argues that the Trial Subpoenas are procedurally defective and should be quashed because they request that the responsive information be delivered to Department 38. Defendant clarified that it would inform the subsequent employers that the information requested should be produced to Department 17, where trial is to be held. As such, the Court does not find this error to be a legitimate basis for quashing the subpoenas.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to quash is denied.

 

It is so ordered.
