DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV00319    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 73

09/24/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

JOSEPH F. FARIVAR INVESTMENT GROUP, INC., et al. v. HPC VINEBURN, LLC, et al. (23STCV00319)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  Kenneth Ehrlich (Elkins Kalt Weintraub Reuben Gartside LLP)

Counsel for Defendants HPC Vineburn, LLC and Highpoint Capital LLC/opposing party: Brian Ledger (Gordon Rees Scully Mansukhani, LLP)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION (filed on 06/05/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first, third, fifth, sixth and ninth causes of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first, third, fifth, sixth and ninth causes of action against Defendants Benedetta Nardon and Marylin Knoop is MOOT.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 6, 2023, Plaintiffs Joseph F. Farivar Investment Group, Inc. (“FIG”) and Bordan Shoe Company, Inc. (“Bordan”) filed this action against Defendants HPC Vineburn, LLC (“HPC Vineburn”); Nardon Liquidating Corp.; Meruelo Maddux Properties – 1919 Vineburn Street, LLC  (“Meruelo Maddux”); Vineburn Property, LLC; Benedetta Nardon, as an individual and as a Trustee of the Nardon Family Trust dated October 27, 1982 and as Trustee of the M Nardon Marital Trust; Marilyn N. Knoop, as an individual; and Patricia J. Minni, as an individual and as Trustee of the Minni Family Trust dated April 18, 1996 f/b/o the Minni Family and as Trustee of the Minni Family Bypass Trust.

 

HPC Vineburn filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff Joseph F. Farivar Investment Group, Inc. On September 26, 2023, the Court sustained Plaintiff’s demurrer to that Cross-Complaint, and HPC Vineburn did not file an amended Cross-Complaint.

 

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed their claims against Defendants Marilyn N. Knoop and Benedetta Nardon. On September 3, 2024, the Court granted Defendants Benedetta Nardon and Marily Knoop’s Notice of Settlement and Application to Determine Good Faith Settlement.

 

On February 28, 2024, filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Continuing Nuisance;

2.      Permanent Nuisance;

3.      Continuing Trespass;

4.      Permanent Trespass;

5.      Negligence;

6.      Negligence Per Se;

7.      Contribution;

8.      Equitable Indemnity; and

9.      Declaratory Relief.

 

A.      Allegations in the FAC

 

The FAC alleges the following.

 

Plaintiff Joseph F. Farivar Investment Group, Inc. is the owner of real property located at 4335 Valley Boulevard, Los Angeles, California 90032 ("4335 Valley Boulevard" or "Farivar Property"). Plaintiff Bordan Shoe Company, Inc. is the current lessee and occupant of 4335 Valley Boulevard.

 

Defendants are current and former owners and/or operators of the real property at 1919 Vineburn Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90032 (the "Nardon Facility"). The Nardon Facility is the former location of the Nardon Manufacturing Company, which conducted on-site metal fabrication. The Nardon Facility is physically adjacent to 4335 Valley Boulevard. The Nardon Facility is also upgradient from 4335 Valley Boulevard, meaning that groundwater flows from the Nardon Facility toward 4335 Valley Boulevard.

 

Defendants and/or their predecessors in interest caused or permitted hazardous substance contamination to the soil, soil vapor, and groundwater at the Nardon Facility. Contaminated groundwater at the Nardon Facility then migrated, and continues to migrate, to the groundwater beneath the Farivar Property.

 

Plaintiffs bring this Complaint to recover damages and other appropriate relief resulting from the migration of contamination from the Nardon Facility to the Farivar Property.

A.      Legal Standard for Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

Motions for summary adjudication are procedurally identical to motions for summary judgment. (Serri v. Santa Clara University (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 859.) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code of Civ. Proc., §¿437c,¿subd. (f)(1).)¿

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment/summary adjudication bears the initial burden of showing that “there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Plaintiff's¿initial¿burden¿of proof in moving for summary judgment, however, d[oes] not include disproving any affirmative defenses asserted by defendants.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co.¿(2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564, citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853 (“Aguilar”) [“summary judgment law in this state no longer requires a¿plaintiff¿moving for summary judgment to disprove any defense asserted by the defendant as well as prove each element of his own cause of action.... All that the¿plaintiff¿need do is to ‘prove[ ] each element of the cause of action’ ”])  Once Plaintiff meets its initial burden of proving each element of the cause of action, “the burden shifts to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action¿or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

A.      First and Third Causes of Action: Continuing Nuisance and Continuing Trespass

 

Plaintiffs assert they are entitled to summary adjudication on the Continuing Nuisance and Trespass causes of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux.

 

To establish an action for private nuisance, (1) “the plaintiff must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of his property”; (2) “the invasion of the plaintiff’s interest in the use and enjoyment of the land must be substantial, that is, that it causes the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage”; (3) “the interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, but it must also be unreasonable, i.e., it must be of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.”  (Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-263, cleaned up.)

 

“The elements of trespass are: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant's intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) lack of permission for the entry or acts in excess of permission; (4) harm; and (5) the defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm.” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 262.) 

 

A trespass on real property may be either continuing or permanent. (Beck Development Co. v. Southern Pacific Transportation Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1219-1222.) “Continuing trespasses are essentially a series of successive injuries, and the statute of limitations begins anew with each injury.” (Starrh & Starrh Cotton Growers v. Aera Energy LLC (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 583, 592.)

 

Nuisances may also be continuing or permanent depending on “the type of harm suffered.” (Baker, v. Burbank-Glendale-Pasadena Airport Authority (1985) 39 Cal.3d 862, 866.) Continuing nuisances involve ongoing or repeated disturbances caused by noise, vibrations, or foul odor and may be abated at any time. (Baker, 39 Cal.3d at p. 868.) Moreover, there is no statute of limitations for continuing trespass and continuing nuisance. (See Starrh, 153 Cal.App.4th at p. 592.; Lyle v. State of California (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 281, 291. )

 

Here, Plaintiff has pled in its FAC that Defendants and/or their predecessors in interest caused or permitted hazardous substance contamination to the soil, soil vapor, and groundwater at the Nardon Facility. Contaminated groundwater at the Nardon Facility then migrated, and continues to migrate, to the groundwater beneath the Farivar Property. Plaintiffs assert that this contamination constitutes an invasion onto, and an interference with, Plaintiffs’ enjoyment and use of their property. Plaintiffs contend Defendants are liable for the Contamination because it occurred—and migrated—during their ownership of the Nardon Facility, and because they constitute successive legal owners to the Nardon Manufacturing Company.

 

In support, Plaintiffs rely on a 2020 Subsurface Report. HPC Vineburn retained Hazard Management Consulting, Inc. (“HMC”) to conduct a subsurface investigation and prepare a report. (McCormick Decl. ¶ 10, Ex. 9.) To prepare the 2020 Subsurface Report, HMC obtained and analyzed samples of soil, soil gas, and groundwater at the Nardon Facility. (McCormick Decl. ¶¶ 2, 9, Ex. 1, pp. 7-14.) The 2020 Subsurface Report revealed that soil and groundwater contamination at the Nardon Facility has migrated to the subsurface at the Farivar Property. (McCormick Decl. ¶¶ 2, 9, Ex. 1, p. 20.) The 2020 Subsurface Report states in relevant part:

 

“The site was formerly used as a metal fabricator for the aerospace industry. Although previous investigations indicated residual concentrations of VOCs, no samples were analyzed for 1,4-dioxane.

Based on this information and historical land use, the LARWQCB requested soil, soil gas, and groundwater samples to be collected at the site to further assess whether the site had contributed to the TCE and 1,4-dioxane issue at the adjacent property.

 

Based on the results of the soil, soil gas, and groundwater investigations completed at the site, elevated concentrations of 1,4-dioxane have been detected in the vicinity of the Former Clarifier; a possible onsite source. Other chemicals, such as 1,1-DCE and PCE, were also noted in soil gas and groundwater collected throughout the area of former chemical use and former manufacturing area of the site. Based on this information, it appears that the site has contributed to the 1,4-dioxane groundwater issues at the Former Square D Facility, and to a lesser extent the residual 1,1-DCE and PCE at that property. Much lower concentrations of TCE have been reported in groundwater beneath the site than those on the Former Square D Facility. Petroleum hydrocarbons and metals do not appear to be a concern at the site.”

 

(McCormick Decl. ¶¶ 2, 9, Ex. 1, pp. 19-20.)

 

Plaintiffs assert that Defendants have failed to remediate the contamination. In support, Plaintiffs submit emails from Justin Schnuelle, a representative of HPC Vineburn, stating that remediation will need to be done to clean up the ground water at Plaintiffs’ property and the Nardon Facility. (Luis Decl. ¶¶ 38- 39; Ex. 15-18.) Plaintiffs contend they will need to remediate the property at a cost of $11,779,000. (Luis Decl., ¶¶ 40-56.) Plaintiffs also assert that as a result of needing to remediate the property, Plaintiff Bordan will lose use of the property during the remedial work which damages Bordan in the amount of $192,887. (Lofgren Decl. ¶¶ 11-12.)

 

Here, the Court finds Plaintiffs have carried their initial burden. Plaintiffs have provided evidence that contaminated groundwater at the Nardon Facility migrated, and continues to migrate, to the groundwater beneath the Farivar Property. Plaintiffs have shown the contamination constitutes continuing nuisance and trespass. (Martin Marietta Corp. v. Ins. Co. of N. Am., 40 Cal. App. 4th 1113, 1132 (1995) (“The migration of pollutants from one property to another may constitute a trespass, a nuisance, or both.”); Resol. Tr. Corp. v. Rossmoor Corp., 34 Cal. App. 4th 93, 99 (1995) (“Failure to clean up contamination causing ongoing damage to property has been held to constitute such a nuisance. . . . Likewise, the same conduct gives rise to an action for continuing trespass.) (citations omitted).) Plaintiffs also provide evidence that the contamination can be remediated, that Defendants have failed to remediate the contamination, and Plaintiffs have been harmed.

 

Therefore, the burden shifts to Defendants.

 

Defendant HPC Vineburn argues in opposition that former operations on Plaintiffs’ property contributed directly to the presence of 1,1-DCA in groundwater, thus creating a genuine issue of material fact on causation of Plaintiffs claimed damages that precludes summary adjudication. HPC Vineburn argues that the former operator of Plaintiffs’ Property, Alba Industries, utilized spray equipment using adhesives compounded with 1,1,1-trichloroethane and/or methylene chloride. In support, Defendants submit a Permit to Operate issued by the South Coast Air Quality Management District (“SCAQMD”) to Alba Industries for equipment at 4301 Valley Boulevard, Los Angeles, California, the property adjacent to Plaintiffs’ property. However, the Court does not find that HPC Vineburn has sufficiently authenticated the evidence it relies upon. (Cal. Evid. Code, § 1401, Ledger Decl., ¶¶ 2-6, Exs. D-I.) No declaration from an SCAQMD custodian accompanies the records, and no evidence suggests how the records came to exist or were obtained. Since the documents have not been sufficiently authenticated, the Court will not consider it. In HPC Vineburn’s motion, it also argues that the alleged nuisance and trespass are not continuing, but permanent. HPC Vineburn contends the court in Mangini v. Aerojet-General Corp. stated that “the crucial distinction between a permanent and continuing nuisance is whether the nuisance may be discontinued or abated” at any time. (Mangini v. Aerojet-General Corp. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 1125, 1146.) However, HPC Vineburn fails to provide any evidence or facts in support of its assertion that the nuisance is permanent. As discussed above, Plaintiffs provided evidence that the contamination can be remediated and abated. (Luis Decl., ¶¶ 40-56.) Finally, HPC Vineburn also argues that there are issues of material fact as to Plaintiffs’ claimed damages. However, HPC Vineburn does not present any admissible evidence in support. Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find HPC Vineburn has carried its burden.

 

Defendant Meruelo Maddux did not file an opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion. Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  Since Defendant Meruelo Maddux fails to present any opposition or evidence to Plaintiff’s motion, Defendant Meruelo Maddux fails to carry its burden of establishing a triable issue of material fact.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication as to the first and third causes of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux is GRANTED.

 

B.      Fifth Cause of Action: Negligence

 

Plaintiffs assert they are entitled to summary adjudication on its Negligence cause of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux.

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) 

 

The FAC alleges that Defendants and/or their predecessors in interest owed a duty of care in handling, storing, and using hazardous and contaminating substantives at the Nardon Facility. The FAC further alleges that Defendants and/or their predecessors in interest also owed a duty of care in preventing hazardous and contaminating substances from migrating onto the Farivar Property. The FAC further alleges that Defendants and/or their predecessors in interest breached their duty of care by releasing, allowing the release of, or failing to prevent the release of, the Contamination, and/or by allowing the Contamination to migrate to the Farivar Property or failing to prevent the same.

 

As discussed above, Plaintiffs have provided evidence that contaminated groundwater at the Nardon Facility then migrated, and continues to migrate, to the groundwater beneath the Farivar Property. (McCormick Decl. ¶¶ 2, 9, Ex. 1, p. 20.) Plaintiffs also provide evidence that the contamination can be remediated, that Defendants have failed to remediate the contamination, and Plaintiffs have been harmed. (Luis Decl., ¶¶ 40-56, Luis Decl., ¶¶ 40-56.) Thus, the Court finds Plaintiffs have carried their initial burden.

 

In Defendant HPC Vineburn’s opposition, it argues that Plaintiff has not shown that HPC Vineburn breached a duty. However, Plaintiffs have submitted evidence that the contamination can be remediated and that Defendants have failed to remediate the contamination. HPC Vineburn does not present admissible evidence establishing a triable issue of material fact. Thus, HPC Vineburn has not carried its burden as to the fifth cause of action.

 

Since Defendant Meruelo Maddux did not file an opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion, Meruelo Maddux also fails to carry its burden of establishing a triable issue of material fact.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fifth cause of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux is GRANTED.

 

C.      Sixth Cause of Action: Negligence Per Se

 

Plaintiffs assert they are entitled to summary adjudication on its Negligence Per Se cause of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux.

 

“The negligence per se doctrine, as codified in Evidence Code section 669, creates a presumption of negligence if four elements are established: (1) the defendant violated a statute, ordinance, or regulation of a public entity; (2) the violation proximately caused death or injury to person or property; (3) the death or injury resulted from an occurrence of the nature of which the statute, ordinance, or regulation was designed to prevent; and (4) the person suffering the death or the injury to his person or property was one of the class of persons for whose protection the statute, ordinance, or regulation was adopted.”  (Spates v. Dameron Hospital Association (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 218, cleaned up.)  “The doctrine of negligence per se is not a separate cause of action[] but creates an evidentiary presumption that affects the standard of care in a cause of action for negligence.”  (Johnson v. Honeywell International Inc. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 549, 555, cleaned up.)

 

The FAC alleges Defendants and/or their predecessors in interest, through their acts and/or omissions, created the Contamination and have allowed the Contamination to migrate from the Nardon Facility to the subsurface beneath the Farivar Property. Such actions or omissions violate California Civil Code § 3479 (creating a nuisance), California Water Code § 13350 (unauthorized discharges of waste and hazardous substances), California Health & Safety Code § 5411 (discharging sewage, waste, or effluent in a manner causing "contamination, pollution or a nuisance"); and California Health & Safety Code § 25249.5 (discharge of cancerous or toxic chemicals into water), among other statutes and regulations. As a direct and proximate result, Plaintiffs have been damaged in an amount to be determined at trial.

 

Based on the Court’s findings above, Plaintiff has carried its initial burden as to its sixth cause of action. Plaintiffs have presented evidence that contaminated groundwater at the Nardon Facility migrated, and continues to migrate, to the groundwater beneath the Farivar Property. (McCormick Decl. ¶¶ 2, 9, Ex. 1, p. 20.) Defendants’ discharge of the contamination constitutes a violation of each of the statutes listed above.

 

In opposition, Defendant HPC Vineburn presents the same argument that Plaintiffs have not established HPC Vineburn has breached any statute. However, as discussed above, HPC Vineburn fails to present any admissible evidence in support. Thus, HPC Vineburn failed to carry its burden.

 

Since Defendant Meruelo Maddux did not file an opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion, Meruelo Maddux also fails to carry its burden of establishing a triable issue of material fact.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication as to the sixth cause of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux is GRANTED.

 

D.     Ninth Cause of Action: Declaratory Relief

 

Plaintiffs assert they are entitled to summary adjudication on its Declaratory Relief cause of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux.

 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.) 

 

“Actual controversy” is a controversy which admits of definitive and conclusive relief by judgment within the field of judicial administration, as distinguished from an advisory opinion on a particular or hypothetical state of facts. The judgment must decree, not suggest, what the parties may or may not do. (Selby Realty Co. v. San Buenaventura (1973) 10 Cal.3d 110.) A mere difference of opinion is not an “actual controversy” within §1060. The summary judgment procedure is appropriate in declaratory relief actions since ‘The propriety of the application of declaratory relief lies in the trial court's function to render such a judgment when only legal issues are presented for its determination.’ (Spencer v. Hibernia Bank, 186 Cal.App.2d 702, 712.)

 

A cause of action for declaratory relief should not be used as a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues raised in another cause of action. (General of America Insurance Co. v. Lilly (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 465, 470.) “The availability of another form of relief that is adequate will usually justify refusal to grant declaratory relief.” (California Insurance Guarantee Association v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1617, 1624.) Further, “there is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved.” (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366, quotation marks omitted.)

 

According to the FAC, Plaintiff requests a declaratory judgment that: (a) Defendants are the cause of the Contamination; (b) Defendants are solely responsible for Plaintiffs’ damages incurred as a result of the Contamination, including but not limited to damages for the loss of use and enjoyment of 4335 Valley Boulevard; the expenses of investigating and remediating the Contamination; diminution of 4335 Valley Boulevard's value; liabilities to third parties, including the applicable governmental agencies arising from Contamination; and all other monetary damages; (c) Plaintiffs are not liable for any such damages; and (d) Defendants must remediate the Contamination on 4335 Valley Boulevard within a timeframe as ordered by the Court.

 

Based on the Court’s findings above, Plaintiff has carried its initial burden as to its ninth cause of action. Plaintiffs have presented evidence that that an actual controversy exists between Plaintiffs and Defendants. Plaintiffs have asserted that contaminated groundwater at the Nardon Facility migrated, and continues to migrate, to the groundwater beneath the Farivar Property. (McCormick Decl. ¶¶ 2, 9, Ex. 1, p. 20.) Plaintiffs also provide evidence that the contamination can be remediated, that Defendants have failed to remediate the contamination, and Plaintiffs have been harmed. (Luis Decl., ¶¶ 40-56, Luis Decl., ¶¶ 40-56.)

 

In opposition, Defendant HPC Vineburn argues that the Court should refuse to award declaratory relief because it is unclear what future work will be conducted. HPC Vineburn asserts that declaratory relief “is not appropriate because the [Los Angeles Regional Water Quality Control Board] has not selected a remedy.” (Oppos., p. 16.) However, HPC Vineburn does not cite to any authority that supports its argument that this would preclude Plaintiffs from seeking summary adjudication. Thus, the Court does not find HPC Vineburn’s argument persuasive, and it fails to carry its burden.

 

Since Defendant Meruelo Maddux did not file an opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion, Meruelo Maddux also fails to carry its burden of establishing a triable issue of material fact.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication as to the ninth cause of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux is GRANTED.

 

DISPOSITION 

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first, third, fifth, sixth and ninth causes of action against Defendants HPC Vineburn and Meruelo Maddux is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first, third, fifth, sixth and ninth causes of action against Defendants Benedetta Nardon and Marylin Knoop is MOOT.

FEAR OF GOD, LLC, et al. v. ART CITY CENTER LLC, et al. (23STCV16554)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  John Cha (Raines Feldman Littrell LLP.)

Counsel for Defendant CBRE Brokerage Service & Phillip Ruhl/moving party: Gregory Bordo (Blank Rome LLP)

 

Demurrer to THIRD amended complaint (filed by Defendants CBRE Brokerage Service & Phillip Ruhl on 08/13/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend as to the third cause of action against Defendants CBRE Brokerage Service and Phillip Ruhl.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 14, 2023, Plaintiffs Fear of God, LLC and FOG Essentials, LLC (collectively “Plaintiffs” or “Tenants”) filed this action against Defendants Art City Center, LLC (“Art City” or “Landlord”), Phillip Ruhl (“Ruhl”), and CBRE Brokerage Service (“CBRE”).

 

On February 26, 2024, the Court sustained Defendant Art City’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint with leave to amend. On March 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint. The Court subsequently sustained in part and overrule in part Defendant Art City’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint.

 

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) declaratory relief, (2) rescission and (3) negligent misrepresentation.

 

The TAC alleges the following. Plaintiffs are tenants to a lease (the “Lease”) for a commercial property located at 1360 E. 6th Street, Los Angeles (the “Premises”). Prior to the Lease, the Premises was used for many years as a warehouse. However, Defendants marketed the Premises as suitable for conversion to an office space. Plaintiffs’ intent and plan, which was communicated to Defendant Art City and its broker, Phillip Ruhl, verbally and in writing, was to create a multi-use space, including a retail showroom, general office, flex office, creative studio, marketing, content production, light manufacturing, light distribution, entertainment and media related space, and its corporate headquarters (“Intended Use”). Of particular concern to Plaintiff during Lease negotiations was the availability of electrical power and its capacity for the Premises such that Plaintiff could properly operate its business in accordance with the Intended Use. Plaintiff was repeatedly assured, both verbally and in writing, by both Defendants and Defendants’ Broker that the Premises has “enough power” for the Plaintiffs’ Intended Use. However, after the Lease was executed, Plaintiff alleges Defendants have failed to provide Plaintiffs with the full power that was promised and that is needed to use the premises for Plaintiffs’ Intended Use.

 

On August 13, 2024, Defendants CBRE Brokerage Service and Phillip Ruhl (“CBRE Defendants”) filed a Demurrer to the TAC, arguing:

·       The third cause of action for negligent misrepresentation fails to state a claim

o   Negligent misrepresentation only applies to past or existing facts. The purported fraud concerned a future condition – whether the property would have sufficient power once the yet-to-be-designed facility was operational.

o   Plaintiffs’ claim for negligent misrepresentation also fails because Plaintiffs do not allege that the CBRE Defendants knew or should have known the alleged representations were not true

·       The Court Should Sustain the Demurrer Without Leave To Amend

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue:

·       The allegations of the TAC are sufficient to support each the third cause action

·       Defendants made misrepresentations to Plaintiffs about the specific amount of power and Defendants knew its representations were not true. Just because Art City affirmatively knew their own representations were false based on their electrician does not mean that the CBRE Defendants did not lack reasonable grounds to make representations about the Premises

 

In reply, Art City maintains that the demurrer should be sustained, arguing:

·       There are no allegations whatsoever that the CBRE Defendants “knew or should have known” that electrical panels were broken or inoperable at the Premises. The TAC only alleges that Art City, via its electrician, knew about purported issues with the electrical panels. (TAC, ¶¶ 17, 19.) To the extent Plaintiffs are relying on an argument that the knowledge of Art City, via its electrician, is imputed to the CBRE Defendants, any such argument is legally flawed. Simply because Art City may have known there were broken or inoperable electrical panels at the Premises, as a matter of law, it does not mean that the CBRE Defendants knew as well.

·       Plaintiffs’ Claim for Negligent Misrepresentation Fails Because it is Based on Non-Actionable Future Conduct or Opinion

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for CBRE Defendants submitted a declaration stating that on August 8, 2024, the parties met and conferred telephonically regarding the alleged deficiencies. (Haring Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

A.     Third Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation

 

Defendants CBRE Brokerage Service and Phillip Ruhl argue that Plaintiffs’ negligent misrepresentation cause of action fails against the CBRE Defendants because the TAC does not allege with specificity (1) that the CBRE Defendants made any representation knowing it was false or without a reasonable ground for believing the representation was true, and (2) a misrepresentation of past or existing material fact.

The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation are: (1) the defendant must have made a representation as to a past or existing material fact; (2) the representation must have been untrue; (3) regardless of the defendant’s actual belief, the defendant must have made the representation without any reasonable ground for believing it to be true; (4) the representation must have been made with the intent to induce the plaintiff to rely on it; (5) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the falsity of the representation and must have acted in justifiable reliance on the truth of the representation; and (6) as a result of reliance on the truth of the representation, the plaintiff must have sustained damage. (Continental Airlines, Inc. v. McDonnell Douglas Corp. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 388, 402 (reh’g denied and opinion modified on Jan. 5, 1990).) 

The TAC alleges the following. “During Lease negotiations, Landlord and Landlord’s Broker represented to Tenant and Williams, on multiple occasions both verbally and in writing, that there was sufficient electrical power available for Tenant’s Intended Use.” (TAC, ¶ 67.) “Even though Landlord and Landlord’s Broker may have believed the representations were true when they made them, they had no reasonable grounds for so believing. The Landlord and through its electrician Mr. Ben-Shahar, were familiar with the Premises, knew the Premises were to be converted from a warehouse without an operable HVAC system to a premiere office space with an HVAC system sufficient to allow Tenants’ employees to comfortably work there. As such, Landlord knew, or should have known, based upon Mr. Ben-Shahar’s knowledge both of the Premises and as an electrician, the electrical load necessary to comply with Landlord’s obligation to provide the Landlord’s Work items, which, among other items, includes an obligation to deliver a new HVAC system to cool or heat the approximately 57,000 square foot Premises sufficient for the Intended Use as well as the additional electrical power that will be required for Tenant to utilize the Premises for the Intended Use. Accordingly, because Landlord’s electrician and Landlord knew or should have known that they would need to use and/or would be using substantially all of the available electrical power capacity set forth in the Power Availability Assessment letter for the HVAC system alone – including Landlord and Landlord’s electrician’s knowledge that certain panels identified in the Power Availability Assessment Letter were inoperable - Landlord knew or should have known that Landlord had no reasonable ground for believing its representation that “the available wattage would definitely be sufficient for Tenants’ Intended Use.” Landlord knew or should have known at the time the representation was made that that additional power capacity would then be needed for the Intended Use and made the representation with an intent to deceive Tenants’ to enter into the Lease.” (SAC, ¶ 69.)

Here, Plaintiffs specify in the TAC how the Landlord, Defendant Art City, knew or should have known their representations were false. Plaintiffs allege that the Landlord and its electrician had knowledge of the Premises and its electrical load. However, Plaintiffs do not include any allegations as to how the CBRE Defendants knew or should have known their representations were false and thus, intended to deceive Plaintiffs. Based on this ground, the Court does not find the TAC’s allegations sufficient as to its negligent misrepresentation cause of action against the CBRE Defendants.

Thus, the demurrer is sustained as to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action against the CBRE Defendants with leave to amend. 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend as to the third cause of action against Defendants CBRE Brokerage Service and Phillip Ruhl.

EBET, INC. v. BOUSTEAD SECURITIES, LLC (24STCP00520)

(1)   MOTION TO VACATE ARBITRATION AWARD (filed 04/05/2024)

(2)   MOTION TO FILE DOCUMENTS UNDER SEAL IN CONNECTION WITH EBET, INC.’S MOTION TO VACATE ARBITRATION AWARD (filed 04/05/2024)

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court DENIES the motion to vacate the arbitration award. Accordingly, the petition to confirm the arbitration award is GRANTED.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 20, 2024, Petitioner Boustead Securities LLC filed a petition for an order confirming the arbitration award in favor of Petitioners and against Respondent Ebet, Inc. (as successor-in-interest to ESEG Limited).

 

The arbitration award in Petitioner’s favor was made on January 1, 2024, by arbitrators Joseph C. Watson, Richard Maurice Robinson and Michael Craig Ditty, and is attached to the petition. The arbitration was related to a dispute that concerned Respondent's failure to pay Petitioner certain fees and other consideration for services Petitioner rendered to Respondent. Respondent also raised a counterclaim in the arbitration concerning alleged tortious interference. The arbitration award requires Respondent to pay Petitioner an award of $ 15,162,689.05. The petition requests the Court confirm the award and enter judgment according to it. 

 

On April 5, 2024, Respondent filed a Motion to Vacate Arbitration Award, arguing:

·       The Arbitrators exceeded their powers by recognizing an unconscionable right of first offer and awarding warrants based on contractual language that does not allow for their proper valuation.

·       The terms of the Engagement Agreement was indefinite and therefore unconscionable.

·       Because the Arbitrators improperly awarded Boustead the full amount of warrants in Count II, based on a manifest disregard for the plain language of Section 2(a)(i)-(ii) of the Engagement Agreement and well-settled principles of contract interpretation, under which the uncertainty should have been interpreted strongly against Boustead, the Arbitrators exceeded their powers.

 

On June 10, 2024, Petitioner filed an Opposition to Respondent’s Motion to Vacate Arbitration Award/Motion to Confirm Arbitration Award, arguing:

·       The Law Requires a Court to Enter an Arbitration Award as a Judgment Except in Extraordinary Circumstances Not Present Here

·       The Arbitrators Properly Issued the Award, Which Should Be Affirmed

o   The Engagement Agreement expressly gave the Panel the authority to render the decision it issued

o   EBET made the same challenges to Counts II and V of the Statement of Claim that EBET raises in its Motion to Vacate. See Wilson Dec., 7; Ex. 4. The Panel implicitly rejected those challenges in finding for Boustead on Counts II and V. Thus, EBET's Motion to Vacate boils down to being an argument that the Panel got the facts or law wrong in deciding Counts II and V. However, that is clearly not a valid basis for vacating an arbitration award.

Respondent filed a reply, arguing:

·       Petitioner did not need to file a Motion to Confirm Award. Petitioner’s motion doubles as an opposition to Respondent’s Motion to Vacate.

·       Boustead does not meaningfully dispute that the arbitrators manifestly disregarded the contract language and well-settled principles of contract interpretation in the Engagement Agreement with respect to the award of warrants.

On April 5, 2024, Respondent filed a Motion to File Documents Under Seal in Connection with Respondent’s Motion to Vacate Arbitration Award. No opposition was filed.

 

On June 11, 2024, counsel for Petitioner filed an Application for Joseph D. Wilson to Appear Pro Hac Vice. No opposition was filed.

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Motions to Vacate/Confirm Arbitration Award

 

a.       Legal Standard

 

Statutes set forth specific grounds upon which an¿arbitrator’s award may be¿vacated (Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.2) or corrected (Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.60).  Except on these grounds,¿arbitration awards are immune from judicial review in proceedings to challenge or enforce the award.¿(Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase¿(1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 12-13;¿Zazueta v. County of San Benito¿(1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 106, 110.)  In deciding an application to vacate an arbitration award, every presumption is made in favor of upholding the arbitration award.  (Popcorn Equipment Co. v. Page (1949) 92 Cal.App.2d 448, 451.) 

 

An award may be vacated only for fraud, corruption, misconduct, an undisclosed conflict, or similar “circumstances involving serious problems with the award itself, or with¿the fairness of the arbitration process.”¿(Moncharsh,¿3 Cal.4th at 12, see¿Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.2, subd. (a).)  “[B]y voluntarily submitting to arbitration, the parties have agreed to bear the risk [of uncorrectable legal or factual error] in return for a quick, inexpensive, and conclusive resolution to their dispute.” (Moncharsh,¿3 Cal.4th at 11.) 

 

 

An award may be set aside entirely if the court finds any of the following grounds,¿Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.2(a)(1)-(6): 

 

(1) The award was procured by corruption, fraud or other undue means. 

(2) There was corruption in any of the arbitrators. 

(3) The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced by misconduct of a neutral arbitrator. 

(4) The arbitrators exceeded their powers and the award cannot be corrected without affecting the merits of the decision upon the controversy submitted. 

(5) The rights of the party were¿substantially prejudiced¿by the refusal of the arbitrators to postpone the hearing upon sufficient cause being shown therefor or by the refusal of the arbitrators to hear evidence material to the controversy or by¿other conduct of the arbitrators contrary to the provisions of this title. 

(6) An arbitrator making the award either: (A) failed to disclose within the time required for disclosure a ground for disqualification of which the arbitrator was then aware; or (B) was subject to disqualification upon grounds specified in¿Section 1281.91¿but failed upon receipt of timely demand to disqualify himself or herself as required by that provision. However, this subdivision does not apply to arbitration proceedings conducted under a collective bargaining agreement between employers and employees or between their respective representatives…” 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.2(a)(1)-(6).)  

 

Limiting grounds for judicial review effectuates the parties’ agreement that the award be final.  It also reflects that arbitrators ordinarily need not follow the law and may base their decisions on “broad principles of justice and equity” – “paths neither marked nor traceable by judicial review. ”¿(Moncharsh, 3 Cal.4th at 11;¿Nogueiro v. Kaiser Found. Hospitals¿(1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 1192, 1195.)   

 

The merits of the controversy are generally not reviewable by the court when a petition to confirm or vacate is presented.¿(Moncharsh, 3 Cal.4th at 11.)  Thus, courts will¿not review¿the sufficiency of the evidence to support the award.¿(Morris v. Zuckerman¿(1968) 69 Cal.2d 686, 691.) 

 

Nor will courts pass upon the¿validity of the arbitrator’s reasoning.¿The court simply may not substitute its judgment for that of the arbitrator.¿ (Morris v. Zuckerman, 69 Cal.2d at 691;¿Department of Pub. Health of City & County of San Francisco v. Service Employees Int'l Union, Local 790¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 429, 433, fn. 4 (“we do not see any logic in the arbitrator’s [decision] … however … the arbitrator had the power [to so decide]”).) 

 

Finally, and most importantly here, errors of law are not reviewable.  Generally, errors of law committed by the arbitrator, no matter how gross, are not grounds for challenging the arbitrator’s award under California law.¿(Moncharsh, 3 Cal.4th at 11;¿Richey v. AutoNation, Inc.¿(2015) 60 C4th 909, 916;¿Baize v. Eastridge Cos.¿(2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 293, 300-302.) There are two reasons:  First, the parties contracted that the arbitrator’s decision, right or wrong, would be conclusive.  Second, the risk of arbitral error has been reduced by statutory provisions allowing courts to vacate or correct for “serious problems with the award itself or with the fairness of the arbitral process.”  (Moncharsh,¿3 Cal.4th at 12.) Thus, for example, awards have been upheld despite erroneous application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel, erroneous application of res judicata, consideration of inadmissible evidence and erroneous denial of attorney’s fees.  (See¿State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Guleserian¿(1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 397, 402;¿see also¿Moore v. First Bank of San Luis Obispo¿(2000) 22 Cal.4th 782, 789;¿Moshonov v. Walsh¿(2000) 22 Cal.4th 771, 779.) 

 

b.      Discussion

 

Here, the petition complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4.  The petition includes a copy of the parties’ agreement to arbitrate.  (Attachment 4(b).) Joseph C. Watson, Richard Maurice Robinson and Michael Craig Ditty, served as the arbitrators.  A copy of the arbitration award is attached to the petition.  (Attachment 8(c).) 

 

Petitioner asserted claims against Respondent for breach of the Engagement Agreement and for an award of attorneys' fees and costs. Respondent asserted a counterclaim for tortious interference with prospective economic advantage. On January 5, 2024, the arbitrators issued an award and determined: (a) Respondent breached the Engagement Agreement; (b) Petitioner suffered $14,641,827.11 in damages as a result of Respondent's breaches; and (c) as the prevailing party, Petitioner was entitled to recover from Respondent $509,499.44 in attorney's fees and $2,700 in costs. (Wilson Decl., Ex. 1, p. 4.)

 

Respondent’s motion challenges the award's rulings on Counts II and V of Petitioner's Second Amended Statement of Claim. Count II pertained to Respondent EBET’s purported breach of the Engagement Agreement for failure to pay Petitioner success fees due to it in the form of warrants to purchase EBET stock. Count V was for Respondent’s purported breach of a right of first refusal provision ("ROFR") in the Engagement Agreement.

 

Respondent argues that the Court should vacate the arbitration award because the arbitrators exceeded their powers. Respondent asserts that the arbitrators exceeded their powers on Count V because the ROFR is allegedly unconscionable and, regarding Count II, because the panel awarded damages for the value of the warrants based on an alleged improper reading of the Engagement Agreement. Respondent contends an award upholding an illegal contract exceeds the arbitrator’s powers: “[A]n award springing out of an illegal contract, which no court can enforce, cannot stand on any higher ground than the contract itself.” (Loving & Evans v. Blick (1949) 33 Cal.2d 603, 610; see also Lindenstadt v. Staff Builders, Inc. (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 882, 892 [public policy requires that the issue of legality be independently determined by the court].)

 

In opposition, Petitioner argues the arbitration panel acted well within the authority granted to it in the Engagement Agreement in issuing the award. Respondent’s arguments that the arbitrators exceeded their powers by basing their award on an unsupported or unconscionable reading of the Engagement Agreement is not a proper ground to vacate an award. Respondent’s raised the same arguments in their motion to the Statement of Claim, and the panel rejected those challenges in finding for Petitioner. Thus, Petitioner argues Respondent’s motion is improperly arguing that the arbitrators got the facts or law wrong.

 

The Court does not find that Respondent has established that the arbitrators exceeded their authority. Respondent ignores that the arbitrators already rejected its arguments and does not address the grounds for this rejection. Respondent does not point to any finding that was not potentially supported by the record. For example, as to Count II, both parties’ experts disagreed as to how the amount of warrants due was calculated. Just because the arbitrators could have found differently is of no moment. "Generally, when faced with a petition to confirm or vacate an arbitration award, a court may not review the merits of the parties' controversy or claims that the arbitrator's decision is either legally or factually erroneous, '[Arbitrators do not exceed their powers merely [by] assign[ing] an erroneous reason for their decision.' 'Nor can an award be vacated because the arbitrator 'reached an erroneous decision."' (Jones v. Humanscale Corp. (Cal. Ct. App. 2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 401, 409 (internal citations omitted).)

 

Based on the above, the Court DENIES the motion to vacate the arbitration award and GRANTS the petition to confirm the arbitration award.

 

B.      Motion to Seal

 

a.       Legal Standard

 

“Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c).) A party may move to seal records pursuant to California Rules of Court, rules 2.550-2.551. “A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record. The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b).) To grant an order permitting a record to be filed under seal, the court must find facts that establish:  

 

(1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; 

(2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; 

(3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; 

(4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and 

(5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest. 

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  

 

b.      Discussion

 

Respondent requests that the Court issue an order sealing:

·       Exhibits A, C, and D to the Declaration of Andrew B. Goodman in support of EBET’s Motion to Vacate Arbitration Award.

·       EBET’s Motion to Vacate Arbitration Award itself (which contains references to Exhibits A, C, and D, supra)

 

Respondent asserts that the if the documents are not sealed, other businesses could gain an advantage against the parties in future litigation or business deals with specific knowledge of the confidentially designated information.

 

The Court finds there are sufficient facts to support the filing of the settlement documents under seal. As Respondent contends, there is an overriding interest supporting sealing the Parties’ confidential Engagement Agreement and two expert reports discussing the Engagement Agreement in detail. No less restrictive means are apparent to achieve the overriding interest in protecting the party’s confidential business information. Petitioner did not file an opposition.

 

The Court GRANTS Respondent’s unopposed Motion to Seal.

 

C.      Application to Appear Pro Hac Vice

 

a.       Legal Standard

 

CRC Rule 9.40(d) provides that an application to be admitted pro hac vice must state the following: (1) applicant attorney’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant attorney has been admitted and dates of admission; (3) a representation that the attorney applicant is a member in good standing in the courts of admission and is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (4) the title of each court and action in which the applicant attorney has appeared pro hac vice in this State in the preceding two years, if any; and (5) the name, address, and telephone number of the active California State Bar member who is counsel of record in the local action. (CRC Rule 9.40(d).) 

 

C.R.C., Rule 9.40(e) provides as follows, “[a]n applicant for permission to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule must pay a reasonable fee not exceeding $50 to the State Bar of California with the copy of the application and the notice of hearing that is served on the State Bar.” 

 

b.      Discussion

 

Petitioner’s application on behalf of Joseph D. Wilson provides the information required by C.R.C., Rule 9.40(d).  The declarations supplied sufficient information to satisfy the requirements. (Wilson Decl., ¶¶ 1-8; Berg Decl., ¶¶ 1-7, Wilson Suppl. Decl., ¶¶ 1-2.)  Petitioner submitted proof that the $50.00 fee was paid to the State Bar of California.  (C.R.C., Rule 9.40(e).) Petitioner also submitted proof of service on the State Bar of California.  (C.R.C., Rule 9.40(c)(1).) Petitioner’s application satisfies rule 9.40. Respondent did not file an opposition.

 

Accordingly, Petitioner’s unopposed motion for an order admitting Joseph D. Wilson to appear pro hac vice is GRANTED. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court DENIES the motion to vacate the arbitration award. Accordingly, the petition to confirm the arbitration award is GRANTED. 

 

Petitioner is to lodge a proposed judgment consistent with the arbitration award within 10 days.

 

The Court GRANTS Respondent’s Motion to Seal.

 

Petitioner’s Pro Hac Vice Application of Joseph D. Wilson is GRANTED.
