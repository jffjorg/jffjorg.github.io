DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV00136    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Monday, October 14, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   20STCV00136

CASE NAME:                        Juan Ocampo v. Kia Motors America, Inc.

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiffs Juan Ocampo and Mari Alonzo

OPPOSING PARTY:             Defendant Kia Motors America, Inc.

TRIAL DATE:                        Post settlement

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

OPPOSITION:                        1 October 2024

REPLY:                                  7 October 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         plaintiffs’ motion for attorney’s fees is granted in the sum of $92,071.75.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law action arising out of the purchase by Plaintiff Juan Ocampo (“Ocampo”) of a 2013 Kia Sorento (the “Vehicle”), manufactured and/or distributed by Kia Motors America, Inc. (“Defendant” or “Kia”). Plaintiff alleges that he received an express written warranty in connection with purchase of the Vehicle, which provided that Defendant would preserve or maintain the Vehicle during the warranty period or repair the Vehicle to warranty if a defect developed during the warranty period. According to Plaintiff, the Vehicle developed defects in a variety of areas during the warranty period, including its engine, transmission system, ignition, and steering. Further, and according to Plaintiff, Defendant was provided sufficient opportunity to repair the Vehicle but was unable to, or failed to, service the Vehicle, impairing its safety, use and/or value. 

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleged the five causes of action: (1) violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(d), (2) violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(b), (3) violation of Civ. Code § 1793.2(a)(3), (4) breach of express written warranty (Civ. Code § 1791.2(a), § 1794), and (5) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability -Civ. Code § 1791.1, § 1794). 

 

On August 14, 2020, Ocampo’s motion for leave to file a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) was granted. The FAC was filed on August 20, 2020. The FAC names Maria Alonzo Cordova (“Cordova”) as a new Plaintiff and adds a sixth cause of action for fraud by omission. On October 28, 2020, Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC’s sixth cause of action was sustained. Defendant’s motion to strike footnotes 4, 7 and 9 from the FAC was granted.  

 

On November 16, 2020, Plaintiffs filed the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). The SAC alleges the same causes of action as the FAC. On April 22, 2021, the court sustained the demurrer to the SAC with leave to amend.

 

On May 21, 2021, Plaintiffs filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleging the same five causes alleged in the original Complaint.

 

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiffs accepted Kia’s CCP § 998 Offer (the “998 Offer”). On September 10, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this Motion seeking attorney’s fees. Defendant Kia opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

Motions for attorneys’ fees

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Under the Civ. Code § 1794(d), the prevailing party in an action that arises out of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act is entitled to fees that were reasonably incurred:¿ “If the buyer prevails under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the Court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794(d).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

 

The lodestar method is the primary method for determining a reasonable attorney fee award under Civ. Code § 1794(d).¿ (See Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 818-19.)¿ “A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney involved in the presentation of the case.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321 [internal quotations omitted].) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved.¿ [citation] The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)¿ “The basis for the trial court's calculation must be the actual hours counsel has devoted to the case, less those that result from inefficient or duplicative use of time.”¿(Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 395.)¿“The law is clear, however, that an award of attorney fees may be based on counsel's declarations, without production of detailed time records.”¿(Raining Data Corp. v. Barrenechea (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1375.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

B.        Time Reasonably Spent on the Litigation¿ 

¿ 

“‘In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence. General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.’ ” (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez¿(2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 488, citing Premier Medical Management Systems, Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2008) 163¿Cal.App.4th¿550, 564.) The court will exercise its discretion in determining whether Plaintiffs’ request for attorney’s fees is reasonable by considering the following factors: the nature of litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in handling the matter, the attention given, the success or failure, and the resulting judgment. (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623.)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

CLE asserts that they reasonably spent about 387.4 hours totaling $177,012.00 in fees litigating this action. (Cohen, Decl., Ex. M.)

 

Defendant Kia objects to the following billing entries:

 

a)         Time Billed for Drafting the Pleadings

 

Defendants object to the fact that CLE billed 21.10 hours and incurred $9,132.50 in fees just to draft the pleadings. However, Defendant fails to show that Plaintiff’s spent 21.10 hours on the pleadings and fails to point to the specific billing objections.

 

Having reviewed the billing entries, the court notes that the original Complaint was reviewed and drafted by Jessica Anvar. On January 1, 2020, Benjamen Beck billed for reviewing and revising the FAC. By January 1,  2020, the FAC, which added the fraud cause of action had already been drafted. Nevertheless, Bejamin Beck and Elvin Vu would both bill time to draft a simple motion for leave to amend the complaint after January 2020. Beginning March 13, 2020, Elvin Vu would also begin billing for drafting and reviewing the FAC. Despite being responsible for drafting the FAC, Elvin Vu billed for administrative tasks such as “review of filing” and “case review on April 14, and April 24, 2020, June 5, 2020, June 12, 2020, June 22, 2020, August 3, 2020.”

 

In drafting the motion for leave to amend and revise the FAC, Elvin Vu should have billed no more than 4.0 hours billed at a rate of $425.00/hour given that she had 9 years of experience. Therefore, of the approximately 13.0 hours spent on the motion for leave to amend and the FAC, 9.0 hours, or $3,825.00 will be subtracted from the lodestar.

 

                        b)        Excessive Time Discussing and Conferring with Other Attorneys/Staff

Defendant Kia asserts that CLE billed at least 6.2 hours ($2,812.50) just to review upcoming hearings and discuss and review the case with other attorneys and/or staff. Having reviewed the CLE billing statements, the court finds that while Elvin Vu, Daniel Choi, Iraida Gonzalez, billed for debriefing and conferring with the supervising attorney, the entries were sporadic, not excessive. However, there are billing discrepancies. On April 8, 2020, Elvin Vu billed 0.6 hours for “conferring with supervising attorney” while on that same date Benjeman Beck billed 0.2 of an hour for the same; “Confer with handling attorney re strategy going forward.” (Cohen, Ex. M.)

 

As the time spent conferring between the handling attorney and the supervising attorney is suspect, the court will deduct 5.0 hours billed at a rate of $460.00, or $2,576.00,  from the lodestar.

 

                        c)         Time Billed For Non-Existent Events.

 

Defendant Kia contends that CLE’s billing records contain entries for non-existent events such as 1.6 hours ($760) to “Appearing for D’s MTC hearing” on February 24, 2022, despite there being no hearing for that date. The records reflects that there was no hearing for said date and Plaintiffs’ reply fails to address this contention.

 

Defendant Kia further complains about entries dated May 21, 2021, but the billing entries do not reflect Defendant Kia’s contention and appear to be proper. The billing entries of September 13, 2022, October 21, 2022, October 24, 2022, and May 19, 2023, appear proper and do support Defendant Kia’s contention that the billing was nonexistent o unnecessary events.

 

Therefore, the only deduction the court will make a deduction for 1.6 hours billed at a rate of $460.00, or $736.00.

 

                        d)        Time Billed for Administrative Tasks and Overhead

 

Defendant Kia asserts that CLE’s billing records should be reduced because its billing statements are filled with entries for clerical and administrative tasks that are not recoverable as attorney’s fees. (See Nadarajah v. Holder (9th Cir. 2009) 569 F.3d 906, 921 [“When clerical tasks are billed at hourly rates, the court should reduce the hours requested to account for the billing errors.”].) Defendant Kia asserts that the billing entries of Virginia Walls and Daisey Hernandez are not recoverable as attorneys’ fees because the two paralegals billed for purely clerical tasks such as updating the calendar, reviewing the docket, labeling exhibits, and making reservations.

 

Plaintiffs maintain that paralegal fees are recoverable as attorney fees. “We think, however, necessary support services for attorneys, e.g. secretarial and paralegal services, are includable within an award of attorney fees.” (Salton Bay Marina, Inc. v. Imperial Irrigation Dist. (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 914, 951.) “An award of attorney fees which does not compensate for paralegal service time would not fully compensate the attorney.” (Guinn v. Dotson (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 262, 269.) The court agrees that Plaintiffs are entitled to recover fees for time billed by paralegals and that such a practice “is consistent with market rates and practices.” (Guinn, at p. 269.)

 

Defendant Kia also takes issue with the fact that attorneys like Corrinna Jiang and Iraida Gonzalez billed for administrative tasks such as “organize exhibition exhibits” on September 9, 2021, and for “receiving documents” on October 11, 2022. “ ‘It is appropriate to distinguish between legal work, in the strict sense, and investigation, clerical work, compilation of facts and statistics and other work which can often be accomplished by non-lawyers but which a lawyer may do because he has no other help available. Such non-legal work may command a lesser rate. Its dollar value is not enhanced just because a lawyer does it. ’ ” (Missouri v. Jenkins by Agyei (1989) 491 U.S. 274, 288, fn. 10.) “[T]he filing, transcript, and document organization time was clerical in nature and should have been subsumed in firm overhead rather than billed at paralegal rates.” (Nadarajah v. Holder (9th Cir. 2009) 569 F.3d 906, 921.)

 

The court agrees that it is inappropriate for clerical tasks performed by attorneys to be billed at attorney rates because the costs of completing such tasks are part of overhead costs and are appropriately absorbed and compensated by an attorney’s higher fee rates. “However, clerical and other overhead expenses are ordinarily included in an attorney's hourly rates and are not separately recoverable unless they represent expenses ordinarily billed to a client.” (City of Oakland v. McCullough (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1, 7.)

 

CLE’s billing records reflect various instances of attorneys billing for clerical tasks or block billing for clerical tasks performed alongside legal work. Elvin Vu billed time to pull documents, review service and filing, and for conferring receipt of “cmc draft” and “discovery extension.” For example, on May 22, 2020, Elvin Vu billed for “Hearing dates and calendaring e email.”

 

On August 3, August 24, and October 4, 2021, Daniel Choi billed entries reflect billing for organizing exhibits. Daniel Choi also billed for conducting electronic service even though such a task could have been completed by a paralegal. Daniel Choi’s billing entries for November 4, November 8, and December 8, 2021, reflect time billed for serving supplemental responses and objections.

 

 Iraida Gonzalez billed time spent for “Assigned LIT CM a task re ex parte to continue trial” and “2022 Assigned tasks to LIT CM regarding trial continuance” on September 29 and September 30, 2022. Iraida Gonzalez also billed for time spent requesting copies from Titan Legal Services when such a task could have been completed by a paralegal and billed at a lower rate. On June 2, 2023, Iraida Gonzalez also billed for “proof of service and served to Opposing Counsel.”

 

As the billing entries of CLE’s counsel include various instances of attorney’s billing for administrative tasks, to account for any billing errors the court deducts 25%, or $40,145.25, from the $160,581.50 lodestar.

Defendant Kia asserts that CLE billed at least 54.90 hours totaling $23,895.00 to depose unnecessary dealership witnesses because the service advisors and technicians who work on hundreds of vehicles cannot remember each one and they already keep a contemporaneous record of their work on the repair orders.

                        j)         Time Billed for Travel

 

Defendant Kia asserts that CLE billed a total of 7.0 hours ($3,387.00) for time spent traveling to hearings and depositions without citing any authority permitting the recovery of such costs. Plaintiffs assert that under the Song-Beverly Act, CLE is entitled to “an award of reasonable attorney fees for time reasonably expended by his or her attorney.” (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 818.)

 

California case law holds that attorneys are compensated for travel costs, not for the attorney's time spent traveling. “As to the out-of-town travel expenses, the court finds that item to be reasonable in amount and the type of expense that would ordinarily be charged to the client. In the circumstances of this case, the court concludes that equity requires that plaintiffs' counsel be reimbursed for travel expenses necessarily incurred during the litigation.” (Keith v. Volpe (C.D. Cal. 1980) 501 F.Supp. 403, 414–415.) “The trial court did not err in awarding costs incurred by Bakersfield attorneys to attend depositions in San Diego County.” (Thon v. Thompson (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1546, 1548.) “The court did not abuse its discretion in allowing the costs of travel to and from depositions in New Mexico and Texas.” (Chaaban v. Wet Seal, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 49, 60.)

 

CLE may recover travel costs, but the court is not persuaded that attorney compensation for time spent merely traveling is reasonable under the Song-Beverly Act. The appearances for which CLE attorneys are seeking fees could have been made remotely.  On June 9, 2023, Andrew Dickson billed 1.5 hours at a rate of $425.00/hour for travel time. Accordingly, $637.50  will be subtracted from the lodestar. Jorden Cohen billed 3.8 hours for travel time. Accordingly, 3.8 hours billed at a rate of $460.00 per hour, or $598.00, will be deducted from the lodestar.

 

                        k)        Deductions for Block Billing

 

Defendant Kia asserts that Plaintiff block billed in the amount of 14.4 hours, totaling $5,985.00. (See chart at Laughlin Decl., ¶ 12(P). “While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable. (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor

 (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) “[T]rial courts are granted discretion ‘to penalize [block billing] when the practice prevents them from discerning which tasks are compensable and which are not.’ [Citation.]” (In re Marriage of Nassimi (2016) 3Cal.App.5th 667, 695-696.)

 

The court agrees that CLE blocked billed many entries and that several entries are vague as to what actual work was performed. The court deducts $5,000.00 from the lodestar.

 

                        l) Time Billed for Fee Motion

 

Defendant Kia asserts that CLE excessively billed 14.6 hours  ($7,665.00) for this fee motion, and that the time Plaintiffs’ counsel anticipates billing to review the opposition and draft the reply are also excessive.

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel asserts that he anticipates billing 7.0 hours to review the opposition, draft a reply, and attend the hearing billed at a rate of $525.00 per hour ($3,937.50.) (Cohen Decl., ¶ 25.) Plaintiffs’ counsel billed 14.6 hours drafting the initial fee motion. However, Plaintiffs’ counsel failed to provide evidence to support the fee request of attorneys Daniel Choi, Andrew Dickson, and Elvin Vu. Plaintiffs’ counsel asserts that the $475.00 billing rate of Rodney Gi’ at $475.00 was reasonable despite Rodney Gi not being an attorney who staffed this matter. Moreover, the fact that CLE’s staff billed at 2024 rates since the inception of this case, lends little credibility that CLE’s billing records were properly audited. (Cohen Decl., ¶ 24.)

 

The court finds that Plaintiffs’ counsel should have billed no more than 6.0 hours to draft this initial fee motion, and 4.5 hours to review, draft, the reply, and attend the hearing.

 

  • 14.6 hours billed at a rate of  $460.00/hour = $6,716.00
  • 6.0 hours billed at a rate of $460.00/hour = $2,760.00
  • $6,716.00 - $2,760.00 = $3,956.00

 

The court deducts $7,893.50 ($3,956.00 + 3,937.50) from the lodestar.

 

C.        Adjusted Lodestar

From the lodestar of $160,581.50, the court makes the following deductions:

Conclusion

 

plaintiffs’ motion for attorney’s fees is granted in the sum of $92,071.75.

Background

 

This action relates to a nuisance case brought by a condominium tenant. On September 18, 2023, Douglas J. DeNoce, as an individual and Trustee of the Savannah N. DeNoce Trust, (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Watergate Property Owners Association, Inc.; S.H. Chavin, Inc. dba Community Property Management (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 25.

 

The First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges two causes of action: (1) private nuisance and (2) public nuisance. On March 6, 2024, the court granted Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages with leave to amend.

 

On March 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging the same two causes of action. On June 24, 2024, the court again granted Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages with leave to amend.

On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleging the same two causes of action. Defendant now again moves to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. Plaintiff opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

Demurrer[1]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.¿ (CCP, § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) In evaluating a demurrer, the court accepts the complainant’s properly pled facts as true and ignores contentions, deductions, and conclusory statements. (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1976) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Moreover, the court does not consider whether a plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations or the possible difficulty in making such proof. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿ The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

II.        Discussion

 

A.        Summary of Allegations in TAC

 

Plaintiff owns a townhouse located in the Watergate community in Westlake Village, CA. (TAC, ¶¶ 1, 8, 15.) The Watergate community is governed by Defendant Watergate Property Owners Association (“WPOA”) which hired S.H. Chavin, Inc. dba Community Property Management (“Community Property Management”) (collectively “Defendants” or “Association” herein) as the property manager to assist in carrying out the operations of WPOA. (Id., ¶¶ 10, 11, 16.) Plaintiff’s townhouse “is located at a congress of driveways, walkways, parking strips, and the service and delivery area.” (Id., ¶ 18.)

 

The TAC alleges Plaintiff suffered an ischemic stroke on or around October 2021 due to the increased noise and vibrations emitted from the parked vehicles of the community members that park in the spaces adjacent to his townhouse. (TAC, ¶¶ 1, 5, 17, 19, 47.) Plaintiff asserts he suffered a stroke due to the “ongoing nuisance from vehicle engine noise, entertainment system noise, door closure noise, and associated vehicle activity in the parking strips adjacent to his townhouse.” (Id., ¶ 17.) Plaintiff asserts that “parking in the parking strips adjacent to Plaintiff’s townhouse has a particular impact on Mr. DeNoce.” (Id., ¶ 18.)

 

While the two lots adjacent to Plaintiff’s townhouse have been present since Plaintiff acquired the townhouse, the noise nuisance did not begin until a few years before because there was minimal parking activity in the two adjacent lots. (TAC, ¶ 26.)  Plaintiff provided notices in writing about the noise to Defendants, including on August 19, 2019, informing Defendants “that the vehicle activity was a nuisance[.]” (Id., ¶¶ 3, 27, 35.) The August 19, 2019 notice as reflected in the meeting notes states: “He is bothered by the cars parking near his unit. It is a nuisance.” (Id., ¶ 27.) On June 18, 2021, he emailed CPM’s CEO and informed him that the nuisance was caused by “residents who park outside [Plaintiff’s] home overnight” and expressed concern about his health. (Id., ¶ 40)

 

Defendants WPOA and CPM control and exercise independent managerial authority of the parking strips next to Plaintiff’s townhouse. (TAC, ¶ 20.) Plaintiff asserts that the Defendants encouraged and failed to abate the noise, causing Plaintiff to suffer a stroke. (Id., 17.) Defendants, either directly or through their agents instructed other residents, like the Cornelison family and Graham family, to park in the two adjacent strips to Plaintiff’s townhouse, despite parking elsewhere prior to Plaintiff’s stroke. (Id., ¶¶ 29-31, 42.) Plaintiff asserts that after the November 19, 2019 notice, parking activity near the two parking strips adjacent to Plaintiff’s townhouse increased. (Id., ¶ 32.) Defendants engaged in this conduct to force Plaintiff to leave the Watergate community as they did not like that Plaintiff had prevented the implementation of WPOA and CPM policies. (Id., ¶ 3.)

 

Plaintiff asserts that WPOA’s Board and CPM’s management were aware that Plaintiff was diabetic and the failure to abate the nuisance affected Plaintiff’s sleep and health. (TAC, ¶¶ 3, 4, 34, 38.) Plaintiff’s doctor assert that the stroke “is consistent with a reasonable medical probability of his stroke having been triggered by hypertension that was caused by chronic disruptions of Mr. DeNoce’s sleep, stress related to Mr. DeNoce’s efforts to allow him to have regular sleep, and ultimately the significant emotional upset[.]” (Id., ¶ 36, Ex. B, C.)

 

Consequently, the WPOA Board, Dr. Theodore Corwin (Board President), Dana Knudsen (Vice President), Michael Lawler (Director), Tina Daniels (Director), Don Hoffman (Director), and Linda Tonkins (Director), and the CEO of CPM (Sheldon H. Chavin) acted with malice, oppression, and fraud in failing to control and abate the nuisance. (TAC, ¶¶ 4, 35.) Defendant asserts that the notices made Defendants aware that they failed to control and abate the nuisance and disrupted the Plaintiff’s sleep in the months and weeks leading up to his stroke. (Id., ¶ 35.)

 

B.        Pleading Standard for Punitive Damages

 

Defendants seek to strike the following from Plaintiff’s TAC:

 

1)     Page 23, Paragraph 68, request for punitive and exemplary damages;

2)     Page 23, Paragraph f., prayer for punitive damages; and

3)     Page 23, Paragraph h., prayer for exemplary damages.

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff fails to plead sufficient facts to support a prayer for punitive damages. Defendants assert the TAC fails to state how Defendants increase the noise and vibrations emitted from the two adjacent parking lots. While the court agrees that Defendants are not responsible for the noise emitted from ingress and egress of the vehicles or the noises caused by engine noise, door closing and music being played, the TAC does allege that Defendants or their agents assisted in creating and worsening the nuisance by instructing  the Cornelison and Graham families to park in the two adjacent strips to Plaintiff’s townhouse, despite parking elsewhere prior to Plaintiff’s stroke. (TAC, ¶¶ 29-31, 42.) At the pleading stage, this is sufficient as Plaintiff is not required to prove or specify which specific Defendant instructed the Cornelison and Graham families to park in the adjacent lots to Plaintiff’s townhouse.  Either Defendants possess such information or it is discoverable through the course of litigation.

 

The TAC alleges that three days before Plaintiff suffered his stroke Defendants encouraged the Cornelison and Graham families and other residents to park their vehicles near Plaintiff despite being aware of Plaintiff’s health issues. (TAC, ¶¶ 3, 4, 34, 38.) WPOA’s President, Theodor Corwin, is a doctor and on February 13, 2020, Plaintiff informed him that he had the “level of hemoglobin A1c was more than double the level for a diagnosis of diabetes.” Dr. Corwin, as a physician, understood the serious complications from arising from diabetes. (Id., ¶ 28.) Despite knowing Plaintiff’s diabetic condition, Defendant failed to abate the nuisance by stopping vehicles from parking adjacent to Plaintiff’s townhouse and instead encouraging vehicles to park there. The TAC further asserts that due to a prior lawsuit involving former members of WPOA filed in 2019, each current member of WPOA and CPM management knew Plaintiff had “a diabetic syncope episode” in 2019. (Id.,¶ 40.) On September 8, 2021, Plaintiff informed CPM’s CEO, Sheldon H. Chavin, that he was “battling serious health issues” and he asked Mr. Chavin and Ms. Tadecki to forward the letter to WPOA’s Board, who later confirmed receipt of the letter. (Id., ¶ 42.)

 

Therefore, the TAC sufficiently alleges facts to show that Defendants had knowledge that Plaintiff was a diabetic. The TAC, however, fails to show how Defendants obtained knowledge that Plaintiff suffered emotional stress, sleep deprivation, and hypertension due to the nuisance. According to Plaintiff’s doctor, in the month leading up to Plaintiff’s stroke, his diabetes was under control. “Prior to suffering the lacunar infarct, Mr. DeNoce had achieved meaningful positive results in his efforts to control his diabetes, hypertension, and to reduce his weight.” (TAC, Ex. C ¶ 10; see also TAC ¶¶ 36 -37, Ex. B at p. 9.) According to Plaintiff’s doctor:

 

The cause of Mr. DeNoce's pontine infarct that he suffered in October 2021 is consistent with a reasonable medical probability of his stroke having been triggered by chiefly by hypertension that was caused by multiple factors of which certainly include: (i) chronic disruptions of Mr. DeNoce's sleep in the few years prior to the pontine infarct, (ii) stress related to Mr. DeNoce's efforts to allow him to have regular sleep, and (iii) stress related to Mr. DeNoce's fractious interactions with his home owners association as to the noise from the parking strips. Those circumstances are consistent with a probable causation of Mr. DeNoce's stroke corresponding to hypertensive events as the primary trigger of the lacunar infarction.

 

(TAC, Ex. C, ¶ 10.)

 

There are no facts in the TAC to show that Plaintiff’s stroke was caused by his diabetes or that Defendants knew or should have known that Plaintiff’s diabetic condition made him particularly susceptible to a health injury due to the noise nuisance in comparison to other member of the Westlake community with the same health condition. “Generally, punitive damages may be awarded only if it is proven that the defendant engaged in conduct intended to cause injury or engaged in despicable conduct with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390, 410.)

 

There are no facts in the TAC showing how Defendants knew Plaintiff was suffering emotional distress, sleep deprivation, and hypertension due to the nuisance. There are no facts to support the allegation that Defendants acted with the intent or incautious disregard to Plaintiff’s health. The written notices to the Defendants only stated that the parking was a nuisance, and the TAC fails to state facts to show that Defendants knew Plaintiff’s health made him particularly vulnerable to suffering a health injury due to the noise. Therefore, the TAC fails to show that Defendants in failing to abate the nuisance acted with the intent or in conscious disregard for Plaintiff’s health. Moreover, the TAC fails to show that Defendants’ intended to worsen Plaintiff’s known diabetic condition when the TAC fails to show diabetes was a contributing or substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s stroke.

 

The court grants Defendants’ motion to strike. This is the third time the court grants Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages. (See CCP, § 435.5 [(e)(1) “In response to a motion to strike and before the case is at issue, a pleading shall not be amended more than three times, absent an offer to the trial court of additional facts to be pleaded that, if pleaded, would result in a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured.”].)  Therefore, unless Plaintiff can show that the pleading can be successfully amended, the leave to amend is denied.

 

The court also strikes Plaintiff’s sur reply in opposition to Defendants’ Motion on the basis that it was filed without leave of court. “Points raised for the first time in a reply brief will not be considered.” (Malmstrom v. Kaiser Aluminum & Chemical Corp. (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 299, 320, 231.)

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is granted without leave to amend.
