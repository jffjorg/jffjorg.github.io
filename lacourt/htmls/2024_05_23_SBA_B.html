CITY OF TORRANCE, et al.,

Hearing Date:                          May 22, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant City of Torrance (“City”)

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Martin Hutto

(1) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories—Employment Law

(2) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admission

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories—Employment is DENIED.

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFAs is DENIED.

            Defendant and Defense counsel are ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $3,500 within 20 days. 

BACKGROUND

            On April 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed this action against his employers, Defendants City of Torrance and Torrance Police Department alleging (1) violation of the Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights Act, Government Code §3300, et seq.; (2) declaratory relief; and (3) Labor Code §1102.5. 

            Plaintiff alleges that he was investigated and given educational reminders in connection with two arrests he made.  Plaintiff arrested suspects in a catalytic converter theft on May 17, 2021 and commented to one of the arrestees, “Welcome to Torrance.”  Plaintiff was investigated for the comment and invoked his rights under the Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights Act (POBRA) and/or the Meyers-Milias Brown Act, including use of a representative of his choice.  Defendants ultimately concluded the investigation and gave Plaintiff an educational reminder for misconduct based on the “Welcome to Torrance” comment.

            Plaintiff arrested a suspect who was disturbing the peace on March 4, 2022.  During the arrest, Plaintiff used force against the suspect, who ultimately stabbed Plaintiff’s partner in the clavical area with a pen and struck Plaintiff in the head.  Plaintiff was administratively investigated for his use of force during the arrest.  Defendant ultimately concluded that Plaintiff should be given an educational reminder to be more thorough in his reports of use of force.

            Plaintiff filed a personnel complaint against Chief Hart for inappropriate comments and social media posts on November 2, 2022.  Plaintiff alleges Torrance Police Department revived the Internal Affairs investigation against him after his complaint. 

            Plaintiff alleges Defendants violated his rights under POBRA, including by failing to provide proper notice of the nature of the investigations, subjecting Plaintiff to punitive action and/or threatening him with punitive action for exercising his rights under POBRA and placing comments adverse to his interest in his personnel file.  Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants committed whistleblower retaliation in response to his complaints about Chief Hart’s inappropriate comments and social media posts. 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §2030.300

(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

            (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

            (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

            (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

(2) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.

(d) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(e) If a party then fails to obey an order compelling further response to interrogatories, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of, or in addition to, that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

CCP §2033.290

(a) On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply:

            (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete.

            (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

            (2) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or any specific later date to which the requesting party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the requesting party waives any right to compel further response to the requests for admission.

(d) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(e) If a party then fails to obey an order compelling further response to requests for admission, the court may order that the matters involved in the requests be deemed admitted. In lieu of, or in addition to, this order, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

            If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories and RFAs. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (Wolcher) (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221; Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Sup.Ct. (Stendell) (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

DISCUSSION

            Parties’ Positions

            In separate motions, Defendant City of Torrance moves to compel further responses to (1) Form Interrogatories—Employment Nos. 201.3 and 207.2 and (2) RFA Nos. 3 and 4.  Defendant argues the objections asserted to the Form Interrogatories and RFAs are meritless.

            Defendant argues Plaintiff’s objection to Form Rog No. 201.3 on grounds that the City failed to list the “Adverse Employment Action” is baseless, because it is only Plaintiff who knows of the alleged adverse employment actions that caused him to file this complaint in the first place. 

            Defendant argues Plaintiff’s objection that the interrogatories seek information protected by the Pitchess process is baseless, because Plaintiff waived any Pitchess confidentiality.  Defendant argues the Form Interrogatories 201.3 and 201.7 do not seek information protected by Pitchess, which protects peace officer personnel records and records maintained by any state or local agency.

            Defendant argues further responses must be compelled in order to achieve the purpose of discovery.  Defendant argues the Form Interrogatories were drafted by the Judicial Council specifically for employment actions and were intended to avoid disputes about wording.

            Defendant makes the same arguments in support of its motion to compel further responses to the RFAs.  Defendant argues the RFAs seek relevant information and are not subject to a Pitchess objection.  Defendant argues the RFAs do not seek Pitchess information, and any Pitchess confidentiality has been waived by the Plaintiff by filing this action.  Defendant argues the remaining objections based on the RFAs being “compound,” seeking discovery on third parties based on the “any agents and assigns” language, the RFAs calling for legal conclusions and being vague and ambiguous are meritless. 

            In connection with each motion, Defendant asks that Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel be sanctioned.  Defendant asks that the Court impose sanctions in the amount of $3,820 in connection with the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories-Employment and $3,750 in connection with the Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFAs. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues the discovery was improperly electronically served.  Plaintiff argues he never agreed to such service and such service can only be stipulated to or ordered by the Court. 

            Plaintiff argues Form Interrogatories 201.3 and 207.2 both trigger the Pitchess statute.  Plaintiff argues this Court has already ordered that all information contained in confidential personnel files of Plaintiff and/or other officers should be filed under seal.  Plaintiff argues Defendant fails to cite any authority holding that Pitchess protections are waived by an officer filing a complaint against his employer.  Plaintiff argues both the public agency and the subject officer hold the confidentiality privilege established by Penal Code 832.7.  Plaintiff argues Defendant can only obtain information contained in the personnel files of a peace officer through a Pitchess motion and subject to the protections of a protective order pursuant to Evidence Code §1045(d) and (e).  Plaintiff argues no such motion has been brought and Defendant cannot obtain the requested information. 

            Plaintiff argues both Form Rog 201.3 and 207.2 trigger Pitchess.  Plaintiff argues 201.3 asks questions regarding adverse employment actions against Plaintiff, which is exactly the type of information subject to Pitchess.  Plaintiff argues 202.2 asks for information regarding Plaintiff’s complaint about unlawful conduct towards him, which again triggers Pitchess because such information is contained in the personnel file of a peace officer. 

            Plaintiff argues 201.3 is also defective, because Defendant did not list the “adverse employment actions” referred to in the interrogatory.  Plaintiff argues Form Rog 201.3 specifically requires Defendant to list the “adverse employment” actions referenced.

            Plaintiff argues the objections to the RFAs are also meritorious.  Plaintiff argues Defendant’s answer never put at issue that Plaintiff allegedly shoved, pushed or guided Tammi Jackson to a bench when she was arrested.  Plaintiff argues the RFA references a policy, which means it is not full and complete in and of itself.  Plaintiff agues the RFA also defines Plaintiff as including his agents and assigns, making it compound, disjunctive and disjunctive.  Plaintiff argues that, as with the Form Interrogatories, the RFAs seek information contained in a peace officer’s personnel file thereby triggering Pitchess.  Plaintiff argues there is no authority provided that he waived Pitchess by filing the complaint.  As to the RFAs, Plaintiff asserts the same Pitchess arguments he asserted to the Form Interrogatories. 

            Plaintiff argues that, as to RFA No. 4, he was required to reference the Torrance Police Department Policy Manual to answer.  Plaintiff argues an incomplete copy of the Policy Manual was attached to the RFAs that was dated after the subject incidents.  Plaintiff also argues whether he believes that an educational reminder falls under the disciplinary policy of Torrance Police Department is irrelevant to the action.  Plaintiff argues RFA No. 4 also does not define “nonpunitive” or “corrective.” 

            Plaintiff argues Defendant’s request for sanctions must be denied.  Plaintiff argues Defendant fails to comply with the notice requirements of CCP §2023.040.  Plaintiff argues the objections were not meritless and Plaintiff was justified in opposing.  Plaintiff argues the amount of sanctions requested for the motions is clearly inflated and should be denied on that ground alone. 

            On reply, Defendant argues the Pitchess objections are without merit.  Defendant argues the interrogatories seek information regarding Plaintiff’s contentions in this lawsuit.  Defendant argues the interrogatories are not seeking information from personnel records or an officer’s recollection of information in personnel records.  Defendant argues that, like the attorney-client privilege, the confidentiality protection under Pitchess does not apply to the underlying facts.  Defendant argues Pitchess only applies to protect records and information contained in the peace officer personnel file, not the details surrounding employment complaints.  Defendant argues the fact that it asserted a Pitchess objection in an administrative proceeding on behalf of the Chief of Police is irrelevant to whether Plaintiff’s Pitchess objection is valid. 

            Defendant argues that for purposes of Form Rog 201.3, only Plaintiff knows the “adverse employment actions” that he complaining of.  Defendant argues the motion was also timely filed on April 2, 2024, because April 1, 2024 was a court holiday.

            Defendant argues that RFA Nos. 3 and 4 are directly relevant to the complaint and defense raised thereto.  Defendant argues the RFAs were also clear and unambiguous.  Defendant argues the terms “shove” and “push” are not uncertain nor do they require a special definition.  Defendant reiterates, as with the Form Rogs, that the Pitchess objection is meritless. Defendant argues the Court should order Plaintiff to respond on behalf of himself alone, if it finds the “agents and/or assigns” language improper. 

            Defendant argues RFA No. 4 does not rely on a copy of the policy that was implemented after the incidents.  Defendant argues the footer date is the date on which the manual was reissued.  Defendant argues Plaintiff should also be familiar with the policy and he should not need to reference any outside documents to answer the RFA.  Defendant argues the use of the terms “nonpunitive” and “corrective action” does not render the RFAs ambiguous, as those terms have commonly understood meanings. 

            Defendant argues the RFAs, like the interrogatories, were properly served by email.  Defendant argues the motions were also timely filed on April 2, 2024.

            Procedural Issues

            The discovery requests were properly served by email pursuant to CRC Rule 2.251(c) and Los Angeles Superior Court rule 3.4.  “Except when personal service is otherwise required by statute or rule, a party or other person that is required to file documents electronically in an action must also serve documents and accept service of documents electronically from all other parties or persons, unless: (A)  The court orders otherwise, or (B)  The action includes parties or persons that are not required to file or serve documents electronically, including self-represented parties or other self-represented persons; those parties or other persons are to be served by non-electronic methods unless they affirmatively consent to electronic service.”  (CRC Rule 2.251(c).)  Electronic filing is mandatory in civil actions in Los Angeles County. (LASC Rule. 3.4(a).)

            The motions were also timely filed on April 2, 2024.  The responses in question were served by email on February 12, 2024. (Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Rogs, Wong Dec., ¶6; Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFAs, Wong Dec., ¶6.)  The motions were therefore due 45 days plus 2 court days after February 12, 2024, which would be April 2, 2024 due to the court holiday on April 1, 2024. 

            Pitchess objections to Form Rogs 201.3, 207.2 and RFA No. 3

            “In any case in which discovery or disclosure is sought of peace or custodial officer personnel records or records maintained pursuant to Section 832.5 of the Penal Code or information from those records, the party seeking the discovery or disclosure shall file a written motion with the appropriate court or administrative body upon written notice to the governmental agency that has custody and control of the records, as follows:

(1) In a civil action, the written notice shall be given at the times prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005 of the Code of Civil Procedure.” (Evid. C. §1043(a).) 

            “The law governing protection for the privacy of police officers in California is found in a series of statutes commonly referred to as ‘Pitchess statutes,’ specifically Penal Code sections 832.7 and 832.8 and Evidence Code sections 1043 and 1045.”  (Pasadena Police Officers Assn. v. Superior Court (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 268, 285.)  “The statutory scheme does not leave room for doubt.  Under subdivision (a) of section 832.7 of the Penal Code, ‘[p]eace officer personnel records ... or information obtained from these records, are confidential and shall not be disclosed in any criminal or civil proceeding except by discovery pursuant to Sections 1043 and 1046 of the Evidence Code....’ (Emphasis added.) Section 832.8 of the Penal Code explains that, as used in section 832.7, ‘personnel records’ means any file maintained by the employing agency under the officer's name and containing records relating to ‘marital status, family members, educational and employment history, home addresses, or similar information,’ medical history, election of employee benefits, complaints or discipline, and ‘[a]ny other information the disclosure of which would constitute an unwarranted invasion of personal privacy.’  Subdivision (a) of section 1043 provides, as relevant, that ‘[i]n any case in which discovery ... is sought of peace officer personnel records ... or information from those records, the party seeking the discovery ... shall file a written motion with the appropriate court ... [and give] written notice to the governmental agency which has custody and control of the records....’ (Emphasis added.) Subdivision (b) of section 1043 lists the items required to support the motion, including a showing by affidavit of ‘good cause for the discovery ... sought, setting forth the materiality thereof to the subject matter involved in the pending litigation....’” (Hackett v. Supr. Ct. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 96, 98-99 (officer’s objection to interrogatories seeking basic contact information and employment information based on Pitchess was proper, even if same information was obtainable elsewhere.) 

            “The fact that information in an officer's personnel records may also be found in an unprotected source does not impact the confidentiality of the personnel records themselves. The Pitchess statutes create a privilege for all information in peace officers' personnel files without regard to information found elsewhere.   The purpose and policy of the Pitchess statutes mandates that waiver of the privilege must be express. This is true even where, as here, the officers themselves placed the information in the public domain.  [¶]  Absent an express waiver of the privilege with respect to the confidential personnel information found in the Report, the officers retain Pitchess protections as to that information, even if the information is the same as or similar to information available elsewhere in the public domain.”  (Pasadena Police Officers Assn. v. Supr. Ct. (“Pasadena”) (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 268, 294.)

            Plaintiff objects to Form Rog 201.3, 207.2 and RFA No. 3 on grounds that it seeks “information” contained in confidential peace officer personnel files and therefore the information is only obtainable pursuant to a Pitchess motion brought pursuant to Evidence Code §1043.  The Court agrees. 

            Form Rog 201.3 asks if there were any other “adverse employment actions” and then contains subparts seeking the reasons for the adverse employment action, the contact information of each person making such adverse employment decision, the contact information of anyone who provided information relied on to take such action and the identity of all documents relied on in making such adverse employment action. 

            Plaintiff alleges whistleblower retaliation.  Plaintiff alleges he filed a complaint with Defendants regarding retaliation.  As such, this information would be part of his personnel file.  Plaintiff’s Pitchess objection is therefore proper as to Form Rog 201.3. 

            Form Rog 207.2 likewise pertains to information that would be contained in Plaintiff’s personnel file.  207.2 asks if Plaintiff every complained to the Defendants about any of the unlawful conduct alleged in the pleadings.  Plaintiff alleges that he did complain to Defendants about the alleged unlawful conduct and that internal investigations were undertaken in response.  Again, such information would be contained in Plaintiff’s personnel file.  Plaintiff’ Pitchess objection is proper as to Form Rog 207.2.

            RFA No. 3 asks “Admit that YOU failed to include in YOUR supplemental report the fact YOU shoved Tammi Jackson towards the bench in the jail booking room while she was handcuffed.”  (Plaintiff’s Opposition, 3:16-19.)  Defendant’s separate statement only contains a redacted version of the RFA but Plaintiff’s opposition sets for the RFA in full.  Whether Plaintiff shoved Jackson towards the bench in the jail booking room is information that is contained in the personnel file of Plaintiff and potentially other peace officers, as reflected by Defendant’s decision to redact the RFA itself.  Plaintiff’s objection based on Pitchess is proper as to RFA No. 3. 

            Defendant also fails to cite any authority holding that an officer implicitly waives any Pitchess protection by filing an lawsuit against his or her employer.  Moreover, such a position would be contrary to the discussion of waiver in Pasadena. 

            Defendant fails to cite to any authority analogizing Pitchess to the attorney-client privilege, such that underlying facts surrounding a protected communication or underlying facts in the communication are not protected.  In fact, such a rule would seem counter to the rules set forth in Pasadena and Hackett, which hold that even information that is available elsewhere can only be discovered pursuant to the Pitchess procedure if it can be found in the personnel file of a peace officer. 

            Objections to Form Rog 201.3 based on failure to list “adverse employment actions”

            Plaintiff’s objection to Form Rog 201.3 on grounds that Defenant failed to complete the interrogatory is proper.  Form Rog 201.3 asks that the Defendant list all the actions it intended to include as “adverse employment actions” for purposes of the interrogatory.  Defendant left that section blank.  Contrary to Defendant’s position and based on the text of the Form Interrogatory, it is Defendant who must define those adverse employment actions as the “asking party” for the Plaintiff to respond.  (Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Rogs—Employment, Wong Dec., Ex. A, Form Rog 201.3, p. 18.) 

            Objections to RFA Nos. 3 and 4 as improperly compound and vague, ambiguous and uncertain

            RFAs, like interrogatories, cannot be “compound, conjunctive or disjunctive.”  (CCP §2033.060(f).)  The “purpose…is to prevent questions worded so as to require more information than could be obtained by 35 separate questions.  How strictly this rule will be applied remains to be seen. Arguably, any question containing an ‘and’ or ‘or’ is compound and conjunctive.”  (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1291.)  The “rule should probably apply only where more than a single subject is covered by the question. Questions regarding the same subject should be allowed although they include an ‘and’ or ‘or.’ For example: ‘State your first name, middle name and last name, and your current address and telephone number.’ Since only one subject is involved—identification of responding party—the question should not be objectionable because of the ‘ands' used.”  (Id. (quoting Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide:  Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter Group 2009), 8:978.1, 8:979.) 

            Defendant’s RFAs are improperly compound.  “YOU” and “YOUR” refer to Plaintiff Martin Hutto, his agents or assigns.  Each RFA therefore covers more than one subject and is impermissibly compound.  For example RFA No.3 asks Hutto to admit that Hutto, an unidentified number of agents and an unidentified number of assigns each failed to include in the supplemental report the fact that Hutto, his agents or assigns shoved Tammi Jackson towards the bench in the jail booking room.  The RFA is also vague and ambiguous as to “agents” and “assigns,” nor does the RFA make sense given that it assumes Hutto’s agents and assigns allegedly shoved Jackson. 

            Defendant’s RFA No. 4 is also vague and ambiguous, because it asks that Plaintiff admit an educational reminder is a “non-punitive” “corrective action” under the Torrance Police Department Manual, Policy 1006.18.2.  Policy 1006.18.2 was attached to the RFAs as Exhibit A.  (Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFA No. 4, Wong Dec., Ex. A, p. 19.)  Nowhere in section 1006.18.2 is the term “non-punitive” defined, nor did Defendant define it in the definitional section of the RFAs.  “Non-punitive” is not entirely subjective and therefore a definition is required; otherwise, it is vague and ambiguous. 

            Defendant’s Motions to Compel Further Responses is DENIED

            Defendant’s Motions to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories 201.3 and 207.2 are denied.  Plaintiff’s objections are meritorious and sustained.

            Sanctions

            As the prevailing party, Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions, unless the Court finds the Defendant acted with substantial justification or other reasons justify denial of the sanctions request.  Defendant fails to establish that it acted with substantial justification. 

            Plaintiff requests $2,500 in sanctions per motion against Defendant and Defense counsel.  Plaintiff’s counsel claims eight hours per motion @ $700/hr hour.  Plaintiff is only requested $2,500 per motion.  The Court finds that the reasonable attorneys fees for the motions is $3,500 total.  Defendant is ordered to pay sanctions within 20 days in the amount of $3,500 total for both motions. 

ORDER

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories—Employment is DENIED.

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFAs is DENIED.

            Defendant and Defense counsel are ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $5000 within 20 days.
