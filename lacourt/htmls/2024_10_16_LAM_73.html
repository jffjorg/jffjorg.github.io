DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30828    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: 73

10/16/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

YEDAM, INC., et al. v. DANIEL LEE, et al. (21STCV30828)

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainants/opposing party:  Dale Park (Law Offices of Dale J. Park)

Counsel for Cross-Defendants/moving party: Frederick Lee (Frederick W. Lee Law Firm APC)

 

(1)   CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE) PROPOUNDED ON CROSS-COMPLAINANT DANIEL N. LEE AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed on 07/01/2024)

(2)   CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (SET ONE) PROPOUNDED ON CROSS-COMPLAINANT DANIEL N. LEE AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed on 07/01/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee is GRANTED.

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee is granted in part and denied in part.  Further responses to Request Nos. 12, 14-18 and 25 are DENIED. Further responses to Request Nos. 1-10 and 19-24 are GRANTED.

 

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Yedam, Inc. (“Yedam") and Eun Kyung Choi (“Choi") brought this breach of loyalty and unfair business practices action against Daniel N. Lee (“Lee") and Jin Sook Lee (together, “the Lees"), and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“DM Cosmetics").

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 5 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Declaratory Relief.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Yedam imports and purchases products including candles and scented oils that are sold at various retail locations such as department stores in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 9.) On or about February 2017 Defendant Daniel Lee began working for Yedam in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 10.) In his capacity as an employee, Defendant Daniel Lee was significantly involved in dealing with: a) manufacturers that produced candles, scented oils or related products; b) the manufacturers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; c) the buyers at the department stores in Korea that purchased such products from Yedam; d) the buyers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; and e) the retail locations in both the United States of America and South Korea where such items were sold at. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Notwithstanding his duty of loyalty, Defendant Daniel Lee, in conjunction with his wife Defendant Angela Lee, have misused Yedam’s proprietary information in order to enrich themselves financially. (FAC, ¶ 24). Defendants’ actions have caused Buyers for Department Stores in South Korea to cease purchasing products from Plaintiff Yedam and/or have caused the closure of retail locations in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 26).

 

The Lees and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“Cross-Complainants" or “Defendants”) cross-complained against Yedam, Choi, Olive House LLC, and Lamis Tania, LLC (“Cross-Defendants"). In Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal. App. 4th 1277 the responding party (to the discovery request) also made an objection based on Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.060(d). Clement, at pages 1287 and 1288.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, counsel for Cross-Defendants submitted a declaration attesting that the parties discussed the alleged deficient responses via email but an agreement was not reached. (Muriella Decl., ¶¶ 8-11.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

B.      Legal Standard

¿  

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).¿ 

¿ 

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

C.      Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One)

Cross-Defendants request further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 1-33.

 

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee objected to each interrogatory, stating:

 

“Objection on the grounds that this set is not in compliance with the Code in that each interrogatory is not full and complete, this set includes instructions and definitions, and this set includes subparts, or compound or conjunctive interrogatories by way of “DEFINITIONS” which include subparts, or a compound or conjunctive interrogatories. CCP Section 2030.060 (d) and (f). Responding Party reserves all of his/her/its right to object on other grounds.”

 

CCP Section 2030.060 (d) requires: “Each interrogatory shall be full and complete in and of itself. No preface or instruction shall be included with a set of interrogatories unless it has been approved under Chapter 17 (commencing with Section 2033.710).” CCP Section 2030.060 (f) provides: “No specially prepared interrogatory shall contain subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive question.”

 

While the Court agrees that Cross-Defendants’ interrogatories are not compliant with CCP Sections 2030.060 (d) and (f), the Court does not find that this deficiency warrants denial of Cross-Defendants’ motion to compel further responses. In Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal. App. 4th 1277, 1287-88, the court found that the rule that each specially prepared interrogatory must be “full and complete” by itself (§ 2030.060, subd. (d)), together with the rule that “[n]o specially prepared interrogatory shall contain subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive question” (§ 2030.060, subd. (f)) “prevent evasion of the statutory limit on the number of interrogatories that one party may propound to another.” As the court stated in Clement:

 

“As to the specific provision at issue in this case, the Reporter's Note continues: “Prefaces, Instructions, Definitions, and Sub–Parts. This provision is included to prevent wrangling about whether a party is evading the 35–question limit by using prefaces, instructions, definitions, and sub-parts to exceed the substance of the restriction it imposes. The Commission does not believe that boiler plate interrogatories, prefaces, instructions, definitions, or sub-parts are per se abusive. Instead, it recognizes the need to control use of these devices lest they become the vehicle for evasion of the 35–question limit.”

 

“Plaintiffs do not contend that any of the interrogatories to which they objected on this basis were unclear, or that the interrogatories, considered either singly or collectively, in any way undermined or violated the presumptive numerical limit of 35 interrogatories of section 2030.030. Yet plaintiffs seized on what might have been at most an arguable technical violation of the rule, to object to interrogatories that were clear and concise where the interrogatories did not even arguably violate the presumptive numerical limitation set by statute. In so doing, plaintiffs themselves engaged in the type of gamesmanship and delay decried by the drafters of the Act.”

 

(Ibid., (emphasis in original).) Similarly here, Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee does not argue that the interrogatories are unclear or violated the presumptive numerical limit of 35 interrogatories. Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that further responses are warranted. Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee is GRANTED.

 

                                                               i.      Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Both parties request the Court impose monetary sanctions. As discussed above, the Court finds further responses are warranted. However, since Cross-Defendants’ interrogatories were not compliant with CCP Sections 2030.060 (d) and (f), the Court does not find that Cross-Complainant acted without substantial justification in objecting to Cross-Defendants’ interrogatories. The Court declines to impose sanctions.  

 

D.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents (Set One)

 

Cross-Defendants request further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 1-10, 12, 14-25.

 

Requests Nos. 1-10 seek all documents identified in response to corresponding Special Interrogatories. As discussed above, the Court finds further responses to the special interrogatories are warranted. Therefore, the Court grants Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

 

Requests Nos. 12 and 14 seek all documents identified in response to corresponding Form Interrogatories. Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee responded by stating that no documents were identified in the underlying form interrogatories, thus, he is unable to comply with this demand. Cross-Defendants fail to show why further responses are warranted. Since Cross-Complainant Lee has stated why he cannot comply with these demands, the Court denies Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

 

Requests Nos. 15-18 and 25 seek documents or ESI relating to communications at any time from April 29, 2019 up to and including the present between Cross-Complainant Lee and any buyer of the PRODUCT. Cross-Complainant objects to each request stating that the term “PRODUCT” is vague, ambiguous, overbroad, too general and too indefinite. The Court agrees. Cross-Defendants define “PRODUCT” as candles, diffusers and/or scented oils. However, Cross-Defendants’ definition is overbroad, and it is unclear specifically what candles, diffusers and/or scented oils they are referring to. For instance, if Cross-Defendants’ are only referring to the products bearing the trademark, then it should be clearly set forth. The Court denies Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

 

Requests Nos. 19-24 seek documents or ESI relating to communications at any time from April 29, 2019 up to and including the present between Cross-Complainant Lee and Eun Kyung Choi regarding either Yedam, Inc., DM Cosmetics, or Dani Mackenzie. Cross-Complainant objects to each request stating that “regarding Yedam, Inc.”, “regarding DM Cosmetics” and “regarding Dani Mackenzie” are vague, ambiguous, overbroad, too general and too indefinite. Further, while Cross-Complainant provides boilerplate objections and states in a conclusory manner that they will suffer undue hardship in searching these documents, Cross-Complainant fails to provide any evidence as to this undue hardship. The party objecting to a discovery request on this basis bears the burden of supplying evidence of “the quantum of work required.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549.)¿¿The Court grants Cross-Defendants’ request for further responses to these requests.

Further responses to Request Nos. 1-10, 19-24 are GRANTED.

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Both parties request the Court impose monetary sanctions. Here, the motion is granted and denied in part. The Court declines to impose sanctions.

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.  In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) 

 

“The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

C.      Denial or Continuance of Motion Pursuant to CCP section 437c(h)

 

As an initial matter, the Court addresses whether Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication should be denied or continued pursuant to CCP section 437c(h).

 

Under CCP section 437c(h), the Court may continue or deny a motion for summary judgment, summary adjudication, or both when a party submits facts in a declaration to demonstrate that essential evidence may exist but cannot then be presented to oppose the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(h).) Where the opposing party submits an adequate affidavit showing that essential facts may exist but cannot be presented in a timely manner, the Court must either deny the motion or grant a continuance. (Dee v. Vintage Petroleum, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 30, 34-35.) 

 

Defendants argue that essential evidence exists but cannot be presented to oppose the instant Motion. Defendants assert in a declaration by their counsel, Randolph Catanese, that they have not been able to take the deposition of Plaintiff or Cross-Defendants. (RJN Ex. B, Catanese Decl., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication references multiple declarations made by Plaintiff and Cross-Defendants. (RJN Ex. B, Catanese Decl., ¶ 10.) Mr. Catanese states that he believes essential evidence may or will be obtained from the Plaintiff and the Cross-Defendants which may or will establish the existence of triable issues of fact, once he has had the opportunity to examine them regarding their recollection, knowledge and information related to documents produced in this case. (RJN Ex. B, Catanese Decl., ¶ 11.) Defendants contend that they issued deposition notices for Plaintiff and Cross-Defendants on April 30, 2024. (RJN Ex. B, Catanese Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff and Cross-Defendants objected to the notices, the parties attempted to meet and confer, but Defendants contend they were not provided alternative deposition dates. (RJN Ex. B, Catanese Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Thereafter, on June 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Motion for the Appointment of a Discovery Referee, which is scheduled for hearing on September 3, 2024, ( the motion was denied that day) the same day as the hearing for Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication. (RJN Ex. B, Catanese Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants had ample time to conduct the depositions of Plaintiff and Cross-Defendants. Plaintiff also argues that Defendants failed to provide the necessary declaration in support of their opposition outlining the facts justifying further discovery.

 

However, the Court notes that Defendants’ provided the declaration of their counsel as Exhibit B to their Request for Judicial Notice. Further, the Court finds that Defendants’ counsel’s declaration sufficiently demonstrates the need for additional discovery. Based on the foregoing, the Court continues Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication to allow Defendants and Cross-Complainants an opportunity to obtain essential evidence necessary to oppose the Motion pursuant to CCP section 437c(h).

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is CONTINUED to November 19, 2024.

 

III. DISPOSITION

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is CONTINUED to November 19, 2024.

MENDEZ v. DINO’S BURGERS FAST FOOD, INC., et al. (23STCV29774)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Eli Banayan (Bana Legal Group, PC)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Timothy R. Casey (Law Offices of Timothy R. Casey)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS TO DEFENDANT DINO’S BURGERS FAST FOOD, INC., SET ONE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 07/15/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, to Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. in the reduced amount of $2,670. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 6, 2023, Plaintiff Diego Lopez Mendez filed this action against Defendants Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.; Dino’s Main St, Inc.; Konstantinos Pantazis and George Pantazis.

 

On January 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

 

C/A 1: Failure To Provide Meal Periods

C/A 2: Failure To Provide Rest Periods

C/A 3: Failure To Pay All Wages Due Upon Termination: Waiting Time Penalties

C/A 4: Failure To Pay All Wages Earned

C/A 5: Failure To Pay Minimum Wage

C/A 6: Failure To Issue Accurate Itemized Wage Statements

C/A 7: Failure To Timely Produce Personnel File

C/A 8: Violations Of California’s Unfair Competition Act

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff was employed by Defendants as a dishwasher from in or about 2017, until the termination of Plaintiff’s employment on August 29, 2023. Plaintiff alleges multiple violations of California’s Labor Code.

 

On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and request for sanctions. No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel declares that a meet and confer letter was sent to Plaintiff’s counsel on June 12, 2024, explaining why the discovery responses were deficient and requesting further responses. (Banayan Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s counsel also states another correspondence was sent on June 26, 2024 asking Defense Counsel if Defendant was amenable to participating in an Informal Discovery Conference to address the issues. (Id., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff did not receive a response. (Id., ¶ 8.) The Court finds that counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” To the extent there is any doubt in whether these records should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) 

  

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

Plaintiff asserts that Defendant improperly responded to numerous requests without stating whether or not the activity demanded would be allowed “either in whole or in part” and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of Defendant and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production. Defendant also failed to state whether or not documents exist, whether or not a reasonable search and diligent inquiry had been conducted.

 

Upon review of Defendant’s responses, the Court agrees and notes that Defendant has failed to oppose the instant motion. “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion…is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.” (Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.” (Id.)

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s  further responses to Request for Production, Set One. 

 

C.      Sanctions

 

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.) 

 

Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, subd. (a).) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, subd. (d).) Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 provides that a court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery even if no opposition was filed.  

 

Here, the Court finds that Defendant engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to make good faith efforts to respond to Plaintiff’s Request for Production, Set One. Plaintiff requests $3,970 in sanctions for counsel’s work on this motion, including 4 hours spent on the instant motion, an anticipated 2 additional hours to review the opposition, prepare a reply and appear at the hearing, and $70 filing fee. The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced total amount of $2,670 (4 hours of attorney time at the rate of $650 an hour and $70 filing fee).

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, to Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. in the reduced amount of $2,670. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order.
