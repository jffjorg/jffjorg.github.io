DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV02921    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: H

Ramirez v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc., Case No. 23PSCV02921

ORDER CONTINUING HEARING ON DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

The Court is inclined to continue the hearing on Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the Second Cause of Action for Fraudulent Inducement—Concealment, in order to permit additional discovery to be conducted. The Court will hear further from counsel at the hearing regarding the timing of a continued hearing date and briefing deadlines.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff Victor Paredes Ramirez (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

 

          On November 2, 2021, Plaintiff entered into an express written contract with American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) regarding a new 2022 Honda CR-V, VIN # 7FARW1H75NE003437. The subject vehicle is equipped with a computerized driver-assisting safety system known as ‘Honda Sensing,” which includes adaptive cruise control, lane departure warnings, and autonomous braking known as the Collision Mitigation Braking System (“CMBS”). The Honda Sensing system suffers from a defect that causes the various subsystems within it to malfunction dangerously while the vehicles are driven (“Sensing Defect”). Defendant knew or should have known about the Sensing Defect before the subject vehicle was sold and failed to disclose same.

 

On September 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Defendant and Does 1-10 for (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act—Breach of Express Warranty, and (2) Fraudulent Inducement—Concealment.

 

The Final Status Conference is set for September 30, 2025. Trial is set for October 14, 2025.

 

Pending before the Court is Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication on the second cause of action for fraudulent inducement—concealment.

 

 

Discussion

Defendant moves the Court for an order granting summary adjudication in its favor and against Plaintiff as follows:

          Issue No. 1: Plaintiff’s second cause of action (i.e., for Fraudulent Inducement—Concealment) fails, because Plaintiff cannot prove that Defendant concealed a material fact;

          Issue No. 2: Plaintiff’s second cause of action fails, because Plaintiff cannot prove that Defendant owed him a duty of disclosure because there was no direct transaction between the parties in which information could be disclosed;

          Issue No. 3: Plaintiff’s second cause of action fails, because Plaintiff cannot prove damages as he lacks evidence to establish the difference in the market value of the Insight and the value of the vehicle he received;

          Issue No. 4: Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages fails, because Plaintiff lacks evidence to establish malicious, fraudulent or oppressive conduct, or corporate ratification of any malicious, fraudulent or oppressive conduct.

 

Request for Continuance

“If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (h).)

“To mitigate summary judgment’s harshness, the statute’s drafters included a provision making continuances—which are normally a matter within the broad discretion of trial courts—virtually mandated.” (Dee v. Vintage Petroleum, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 30, 34 [quotations and citation omitted].) “Where the opposing party submits an adequate affidavit showing that essential facts may exist but cannot be presented timely, the court must either deny summary judgment or grant a continuance.” (Id. [citation omitted].)

On December 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed an “Affidavit of Timothy Lupinek Regarding Plaintiff’s Opposition to Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(h).”

Timothy Lupinek (“Lupinek”) attests, in relevant part, that depositions of Defendant’s Person Most Qualified and Custodian of Records, Scott Hunter (“Hunter”) and Chris Martin (“Martin”) were taken on December 13 and 18, 2024, respectively, in case styled Meraz v. Case Number: 24PSCV00895    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: H

Guerrero Jr. v. FCA US LLC, Case No. 24PSCV00895

ORDER ON MOTION TO COMPEL

Plaintiff Fernando Guerrero Jr.’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Qualified is GRANTED in part. The parties are directed to further meet and confer as to the categories of documents, following a review of the Department H Case Management Conference Standing Order.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff Fernando Guerrero Jr. (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

 

On December 22, 2021, Plaintiff purchased a 2022 Jeep Wagoneer, VIN No. 1C4SJVBT0NS104042 (“subject vehicle”). The subject vehicle suffers from various defects which have not been repaired within a reasonable number of attempts.

 

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against FCA US LLC (“Defendant”) and Does 1-50 for: (1) Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code section 1793.2, (2) Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 1793.2. (3) Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code section 1793.2, (4) Breach of Express Written Warranty (Civil Code section 1791.2  subdivision (a); section 1794), and (5) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civil Code section 1791.1; section 1794).

 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s Personal Most Qualified (“PMQ”). For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted in part.



Legal Standard

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.230).

“If, after service of a deposition notice, . . . a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection. . ., fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

“A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. . ., or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contracted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b).)

“If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction. . ., unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, for an order compelling Defendant to designate and produce for deposition its PMQ on all categories identified in Plaintiff’s “Notice of Deposition of the Person Most Qualified for FCA US, LLC and Demand to Produce Documents at Deposition” (“Deposition Notice”).

Plaintiffs’ counsel Mark W. Gibson (“Gibson”) represents, and/or the accompanying exhibits reflect, as follows:

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with the Deposition Notice, setting the deposition for August 16, 2024. (Gibson Decl., ¶ 11, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff never received an objection to the deposition. (Id.) On August 16, 2024, Gibson requested a “Certificate of Non-Appearance.” (Id., ¶ 12, Exh. 3.) On August 19, 2024, Gibson was served with the “Certificate of Non-Appearance.” (Id., ¶ 16, Exh. 5.) On October 10, 2024, Gibson emailed Defendant’s counsel, requesting three available dates for the deposition. (Id., ¶ 13, Exh. 4.) On October 11, 14, 15 and 16, 2024, Gibson reiterated his request to Defendant’s counsel. (Id., ¶ 14, Exh. 4.) Defendant has failed to provide Plaintiff with any available deposition dates, as of the October 18, 2024 motion filing date. (Id., ¶ 15).

In opposition, Defendant argues that the motion should be denied because Defendant “has already proposed a date to set the PMQ deposition.” Defendant goes on to suggest that Plaintiff “seeks to manufacture a discovery dispute where there is none.” However, this argument is not well-taken because Defendant did not offer a deposition date until December 31, 2024—more than two months after Plaintiff’s motion was filed and five days after Defendant’s opposition was due.[FN] Moreover, the deposition date offered (March 12, 2025) is almost seven months after the deposition date that was initially noticed by Plaintiff.

Accordingly, the motion is granted in part and the deposition is ordered to go forward. As to the deposition date, the parties are ordered to meet and confer as to Defendant’s proposed deposition date of March 12, 2025. Moreover, Defendant is instructed to come prepared to the hearing with at least two alternative dates its PMQ is available for deposition in February or March 2025. 

As to the categories of documents sought in connection with the deposition, the parties are directed to meet and confer, either in person or by telephone or videoconference call, and file a status report by no later than January 31, 2025, to the extent that there are remaining disputes. The Court is not inclined to require Defendant to produce documents that go beyond the scope of its Case Management Standing Order. (See Department H Case Management Standing Order, Section D(2) (“Song-Beverly Warranty Litigation”.)

Lewis v. Mann, et al., Case No. 24PSCV01176

 

ORDER ON MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL 

Counsel for Plaintiff Ahlana Lewis’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED, effective upon the filing of a proof of service showing service of the final order upon Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last known address.  

 

Background 

Plaintiff Ahlana Lewis (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she sustained injuries and damages in a June 19, 2023 motor vehicle collision at the 210 Freeway eastbound and Sunflower Avenue in Glendora. 

On April 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Jaswinder Mann, Chandi Transport Inc., and Does 1-20 for (1) motor vehicle and (2) general negligence. 

Pending before the Court is Victory Law Group, LLP’s motion to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff. 

 

Discussion

Victory Law Group, LLP seeks to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff. 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398.) 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure § 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)). The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

Attorney Erik S. Velie (“Velie”) attests that “[t]here exists a total breakdown in communication between Plaintiff and counsel to the extent that this Counsel is prevented from effectively representing Plaintiff in prosecuting this action. There has been a complete, absolute and irreconcilable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship.” 

Velie states that he served Plaintiff by mail at Plaintiff’s last known address with copies of the motion papers served with his declaration and that he has confirmed within the past 30 days that the address is current, by telephone.

The motion is not opposed. The Court determines that the requirements of Rules of Court Rule 3.1362 enumerated above have been sufficiently met.

Accordingly, the motion is granted, effective upon the filing of a proof of service showing service of the final order upon Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last known address.    

Case Number: 24PSCV01437    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: H

          Pimentel v. Rentokil North America, Inc., et al., Case No. 24PSCV01437

ORDER ON MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

Defendants Rentokil North America, Inc. and Richard Thongsavanh’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. The case is ordered stayed, pending completion of the arbitration.


I.        Background

In this wrongful termination case, plaintiff Jose Antonio Pimentel (“Pimentel”) has sued his former employer, Rentokil North America, Inc. (“Rentokil”), and his former manager, Richard Thongsavanh (“Thongsavanh”) (together, “Defendants”), in connection with Pimentel’s brain cancer diagnosis. Specifically, Pimentel’s complaint alleges that before being diagnosed with cancer, he worked as a pest control technician for Rentokil but never received the proper training for the handling of hazardous chemicals, even though his manager falsely certified that Pimentel had received the requisite training. Pimentel further alleges that after being diagnosed with cancer and while he was undergoing chemotherapy, Rentokil unlawfully discriminated against him and terminated him based on his medical condition.  

On August 5, 2024, Guadalupe Pimentel (“G. Pimentel” or “Plaintiff”) was substituted in as Plaintiff pursuant to a motion granted by the Court.

Plaintiff’s First Amendment Complaint asserts several causes of action against Rentokil, Thongsavanh, and Does 1 through 10, including, among others, discrimination, retaliation, wrongful termination, failure to provide overtime wages, unlawful and unfair business practices, and negligent hiring, supervising, and retention. 

Pending before the Court is Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration and stay all further proceedings pending completion of arbitration. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted. 

II.      Legal Standard 

“A written provision in . . . a contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract . . . or an agreement in writing to submit to arbitration an existing controversy arising out of such a contract. . . shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. . .” (9 U.S.C. § 2.) 

“A party seeking to compel arbitration has the burden under the FAA to show (1) the existence of a valid, written agreement to arbitrate; and, if it exists, (2) that the agreement to arbitrate encompasses the dispute at issue.” (Ashbey v. Archstone Property Management, Inc. (2015) 785 F.3d 1320, 1323.)

III.     Discussion

Defendants move the Court for an order compelling binding arbitration of all of Plaintiff’s claims and staying all further judicial proceedings pending completion of arbitration.

A.       Evidentiary Objections 

The Court rules on Defendants’ evidentiary objections as follows: Sustained as to Nos. 1-6 and overruled as to No. 7. 

The Court rules on Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections as follows: Sustained as to No. 4 and Sustained as to No. 7 in part (i.e., as to the phrase “To ensure that all mailings were promptly received by the proper Rentokil employee” only) and overruled as to Nos. 1-3, 5, 6 and 8, and No. 7 in part.
 

B.       Business and Professions Code Section 17918 

At the outset, the Court addresses Plaintiff’s contention that Defendants’ motion is barred by Business and Professions Code section 17918, on the basis that Rentokil’s fictitious business name, “Western Exterminator Company,” was never registered.

“Section 17918 provides. . . that where a party transacts business under a fictitious business name, the party may not ‘maintain any action upon or on account of any contract made, or transaction had, in the fictitious business name in any court of this state until the fictitious business name statement has been executed, filed, and published as required by this chapter.’” (Villareal v. LAD-T, LLC (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 446, 457.) “Although the requirement for a fictitious business statement ordinarily applies to bar a plaintiff from maintaining an action on a contract in the name of a fictitious business, the requirement similarly applies to motions to compel arbitration because ‘[a] proceeding to compel arbitration is in essence a suit in equity to compel specific performance of a contract.’” (Id. at 457.) 

Plaintiff points out that Defendants’ counsel produced a Fictitious Business Name Statement with a June 5, 2024 filing date (“June 5, 2024 Statement”) for “Western Exterminators” but not “Western Exterminator Company,” which improperly reflects a residential address as the principal place of business and incorrectly lists the “date registrant commenced to transact business under the fictitious name” as  “N/A.” (J. Govea Decl., ¶ 11, Exh. J.) In response, Defendants explain that (1) the June 5, 2024 Statement was prepared by former employee, Teresa Gifford, who worked remotely and listed her residential address and “-LEGAL,” from where she worked full-time, as Rentokil’s street address (Thaggard Decl., ¶¶ 3-8, Exh. 1); (2) Rentokil uses its brand names “Western Exterminators” and “Western Exterminator Company” in California interchangeable (Id., ¶ 11); (3) Rentokil maintains a City of Los Angeles Tax Registration Certificate in the name of “Rentokil North America Inc.,” as well as a City of Santa Monica Business License Certificate in the business names of “Western Exterminator Company” and “Rentokil North America Inc.” (Id., ¶¶ 9 and 10, Exhs. 2 and 3) and that (4) on December 31, 2024, Rentokil submitted an amended Fictitious Business Name Statement (“December 31, 2024 Statement”) identifying both “Western Exterminators” and “Western Exterminator Company” as Rentokil’s fictitious business names. (Id., ¶ 12, Exh. 4.) The December 31, 2024 Statement further reflects that the “date registrant commenced to transact business under the fictitious name” has been corrected from “N/A” to “09/2012.” (Id.) 

The Court agrees with Defendants’ position that the motion should not be denied on this basis, even if Rentokil had not properly filed a Fictitious Business Name Statement until December 31, 2024, because any such deficiency has been adequately corrected by the December 31, 2024 Statement. (Villareal, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at 457 [“Failure to comply with the fictitious-name statutes does not make the parties’ promises, agreements, and transactions invalid as such. Noncompliance merely prevents a fictitiously named business from enforcing obligations owed to it until it places on record its true nature and ownership”]; see also, Templeton Action Committee v. County of San Luis Obispo (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 427, 432 [the “sole penalty” for failure to comply “is a bar from maintaining an action on contracts made in the fictitious business name until the statement is filed”].)

C.       Meet and Confer 

Next, the Court addresses Plaintiff’s argument that Defendants failed to meet and confer prior to filing the instant motion. As an initial matter, the Court is not aware of any statutory requirement mandating that a meet and confer be conducted. 

Regardless, the Court notes that Defendants’ counsel provided Plaintiff’s counsel with numerous documents and information prior to filing the instant motion, including Plaintiff’s personnel records, Rentokil’s fictitious business name registration, Plaintiff’s training completion record, and an explanation of the onboarding process by which Rentokil employees logged into Rentokil’s online human resources system (“UKG”). (Jackson Decl., ¶ 4, 2:22-23, 3:16-20.) Defendants’ counsel averred in his declaration accompanying the motion that Plaintiff’s counsel advised that Plaintiff’s spouse and mothers were “confident that Plaintiff would not write his name the way it appears on the [Employment Agreement],” but did not provide any further explanation. (Id., ¶ 4, 3:7-9 and ¶ 5.)

Accordingly, the Court rejects Plaintiff’s meet and confer argument and will proceed to the merits of the motion.

D.      Merits

 

          (1)      Existence of Agreement to Arbitrate

Defendants contend, and Plaintiff does not dispute, that the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies here.

“Under both federal and state law, the threshold question presented by a petition to compel arbitration is whether there is an agreement to arbitrate.” (Cheng-Canindin v. Renaissance Hotel Associates (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 676, 683; Van Ness Townhouses v. Mar Industries Corp. (1988) 862 F.2d 754, 756 [the “threshold inquiry is whether the parties agreed to arbitrate”].)  

“[C]ourts generally apply state law principles governing the formation of contracts when deciding whether an agreement to arbitrate exists in the first instance.” (Sellers v. JustAnswer LLC (2021) 73 Cal.App.5th 444, 462, fn. 4.) The essential elements of a contract are (1) parties capable of contracting, (2) their consent, (3) a lawful object and (4) a sufficient cause or consideration. (Civ. Code § 1550.) 

Defendants seek to compel arbitration based on a “Mutual Arbitration Agreement and Class Action Waiver” provision (the “Arbitration Agreement”) contained within his Employment Agreement, which they assert now-deceased former Plaintiff Jose Antonio Pimentel (“Pimentel”) electronically signed on October 5, 2022. (Alson Decl., ¶ 14, Exh. 4.) Rentokil Onboarding/Pre-Hire Specialist Edith Alson (“Alson”) explains that (1) in October 2022, Rentokil used an on-line application system known as ICIMS and a human resources information system known as UKG (Alson Decl., ¶¶ 6-7), (2) she launched the onboarding process for Pimentel (Id., ¶ 8), (3) before launching Pimentel’s onboarding, she confirmed that Pimentel had completed all the information in his ICIMS account (Id., ¶ 10), (4) a copy of the sign-in screen for Pimentel’s ICIMS account showed that he created the account under the name “Jose A Pimentel” and provided jose.pimentelperez@hotmail.com as an email address (Id., ¶ 10, Exh. 2), (5) when she launched Pimentel’s onboarding, the UKG system sent him an email to the email address listed in ICIMS, which contained a link to the onboarding page in UKG and a unique login and instructed Jose that he would need to create a unique password when he first accessed UKG (Id., ¶ 11), (6) Pimentel logged into UKG on October 5, 2022. Once logged in, the system provided a list of documents Pimentel would need to review and acknowledge or sign, with a link to each document. Once a document link is clicked, the UKG system pulls up the document to be reviewed and at the end of the document is a box for the individual to check that states “by checking the box I agree I have reviewed and am electronically signing the document in agreement.” Once the box is checked, the UKG system then pulls the individual’s name as it appears in ICIMS, electronically “signs” the document and stores it in the UKG system (Id., ¶ 12) and that (7) the only way for the UKG system to generate and store an electronically signed Employment Agreement was for Pimentel to click on the link in the onboarding email he was sent, login to UKG with his unique UKG login and self-created password, click on the link to the Employment Agreement in UKG during the onboarding session and then check the “I agree” box at the end of the document (Id., ¶ 16). 

The Arbitration Agreement provides, in relevant part, as follows:

                “In consideration of the undersigned EMPLOYEE’s employment with EMPLOYER the promises contained in this Agreement, both EMPLOYER and EMPLOYEE agree that all legal disputes and claims that have existed, currently exist or that may arise between them, including without limitation any dispute or disagreement between them relating to this Agreement or EMPLOYEE’s employment with EMPLOYER, or separation from employment (‘Covered Claims’), shall be determined exclusively by final and binding arbitration before a single, neutral arbitrator pursuant to the National Rules for the Resolution of Employment Disputes then in effect and by and before the American Arbitration Association . . . Claims against the EMPLOYER subject to this Mutual Arbitration Agreement and Class Action Waiver shall include claims against the EMPLOYER’’s parents, subsidiaries, affiliates, agents, divisions, officers, directors, employees and any alleged co-employers, where [sic] past or present. . .

                THE PARTIES HAVE READ AND UNDERSTAND THIS SECTION, WHICH BINDS THE PARTIES TO ARBITRATION. THE PARTIES UNDERSTAND THAT BY SIGNING THIS AGREEMENT, EACH PARTY AGREES TO SUBMIT ANY CLAIMS BETWEEN THEM TO BINDING, INDIVIDUAL ARBITRATION, AND THAT THIS ARBITRATION CLAUSE CONSTITUTES A WAIVER OF SUCH PARTY’S RIGHT TO A JURY TRIAL AND RELATES TO THE RESOLUTION OF ALL DISPUTES RELATING TO ALL ASPECTS OF THE EMPLOYER/EMPLOYEE RELATIONSHIP, INCLUDING BUT NOT LIMITED TO, DISCRIMINATION CLAIMS.” (Id.) 

Defendants have met their initial burden of establishing the existence of an arbitration agreement by attaching to their motion a copy of the purported Employment Agreement bearing Pimentel’s electronic signature. (Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1060; Iyere v. Wise Auto Group (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 747, 755). 

“If the movant bears its initial burden, the burden shifts to the party opposing arbitration to identify a factual dispute as to the agreement’s existence—in this instance, by disputing the authenticity of their signatures. To bear this burden, the arbitration opponent must offer admissible evidence creating a factual dispute as to the authenticity of their signatures. The opponent need not prove that his or her purported signature is not authentic, but must submit sufficient evidence to create a factual dispute and shift the burden back to the arbitration proponent, who retains the ultimate burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, the authenticity of the signature.” (Iyere, supra, 87 Cal.App.5th at 755). 

Plaintiff challenges the validity of Pimentel’s signature on the Employment Agreement, asserting that he “always” wrote and signed documents using his full last name, “Pimentel Perez,” and not just “Pimentel” as it appears on the Employment Agreement. (Opp., 6:4-5.) Plaintiff also contends that the typed name “Jose A Pimentel” on the Employment Agreement was not an act by Pimentel. (Id., 2:8-9.) Plaintiff posits that Alson’s declaration “is rife with inaccuracies and information gaps” because she “fails to mention” that Thongsavanh was copied on the October 4, 202 email containing a link to the onboarding page in UKG (Id., 3:3-7; J. Govea Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. D), that on October 14, 2022, she emailed Pimentel his UKG login information, copying Thongsavanh and District Manager Sean Turk and advising therein that his password was set to his birthdate as a default (Opp., 3:7-10; J. Govea Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. H), that Thongsavanh printed the October 14, 2024 email out on October 17, 2022 and gave it to Pimentel (Opp., 3:14-19; J. Govea Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. H), and that one onboarding document signed on October 5, 2022 (i.e., “USCIS Form I-9”) is signed “Jose Pimentel” with the “A” missing, instead of “Jose A Pimentel” (Opp., 3:19-22; J. Govea Decl., ¶ 13, Exh. L). 

The Court agrees that the statements contained in the declarations of Pimentel’s family members averring, inter alia, that Pimentel stated that he never signed an arbitration agreement with Rentokil, that Pimentel called Thongsavanh for answers to the onboarding training module exam questions, that Pimentel stated that Thongsavanh completed the onboarding paperwork and module training for him, that Pimentel specifically requested, when applying for United States citizenship, that his name not be changed from “Jose Antonio Pimentel Perez” on legal documents, and that Pimentel always filled out any document as “Jose Antonio Pimentel Perez,” are hearsay, speculative, and/or not based on personal knowledge; even if considered, the Court determines that Defendants have established the authenticity of Pimentel’s electronic signature by a preponderance of the evidence, a standard which “simply requires the trier of fact to believe that the existence of a fact is more probable than its nonexistence.” (Katie V. v. Superior Court (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 586, 594 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) 

Civil Code section 1633.9 provides, in relevant part, that “(a) An electronic record or electronic signature is attributable to a person if it was the act of the person. The act of the person may be shown in any manner, including a showing of the efficacy of any security procedure applied to determine the person to which the electronic record or electronic signature was attributable.” 

Plaintiff cites to Ruiz v. Moss Brothers Auto Group (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836 in support of the contention that “[c]ourts have denied motions to compel arbitration for defendants’ failure to authenticate electronic signatures based on similarly lacking declarations.” (Opp., 10:6-7.) In Ruiz, however, the court determined that the defendant failed to show how it “inferred” that the electronic signature on the arbitration agreement was plaintiff’s act because the defendant did not explain that (1) the electronic signature could only have been placed on the arbitration agreement by a person using the plaintiff’s “unique login ID and password,” (2) the date and time printed next to the electronic signature indicated the date and time the electronic signature was made, (3) all of the defendant’s employees were required to use their unique login ID and password when they logged into the HR system and signed electronic forms and agreements, and that (4) and the electronic signature on the 2011 agreement was, therefore, apparently made by the plaintiff at the date and time listed next to the signature. (Id. at 844.)

The court in Espejo distinguished the facts before it from those in Ruiz in concluding that the defendant properly authenticated the electronic signature on the plaintiff’s arbitration agreement, explaining that a supplemental declaration from the defendant’s systems consultant, which “detailed [the defendant’s] security precautions regarding transmission and use of an applicant’s unique username and password, as well as the steps an applicant would have to take to place his or her name on the signature line of the employment agreement,” “offered the critical factual connection that the declarations in Ruiz lacked.” (Espejo, supra, 246 Cal.App.4th at 1062.)

The Court determines that Alson’s initial and supplemental declarations likewise “offer[] the critical factual connection that the declarations in Ruiz lacked.” In addition to the information proffered in her initial declaration, Alson explains that, although the October 4, 2022 email copied Thongsavanh, only Pimentel could access the remaining steps to log in and create his own password because the second link was sent only to the specific email address Pimentel provided. (Alson Supp. Decl., ¶ 15.) Then, when Pimentel clicked on the second link, he was taken to a sign-in page where he had to click “create new password” to log-in; once he clicked this, he received a four-digit code via email which he was required to enter in order to create his new password. (Id., ¶ 15, Exhs. 5 and 6.) Alson further explains that only once Pimentel went through this process was he able to log into his new hire UKG account and that, once logged into said account, he was required to input his social security number twice to receive an Employee ID. (Id., ¶ 17.) She explains that the system required that Pimentel have an employee ID before he could complete the onboarding forms and that a new hire cannot receive an employee ID without creating a unique password. (Id., ¶ 18.)

Alson explains that, while the UKG system pulled Pimentel’s name as it appeared in ICIMs when he manually clicked on the hyperlink to each onboarding document, the Form I-9 completed by Pimentel required that he provide additional information as opposed to a simple acknowledge and accept or “check the box,” thus requiring him to manually type in the required information and his electronic signature instead of his name being auto populated as with the other acknowledgments. (Id., ¶¶ 22-25, Exhs. 8 and 9.)

Alson next explains that Pimentel received the October 14, 2022 email after he reviewed and acknowledged the onboarding documents, that this email included links to access his UKG colleague information such as payroll and benefits, and that to access this information, he was required to create a new password with the default being the employee’s birthdate. (Id., ¶ 26.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendants have demonstrated the existence of a valid, written agreement to arbitrate.

       (2)      Coverage of Disputes

Defendants must also demonstrate that “the agreement encompasses the dispute at issue.” (Chiron Corp. v. Ortho Diagnostic Systems, Inc. (9th Cir. 2000) 207 F.3d 1126, 1130.) The First Amended Complaint includes causes of action for Discrimination Based on Disability and Medical Condition in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”), Retaliation in Violation of the California Family Rights Act (“CFRA”), Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process in Violation of FEHA, Failure to Accommodate, Failure to Prevent Discrimination in Violation of FEHA, Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy, Failure to Provide Meal Periods, Failure to Provide Rest Periods, Failure to Pay Overtime Wages, Failure to Provide Accurate Itemized Wage Statements, Waiting Time Penalties, Unlawful and Unfair Business Practices (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq.), Intentional Misrepresentation, and Negligent Hiring, Supervision & Retention. Each of these claims relates to Plaintiff’s employment, or separation of employment, with Rentokil and are asserted against Rentokil and Thongsavanh, an employee of Rentokil.

The language quoted above from the Arbitration Agreement encompasses all of Plaintiff’s claims.                                                                                                   

IV.     Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, the Court grants the motion and orders the case stayed, pending completion of the arbitration.
