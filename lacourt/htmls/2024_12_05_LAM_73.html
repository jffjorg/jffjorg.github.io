DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV41875    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: 73

12/05/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

DUERSON v. CARTER, et al. (20STCV41875)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Mark Gomez (Gomez Law, APC)

Counsel for Defendant/ opposing party: Timothy Carter (Pro Per)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed on 10/30/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 2, 2024, Plaintiff Herschell Duerson, individually and as Successor Trustee of the Marjorie Hubbard Revocable Living Trust u/a/d October 5, 2018 brought this action against Defendants Timothy Carter; Bradley Michael Staple; Staple’s Building Solutions; and Jess Staple.

 

On February 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Contract

2.      Promissory Estoppel

3.      Fraud

4.      Unjust Enrichment

5.      Violation of Business and Professions Code Section 7159.5(a)

 

The FAC alleges the following. Defendants fraudulently deceived Marjorie Hubbard, an elderly woman, into entering into a written contract to reconstruct her home, the Subject Property, that was destroyed by a fire. On or about February of2019, Marjorie Hubbard paid the sum of $351,000.00 to Defendants pursuant to the Agreement to repair fire damage and to reconstruct the Subject Property. However, Plaintiff alleges Defendants never intended to perform the reconstructions. Defendants failed to perform under the terms of the Agreement and Defendants have not refunded the sum of $351,000.00.

 

On October 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for attorneys’ fees, arguing:

·       The motion is made pursuant to California Civil Code § 3336 and is based on the following grounds: (1) Plaintiff prevailed, in full, on the Breach of Contract, Promissory Estoppel, Fraud, and Unjust Enrichment actions; (2) judgment having been entered in Plaintiff’s favor, Plaintiff is entitled to recover his attorney’s fees and costs incurred in this action under California Civil Code § 3336; and (3) the fees and costs Plaintiff incurred in obtaining the judgment in this action (and in seeking his attorney’s fees) are reasonable and fully recoverable under California Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5 (a)(10).

·       Civil Code § 3336 and California Code of Civil Procedure §1033.5 (a)(10) collectively provide for the recovery of attorney’s fees in cases involving fraud, deceit, and when authorized by statute or law.

·       Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees in the amount of $156, 289.04.

 

No opposition or reply were filed.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff seeks to recover attorney’s fees incurred in this action based on California Civil Code § 3336 and California Code of Civil Procedure §1033.5 (a)(10).

 

On August 12, 2024, judgment was entered in this case in Plaintiff’s favor. Plaintiff prevailed on his Breach of Contract, Promissory Estoppel, Fraud, and Unjust Enrichment causes of action.

 

Plaintiff argues that under California Civil Code § 3336 a person defrauded is entitled to compensation that includes the expenses incurred due to fraud, including attorney’s fees. California Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5 (a)(10) provides: “Attorney’s fees, when authorized by any of the following: (A) Contract, (B) Statute, (C) Law.” Thus, Plaintiff asserts that the attorney’s fees incurred are a direct and necessary result of Defendants’ fraud and should be recoverable under these statutes.

 

However, the Court does not find that Plaintiff’s cited authority allows an award of attorney’s fees related to the instant motion.

 

California Civil Code § 3336 states:

 

“The detriment caused by wrongful conversion of personal property is presumed to be:

 

First- The value of the property at the time of the conversion, with the interest from that time, or, an amount sufficient to indemnify the party injured for the loss which is the natural, reasonable, and proximate result of the wrongful act complained of and which a proper degree of prudence on his part would not have averted; and

 

Second- A fair compensation for the time and money properly expended in pursuit of the property.”

 

Therefore, California Civil Code § 3336 allows the recovery of damages for the money expended in pursuit of converted property. However, it does not mention recovery of attorney’s fees incurred due to fraud.

 

Plaintiff also cites to Gray v. Don Miller & Assocs., Inc. (1984) 35 Cal. 3d 498 in support of their assertion that attorney’s fees are recoverable in cases involving fraud or deceit. But, Gray does not support this assertion.

 

In Gray, an unsuccessful purchaser of property brought an action against a real estate brokerage, alleging that the real estate agent misrepresented that vendors had accepted his offer. The court discussed whether plaintiff could recover attorney’s fees because defendant, a fiduciary, committed a fraud against him. (Gray, supra, 35 Cal.3d at 505.) However, the court found that a plaintiff is not entitled to recover attorney fees as an element of damages in actions for fraud in which the defendant is a fiduciary. Thus, the court did not hold that attorney’s fees are recoverable in cases involving fraud or deceit. Instead, the Gray court found that the plaintiff was entitled to recover attorneys fees based on the “tort of another exception.” “[T]he ‘tort of another’ or ‘third party tort’ exception, allows a plaintiff attorney fees if he is required to employ counsel to prosecute or defend an action against a third party because of the tort of the defendant.” (Ibid.) This is not applicable here as Plaintiff does not claim to have incurred legal fees to prosecute or defend a third-party action because of the tortious conduct of Defendants. As the Gray court stated: “Under the American rule, as a general proposition each party must pay his own attorney fees. This concept is embodied in section 1021 of the Code of Civil Procedure, which provides that each party is to bear his own attorney fees unless a statute or the agreement of the parties provides otherwise.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not provided a statute or authority that would entitle him to an award of attorney’s fees in this case. Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

MIGLIORE v. FCA US LLC, et al. (24STCV13115)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Sam Azimtash (Azimtash Law Firm PC)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Erin Hanson (Clark Hill LLP)

 

(1)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT PREMIER AUTOMOTIVE OF PLACENTIA, LLC DBA PREMIER CHRYSLER DODGE JEEP RAM OF BUENA PARK’S OBJECTION-FREE RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE (filed 09/04/24)

(2)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT PREMIER AUTOMOTIVE OF PLACENTIA, LLC DBA PREMIER CHRYSLER DODGE JEEP RAM OF BUENA PARK’S OBJECTION-FREE RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE (filed 09/04/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

(1)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park’s responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED. Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park is ordered to provide verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, within 20 days of this order.  

 

(1)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED. Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park is ordered to provide verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, within 20 days of this order.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff Merik Migliore filed the instant action against Defendants FC US LLC, Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park and Does 1-100, alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code section 1793.2

2.      Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 1793.2

3.      Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code section 1793.2

4.      Breach of Express Warranty

5.      Breach of Implied Warranty

6.      Negligent Repair

Plaintiff alleges this action arises out of the warranty obligations of Defendants in connection with a vehicle purchased by Plaintiff and for which Defendants issued a written warranty.

On September 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park’s objection free responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One and a Motion to Compel Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One. No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A responding party has 30 days after service of interrogatories to serve their responses on the propounding party. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) However, if interrogatories are served by electronic service, a responding party has an additional two (2) court days to respond. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(B).)  

 

If the responding party fails to timely respond to the interrogatories, “The party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010).” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)  

  

Furthermore, the party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories. (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).) And “Unlike a motion to compel further responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45-day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)   

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.”   

 

B.      Discussion

On July 5, 2024, Plaintiff served Requests for Production of Documents (Set One) and Special Interrogatories (Set One) on Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park. (Azimtash Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.) The responses were due on or about August 7, 2024. As of the date of this motion, no requests for an extension of time or responses have been served. (Id. at ¶ 8.) No opposition to Plaintiff’s motions have been filed. As such, the Court GRANTS both of Plaintiff’s motions.

III. DISPOSITION

 

(1)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park’s responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED. Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park is ordered to provide verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, within 20 days of this order.  

 

(2)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED. Defendant Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC dba Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park is ordered to provide verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, within 20 days of this order.

FUENTES, et al. v. 2416 BELLEVUE, INC., et al. (24STCV17672)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Gerald Ohn (Law Offices of Gerald Ohn)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Heather Mills (Skane Mills LLP)

 

MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE complaint (filed 10/04/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 16, 2024, Plaintiffs Liliam Fuentes, Jose Fuentes, and Alexander Fuentes filed this action against Defendants 2416 Bellevue, Inc. and J.K. Residential Services, Inc.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

2.      Negligence

3.      Nuisance

4.      Violations of Civil Code Section 1942.4

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Defendants owned, operated and/or managed the residential property located at 2416 Bellevue Ave., Apt. 110, Los Angeles, California 90026 (“the Property”). Plaintiffs were tenants of the Property and allege that they experienced various issues with the premises, including insect infestations, mold and other building code and health and safety code violations. Among other times, on or about April 11, 2022; June 13, 2023; and November 8, 2023 Defendants were cited by the Los Angeles County Department of Public Health for numerous violations of applicable health and safety codes or housing laws, among other things.

 

On October 10, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion to strike, arguing:

·       The factual allegations of Plaintiffs’ Complaint do not rise to the level(s) of malice, oppression, and fraud, as required by Section 3294. Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages is not supported by allegations of Defendants’ behavior of the requisite malicious severity.

·       Punitive damages are judicially disfavored

 

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The Complaint’s allegations are sufficient to show the requisite state of mind sufficient for punitive damages- namely that Defendants were aware the dangerous conditions of the Subject Property was causing Plaintiffs' pain and suffering and yet still refused to repair the conditions and thereby intentionally caused Plaintiffs' pain and suffering. Liberally construed, these facts satisfy the burden of alleging required for despicable conduct, malice and oppression under Civ. Code § 3294. Whether the jury will find that Defendant acted maliciously is a matter of proof at trial, not for a motion to strike.

 

No reply was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Defendants argue that the Complaint’s claims for punitive damages must be stricken. Specifically, Defendants request the Court to strike portions of the Complaint as follows:

1.      Paragraph 17, lines 11-17: “The aforementioned acts of Defendants were done maliciously, oppressively, fraudulently, recklessly and with the intent to cause injury to or deprive Plaintiffs of their legal rights or with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of Plaintiffs. Defendants’ conduct was despicable and subjected Plaintiffs to cruel and unjust hardship. Indeed, even though Defendants and their agents were aware of dangerous conditions at the Property, some of which were directly affecting the health of the Plaintiffs, Defendants intentionally failed to make timely repairs and proper repairs.”

2.      Prayer 3, line 2: “Punitive and exemplary damages.”

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs fail to plead that Defendants have been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.

 

However, the Court disagrees. The Complaint contains the following allegations:

 

“The following conditions, among others, have existed at the Property in the common areas, and in some or all of the plaintiffs' apartment units, some of which conditions will continue to exist in the future for an uncertain duration until such time as defendants abate the conditions: a) Insect infestations and lack of insect control; b) Damaged or deteriorated carpeting; c) Holes in the ceilings and/or interior walls; d) Damaged, broken, buckled or deteriorated walls and/or ceilings; e) Water leaks; f) Water intrusion; g) Water damage; h) Excessive moisture; i) Toxic mold causing mold spores to float in the air and be inhaled by plaintiffs, thereby causing serious medical and health problems; j) Leaking pipes in the parking lot; k) Large amounts of trash strewn across the common areas of the property, which exacerbate the insect infestations noted above; l) An unsafe laundry room; and m) Building code and health and safety code violations.” (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

 

“As alleged herein, notice of the materially defective conditions was given to Defendants within a reasonable time after the plaintiffs discovered or should have discovered the breaches of warranty. Defendants have had actual and constructive knowledge of the materially defective conditions affecting habitability, and were given a reasonable time to correct them, but failed to remedy them.” (Complaint, ¶ 24.)

 

“Defendants received citations by the Los Angeles County Environmental Health Services Department for numerous violations of applicable health and safety codes on or about April 11, 2022; June 13, 2023; and November 8, 2023. Notwithstanding the receipt of these formal citations, Defendants failed to timely make repairs for a period of more than 35 days after service of citations upon Defendants, and the delay was without good cause.” (Complaint, ¶ 44.)

 

“Defendants’ acts, omissions and conduct were a direct, substantial, legal and proximate cause of and factor in the injuries, damages and harm to Plaintiffs who have suffered, and will continue to suffer from, including but not limited to: pain and suffering; injury to Plaintiffs’ physical health and activity; shock to Plaintiffs’ nervous system; emotional distress; irritation; anguish, fright; anger; anxiety; sadness; insomnia; allergies; coughing; watery eyes; congestion; mental suffering; medical expenses; and inconvenience.” (Complaint, ¶ 16.)

 

“The aforementioned acts of Defendants were done maliciously, oppressively, fraudulently, recklessly and with the intent to cause injury to or deprive Plaintiffs of their legal rights or with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of Plaintiffs. Defendants' conduct was despicable and subjected Plaintiffs to cruel and unjust hardship. Indeed, even though Defendants and their agents were aware of dangerous conditions at the Property, some of which were directly affecting the health of the Plaintiffs, Defendants intentionally failed to make timely repairs and proper repairs.” (Complaint, ¶ 17.)

 

The Court finds these allegations are sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged despicable conduct, malice and oppression under Civ. Code § 3294. Plaintiff has alleged (1) uninhabitable conditions on the Subject Property, (2) that Defendants had notice of the conditions and failed to correct them while still collecting rent from Plaintiffs, (3) that Defendants also failed to remedy the conditions after receipt of notices of violations by housing authorities and (4) that as a result of Defendants’ conduct, Plaintiffs’ sustained injuries. This factual scenario is sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages at the pleading stage. Pleading specific facts is not mandated for punitive damages, which merely requires ultimate facts. The court in Perkins v. Superior Court noted that “’the distinction between conclusions of law and ultimate facts is not at all clear and involves at most a matter of degree’ [Citation.] . . . . What is important is that the complaint as a whole contain sufficient facts to apprise the defendant of the basis upon which the plaintiff is seeking relief. [Citations.]” ((1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1, 6–7.) Taken in context of the Complaint, Plaintiffs have adequately pled a claim for punitive damages. The final decision of “[w]hether to award punitive damages . . . and the amount of such an award are questions committed to the trier of fact. [Citation.]” (Uzyel v. Kadisha (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 866, 925.) 

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED.
