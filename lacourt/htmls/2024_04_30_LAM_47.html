DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV15786    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     April 30, 2024                        TRIAL DATE: May 21, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         James Weldon Schott v. Miguel A. Melgoza

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV15786           

 

(1) MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

(2) MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1)(2) Plaintiff James Weldon Schott

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1) Defendant Miguel A. Melgoza in propria persona

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/24/20: Complaint filed.

·         09/27/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for partition of a parcel of real property and for fraud, conversion, and breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant induced him to loan money for the purchase of the property to operate it as a dispensary.

 

Plaintiff moves for terminating sanctions. Plaintiff also moves for summary judgment.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant Miguel Melgoza’s Answer to the First Amended Complaint is hereby stricken in its entirety and default entered this date against Defendant.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED as moot.

 

            All future hearings are advanced to this date and vacated.

 

            The Court will set a default prove-up hearing in this partition action at the hearing on the motions.  

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for Terminating Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff moves for terminating sanctions for Defendant’s failure to respond to discovery despite Court orders.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of its October 31, 2023 Minute Order in this action granting Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production and his Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admissions, and imposing monetary sanctions on Defendant. Plaintiff’s request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

Legal Standard for Terminating Sanctions

 

The Court has the authority to impose sanctions against a party that engages in any misuse of the discovery process (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030), including “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2023.010(d).) A party engaging in this conduct may be subject to sanctions including monetary sanctions (Code Civ Proc. § 2023.030(a)), evidence sanctions (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(c)), or terminating sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(d).)  “[T]rial courts should select sanctions tailored to the harm caused by the misuse of the discovery process and should not exceed what is required to protect the party harmed by the misuse of the discovery process.” (Dept. of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 191, disapproved of on other grounds in Presbyterian Camp & Conference Centers, Inc. v. Superior Court (2021) 12 Cal.5th 493.) Sanctions are generally imposed in an incremental approach. (Id.) Generally, the appropriate sanctions when a party repeatedly and willfully fails to provide evidence to the opposing party as required by the discovery rules is preclusion of that evidence from the trial. (Juarez v. Boy Scouts of America, Inc. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 377, 390, disapproved of on other grounds by Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204.)

 

In considering a motion for terminating sanctions, the Court is to attempt to “tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.” (Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1618-1619.) 

 

[T]he question before this court is not whether the trial court should have imposed a lesser sanction; rather, the question is whether the trial court abused its discretion by imposing the sanction it chose. [Citation.] Moreover, imposition of a lesser sanction would have permitted [defendants] to benefit from their stalling tactics. [Citation.] The trial court did not abuse its discretion by tailoring the sanction to the particular abuse.

 

(Id. at 1620.), and

 

            Moreover, in deciding whether to impose a terminating sanction, the trial court is to consider the totality of the circumstances: the “conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.” (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)

 

Defendant’s Failure to Respond to Discovery

 

            Plaintiff argues that terminating sanctions should be granted because Defendant has failed to respond to basic discovery, has failed to comply with Court orders to produce responses to that discovery, and has failed to pay sanctions for misuse of the discovery process.

 

            On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant with form interrogatories and requests for production via overnight service. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 1.A [Form interrogatories]; 2.A [Requests for Production].) After Defendant failed to provide responses within the deadlines provided, Plaintiff moved to compel responses on July 31, 2023. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 1-2.) The Court granted the motions on October 31, 2023, ordering Defendant to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days, and ordering sanctions against Defendant only in the amount of $2,180, to be paid in the same timeframe. (RJN Exh. A.) On December 7, 2023, the Court granted a motion by Defendant’s former counsel of record to withdraw from representation in this action on the basis that Defendant had ceased responding to his counsel. (See December 6, 2023 Minute Order.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that he attempted to contact Defendant Melgoza via letter on December 29, 2023 outlining the procedural status of the case, informing Defendant of his noncompliance, and advising Defendant of Plaintiff’s intent of filing this motion. (Declaration of Matthew T. Ward ISO Mot. Exhs. 4-5.) As of the date this motion was filed, Defendant had not responded, nor has he paid the sanctions owed. (Ward Decl. ¶¶ 8-9.)

 

            Plaintiff contends that terminating sanctions are warranted because Defendant has failed to comply with Court orders, ignored monetary sanctions, and appears to have abandoned the case altogether. However, when this matter initially came for hearing on March 14, 2024, Defendant appeared pro se and orally requested a continuance to respond to this motion. The Court granted this request and ordered Defendant to serve and file any opposition to this motion and a declaration accounting for his lack of responsiveness and failure to participate in the case. (March 14, 2024 Minute Order.)

 

            Defendant did not file any opposition to this motion, and while he has filed a declaration which purports to explain his conduct, no proof of service was attached to the declaration, and Plaintiff represents in its reply brief that the declaration was not served. Nevertheless, as Plaintiff was able to retrieve the declaration and prepare a response, the Court will address the declaration.

 

            At the outset, the Court observes that the declaration is not made under penalty of perjury, as pointed out by Plaintiff. For any matter which is to be evidenced or supported by an unsworn declaration, that declaration must instead contain a certification or declaration of its truth under penalty of perjury under the laws of the state of California which is dated and signed by the declarant. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2015.5.) Accepting, for the moment, that the declaration is true, Defendant contends that he was absent from the case because of a family crisis between November 2023 and January 2024 because his family was a victim of an unspecified crime in Mexico. Defendant claims that he had limited communication while abroad, and that this was the cause of his absence and the loss of his attorney. Defendant contends that he wishes to continue the case, will retain new counsel, and will fully cooperate with the court. The declaration does not clearly state that Defendant will pay the sanctions previously ordered, nor that he will provide responses to outstanding discovery, and provides no timetable for when the Court can expect him to effect compliance. Moreover, as Plaintiff argues in reply, the declaration does not account for the complete lack of responses or appearance from Defendant between May and October 2023, nor does it account for the period after January 2024.

 

            The Court is persuaded by Plaintiff’s arguments. Defendant has failed to comply with basic discovery requests for almost a full year and is four months overdue on paying the monetary sanctions imposed by this Court. Defendant’s declaration is not competent evidence because it is not made under penalty of perjury, and, even if it were, Defendant has not accounted for his failure to respond for mor than five months or to rectify his noncompliance since January 2024. The Court is therefore forced to conclude that Defendant’s failure to comply with his discovery obligations and with this Court’s orders is willful, and that no lighter measure will serve to remedy the prejudice to Plaintiff or deter future violations by Defendant. Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an order granting terminating sanctions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

            Plaintiff also moves for summary judgment against Defendant. However, as the Court has granted terminating sanctions against Defendant, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is MOOT.

Legal Standard for Default Judgment

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 585(b) governs default judgments in actions other than an action arising upon contract or judgment for recovery of money or money damages only. Under this provision:

 

[I]f the defendant has been served, other than by publication, and no answer, demurrer, notice of motion to strike of the character specified in subdivision (f), notice of motion to transfer pursuant to Section 396b, notice of motion to dismiss pursuant to Article 2 (commencing with Section 583.210) of Chapter 1.5 of Title 8, notice of motion to quash service of summons or to stay or dismiss the action pursuant to Section 418.10 or notice of the filing of a petition for writ of mandate as provided in Section 418.10 has been filed with the clerk of the court within the time specified in the summons, or within further time as may be allowed, the clerk, upon written application of the plaintiff, shall enter the default of the defendant. The plaintiff thereafter may apply to the court for the relief demanded in the complaint. The court shall hear the evidence offered by the plaintiff, and shall render judgment in the plaintiff's favor for that relief, not exceeding the amount stated in the complaint, in the statement required by Section 425.11, or in the statement provided for by Section 425.115, as appears by the evidence to be just. If the taking of an account, or the proof of any fact, is necessary to enable the court to give judgment or to carry the judgment into effect, the court may take the account or hear the proof, or may, in its discretion, order a reference for that purpose. If the action is for the recovery of damages, in whole or in part, the court may order the damages to be assessed by a jury; or if, to determine the amount of damages, the examination of a long account is involved, by a reference as above provided.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 585(b).)

Service of Process

 

            A default or default judgment may only issue under Code of Civil Procedure section 585(b) “if a defendant has been served, other than by publication.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 585(b). Service of process may be accomplished via personal service (Code Civ. Proc. § 415.10), substituted service (Id. § 415.20), or by mail (Id. § 415.30.) Service on a corporation may be accomplished by delivering a copy of the summons and complaint to the person designated as agent for service of process or to a head, senior officer, or other person authorized by the corporation to accept service of process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 416.10 (a)-(b).)

 

            The Amended Proof of Service filed with the Court on February 8, 2024, states that personal service of the Summons and Complaint was made on September 5, 2023 at 4:00 PM on Jamie Padilla, a person authorized to accept service, at 628 S. Aviation Boulevard, Manhattan Beach, CA 90266. According to the California Secretary of State’s public records, this address is the address of MMTS, Inc. (RJN Exh. 3.) MMTS Inc. is listed as the designated agent for Defendant. (RJN Exh. 1.) Plaintiff has thus demonstrated proper service of process.

 

Entry of Default

 

            A default may not be entered until the time for the party to respond to the complaint has expired. (Code Civ. Proc. § 585 (b).) When a default is sought, the default must include an affidavit stating that it was mailed to the defendant’s attorney of record or the last known address of the Defendant. (Code Civ. Proc. § 587.)

 

            The operative request for entry of default and default judgment was filed on February 15, 2024, more than five months after service of the summons and complaint. (Request for Entry of Default/Judgment.) The request was served by mail on the same address that service of process was effected on February 15, 2024. (Id. POS.) Default was entered the date the request was filed. (Id. p. 1.)

 

Circumstances Voiding Default

 

            Plaintiff has not amended the Complaint since the request was filed, and Defendant has never appeared in the action. Moreover, although no notice of punitive damages pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.115(f) has been served, Plaintiff is not seeking to recover punitive damages, notwithstanding the prayer for such relief in the Complaint. The Court therefore finds that there are no circumstances which warrant setting aside the default as void under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(d) at this time.

In an unlawful detainer case, a party may, in addition, use optional Declaration for Default Judgment by Court (form UD-116) when seeking a court judgment based on declarations. (4)  A memorandum of costs and disbursements;

(Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1800(A).) Plaintiff’s memorandum of points and authorities includes a summary of the case. (Memorandum of Points & Authorities p.1:19-4:28.)

 

Well-Pled Allegations

 

            Plaintiff asserted six causes of action against Defendant: (1) Conversion; (2) Aiding and Abetting Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (3) Breach of Privacy Rights; (4) Civil Conspiracy to Violate Penal Code section 502; (5) Violation of Penal Code section 496; and (6) Unfair Competition.

 

            In a default case, no evidence is required to establish liability. (Kim v. Westmoore Partners, Inc. (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 267, 281-82.) However, a defendant’s failure to answer the complaint admits only well-pleaded allegations in the complaint. (See Molen v. Friedman (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1153-1154.) To obtain any form of relief, Plaintiff must therefore have pled sufficient facts to state a cause of action.

 

//

 

1.      Conversion

 

            To establish a claim for conversion, a plaintiff must plead and prove (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.

 

            Here, the Complaint alleges that Defendant, through its principal, knowingly interfered with Plaintiff’s possession of its confidential claimant information by misappropriating it and using that information for itself, causing Plaintiff to incur monetary losses, including the costs of efforts to retrieve that information without litigation. (Complaint ¶¶ 27-32, 35-37.) Plaintiff has therefore pled sufficient facts to support a claim of conversion.

 

2.      Aiding and Abetting Breach of Duty of Loyalty

 

            To allege a breach of the duty of loyalty, a party must plead (1) the existence of a relationship giving rise to a duty of loyalty; (2) a breach of that duty, and (3) resulting damage proximately caused by that breach. (See, e.g., Huong Que Inc. v. Luu (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 400, 510.) A defendant is liable for aiding and abetting the commission of an intentional tort if the defendant knows the others’ conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other. (See Nasrawi v. Buck Consultants, LLC (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 328, 343.)

 

            The Complaint alleges that Keyan Aghili owed Plaintiff a duty of loyalty as a Claimants Manager and, later, a Claims Supervisor. (Complaint ¶ 41.) The Complaint alleges that Aghili breached that duty by stealing confidential claimant information; and that Defendant abetted this breach by receiving that information and hiring Aghili to do work for Defendant that created a conflict of interest between Aghili and Plaintiff. (Complaint ¶¶ 42-46.) Plaintiff has therefore pled a claim for aiding and abetting a breach of a duty of loyalty.

 

3.      Breach of Privacy Rights

 

            To allege a breach of privacy rights, a plaintiff must plead (1) a legally protected privacy interest; (2) a reasonable expectation of privacy; (3) the offensiveness or seriousness of the intrusion; (4) resultant harm; and (5) that the Defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing that harm. (See Hill v. Nat. Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 24; Hernandez v. Hillsides, Inc. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 272, 287-88.) Privacy must be evaluated in reference to the identity of the alleged intruder and the nature of the intrusion. (Sanders v. Am. Broadcasting Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 907, 914-915.)

 

            The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff and its clients and claimants have a privacy interest with respect to their personal information, including medical conditions and history, employment, social security numbers, dates of birth, and financial information, in which they had a reasonable expectation of privacy. (Complaint ¶¶ 14, 52; Cal. Const. art. I § 1.) The Complaint alleges that Aghili’s theft of that information and Defendant’s facilitation of that theft is both serious and offensive, that it has harmed the clients and claimants, and that Defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing that harm. (Id. ¶¶ 52-53.) Plaintiff, as custodian of that information, has standing to assert the privacy interests of its customers. (See Overstock.com, Inc. v. Goldman Sachs Group, Inc. (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 471, 503.) Plaintiff has therefore pled facts sufficient to support this cause of action.

 

4.      Conspiracy to Violate Penal Code Section 502

 

            To allege a civil conspiracy, a plaintiff must plead (1) the formation and operation of the conspiracy; (2) wrongful conduct in furtherance of that conspiracy; and (3) resulting damages. (Wittenberg v. Bornstein (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 303, 312 fn. 7.) To establish a claim under Penal Code section 502, Plaintiff must prove: (1) Plaintiff is the owner of the computer system, computer network, computer program and/or data; (2) Defendant knowingly accessed and without permission (i) used any data, computer, computer system, or computer network in order to wrongful control or obtain money, property, or data and/or (ii) took, copied, or made use of any data from a computer, computer system, or computer network, or took or copied any supporting documentation, whether existing or residing internal or external to a computer, computer system, or computer network; (3) Defendant’s use of the computer systems was without Plaintiff’s permissions; (4) Plaintiff was harmed; and (5) Defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm. (Penal Code, § 502(c)(1)-(2).)

 

            The Complaint plainly alleges that Aghili planned with Defendant to access Plaintiff’s computer systems and steal customer and claimant information stored therein for the benefit of Defendant. (Complaint ¶¶ 14-15, 18, 23-32; 58-60.) The Complaint alleges that Defendant’s conduct harmed Plaintiff by obligating Plaintiff to act to preserve its clients’ privacy. (Complaint ¶ 61.) Plaintiff has therefore pled facts sufficient to support this claim.

 

5.      Violation of Penal Code Section 496

 

            To plead a violation of Penal Code section 496(a), a plaintiff must allege (1) that property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft; (2) the Defendant knew the property was stolen; and (3) the Defendant received possession of that property. (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.)

 

            The Complaint squarely alleges that Defendant received confidential information which was the property of Plaintiff that was stolen by Aghili with knowledge that the property was stolen. (Complaint ¶¶ 64-65.) Plaintiff has therefore pled facts sufficient to support this cause of action.

 

6.      Unfair Competition

 

            Section 17200 of the Business and Professions Code prohibits “any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practice.” (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.) Any person who has been injured by a violation of section 17200 is entitled to restitution and injunctive relief. (Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17203, 17204.) Violation of “[v]irtually any law—federal, state, or local—can serve as a predicate for an action” under this section. (Ticconi v. Blue Shield of Cal. Life & Health Ins. Co. (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 528, 539.) This cause of action is therefore derivative of the previous five causes of action. (See Complaint ¶ 69.) Thus, as Plaintiff has pled sufficient facts to state those causes of action, it has likewise sufficiently pled the sixth cause of action.

 

Compensatory Damages

 

            Plaintiff first requests an award of compensatory damages in the amount of $408,030.20. A party who prevails on a claim for breach of a duty of loyalty is entitled to damages which will compensate for all the detriment proximately caused by the breach. (Civ. Code § 3333.) In the case of a breach of the duty of loyalty to an employer by an employee, disgorgement of salary and benefits by the employee is a proper remedy. (Service Employees Inter’l Union, Local 250 v. Colcord (2008) 160 Cal.4th 362, 371.) However, Plaintiff cites no authority standing for the position that disgorgement of these benefits can be sought from Defendant, rather than from Aghili himself. Similarly, Plaintiff cites no authority standing for the position that Business & Professions Code section 17203 contemplates such relief. Moreover, even assuming that Plaintiff had demonstrated its entitlement to disgorgement of Aghili’s salary from Defendant, Plaintiff states that Kenyan Aghili’s gross salary from January 1, 2021, through the date of his resignation was $408,030.20. This assertion is not supported by the evidence. Although Plaintiff cites to the declaration of Victoria Gonzales as evidence for this amount, Ms. Gonzales only states that Aghili was paid $33,635.28 in gross salary since January 22, 2023. (Declaration of Victoria Gonzales ISO Request for Default Judgment ¶ 16.) None of the other papers filed by Plaintiff support the claimed amount.

 

            Indeed, the only monetary damages which are accounted for in the papers are the costs of the investigation to determine what Aghili and Defendant had done to Plaintiff’s computer systems, totaling $2,863.65. (Declaration of Michael Kunkel ISO Request for Entry of Default ¶¶ 5-11.) Expenditures that are “reasonable and necessarily incurred by the owner or lessee to verify that a computer system, computer network, computer program or data was or was not altered, damaged, or deleted by the access.” (Pen. Code § 502 (e)(1).) These costs alone are recoverable. Further, as Plaintiff has justified these costs, Plaintiff has likewise justified in claiming an additional $5,727.30, accounting for treble damages under Penal Code section 496(c).

 

            As the request for entry of default judgment seeks compensatory damages which have not been justified by the supporting papers, however, the Court cannot grant the relief requested. Plaintiff must submit a new request which either eliminates the request for Aghili’s salary or provides authority and evidence to support that award as against Jackson Risk Management.

 

            Because Plaintiff has not demonstrated its entitlement to the compensatory damages claimed, the Court does not address the request for injunctive relief or for fees and costs at this time.
