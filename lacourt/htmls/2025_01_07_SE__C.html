DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23NWCV02312    Hearing Date: January 7, 2025    Dept: C

HIGGINS v. LBJ HEALTHCARE PARTNERS, INC., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV02312

HEARING:  January 7, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#3

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendants Special Service for Groups, Inc. and Linda Torres’s Demurrer to the Fourth Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

Defendants to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a wrongful death action. On July 24, 2023 Plaintiff Patrick Higgins (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants LBJ Healthcare Partners, Inc. Special Service for Groups, Inc., Amada Enterprises, Inc., Linda Torres, and Does 1 to 50. On October 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint adding Laura Clark as a Plaintiff, pursuant to the parties’ stipulation. On February 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Third Amended Complaint. On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Fourth Amended Complaint (FAC). The FAC alleges that Nicole Lacea Higgins (“Decedent”) was struck by a motor vehicle and died after walking out of Villa Luren, a healthcare facility owned by Defendant LBJ Healthcare Partners, Inc. The FAC alleges that the County of Los Angeles Department of Mental Health, Office of the Public Guardian, was appointed by the Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles, as a conservator for Decedent. Defendant Special Service for Groups, Inc. contracts with the County of Los Angeles, and other Defendants, to coordinate mental health services for disabled adults. Defendant Linda Torres is a case manager that works for Special Service for Groups, Inc. Defendant Amada Enterprises, Inc. owns View Heights Convalescent Hospital, the nursing facility that Decedent previously resided at before the incident.

 

The FAC alleges that Defendants negligently placed Decedent in a facility with a lower level of supervision, Villa Luren, even though Defendants were on notice of Decedent’s history of walking away. The FAC alleges Defendants’ acts were the proximate cause of the death of Decedent. The FAC alleges the following causes of action: 1) Negligence, 2) Premises Liability, 3) Wrongful Death, and 4) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, or Retention of Employee.

 

Defendants Special Service for Groups, Inc. and Linda Torres (collectively, “Defendants”) now demur to the first cause of action for negligence and third cause of action for wrongful death on the grounds of uncertainty and failure to state a cause of action.

 

Legal Standard

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

  

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a) requires meeting and conferring “in person or by telephone.” Defendants’ counsel has met and conferred numerous times with Plaintiffs’ counsel regarding the first Complaint. (Rosenberg Decl., ¶ 5.) Defendants sent meet and confer letters regarding the second and third Complaints, but Plaintiff did not thereafter remedy the deficiencies identified. (Ibid.) On April 19, Defendants’ counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the Fourth Amended Complaint, but there was no response. (Ibid.) Although the Court finds the meet and confer requirement has not been met because the parties met and conferred by letter only, the Court will address the merits of the Demurrer in the interests of judicial efficiency.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Discussion

 

First Cause of Action for Negligence (Survival Action) and Third Cause of Action for Wrongful Death

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) “The elements of a cause of action for wrongful death are a tort, such as negligence, and resulting death.” (Lopez v. City of Los Angeles (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 675, 685.)

 

Defendants argue the allegations are uncertain, ambiguous, conclusory, and are not stated with particularity—the FAC does not state specific facts regarding the role of Defendants in Decedent’s death. Defendants further argue that the FAC does not identify the specific type of duty Defendants owed to Decedent.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that negligence may be alleged in general terms, and the FAC contains sufficient allegations to support causes of action for negligence and wrongful death. Plaintiffs argue that no heightened pleading standard applies at the demurrer stage. Defendants rely on cases at the motion for summary judgment stage to support their contention that the element of duty must be specifically pled.

 

In reply, Defendants reiterate that the Complaint fails to allege a specific duty they owed to Decedent. 

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled when the facts as to which the complaint is uncertain are presumptively within the defendant's knowledge. (Chen v. Berenjian (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822.)

 

The Court finds the FAC sufficiently alleges causes of action for negligence and wrongful death. At the demurrer stage, no heightened pleading standard is required—the Court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (E-Fab, Inc., supra, 153 Cal.App.4th at p. 1315.) Here, the FAC allege Decedent was in the care of Defendants, and that Decedent and Defendants were in a “a special relationship due to Decedent’s disabling mental health disorder that rendered her incapable of taking care of herself.” (FAC, ¶ 35.) The FAC alleges Defendants owed a duty of care to keep Decedent safe from harm. (FAC, ¶ 36.) It can be reasonably inferred from the allegations that Defendants, as contractors with County of Los Angeles, the conservator of Decedent, were involved in the management Decedent’s mental health services. (FAC, ¶¶ 7-8, 22-28.) The FAC alleges Defendants breached their duty by failing to properly evaluate Decedent, recommending an inappropriate level of residential living care, and allowing her to walk out of the facility despite their knowledge of Decedent’s mental health history. (FAC, ¶ 37.) The FAC alleges the breach was a proximate cause of Decedent’s injuries and death. (FAC, ¶¶ 38-49.) Taking the allegations as true, Court finds all elements of negligence and wrongful death have been adequately alleged.

 

Application of MICRA

 

Defendants further demur on grounds that medical malpractice is the only appropriate cause of action, where Plaintiffs allege Defendants are healthcare providers that owed a special duty to Decedent to provide healthcare services. Defendants argue that the FAC should allege medical malpractice, such that Medical Injury Compensation Recovery Act (MICRA) should apply. Defendants argue that, if a plaintiff brings a claim against a healthcare professional other than one for professional negligence, that a court must nonetheless determine whether the claim is based on the provider’s professional negligence and whether MICRA would apply, citing Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 336, 347.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that they are entitled to plead general negligence, as opposed to medical malpractice or professional negligence. Defendants misstate that the FAC designates Defendants as “healthcare providers.” Plaintiffs argue that MICRA only applies to healthcare providers and healthcare institutions as defined by law, citing Kotler v. Alma Lodge (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1381. Lastly, Plaintiffs argue this issue should be determined at the summary judgment stage, where Defendants bear the burden of production to show they fall under the procedural regulations of MICRA.

 

In reply, Defendants argue the scope of professional negligence is broad, citing Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital (2016) 63 Cal.4th 75, 88. Defendants argue that the Complaint alleges Defendants provided a healthcare service by “authorizing step-down to a facility that provided a lower level of supervision and care.” (FAC, ¶ 23.) Defendants argue such conduct is a healthcare service that allegedly led to Plaintiff’s injury. Thus, Plaintiff’s claims should have been alleged as professional negligence and covered by MICRA.

 

The Court initially finds the Larson and Flores cases are inapplicable because there, it was presupposed that defendants, an anesthesiologist and hospital, respectively, were healthcare providers. In those cases, the issue was whether Defendants’ conduct was a healthcare service and should have been alleged as professional negligence. Here, it is unclear whether Defendants are healthcare providers. Thus, the threshold issue is whether Defendants are healthcare providers such that MICRA should apply.

 

MICRA defines “health care provider” as the following:

 

“(1) “Health care provider” means any person licensed or certified pursuant to Division 2 (commencing with Section 500) of the Business and Professions Code, or licensed pursuant to the Osteopathic Initiative Act, or the Chiropractic Initiative Act, or licensed pursuant to Chapter 2.5 (commencing with Section 1440) of Division 2 of the Health and Safety Code; and any clinic, health dispensary, or health facility, licensed pursuant to Chapter 1 (commencing with Section 1200) or Chapter 1.3 (commencing with Section 1248) of Division 2 of the Health and Safety Code, and does not include health care institutions that are defined in paragraph (2).”View Options

(Civ. Code, § 3333.2.)

 

The issue of whether a Division 2 licensed residential facility was a health care provider under MICRA was at issue in Kotler v. Alma Lodge (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1381, 1391. The Court of Appeals first considered the legislative intent of MICRA, which was to “remedy a health care crisis attributed to the high cost of malpractice insurance.” (Ibid.) The Court found that “only Division 2 licensees entitled to the protections of MICRA are those licensed as clinics, health dispensaries or health facilities.” (Id. at p. 1391.) The court next examined the statutory definitions of “clinic,” “health dispensary,” and “health facility.” (Ibid.) A clinic is “an organized outpatient health facility which provides direct medical, surgical, dental, optometric, or podiatric advice, services, or treatment to patients who remain less than 24 hours.” (Health & Saf. Code, § 1200, subd. (a).) A health facility is “any facility, place, or building which is organized, maintained, and operated for the diagnosis, care, prevention, and treatment of human illness, physical or mental, including convalescence and rehabilitation. . .to which the persons are admitted for a 24-hour stay or longer. . .” (Health & Saf. Code, § 1250.) The court found that, while “health dispensary” is not statutorily defined, a one case declared a blood bank is a health dispensary. (Id. at p. 1394.) The Court reasoned Defendant was not a health dispensary because it was not licensed to distribute health products. (Id. at p. 1395.) The court ultimately found defendant was not a healthcare provider under MICRA, and MICRA did not apply. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds that Defendants have not adequately substantiated how either of them are “healthcare providers” as defined by MICRA. Defendants merely argue that, because the FAC alleges Defendants provided services to coordinate Decedent’s mental health services, that they are healthcare providers. The Court finds that nothing in the FAC suggests that either Defendant falls under the statutory definitions of “healthcare provider.” Defendants have provided no additional evidence. Because it is unclear whether Defendants are healthcare providers under MICRA, the Court finds no grounds for demurrer based on failure to allege medical malpractice.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court overrules the Demurrer to the first and third causes of action.

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Counsel for Defendants’ Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

 

Counsel to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a breach of contract action. Counsel for all MedMen Defendants, Scott J. Leipzig and Tim C. Hsu, on behalf of Allen Matkins Leck Gamble Mallory & Natsis LLP, now move to be relieved as counsel.

 

On December 27, 2024, Counsel filed a notice of non-opposition to the Motion.

 

Legal Standard

 

Good cause exists to grant a motion to be relieved as counsel based on any of the grounds under Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3-700(c). Rule 3-700(c) provides that an attorney may withdraw based on any of the following: (1) The client (a) insists upon presenting a claim or defense that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law, or (b) seeks to pursue an illegal course of conduct, or (c) insists that the member pursue a course of conduct that is illegal or that is prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (d) by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively, or (e) insists, in a matter not pending before a tribunal, that the member engage in conduct that is contrary to the judgment and advice of the member but not prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (f) breaches an agreement or obligation to the member as to expenses or fees; (2) The continued employment is likely to result in a violation of these rules or of the State Bar Act; (3) the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal; (4) The member's mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively; (5) The client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the employment; or (6) The member believes in good faith, in a proceeding pending before a tribunal, that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal. 

 

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-052)); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-053)). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (d), (e).) 

 

Discussion

 

Counsel has filed a filed a Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. (See Form MC-051.) Counsel has also filed a proposed order. (See Form MC-053.) Attorney Tim C. Hsu’s declaration demonstrates good cause for withdrawal based on an irreconcilable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship, such that “ethical and professional considerations and obligations justify a termination of the relationship.” (Hsu Decl., ¶ 2.) Attorney Hsu states that the clients were served copies of the motion papers by mail at the clients’ last known addresses, which were confirmed by public filings and electronic mail in the past thirty (30) days. (Hsu Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Accordingly, the Court grants the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel.
