Case Number: 21STCV14537    Hearing Date: January 11, 2024    Dept: X

   Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

 

 

HEARING DATE:     January 11, 2024                                 TRIAL DATE: August 20, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         JANE DOE, an individual, v. LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT, a public entity; JEREMY HULES, an individual; and DOES 1 through 60, inclusive.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV14537

 

           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      No response filed.

 

SERVICE:                              Filed November 6, 2023

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

             Plaintiff moves for an order compelling Delilah Salas to appear for deposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

             This case arises out of Plaintiff Jane Doe’s (“Plaintiff”) claim that she was the victim of childhood sexual assault during the 2011/2012 school year when she was a student at Mount Gleason Middle School in Los Angeles California. Plaintiff filed this complaint on April 16, 2021.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Plaintiff’s motion for an order compelling Salas to comply with the deposition subpoena is GRANTED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

 

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

            On August 25, 2023, Plaintiff served a deposition subpoena for Salas with the deposition date scheduled for September 7, 2023. (Suh Decl. ¶ 2, Exhibit 1.) Salas appeared remotely for deposition at 10:06 a.m. on September 7, 2023. (Id. ¶ 3.) At around 11:04 a.m., Salas exited the remote deposition. (Ibid, Exhibit 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel contacted Salas to resume the deposition, but Salas refused to resume the deposition. (Id. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff has demonstrated that she served a deposition subpoena on Salas, but that Salas refused to continue with the deposition.

 

            Plaintiff’s motion for an order compelling Salas to comply with the deposition subpoena served on August 25, 2023, is granted. Plaintiff is ordered to confer with Salas regarding a potential date to resume deposition.

 

CONCLUSION

 

            Plaintiff’s motion for an order compelling Salas to comply with the deposition subpoena is GRANTED.

                                                                                    Joel L. Lofton

                                                                                    Judge of the Superior Court

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Doe 1 (“Pasadena Unified School District” or “PUSD”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      No response filed.

 

SERVICE:                              Filed December 6, 2023

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

            PUSD moves to set aside the default entered against it on October 16, 2023.

 

BACKGROUND

 

             This case arises out of Plaintiff Jane Doe K.B.’s (“Plaintiff”) claim she was the victim of childhood sexual assault and abuse between 1975 and 1977. Plaintiff filed this case on November 21, 2022 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            PUSD’s motion to set aside the entry of default is GRANTED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief. [Citation.]” (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.)  The discretionary relief provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subd. (b) provide in relevant part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” 

 

The mandatory relief provision provides that a court shall grant relief “whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. section 473, subd. (b).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

            PUSD moves to set aside the default that was entered against it. Plaintiff filed this lawsuit on November 21, 2022. Plaintiff served PUSD on April 10, 2023, by serving Diana Orona. Orona retired shortly after the lawsuit was served and no other employee recalled receiving notice. (Wadlington ¶ 4.) Default was entered against PUSD on October 16, 2023. PUSD has demonstrated that its failure to respond to the present lawsuit was based on the mistake of one of its employees. Further, once PUSD was notified of the default, it moved to set aside the entry of default.

 

            PUSD’s motion to set aside the entry of default is granted.

 

CONCLUSION

 

            PUSD’s motion to set aside the entry of default is GRANTED.

Case Number: 23AHCV00677    Hearing Date: January 11, 2024    Dept: X

   Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

 

 

HEARING DATE:     January 11, 2024                                             TRIAL DATE: No date set.

                                                          

CASE:                         DIANA TURK BARNES, Trustee of the DB Living Trust, dated April 29, 2022, v. JACOB S. SZYMANSKI and CAROLINE F. SZYMANSKI, Co-Trustees of the Szymanski Community Property Trust Dated December 6, 2018, and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive.  

 

CASE NO.:                 23AHCV00677

 

           

 

MOTION TO APPOINT A RECEIVER

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Diana Turk Barnes (“Plaintiff”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      Defendants Jacob S. Szymanski and Caroline F. Szymanski (“Defendants”)

 

SERVICE:                              Filed December 28, 2023

 

OPPOSITION:                       Filed December 7, 2023

 

REPLY:                                   Filed January 2, 2024

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

             Plaintiff moves for the appointment of a limited receiver over Defendants’ property.

 

BACKGROUND

 

             This case arises out of Plaintiff Diana Turk Barnes’s (“Plaintiff”) claim that an adjacent property poses a risk to her property. Plaintiff owns property located at 370 Sequoia Drive in Pasadena, California (“Plaintiff’s Property”). Plaintiff alleges that Defendants Jacob S. Szymanski and Caroline F. Szymanski (“Defendants”) own property located at 353 Anita Drive in Pasadena California (“Defendants’ Property”). Plaintiff alleges that the two properties share a common slope and that the condition of Defendants’ property poses a risk to her property. Plaintiff filed this complaint on March 28, 2023, alleging six causes of action for (1) negligence, (2) trespass, (3) deprivation of subjacent and lateral support, (4) nuisance, (5) injunctive relief, and (6) declaratory relief.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion for the appointment of a receiver is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiff moves for the appointment of a limited receiver over Defendants’ property. Although Plaintiff titles her motion one for a “limited” receiver, in her notice of motion, Plaintiff seeks a receiver to oversee the investigation, remediation, and all related transactions related to the alleged dangerous slope condition on Defendants’ property, which forms the entire basis for this lawsuit.

 

            “A receiver is an officer of the court and is subject to the court's continuing control; a receiver only has those powers granted to it by statute or an order of the court. [Citation.] The receiver, acting for the court, is not the agent of any party but acts for the benefit of all holding an interest in the receivership property.” (City of Sierra Madre v. SunTrust Mortgage, Inc. (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 648, 657.) “Most matters related to receiverships rest in the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Id. at p. 657.)

            Plaintiff cites to two code sections as the legal basis for her current motion. The first code section cited is Code of Civil Procedure section 564, subdivision (b)(9), which provides that a receiver may be appointed “[i]n all other cases where necessary to preserve the property or rights of any party.” This section may potentially apply here.

 

            The other section cited by Plaintiff is Code of Civil Procedure section 564, subdivision (b)(1), which provides that a receiver may be appointed “[i]n an action by a vendor to vacate a fraudulent purchase of property, or by a creditor to subject any property or fund to the creditor's claim, or between partners or others jointly owning or interested in any property or fund, on the application of the plaintiff, or of any party whose right to or interest in the property or fund, or the proceeds of the property or fund, is probable, and where it is shown that the property or fund is in danger of being lost, removed, or materially injured.” This section, despite Plaintiff’s attempt to cite only portions of the language, is not relevant in the present case. Thus, the court will analyze Plaintiff’s motion under subdivision (b)(9).

 

            Thus, the issue presented is whether Plaintiff has demonstrated a sufficient threat to the preservation of her property or property rights to justify the drastic remedy of appointing a receiver over Defendants’ property.

 

            Plaintiff relies primarily on the declaration of Sean D. Wilson ("Wilson"), an engineering geologist, to support her claim that Defendants’ property require immediate and drastic oversight. Wilson provides an unpermitted tennis court was constructed on Defendants’ property between 1926 and 1944. (Wilson Decl. ¶¶ 8(a)-(b).) He also provides that a near-vertical drop exists on property. (Id. ¶ 8(c).) He opines that the slope will continue to erode and fail absent “immediate” measures. (Id. ¶ 8(d).) He also references last year’s rain condition and the anticipated rain conditions for the coming year to argue that slope presents a dangerous condition. (Id. ¶ 8(f)-(i).)

 

            Additionally, Plaintiff argues that a receiver should be appointed because Defendants’ property violates Pasadena Municipal Code section 14.05.170, subdivision (A), which provides that “[t]he slope of cut surfaces shall be no steeper than is safe for the intended use including landscaping or vegetation for erosion control. Cuts shall be no steeper than 2 horizontal to 1 vertical.” Plaintiff also argues that other slope failures in the nearby area within the last eighteen years support her motion. (Motion at pp. 10:16-11:10.)

 

            However, despite her various contentions, Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate that a receiver should be appointed in the present case.

 

“Receivers are often legal luxuries, frequently representing an extravagant cost to a losing litigant. When it appears that no reasonably certain benefit will result to one litigant, and a distinct disadvantage will result to another, courts should weigh carefully the propriety of appointing a receiver.” (Elson v. Nyhan (1941) 45 Cal.App.2d 1, 5.) “Ordinarily, if there is any other remedy, less severe in its results, which will adequately protect the rights of the parties, a court should not take property out of the hands of its owners.” (Alhambra-Shumway Mines, Inc. v. Alhambra Gold Mine Corp. (1953) 116 Cal.App.2d 869, 873.)

 

First, the court notes that in many cases, a trial court appoints a receiver after the substantive issues have been adjudicated. (See City of Sierra Madre v. SunTrust Mortgage, Inc. (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 648, 653-54 [receiver appointed after court issued a preliminary injunction ordering remediation of the property]; County of Sonoma v. Quail (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 657, 665-66 [receiver appointed after stipulated judgment and parties’ failure to respond].) Here, Plaintiff seeks the appointment of a receiver before any issues have been heard or adjudicated.

 

Second, the evidence provided in support of Plaintiff’s is insufficient to justify such a drastic remedy. Plaintiff’s expert opines of a dangerous condition and possible harm in the future. However, Wilson’s declaration is speculative and lacks reasoned support. To borrow language commonly applied to the summary judgment standard, it is insufficient for a party to provide “laconic expert declarations which provide only an ultimate opinion, unsupported by reasoned explanation.” (Kelley v. Trunk (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 519, 525.) Wilson fails to establish why the hazardous condition on Defendant’s property requires “immediate” remediation other than speculatively asserting that this year’s rain could cause harm. Wilson cites to evidence nor does he present analysis for why, despite the hazardous condition existing for between 80 to 100 years, “immediate” measures must be taken.

 

Third, the remainder of Plaintiff’s argument fail to demonstrate that the appointment of a receiver is necessary. Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendant’s property violates the City of Pasadena’s municipal code and her allegations that Pasadena has failed to order Defendant to remedy the violation is not sufficient to establish wresting Defendants’ property rights away from them. This is not a writ proceeding, and Plaintiff has failed to establish why an alleged municipal code violation warrants such drastic measures.  Further, Plaintiff’s citations to three other cases within eighteen years is not sufficient to establish that action must be taken now to remedy the alleged hazardous condition on Defendants’ Property, which is not the property at issue in the other lawsuits.

 

Lastly, Plaintiff fails to cite legal authority to support her claim. The two primary cases cited by Plaintiff for the proposition that a receiver should be appointed are readily distinguishable. In City of Sierra Madre, supra, 32 Cal.App.5th at p. 651, the first case cited by Plaintiff, the issue on appeal was not the appointment of a receiver but the issuance of a super-priority lien.

 

In City and County of San Francisco v. Daley (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 734, 737, the trial court had entered judgment against owners of a residential property with numerous unsafe conditions and found the property to be a public nuisance. After years of the defendants’ noncompliance, the trial court ordered the appointment of a receiver. (Id. at p. 741.) The appellate court affirmed the appointment of a receiver and stated “[t]he procedural history of this case . . . demonstrates beyond peradventure that this is an exceptional case warranting the appointment of a receiver.” (Id. at p. 744.) The Court stated that the trial court correctly appointed a receiver because the defendants refused to abate the public nuisance and code violations, transferred title to attempt to avoid responsibility, repeatedly blamed each other, and failed to follow court orders. (Id. at pp. 744-45.)

           

            Here, unliked in Daley, there has been no adjudication on the merits of the case and there is no procedural history establishing Defendants’ noncompliance with court orders. Thus, Plaintiff has not established that a receiver should be appointed in the present matter.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff’s motion for the appointment of a receiver is DENIED.
