DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SCV12898    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

Hearing Date: 6/25/24

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV12898 GEVORG BADALYAN vs. BROOKFIELD PROPERTIES

Moving Party: Defendant Watch Zone

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION TO STRIKE IS GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Legal Standard

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768 (“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”).)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

 

Punitive Damages

 

A plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.¿ To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.¿ (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042; Today's IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.)

 

“An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294(b).) 

 

Discussion

On April 18, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present complaint against Defendants Brookfield Properties, Helzberg's Diamond Shops, LLC, and Glendale Galleria. Plaintiff alleges that an employee from Helzberg Diamond accused Plaintiff of stealing jewelry from the store. As a result, Plaintiff was arrested, falsely accused of theft, and unlawfully detained. On March 1, 2024, Defendant Watch Zone was added to the case as a Doe amendment. Defendant Watch Zone moves to strike from Plaintiff’s Complaint Plaintiff's prayer for "punitive and exemplary damages” in the Third, Fourth, Sixth, and Eighth causes of action" as stated in Plaintiff’s Complaint, page 33, lines 3-4.

Although the complaint contains various allegations against named Defendants, no specific allegations were made against Defendant Watch Zone, who was a Doe Defendant in the original complaint. The Court is not sure how Defendant Watch Zone is involved in this matter, though from the Complaint and briefing on this motion it appears that Defendant Watch Zone was involved in reporting to the police that Plaintiff had stolen something from Defendant Helzberg Diamond. In the opposition, Plaintiff claims that the following allegations are sufficient to sustain a prayer for punitive damages: “Based on knowledge of past incidents of inappropriate conduct and behavior of its employees and/or agents, Brookfield Properties, Helzberg's Diamond, and/or Glendale Galleria, Does 2-10, Does 12-100, and each of them, knew, or in the exercise of reasonable diligence should have known that such behavior, as that exhibited by Defendant Doe 1, was likely to reoccur and cause physical injury and emotional distress to other similarly situated patrons, like Plaintiff. However, the Defendants failed to properly investigate the prior incidents resulting in the occurrence of the subject incident and the baseless accusation of the Plaintiff for stealing jewelry from the jewelry store.”

The court is not persuaded for several reasons. First, the allegations relied on by Plaintiff are not specific to Watch Zone; the allegations apply to multiple other Defendants. Legal Standard

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768 (“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”).)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

 

Punitive Damages

 

A plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.¿ To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.¿ (Smith v. “An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”  (Civ. Discussion

Although the complaint contains various allegations against named Defendants, no specific allegations were made against Defendant Watch Zone, who was a Doe Defendant in the original complaint. The court is not persuaded for several reasons. First, the allegations relied on by Plaintiff are not specific to Watch Zone; the allegations apply to multiple other Defendants. Second, Plaintiff failed to plead facts with specificity how Defendant Watch Zone had any knowledge about anything regarding Doe 1, the Helzberg’s Diamond sales associate who allegedly falsely accused Plaintiff of stealing jewelry that led to Plaintiff being falsely accused of theft and wrongfully detained -- Doe 1 is an employee of Helzberg Diamond, not Watch Zone. (Complaint ¶ 24). There is an allegation relating to Watch Zone, but that allegation states that an employee of Watch Zone refused to falsely lie and falsely accuse Plaintiff of cheating when asked by Doe 1 (Complaint ¶ 30).

Legal Standard 

 

A cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the initial complaint or cross-complaint against the cross-complainant must be filed before or at the same time as the answer to the initial complaint or cross-complaint, which answer must be filed within 30 days of service of the complaint or cross-complaint.¿(CCP §§ 412.20(a)(3), 428.50(a), 432.10.)¿Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a trial date.¿(CCP §428.50(b).)

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324 requires a copy of the proposed cross complaint to be attached to the motion.

Defendant also attached a copy of the proposed cross-complaint. All requirements for leave have been met, and the Court hereby grants the present motion.

MOTION TO SET ASIDE

Hearing Date: 6/25/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV16693 KHALIFA LAHUT vs ALBERTSONS COMPANIES

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO SET ASIDE IS GRANTED 

 

Legal standard

 

“It is … well established that it is the policy of the law to bring about a trial on the merits whenever possible, so that any doubts which may exist should be resolved in favor of the application, to the end of securing to a litigant his day in court and a trial upon the merits.” (Frank E. Beckett Co. v. Bobbitt (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d Supp. 921, 928.)

 

Section 473, subdivision (b) provides for two distinct types of relief—commonly differentiated as “discretionary” and “mandatory”—from certain prior actions or proceedings in the trial court. (Luri v. Greenwald (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1119, 1124.) “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of ‘mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,’ the court has discretion to allow relief from a ‘judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against’ a party or his or her attorney.  Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of ‘mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,’ the court shall vacate any ‘resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.’” (Ibid., internal citations and quotation marks omitted, quoting CCP § 473, subd. (b).)

 

An application for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) must be made within a reasonable time and in no case, exceeding six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought. (CCP § 473, subd. (b).)

 

The mandatory portion of section 473 requires the court to, “whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Discussion

On May 20, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present premises liability case against Defendant Albertsons. On November 17, 2023, the Court dismissed the action for failure to prosecute because Defendant Albertsons Companies, Inc. was not served and no one appeared at the hearing. Plaintiff's counsel states that the reason for not appearing at the hearing was due to counsel’s assistant failing to properly set the alarm for the hearing; therefore, he was not aware of the hearing and did not attend. On May 10, 2024, Plaintiff moved the Court for relief.

Plaintiff’s request is timely filed. The Court dismissed the case on November 17, 2023, and Plaintiff filed the present motion on May 10, 2024, within six months of the dismissal. Furthermore, Defendant’s relief is mandatory. The court order at issue is an order of dismissal, and the moving papers contain the attorney’s affidavit attesting to his mistake that resulted in the dismissal of the case. Furthermore, no opposition was filed alleging prejudice, and there is no reason for the Court to find that the dismissal was not a result of counsel’s mistake. All requirements for mandatory relief have been met. Thus, the Court grants the present motion.

MOTION TO STRIKE

Moving Party: Defendant Simon Jihoon Sohng

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO STRIKE IS DENIED

 

Background

On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed his first amended complaint, alleging that at the time of the incident, he was employed as a firefighter/paramedic and was seated in the front passenger seat of an on-duty ambulance with its sirens activated. He was struck by Defendant Simon Jihoon Sohng, who was under the influence of methamphetamine at the intersection of Laveta Terrace and Temple Street. In the FAC, Plaintiff seeks punitive damages against Defendant. Defendant now moves the Court to strike the following allegation in the FAC concerning punitive damages.

1. Page 4, paragraph 11, line 6: “knowingly and intentionally”.

2. Page 4, paragraph 12, lines 14-20: Starting with “12. Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereupon alleges that on the same day, and immediately prior to, the SUBJECT INCIDENT, Defendant Simon Jihoon Sohng willfully and intentionally consumed METH

. . .” and ending with “. . . on the public streets and busy roadways of the City of Los Angeles.”

 3. Page 5, paragraph 14, lines 4-5: “with willful and conscious disregard for the safety of others and the public at large,”

4. Page 5, paragraph 15, lines 9-13: Starting with “15. Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereupon alleges, that, on the same day, but prior to, the SUBJECT INCIDENT, Defendant Simon Jihoon Sohng was well aware that consumption of METH. . .” and ending with “. . . densely populated urban center such as the Cityof Los Angeles.”

5. Page 5, paragraph 16, lines 14-20: Starting with “16. Furthermore, Plaintiff is also informed and believes and thereupon alleged that prior to and at the time of the SUBECT INCIDENT, Defendant Simon Jihoon Sohng was well aware . . .” and ending with “. . . persons lawfully upon the roadways including other drivers, pedestrians, and bystanders.”

6. Page 6, paragraph 18, lines 4-5: “, knowingly, despicably and with conscious disregard for the safety of others and the public at large,”

7. Page 6, paragraph 18, lines 8-9: “and highly probable”

8. Page 6, paragraph 18, line 9: “willful”

9. Page 7, paragraph 21, lines 3-4: “with an intentional, knowing, and wanton disregard,”

10. Page 7, paragraph 21, line 6: “probably

11. Page 10, paragraph 35, lines 10-22: Starting with “35. Per California Civil Code, § 3294 (which gives a Plaintiff the right to seek punitive damages . . .” and ending with “. . . for his malicious actions against Plaintiff, which did cause him severe physical and emotional injuries.”

12. Page 11, paragraph 6, lines 5-6: “For punitive and exemplary damages in an amount to appropriately punish Defendant Simon Johoon Sohng, DOES 1 through 50, inclusive, and each of them;”

Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768 (“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”).)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  

 

Driving While Intoxicated

The case of Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890 is instructive with respect to punitive damages for driving while under the influence. There, the court discussed punitive damages and fell short of holding that punitive damages are always appropriate in cases involving driving while intoxicated. (Taylor, 24 Cal.3d at 892.)  The Taylor court noted, “we have concluded that the act of operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated may constitute an act of ‘malice’ under section 3294 if performed under circumstances which disclose a conscious disregard of the probable dangerous consequences.”  (Id. (emphasis added).)

 

Subsequently, in Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89, the court held that driving while intoxicated does not always give rise to a claim for punitive damages: “[W]e do not agree that the risk created generally by one who becomes intoxicated and decides nevertheless to drive a vehicle to the public streets is the same as the risk created by an intoxicated driver’s decision to zigzag in and out of traffic at 65 miles per hour in a crowded beach recreation area at 1:30 in the afternoon on a Sunday in June. The risk of injury to others from ordinary driving while intoxicated is certainly foreseeable, but it is not necessarily probable.” In Dawes, the Court found that the risk of injury to others from the defendant’s conduct under the circumstances was probable. (Id.)

 

The Dawes court went on to note, “. . . petitioners pleaded specific facts from which the conscious disregard of probable injury to others may reasonably be inferred.  Justice Franson aptly noted the distinction in his article on punitive damages in vehicle accident cases: ‘Allegations of intoxication, excessive speed, driving with defective equipment or the running of a stop signal, without more, do not state a cause of action for punitive damages. [Par.] On the other hand, if the facts show that the defendant intentionally drove his vehicle at a high speed into an intersection crowded with pedestrians, or if he drove at a high speed through a crowded residential area where children were playing in the street, a legitimate inference of actual malice perhaps could arise. (Id. at 90.)

 

Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff has pled sufficient facts to support a prayer for punitive damages under the "drunk driving plus" standard adopted by the progeny in Taylor and Dawes. First, Defendant was not merely drinking but was under the influence of methamphetamine, a substance whose mind-altering capabilities far exceed that of alcohol prior to the incident. (FAC ¶ 11.) Second, Defendant was driving at excessive speeds, approximately 40-60 mph on a small road, Laveta Terrace. (FAC ¶ 22.) Third, Defendant ran the red light and as a result struck the ambulance carrying Plaintiff at the intersection. (FAC ¶ 10.) The combination of methamphetamine use, running red lights, and excessive speeding demonstrates that the risk of injury to others was not only foreseeable but necessarily probable. Thus, the present motion to strike is DENIED.

Plaintiff originally noticed Defendant’s deposition for December 6, 2023. Counsel for the parties met and conferred, agreeing to reschedule Defendant’s deposition to February 28, 2023. On that date, Defendant failed to appear for his noticed deposition, without serving any objections or providing any reasons or explanations to justify his nonappearance. Consequently, a certificate of nonappearance was issued.

Defendant presents several procedural arguments. Defendant asserts that the motion was mistakenly brought under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.480, while the correct statute is § 2025.450. The Court, after reviewing the moving papers, has verified that the notice correctly cited the motion under § 2025.450(a). (Moving Paper at pg. 2.) Second, Defendant requests a protective order in the opposition. This is procedurally improper as Defendant must file a motion for a protective order. Making such a request within the opposition fails to provide Plaintiff with proper notice, does not give Plaintiff the statutorily allotted time to formulate an opposition, and deprives the Court of a full briefing on the issue.

As to the substance of the motion, counsel for Defendant does not dispute that Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling Defendant’s deposition (Opposition at pg. 4), but requests additional time to comply as counsel has lost contact with Defendant.

Similarly, Defendant requests that sanctions not be imposed against counsel, noting that considerable efforts have been made to locate Defendant, including the hiring of a private investigator. Under these circumstances, the Court orders Defendant to sit for his deposition within 60 days from today (instead of the standard 20 days), and sanctions in the amount of $2,204.00, which the Court finds reasonable, to be paid to Plaintiff within 60 days by Defendant only.

MOTION TO VACATE

Hearing Date: 6/25/24 

CASE NO./NAME: BC649616 ROSA VELASCO VS RICHARD ESCOTO

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO VACATE IS DENIED

 

Legal standard 

 

CCP Section 473(b) provides for two distinct types of relief—commonly differentiated as “discretionary” and “mandatory”—from certain prior actions or proceedings in the trial court. (Luri v. Greenwald (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1119, 1124.) “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of ‘mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,’ the court has discretion to allow relief from a ‘judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against’ a party or his or her attorney.  Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of ‘mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,’ the court shall vacate any ‘resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.’” (Ibid., internal citations and quotation marks omitted, quoting CCP § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

An application for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) must be made within a reasonable time and in no case, exceeding six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought. (CCP § 473, subd. (b).)

 

The mandatory portion of section 473 requires the court to, “whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Discussion

 

On February 22, 2017, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendant Janet Delaney. On June 11, 2019, the complaint was amended to add Defendant Richard Escoto. On February 28, 2020, Defendant Escoto was served. On June 21, 2021, Defendant Delaney’s motion for summary judgment was granted. On November 8, 2023, after multiple failures to acquire a default judgment against Escoto, the Court informed Plaintiff during the hearing that the case would be dismissed. On May 9, 2024, Defendant moved the Court for mandatory relief under CCP § 473(b), alleging a mistake by Defendant’s counsel.

In the November 8, 2023, minute order, the Court heard from Plaintiff and indicated, “There being no default judgment entered on this date and given the age of the case, the Court dismisses this action without prejudice pursuant to CCP § 581(g), CCP § 583.410, and CRC Rule 3.110(h).”

This case was dismissed on November 8, 2023, and the present motion for relief was filed more than six months later on May 9, 2024. CCP § 473(b) requires a motion for relief to be filed within “six months after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” Furthermore, no proof of service was attached to the moving papers showing that Defendant Escoto was notified of the present motion.  Also, as required under CCP § 473(b), the proposed document to be filed to correct the issues that arose regarding the default judgement was not included with Plaintiff’s motion.

Defendant argues that the five-year period for bringing Plaintiff's matter to trial was tolled on December 22, 2021, when default was entered against Defendant. This argument is irrelevant. The dismissal was pursuant to CCP § 583.410, where a court has discretion to dismiss an action for delay in prosecution (see Code Civ. Proc., § 583.410(a).) Pursuant to CRC Rule 3.1340, the Court can dismiss a case under CPP 583.410 for failing to be brought to trial or conditionally settled after two years. Technicalities aside, the point is that Defendant was added in June 2019, and as of November 2023, more than four years later, Plaintiff had still not obtained a default judgment against Defendant despite numerous attempts. It was not one mistake that led to the Court’s decision, but rather the overall conduct of Plaintiff’s counsel throughout the case. Thus, the court does not find that the dismissal was caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. Therefore, the present motion is denied.
