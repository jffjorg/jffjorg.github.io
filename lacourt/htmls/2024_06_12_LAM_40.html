DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV18241    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

AIDEE JACQUELINE TINOCO BUCIO

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KHOWSROW BENJI, an individual; ANNA BENJI, an individual; DOES 1 THROUGH 100, inclusive.

                        Defendants.

______________________________________

KHOSROW BENJI, an individual,

                        Cross-Complainant,

            v.

AIDEE JACQUELINE TINOCO BUCIO, an individual; and ROES 1 through 5, inclusive,

                        Cross-Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV18241

 Hearing Date:   6/12/24

 Trial Date:        11/12/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio’s Motion to Compel Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji’s Response to Request for Statement of Damages; and

Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio’s Request for Sanctions Against Defendant and Counsel of Record Bent Caryl & Kroll, LLP in the Amount of $1,190.00.

 

I. Background

On February 26, 2024, Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji filed and served a Cross-Complaint in this action suing Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio (Tinoco Bucio) and Roes 1 through 5 based on claims of (1) Negligence, (2) Assault, (3) Battery, (4) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED), and (5) Physical Elder Abuse.

On March 15, 2024, Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio served a request for statement of damages on Khowsrow Benji.

Khowsrow Benji’s response to the request was due on April 3, 2024.

On April 4, 2024, based on Khowsrow Benji’s nonresponse, Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio filed a motion to compel a response to Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s request for a statement of damages. The motion also seeks sanctions pursuant to the Discovery Act against Khowsrow Benji and counsel.

That same day, Khowsrow Benji served a response to Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s request for a statement of damages.

On May 31, 2024, Khowsrow Benji filed and served an opposition to Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s motion. The opposition was filed one court day late.

On June 3, 2024, Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio filed a reply to Khowsrow Benji’s opposition.

Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Reply Request to Not Consider Untimely Opposition: DENIED.

A. Legal Standard

The principal purpose of the requirement to file and serve a notice of motion a specified number of days before the hearing is to provide the opposing party with adequate time to prepare an opposition. That purpose is served if the party appears at the hearing, opposes the motion on the merits, and was not prejudiced in preparing an opposition by the untimely notice. Consequently, a party who appears at the hearing on a motion and contests the motion on the merits without objecting to a defect or irregularity in the notice of motion, including the failure to serve a notice of motion the prescribed number of days before the hearing, ordinarily is deemed to waive the defect or irregularity. (Arambula v. Union Carbide Corp. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 333, 343.)

Even when the opposing party does expressly object to inadequate notice but then opposes the motion on the merits, the issue of defective notice may be waived unless the opposing party makes a request for a continuance or demonstrates prejudice from the defective notice. (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697 (Carlton); Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1288 (Reedy); see also Robinson v. Woods (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1264 [When a party only “respond[s] to [a] [late-served] motion by filing a written opposition containing only the notice objections and never argue[s] the merits,” no waiver occurs, such that the objecting party need “not … [even] claim or show prejudice [to object to the late-served motion] because the[] [plaintiffs] did not address the merits [of the motion], in writing or otherwise”]; contra. Boyle v. CertainTeed Corp. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 645, 650 [“A party’s participation in a hearing after the party’s objection to the hearing as unauthorized does not constitute waiver by acquiescence”].)

B. Analysis

The Court DENIES Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s reply argument that the Court should not consider the opposition filed by Khowsrow Benji because it was filed one court day late and has allegedly prejudiced Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio.

The Court relies on Carlton and Reedy for its determination. Simply, the reply does not sufficiently demonstrate that prejudice arises from service of the opposition one court day late. Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio is correct in arguing that the opposition should have been filed on May 30, 2024, nine court days before the hearing, rather than on May 31, 2024, eight court days before the hearing. However, Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio alleges undue prejudice based on insufficient time to adequately research, prepare, and file a responsive reply brief. (Reply, p. 2.) This contention is belied by the reply’s points and authorities discussing the arguments raised in the opposition on the merits. The Court thus proceeds with a full analysis of the papers filed with the Court.

 

III. Motion to Compel Response to Statement of Damages: MOOT.

A. Legal Standard

A defendant or cross-defendant that wants to know the nature and amount of damages the plaintiff is seeking may at any time serve a written request for that information on the plaintiff. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11, subd. (b).)

The plaintiff must serve a responsive statement within 15 days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11, subd. (b).)

If the plaintiff fails to serve any statement of damages within 15 days after service of the defendant’s request, the defendant may, on noticed motion, obtain an order compelling the plaintiff to serve a statement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11, subd. (b).)

B. Analysis

The purpose of Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s motion was to compel a response to the request for statement of statement of damages that she served on Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji on March 15, 2024, via email. (Mot., pp. 2, 3; Mot., Akhavan Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.)

The parties’ papers do not dispute that service of the response was due on April 3, 2024, but served on April 4, 2024. (Opp’n, p. 2 & Opp’n, Kroll Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1; Reply, p. 4 [“It was not until April 4, 2024[] that Cross-Complainant [Khowsrow Benji] served his statement of damages”].)

The reply does not challenge the April 4th response on the ground that the response is inadequate. (See Reply, pp. 2-4.)

Accordingly, the Court determines that Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s motion is MOOT.

 

IV. Request for Sanctions: DENIED.

A. Legal Standard

Monetary discovery sanctions may be imposed under Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 only to the extent authorized by another provision of the Discovery Act, where Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 describes conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process but does not authorize the imposition of sanctions. (City of Los Angeles v. PricewaterhouseCoopers, LLC (Oct. 20, 2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 466, 475.)

California Civil Discovery Act is contained in Code of Civil Procedure sections 2016.010 to 2036.050.

B. Analysis

The Court DENIES the request for sanctions.

Here, Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s request for sanctions is premised on Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010, 2023.030, and 2030.290. (Mot., pp. 2, 4.)

However, PricewaterhouseCoopers has made clear that sanctions pursuant to the Discovery Act must be tied to another provision of the Discovery Act, and section 425.11 of the Code of Civil Procedure is not a statutory section in the Discovery Act.

Moreover, the Court notes that Argame v. Werasophon (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 616, 618, fn. 3 (Argame) is distinguishable.

First, the footnote relied on by Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio is dicta because it simply noted that the trial court could have imposed sanctions for the conduct at issue in that case. (Ibid. [“… Considering counsel’s disregard for the requirements of section 425.11 displayed here, the court would have been justified in requiring Argame (or more appropriately her counsel) to reimburse defendants for costs incurred in making such a motion”].) For this reason, the cited footnote in Argame is, at most, a dictum that has no force as precedent and is not binding on this Court. (Katie V. v. Superior Court (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 586, 598 [“Dicta, of course, is not binding authority”]; People v. Linwood (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 59, 69 [“Dictum has no force as precedent”], citing Ginns v. Savage (1964) 61 Cal.2d 520, 524, fn. 2.)

Second, even had the Court considered monetary sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128, pursuant to the Court’s inherent authority to make case-specific orders with due notice given, or pursuant to some other applicable authority, the circumstances before the Court would not have merited sanctions. The response to the statement of damages was served one day late based on a 15-day timeframe. While this is the second time the Court discusses a one-day delay by Khowsrow Benji (see Section II above), again, the Court finds insufficient prejudice arose from this late service to merit the relief requested—here, sanctions.

For these reasons, Cross-Defendant Tinoco Bucio’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

V. Conclusion

A. Motion to Compel Response to Statement of Damages

Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio’s Motion to Compel Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji’s Response to Request for Statement of Damages is MOOT.

B. Request for Sanctions

Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio’s Request for Sanctions Against Defendant and Counsel of Record Bent Caryl & Kroll, LLP in the Amount of $1,190.00 is DENIED.

II. Motion to Compel Further Responses to Production and Request for Monetary Sanctions

A. Mootness

The Court briefly notes that Plaintiff Rueda’s service of supplemental responses to RPDs, Set Eight, on May 30, 2024, did not automatically moot a consideration on whether the Court should order further responses.

“Whether a particular response does resolve satisfactorily the issues raised by a motion is a matter best determined by the trial court in the exercise of its discretion, based on the circumstances of the case.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 409 (Sinaiko).)

B. Motion to Compel Further Responses to Production: GRANTED in part.

1. Legal Standard

a. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310

A motion to compel a further response is used when a party gives unsatisfactory answers or makes untenable objections to demands to produce. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).)

To request further production, a movant must establish:

(1) Good cause for the production (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1); Sinaiko, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 403); and

(2) That a further response is needed because (a) the responding party’s statement of compliance with the demand to produce is incomplete Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(1)), (b) the responding party’s representation that it is unable to comply is inadequate, complete, or evasive (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(2)), (c) the responding party’s objection in the response is without merit or is too general (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(3); Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1127), or (d) if the responding party objected to the production of ESI on the ground that it is not reasonably accessible the movant can show that the (i) ESI is reasonably accessible or (ii) there is good cause for production of the ESI regardless of its accessibility (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (e)).

b. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320

A discovering party may file a motion to compel compliance with a demand to produce when the responding party does not permit inspection, copying, testing, sampling, or measuring in accordance with the compliance statement the responding party serves in respond to the demand to produce. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (a).) 

2. Analysis

a. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310

i. RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, 13-40, “Relevance”

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Rueda.

In response to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, and 13-40, Plaintiff Rueda stated objections and a substantive response stating that Plaintiff Rueda “will produce all responsive, relevant, non-privileged documents in its possession, custody, or control that have not already been produced.” (Mot., Sharon Decl., Ex. 8; Mot., Separate Statement, pp. 1-75.)

Showtime’s motion objects to the qualifier “… relevant … documents in [Plaintiff’s] possession, custody, or control,” which Plaintiff Rueda’s opposition argues is appropriate under the language of Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.010. Plaintiff Rueda’s opposition also argues that his May 30, 2024, supplemental responses removed the word “relevant” from his responses to these production requests, thus mooting Showtime’s motion. (Opp’n, Khan Decl., Ex. E [May 30, 2024, supplemental responses showing original and amended responses to production requests].) Showtime argues that the amendments are a step in the right direction but do not amount to the production of responsive documents, the production of which is imperative for Showtime to prepare for trial in November 2024. (Reply, p. 4.)

The parties do not dispute that Plaintiff Rueda served responses that “removed the ‘relevant’ qualifier.” (Reply, p. 4.)

The Court thus determines that Showtime’s motion is MOOT as to an order compelling further responses to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, and 13-40 that remove the “relevant” qualifier to the substantive portion of Plaintiff Rueda’s initial responses to this discovery.

ii. RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40 and Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230

RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40 seek production of photographs, video recordings, and audio recordings of the various non-parties whom Plaintiff alleges watched, stalked, harassed, and/or followed him. (Mot., Sharon Decl., Ex. 2.)

In response to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, and subject to objections, Plaintiff Rueda offered a substantive response containing a statement of compliance offering to comply with the production requests. (Mot. Separate Statement, pp. 29-75.)

Later, Plaintiff Rueda produced a series of documents that Showtime described as a compilation video mixing photographs and video clips with none of the original metadata attached to the file. (Mot., p. 2.)

Moreover, Plaintiff Rueda amended his discovery responses to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, all of which now contain a substantive response: “Responding Party has produced all non-privileged documents in his possession, custody, and control. To the extent this Request seeks metadata, Responding Party is unable to produce the requested metadata because, following a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry, Responding Party has determined the metadata has been lost, misplaced, or stolen.” (Opp’n, Khan Decl., Ex. E, pp. 17-63.)

Showtime “moves this Court for an order requiring him to provide a statement of inability to comply specifying, inter alia, what documents are missing, what happened to the documents, and the name and address of any individual known or believed to have possession, custody, or control of th[ese] missing documents.” (Mot., p. 2, emphasis added.)

The Court agrees that Plaintiff Rueda has failed to provide the names and addresses of persons or organizations Plaintiff believes have control of responsive documents, or to state that he does not know. (Opp’n, Khan Decl., Ex. E.) However, given the facts alleged by Rueda it is unlikely that he will be able to identify those who are in possession of the responsive documents. Nevertheless, Showtime’s motion is GRANTED as to compelling further responses to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, specifically to supplement the responses to “set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item,” if any. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.) The Court clarifies that only amended responses, not further production, is being ordered.

However, the Court DENIES Showtime’s motion insofar as it requests an order compelling further responses explaining why documents were, for example, lost or destroyed.

The “what happened to the documents” language for which Showtime advocates is not plain or directly inferable language from the statute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230, subd. 2031.230 [“… This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party[,] [and] shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item”].)

Other than citing to the statute itself, Showtime’s papers do not cite any authority supporting its position that section 2031.230 contemplates ‘the reasons for the reasons for inability to comply’ as part of the statute’s requirements. (See Mot., pp. 1-12 [citing § 2031.230 six time, only once supported by case law at p. 10, where case law is inapposite to this question].)

Showtime thus does not provide an authority that on its face states that compliance with section 2031.230 includes stating not only the reason for inability to comply—e.g., “we cannot comply because document(s) were lost or destroyed, etc.”—but also the reason for the reason for inability to comply—e.g., “we cannot comply because document(s) were lost or destroyed, etc., when A, B, and C, happened.”

Typically, a party would follow up on any such response at a deposition or with further discovery. In this case, there has already been significant discovery on the issue of the lost metadata in the phone which apparently took the photos. Thus while the Court sympathizes with the ShowTime Defendants as to the apparent loss of the metadata, the Court has not been directed to, nor able to locate, any authority requiring such information in order to constitute a code-compliant response, and the strict language of the statute does not support Showtime’s interpretation.

iii. RPDs, Set Seven, No. 56, Privileges, Etc.

The Court finds in favor of Showtime.

Where a medical patient tenders his or her medical condition at issue, the patient-litigant exception applies and communications between the physician employed to treat a patient and the patient are not privileged under the physician-patient privilege. (City & County of San Francisco v. Superior Court In and For City and County of San Francisco (1951) 37 Cal.2d 227, 237-238 (City & County of San Francisco); see People v. Hines (1975) 13 Cal.3d 500, 515, fn. 17 (Hynes) [summarizing City & County of San Francisco].)

On the other hand, fundamental principles mandate that a counsel’s right to appointment of necessary experts, including medical or mental health experts, also includes the right to have communications made to the experts remain confidential to the same extent as communications directly between client and lawyer. (Elijah W. v. Superior Court (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 140, 151-153 (Elijah).)

I. Request

Here, RPDs, Set Seven, No. 56 requests production of “ALL COMMUNICATIONS RELATING TO this LAWSUIT between any of YOUR ATTORNEYS OF RECORD and any of YOUR medical and/or mental health providers.” (Mot., Separate Statement, p. 6.)

II. Objections Discussed

Plaintiff Rueda responded to this request with objections alone, most of which the Court finds inapplicable, and none of which are fatal. (Mot., Separate Statement, pp. 6-7.)

The request is not unintelligible, or uncertain, instead clearly seeking communications that relate to this lawsuit as between Plaintiff’s attorneys of record and Plaintiffs medical and/or mental health providers. The request is a little ambiguous as to the meaning of “provider,” as discussed below, but this vagueness is not fatal to the request.

No overbreadth, overbroad tailoring, undue and unreasonable burden, oppression, or harassment objection applies to this the request because, contrary to Plaintiff Rueda’s arguments, the evidence at issue could lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. As argued in the moving papers: “Understanding whether Plaintiff’s attorneys have been communicating with these percipient fact witnesses who possess such key knowledge is critically important to avoid surprise and prepare for trial. Indeed, none of Plaintiff’s treating physicians has even been deposed at this point. Knowing what, if anything, Plaintiff’s counsel has shared with these witnesses or represented to them about the litigation or the parties involved is essential for Showtime to conduct effective discovery regarding Plaintiff’s tort-related allegations.” (Mot., p. 9.)

While a discovery request seeking expert witness communications could merit a premature discovery objection, the request by its terms does not apply to experts hired by plaintiff but rather to “medical and/or menta health providers.” The objection otherwise fails because it is not clear how the information that is sought here is “premature” in this eight-plus-years litigation.

 The confidential, proprietary, and sensitive business information objections fails, as it is not clear what proprietary or sensitive business information could arise from communications between Plaintiff’s counsel and Plaintiff’s medical and/or mental health providers. Such arguments are not elaborated in the objections or in the opposition papers. Confidentiality, in turn, is undermined by Plaintiff alleging an IIED claim that has put his medical and/or mental health condition at issue.

The objection based on third-party privacy rights is not applicable. The discovery at issue relates to communications relating to this lawsuit, as between Plaintiff’s counsel and Plaintiff’s medical and/or mental health providers. Plaintiff’s privacy appears to be the only true privacy interest at issue within that framework, and as discussed above, Plaintiff has put his medical and/or mental health condition in relation to his IIED claim and damages. If privacy relates to the “providers,” the names and addresses or potential witnesses are generally discoverable (Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1249-1250), and the communications with Plaintiff’s counsel do not clearly fall into any privilege not waived (e.g., patient-litigant exception).

The objection that Plaintiff is being asked to produce documents on behalf of another person or entity is not sufficiently elaborated in the objection or opposition papers. Moreover, it is unclear how Plaintiff does not have access to counsel and counsel’s communications with Plaintiff’s providers.

The attorney-client privilege, work product doctrine, and kitchen-sink privilege objections also fail. The specific attorney-client objection fail based on the principles discussed in City & County of San Francisco, as summarized by the California Supreme Court in Hines, supra, 13 Cal.3d at p. 515, fn. 17, stating that “if a physician is employed to treat a patient rather than as an intermediary to interpret defendant’s condition to the patient’s attorney, then the results of such examination are protected by the physician-patient privilege but not by the attorney-client privilege.” The work product objection is not sufficiently supported by the objection itself or the opposition papers, as discussed below. And the kitchen-sink argument fails based on lack of specificity.

The right to privacy objection is undermined by Plaintiff Rueda putting his medical and/or mental condition at issue in relation to his IIED claim and damages. A party can waive its right to privacy by raising a claim or defense in a suit that puts the party’s personal information at issue. (See, e.g., John B. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1199.)

III. Opposition Arguments Discussed

The Court rejects the privilege argument as discussed in Elijah and other authorities. City & County of San Francisco makes clear that a treating physician’s communications with a patient are privileged insofar as they do not fall into the patient-litigant exception. Here, Plaintiff has brought an IIED claim and emotional distress damages claims. There is some component of Plaintiff’s medical condition at issue. Reliance on Elijah specifically is misplaced where neither Plaintiff’s objections nor his opposition—no separate statement filed—explain how all communications between his counsel and his medical and/or mental health providers ONLY involve(d) communications between counsel and physicians hired to interpret Plaintiff’s condition to his attorneys of record.

The opposition’s Evidence Code section 952 argument is inapplicable where the request does not involve a request for communications from a plaintiff to his attorney made through or in the presence of a treating physician.

The opposition’s Code of Civil Procedure section 2018.030 argument—work product, etc.—may be applicable. However, Plaintiff has not explained what portion of the communications at issue will result in the production of documents that show only attorney work product, contrasted from, for example, percipient witness observations regarding Plaintiff Rueda’s medical and/or mental health condition. Without that information, the work product argument fails, or at a minimum, such documents should be included in a privilege log.

Last, the Court is not convinced by Plaintiff’s argument that he need not provide a privilege log in relation to this discovery. To the extent the May 11, 2018, protective order excludes “counsel’s communications with experts, consultants or employees … assisting or consulting in the prosecution or defense of this case,” and it is, again, not clear that the communications requested involved only conversations with experts.

IV. Court’s Determination

The Court GRANTS Showtime’s motion as to RPDs, Set Seven, No. 56.

The Court notes that if Plaintiff has retained as expert witnesses any of his treating physicians who are also fact witnesses—the “medical and/or mental health providers” in the request— those relationships must be disclosed.

The Court also notes that as discussed above, a privilege log exception in the May 11, 2018, protective order does not appear applicable here, thus necessitating a privilege log if privileges are raised in response to this discovery, such as in relation to attorney-client, work product, or a medical-based privilege.

Last, the Court notes that a representation of inability to comply with the particular production request must affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand, whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party, and set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)

b. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320

i. RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, Native Files

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Rueda.

I. Metadata

RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40 seek production of photographs, video recordings, and audio recordings of the various non-parties whom Plaintiff alleges watched, stalked, harassed, and/or followed him. (Mot., Sharon Decl., Ex. 2.)

Part of the request, to which Plaintiff did not object, was defining “DOCUMENT(S)” in the requests as involving electronically stored information under Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.020, subdivision (e), and Instruction No. 2, which included “metadata pertaining to electronic DOCUMENTS, in their original file folder(s)” as part of the ESI to be produced. (Mot. Sharon Decl., Exs. 2, pp. 1-2 [RPDs, Set Eight, define “DOCUMENT(S)”], 8 [Plaintiff Rueda’s initial responses].)

In response to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, and subject to objections, Plaintiff Rueda offered a substantive response containing a statement of compliance offering to comply with the production requests. (Mot. Separate Statement, pp. 29-75.)

Later, Plaintiff Rueda produced a series of documents that Showtime described as a compilation video mixing photographs and video clips with none of the original metadata attached to the file. (Mot., p. 2.)

Plaintiff Rueda does not dispute this characterization, instead arguing that Plaintiff Rueda does not have control of the devices from which metadata could be obtained and further explaining why Plaintiff Rueda does not have access to those devices and how Plaintiff has already relayed these circumstances to Showtime in the past. (See Opp’n, pp. 7-10.)

Beyond the above production, Plaintiff Rueda also amended his discovery responses to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, all of which now contain a substantive response: “Responding Party has produced all non-privileged documents in his possession, custody, and control. To the extent this Request seeks metadata, Responding Party is unable to produce the requested metadata because, following a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry, Responding Party has determined the metadata has been lost, misplaced, or stolen.” (Opp’n, Khan Decl., Ex. E, pp. 17-63.)

Through the above discussed production and his amended responses, Plaintiff has MOOTED the need for production in relation to metadata and RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40—contrasted from the need to supplement the responses to set forth contact information for individuals who may have control over the requested documents.

II. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320 Generally

The Court also notes that to the extent that Showtime seeks relief in relation to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40, based on statements of compliance in Plaintiff’s initial December 2023 responses to these requests (Mot., pp. 8-9), those statements of compliance were superseded in Plaintiff Rueda’s May 30, 2024, supplemental responses, which indicate that “[Plaintiff Rueda] has produced all non-privileged documents in his possession, custody, and control” in relation to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40.

Showtime’s motion is thus MOOT in this regard.

ii. RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, 13-15

The Court finds in favor of Showtime, with a caveat as to how Plaintiff should respond.

In response to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, and 13-15, Plaintiff initially served statements of compliance qualified by objections, and then served amended responses deleting the term “relevant” from the objections. (Opp’n, Khan Decl., Ex. E, pp. 4-17 [initial and amended responses].)

The Showtime Defendants argue in Reply that plaintiff has both claimed to have already produced all responsive non-privileged documents, and that he will produce all responsive non-privileged documents. (Reply at p. 5.) Rueda does not clearly indicate in the Opposition whether there are in fact any additional responsive non-privileged documents that have not already been produced. If there are, Rueda should indeed produce these now pursuant to 2031.320. If he has produced all such responsive documents, he should at this time clearly so state in a verified response.

Showtime’s motion is thus GRANTED as to production in relation to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, and 13-15 insofar as relief is sought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320. (Mot., pp. 1-2 [Notice], 8-9 [explicit request for this relief].)

C. Request for Sanctions

1. Legal Standard

a. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310

Except in certain circumstances involving electronic stored information, the court must impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

b. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320

Except as related to certain ESI production, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel compliance with a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (b).) 

c. Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)

2. Analysis

Here, the Court has determined that portions of Showtime’s motion are meritorious and that portions of Plaintiff Rueda’s opposition arguments are meritorious.

Thus, while both parties made reasonable arguments, despite claiming that the other’s perspective lacked merit, the Court will grant sanctions based on the fact that Plaintiff’s supplemental responses were not served until the motion to compel was filed and an opposition was due. However, as the Court did not find merit to all of Showtime’s arguments, the Court will reduce the sanctions to $2000.

Last, the Court notes that its order is made jointly and severally against Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda and his counsel of record, Withers Bergman LLP.

Sanctions are thus GRANTED in part in the amount of $2,000, as against Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda and his counsel of record, Withers Bergman LLP, jointly and severally, to be paid at the conclusion of the matter.

 

III. Conclusion

A. Motion to Compel Further Responses to Production

Defendant Showtime Networks, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Provide Further Responses to Requests for Production and Compel Compliance with Production of Documents is MOOT in part, GRANTED in part, and DENIED in part as follows:

(1) MOOT as to an order compelling further responses from Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda in relation to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, and 13-40, specifically as relates to removing the “relevant” qualification in Plaintiff Rueda’s initial responses to this discovery;

(2) GRANTED as to an order compelling a further response from Plaintiff Garbriel Rueda in relation to RPDs, Set Seven No. 56;

(3) GRANTED as to an order compelling further responses from Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda that “set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item” in RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40;

(4) Otherwise DENIED as to an order compelling further responses or production in relation to RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 16-40; and

(5) GRANTED as to an order compelling Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda to comply with statements of compliance in relation RPDs, Set Eight, Nos. 1, 3-5, 7, 9, 10, and 13-15.

Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda is ordered to produce all responses and non-privileged responsive documents to Defendant Showtime Networks, Inc. within fourteen days of this ruling.

B. Request for Sanctions

Defendant Showtime Networks, Inc.’s Request for Monetary Sanctions in the Amount of $6,000 is GRANTED in part in the amount of $2,000 as against Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda and his counsel of record, Withers Bergman LLP, jointly and severally.

Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda and his counsel of record, Withers Bergman LLP, are ordered, jointly and severally, to remit sanctions of $2,000 to Defendant Showtime Networks, Inc. within thirty days of this ruling.
