SUBJECT:                 MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES AS PREVAILING PARTY

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants Sean Besser and Samantha Besser

RESP. PARTY:         Plaintiff Debbie Paperman 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

            Defendants Sean Besser and Samantha Besser’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED. Defendants are the prevailing party and are entitled to attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to the parties lease agreement. The Court has reduced the Defendants requested rate of $889/hr. to the reasonable rate of $475/hr. Thus, the Court orders Plaintiff Debbie Paperman to pay $37,810 (79.6 hrs @ $475/hr.) in attorney’s fees and costs to Defendants Sean Besser and Samantha Besser pursuant to CCP § 1717.  Defendants are to submit the proposed amended judgment.

 

REASONING

            “Except as attorney's fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties; but parties to actions or proceedings are entitled to their costs, as hereinafter provided.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1021.) “In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” (Civ. Code, § 1717.)

 

            Courts can use the Lodestar Method to calculate fee awards, and the Laffey Matrix to show the reasonable market rate for attorneys. (See Chacon v. Litke (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1234, 1259 [“The court determined the lodestar method was appropriate for calculating fees and it was correct in doing so”]; Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 703 [“the trial court did not abuse its discretion in its award of attorney fees [based on the Laffey Matrix] to defendants in this case”.) However, Courts do not have to “follow the Laffey Matrix nor to adopt the rate defense counsel opined was the “market rate” for services of this type,” and can determine “that the actual rate charged was the reasonable rate for the type of services rendered. (Syers Properties III, Inc, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 702.)

            “Under the lodestar method, attorney fees are calculated by first multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended on the litigation by a reasonable hourly rate of compensation.” (Ibid.) “The reasonable market value of the attorney's services is the measure of a reasonable hourly rate. [Citations.] This standard applies regardless of whether the attorneys claiming fees charge nothing for their services, charge at below-market or discounted rates, represent the client on a straight contingent fee basis, or are in-house counsel.” (Id., at p. 1260.)

 

            It is undisputed that Defendants Sean Besser and Samantha Besser (“Defendants”) are the prevailing party on a claim filed by Plaintiff Debbie Paperman (“Paperman”) arising out of the parties lease agreement. (See 2-26-24 Judgement of Dismissal; see also Motion, Ex. A “Lease Agreement”.)  Pursuant to the parties lease agreement, the “landlord shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees and costs” when the Landlord “finds it necessary to enforce the provisions of this agreement against the tenant.” (Lease Agreement, ¶ 9.) Paperman does not argue this language within the lease agreement does not entitle Defendants to attorney’s fees, and the language in the contract is broad, thus the Court finds this clause entitles Defendants to attorney’s fees as the prevailing party.

 

            Defendants Counsel, Brian Condon (“Condon”), declares he over 36 years of “experience in complex business litigation, real estate, landlord-tenant, torts, false claims, collections, and anti-fraud litigation,” and generally charges between “$375 and $475 per hour but can be as much as $650,” on complicated matters. (Condon Decl., ¶ 12.) Condon requests a reasonable rate of $889/hr. pursuant to the Lodestar Rate and Laffey Matrix due to Condon experience and the nature of the litigation. (Id., ¶ 14.) Condon requests a total of $70,764.40 based on 79.6 hours of work at $889.00 per hour. (Id., ¶¶ 11, 14.) Condon provides itemized bills detailing the exact nature of the work plus the hours associated with that work, and whether Condon or a paralegal conducted the work. (Id., ¶ 9; Ex. B.) The itemized bills show that Condon billed for 75.4 hours of work at hourly rates between $225/hr. and $425/hr. (Ibid.) Condon argues that reviewing the opposition and writing the reply will likely add 4.5 hours, creating a total of 79.6 [which should total 79.9 hours] but the Court will honor the requested amount of 79.6 hours. (See id., ¶ 10.)

 

            The Court finds the itemized hours and billing statements to be reasonable, and thus the total of 79.6 hours is a reasonable hourly figure. Paperman argues the fees are unreasonable in light of the Condon’s billed hourly rate provided in the motion, and the Court agrees. (See generally Oppo.) Courts have lowered the requested fee when the requested fee is more than double the actual hourly billed rate. (See El Escorial Owners' Ass'n. v. DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1337, 1365–1367, 65 Cal.Rptr.3d 524 [affirming the trial court's reduction of a fee request where, among other things, the trial court found the rates sought were twice the rates billed to the insurance company].)  The Court finds a reasonable rate to be the $475/hr. rate as declared by Condon to be a general hourly charge when not involved in complex anti-fraud litigation or appeals, and adjusts the reasonable rate as such. The merits of this case were not complex.  Nor was the case resolved on the merits of Plaintiffs’ claims.   Indeed, the Defendants prevailed because of Plaintiff’s failure to timely effect service of the summons and complaint on the defendant. (See Judgement of Dismissal filed 2-26-2024.)  The only complicating aspect of the case was the Plaintiff’s counsel’s machinations to obtain the clerks entry of default the default of defendant based on defective proofs of service of the summons and complaint, and thereafter obtain an improper default judgment after the entry of default had already been set aside. (See Minute orders of 10-26-2024, 1-19-2023 and 12/6/2021)  It took the efforts of three judges to unravel Plaintiff errors in obtaining the entry of default and ultimately a default judgment. Only after that judgment was vacated, were the defendants then able to pursue the relatively straight forward motion to quash  that ultimately led to defendants successful motion to quash service and ultimate dismissal because of Plaintiff’s failure to serve the summons and complaint within three years of the filing of the complaint. (CCP§583.210, 583.250.)  After considering the experience of defendants’ counsel, and the nature and extent of the work completed in this case, together with this Court’s knowledge of the reasonable market rates charged in the community for comparable work, the finds the reasonable hourly rate for the work performed by defendant’s attorney is $475 per hour.  The Court finds the adjusted loadstar attorney’s fee of $37,810 is reasonable (79.6 hrs @ $475/hr.).  Thus, the Court orders Paperman to pay $37,810 in attorney’s fees and costs to Defendants pursuant to CCP § 1717.

SUBJECT:                 DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority; and Marco Garcia

RESP. PARTY:         Plaintiff Aaron Celious

 

TENTATIVE RULING

            Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, and Marco Garcia’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED as to Government Claims Act Compliance, and as exhausting administrative remedies prior to filing the Complaint. Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, and Marco Garcia’s Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the argument that the Government Claim form was defective for lack of standing as to Plaintiff Aaron Celious.

 

            Defendant Marco Garcia’s Motion to Strike Punitive Damages is GRANTED

 

I.                Govt Claims Act (“GCA”) Compliance – SUSTAINED as to all causes iof action.

            Compliance with the [GCA] claims statutes is mandatory; and failure to file a claim is fatal to the cause of action,” (City of San Jose v. Superior Court (1974) 12 Cal.3d 447, 454.)

            “A claim relating to any other cause of action [other than for death or for injury to person, personal property or growing crops] shall be presented as provided in Article 2 (commencing with Section 915) not later than one year after the accrual of the cause of action.” (Gov. Code, § 911.2.)

            “In general, a claim accrues upon the occurrence of the last element essential to the cause of action, even if the plaintiff is unaware of the cause of action. Under the “delayed discovery rule,” however, the accrual date of a cause of action is delayed until the plaintiff is aware of his or her injury and its cause. The plaintiff is charged with this awareness as of the date he or she suspects or should suspect that the injury was caused by someone's wrongful act. The period of limitations, therefore, will begin to run when the plaintiff has a “suspicion of wrongdoing”; in other words, when he or she has notice of information of circumstances to put a reasonable person on inquiry.” (Brandon G. v. Gray (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 29, 35.)

            “[U]nder the delayed discovery rule, a cause of action accrues and the statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff has reason to suspect an injury and some wrongful cause, unless the plaintiff pleads and proves that a reasonable investigation at that time would not have revealed a factual basis for that particular cause of action. In that case, the statute of limitations for that cause of action will be tolled until such time as a reasonable investigation would have revealed its factual basis.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 803.) “The discovery rule does not encourage dilatory tactics because plaintiffs are charged with presumptive knowledge of an injury if they have information of circumstances to put [them] on inquiry or if they have the opportunity to obtain knowledge from sources open to [their] investigation.” (Id., at pp. 807–808.)

            “In order to adequately allege facts supporting a theory of delayed discovery, the plaintiff must plead that, despite diligent investigation of the circumstances of the injury, he or she could not have reasonably discovered facts supporting the cause of action within the applicable statute of limitations period.” (Id., at p. 809, emphasis added.)

            “[F]ailure to allege facts demonstrating or excusing compliance with the claim presentation requirement subjects a claim against a public entity to a demurrer for failure to state a cause of action.” (State of California v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1239.)

            Plaintiff Aaron Celious (“Celious”) fails to plead that a reasonable investigation would not have revealed a factual basis to support the causes of action in the complaint.

            The causes of action accrued in 2014 when Maroon was allegedly replaced with another subcontractor, meaning that Celious would have needed to file a Government Claim at some point in 2015. Celious alleges that the discovery rule tolls the accrual period to 8-22-22 upon the google search revealing the final element of the alleged breach. However, the discovery rule only tolls the cause of action until the Plaintiff “suspects or should suspect that the injury was caused by someone's wrongful act,” not until all elements of the cause of action are realized. (Brandon G., supra, 111 Cal.App.4th at p. 35.) Unless Celious pleads that a google search would not have put him on notice earlier than 8-22-22, the cause of action does not successfully plead the discovery rule. (See Fox, supra, 35 Cal.4th at pp. 803, 807–809.)

            Additionally, the FAC alleges if the Discovery Rule does not reach the necessary tolling, than Celious requests the Court to utilize equitable tolling. “Under California law, a plaintiff must meet three conditions to equitably toll a statute of limitations: “(1) defendant must have had timely notice of the claim; (2) defendant must not be prejudiced by being required to defend the otherwise barred claim; and (3) plaintiff's conduct must have been reasonable and in good faith.” (Fink v. Shedler (9th Cir. 1999) 192 F.3d 911, 916, as amended on denial of reh'g and reh'g en banc (Dec. 13, 1999).) Celious conclusively alleges all the elements of equitable tolling in the FAC, however, in the opposition Celious does not provide any authority that equitable tolling has been utilized when a cause of action technically accrued over eight years prior to filing, nor that equitable tolling can be used to toll a government claim filing. (See FAC, ¶¶ 40–43.) California Courts have rarely, if ever, utilized Equitable Tolling to toll a filing of a government administrative claim filing. (See Willis v. City of Carlsbad (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 1104, 1121 [“We conclude the doctrine of equitable tolling cannot be invoked to suspend section 911.2's six-month deadline for filing a prerequisite government claim.”]; Bjorndal v. Superior Court (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 1100, 1109 [“equitable tolling has never been applied to allow a plaintiff to extend the time for pursuing an administrative remedy by filing a lawsuit. Despite broad language used by courts in employing the doctrine, equitable tolling has been applied almost exclusively to extend statutory deadlines for judicial actions, rather than deadlines for commencing administrative proceedings.”].)  The Court declines to apply equitable tolling here.

 

II.             Demurrer to the entire claim since the government claim form was defective - OVERRULED

            “A claim shall be presented by the claimant or by a person acting on his or her behalf and shall show all of the following” (Gov. Code, § 910.)

            Metro argues that the Government Claim was not filed correctly since Celious does not have standing to sue for Maroon Society, Inc. (“Maroon”), as it is Maroon’s rights that are alleged to have been violated. (See Demurrer, p. 5.) The Court does not find this argument persuasive. Metro does not include any authority for the argument that a person cannot file a claim in place of another when the code specifically states “or by a person acting on his or behalf.” (See Gov. Code, § 910.) The Government Claim is attached to the FAC as Ex. 1. Attached to the Government Claim is the Claim for Damages, and in the first paragraph Celious states he bringing the claim “on behalf of Maroon Society, Inc.” (FAC, Ex. 1 at p. 26.) The claim for damages put Metro on notice that the Celious was filing the claim on behalf of Maroon. (See Stockett v. Association of Cal. Water Agencies Joint Powers Ins. Authority (2004) 34 Cal.4th 441, 446 [“the purpose of the claim is to give the government entity notice sufficient for it to investigate and evaluate the claim, not to eliminate meritorious actions.”].)

            Thus, Metro’s Demurrer to the Complaint arguing the Government Claim Form was Defective for Lack of Standing as to Celious is OVERRULED.

III.           Demurrer to Complaint - Celious has No Right of Action Against Metro other Than a Writ—SUSTAINED.

            Metro argues that since Celious brought this action as a violation of Gov. Code § 4100 [which is now under Pub. Contract Code, § 4107], or the Subletting and Subcontracting Fair Practices Act (the “Act”), Celious can only maintain a cause of action against the prime contractor, Co-Defendant Nustats, LLC (“Nustats”), and not against Metro as the awarding authority. Metro argues Celious’ only remedy against Metro is to file a writ of mandamus against Metro in order to exhaust his administrative remedies. (See Demurrer, pp. 6–7.)  

            “Under the Act, a general contractor submitting a bid on a public contract must list each subcontractor that will perform more than one-half of one percent of the total bid.” (E. F. Brady Co. v. M. H. Golden Co. (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 182, 188, citing Gov. Code § 4104, subd. (a).) “Once the general contractor's bid is accepted, however, the general contractor has a statutory duty to use the listed subcontractor, unless one of seven statutory enumerated circumstances exist.” (Ibid.)

            “The Act thus binds a contractor to its listed subcontractors, even though the parties have not yet entered into a contractual relationship . . . . If the named subcontractor is unlawfully removed or substituted for a reason not authorized by the statute, the “removal permits the subcontractor to maintain a cause of action against the prime contractor.” (Id., at p. 189, citing Interior Systems, Inc. v. Del E. Webb Corp. (1981) 121 Cal.App.3d 312, 315.)

            The Act, “confers the right on the listed subcontractor to perform the subcontract unless statutory grounds for a valid substitution exist. Moreover, that right may be enforced by an action for damages against the prime contractor to recover the benefit of the bargain in listed subcontractor would have realized had he not wrongfully been deprived of the subcontract.” (Southern California Acoustics Co. v. C. V. Holder, Inc. (1969) 71 Cal.2d 719, 727.) “[T]here is no statutory provision for the recovery of damages against a public entity for its consenting to a substitution of subcontractors in violation of” the Act. (Ibid.)

            “A party must exhaust its administrative remedies before a court will act . . . . This doctrine of exhaustion of administrative remedies may not be circumvented by bringing actions other than administrative mandamus . . .” (Interior Systems, Inc. v. Del E. Webb Corp. (1981) 121 Cal.App.3d 312, 319 [Subcontractor cannot sue public agency for damages based on improper substitution without first challenging the public agency's decision via a writ of mandamus].

            Celious argues that since “Metro held no quasi-judicial process and violated their ministerial duty to provide Plaintiff notice and hearing, a writ of mandamus would be improper,”   (Oppo., p. 9:12–15.) Celious pleads Metro failed to take the proper steps to replace or substitute a subcontractor “including the requisite notice to allow Maroon and Plaintiff a hearing.” (FAC, ¶¶ 97iii, 105, 112.) Celious pleads he filed a government tort claim on 1-16-23, and on 2-2-23 “the County Board of Supervisors mailed [Celious] a notice of rejection of all his claims.” (FAC, ¶¶ 23, 25.) However, Celious does not plead that upon receiving the rejection letter Celious filed a writ of mandamus to appeal the decision by the County Board of Supervisors as statutorily mandated. Celious must exhaust his administrative remedies prior to filing a claim with the Courts, so without allegation of Celious’s exhaustion of remedies the claims against Metro cannot stand.

            Thus, Metro’s Demurrer to the Complaint for lack of pleading Administrative Exhaustion via a writ of mandamus is GRANTED.

IV.           Garcia’s Motion to Strike – GRANTED.

            CC §3294(a) provides, “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.” (Cal Civ. Code § 3294, subdiv., (a).)

           

             “[E]ven though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of punitive damages.” (Monge v. Superior Ct., (1986)176 Cal. App. 3d 503, 510.)Furthermore, “[m]alice and oppression may be inferred from the circumstances of a defendant's conduct.” (Id., at p. 511.)

            Defendant Marco Garcia (“Garcia”) individually moves to strike Celious’s punitive damages claim on grounds that punitive damages claims are not assignable. Celious argues that he has standing to claim punitive damages “as a third-party beneficiary to the contract, as a party harmed by the fraud by omission by Defendant Garcia, and as an individual deprived of his constitutional right to equal protection and due process.” (Oppo., at p. 3:15–17.)

            The Court agrees with Garcia that claims providing for the recovery of punitive damages are not assignable. (See MTS, p. 2; see also Dagher v. Ford Motor Co. (2015) 238 Cal. App. 4th 905, 926 ["Any potential rights to recover damages for ... punitive damages are not assignable (because they are " " `founded upon wrongs of a purely personal nature' " ' ")]; Essex Ins. Co. v. Five Star Dye House, Inc. (2006) 38 Cal. 4th 1252, 1263 ["damages for emotional distress and punitive damages, are not assignable."].)  Plaintiff does not provide any authority to support his contention that an assigned claim for punitive damages can be sought where one subcontractor assigns their rights to another subcontractor in order to sue on their behalf.

            Garcia’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED.
