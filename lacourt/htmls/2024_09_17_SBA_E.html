[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS AGAINST PLAINTIFF NASTASSJA GREENE

 

Dept. E

8:30 a.m.

September 17, 2024

 

Having considered defendants’ motion and the associated exhibits, the court rules as follows: The motion for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Nastassja Greene is granted in part.  

 

I.                PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On October 1, 2019, Plaintiffs Nastassja Greene (“Greene”) and Ami Miller (“Miller”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Wyndham Worldwide Corporation, Wyndham Destinations, Inc., Ramada by Wyndham Hawthorne/LAX, Jimmy Kim (“Kim”), Clara Gaeta (“Gaeta”) (collectively “Defendants”), and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, alleging the following causes of action: (1) battery; (2) negligence; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (4) fraudulent concealment; (5) private nuisance; and (6) public nuisance.

 

On June 15, 2021, Defendant Travel & Leisure Co. (“TLC”), erroneously sued as Wyndham Destinations, Inc., formerly known as Wyndham Worldwide Corporation, filed an Answer to the Complaint.

 

On January 12, 2022, Defendants Kim and Gaeta filed a joint demurrer as well as a motion to strike. Also, on such date, Defendant 401 South Hoover Property, Inc. (“SHP”) dba Ramada by Wyndham Hawthorne/LAX (erroneously sued and served as Ramada by Wyndham Hawthorne/LAX) also filed a demurrer and motion to strike.

On February 3, 2022, Department 1 of this Court reassigned this action on the grounds that this case is not a personal injury action. (02/03/22 Minute Order.) Department 1 reassigned this action to the Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka in Department B at the Torrance Courthouse for all further proceedings. (02/03/22 Minute Order.)

 

On February 8, 2022, the hearings on the respective demurrers were taken off-calendar in Department 32 at the Spring Street Courthouse due to the case being deemed non-PI and transferred to Torrance Courthouse, Department B. (02/08/22 Minute Order.)

 

On June 17, 2022, Defendants Kim and Gaeta filed a joint demurrer and motion to strike. Also, on such date, Defendant SHP filed a demurrer to the Complaint as well as a motion to strike.

 

On December 21, 2022, after hearing oral argument, the Court sustained the demurrers to the Complaint with leave to amend and granted, in part, the motions to strike with leave to amend. (12/21/22 Minute Order.)

 

On February 6, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On April 14, 2023, Defendants TLC and SHP filed a joint demurrer and motion to strike as to the FAC. Also, on such date, Defendants Kim and Gaeta filed a joint demurrer and motion to strike as to the FAC.

 

On May 16, 2023, Defendant SHP filed numerous discovery motions pertaining to Plaintiffs.

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

 A misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)  A misuse of the discovery process also includes disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).) A court may impose issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, or monetary sanctions against a party engaging in misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030.) Where an issue sanction is imposed, “designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).) An issue sanction may also involve “any party engaging in misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).) An evidence sanction involves “an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (c).)

A terminating sanction may be imposed by the court by one of the following orders: (1) an order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process; (2) an order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) an order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; or (4) an order rending judgment by default against that party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(1)-(4).)

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)  “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Ibid.) “[C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  (Ibid.) Where discovery violations are “willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid.)  A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions. (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) Where discovery sanctions are requested against a party, there must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful. (Ibid.) 

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.” (Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702.) A trial court’s order to impose terminating sanctions will be reversed only if it “was arbitrary, capricious, or whimsical.” (Ibid.) Trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders. (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.) Terminating sanctions are warranted when a party’s lack of compliance with the discovery process has caused the opposing party prejudice. (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc., supra, 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 989.)

California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 2023.030 provides that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

Motion’s Merits

 

Procedural Defects as to Defendants’ Request for Evidence Sanctions

 

The Court will initially address Defendants’ request for evidence sanctions.

 

A motion for issue or evidentiary sanctions requires a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(7).) A separate statement is not required when: (1) no response has been provided to the request for discovery; or (2) a court has allowed the moving party to submit—in place of a separate statement—a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (b)(1)-(2).) Also, “[a] request for sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.” (Sole Energy Co. v. Hodges (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 199, 207.) “The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Id. at p. 208.)

 

Here, the Court finds that Defendants’ request for evidence sanctions is procedurally deficient. While it is undisputed that Plaintiff Greene has failed to provide any responses to discovery, and thus a separate statement is not required, Defendants do not articulate what specific evidence sanctions they seek against Plaintiff Greene. Defendants merely argue that they “[a]lternatively . . . request the Court grant an order precluding Plaintiff from introducing evidence at trial that should have been disclosed during discovery.” (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at p. 14:16-17.) However, Defendants have failed to articulate in their notice of motion or elsewhere the matters they seek to prohibit Plaintiff Greene from introducing into evidence. (CCP § 2023.030(c).) As such, the Court has no basis to ascertain what specific evidentiary sanctions are sought.

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ request for evidentiary sanctions. The Court will, however, assess Defendants’ request for terminating sanctions and monetary sanctions.

Appropriateness of Terminating Sanctions

 

Given that the instant motion is unopposed, it is undisputed that Plaintiff Greene has failed to comply with the Court’s September 28, 2023 order. Given that the motion is unopposed, the Court finds that Plaintiff Greene’s non-compliance is willful.

 

The Court, however, finds that terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Greene are not appropriate at this time. Although Plaintiff Greene has failed to comply with the Court’s September 28, 2023 order, the Court must take an incremental approach to discovery sanctions pursuant to Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc., supra, 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992. Plaintiff Greene has only failed to comply with one discovery order of this Court. While evidence sanctions might have been appropriate, the Court cannot grant such sanctions given the defects as to Defendants’ request for evidence sanctions.

 

As such, the Court DENIES Defendants’ request for terminating sanctions as to Plaintiff Greene.

            On June 17, 2024, Defendants filed a motion for summary judgment. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

            Trial is set for May 19, 2025.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties' pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Trial judges are required “to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) Once the defendant has met that burden, “the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Id.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.” (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Statement of Facts

 

ERMC Aviation provides various aviation support services, including ticketing, cabin and airport cleaning, ground handling, and cargo at airports across North America, including the Los Angeles Airport (LAX). (Comp., Ex. 2, Bitar Decl., ¶ 3.) ERMC enforces comprehensive policies to prevent misconduct and mandates all employees to undergo training on anti-discrimination, anti-harassment, anti-retaliation, and safety protocols. (UMF 1, 2.) Employees, including janitors like Ms. Portillo, are trained to use proper signage when cleaning, including in bathrooms. (UMF 1, 6.) ERMC also requires thorough background checks for new hires, which Ms. Portillo passed before she began her duties at LAX from September 17, 2020, to April 30, 2021. (UMF 3, 5.)

 

On April 24, 2021, Plaintiff walked into a restroom at LAX being cleaned by Ms. Portillo. Plaintiff alleged that after a verbal altercation between them, Ms. Portillo took a photograph of him. (UMF 7; Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo.) Plaintiff admitted there was no physical contact between them, and he kept his distance to avoid any accusations of inappropriate behavior. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo.) Shortly after the incident, Plaintiff saw Ms. Portillo showing her phone to another employee and complained to Delta management about the photograph. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo.) ERMC promptly suspended Ms. Portillo and began an investigation. Ms. Portillo and another Delta employee provided statements indicating that Ms. Portillo took the photograph after a confrontation with Plaintiff. (Comp., Ex. 2, Bitar Decl., Ex. J.). An investigation confirmed that the photo showed Plaintiff’s upper torso and face, not nudity. (Comp., Ex. 3, Abarca Decl., ¶ 3.) ERMC concluded that Ms. Portillo failed to follow procedures for posting signage and took a photo inside the restroom, leading to her termination on May 4, 2021. (UMF 6, 8, 12.)

 

Following the incident, Plaintiff filed a police report accusing Ms. Portillo of sexual assault. (Comp., Ex. 1(A), Complaint, ¶ 17.) Plaintiff sent several aggressive emails to ERMC management demanding updates and threatening them, which prompted ERMC to share the correspondence with Delta. (Comp., Ex. 2, Bitar Decl., ¶ 14.) Ultimately, Plaintiff’s employment with Delta was terminated on September 17, 2021, due to his inappropriate, threatening, and aggressive behavior. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 124:14-125:18.)

 

Analysis

 

            Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention, and Negligent Failure to Warn, Train or Educate Claims

 

Plaintiff’s first cause of action alleges that ERMC negligently hired, supervised, and retained Ms. Portillo, causing him damage, while the second cause of action claims that ERMC negligently failed to train its employees, including Ms. Portillo. (Comp., Ex. 1(A), Complaint, ¶¶ 21-38.)

 

Defendants argue that an employer is only liable for negligent hiring, supervision, or retention if the employer knew or should have known that an employee posed an undue risk of harm based on the nature of their work. This standard is not met here. Plaintiff’s claims rest on speculation that Ms. Portillo took a photograph showing his genitals, but Plaintiff testified he does not know what the photograph captured. Furthermore, a witness confirmed the photograph did not show any nudity, depicting only Plaintiff’s fully clothed upper torso and face. (UMF 10, 11; Comp., Ex. 3, Abarca Decl., ¶ 3.) ERMC demonstrated that it conducts thorough background checks and provides comprehensive training on workplace policies to all employees, including Ms. Portillo. (UMF 1, 3, 5, 6.) Plaintiff admitted he has no knowledge of ERMC’s hiring process, Ms. Portillo's employment details, her training, or any disciplinary actions taken against her. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 59:1-60:25.) Moreover, when Plaintiff complained about the incident, ERMC promptly conducted a thorough investigation and terminated Ms. Portillo’s employment. Plaintiff agreed that this was an appropriate action. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 71:18-72:19.) Given the lack of evidence showing that ERMC knew or should have known that Ms. Portillo posed a risk, Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot establish his claims of negligent hiring, supervision, retention, or failure to train.

 

An employer may owe a duty of care in hiring and supervising its employees so as to avoid exposing third persons to an unreasonable risk of harm: “An employer may be liable to a third person for the employer’s negligence in hiring or retaining an employee who is incompetent or unfit.” (Federico v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1207, 1210-1211.) The basic elements of a cause of action for negligence are: (1) the existence of a legal duty to use due care; (2) breach of that duty; and (3) the breach as a proximate cause of the plaintiff’s injury. (Ibid.; see also CACI 400.) Foreseeability of the harm is key. The employer is not liable merely because its employee is incompetent, vicious or careless. A duty of care to third persons arises only when a risk of harm to such persons by the employee was reasonably foreseeable; i.e., only “when the employer knows, or should know, facts which would warn a reasonable person that the employee presents an undue risk of harm to third persons in light of the particular work to be performed.” (Federico, supra, 59 Cal.App.4th at p. 1214; see also Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1055 [“cornerstone of a negligent hiring theory is the risk that the employee will act in a certain way and the employee does act in that way”].) 

 

The Court finds that Defendants show there are no triable issues of fact that ERMC knew or should have known that Ms. Portillo posed a risk of harm based on the nature of her work or any specific conduct. The allegations are speculative, with no concrete evidence showing that Ms. Portillo took a photograph depicting nudity or engaged in any conduct that would suggest an undue risk. Furthermore, ERMC has demonstrated that it conducts thorough background checks and provides comprehensive training to its employees. When the incident occurred, ERMC took prompt and appropriate action by investigating and terminating Ms. Portillo’s employment, and Plaintiff acknowledged that this response was appropriate. Thus, Defendants satisfy its burden and Plaintiff is unable to rebut this showing and point to evidence to support that ERMC breached its duty of care in hiring, supervising, retaining, or training Ms. Portillo, as required under the legal standard for negligent hiring, supervision, or retention.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention, and Negligent Failure to Warn, Train or Educate Claims.

 

            Sexual Harassment

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s third cause of action for sexual harassment under Section 51.9 fails because this statute applies only to specific business relationships outside the workplace, such as relationships with physicians, attorneys, or landlords, where a party cannot easily terminate the relationship. Plaintiff must prove there was a qualifying business relationship between himself and ERMC or Ms. Portillo. However, Plaintiff admitted he had no relationship or prior association with ERMC or Ms. Portillo before the alleged incident, eliminating his standing to pursue a claim under this section. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 59:1-17, 60:5-10.)

 

Even if Plaintiff had standing, Defendants state that Plaintiff’s claim still fails because there is no evidence of severe or pervasive conduct based on gender, as required by California Civil Code Section 51.9(a)(2). The statute mandates proof of unwelcome conduct of a sexual or hostile nature that is both severe and pervasive. Here, the evidence shows that Ms. Portillo was merely cleaning the restroom and had a brief verbal exchange with Plaintiff, which was not of a sexual nature or based on Plaintiff's gender. Plaintiff himself admitted that Ms. Portillo's actions, including taking a photograph, might have been innocent. (UMF 10, 11, 15.) Additionally, Plaintiff's claim is based on a single interaction with Ms. Portillo and his speculation that the photograph taken might have captured his private parts. However, Plaintiff acknowledged he does not know what the photograph shows, and there is no evidence that the photograph captured any nudity. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 66:11-17, 85:5-86:16.) The interaction does not meet the threshold of being sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment or create a hostile work environment. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.App.4th 1035, 1043 (2009).)

 

The Fair Employment and Housing Act prohibition on harassment states: “It is an unlawful employment practice. . . .[f]or an employer, labor organization, employment agency, apprenticeship training program or any training program leading to employment, any other person, because of race, religious creed, color, national origin, ancestry, physical disability, mental disability, medical condition, genetic information, marital status, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, age, sexual orientation, or military veteran status, to harass an employee, an applicant, an unpaid intern or volunteer, or person providing services pursuant to a contract.” (Govt. Code, § 12940(j)(1).) A hostile work environment is a recognized form of harassment. To establish a hostile work environment, harassment must be so severe or pervasive as to alter the conditions of the victim’s employment and create an abusive working environment based on the protected characteristic. (See Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1045, 1043.) Harassment must be of a repeated, routine, or generalized nature when the harassing conduct is not severe. (See Lyle v. Warner Bros. Television Productions (2006) 38 Cal.4th 264, 283.) Harassment that is occasional, isolated or sporadic is insufficient. (See id.)  

 

The Court finds that Defendants have demonstrated that Plaintiff’s third cause of action for sexual harassment under Section 51.9 fails because this statute applies only to specific business relationships outside the workplace, such as relationships with physicians, attorneys, or landlords, where a party cannot easily terminate the relationship. Plaintiff admits that there was no prior relationship or association with ERMC or Ms. Portillo, which eliminates his standing to pursue a claim under this section. Even if Plaintiff had standing, it appears that there is no evidence of severe or pervasive conduct based on gender. The incident in question involved a brief interaction in which Ms. Portillo was cleaning the restroom and engaged in a brief verbal exchange with Plaintiff that was not of a sexual nature or based on Plaintiff's gender. Plaintiff himself admitted that Ms. Portillo’s actions, including taking a photograph, might have been innocent, and he does not know what the photograph captured. There is no evidence that the photograph depicted any nudity or that the conduct was of a sexual or hostile nature. The Court also finds that the conduct described does not rise to the level of being sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment or create a hostile work environment, as required by the Fair Employment and Housing Act. The interaction was a single, isolated incident that does not meet the threshold of harassment necessary to support a claim. Plaintiff fails to provide an arguments in opposition.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication motion as to the sexual harassment claim.

 

            Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED) should be dismissed because it fails for the same reasons as his claim under Section 51.9 of the California Civil Code. Since the Section 51.9 claim is not viable, Plaintiff’s IIED claim, which is based on the same alleged facts, also lacks merit. To establish an IIED claim, Plaintiff must show that ERMC’s conduct was outrageous, intended to cause severe emotional distress, or acted with reckless disregard for causing such distress. However, the only conduct Plaintiff points to is the incident in which Ms. Portillo allegedly took a photograph of him in the restroom. Plaintiff admitted he had no knowledge of what the photograph showed and cannot prove that Ms. Portillo acted with ill intent. (UMF 10, 11; Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 61:1-65:22, 165:14-24.) Furthermore, Defendants assert that Plaintiff has not identified any outrageous conduct by any other employee at ERMC. Although he criticized ERMC manager Mr. Bitar for allegedly not responding to a detective in a timely manner, Plaintiff conceded that he has no knowledge of whether or when Mr. Bitar actually responded. (Comp., Ex. 1(B), Plaintiff’s Depo, 95:1-13, 101:17-21.) Defendants state that Plaintiff’s accusations against Mr. Bitar are undermined by Plaintiff’s own threatening and inappropriate behavior, including a statement wishing sexual assault on Mr. Bitar’s children. (Comp., Ex. 1(G).) Plaintiff showed no remorse for these comments, which he admitted were intended to cause distress. This conduct illustrates that Plaintiff himself engaged in behavior that is extreme and outrageous, rather than ERMC or its employees.

 

            To state a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, a plaintiff must allege “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant’s extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the severe emotional distress.” (Crouch v. Trinity Christian Center of Santa Ana, Inc. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 994, 1007.)

 

The Court finds that Defendants have established that Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED) should be dismissed because it relies on the same facts as the failed claim under Section 51.9 of the California Civil Code. Since Plaintiff's Section 51.9 claim is not viable, the IIED claim, which is grounded in the same alleged facts, also lacks merit. There is no evidence to show that Defendants’ conduct was extreme and outrageous, with the intention of causing severe emotional distress or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing such distress. The only conduct cited by Plaintiff is the alleged incident where Ms. Portillo took a photograph of him in the restroom. Plaintiff admitted that he has no knowledge of what the photograph shows and cannot prove that Ms. Portillo acted with any ill intent. Furthermore, there is no evidence of any other outrageous conduct by any ERMC employee. Plaintiff’s additional allegations against ERMC manager Mr. Bitar also do not support an IIED claim. Plaintiff criticized Mr. Bitar for allegedly not responding to a detective in a timely manner, but he admitted to having no knowledge of the details of Mr. Bitar's response.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication motion as to the IIED claim.

 

            Punitive Damages

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages should be dismissed because there is no evidence of malicious, fraudulent, or oppressive conduct by ERMC or its employees. Under California law, punitive damages are only warranted with clear and convincing evidence of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) Courts apply a stringent evidentiary standard to such claims, and in this case, Defendants state that Plaintiff lacks the necessary evidence to meet that standard. (American Airlines, Inc. v. Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 1017, 1046-54.) Plaintiff has admitted that he does not know what was captured in the photograph taken by Ms. Portillo, and the only evidence, as confirmed by Mr. Abarca, is that the photograph showed Plaintiff's face and covered torso, not nudity. (Comp., Ex. 3, Abarca Decl., ¶ 3.) This does not rise to the level of “despicable” conduct required to justify punitive damages. Additionally, Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot prove by clear and convincing evidence that any managing agent of ERMC acted with malice, oppression, or fraud. Under California law, a “managing agent” is defined as an employee who exercises substantial independent authority and judgment in corporate decision-making, influencing corporate policy. (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 566-67 (1999).) Ms. Portillo, a janitor, had no authority to affect corporate policy, draft or implement corporate policies, or supervise other employees. (UMF 16.) Similarly, Plaintiff’s allegations against Mr. Bitar, including his alleged untimely follow-up with law enforcement, do not amount to vile or despicable conduct, nor does Mr. Bitar have the authority to influence or determine corporate policy. (UMF 14, 17.)

 

Punitive damages are available in non-contract actions where a defendant is guilty of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is “despicable conduct with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294, subd. (c)(1).)  Malice is “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences. (Angie M. v. Superior Ct. (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 1217, 1228) Fraud is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

 

The Court finds that Defendants have demonstrated that Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages should be dismissed due to a lack of evidence of malicious, fraudulent, or oppressive conduct by ERMC or its employees, as required under California law. (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) Plaintiff has admitted that he does not know what was captured in the photograph allegedly taken by Ms. Portillo, and the only evidence available, as confirmed by Mr. Abarca, indicates that the photograph showed Plaintiff's face and covered torso, not nudity. This evidence does not meet the standard of “despicable” conduct required to justify punitive damages. Moreover, Defendants sufficiently show that Plaintiff cannot prove by clear and convincing evidence that any managing agent of ERMC acted with malice, oppression, or fraud. Under California law, a “managing agent” is defined as an employee with substantial independent authority and judgment in corporate decision-making that affects corporate policy. Ms. Portillo, a janitor, does not have such authority to affect or determine corporate policy, nor does she supervise other employees. Similarly, Plaintiff’s allegations against Mr. Bitar, including his alleged delay in responding to law enforcement, do not rise to the level of vile or despicable conduct required for punitive damages. Additionally, Mr. Bitar does not have the authority to influence or establish corporate policy. Given the lack of evidence of malice, oppression, or fraud, and the absence of any conduct by a managing agent of ERMC that meets the required legal standard, the Court concludes that Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages is not supported by the facts.

 

Therefore, Defendants’ request to dismiss Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages is granted.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary judgment.

RULING

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorney’s Fees is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 18, 2024, Plaintiffs Kenneth E. Follmar, III and Lauren C. Follmar filed a complaint against Defendants Selene Finance LP, U.S. Bancorp dba US Bank Trust National Association, not in its individual capacity, but solely as owner trustee for RCF 2 Acquisition Trust, and MTC Financial Inc. dba Trustee Corps, alleging (1) Violation Of Civil Code § 2923.5

(2) Violation Of Civil Code § 2923.7; (3) Declaratory Relief; (4) Violation Of Business & Professions Code § 17200.

 

Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiffs obtained a first lien mortgage loan secured by the property located at 44 Buckskin Lane, Rolling Hills Estates, CA 90274, through a Deed of Trust, recorded on May 5, 2016. Plaintiffs suffered from financial hardship. On or around July 12, 2022, Plaintiffs entered into a loan modification agreement with Bank of America. Thereafter, Bank of America transferred the servicer rights to Selene Finance (“Selene”). Plaintiffs requested a repayment plan with Selene, but Selene did not offer a plan that Plaintiffs could manage. MTC Financial Inc. recorded a notice of default. Prior to recording the NOD, Defendants did not contact Plaintiff to assess the borrower’s financial situation and explore options for the borrower to avoid foreclosure, as required by Civ. Code § 2923.5(b). Defendants did not provide a single point of contact as required by Civ. Code § 2923.7.

 

            On March 21, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ ex parte application for a temporary restraining order.

 

            On April 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion for attorney’s fees. On September 4, 2024, Defendants Selene and US Bancorp filed an opposition. On the same date, Defendant MTC Financial Inc. filed a joinder to the opposition. On September 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply.

 

            On April 24, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ application for preliminary injunction and dissolved the temporary restraining order the same date.

 

            There is a hearing for demurrer and case management conference set for October 1, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A court can award reasonable attorney’s fees and costs to a borrower who prevails in an action for injunctive relief to stop a foreclosure due to a material violation of certain statutory provisions, such as Sections 2923.55, 2923.6, 2923.7, 2924.9, 2924.10, 2924.11, or 2924.17. (Cal. Civ. Code § 2924.12(a)(1).) A borrower is considered to have prevailed if they have obtained injunctive relief or been awarded damages under this section. (Cal. Civ. Code §

2924.12(h).)

 

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the lodestar, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award.” (PLCM Grp. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal 4th 1084, 1095.) The reasonable hourly rate is the prevailing rate for similar work in the community. (Ibid.) “The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Ibid.)  

 

After the lodestar is determined, the court in its discretion may, but is not required to, apply a fee enhancement or multiplier.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiffs seek injunctive relief and attorney’s fees, alleging violations of California Civil Code §§ 2923.5 and 2923.7 and California Business & Professions Code § 17200. They filed a lawsuit requesting declaratory relief and an injunction to prevent the sale of their home at 44 Buckskin Lane, Rolling Hills Estates, CA. In support of their claims, Plaintiffs applied for a TRO to halt the sale, arguing that the Defendants violated the relevant Civil Code sections and Business & Professions Code § 17200. The Court granted the TRO on March 21, 2024, which temporarily stopped the sale pending further litigation. Plaintiffs argue that they are the prevailing party under Civil Code § 2924.12(h) because they successfully obtained injunctive relief.

 

In addition to the TRO, Plaintiffs assert that they are entitled to attorney’s fees and costs. They emphasize that under California law, courts have discretion to award fees when a borrower secures injunctive relief, including a TRO or preliminary injunction. To support their position, they reference cases such as Bustos v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 369, 382 [“[A]mong the relevant factors a trial court may consider in making this determination are the type of injunctive relief obtained by the borrower and whether the borrower forced compliance with the HBOR. [Citation.]”] and Hardie v. SELENE Mortgage LLC (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 714, 725 [“The ex parte nature of the proceedings, the relative merits of the TRO application, and a party’s ultimate ability to obtain statutory compliance through imposition of an injunction are relevant factors the court may consider in determining whether to award fees.”], which discuss the factors a court may consider when determining entitlement to fees, including the nature of the relief obtained and whether the borrower compelled compliance with the Homeowner Bill of Rights (HBOR). Plaintiffs argue that their TRO was valid, meritorious, and successful, which justifies an award of attorney’s fees. Alternatively, they propose that the Court could reserve ruling on the fee motion until it decides on the order to show cause. Overall, Plaintiffs contend that they are entitled to attorney’s fees as the prevailing party due to their successful acquisition of injunctive relief against the foreclosure of their property.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the requested attorney’s fees of $4,770.00 are reasonable and substantiated by the evidence. Attorney Safora Nowrouzi, who handled the case, has provided evidence demonstrating her familiarity with the prevailing rates in the Los Angeles County area and the number of hours expended in this matter. The fees are calculated based on the hours worked by both the attorney and paralegal, along with associated costs, and Plaintiffs claim they are justified given the complexity and scope of the legal actions undertaken to oppose the motion. (Nowrouzi Decl. ¶¶ 5-9.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that an award of attorney’s fees and costs to Plaintiffs is not warranted because the HBOR statute grants courts the discretion to award such fees only to a “prevailing borrower.” They cite California Civil Code § 2924.12(h) and case law, including Bustos v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 369, 374 and Hardie v. Nationstar Mortgage LLC (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 714, 724, which clarify that awarding attorney’s fees is discretionary and not mandatory. Defendants emphasize that in determining whether to award fees, the court should consider factors such as the type of injunctive relief obtained (TRO versus preliminary injunction), the merits of the application for relief, and whether the borrower compelled compliance with the HBOR through the injunctive relief obtained. Defendants argue that, in this case, Plaintiffs were only granted a TRO on an ex parte basis, which was temporary and dissolved once Defendants had an opportunity to present their opposition. Additionally, the Court denied the application for a preliminary injunction on the merits, making it clear at the hearing that Plaintiffs had failed to meet their burden of demonstrating a likelihood of prevailing at trial on their HBOR and non-HBOR claims. The Court specifically found that Plaintiffs did not establish that Defendants committed a material violation of the HBOR. Given that Plaintiffs only obtained limited and temporary relief and did not meet the required burden of proof, Defendants contend that an award of attorney’s fees and costs is not justified in this instance.

 

            Defendant MTC Financial, Inc. (“Trustee Corps”) argue that it is not subject to an award of attorney’s fees or costs under Civil Code § 2924l, as no objection was filed to its Declaration of Nonmonetary Status (DNMS). According to California law, when a foreclosure trustee files a DNMS and no party objects within 15 days, the trustee is not required to participate further in the action and is exempt from any monetary awards for damages, attorneys’ fees, or costs. In this case, Trustee Corps filed its DNMS, and since Plaintiffs did not file an objection within the required period, Trustee Corps is not liable for any fees or costs. Additionally, Trustee Corps argue that it is protected by the trustee privilege, which limits the trustee’s role to non-judicial foreclosure or reconveyance duties. They contend that California law grants immunity to foreclosure trustees from liability for actions taken within the scope of their duties unless done with malice or outside their defined role. Trustee Corps’ actions, including recording the Notice of Default and Notice of Sale, are privileged under Civil Code §§ 2924(d) and 47. Since Plaintiffs have not demonstrated that Trustee Corps acted with malice or outside its role, their claims against Trustee Corps are barred. Finally, Trustee Corps asserts that the claims against it fail under the “agent’s immunity rule,” which protects agents acting within their official capacity for a disclosed principal from liability. Since Trustee Corps acted as an agent for US Bank and Selene, it is immune from liability under this rule. Furthermore, Plaintiffs have not alleged that Trustee Corps acted for its own financial gain or in violation of its duties, which are necessary exceptions to the agent's immunity rule. Therefore, Trustee Corps states that it is shielded from liability and not subject to attorney’s fees or costs.

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that an award of attorney’s fees under California Civil Code § 2924.12(h) is warranted based on the holdings in Hardie and Bustos. Plaintiffs contend that Defendants’ reliance on a multifactor test, which includes the type of injunctive relief obtained, the merits of the application, and whether the borrower forced compliance with the HBOR, is misplaced. They point out that neither Hardie nor Bustos support the analysis Defendants propose. Instead, they claim that Bustos explicitly rejects the requirement that borrowers must force compliance with the HBOR to be awarded attorney’s fees, stating that such a requirement is a policy decision reserved for the Legislature, which the statute does not impose. Similarly, Hardie clarifies that the statute makes no distinction between different types of injunctive relief, affirming that attorney’s fees are authorized for obtaining a TRO, regardless of whether it compels compliance with the HBOR. Plaintiffs also argue that Defendants’ reference to the denial of a preliminary injunction is irrelevant to their designation as the prevailing party under section 2924.12(h). Citing Hardie, Plaintiffs maintain that the statute does not differentiate between temporary, preliminary, or permanent injunctive relief. They are seeking attorney’s fees based on obtaining a TRO, not on the outcome of the preliminary injunction, making the latter irrelevant to whether they qualify as the prevailing party.

 

Furthermore, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants’ claim that the requested attorney’s fees are excessive is entirely conclusory and unsupported by any evidence. Defendants provide no proof or basis for describing the fees or legal filings as “generic” or “cookie-cutter.” Plaintiffs note that their billing is reasonable, amounting to 9.5 attorney hours and 2.1 paralegal hours for tasks including the ex parte application, preliminary injunction hearing, and preparation of necessary documents, all charged at industry-standard rates.

 

Based on the arguments presented, the Court finds that Plaintiffs are not entitled to attorney’s fees under California Civil Code § 2924.12(h). While the statute allows for discretion in awarding attorney’s fees to a “prevailing borrower,” here, Plaintiffs only obtained a TRO on an ex parte basis, which was subsequently dissolved after Defendants had an opportunity to present their opposition on April 24, 2024. The Court also notes that the TRO did not provide a substantive determination of the underlying issues but merely maintained the status quo pending a hearing. Additionally, the application for a preliminary injunction was denied on the merits, indicating that Plaintiffs did not meet the burden of proof to show a likelihood of prevailing on their claims at trial.

 

Thus, the Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion for attorney’s fees.
