DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV00682    Hearing Date: June 28, 2024    Dept: 73

5/16/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

EMPLOYERS CHOICE ONLINE, INC. v. WRINKLE FREE IT INC. (23STCV00682) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  David Scott Kadin (Kaplan, Kenegos & Kadin) 

Counsel for Defendant: Michael Eyre (Law Offices of Michael Eyre)

 

pLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 3/27/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Court denies Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment without prejudice.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 12, 2023, Plaintiff Employers Choice Online, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendant Wrinkle Free It, Inc. (“Defendant”) alleging causes of action for:

 

            C/A 1: Gross Negligence

            C/A 2: Conversion

            C/A 3: Intentional Misrepresentation and Intentional Concealment

 

Plaintiff alleges the following. Plaintiff is engaged in the business of performing background screening services, drug and health screening services, drug and health screening services, screening software and technology services for employers wishing to provide a safe, comfortable work environment for their employees. In connection with Plaintiff's operation of its business, it utilized a business computer laptop "the Laptop", whose Hard Drive disk ("the Hard Drive") contained valuable information about Plaintiff's hundreds of clients, their contact information, and details about services which Plaintiff has provided to them over the years and were currently providing to them. Plaintiff began contracting with Defendant in or about August 2021, to provide to Plaintiff information technology services. In or about October 2021, the Hard Drive became damaged and Plaintiff was unable to 14 access the information in it and contacted Defendant about recovering the data. On or about May 20, 2022, Defendant notified Plaintiff that the Hard Drive was disposed of. Plaintiff never authorized the destruction of the Hard Drive and Plaintiff never received any such notice that the Hard Drive would be destroyed. Based on Plaintiff's income and expenses, and profit and loss from the preceding and following Defendant's mishandling, loss or destruction of the Hard Drive, Plaintiff has sustained damages from loss of business profits and to replace the missing laptop.

 

On April 20, 2023, default was entered against Defendant.  

 

On April 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an OSC re: Default Judgment. Plaintiff seeks judgment against Defendants in the total amount of $195,547.58, consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $195,028.23 and $519.35 in costs. 

 

II.ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.     Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.     Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                      Yes 

2.     Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                    Yes 

3.     Declaration of non-military status for each defendant                     Yes 

4.     Summary of the case                                                                          Yes 

5.     585(d) declarations/admissible evidence in support                        Yes 

6.     Exhibits (as necessary)                                                                       Yes 

7.     Interest computation (as necessary)                                                 Yes 

8.     Cost memorandum                                                                            Yes

9.     Request for attorney fees (Local Rule 3.214)                                     Yes

CARTER v. NEIGHBORHOOD HOUSING SERVICES OF LOS ANGELES (23STCV16156)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Orlando Cabanday (Cabanday Law Group)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Nicole Sheth (Kaufman Borgeest & Ryan LLP)

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES – EMPLOYMENT LAW, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 05/06/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Form Interrogatories – Employment Law, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $1,590. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff Moieshai Carter (“Plaintiff”) filed this employment action against Defendant Neighborhood Housing Services of Los Angeles County, a California Corporation, doing business as NHS LA County (“NHS” or “Defendant”).

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

 

C/A 1: Disability Discrimination

C/A 2: Racial Discrimination

C/A 3: Sexual Discrimination

C/A 4: Retaliation

C/A 5: Wrongful Termination in violation of Public Policy

C/A 6: Hostile Work Environment – Harassment

C/A 7: Failure to Provide Employment File

C/A 8: Unpaid Wages

C/A 9: Failure to Pay Overtime

C/A 10: Failure to Provide Rest Periods

C/A 11: Failure to Provide Off-Duty Meal Period

C/A 12: Failure to Furnish Accurate Wage Statements

C/A 13: Waiting Time Penalties

 

The Complaint alleges that in October 2012, NHS hired Plaintiff as a lending specialist. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff worked under the management of its Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, JP Veen. (Ibid.) Plaintiff continuously worked for NHS until November 2022 when she was wrongfully terminated while on disability. (Compl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff alleges multiple violations of California’s Labor Code and FEHA by NHS.

 

On May 6, 2024, Defendant NHS filed the instant Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Form Interrogatories – Employment, Set One, and request for sanctions. No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

 

Here, Defendant’s counsel declares that a meet and confer letter was sent to Plaintiff’s counsel on April 29, 2023 explaining why the discovery responses were deficient and requesting supplemental responses. (Whang Decl., ¶ 7.) Defendant did not receive a response. (Id., ¶ 8.) The Court finds that counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form Interrogatories – Employment, Set One.

 

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) 

 

It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

 

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

Furthermore, to the extent there is any doubt in whether these records should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff responded to every Form Interrogatory – Employment request, nos. 200.1, 200.2, 200.3, 200.4, 200.5, 200.6, 201.1, 202.1, 202.2, 203.1, 204.1, 204.2, 204.3, 204.4, 204.5, 204.6, 204.7, 205.1, 207.2, 208.1, 209.1, 210.1, 210.2, 210.3, 210.4, 210.5, 210.6, 212.1, 212.2, 212.3, 212.4, 212.5, 212.6, 212.7, 213.1, 213.2, 215.1, 215.2, with the same objection:

 

“Plaintiff objects to this request to the extent that it seeks confidential, trade secret, proprietary, financial, and/or commercially sensitive information that is privileged and/or confidential and protected from disclosure by applicable law. Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground that the information sought is in the possession of, known to, or otherwise equally available to the propounding party. Plaintiff objects to this request on the grounds that it seeks information that is neither relevant to the subject matter of this action nor reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Plaintiff objects to this Form Interrogatories to the extent that it seeks disclosure of copies of and/or information contained within Plaintiff's federal and/or state tax returns. Plaintiff objects to this request to the extent it seeks information protected from disclosure by the attorney work-product rule. Plaintiff objects on the grounds that the Interrogatory is vague and ambiguous.”

 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s boilerplate objections are too general and unmeritorious because they do not state how or why Plaintiff believes Defendant’s Interrogatories are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence or in what way they are confidential, vague or ambiguous. The Court agrees, and notes that Plaintiff has failed to oppose the instant motion. Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form Interrogatories – General, Set One. 

 

C.     Sanctions

 

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.) 

 

Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, subd. (a). ) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, subd. (d).) Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 provides that a court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery even if no opposition was filed.  

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to make good faith efforts to respond to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories - Employment. Defendant requests $2,650 in sanctions for counsel’s work on this motion, including 5 hours spent on the instant motion and an anticipated 5 additional hours to review the opposition, prepare a reply and appear at the hearing. The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced total amount of $1,590 (6 hours of attorney time at the billing rate of $265 an hour.)

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further response to Form Interrogatories – Employment Law, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $1,590. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order.

ANIMAL PROTECTION AND RESCUE LEAGUE, INC., et al. v. GINOR, et al. (20STCV34229)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Bryan Pease (Pease Law, APC); David Tenenbaum (Law Offices of G. David Tenenbaum); John Maher (Law Offices of John Maher)

 

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Michael Tenenbaum (The Office of Michael Tenenbaum); Thomas Rickeman (Brown Neri Smith & Khan LLP)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ Motion to COMPEL (filed 03/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel is CONTINUED to July 16, 2024.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 4, 2020, Plaintiffs Animal Protection and Rescue League, Inc. (“APRL”), and Dina Kourda (“Kourda”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint.  The operative pleading is the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) which alleges causes of action for (1) Consumer Legal Remedies Act, (2) unfair business practices, and (3) declaratory relief against Defendants Michael Ginor, Izzy Yanay, HVFG LLC, D’Artagnan, Inc., D’Artagnan, LLC, Ariane Daguin, Andy Wertheim, Bella Bella Gourmet Foods LLC, La Belle Farm, Inc., Herman Lee, French Laundry Partners, L.P., Thomas Keller, Michael’s Gourmet Pantry, Inc.,  MGP Specialty Foods, J. Michael Stamm, and Cathy Stamm, Tyler Charman, Brian Redzikowski, Matthew Spencer, Kettner House LP, SDCM Inc., and Petrossian, Inc. 

 

Plaintiffs filed the instant lawsuit to enjoin the sale of foie gras, i.e., fattened duck liver, which is obtained from forced fed duck and geese, and which violates Health & Safety Code section 25982.  Plaintiffs also seek to enjoin false and misleading statements made by Defendants regarding the foie gras.  

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff APRL filed the instant Motion to Compel, arguing:

·       APRL moves for an order compelling production of documents and further deposition of HVFG, LLC vice president Marcus Henley regarding those document, and for HVFG, LLC to be required to pay the cost of the continued deposition

·       Discovery is open as to this deposition by agreement of the parties

o   As stated in the February 13, 2024 Declaration of Michael Tenenbaum, paragraph 8: “the parties agreed that these party depositions — as well as depositions of identified representatives of Defendants — would proceed after the cut-off.” (Declaration of Attorney Bryan Pease (“Pease Decl.”), ¶6, Ex. 5.)

o   discovery is still open as to this particular deposition, which was noticed prior to the discovery cutoff and continued by agreement of the parties.

·       The motion is timely

·       Defendants did not serve valid objections to the document request

o   Defendants served an objection to the original deposition notice, but not to the second amended deposition notice.

·       There is good cause to require production of documents

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that:

·       APRL Failed to Meet-and-Confer with Defendants

·       APRL’s Motion is Time-Barred

o   APRL’s Motion was Filed more than 60 Days After the Completion of the Record of Mr. Henley’s Deposition

o   APRL’s Motion was Filed After the Discovery Motion Cutoff

o   APRL Fails to Show Good Cause for the Production of Documents

o   HVFG’s Objections Are Meritorious

o   APRL Should Be Ordered to Pay Sanctions

In reply, APRL maintains that the Court should grant the instant motion.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.230, fails to appear for examination, to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, without having served a valid objection under Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.410, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450(a).)

 

B.     Inadequate Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain 'an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016 (internal quotations and citations omitted) (emphasis added).) More specifically, “the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.” (Townsend v. Superior Ct. (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.) This rule is designed “to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order....” (McElhaney v. Cessna Aircraft Co. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 285, 289.) “This, in turn, will lessen the burden on the court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by litigants through promotion of informal, extrajudicial resolution of discovery disputes.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.) “A determination of whether an attempt at informal resolution is adequate . . . involves the exercise of discretion.” (Stewart, supra, (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th at 1016.)

 

On March 18, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel sent Defendants’ counsel a letter setting forth Plaintiff’s position as stated at the deposition and asking if Defendants would voluntarily produce the documents and Mr. Henley for his continued deposition. (Pease Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states he did not receive a response. (Pease Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs filed the instant motion the next day on March 19, 2024.

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiffs did not adequately meet and confer. First Plaintiff APRL made the hearing reservation for this motion on February 22, 2024, but waited the day before the motion was filed to attempt to meet and confer. Defendants also contend that counsel for APRL was informed via an automated “Out of Office” reply, that one of Defendants’ two primary counsel in this matter was out of the office traveling the day that APRL sent its email and Defendants were therefore unable to respond right away. (Tenenbaum Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Here, the Court agrees that Plaintiffs’ meet and confer effort was inadequate. Sending a letter the day before Plaintiffs filed the motion is an insufficient attempt by the parties to resolve the matter informally and without court intervention. The Court does not find this was a serious attempt to resolve the matter informally.  (See Stewart 87 Cal.App.4th at 1016.) It would lessen the burden on the Court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by the parties and the Court should the parties here reach informal resolution to all or part of the discovery in dispute. (See Townsend 61 Cal.App.4th at 1435.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel is CONTINUED and the parties are ordered to meet and confer further.  If the parties are able to fully resolve the discovery dispute, the Court will take the Motion off calendar.  If the parties are unable to resolve part or all of the dispute, the Court will hear the dispute remaining after the parties adequately meet and confer. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel is CONTINUED to July 16, 2024.
