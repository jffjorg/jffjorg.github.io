DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 22AHCV00842    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: X

DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that the complaint is barred by the statute of limitations.

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint asserts a sole cause of action against Defendant Smith and her law office for legal malpractice in connection with Defendant Smith’s representation of Plaintiff Vartanyan in two cases: as a petitioner in a family law divorce case (LASC Case No. 17VEFL00447), and as a plaintiff in a legal malpractice case LASC Case No. 20STCV47588).  Defendant Smith moves for summary judgment arguing the legal malpractice Complaint in this matter is time-barred, having been filed past the limit of one-year for malpractice.

 

Tentative Ruling

 

Defendant Smith’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant’s motion requests judicial notice of the following:

 

1.     The Complaint filed in this action (Smith Decl., Exh. 1).

2.     The Answer to the Complaint in this action (Smith Decl., Exh 2).

3.     The complaint in the action entitled Vartanyan v. Weiss, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 20STCV47588 (Smith Decl., Exh. 3).

4.     The substitution of attorney filed in Vartanyan v. Sadeghi, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 17VEFL00447 on October 12, 2021 (Smith Decl., Exh. 6).

 

Defendants request for judicial notice as to items 1 to 4 is GRANTED.  (Evid. Code, § 452(d).) However, while the Court may take judicial notice of the legal effect of these documents, the Court declines to take judicial notice of the truth of matters stated therein.  (Id., at § 452, subds. (g), (h).)

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendant’s objection no. 1 to the Vartanyan Declaration in opposition (10/25/24) for lack of oath under penalty of perjury is SUSTAINED.  The rest of the objections are OVERRULED.

 

Relevant Facts

 

Plaintiff Vartanyan’s instant legal malpractice Complaint was filed on October 14, 2022.  In it, Plaintiff Vartanyan alleges that Defendant Smith was negligent in handling two actions: a family law case (17VEFL00447) and a civil case (20STCV47588).

 

In the family law case (17VEFL00447), Plaintiff Vartanyan alleges that Defendant Smith failed to exercise reasonable care and skill in performing legal tasks for Plaintiff Vartanyan when Defendant Smith was her attorney, between November 2020 to October 8, 2021.  In the civil case (20STCV47588), Plaintiff Vartanyan alleges that Defendants failed to include all parties in the civil complaint.

 

On September 20, 2021, Defendant Smith advised Plaintiff Vartanyan that she would no longer represent Plaintiff Vartanyan with respect to either the family or civil matters.  (SSUMF 7.)  The letter included the following language:

 

Anticipating that you will ask either to appeal the Court’s ruling or file a motion for reconsideration [with respect to the Subject Matter], I recommend against either as the likelihood of a successful appeal is practically nil and there are no grounds for a motion for reconsideration.  If you want to explore those options, it will need to be with other counsel.  This office will not file an appeal nor file a motion for reconsideration.

[…]

If you decide you want to go forward, you will need to retain new counsel (or you may represent yourself).  This office will not represent you at the November 22, 2021 hearing nor will this office undertake any further action on your behalf (such as preparation of the Income and Expense declaration).

 

You must let me know of your decision no later than October 1.  If I have not heard from you by that date, I will proceed with a motion to be relieved as counsel which, if granted, you will be representing yourself.

[…]

If you will not authorize me to dismiss the [malpractice] case, you will need to sign a substitution as I will no longer represent you in that case.  If you will not sign a substitution, then I will file a request with the court for permission to withdraw.  You may also retain new counsel who can substitute into the case.  But you must let me know your decision by October 1 as there are deadlines coming up and I will not spend more time on the case.

[…]

This office can no longer represent you.

 

(SSUMF 7; Smith Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. 4.)  On October 8, 2021, Plaintiff Vartanyan signed a substitution of attorney, removing Defendant Smith as her attorney of record in the family law case (17VEFL00447).  The substitution of attorney was filed on October 12, 2021.  (SSUMF 9.)

 

Legal Analysis

 

Defendant Smith’s motion hinges on two theories.  First, that Defendant Smith’s September 20, 2021 letter of withdrawal to Plaintiff Vartanyan unequivocally notified Vartanyan that Smith would no longer provide any services to Plaintiff Vartanyan in the divorce case or the civil complaint.  Therefore, the statute of limitations expired on September 20, 2022.  Second, and at the very least, that the instant legal malpractice complaint is time-barred and should have been filed two days earlier, by October 12, 2022, with a year after the date on which the substitution of attorney in the divorce case was filed with the court.

 

The Attorney-Client Relationship Ended In September 2021; Plaintiff Vartanyan’s Instant Legal Malpractice Complaint is Time-Barred

 

“The applicable statute of limitations for legal malpractice claims is section 340.6.”  (Truong v. Glasser (2009) 181 Cal.App.4th 102, 110.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 340.6 provides that a malpractice action must be commenced “within one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts constituting the wrongful act or omission....”  (§ 340.6, subd. (a).)  The running of the statute of limitations is tolled during the time that “[t]he attorney continues to represent the plaintiff regarding the specific subject matter in which the alleged wrongful act or omission occurred.”  (Id., at subd. (a)(2); see also Beal Bank, SSB v. Arter & Hadden, LLP (2007) 42 Cal.4th 503, 505 [“Under California law, the statute of limitations for attorney malpractice claims arising from a given matter is tolled for the duration of the attorney’s representation of the client in that matter”].)  The tolling is referred to as “the continuous representation exception.”  (Id., at p. 511; Truong v. Glasser, supra, 181 Cal.App.4th at p. 115.)

 

“[T]he failure to formally withdraw as attorney of record, standing alone, will not toll the statute of limitations under the rubric of continued representation.”  (Shapero v. Fliegel (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 842, 846.)  “[C]ontinuity of representation ultimately depends, not on the client’s subjective beliefs, but rather on evidence of an ongoing mutual relationship and activities in furtherance of that relationship.”  (Worthington v. Rusconi (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1488, 1498.) Where the attorney’s acts are only “tangentially related to the legal representation” of the client, the statute is not tolled.  (Von Tott v. Johnson (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 608, 613.)  Thus, the specific inquiry is not whether an attorney-client relationship still exists, but when the representation of the specific matter terminated.  (Ibid.; Foxborough v. Van Atta (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 217.)

 

Ordinarily, an attorney’s representation ends “ ‘ “when the client discharges the attorney or consents to a withdrawal, the court consents to the attorney’s withdrawal, or upon completion of the tasks for which the client retained the attorney.” ’ ”  (Nguyen v. Ford (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 1, 13, quoting GoTek Energy, Inc. v. Socal IP Law Grp., LLP (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 1240, 1246.)  “ ‘ “[I]n the event of an attorney’s unilateral withdrawal or abandonment of the client, the representation ends when the client actually has or reasonably should have no expectation that the attorney will provide further legal services.  [Citations.]  That may occur upon the attorney’s express notification to the client that the attorney will perform no further services.” ’ ”  (Nguyen, supra, p. 13, quoting GoTek, supra, 3 Cal.App.5th at p. 1247.)  Clerical and ministerial activity of counsel or the process of formally withdrawing from proceedings does not constitute continuing representation.  (GoTek, supra, 3 Cal.App.5th at p. 1245.)

 

“After a client has no reasonable expectation that the attorney will provide further legal services, however, the client is no longer hindered by a potential disruption of the attorney-client relationship and no longer relies on the attorney’s continuing representation, so the tolling should end.”  (Gonzalez v. Kalu (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 21, 31.)  “The client’s reasonably objective belief controls in all cases.”  (Flake v. Neumiller & Beardslee (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 223, 231.)

 

There is no dispute that Plaintiff Vartanyan received Defendant Smith’s September 20, 2021 letter.  The clear and unequivocal language in Smith’s letter shows Plaintiff Vartanyan could not have an objectively reasonable belief that Defendant Smith continued to represent her in both the family and civil cases.  (See SSUMF 7; Smith Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. 4. [“This office can no longer represent you”, and “You must let me know of your decision no later than October 1.  If I have not heard from you by that date, I will proceed with a motion to be relieved as counsel.”].)  As such, Defendant Smith has established that the attorney-client relationship ended no later than September 21, 2021.  Where the instant complaint was filed on October 14, 2022, more than a year later, it is time-barred.

 

Plaintiff’s Opposition Does Not Raise A Triable Issue

 

As Defendant Smith has established the absence of a triable issue of fact, the burden shifts to Plaintiff Vartanyan to establish that a triable issue of fact exists.  Plaintiff opposition does not meet this burden.

 

First, Plaintiff Vartanyan has proffered no new facts in the separate statement.  Indeed, the facts are not disputed, except for the date that Plaintiff Vartanyan received the letter.  The Court considers Plaintiff Vartanyan’s late-filed response to the Defendant Smith’s separate statement, in which she asserts that Defendant Smith’s letter was delivered on September 21, 2022, not September 20.  This fact does not create a triable issue where Plaintiff Vartanyan’s instant legal malpractice action was not filed for another few weeks, until October 14, 2022.

 

Second, the Court cannot consider Plaintiff Vartanyan’s declaration and any facts submitted therein, because it has not been signed under penalty of perjury.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2015.5.)

 

Third, even if the Court considers Plaintiff Vartanyan’s declaration and/or additional facts, Plaintiff Vartanyan has not pled delayed discovery in her Complaint.  The scope of a motion for summary judgment is limited to the allegations in the complaint.  (See Hutton v. Fidelity National Title Company (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 486, 493; Jacobs v. Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Co. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 438, 443-444 [evidence offered on unpleaded claim, theory or defense irrelevant because outside scope of pleadings].)  If plaintiff relies on the discovery rule, the complaint must specifically allege facts showing “(1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.”  (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 808.)

 

Accordingly, where Plaintiff Vartanyan fails to raise a triable issue of fact, the motion for summary judgment is granted.
