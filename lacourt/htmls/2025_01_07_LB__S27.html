DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV19312    Hearing Date: January 7, 2025    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Claudia and Josue Morales filed this action against Defendants, Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Memorial Health Services, Praveen R. Jonnala, M.D., and Ali Khoynezhad, M.D., PhD for negligence, hospital negligence, and loss of consortium.  Plaintiff added Ivana Radeva, M.D. as Doe 1 and California Anesthesia Associates Medical Group, Inc. as Doe 5.  The complaint arises out of Defendants’ care and treatment of Claudia Morales for cardiothoracic surgery. 

 

Claudia ultimately passed away, and Josue, along with Plaintiffs’ two children, filed 24LBCV19312, an action for wrongful death.  The Court consolidated the two actions.  On 8/09/24, Plaintiffs filed their amended consolidated complaint.  The consolidated complaint includes causes of action for negligence, hospital negligence, loss of consortium, corporate negligence, fraud by concealment, breach of fiduciary duty, and willful and wanton misconduct. 

 

2.     Motion to Bifurcate

a.     Parties’ Positions

Khoynezhad (referred to by Defendants as “Dr. K”) and LBMMC move to bifurcate the case for trial purposes.  Specifically, they seek an order that the jury will hear the medical negligence cause of action against Dr. K before it hears the corporate negligence cause of action against LBMMC.  They contend they will be prejudiced if the case is not bifurcated.  They contend this is so because Plaintiffs intend to prove their case for corporate negligence by submitting prejudicial evidence about Dr. K’s treatment of other patients, and by using his pejorative nickname, AK-47.  They contend none of this evidence is relevant to the medical negligence cause of action, and the corporate negligence cause of action will be moot if Dr. K is not found to have engaged in medical negligence.  They also contend hearing the medical negligence cause of action prior to the corporate negligence cause of action could potentially shorten the trial time.

 

Plaintiffs oppose the motion.  They argue Defendants have not met their moving burden to show they are likely to prevail on the medical negligence claim.  They note that Defendants have not indicated whether and when the intentional tort claims should be tried against them.  They contend the issues in the two causes of action sought to be bifurcated are necessarily intertwined, such that bifurcation is not proper.  Finally, they contend Defendants’ claims of prejudice are unsupported hyperbole.    

 

Defendants, in reply, contend they have sufficiently shown prejudice, they do not have to show they will prevail on their medical negligence claim in order to be entitled to bifurcation, and the intentional tort causes of action are not properly pled and do not preclude bifurcation. 

b.     Law Governing Bifurcation

Bifurcation is the procedure whereby the court may order separate trials of issues of parties joined in a single action.  The objective of bifurcation is to avoid wasting time and money on the trial of damages issues if the liability issue is resolved against the plaintiff.  Also, the procedure is not limited to separate trials of liability and damages; nor is it limited to dividing a case into only two parts.  A party seeking bifurcation should request such relief as soon as the need becomes apparent.  Delay may be a factor affecting the court’s exercise of discretion.  Generally, the court has the inherent power to regulate the order of trial and, therefore, can entertain a motion to bifurcate at any time, even during the trial.  However, where bifurcation is sought pursuant to CCP §598 (e.g., to obtain bifurcation of liability issue), the order must be made no later than 30 days before trial.  (I.e., the motion must be heard and order made more than 30 days before trial.)

 

Pursuant to CCP §1048(b), “The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of the state or of the United States.”

 

c.     Analysis

The motion is denied for two reasons.  First and foremost, as Plaintiffs correctly note in opposition to the motion, the operative consolidated complaint includes causes of action for fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, willful and wanton misconduct, and battery against the moving defendants.  Defendants, in reply, contend these causes of action do not change the analysis because they are improper.  The causes of action, however, have been challenged on demurrer and remain in the operative pleading.  Defendants’ moving papers do not explain when they contemplate having these causes of action tried.  The moving papers contemplate ONLY a trial on the medical negligence cause of action and then a subsequent, if necessary, trial on the corporate negligence cause of action. 

 

Second, the Court finds any prejudice to Defendants is outweighed by the efficiency of having the causes of action tried together.  The Court finds any potential prejudice can be managed by way of motions in limine and/or jury instructions. 

 

Moving Defendants are ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 22LBUD00487    Hearing Date: January 7, 2025    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, U.S. Bank NA, etc., filed this action against Defendant, Yuri I. Lee on 4/22/22.  The complaint is for unlawful detainer.  On 6/13/22, Defendant, using the name Yuri-Imuta: Lee filed a motion to quash service of the summons and complaint.  On 6/29/22, the Court, Honorable Michael P. Vicencia presiding, denied the motion.  On 8/03/22, Defendant filed a demurrer to the complaint.  On 9/07/22, the Court overruled the demurrer.  On 9/12/22, Defendant filed an answer to the complaint. 

 

On 11/01/22, Plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment on the complaint.  On 11/18/22, Defendant filed opposition to the motion.  On 11/22/22, the Court, Judge Vicencia presiding, granted the motion. 

 

The same day, Defendant filed an appeal.  The appeal was heard by the appellate division of the Superior Court, which denied the appeal on 11/17/23 and affirmed the original order granting summary judgment.  On 2/08/24, the appellate division issued a remittitur, indicating the judgment was now final and Plaintiff was to recover its costs on appeal.

 

2.     Prior Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Judgment

On 7/25/24, the Court heard Defendant’s motion to set aside and vacate the judgment pursuant to CCP §§473(b), 473.5, and 473(d).  The crux of Defendant’s motion was that she was erroneously sued and served in this action as Yuri I. Lee, when her name is Yuri Imuta.  The Court denied the motion, ruling, in pertinent part:

To the extent the motion is filed per CCP §473(b) or §473.5, it is not procedurally improper.  These sections can be used to attack a default or default judgment, but Plaintiff obtained judgment after hearing on a summary judgment motion, not via default or default judgment.  See Las Vegas Land & Development Co., LLC v. Wilkie Way, LLC (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1086, 1091, holding that this type of relief is not available after hearing on a contested judgment. 

 

Defendant also argues the judgment against her is void because she was never served with the summons and complaint.  Her contention is that the summons and complaint were served on Yuri I. Lee, but she is Yuri Imuta. 

 

The complaint in this action was filed against Yuri I. Lee.  The judgment, entered on 11/22/22, is against Yuri I. Lee aka Yuri-Imuta Lee.  Defendant fully adjudicated this issue during the course of the proceedings, including the appeal, and has made no showing that she lacked notice of the proceedings against her. 

 

3.     Renewed Motion to Vacate Judgment

On 8/06/24, Defendant filed a renewed motion to vacate the judgment entered against her.  On 12/09/24, she filed an addendum to the motion.  Defendant contends new or different facts exist, specifically, that Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. is now claiming to be the lender on the loan secured by the subject deed of trust.  Additionally, Defendant contends Homeward Opportunities Fund I Trust 2019-2 v. Taptelis (2023) H049791 constitutes new law that requires the prior motion to be granted.

 

Defendant’s addendum is not entirely clear, but appears to add a request that the case be reclassified as an unlimited jurisdiction civil case.

 

Plaintiff filed timely opposition to the motion.  Plaintiff argues there are no new or different facts, circumstances, or law, and there are no grounds for vacating the final judgment that has been entered.

 

Defendant filed timely reply papers.  She contends her newly discovered evidence supports renewal of her motion to vacate the judgment, which should be granted.

 

The motion is denied.  As the Court noted in connection with its prior ruling on Defendant’s motion to vacate the summary judgment entered against her, a motion to vacate is not procedurally proper after summary judgment is entered.  If a motion to vacate is not procedurally proper in the first instance, then a renewed motion to vacate is also not procedurally proper, regardless of whether there are new facts, circumstances, or law.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV01383    Hearing Date: January 7, 2025    Dept: S27

On 8/22/24, Plaintiffs filed a Notice of Settlement.  On 10/23/24, the Court held an OSC re: dismissal (settlement).  Because three of the plaintiffs are minors, the Court specially set a hearing on petitions to approve their settlements for 12/05/24, and also continued the OSC to 12/05/24. 

 

Petitioner filed papers in support of the petition on 12/03/24.  Because the papers were not filed with sufficient time for review, the Court continued the hearing to 1/07/24.  Petitioner gave notice of the continuance of the hearing. 

 

Plaintiffs and Defendant have agreed to settle the action for the total sum of $100,000.  If the settlement is approved, the adult plaintiff will receive $85,000, and each of the three minor plaintiffs will receive $5000.  Notably, this is a habitability case, and none of the minor children suffered physical damages as a result of the claims.  The Court finds the apportionment is appropriate, as the bulk of the claimed damages consists of back rent and relocation expenses. 

 

If the settlement is approved, each of the minors will pay $1250 in attorneys’ fees.  The remaining $3750 will be given to Petitioner, without bond, to use for the benefit of the children. 

The Court is concerned about Petitioner’s declarations in support of the petitions.  Petitioner declares that the total value of the minor children’s settlements does not exceed $5000.  Probate Code §3401(c), however, makes clear that the proceeds of the settlement can only be paid directly to a parent if the entire value of the child’s ESTATE, including the settlement payment, does not exceed $5000.  This would include any bank accounts or other assets held by the children.  The Court will hear from Petitioner on this issue at the time of the hearing.  If the Court is satisfied, the proposed orders will be signed.  If the Court is not satisfied, the monies will need to be placed in a blocked account, and the Court will order Petitioner to provide proposed orders to place the monies in blocked accounts.

 

Pursuant to CRC 7.952, Petitioner and the minor plaintiffs must appear at the hearing unless the Court finds good cause to excuse their appearance.  The minor children are two (RH L), four (RO L), and sixteen (LV) years old.  The Court excuses RH L and RO L from appearing, but will require LV to appear and testify before it grants the petitions.
