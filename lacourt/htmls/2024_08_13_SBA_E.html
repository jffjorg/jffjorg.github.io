Case Number: 23TRCV01689    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

FADRIKO DILLON,

 

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV01689

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

CENTINELA HOSPITAL MEDICAL CENTER, A BUSINESS ORGANIZATION, FORM UNKNOWN, et al.,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             August 13, 2024        

 

Moving Parties:          Defendants, Prime Healthcare Centinela, LLC., dba Centinela Hospital Medical Center; Prime Healthcare Services, LLC; and Veritas Health Services, Inc. (“Prime Defendants”)

Responding Party:      Plaintiff, Fadriko Dillon

HEARING: DEMURRER TO THE FOURTH AMENDED COMPLAINT

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

 

RULING

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Fourth Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the premises liability and general liability claims and SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the landlord liability claim. The demurrer is OVERRULED as MOOT as to the gross negligence and dereliction of duty claims.

 

BACKGROUND

On May 26, 2023, Plaintiff, Fadriko Dillon (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, Centinela Hospital Medical Center, Prime Healthcare Services, LLC, and DOES 1 through 50. On June 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”). On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Fourth Amended Complaint (“4AC”) against Defendants, Prime Healthcare Centinela, LLC dba, Centinela Hospital Medical Center, Veritas Health Services, Inc., Prime Healthcare Services, LLC, and DOES 1 through 50. The 4AC alleges causes of action for: (1) Premises Liability; (2) General Negligence; (3) Gross Negligence; (4) Landlord Liability; and (5) Dereliction of Duty/Negligent Undertaking.

 

This case involves an incident outside Centinela Hospital Medical Center on May 27, 2021, where Plaintiff Fadriko Dillon was shot and injured by an unidentified third party. Plaintiff claims that the security guards at the hospital should have either thoroughly checked all entrants into the emergency department or taken unspecified steps to prevent the attack.

 

Now, Defendants, Prime Healthcare Centinela, LLC dba, Centinela Hospital Medical Center, Veritas Health Services, Inc., Prime Healthcare Services, LLC (collectively, “Defendants”) file a Demurrer to the 4AC.

 

On July 9, 2024, Defendants filed this demurrer to the 4AC. On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On August 6, 2024, Defendants filed a reply brief.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012)

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants demur to the 4AC on the grounds that Defendants argue the first, second, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action against the Defendants.

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (emphasis added, CCP § 430.41(a).)

 

Defendant’s counsel’ Cameron D. Aronson declares that on June 10, 2024, counsel sent a written meet and confer letter to Plainitff’s counsel regarding the 4AC. (Aronson Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. A.) The Court finds this sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. 

 

B.    Gross Negligence and Dereliction of Duty

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff'’ inclusion of the gross negligence and dereliction of duty causes of action in the 4AC is improper because Plaintiff had previously agreed to strike these claims in response to the Defendants’ demurrers to the TAC. In Plaintiff’s opposition brief, Plaintiff stipulates to strike his third cause of action for Gross Negligence and fifth cause of action for Dereliction of Duty.

 

            Thus, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer as to the gross negligence and dereliction of duty claims as MOOT.

 

C.    Premises Liability

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for premises liability remains deficiently pleaded. The Court previously sustained Defendants’ demurrers to this claim in the TAC, finding that the allegations were insufficient to impose the alleged duty on Defendants to check every entrant into the emergency department for weapons. Defendant states that the 4AC fails to address this defect. Defendants emphasize that the existence of a duty is a legal question for the Court. California case law generally considers factors such as the foreseeability of danger on the premises and the burden of implementing precautionary measures when determining whether a business has a duty to protect patrons from third-party criminal conduct. (Sharon P. v. Arman, Ltd. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 1181, 1188.) In cases where the burden of providing a precautionary measure is significant, courts have required a “heightened” or “high degree” of foreseeability to impose such a duty. (See Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal.4th 678, 679.) In the TAC, Plaintiff attempted to satisfy the foreseeability requirement by citing two prior incidents of gun violence at the hospital, but these incidents were found insufficient as they were not similar to the current case and were temporally remote. The 4AC did not add any new allegations about these incidents or any other similar incidents at the hospital. Instead, Plaintiff included generic allegations about workplace violence in hospitals, statistics from the U.S. Department of Labor and the World Health Organization, and the presence of a mental health facility nearby. Defendants argue that these new allegations do not relate to criminal acts by third parties at the hospital and thus do not satisfy the foreseeability requirement needed to establish a duty. Defendants also highlight that imposing a duty to check every entrant into an emergency department, as suggested by Plaintiff, would be overly burdensome and is not recognized by any court in California or federal authority.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that premises liability claim is valid because it is grounded in Defendants’ possession and control of the premises, which imposes an affirmative duty to act. Citing Ortega v. Kmart (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, Plaintiff contends that a property owner, including a hospital, has a duty to exercise reasonable care in keeping the premises safe. Under Ortega, if a property owner fails to inspect the premises within a reasonable time, it can be inferred that a dangerous condition existed long enough to have been discovered by someone exercising reasonable care. Plaintiff states he plans to argue at trial that Defendants failed to adequately inspect the premises and that this failure contributed to the dangerous condition that led to the shooting incident. Although the specific details of the shooter’s presence on the premises are not yet known, Plaintiff has taken steps to investigate, including serving a preservation of evidence letter on Defendant. Plaintiff states he also intends to introduce evidence of other violent crimes reported on Defendant’s premises in recent years to establish a heightened foreseeability of crime. Plaintiff argues that while Defendant downplays the significance of prior shootings in 2012 and 2015, these incidents, combined with broader trends in increasing violence in the healthcare sector, demonstrate a need for heightened security measures. Plaintiff notes that California’s recent Senate Bill (SB) 553 mandates that all employers, particularly hospitals, implement workplace violence prevention plans by July 1, 2024. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s provision of security personnel at the time of the incident indicates an acknowledgment of the need for increased security. Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s argument that additional safety measures would be costly or burdensome is premature, as the FAC does not specify particular safety measures that should have been adopted. Plaintiff believes these details will be clarified through discovery, expert opinions, and witness testimony.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that the 4AC fails to address the key issue in the premises liability cause of action: the unrecognized duty to check all entrants into the premises. Plaintiff’s opposition relies heavily on Ortega, which Defendants state is not applicable to this case as it deals with physical inspections of premises for dangerous conditions, not the inspection of third parties for weapons. Defendants assert that Ortega does not support the idea that business owners, such as the Prime Defendants, have a duty to inspect people entering the premises for potential criminal behavior. Defendants further contend that Plaintiff’s argument, which references two prior shootings and general calls for increased reporting of violent incidents in the healthcare sector, does not establish the foreseeability required to impose such a duty. Defendants assert that the prior incidents cited are dissimilar to the current case and do not demonstrate a “heightened” or “high degree” of foreseeability of the danger that would justify imposing a new duty on Defendants. Additionally, Defendants refute Plaintiff’s claim that the mere presence of security personnel on the premises indicates an acknowledgment of the need for increased security measures. Citing Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 249, Defendants argue that the presence of security does not automatically imply a duty to protect patrons from third-party criminal acts.

 

While those who own, possess, or control property are not insurers of the safety of their patrons, they generally have a duty to exercise reasonable and ordinary care in keeping the premises reasonably safe and in managing the property to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp.(2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232¿Cal.App.4th 32, 37.) The existence and scope of duty are legal questions for the court. (Annocki, 232 Cal.App.4th. at 36.) “A store owner exercises ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the portions of the premises open to customers, and the care required is commensurate with the risks involved.” (Ibid.) If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition reasonably safe for their [customers’] use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.” (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.) The party asserting the cause of action has the burden to prove that the owner had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to correct it. (Ortega, 26 Cal.4th at 1203, 1206.)

 

The liability of landowners for injuries to people on their property is governed by general negligence principles. (Pineda v. Ennabe (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1407 (Pineda).) A cause of action for negligence requires (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care, (2) breach of that duty, (3) causation, and (4) damage to the plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.) The existence of a legal duty is a question for the court to determine, and “foreseeability is a ‘crucial factor’ in determining the existence and scope of a legal duty.” (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 237, citing Ann M., supra, 6 Cal.4th at pp. 674, 676 (Delgado).) However, a high degree of foreseeability is required in order to find that the scope of a landlord’s duty of care includes the hiring of security guards. . . . [T]he requisite degree of foreseeability rarely, if ever, can be proven in the absence of prior similar incidents of violent crime on the landowner’s premises. To hold otherwise would be to impose an unfair burden upon landlords and, in effect, would force landlords to become the insurers of public safety, contrary to well established policy in this state.” (Ann M., supra, 6 Cal.4th at p. 679, footnote omitted.) “Heightened foreseeability is satisfied by a showing of prior similar criminal incidents (or other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults in that location) and does not require a showing of prior nearly identical criminal incidents. (Delgado, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 245.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s 4AC contends that while he was at the premises, a patron, employee, agent, customer, or person otherwise present with permission of the owner of the premises, exited the ER and assaulted Plaintiff by grabbing him and inflicting a gunshot wound to Plaintiff’s leg. (4AC, p. 4.) Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants who owned the premises had security, but failed to either check the individual who committed the assault for a weapon or prevent the crime upon their premises. (4AC, p. 4.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants knew or should have known of this dangerous condition and that this type of assault was reasonably foreseeable based on crime in the area and prior similar incidents. (4AC, ¶ 4.) The 4AC also alleges that Defendants had a duty to protect invitees, including Plaintiff, from a reasonably foreseeable incident based on crime in the area and prior similar incidents. (4AC, p. 4.) Moreover, Plaintiff alleges:

 

Pursuant to CCP § 1714(a) Defendant’s want of ordinary care in management of their property resulted in injury to Plaintiff. Under CCP §1714, hospitals owe a minimum, a duty of care to provide a safe environment to invitees, patients and employees. Defendants had a duty to protect invitees including Plaintiff from this reasonably foreseeable type of harm and failed to do so, resulting in injury to Plaintiff. Defendants knew or should have known of the risk of violence on the Premises because such violence was reasonably foreseeable. Hospitals are places where emotions run high. There was prior gun violence at the Premises at least twice, on or about July 25, 2015 and October 12, 2012. Further the Premises is across the street from Didi Hirsch, a mental health facility and in a high crime area. In addition, 73% of all non-fatal workplace violent injuries occurred in hospitals in 2018 according to the US Bureau of Labor statistics.

 

(4AC p. 5.)

 

As noted above, a party alleging the need for security, or failure to provide ample security, needs to show prior similar criminal incidents that would lead to this heightened foreseeability. It is not enough for Plaintiff to merely allege, in a conclusory manner, that prior criminal incidents took place in the area and on Defendants’ premises. Plaintiff argues, as he did previously, in his opposition, that this case is to be instructed by Ortega v. Kmart (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200. In Ortega, the California Supreme Court stressed the requirement that an “owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is a key to establishing its liability.” (Ortega, 26 Cal.4th 1200.) It appears that Plaintiff attempts to argue that Defendants’ precautionary safety measure of providing security already should create an inference that Defendants had actual or constructive knowledge that the dangerous condition on their property existed prior to Plaintiff’s injury. Plaintiff also adds a statistic that “73% of all non-fatal workplace violent injuries occurred in hospitals in 2018 according to the US Bureau of Labor statistics. “ However, the Court is not persuaded by these arguments. Hospitals often have security guards present for a number of reasons, but the scope of the claimed duty is not outlined for the Court here. Plaintiff also fails to provide more detail in the 4AC as to the prior incidents however, such that the Court cannot determine whether they are both “similar” to the subject incident.

 

Thus, this Court SUSTAINS demurrer as to the premise liability cause of action, with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

 

D.    General Negligence

 

Next, Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot maintain a cause of action for general negligence as it fails to allege a cognizable duty. In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s general negligence cause of action fails to state a claim because it is based on the same alleged conduct as the premises liability cause of action and suffers from similar deficiencies. Specifically, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff has not established the existence of a cognizable duty that the Prime Defendants allegedly breached. The $AC remains vague and uncertain regarding what the actual breach of duty is. In the premises liability cause of action, Plaintiff claims the breach occurred when security failed to check the shooter or prevent the crime. (4AC p. 5.) However, in the general negligence cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that the breach occurred due to inadequate training and staffing of security personnel, failure to screen invitees for weapons, and failure to adequately surveil the premises using CCTV. Defendants argue that these allegations are vague and uncertain, particularly regarding whether the issue is with security training, staffing, or surveillance. Moreover, the AC admits that CCTV was operational during the incident, undermining the claim that surveillance was inadequate. Defendants also contend that the alleged duty to screen invitees for weapons is not recognized by California courts, and the addition of language about inadequate security training does not establish a new, cognizable duty.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant owed a duty of ordinary and reasonable care to Plaintiff as a licensee on the premises, as established by California Civil Code § 1714. Plaintiff claims to have established a prima facie case for general negligence by alleging that he encountered a hazardous condition on Defendant’s premises and was injured as a result. Plaintiff asserts that hospitals, including Defendant’s facility, have a duty to provide a reasonably safe environment for invitees, visitors, patients, and employees, grounded in CCP § 1714, contrary to Defendant’s assertion that this statute is irrelevant. Plaintiff clarifies that the argument is not about whether Defendant was required to provide security guards, as in the Ann M., but rather about whether Defendant exercised reasonable care in the security measures it did provide. Plaintiff points out that Defendant had already determined the need for some security personnel on the premises at the time of the incident, indicating an acknowledgment of the need for security. Plaintiff seeks to explore through discovery how Defendant made decisions about the number of security personnel, their training, and their response to violence. Additionally, Plaintiff suggests that the relationship between the shooter and Defendant could provide further theories of liability, which may be uncovered through ongoing discovery. Plaintiff also contends that the existence of duplicative causes of action is not grounds for striking them, as Defendant’s demurrer suggests.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s Opposition relies on the incorrect citation of CCP § 1714, which concerns foreign-country money judgments and is irrelevant to the premises liability and negligence claims based on the criminal acts of third parties. Even if Plaintiff intended to cite California Civil Code § 1714, it would not cure the deficiencies in the general negligence cause of action. Defendants assert that Plaintiff has failed to allege a breach of duty by the Defendants, specifically arguing that the proposed duties—such as screening invitees for weapons or actively surveilling CCTV monitors—are not recognized by law. Plaintiff also does not provide any legal authority to support these proposed duties, which are central to the negligence claim. The existence of a duty is a question of law for the Court, and without a recognized duty, the claim cannot stand. Additionally, Defendants assert that Plaintiff references an unspecified public record that allegedly shows Defendant’s security personnel lacked sufficient training, but this reference is not included in the operative complaint and cannot be considered. Defendants also refute Plaintiff’s argument that the presence of security personnel implies foreseeability of criminal activity, noting that this does not establish any admission of duty or liability. Defendants emphasize that Plaintiff has failed to provide any legal authority to support the alleged duties, and that the causes of action in the complaint are duplicative, all based on the same facts and unrecognized duties.

 

In the 4AC, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants, who own and operate Centinela Hospital Medical Center, negligently managed the premises, leading to his injuries when he was shot by an individual who exited the hospital’s emergency room. (4AC, p. 6.) Plaintiff contends that Defendants failed to implement adequate security measures, such as screening for weapons or providing sufficient surveillance, despite knowing or should have known about the heightened risk of violence on the premises due to prior incidents and the general environment of hospitals.  (Id.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants owed him a duty of care as an invitee, which they breached by not preventing the foreseeable harm. (Id.) Plaintiff references studies and statistics on workplace violence in healthcare settings, highlighting the known risks and the need for proper security measures. (Id.)

 

As with the premises liability, Plaintiff fails to sufficiently allege that Defendants had a heightened duty to protect Plaintiff from the incident that took place. The Court finds that while Plaintiff has articulated a general duty of care owed by Defendants as owners and operators of the hospital, the 4AC does not demonstrate that this duty extended to a specific obligation to prevent the criminal actions of a third party, such as the shooter in this case. The allegations rely heavily on the notion that hospitals, given their nature as high-risk environments, should anticipate and guard against all forms of violence. However, Plaintiff has not provided adequate factual support to establish that Defendants were or should have been aware of an imminent threat specific to Plaintiff’s situation that would trigger a heightened duty of care. The mere existence of past incidents or general risks associated with hospitals does not automatically impose an extraordinary duty on the Defendants without clear evidence of foreseeability related to the specific harm that occurred. Furthermore, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s reliance on prior violent incidents and general statistics about workplace violence in healthcare settings is insufficient to elevate the standard duty of care to one that includes proactive screening of all individuals entering the hospital or other extensive security measures. To impose such a heightened duty, the Plaintiff would need to allege more concrete connections between the specific incident and the Defendants’ knowledge or notice of similar, specific risks that would necessitate such extraordinary precautions.

 

Thus, the Court SUSTAINS demurrer as to the general negligence cause of action, with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

 

E.    Landlord Liability

 

Defendants argue that the 4AC pleading for this cause of action is identical to the TAC and has not been amended. Defendants contend that the claim fails as a threshold issue because Plaintiff was not a tenant, which is essential for a landlord liability claim. Moreover, Plaintiff has not established a breach of duty. The 4AC admits that security was present at the hospital during the incident, indicating that Defendants took reasonable precautions to prevent injuries. Additionally, Plaintiff has not provided sufficient facts to support the allegation that Defendants knew or should have known about the risk of gun violence against invitees. The two prior incidents cited by Plaintiff were temporally remote and unrelated to the current case. Defendants further argue that given the lack of prior similar incidents, the likelihood of injury to Plaintiff was low, and the burden proposed in the 4AC is high.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that a landlord has a duty to take reasonable measures to secure areas under their control against foreseeable criminal acts of third parties, as established in Castaneda v. Olsher (2007) 41 Cal.4th 1205, 1213. Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s argument, claiming that the proposed duty to enhance security is too burdensome, is premature and conclusory. Plaintiff alleges that the premises were unsafe and that additional security measures should have been implemented, but determining the extent of these measures requires expert analysis and further discovery. Plaintiff suggests that various security enhancements could have been employed, such as improved surveillance systems, better lighting, effective locks, and properly trained security personnel. The key question for the jury will be what security measures were reasonably available to Defendant at the time of the incident and the “burdensomeness, vagueness, and efficacy” of those proposed measures, as discussed in Wiener v. Southcoast Childcare Centers, Inc. (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1138, 1146-1147.

 

            In reply, Defendants argue that the Landlord Liability claim is fundamentally flawed because it does not establish the necessary landlord-tenant relationship between the parties. Both the 4AC and Plaintiff’s Opposition fail to justify how the Defendants could be liable under a landlord liability theory when Plaintiff is not a tenant. Plaintiff’s own Opposition acknowledges that a landlord-tenant relationship is required for such a claim but does not address how this requirement is met. Defendants also point out that Plaintiff’s Opposition does not address the arguments made in the demurrers, particularly the lack of a cognizable duty owed by the Prime Defendants to the Plaintiff. Instead, Plaintiff repeats the same arguments made in opposition to the TAC without any attempt to amend or substantiate the claims. Furthermore, the Opposition does not address the issue of foreseeability, which is critical in a landlord liability claim. Plaintiff fails to connect the prior instances of gun violence with the incident in question, despite relying on the Castaneda case.

 

The California Supreme Court has unequivocally stated that a duty is owed by the landlord to its tenant, arising out of their special relationship. (Castaneda v. Olsher (2007) 41 Cal.4th 1205, 1213.) This case is relied upon by Plaintiff in his opposition brief, however, the clear issue with this reliance is that Castaneda speaks to a landlord-tenant relationship. Here, Plaintiff was not a tenant of Defendants’ premises. Further, as relied upon by Defendants in their moving papers, “[t]he existence of the landlord’s duty to others to maintain the property in a reasonably safe condition is a question of law for the court.” (Johnson v. Prasad (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 74, 79.) “The question is whether in the management of his property, the possessor of land has acted as a reasonable person under all the circumstances. The likelihood of injury to plaintiff, the probable seriousness of such injury, the burden of reducing or avoiding the risk, the location of the land, and the possessor’s degree of control over the risk-creating condition are among the factors to be considered by the trier of fact in evaluating the reasonableness of a defendant’s conduct.” (Sprecher v. Adamson Companies (1981) 30 Cal.3d 358, 371.)

 

Thus, the Court SUSTAINS demurrer as to the landlord liability cause of action, without leave to amend.

            The Court considered the moving and opposition papers. 

 

RULING

Defendant’s Motion to Disqualify is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

            On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff Simyoung Lee filed a complaint against Defendants JRDTSP LLC dba Scott Robinson Chrysler Dodge, Jeep, FCA US LLC, and Does 1 through 10, alleging violations of the Song Beverly Act (Breach of Express and Implied Warranty) and Negligent Repair.

 

            On July 3, 2024, Defendant FCA US LLC filed a notice of motion to disqualify Plaintiff’s counsel and firm. On August 1, 2024, Plaintiff opposed. To date, no reply has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A judge’s authority to disqualify an attorney has its origins in the inherent power of every court in the furtherance of justice to control the conduct of ministerial officers and other persons in pending judicial proceedings.”¿(Neal v. Health Net, Inc.¿(2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 831, 840;¿see also¿Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a)(5) [“Every court shall have the power to . . . control in furtherance of justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto”].) “The power is frequently exercised on a showing that disqualification is required under professional standards governing avoidance of conflicts of interest or potential adverse use of confidential information.”¿(Responsible Citizens v. Superior Court¿(1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 1717, 1723-1724.)¿ 

¿ 

“Motions to disqualify counsel present competing policy considerations. On the one hand, a court must not hesitate to¿disqualify an attorney when it is satisfactorily established that he or she wrongfully acquired an unfair advantage that undermines the integrity of the judicial process and will have a¿continuing effect on the proceedings before the court. [Citations.] On the other hand, it must be kept in mind that disqualification usually imposes a substantial hardship on the disqualified attorney’s innocent client, who must bear the monetary and other costs of finding a replacement. A client deprived of the attorney of his [or her] choice suffers a particularly heavy penalty where ... his [or her] attorney is highly skilled in the relevant area of the law.”¿(Gregori v. Bank of America (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 291, 300.) “The paramount concern must be to preserve public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar. The important right to counsel of one’s choice must yield to ethical considerations that affect the fundamental principles of our judicial process.”¿(People ex rel. Dept. of Corporations v. SpeeDee Oil Change Systems, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1135, 1145.) Generally, the disqualification of an attorney vicariously disqualifies his or her firm.¿(William H. Raley Co. v. Superior Court (1983)¿149 Cal.App.3d 1042, 1048–1049.)¿ 

 

DISCUSSION

 

On a preliminary note, the Court declines to rule on Defendant’s requests for judicial notice as it failed to file a separate motion with a proposed order pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 9.252(a)(1).

 

Logan Hensley is an attorney that works at Plaintiff’s counsel’s firm. He previously worked at Defendant’s counsel’s firm. (Yasuzawa Decl. ¶¶ 3, 5.) Hensley worked for RoseWaldorf for 1.5 years, during which he allegedly defended FCA US in numerous cases under California’s lemon law and signed a Confidentiality and Non-Disclosure Agreement. (Yasuzawa Decl. ¶¶ 4, 5.) Hensley joined Plaintiff’s counsel’s firm, The Lemon Daddy (Drake Law), four business days after leaving RoseWaldorf. (Yasuzawa Decl. ¶¶ 7, 18.) Defendant argues that this swift transition, along with Hensley’s failure to obtain FCA US’s consent for his new representation, violates ethical rules and undermines the integrity of the legal profession. (Yasuzawa Decl. ¶¶ 18, 29.) Defendant also raises concerns about Hensley’s conduct after leaving RoseWaldorf, including his failure to update his State Bar profile and potential misuse of confidential information. (Yasuzawa Decl. ¶¶ 25, 26, 27.) Thus, Defendant seeks the disqualification of Hensley and his new firm from representing Plaintiff.

 

Under California Rules of Professional Conduct, an attorney cannot represent a new client whose interests are adverse to a former client in a substantially related matter without informed written consent. Defendant argues that Hensley’s representation of Plaintiff against FCA US violates this rule because he possesses confidential information from his prior representation of FCA US, which creates a conflict of interest. (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, rule 1.9(a), (b)). Defendant further asserts that Hensley breached his duties of confidentiality and loyalty to FCA US by representing Plaintiff in the same matter he was previously defending. Additionally, Defendant argues that Hensley acquired significant confidential information during his time representing FCA US, which is directly relevant to the current case, and the substantial relationship between his former and current representations justifies his disqualification. Defendant also contends that Hensley’s disqualification should be imputed to his new firm, The Lemon Daddy, because there is no evidence that the firm has implemented any screening procedures to prevent Hensley from participating in FCA US matters. California law generally requires that if one attorney in a firm is disqualified, the entire firm is vicariously disqualified, especially when the attorney possesses confidential information from the opposing side in the same lawsuit. (California Self-Insurers’ Security Fund v. Superior Court (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 1065, 1072). Defendant highlights that Lemon Daddy failed to provide written notice of any screening procedures, further demonstrating a disregard for ethical obligations. (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, rule 1.10.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the motion to disqualify should be denied for several reasons. First, RoseWaldorf has not met the high burden of proving that Hensley received any confidential information material to the case. Plaintiff points out that previous rulings, including the Khani v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 916, have rejected similar “playbook” arguments, and collateral estoppel should apply since a similar motion was already denied in a related case, Ryan Michael Nelson v. FCA US LLC (Case No. 23TRCV04158). Additionally, Plaintiff argues that the issue of disqualification is now moot because The Drake Law Firm has implemented comprehensive screening procedures to isolate Hensley from any cases involving FCA, including the current matter. Within the first six weeks of Hensley’s employment, the firm established measures that exceed the requirements of Rule 1.10(a)(2) of the California Rules of Professional Conduct. (Serrano Decl., Ex. A & B.) These steps ensure that Hensley has no access to case files, emails, or communications related to FCA, and he works remotely, approximately 60 miles away from the firm’s main office. (Hensley Decl. ¶ 6.) Additionally, Hensley is not involved in any fee-sharing related to FCA cases. (Hensley Decl. ¶ 7.) Plaintiff asserts that these robust procedures create an effective ethical barrier, preventing any conflict of interest and ensuring compliance with the rules. (Rule 1.10(a)(2); Kirk v. First American Title Ins. Co. (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 776, 813-814.) The firm has also ensured that all employees are aware of and adhere to the screening measures, which include prohibiting communication across the screen and blocking Hensley’s access to any FCA-related materials. (Serrano Decl. ¶ 9-11, Ex. A & B; National Grange of Order of Patrons of Husbandry v. Cal. Guild (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 706, 715.) Therefore, Plaintiff contends that there is no basis for disqualification, as the firm has fully satisfied the requirements of Rule 1.10(a)(2).

 

Plaintiff argues that the vicarious disqualification of The Drake Law Firm is not justified because all elements of California Rule of Professional Conduct 1.10(a)(2) have been met. First, Hensley’s participation in FCA matters was minimal, primarily involving non-substantial tasks such as handling appearances and filling out templated documents without direct communication with FCA or involvement in strategic decisions. (Hensley Decl. ¶ 8-14.) Given his limited role, Plaintiff contends that Hensley’s previous work does not qualify as substantial participation in any FCA-related matter. Second, The Drake Law Firm has implemented timely and robust screening procedures to ensure that Hensley is completely isolated from any cases involving FCA, which further justifies the firm's continued representation of the plaintiff. Additionally, Hensley has not shared any confidential information with his new firm, and no evidence has been presented to suggest otherwise. Third, Plaintiff asserts that written notice of the screening procedures was promptly given to FCA, satisfying the requirements of Rule 1.10(a)(2). The notice was provided within six weeks of Hensley’s employment, well within the timeframe considered prompt by legal standards. (Serrano Decl. Ex. A & B.)

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that disqualification would impose an undue hardship on the client, who has the right to choose their counsel, and that the motion appears to be motivated by personal animus and retaliation against Hensley for leaving RoseWaldorf. The motion lacks substantial evidence and relies on speculative claims, making it an inappropriate and abusive use of the disqualification process. Plaintiff concludes that, given the lack of substantial participation by Hensley in FCA matters, the proper and timely screening procedures, and the potential harm to the client, the motion to disqualify The Drake Law Firm should be denied.

 

In Kirk v. First American Title Ins. Co., the court addressed the requirements for an effective ethical screen when an attorney within a law firm is tainted with confidential information that could lead to a conflict of interest. Once the moving party in a motion for disqualification has established that an attorney is tainted with confidential information, there is a rebuttable presumption that this information has been shared with others in the firm. The burden then shifts to the law firm to prove that the attorney has been effectively screened from any involvement in the litigation. (Kirk, supra, at 809-810.) The court outlined that an effective screen must be timely implemented as soon as the conflict arises, and it must include preventive measures that guarantee confidential information will not be disclosed. (Id.) Simply asserting that information was not conveyed is insufficient; practical and formal screening measures are necessary. (Id.) “The typical elements of an ethical wall are: [1] physical, geographic, and departmental separation of attorneys; [2] prohibitions against and sanctions for discussing confidential matters; [3] established rules and procedures preventing access to confidential information and files; [4] procedures preventing a disqualified attorney from sharing in the profits from the representation; and [5] continuing education in professional responsibility.” (Id. at 811 [internal quotations removed].)

 

Here, the facts indicate that Hensley is not the attorney assigned to this case, that he was screened within six weeks of his employment, and that Plaintiff gave timely notice of the screening to Defendant. The screening measures implemented by The Drake Law Firm are sufficient and in compliance with the requirements of California Rule of Professional Conduct 1.10(a)(2). Plaintiff shows that Hensley has no access to any FCA-related case files. (Serrano Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. A & B.) The Drake Law Firm uses Clio, an electronic case management software, where Hensley has been blocked from accessing any FCA files. (Id.) Additionally, the firm has prohibited any communication across the screen, ensuring Hensley does not receive any emails or communications referencing FCA. (Serrano Decl., ¶ 10, Ex. B.) All staff, including lawyers and support personnel, have been instructed not to communicate with Hensley regarding any FCA matters, further preventing information sharing across the screen. (Serrano Decl., ¶ 11.) Moreover, Hensley works remotely, approximately 60 miles away from the firm’s main office, reducing the risk of unintentional disclosure due to physical separation. (Hensley Decl. ¶ 6.) Lastly, Hensley has no fee-sharing arrangement with the firm for FCA cases, ensuring no financial conflict. (Hensley Decl. ¶ 7.). These comprehensive measures constitute a compliant ethical wall sufficient to satisfy the requirements for timely screening and maintaining the integrity of the screening process. (See Rule 1.10(a)(2).) Plaintiff also indicates that written notice of the screening was provided, and Defendant does not set forth any arguments in reply.

 

Thus, the Court DENIES Defendant’s motion to disqualify.
