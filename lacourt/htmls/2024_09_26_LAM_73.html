DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV01204    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: 73

9/26/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

JAMES CHEUNG v. CHARLES CHEUNG, et al. (22STCV01204)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  David King (King Cheng Miller & Jin, LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Kousha Berokim (Berokim Law)

 

PLAINTIFF’S motion for AN ORDER (1) GRANTING ISSUE AND/OR EVIDENTIARY SANCTIONS AGAINST CHARLES CHEUNG AND PINNACLELGS, INC., AND (2) MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST CHARLES CHEUNG, PINNACLELGS, INC. AND THEIR ATTORNEY (filed on 08/21/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Granting Issue and/or Evidentiary Sanctions against Charles Cheung and Pinnaclelgs, Inc. and monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 12, 2022, Plaintiff James Cheung (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Charles Cheung, Wenjun Long and PinnacleLGS, Inc. Plaintiff’s Complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) Dissolution of Partnership; (2) Accounting; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (4) Fraud; and (5) Breach of Contract.  

 

The Complaint alleges that this case is “about the Dissolution of a Fifty/Fifty (50/50) Partnership and about an Accounting arising out of the Dissolution of that Partnership. The Partnership started off as a series of Joint Ventures that involved acquiring improved real properties that have the land value potential and that are to be demolished and developed into new multi-family projects. Plaintiff was to fund the down payment, and Defendant Charles Cheung was to fund some down payment and the monthly mortgages of the Purchase Money Loans.” (Compl., ¶ 7.)

 

On March 16, 2022, a Cross-Complaint was filed by Pinnacle Wilton LLC, Pinnacle Normandie LLC, Pinnacle LGS, Inc., Charles Cheung and Wejun Long against Cross-Defendants BB Wells Investments, Inc. and James Cheung. The Cross-Complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) Usury and (2) Quiet Title.

 

On August 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion for Sanctions against Defendants Charles Cheung and PinnacleLGS, Inc. (“Defendants”) seeking:

·       An order granting issue sanctions against Charles and PinnacleLGS, precluding them from presenting any evidence in opposition to the claims being asserted in Plaintiff’s Complaint or any evidence in support of their Cross-Complaint, and designating the following facts as established:

o    James and Charles were engaged in a joint venture.

o   That the loan made by James Cheung alleged in Cross-Complainants’ CrossComplaint was made in the course of and in furtherance of a partnership and joint venture between James Cheung and Charles Cheung, and is not usurious, and/or

o   Charles breached his fiduciary duties to James in connection with their joint venture.

·       In the alternative, an order granting evidentiary sanctions against Charles and PinnacleLGS, precluding them from (a) introducing at the time of trial any evidence in support of their affirmative defenses to Plaintiff’s Complaint; (b) introducing any evidence to rebut the claims being asserted against them in Plaintiff’s Complaint; or (c) introducing any evidence in support of their Cross-Complaint.

·       Awarding Plaintiff monetary sanctions against Charles, PinnacleLGS, and their attorney, Kousha Berokim, jointly and severally, in the amount of $6,300.00 for their misuse of the discovery process, as well as enforcing the prior sanctions award of $9,000.

·       Procedural background

o   On March 26, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s six discovery motions against Charles and PinnacleLGS, requiring that:

§  (a) Charles serve verified responses to Plaintiff’s (i) Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, and (ii) Special Interrogatories, Set One, within thirty days, and pay $500.00 in monetary sanctions. Charles and his attorney have ignored the Court’s order.

§  (b) PinnacleLGS serve verified responses to Plaintiff’s (i) Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, (ii) Special Interrogatories, Set One, (iii) Requests for Admission, Set One, and (iv) Form Interrogatories, Set Two, within thirty days, and pay $4,000.00 in sanctions. PinnacleLGS and its attorney have also ignored the Court’s Order.

o   Charles, PinnacleLGS and their counsel, Kousha Berokim, ignored the March 26, 2024, Court Order, thereby requiring James to file his first motion for terminating, issue, and/or evidentiary sanction on May 6, 2026

o   On June 26, 2024, the same date that the opposition to James’ motion for sanctions was due, Charles and PinnacleLGS tried to moot the terminating sanctions motion by serving further responses, which remained deficient.

o   The parties met and conferred regarding the purportedly deficient responses, Charles and PinnacleLGS missed several deadlines to serve further responses to requests for production.

·       Plaintiff argues that Defendants have not complied with the Court’s March 26, 2024, Order because Defendants’ further responses are deficient

·       Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010 and 2023.030 permits a court to award issue, evidentiary, and/or monetary sanctions against any party that disobeys a court order.

·       Plaintiff has incurred $6,300.00 in legal fees in bringing this Motion

Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendants further responses served on June 26, 2024 are code compliant. Defendants have complied with the Court’s orders.

·       Plaintiff’s Motion suggests that the June 26, 2024 responses were deficient, but does not meet its burden to establish such deficiencies, let alone establish good cause for the extreme and dispositive reliefs the Motion seeks in evidentiary and issue sanctions.

·       Plaintiff’s Motion misrepresents and misquotes the text of his requests

·       The sought dispositive evidentiary sanctions do not relate to, and are not justified, by the alleged discovery deficiencies

·       Sanctions should be imposed against Plaintiff for bringing this motion

Plaintiff filed a reply maintaining that Defendants’ further responses are not code compliant.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

A misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)  A misuse of the discovery process also includes disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).) A court may impose issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, or monetary sanctions against a party engaging in misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030.) Where an issue sanction is imposed, “designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).) An issue sanction may also involve “any party engaging in misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).) An evidence sanction involves “an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (c).)  

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)  “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Ibid.) “[C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  (Ibid.) Where discovery violations are “willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid.)  A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions. (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) Where discovery sanctions are requested against a party, there must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful. (Ibid.)   

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for issue and/or evidentiary sanctions against Defendants on the grounds that Defendants failed to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024 Order. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions.  

On March 26, 2024 the Court granted Plaintiff’s requests for further discovery responses and granted monetary sanctions against Defendants. The Court ordered “compliance with discovery orders and payment of sanctions (total $4,500 against both Defendants and their counsel) to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days.” (Court’s 03/26/24 Minute Order, p. 5.)

On May 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed his first motion for terminating, issue and/or evidentiary sanctions, and monetary sanctions, against Defendants for failure to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024 order. In Plaintiff’s motion, he asserted that Defendants had failed to serve any further discovery responses in violation of the Court’s order.

On June 26, 2024, Defendants served further discovery responses.

On July 10, 2024, the Court ruled that because Defendants had served further responses, and without taking a position as to whether those further responses complied with the Court’s March 26, 2024, Order, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for terminating, issue, and/or evidentiary sanctions, but did award monetary sanctions of $4,500 against Defendants and their counsel jointly and severally. (Court’s 07/10/24 Minute Order, pp. 6-7.) The parties subsequently met and conferred regarding the purportedly deficient responses. (King Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff asserts that Defendants agreed to provide further responses. (Ibid.) On August 19, 2024, Defendants served further responses to interrogatories and requests for admission, but not requests for production. (Ibid.) Defendants have not served further responses to the requests for production. (Ibid.)

Now, in Plaintiff’s instant motion for sanctions, Plaintiff argues that Defendants have still failed to comply with the Court’s March 26, 2024 Order because Defendants have not served code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s requests for production. Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ further responses to the requests for production served on June 26, 2024 were not code compliant and that Defendants have failed to supplement these responses.

In opposition, Defendants argue the motion should be denied because Defendants have complied with the Court’s March 26, 2024 Order. Defendants assert that the further responses to Plaintiff’s requests for production served on June 26, 2024 are code compliant.

The Court notes that since the parties dispute whether Defendants’ further responses are code compliant, Plaintiff should have filed a motion to compel further responses. In determining whether Defendants’ further responses are deficient, the Court is required to analyze each objection made to determine whether valid objections were made and this type of analysis requires a separate statement. Motions to compel further responses must be accompanied by a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(3).) The substantial rights of the parties are affected when there is no separate statement. Here, Plaintiff did not file a separate statement. Instead, the parties’ papers reference some of the requests and purportedly deficient responses. However, Defendants assert in their opposition that Plaintiff’s motion failed to accurately cite the requests.  A properly prepared Separate Statement enables both the parties and the Court to accurately analyze relevance and objections. Therefore, the Court cannot determine at this time whether Defendants’ further responses to requests for production complied with the Court’s March 26, 2024. Since the Court cannot determine whether Defendants have failed to comply with the Court’s order, the Court does not find that issue, evidentiary or monetary sanctions are appropriate at this time.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Granting Issue and/or Evidentiary Sanctions against Charles Cheung and Pinnaclelgs, Inc. and monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

 II. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Granting Issue and/or Evidentiary Sanctions against Charles Cheung and Pinnaclelgs, Inc. and monetary sanctions is DENIED.

(Lakhman Decl. at p. 8, ¶17:21-23 & Exhibit “L”).

On July 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply arguing:

·       Plaintiff is not an alter ego of its attorney. Defendant misapplies the alter ego doctrine

o   The alter ego doctrine is an equitable doctrine of liability that applies to owners of fictitious entities. SEC v. Hickey (9th Cir. 2003) 322 F. 3d 1123, 1128-1129 amended 335 F. 3d 834.

o   Even if Plaintiff’s counsel owned Plaintiff (he does not) and Defendant’s alter ego theory (as to which Defendant carries the burden) had been substantiated by actual evidence of alter ego (which it is not), the determination that Plaintiff is Plaintiff’s counsel’s alter ego would be completely irrelevant to what is at issue on this motion.

·       Plaintiff is not a self-represented litigant

o   Plaintiff’s cause of action against the Defendant in this action is not tied to any personal rights of the Plaintiff

o   The Plaintiff’s claims, which are solely for injunctive relief and not for any damages remedy, instead, belong to the state, and the Plaintiff is merely deputized by the state to carry out the state’s interests in enforcing the Immigration Consultants Act (ICA) against immigration consultants by obtaining injunctive relief on “behalf of the general public. . . .” Bus. & Prof. Code § 22446.5(b)

o   No personal interest of the Plaintiff is being prosecuted in this action, rendering the Trope doctrine completely inapplicable to the Plaintiff

o   Plaintiff is wholly owned by its parent, IRDC Holdings, LLC. Plaintiff’s counsel and his law firm, which itself is a professional corporation distinct from the Plaintiff, have no ownership stake in the Plaintiff or its parent company whatsoever. (Decl. of Medvei, ¶ 4.)

o   The law is well-established that an organizations cannot be a pro se litigant unless it is a law firm. See e.g. PLCM Group, Inc. v. Ehrler (1991) 499 U.S. 432, 436 fn. 7).

·       An interest in recovering attorney’s fees as costs in litigation is not a personal interest in the outcome of litigation that invokes the Trope doctrine

·       Defendant’s claim that some Defendants continue to violate the ICA is irrelevant

·       Defendant’s reliance on hearsay news articles is improper

·       Plaintiff’s fee request is reasonable in amount

D.     Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

E.     Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees

 

Business and Professions Code section 22446.5 “sets forth three classes of plaintiffs who may pursue actions against immigration consultants who violate the ICA: (1) ‘A person claiming to be aggrieved by a violation of this chapter by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action or injunctive relief or damages, or both’ (subd. (a)); ‘Any other party who, upon information and belief, claims a violation of this chapter has been committed by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action for injunctive relief on behalf of the general public’ (subd. (b)); and (3) ‘The Attorney General, a district attorney, or a city attorney who claims a violation of this chapter has been committed by an immigration consultant, may bring a civil action for injunctive relief, restitution, and other equitable relief against the immigration consultant in the name of the people of the State of California’ (subd. (c)).” (Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC v. Hudson Ins. Co. (2022) 84 Cal. App. 5th 305, 309-310.) “The first two categories of plaintiffs who prevail in their actions are entitled to ‘reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs.’” (Id. at 310.) Indeed, such fees and costs are mandatory, as opposed to other statutes that provide for discretionary awards. (Bus. & Prof. Code § 22446.5(b) (stating that a private attorney general plaintiff, “upon prevailing, shall recover reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs”—use of the word shall makes such fees and costs mandatory); see e.g. Grossman v. Park Fort Washington Assn. (2012) 212 Cal. App. 4th 1128, 1133 (where statutory conditions for an award of statutory attorney fees is met, award is “mandatory”); Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal. 3d 621, 633.)

 

In Defendant’s opposition, he argues that Plaintiff has not met the statutory requirements under private attorney general fee statute codified in Code of Civil Procedure §1021.5 for an award of attorney’s fees. However, Defendant’s argument is inapplicable as Plaintiff is entitled to mandatory attorney’s fees pursuant to Business & Professions Code section 22446.5(b). Further, the parties’ stipulation provides that Plaintiff was awarded an injunction against Defendants and Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees and costs.

 

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff is not entitled to attorneys fees because no Attorney-Client relationship exists between Plaintiff Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC and Plaintiff’s counsel, Sebastian Medvei of Medvei Law Group, APC. Plaintiff contends that Mr. Medvei’s pecuniary interest in the outcome of the litigation is interchangeable with and legally indistinct from Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC, his alter ego.  However, the Court does not find Plaintiff’s argument or authority in support, persuasive or applicable here. Plaintiff argues that there is no abuse of discretion for the trial court to deny private attorney general fees based on Plaintiff’s personal and pecuniary motives to bring suit. (Children & Families Com. of Fresno County v. Brown (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 45.) However, as discussed above, this assertion is inapplicable as Plaintiff is not claiming an award of attorney’s fees under private attorney general fee statute codified in Code of Civil Procedure §1021.5. In addition, Defendant argues that Plaintiff is self-represented, similar to Trope v. Katz (1995) 11 Cal. 4th 274. However, Trope involved a law firm which chose to represent itself in an action in which it had sued a former client for breach of contract. Even though the parties had entered an agreement allowing the court to award reasonable attorney fees in event of litigation, the court found that the law firm was not entitled to attorneys fees because they were representing themselves. (Id. at 283.) Here, Plaintiff is not a law firm and Defendant does not establish that Plaintiff is self-represented. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal. 4th 1084, 1093-1094 (explicitly holding that in-house counsel for a fictitious entity can recover attorney’s fees and rejecting any application of Trope to instances where a corporation is represented by counsel, in-house or otherwise); see also id. at 1094 (“[A]n organization is not comparable to a pro se litigant because the organization is always represented by counsel, whether in-house or pro bono, and thus, there is always an attorney-client relationship.”) (citing Kay v. Ehrler (1991) 499 U.S. 432, 436 fn. 7).)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs.

 

F.      Hourly Rate

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) An attorney’s own billing rates carry a presumption of reasonableness. (Mandel v. Lackner (1979) 92 Cal.App.3d 747, 761)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel asserts his hourly rate of $750 is a reasonable base rate for the lodestar calculation. Plaintiff’s counsel states this hourly rate is well within the range of current market rates in Los Angeles for attorneys of similar experience. (Medvei Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

The Court finds that the rate requested by Plaintiff’s counsel is reasonable. Counsel’s declaration sufficiently attests to his experience and the reasonableness of the rates. (Medvei Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

G.    Reasonable Hours Incurred and Costs

 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.”  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) 

 

While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) 

 

Plaintiff’s counsel states that he expended 73.9 hours litigating this case. Plaintiff also seeks a lodestar enhancement of 1.5 due to: 1) the contingent nature of Plaintiff’s counsel’s compensation and likely delays in receiving payment; and 2) the public interest nature of the litigation.

 

The Court has reviewed all of the claimed hours. Utilizing a lodestar method, the Court finds that the total amount of reasonable hours spent by Plaintiff’s counsel in this matter to date is 73.9 hours. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s 8 hours claimed for pre-filing investigation, 1 hour spent preparing the Complaint, 1 hour spent propounding discovery and 2 hours spent reviewing discovery responses are unreasonable. However, Plaintiff does not put forth sufficient evidence that the claimed hours are unreasonable. Therefore, the lodestar attorney’s fees award is $55,425 (73.9 x $750/hr.). Plaintiff’s request for a lodestar enhancement/multiplier of 1.5 is granted. Thus, the Court awards Plaintiff’s requested attorneys fees in the sum of $83,137.50 plus $60 in the filing of the instant Motion. 

 

In sum, this Court grants Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees and costs totaling $83,197.50.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiff $83,197.50 in attorney fees and costs.
