DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV38437    Hearing Date: March 5, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 3-5-24

Case # 21STCV38437

Trial Date: 5-13-24

 

TERMIMATING SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, National General Insurance Company, et al.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs, Pamela and Clifford Cole

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion for Terminating, Evidentiary or Issue Sanctions

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiffs Pamela and Clifford Cole own certain real property located at 28913 Medea Mesa Rod., Agoura Hills, CA. Plaintiffs maintain an active fire insurance policy with defendants National General Insurance Company, National General Premier Insurance Company, and Integon Insurance Company, at all relevant times.

 

On November 9, 2018, Plaintiffs home sustained damage during the Woolsey Fire. Plaintiffs specifically identify damage to a retaining wall abutting a tributary of Malibu Creek, and a deck at least partially over the retaining wall. Plaintiffs allege the wall constitutes a covered item under the “other structure” provision of the policy, with a coverage amount of up to $226,522, and potentially doubled in case of a brush fire to $453,044. Plaintiffs also allege a “code upgrade” provision in the policy, whereby any reconstruction/repair requiring an update to current code provisions, an entirely new concrete structure in this case, are covered. Plaintiffs maintain Defendants improperly refuse to pay the contractually due amount for wall repair and update, as well as for replacement of the damaged deck.

 

On October 19, 2021, Plaintiffs filed a complaint for Breach of Contract, Tortious Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, and Violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200. On February 24, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint. On April 27, 2022, the court sustained the demurrer with 10 days leave to amend. On June 17, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a second amended complaint for Breach of Contract, and Tortious Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing.

 

RULING: Denied.

Request for Judicial Notice: Granted.

The court takes judicial notice of all filed documents, but cannot take judicial notice of the content of the pleadings for the truth of the matter asserted. The court also takes judicial notice of the court orders for purposes of considering the basis of the instant motion.

 

Defendants National General Insurance Company, National General Premier Insurance Company, Integon Insurance Company collectively moves for terminating, issue and/or evidentiary sanctions against plaintiffs Pamela and Clifford Cole, due to late compliance with prior orders compelling responses, and outstanding responses to a request for production of documents served on November 7, 2023. [Declarations of Tony Cheng, ¶¶ 13-18, Exhibits.] Plaintiffs in a one day late opposition contend the motion lacks any valid basis: Plaintiffs provided responses to all outstanding items albeit late, but not incomplete. Plaintiffs deny any prejudice. Defendants in a one day late reply contend the subject motion culminates a “history of discovery abuse” and prejudices Defendants.

 

“Discovery sanctions ‘should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.’” (Young v. Rosenthal (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 96, 118-119 citing Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793; Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 613.) A prerequisite to the imposition of the dismissal sanction is that the party has willfully failed to comply with a court order. (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280; Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 481, 487 overruled on other grounds in Garcia v. McCutchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, fn. 4.); Young v. Rosenthal (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 96, 114.) Preventing parties from presenting their cases on the merits is a drastic measure; terminating sanctions should only be ordered when there has been previous noncompliance with a rule or order and it appears a less severe sanction would not be effective.  (Link v. Cater (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1326; Department of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Howell¿(2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 191 [“Terminating sanctions are to be used sparingly because of the drastic effect of their application.”].) “The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez¿(2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)  

 

Evidence or issue sanctions may be imposed only after parties violated discovery orders compelling further responses, except in exceptional circumstances, including where there was sufficiently egregious misconduct regarding a failure to respond to discovery, or a prior discovery order would be futile. (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1428.) “The discovery statutes thus ‘evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.’ (Citation.) Although in extreme cases a court has the authority to order a terminating sanction as a first measure (Citations), a terminating sanction should generally not be imposed until the court has attempted less severe alternatives and found them to be unsuccessful and/or the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective.” (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604.) To avoid sanctions, the burden of proving that a discovery violation was not willful is on the party on whom the discovery was served. (Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250, 252- 253.)

 

The motion lacks a separate statement is statutorily required under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(7). The court finds the missing statement in no way precludes or inhibits consideration of the subject matter. The court therefore addresses the substance of the requested relief.

 

The essence of the motion appears as a result of delayed responses to previously court ordered discovery, as well as outstanding responses to an item recently addressed by court (see 2-27-24 order on motion to compel responses to supplemental request for production of documents). The latter items remain within the ordered compliance period, and the court therefore finds no violation as of the date of this order. It remains undisputed, the previously court ordered responses were in fact provided.

 

On these bases, the court finds no actual basis for a violation of a court order justifying terminating, issue, or evidentiary sanctions simply based on either delayed service or a premature declaration of non-compliance with a pending court order. Additionally, failure to pay monetary sanctions never supports the imposition of terminating sanctions. (Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th at p. 615.) The court declines to impose any further sanctions based on the failure to pay outstanding sanctions.

 

Public policy also disfavors such sanctions simply under the circumstances where the result would lead to an effective “avoidance of a decision on the merits.” (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1194 accord McGinty v. Superior Court (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 204, 214 [“On balance, we conclude that it was excessive and an abuse of discretion for the trial court to impose the preclusive witness and evidence sanction in this case for Hood's violation of the authority of another court, where no real harm has been done to Avco, and where the punishment falls most harshly not on the supposed malefactor but on the petitioners in their ability to have their case decided on the merits.”]

 

A court can impose additional monetary sanctions for the costs associated with a valid motion. (Manlin v. Milner (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 1004, 1025 [“For a trial court to decline to award any amount of monetary sanctions when it finds discovery misconduct has occurred is arbitrary and an abuse of discretion”].) The court finds no justification for this particular motion simply due to late responses and a lack of a current violation on pending orders. Nothing in the motion establishes a violation of any prior order, thereby establishing necessity for another motion. Plaintiffs were already sanctioned for the prior delayed responses.

 

Motion for summary judgment on March 6, 2024. Trial remains set for May 13, 2024.

 

Moving party to give notice.

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Marc Benshemhoun leased certain premises owned and/or managed by defendants ROHCS Property Management Co., Aryeh Robbins, Fred Schor, Josh Gottlieb, Tzvi Haber, and 330 North Genesee Avenue LLC, and located at 332.5 Genesee Ave., Los Angeles beginning on May 15, 2014. Beginning in September 2017, Plaintiff discovered mold on the premises, and notified defendants. No remediation was undertaken.

 

On February 18, 2022, Defendants filed an unlawful detainer action against Plaintiff, which was dismissed. On May 3, 2022, Defendants served a Three Day Notice to Pay Rent or Quit. A secont Three Day Notice to Pay Rent or Quit was served on May 16, 2022.

 

On July 29, 2022, plaintiff Marc Bensemhoun filed a complaint for Breach of Warranty of Habitability (Violation of Civil Code § 1941.1), Breach of Warranty of Habitability (Health & Safety Code § 17920.3), Breach of Warranty of Habitability (Violation of Civil Code § 1942.4), Negligence - Premises Liability, Nuisance, Battery, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress,  Breach of Contract, Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment, Unfair Business Practices (Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200, et. seq.), Fraudulent Concealment, Moratorium, Ordinance Nos.186585 and 186606, Violation of Los Angeles Tenant Anti Harassment Ordinance, Los Angeles Tenant Anti Harassment Ordinance Section 45.33, and Retaliation (Cal. Civ. Code § 1940.35),

 

RULING: Denied.

Plaintiff Marc Bensemhoun moves to compel further responses to request for production of documents, numbers 1, 2, 10 through 20, 46 through 56, 58, 59, 61, 62, 64, 65, 66 through 70, 82, 84, 86, 95, 96, 116, 118, 119 through 122, 130, 131, 134, and 146. Defendant ROHCS Property Management Co. in opposition maintains the motion is moot as the parties settled the instant action via a stipulated unlawful detainer judgment. [Declaration of Benjamin Kiss, Ex. A.] ROHCS additionally challenges the necessity of the items given the lack of any relationship to the action. Plaintiff in reply denies any settlement agreement on the instant action; the agreement only applies to the separate unlawful detainer action. Plaintiff selectively reiterates the basis for discovery of certain items.

 

The request was served on July 9, 2023; Responses were served on August 24, 2023. [Declaration of Michael Yadegari.] The responses consist of the following:

 

“Responding party incorporates into this particular response the General Objections set forth above. Responding party further objects to each request for production to the extent that it seeks to burden and harrass answering party and in particular is propounded upon a perjurious declaration and seeks requests relating to 34 affirmative defenses when there are only 13 and furthermore seems directed to a short term rental such as airbnb which are not part of Plaintiffs complaint as such are irreleveant and not calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

Objection. Demand is overbroad and seeks documents protected by attorney-client or work-product privileges. It also seeks information from third parties and information not within its possession, custody, control, or personal knowledge and seeks to invade privacy and disclosure of non-relevant financial information and seeks personal records such as time sheets when the answering party is a Corporation.

Notwithstanding the foregoing objections, responding party responds as follows: Responding party will provide documents that relate to the herein lawsuit.”

 

The motion lacks any separate statement, and otherwise only offers a generalized statement regarding the lack of a good faith effort to produce the information. (Cal. Rules Ct., rule 3.1345(a)(3), (c), (d).) The court may proceed without a separate statement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(3).)

 

The instant motion was filed on January 23, 2024, which is more than 45 days from the August 24, 2023, date of service. The motion lacks any proof of extension of the time to file the instant motion. The motion is therefore untimely. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).) The court denies the motion on this basis.

 

Even if the court considers the motion on the merits, the court cannot definitively determine the impacts of the settlement of the unlawful detainer on the instant discovery. Plaintiff denies any impacts in reply.

 

The court declines to otherwise address each every of the identified items—1, 2, 10 through 20, 46 through 56, 58, 59, 61, 62, 64, 65, 66 through 70, 82, 84, 86, 95, 96, 116, 118, 119 through 122, 130, 131, 134, and 146—for a determination of the sufficiency of the responses simply based on the conclusive argument regarding insufficient responses. Although Plaintiff in reply also apparently seeks to compel promised production on number 61, 62, 66-70, 82, 84, 116, 119-122, 134, and 146, nothing in the motion actually provides a basis of relief to compel enforcement of the represented promise for production. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (a).) Plaintiff also adds in new argument in the reply regarding the insufficient of the failure to account for the inability to produce or identify documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230.

 

Notwithstanding the procedural bar to the motion, and partial lack of cited, articulated support until the reply, to the extent any part of the motion remains viable and active thereby allowing the court to address the merits, the court orders Defendant to produce all non-privileged documents as promised. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (a).) Defendant may also produce a privilege log where applicable to be determined in a meet and confer under Code of Civil procedure section 2031.240, subdivision (c), as well as responses in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230. While the court cannot order the latter part due to the procedural bars on the motion, should these issues return to the court in a different form, the court will be required to impose mandatory sanctions for any failure to produce documents, which begin at $1,000/motion, against the losing party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.050, subd. (a).)

 

The court will not conduct any informal discovery conferences, but will consider the meet and confer efforts of the parties. In addition to the possibility of sanctions, continued multiple motions to compel further responses involving voluminous amounts of items may also result in the setting of an OSC re: Appointment of Referee.

 

Sanctions not rewarded in favor of Plaintiff at this time. The court also declines to impose sanctions in favor of defendant in opposition. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.050, subd. (c), 2031.310, subd. (h).)

OSC RE: DEFAULT

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff, Mural Media, LLC 401K Plan

RESPONDING PARTIES: Defendant, Linda Maultsby

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Hearing on OSC re: Entry of Default

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On May 9, 2023, plaintiff Mural Media, LLC 401K Plan acquired a single parcel of real property with a multi-unit building. The property was sold pursuant to a trustee sale following the default on the secured loan to the property by defendant Linda Maultsby. On September 1, 2023, plaintiff filed a complaint for unlawful detainer as to 5109 Westhaven St., Los Angeles.

 

On October 2, 2023, the clerk entered a default against all unnamed occupants. On October 24, 2023, the court denied Defendant’s motion to quash service of the summons and complaint. The court ordered Defendant to serve a responsive pleading within five (5) days of service of the order. On November 2, 2023, Defendant filed a notice of filing of a petition for writ of mandate. On November 30, 2023, the Appellate Division issued an order denying the writ of mandate.

 

On December 12, 2023, the clerk rejected the request for entry of default applications seeking possession of the premises, due to the failure to dismiss all unnamed occupants. At the time of the January 5, 2024, Case Management Conference, the court set an OSC re: Why Default Should Not Be Entered Based On The Determination Of The Writ Proceedings Before The Court of Appeals for January 19, 2024. On January 19, 2024, the court acknowledged the petition to the California Supreme Court, and set a hearing date for March 5, 2024.

 

RULING: Off-Calendar.

Plaintiff, Mural Media, LLC 401K Plan, in its supplemental brief represents that the California Supreme Court rejected the writ of mandate. A motion to transfer and demurrer were also filed on January 29, and February 1, 2024, thereby constituting at least one responsive document in compliance with the prior motion to quash.

 

The court therefore takes the subject OSC off-calendar. Motion to Transfer and Demurrer set for March 26 and March 27, 2024, respectively.

 

Clerk to give notice to all parties.
