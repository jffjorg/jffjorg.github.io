DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV01362    Hearing Date: July 30, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

DANTE HIGGINS,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22TRCV01362

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

EXPLORIFY MOTORCYCLE RENTALS, LLC,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          July 30, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Attorney Christopher W. Olmsted and Ogletree, Deakins, Nash, Smoak & Stewart, P.C., for defendant Explorify Rental Los Angeles, LLC

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel

The Court considered the moving papers.

RULING

The Court requires information as to the reason why defense counsel seeks to be relieved as counsel, which defense counsel may provide in camera at the hearing.

BACKGROUND

            On November 30, 2022, plaintiff Dante Higgins filed a complaint against Explorify Motorcycle Rentals, LLC for Labor Code violations and wrongful termination in violation of public policy.  The complaint alleges that plaintiff was employed by defendant on April 8, 2022 as a general manager.  Complaint, ¶6.  Plaintiff was misclassified as an exempt employee during his employment with defendant.  Id., ¶7.  On May 30, 2022, plaintiff informed defendant via text message that he was sick and tested positive for COVID-19 and that his doctor instructed him not to return to work and remain in quarantine for ten days.  Id., ¶9.  On June 8, 2022, the same day plaintiff returned back to work following his sick leave, his direct supervisor and the co-owner and/or manager of defendant called him into his office and terminated plaintiff’s employment.  Id., ¶10.  Defendant terminated plaintiff’s employment in retaliation for him disclosing a positive COVID-19 test and for being ordered to quarantine and isolate pursuant to Labor Code §6409.6.  Id., ¶11.  During plaintiff’s employment, defendant engaged in a system of willful violations of the Labor Code, including failing to pay plaintiff all wages earned, failing to keep and provide accurate and timely records of wages earned and other mandated records, failing to pay overtime wages, and failing to reimburse for necessary business expenses.  Id., ¶12.

            On April 2, 2024, the Court granted plaintiff’s motion to quash to modify the subpoenas to California DMV and LA Care.

            Trial is set for September 3, 2024, having been continued by stipulation filed April 25, 2024 from June 5, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

The court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.  See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal. App. 2d 398.

CRC Rule 3.1362 (Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel) requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Explorify Rental Los Angeles, LLC’s attorney Christopher W. Olmsted and Ogletree, Deakins, Nash, Smoak & Stewart, P.C. seeks to be relieved as counsel.

            In the declaration, counsel does not provide any reason for seeking to be relieved, although counsel states that: “In the event the Court desires further information to ascertain the good faith basis of this motion and for withdrawal, it is respectfully requested that the Court have an in camera hearing outside the presence of all other parties so that the specific facts demonstrating good cause for the withdrawal may be demonstrated to the Court.”

The Court finds that although the attorney submitted a declaration establishing that the service requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, have been satisfied, counsel has not shown sufficient reason why the motion to be relieved as counsel should be granted.  Accordingly, the Court requests that counsel provide information at the hearing, in camera as necessary.

Motion to Disqualify Plaintiff’s Counsel and Firm

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On December 13, 2023, Ryan Michael Nelson filed a complaint against FCA US LLC for violations of Song-Beverly Act.  Plaintiff allege that on October 18, 2021, he purchased a 2021 RAM 1500 Big Horn and that in June 2023, with 29,926 miles, plaintiff towed the vehicle to a repair facility because of a catastrophic engine failure caused by its defective high pressure fuel pump.  The service technician discovered intrusion of metal shavings into the fuel system components.

            On January 22, 2024, defendant filed an answer.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Under CCP §128(a)(5), one of the court’s powers is to control the conduct of its ministerial officers and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it in furtherance of justice.  That power includes disqualifying an attorney.  “The issue of disqualification ‘ultimately involves a conflict between the clients’ right to counsel of their choice and the need to maintain ethical standards of professional responsibility.  The paramount concern, though, must be the preservation of public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar.  The recognized and important right to counsel of one’s choosing must yield to considerations of ethics that run to the very integrity of our judicial process.’”  Metro-Goldwyn Mayer v. Tracinda Corp. (1995) 36 Cal. App. 4th 1832, 1838.   

In exercising its discretion to disqualify an attorney, the court is required to make a reasoned judgment which complies with the legal principles and policies applicable to the issue at hand.  Henriksen v. Great Am. Sav. & Loan (1992) 11 Cal. App. 4th 109, 113. 

“Where . . . a motion for disqualification is based on an asserted breach of confidentiality or conflict of interest, the trial court must cautiously balance the competing interests.”  Med-Trans Corp. v. City of Cal. City (2007) 156 Cal. App. 4th 655, 663.  “’The court must weigh the combined effect of a party’s right to counsel of choice, an attorney’s interest in representing a client, the financial burden on a client of replacing disqualified counsel and any tactical abuse underlying a disqualification proceeding against the fundamental principle that the fair resolution of disputes within our adversary system requires vigorous representation of parties by independent counsel unencumbered by conflicts of interest.’”  Id. at 663-64 (citation omitted).

Under Rule 1.9, “(a) A lawyer who has formerly represented a client in a matter shall not thereafter represent another person in the same or a substantially related matter in which that person's interests are materially adverse to the interests of the former client unless the former client gives informed written consent.

(b) A lawyer shall not knowingly represent a person in the same or a substantially related matter in which a firm with which the lawyer formerly was associated had previously represented a client (1) whose interests are materially adverse to that person; and (2) about whom the lawyer had acquired information protected by Business and Professions Code section 6068, subdivision (e) and rules 1.6 and 1.9(c) that is material to the matter; unless the former client gives informed written consent.

(c) A lawyer who has formerly represented a client in a matter or whose present or former firm has formerly represented a client in a matter shall not thereafter:  (1) use information protected by Business and Professions Code section 6068, subdivision (e) and rule 1.6 acquired by virtue of the representation of the former client to the disadvantage of the former client except as these rules or the State Bar Act would permit with respect to a current client, or when the information has become generally known; or (2) reveal information protected by Business and Professions Code section 6068, subdivision (e) and rule 1.6 acquired by virtue of the representation of the former client except as these rules or the State Bar Act permit with respect to a current client.”

“‘[A]ttorney disqualification can be impliedly waived by failing to bring the motion in a timely manner.’”  Ontiveros v. Constable (2016) 245 Cal. App. 4th 686, 701 (citation omitted).   “However, to result in a waiver, the ‘delay [and] . . . the prejudice to the opponent must be extreme.’  Factors relevant to the reasonableness of a delay include the ‘stage of litigation at which the disqualification motion is made’ and the complexity of the case.  Delay can also be ‘an indication that the alleged breach of confidentiality was not seen as serious or substantial by the moving party,’ and can suggest ‘the possibility that the party brought the motion as a tactical device to delay litigation.’  ‘If the opposing party makes a prima facie showing of extreme delay and prejudice, the burden then shifts to the moving party to justify the delay.’”  Id. (citations omitted).    

DISCUSSION

            Pursuant to CCP §128 and Rules of Professional Conduct 1.9 and 1.10, defendant FCA US LLC requests that the Court disqualify plaintiff’s counsel Logan Hensley and The Lemon Daddy aka Drake Law from continuing to represent plaintiff.

            Defendant contends that on December 19, 2022, attorney Hensley began working for RoseWaldorf LLP, and that over the next one and a half years, Hensley defended FCA US on numerous claims filed under the Song-Beverly Act.  On January 25, 2023, Hensley executed a Confidentiality and Non-Disclosure Agreement with FCA US, which states in part that he agreed to “not use any of the information described [in the agreement] to assist in opposing FCA US in litigation for a period of not less than 18 months after leaving the employ of [RoseWaldorf LLP] . . . .”  Defendant asserts that Hensley billed 672.8 hours defending FCA US and worked on 564 different FCA US matters.  Defendant notes that the present claim was filed on December 18, 2023, while Hensley was employed by RoseWaldorf LLP and while he was currently representing FCA US.  On April 30, 2024, Hensley provided notice that he was leaving the firm and his final day at RoseWaldorf was May 10, 2024.  Defendant states that on information and belief, Hensley’s employment with Drake Law started no later than May 16, 2024.  Defendant argues that there is a “clear conflict of interest and violation of Hensley’s ethical duties to FCA US.”  Defendant asserts that throughout the course of his employment with RoseWaldorf, he personally evaluated claims on behalf of FCA US and was involved in strategy decisions on FCA US’s defense to such claims; he was involved with direct client contact regarding FCA US’s defenses and strategies of various cases.  Hensley also “received extensive and almost constant training regarding FCA US’s litigation strategies and the manner in which those litigation strategies were developed and implemented across all cases.”  Defendant also contend that underlying Hensley’s ability to draft objections and responses to discovery on behalf of FCA US was his extensive knowledge of FCA US.  He was privy to and responsible for maintaining privileged and confidential information.  Moreover, defendant argues, Hensley’s disqualification should be imputed to the law firm, which defendant asserts consists of four attorneys, as there has been no attempt to screen Hensley from participation in any of the matters and there is a central email address.  When attorneys practice together in a firm and presumptively share access to privileged and confidential matters, the disqualification of one attorney extends vicariously to the entire firm, citing to (Flatt v. Superior Court (1994) 9 Cal. 4th 275, 283.  Also, defendant contends, Hensley was “not upfront with RoseWaldorf during his departure, and he purposefully concealed his intent to join a firm currently suing FCA US” and “waited almost one month after leaving RoseWaldorf to update his bar profile and led RoseWaldorf to believe he did not know where he was going upon his departure.”  See Brian Yasuzawa decl.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that RoseWaldorf failed to meet the high burden of making a showing that Hensley ever received confidential information material to the herein case, or that any substantial relationship exists between the matters Hensley worked on at RoseWaldorf and the herein case.  Hensley states in his declaration that his work for FCA consisted almost entirely of templated drafts, performing stand-ins for appearances, and zero direct communication with FCA regarding substantive matters or litigation strategy.  He states that he “simply did not perform substantive work for FCA.”  He also states that during his time at RoseWaldorf, he never worked on, was involved with, or even heard of the “prior matter,” which is the herein case.  He further asserts that he was never apprised of anything relating to the instant matter, other than this motion.  He also declares that he never received or took part in any training regarding FCA’s litigation strategies.  He was never tasked with substantively responding to discovery requests.  He attended three or four vehicle inspections in order to fill in for the attorneys actually assigned to the cases and did not perform any substantive work.  As for his work at the Drake Law firm, he states that he is assigned to handle sixty-four cases, and none of them are against FCA US.  Logan Hensley decl.  Further, plaintiff contends, the issue is now moot as Hensley has been properly and timely screened from all matters against FCA US.  Clarence Serrano decl.  Moreover, plaintiff argues, no conflict can be imputed to the Drake Law Firm as they have complied with California Rule of Professional Conduct 1.10(a)(2).  Also, plaintiff argues, plaintiff’s right to choose his own counsel prevails over any appearance of impropriety.

            In reply, defendant disputes that Hensley did not receive confidential information while at RoseWaldorf and that he was a mere “appearance” attorney.  Defendant further argues that Hensley has not been properly screened and that Drake Law is not in compliance with Rule 1.10.  Defendant also contends that the “instant matter is not substantially related—it is the same matter” and that “even if it were another matter, the matters are substantially related as the same defenses and strategies during the time” Hensley represented FCA US “are being utilized now.”

            The Court rules as follows:  Before a former client can disqualify an attorney and its firm under a theory of successive representation, “the former client must show that the subjects of the successive representations are substantially related.”  Khani v. Ford Motor Company (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 916, 920 (citation omitted).  “[D]isqualification will depend upon the strength of the similarities between the legal problem involved in the former representation and the legal problem involved in the current representation.”  Jessen v. Hartford Casualty Ins. Co. (2003) 111 Cal. App. 4th 698, 709.  “[E]ven if the issues are similar, there must be some basis to conclude that an attorney . . . was privy to confidential information.”  Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2006) 143 Cal. App. 4th 50, 68. 

This case is somewhat similar to Khani, supra.  In Khani, plaintiff’s counsel, Payam Shahian initiated a lemon law action against Ford Motor Company and its dealership for defects in a 2008 Lincoln Navigator.  Shahian worked for Ford’s defense counsel between June 2004 and July 2007.  Ford moved to disqualify Shahian and his law firm.  The trial court granted the motion. The Second District Court of Appeal reversed.  The court explained that the evidence “does not establish that any information to which Shahian was exposed during his representation of Ford would be material to his representation of Khani in this case.”  Id. at 922.  Further, the court determined that Ford failed to establish that any confidential information that Shahian learned while defending Ford would be at issue.

Such is the case here.  Defendant has not shown that the allegedly defective RAM 1500 Big Horn nor its repair history was the subject of any lawsuit in which Hensley represented FCA US, although the case had been filed while Hensley was at his prior firm.  Hensley states that he never worked on the herein case or was assigned that case while at his prior firm, and that he has not been assigned any FCA US cases while at his new firm.  In lemon law actions, each vehicle and consumer experience is unique, with its own defects and repair history, different dealerships and repair facilities. The Khani court held that the trial court abused its discretion in concluding that the prior cases were substantially related to the current case “just because they involved claims under the same statute” and that the trial court also “incorrectly assumed that Shahian's exposure to playbook information in prior lemon law cases was sufficient to disqualify him in this case without any showing of its materiality.”  Further, the evidence indicates that Hensley has not been assigned any FCA US cases at his new firm and that there are procedures in place to keep him screened from the FCA US cases.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

BACKGROUND

            On January 31, 2024, plaintiff Laureano Azurdia Camey filed a complaint against City of Gardena, Garabet Kurbessoian and Garabet Kurbessoian dba S & M Service Station Inc. for (1) premises liability and (2) dangerous condition of public property.

            On March 1, 2024, plaintiff filed a FAC, adding City of Los Angeles and City of Carson as defendants.

            On April 26, 2024, defendants Garabet Kurbessoian and Garabet Kurbessoian dba SM Service Station Inc. filed a cross-complaint against City of Gardena and City of Los Angeles for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant City of Gardena demurs to the complaint and the second cause of action for premises liability based on dangerous condition on the ground that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.  Defendant filed the demurrer after the FAC was filed and, thus, the Court will construe the demurrer as a demurrer to the FAC.  The allegations in the complaint and FAC are the same except to the extent the FAC adds two public entities as defendants.  Further, defendant attached the FAC to its request for judicial notice.

The FAC alleges that plaintiff was traveling westbound on Gardena Blvd., west of Figueroa Street, when a dangerous overgrown tree fell on top of plaintiff’s vehicle crushing the roof while located in front of 467 W. Gardena Blvd, property address 16435 S. Figueroa St., Gardena.  The subject tree is owned, maintained, and managed by defendants City of Gardena, City of Los Angeles, and City of Carson, who failed to control the subject tree which had a natural propensity to drop its limbs posing a significant hazard to others, failed to inspect, evaluate the part of the tree likely to fail, the likelihood of failure, and the likelihood that a failure will result in injury, and failed to keep subject tree trimmed among other breaches of duty.  Said defendant tree owner knew or should have known of said danger due to a long history of repairing the area around said tree and had notice of the danger with sufficient time to remedy the situation, causing severe injuries and damages to plaintiff. 

The FAC also alleges that City of Carson alleges defendant City of Gardena is responsible for the subject tree.  City of Gardena alleges defendant City of Los Angeles is responsible for the subject tree. 

Defendant City of Gardena argues that the complaint fails to establish that the accident took place on property that was owned, operated, or maintained by the City of Gardena.  Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of the County of Los Angeles Geographic Information System (GIS-NET) regarding land use and planning and zoning for areas within Los Angeles County (Exh. B), City of Los Angeles ZIMAS property system website (Exh. C), and Google Maps (Exh. D).  Defendant contends that the County maps were prepared by the Los Angeles County Department of Regional Planning in coordination with the County Chief Information Office.  Defendant contends that the maps show that the accident took place in the City of Los Angeles and not in the City of Gardena.  Defendant also argues that the allegations are conclusory and insufficient to support a claim for premises liability based on dangerous condition because they do not show that City of Gardena actually owned, operated, or maintained the property where the tree was located or had a legal duty to do so at the time of the accident.

There is no opposition.

The Court finds that based on the map exhibits attached to defendant’s request for judicial notice, that the location of the incident occurred in the City of Los Angeles and not the City of Gardena.  Thus, the allegations are insufficient to show that City of Gardena owned, maintained, managed, or operated the premises where the accident occurred.  Plaintiff had chosen to not oppose this motion and hence does not argue otherwise.  So too, Plaintiff has not provided any information that would lead the Court to conclude that Plaintiff could amend the complaint.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the FAC and the second cause of action.

            Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
