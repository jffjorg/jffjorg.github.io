DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01968    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

AYUS & CO, INC, A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION,

 

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

R BROTHERS, INC., A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, et al.

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   22VECV01968                 107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Demurrer - with Motion to Strike (CCP 430.10)

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Ayus & Co, Inc (“Plaintiff”) and defendant R Brothers, Inc. (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”) are both licensed contractors. In 2017, 2018, and 2019, Defendant hired Plaintiff as a subcontractor to do plumbing work at three schools for contracts Defendant had with the Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”). Plaintiff performed this plumbing work, but Defendant did not pay Plaintiff.

 

On November 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint (the “Complaint”). On January 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”), which dismissed a former defendant.

 

On May 31 and June 1, 2023, Defendant demurred to the FAC and moved to strike portions of the FAC. On August 8, 2023, this court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC with leave to amend and granted part of Defendant’s motion to strike portions of the FAC.

 

On September 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (the “SAC”), which alleges causes of action for (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of oral agreement, (3) common count for work, labor, and services rendered—agreed price, (4) common count for work, labor, and services rendered—reasonable value, (5) breach of written contract, (6) common count for work, labor, and services rendered—agreed price, (7) common count for work, labor, and services rendered—reasonable value, (8) breach of written contract, (9) common count for work, labor, and services rendered—agreed price, and (10) common count for work, labor, and services rendered—reasonable value.

 

On April 19, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) to the SAC and a motion to strike (the “Motion to Strike”) portions of the SAC. On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike, and five days later, Defendant replied to Plaintiff’s opposition.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Similarly, per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” which is 30 days. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿412.20, subd. (a)(3).) 

¿¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.) 

 

Plaintiffs have not filed a Proof of Service of the Complaint in this case. We therefore cannot determine if the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike are timely brought. The Demurrer, the Motion to Strike, the opposition, and both replies were timely filed as against the hearing date.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) and §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. Each section requires that such meet and confer be done “in person or by telephone.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§¿430.1, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).) 

 

Defendant submits with each of the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike a declaration by its counsel which confirms that he and Plaintiff’s counsel “engaged in a telephonic meet and confer concerning the basis for Defendant’s proposed demurrer and motion to strike.” (Wasserman Decl. ¶ 5.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10, subd. (a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿ 

 

1.      The Statute of Limitations

 

Plaintiff contends that the second through tenth causes of action are barred by the statute of limitations. (Demurrer p. 10.) Parties disagree as to when Plaintiff’s causes of action accrued.

 

“A complaint showing on its face the cause of action is barred by the statute of limitations is subject to general demurrer.” (Iverson, Yoakum, Papiano & Hatch v. Berwald (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 990, 995.)

 

An action upon a written contract must be brought within four years. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 337, subd. (a).) An action upon an oral contract must be brought within two years (Id. at § 339, subd. (1).) “[W]here services have been rendered under a contract which is unenforceable because not in writing, an action generally will lie upon a common count for quantum meruit. The statute of limitations for quantum meruit claims is two years.” (Iverson, Yoakum, Papiano & Hatch v. Berwald, supra, 76 Cal.App.4th 990 at 996.) Emergency¿Rule¿9¿was adopted by the Judicial Council during the COVID-19 pandemic and, as amended, reads: “(a) Tolling statutes of limitations over 180 days [¶] Notwithstanding any other law, the statutes of limitations and repose for civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.” (People v. Financial Casualty & Surety, Inc.¿(2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 879, 885.)

 

“Generally speaking, a cause of action accrues at the time when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 34 Cal.4th 787, 806.) “The elements of a breach of oral contract claim are the same as those for a breach of written contract: a contract; its performance or excuse for nonperformance; breach; and damages.” (Stockton Mortgage, Inc. v. Tope (2014) 233 Cal.App.4th 437, 453.) “Where a common count for services rendered alleges they were performed at the request of defendants, that they promised to pay for them and failed to do so, such count is good.” (Haggerty v. Warner (1953) 115 Cal.App.2d 468, 475.)

 

For both the causes of action for breach of contract, written or oral, and the common count causes of action for work, labor, and services rendered, the causes of action would not have been complete with all their elements until, as alleged, Defendant failed to pay Plaintiff and thereby breached the contract or reneged on its earlier promise to compensate Plaintiff. Therefore, the causes of action in the Complaint all accrued upon Defendant’s alleged failure to pay Plaintiff when such payment became due. They did not accrue and the statutes of limitations did not begin to run when the contracts between Parties were first entered, as Defendant argues. (Demurrer pp. 10–11.)

 

2.      The First, Fifth, and Eighth Causes of Action for Breach of Written Contract

 

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) The SAC pleads each of these elements and therefore states facts sufficient to constitute the cause of action.

 

Defendant argues that “[p]ursuant to the contracts attached to the SAC, Plaintiff, as subcontractor could not be paid for any amount for which [Defendant] was not paid.” (Demurrer p. 12.) The SAC attaches copies of the three subcontracting agreements on which the first, fifth, and eighth causes of action for breach of a written contract are brought. (SAC Exh. A, Exh. B, Exh. C.) Each such subcontracting agreement includes conditions on payments from Defendant to Plaintiff becoming due, among other conditions, that state that Defendant’s obligations to pay Plaintiff do not arise until after either Defendant is paid for work Plaintiff performed or Defendant received final payment from LAUSD. (Ibid.) “A pay if paid provision makes payment by the owner to the general contractor a condition precedent to the general contractor's obligation to pay the subcontractor for work the subcontractor has performed.” (Wm. R. Clarke Corp. v. Safeco Ins. Co. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 882, 885.) “Pay if paid” provisions in construction subcontracts are unenforceable, even if the contract is for a school public works. (Id. at 890; Capitol Steep Fabricators, Inc. v. Mega Construction Co. (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1049, 1058, 1060–62.) These provisions of the three subcontracting agreements between Parties are therefore unenforceable, and Plaintiff need not plead that they have been satisfied.

 

Defendant further argues that the amounts of damages Plaintiff seeks by each of the causes of action for breach of written contract include “certain lost profits and/or progress payments and/or retention payment and/or change order work,” but that “without sufficient allegations concerning the change orders, it is impossible to understand the legal effect of the agreements, written and oral, sued upon in the SAC.” (Demurrer pp. 12–13.) Defendant emphasizes its confusion about whether Plaintiff is seeking amounts due and owing under submitted change orders or under approved change orders. (Ibid.) Parties agree that change orders for each of the three subcontracting agreements exist, but Plaintiff rebuts that the attached subcontracting agreements “state all the material terms of the agreements between [Plaintiff and Defendant].” (Opposition p. 5.)

 

“Demurrers for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification, and apply where defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.) For a written contract, the plaintiff may plead it “by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489, abrogated on other grounds in Freeman v. Quicken Loans Inc. (2012) 566 U.S. 624.) To plead a contract’s legal effect, the plaintiff’s pleading must include “the substance of its relevant terms. This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.” (Ibid.) Thus, although a contract may be set out verbatim in its entirety in a pleading, it need not be so long as its legal effect is pleaded.

 

We agree that Plaintiff that even if subsequent change orders are missing, the SAC pleads the legal effect of the three subcontracting agreements by attaching the base agreements so that copies of the change orders are not necessary for Defendant to understand what issues are being raised by these causes of action and what claims are being stated against it. The further specificity that Defendant seeks, such as exactly which change orders are being included in Plaintiff’s calculation of damages, may be obtained through discovery.

 

Additionally, demurrers do not lie as to only parts of causes of action where some valid claim is alleged. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119 [“A demurrer must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained.”]; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Each breach of written contract cause of action also seeks damages for progress payments, which Plaintiff alleges it is owed per the terms of the attached base agreements. This portion of each cause of action states an intelligible cause of action and the entire cause of action must therefore survive a demurrer.

 

3.      The Second Cause of Action for Breach of Oral Contract

 

Defendant contends that because the SAC’s second cause of action for breach of oral contract “re-alleges and incorporates allegations that Plaintiff entered into a written contract” from earlier in the SAC, it is unclear if the second cause of action is asserting a cause of action on the written contract. (Demurrer p. 14.) We disagree. The SAC is clear that this cause of action is being argued in the alternative for one of its causes of action for breach of written agreement and that the SAC alleges that Parties orally agreed to written terms, which are set out in the unsigned subcontracting agreement. (SAC pp. 4–5.)

 

Defendant argues that this cause of action fails to plead the material terms of the oral agreement. (Demurrer p. 14.) We disagree for the same reason. The SAC alleges that Parties orally agreed to written terms, which are set out in the unsigned subcontracting agreement. (SAC pp. 4–5.) The SAC thus pleads the material terms of the oral agreement.

 

4.      The Third, Fourth, Sixth, Seventh, Ninth, and Tenth Common Count Causes of Action for Work, Labor, and Services Rendered

 

“When a common count is used as an alternative way of seeking the same recovery demanded in a specific cause of action, and is based on the same facts, the common count is demurrable if the cause of action is demurrable.” (Berryman v. Merit Property Management, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1560.)

 

Defendant contends that these common counts causes of action are subject to demurrer because the breach of contract causes of action, which they seek the same recovery as, are subject to demurrer. However, we overrule the Demurrer as to the breach of contract causes of action, and therefore also overrule the Demurrer as to these causes of action.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

  1. The Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)¿“[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) 

 

Defendant moves to strike each of the SAC’s causes of action in its entirety as well as each of the SAC’s prayers for relief in its entirety. (Notice of Motion pp. 2–4.) It does so on the same grounds and supported by the same arguments it makes in the Demurrer, and our findings and response are the same to each such argument.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Strike is DENIED.

I. BACKGROUND

On May 13, 2022, plaintiff Tatiana Vershinina (“Vershinina”) was robbed by a third party while she was depositing cash at a branch of defendant State Bank of India (“Defendant”) on behalf of plaintiff Sunoil Retail Group (“Sunoil” and, together with Vershinina, “Plaintiffs” and Plaintiffs, together with Defendant, “Parties”).

 

Plaintiffs filed this action on August 22, 2023, stating claims for (1) negligence, (2) premise liability, (3) unsafe conditions, and (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress. 

 

On February 13, 2024, this court granted Plaintiffs’ former counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

 

Plaintiffs failed to appear at the March 14, 2024 case management conference in this case, prompting this court to set a hearing for April 11, 2024 for an order to show cause why sanctions or dismissal of the entire action should not be imposed for failure to prosecute by Plaintiffs. (03/14/2024 Minute Order) Plaintiffs were ordered to appear at this hearing. (Ibid.) On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs failed to appear and thereby violated this court’s March 14 court order. (04/11/2024 Minute Order.)

 

On April 29, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions (the “Motion”), which Plaintiffs opposed on May 17, 2024.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

A court “may in its discretion dismiss an action for delay in prosecution pursuant to this article on its own motion or on motion of the defendant if to do so appears to the court appropriate under the circumstances of the case.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 583.410, subd. (a).) California Code of Civil Procedure section 583.420 sets out the conditions without which a court “may not dismiss an action pursuant to this article for delay in prosecution,” including that “[t]he action is not brought to trial within the following times: … [t]wo years after the action is commenced against the defendant if the Judicial Council by rule adopted pursuant to Section 583.410 so prescribes for the court.” California Rules of Court Rule 3.1340, subdivision (a) reads, “[t]he court on its own motion or on motion of the defendant may dismiss an action under Code of Civil Procedure sections 583.410–583.430 for delay in prosecution if the action has not been brought to trial or conditionally settled within two years after the action was commenced against the defendant.”

 

“Additionally, the statutes recognize that the courts have the inherent authority to dismiss an action.” (Del Junco v. Hufnagel (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 789, 798, citing Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 581, subd. (m) and § 583.150.) “[B]ecause delay in prosecution interferes with the orderly process of litigation and may make a fair trial unlikely, California courts have inherent power to dismiss civil cases for unreasonable, inexcusable delay in prosecution.” (Stephen Slesinger, Inc. v. Walt Disney Co. (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 736, 758.) “When a plaintiff's deliberate and egregious misconduct in the course of the litigation renders any sanction short of dismissal inadequate to protect the fairness of the trial, California courts necessarily have the power to preserve their integrity by dismissing the action.” (Id. at 762.) “The essential requirement is to calibrate the sanction to the wrong. Whether the misconduct violates a court order is relevant to the exercise of inherent power, but it does not define the boundary of the power.” (Id. at 763.) “The decision whether to exercise the inherent power to dismiss requires consideration of all relevant circumstances, including the nature of the misconduct (which must be deliberate and egregious, but may or may not violate a prior court order), the strong preference for adjudicating claims on the merits, the integrity of the court as an institution of justice, the effect of the misconduct on a fair resolution of the case, and the availability of other sanctions to cure the harm.” (Id. at 764.)

 

“Trial courts should only exercise this authority in extreme situations, such as when the conduct was clear and deliberate, where no lesser alternatives would remedy the situation, the fault lies with the client and not the attorney, and when the court issues a directive that the party fails to obey.” (Del Junco v. Hufnagel, supra, at 798, internal citations omitted.) “[D]ismissal is always a drastic remedy to be employed only in the rarest of circumstances.” (Stephen Slesinger, Inc. v. Walt Disney Co., supra, at 764, emphasis original.) For example, the court could dismiss an action where the plaintiff where plaintiff filed petition, failed even to serve it on state, was so dilatory in his prosecution of it that superior court consigned file to its archives, and offered no excuse for his failure to take slightest action to have petition determined for over five years. (Binyon v. State of California (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 952, 957.)

 

The court may impose a terminating sanction by an order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(1).) “The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

Defendant moves “for an order dismissing this action with prejudice on the grounds that [Plaintiffs] have withdrawn from their case by failure to prosecute.” (Notice of Motion p. 2.) Defendant moves “alternatively… for an order of terminating sanctions dismissing this action with prejudice pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.10 et seq. on grounds that Plaintiffs violated the Court’s order to appear at the April 11 hearing.” (Ibid.)

 

This lawsuit has existed for less than a year and a proof of service of the Complaint was filed. This court therefore does not have authority to dismiss it for failure to prosecute under California Code of Civil Procedure section 583.410, subd. (a).

 

This court has inherent powers to dismiss this action for failure to prosecute if such failure is deliberate and egregious and other sanctions would not adequately protect the fairness of the action to Defendant. In opposition to the Motion, Plaintiffs submit a declaration by their attorney (the "Lukyanov Declaration") stating that although her law firm withdrew as Plaintiffs’ counsel in February, it had since recently been re-hired to represent Plaintiffs in this case. (Lukyanov Decl. ¶¶ 2, 5.) The Lukyanov Declaration observes that Plaintiffs’ failures to appear at case management conferences or hearings occurred only while Plaintiffs were unrepresented by counsel. (Id. at ¶¶ 3–4.) It also states that “[t]he delay in prosecution of this case was only about four (4) months long and was caused by circumstances beyond Plaintiffs’ control.” (Id. at ¶ 6.) Given these facts and changes circumstances, as well as the fact that Plaintiffs have only violated one court order, we fail to find that Plaintiffs’ failures to prosecute their case or to appear when necessary were deliberate and egregious. We therefore decline to dismiss this action under our inherent powers.

 

Terminating sanctions are authorized by California Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.030, subdivision (d)(1) when the sanctioned party has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process. California Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.010 provides examples of misuses of the discovery process, which include disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.10, subd. (g).)

 

The court order that Plaintiffs violated in this case by failing to appear at the April 11 hearing did not relate to discovery. California Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.10, et seq., which Defendant cites in support of its motion for terminating sanctions, is therefore inapplicable to the instant case. Seeing no statutory grounds for terminating sanctions, this court denies Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion.

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness

Similarly, per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” which is 30 days. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿412.20, subd. (a)(3).) 

¿¿

Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.) 

 

Plaintiffs have not filed a Proof of Service of the Complaint in this case. We therefore cannot determine if the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike are timely. The Demurrer, the Motion to Strike, both oppositions, and both replies are timely as against the hearing date.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) and §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. Each section requires that such meet and confer be done “in person or by telephone.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§¿430.1, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).)

(Basria Decl. ¶ 7.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10, subd. (a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿ 

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds that the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute the cause of action under California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e). (Notice of Demurrer p. 3; Demurrer p. 5.)

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) the defendant engages in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffers extreme or severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant's extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's extreme or severe emotional distress. Outrageous conduct is conduct that is intentional or reckless and so extreme as to exceed all bounds of decency in a civilized community. The defendant's conduct must be directed to the plaintiff, but malicious or evil purpose is not essential to liability. Whether conduct is outrageous is usually a question of fact.” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 203, citing Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal citations omitted.) “‘Recklessness’ means the deliberate disregard of the high degree of probability that an injury will occur. It rises to the level of a conscious choice of a course of action with knowledge of the serious danger to others.” (Samantha B. v. Aurora Vista Del Mar, LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 85, 99.) “In order to avoid a demurrer, the plaintiff must allege with ‘great specificity’ the acts which he or she believes are so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.)

 

In Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 912–13, 922, a residential tenant stated a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress against her landlord sufficient to overcome a demurrer when she alleged that the landlord was aware of substandard habitability conditions, including leaking sewage, defective electrical wiring, and a “heavy” cockroach infestation; that these conditions had caused the county health department to issue a notice to vacate and demolish the property; and that the landlord failed to correct the conditions after a reasonable time. The court in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, at 922, stated that whether “the landlord’s acts are extreme and outrageous and result in severe mental distress” “presents a factual question [so that] it cannot be said as a matter of law that appellant has not stated a cause of action.”

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs fail to plead all the elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress because the Complaint does not plead outrageous conduct. (Demurrer p. 6.) Defendants argue that Complaint merely “allege[s] in a conclusory fashion that Defendants' conduct was intentional” and is “devoid of any facts showing that Defendants specifically intended to cause harm to Plaintiffs or engaged in any particular acts that were reckless.” (Ibid.)

 

In the Complaint, Plaintiffs plead that Plaintiffs first alerted Moss to the cockroach infestation on July 19, 2022. (Complaint p. 4.) Plaintiffs made at least one additional subsequent complaint to Moss about the infestation on November 15, 2022. (Ibid.) After Plaintiffs lodged a complaint with the County of Los Angeles Department of Public Health (“LADPH”), Defendants were given “a notice to eradicate the cockroach infestation on or before October 6, 2023,” and following LADPH’s inspection of the Property on October 13, 2023, Defendants were issued a notice of violation. (Id. at p. 5.) Plaintiffs made an additional subsequent complaint to Moss about the infestation on November 3, 2023. (Ibid.) Additionally, Plaintiff pleads on information and belief that “other past and current tenants of the [Property] have repeatedly complained to Defendants about the illegal and substandard condition of the [Property].” (Id. at pp. 5–6.)

 

Thus, the Complaint includes allegations that Defendants were made aware of the cockroach infestation, of its severity, and of its ongoing nature. Defendants point out that “Plaintiffs have failed to allege any facts showing that Defendants knew of the habitability issues prior to the initial notice provided in July 19, 2022.” (Demurrer p. 6.) We agree that the Complaint does not allege Defendants had any knowledge of the infestation before July 19, 2022. However, the Complaint alleges that the infestation persisted long after Defendants were alerted to the infestation on July 19, 2022; that Plaintiffs “waited 5 months to address the issue with [Moss]” while attempting to resolve the infestation themselves is irrelevant. (Ibid.) Defendants argue that “Plaintiffs also inconsistently followed up with [Moss] and provided notices several months apart such that it cannot be said that Defendants knew of the ongoing problem or intentionally allowed it to persist.” (Ibid.) Plaintiffs plead that they e-mailed Moss on May 19, 2023 inquiring about the individual who had entered the Property and not performed any pest control services earlier that day. (Complaint p. 4.) Defendants were thus informed that no pest control services had been conducted on the Property at that time. Defendants had knowledge that a cockroach infestation existed at the Property and that no pest control services had been conducted in response to it; it reasonably follows that Defendants were aware that the infestation, having not been treated in any way, persisted. Plaintiffs are not required to have repeatedly notified Defendants about the same habitability problem a certain number of times over a certain time period for Defendants to have knowledge of the problem. A single notification, followed by Defendants’ inaction, is sufficient. That Defendants were subsequently periodically reminded by Plaintiffs of the ongoing infestation and given notices by LADPH only adds to Defendants’ knowledge of the existence, severity, and ongoing nature of the problem.

 

Like the tenants in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 912–13, 922, Plaintiffs here adequately state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress to overcome a demurrer by alleging that their landlord, Defendants, knew of the substandard conditions in which Plaintiffs were living, received a notice from the county public health department about conditions at the Property, and yet failed to correct the conditions after a reasonable amount of time. And, following Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, at 922, whether Defendants’ conduct in doing so was extreme and outrageous so as to cause Plaintiffs’ emotional distress is a question of fact, but we cannot find here that Plaintiffs have failed to state the cause of action as a matter of law.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is OVERRULED,.

 

  1. The Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)

 

Defendants move to strike the Complaint’s prayers for punitive and exemplary damages and certain allegations of the Complaint that relate to its prayers for punitive and exemplary damages. (Notice of Motion pp. 2–3.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.)¿ 

¿ 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”¿ (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive¿damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice.¿However, malice does not require actual intent to harm.¿Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)¿¿ 

¿ 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves, supra at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,” within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].)¿ 

 

In Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 920, a residential tenant “pleaded sufficient facts to support her prayer for exemplary damages” when her complaint both “alleged that defendant had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises including leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions” and “alleged that defendants ‘In maintaining said nuisance,… acted with full knowledge of the consequences thereof and the damage being caused to plaintiff, and their conduct was willful, oppressive and malicious.’” And, like the plaintiff in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 920, Plaintiffs here plead that Defendants had actual knowledge of the cockroach infestation and that “Defendants' conduct described above was willful, wanton, intentional, despicable, malicious and initiated with malice and with the intent to knowingly take advantage of, oppress, and injure Plaintiffs. Defendants at all times acted with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of safety of Plaintiffs. Defendants were at all times aware that there was a high probability that their intentional and/or negligent failure to repair and maintain the Subject Property would injure Plaintiffs and cause them emotional distress and other damages.”

 

Defendants argue that punitive damages are not properly pled by the allegations of the Complaint because “it cannot be said that, on its face, Defendants knew the cockroach infestation was ongoing and intentionally allowed it to persist” and “Plaintiffs' allegations with respect to punitive damages lack specificity, and are instead merely conclusory and speculative.” (Reply p. 2.) However, as we explain above in our discussion of the Demurrer, we find that Plaintiffs plead that Defendants were notified of the existence of the cockroach infestation by July 19, 2022 and that subsequent complaints by Plaintiffs and the notices from LADPH further notified Defendants that the infestation was severe and ongoing. These allegations are sufficient to allege that Defendants had actual knowledge of the cockroach infestation. Plaintiffs also allege that Defendants, in response, largely either provided false assurances or ignored Plaintiffs for months. (Complaint pp. 4–5.)

 

Although Defendants emphasize Plaintiffs’ delay in first alerting Defendants to the infestation while they attempted to abate the infestation themselves before July 19, 2022, Plaintiffs also allege that Defendants’ first attempt to treat the Property for pests did not occur until May 19, 2023, ten months later, and that although Defendants were notified that the pest control treatment did not take place, they did not re-attempt to treat the Property for pests until after Defendants were issued a notice by LADPH, on October 19, 2023, fifteen months after being notified of the infestation. We are therefore unpersuaded by Defendants’ point about the five-month delay before July 19, 2024, since we may conclude from Defendants’ inaction over the subsequent fifteen months that Plaintiffs have pled in the Complaint that Defendants permitted the infestation to continue despite having knowledge of it.

 

In the Motion to Strike, Defendants observe that “[t]here are few cases where punitive damages, based on a conscious disregard of the rights and safety of others are established where no intentional torts are alleged.” (Motion p. 7.) They argue “[n]otably, these allegations arise to no more than negligence, if even at all, without any facts describing despicable conduct by the Defendants” and “[a]s an initial matter, punitive damages do not apply to negligence based causes of action.” (Ibid.)

 

Courts have held that “to establish malice, it is not sufficient to show only that the defendant's conduct was negligent, grossly negligent or even reckless. There must be evidence that defendant acted with knowledge of the probable dangerous consequences to plaintiff's interests and deliberately failed to avoid these consequences.” (Flyer’s Body Shop Profit Sharing Plan v. Ticor Title Ins. Co. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 1149, 1155, internal citations omitted.) “Conduct which may be characterized as unreasonable, negligent, grossly negligent or reckless does not satisfy the highly culpable state of mind warranting punitive damages. Conduct which warrants punitive damages must be of such severity or shocking character as warrants the same treatment as accorded to willful misconduct—conduct in which defendant intends to cause harm.” (Woolstrum v. Mailloux (1983) 141 Cal.App.3d Supp. 1, 10, emphasis original.)

 

The Complaint prays for punitive damages for only its second cause of action for tortious breach of the warranty of habitability, its third cause of action for negligence, its fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, and its fifth cause of action for private nuisance. Of these, only the third cause of action for negligence is a negligence-based cause of action. “A nuisance may be either a negligent or an intentional tort. If the latter, then exemplary damages are recoverable.” (Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 920.) Because punitive damages are sought on the fifth cause of action for private nuisance, it follows that the Complaint brings it as an intentional tort. We agree, however, that the third cause of action for negligence cannot support the recovery of punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion to Strike only as to paragraphs 44 and 70 of the Complaint and DENIES the Motion to Strike as to the other portions of the Complaint.

 

  1. Leave to Amend

 

“Where the defect raised by a¿motion¿to¿strike¿or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave¿to¿amend¿is routinely and¿liberally¿granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) “[An order granting a motion to strike] is analogous to an order sustaining a demurrer.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303.) 

 

Although Plaintiff has not amended the Complaint in this case, the only portion of the Complaint which we strike is the prayer for punitive damages only as they are sought by the third cause of action for negligence. Because causes of action for negligence cannot support punitive damages, we do not see any possible amendment that could cure this defect.

 

Accordingly, this court does not grant leave to amend.
