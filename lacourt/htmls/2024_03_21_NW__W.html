DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20VECV00691    Hearing Date: March 21, 2024    Dept: W

SPOTLIGHT TICKET MANAGEMENT, INC. v. EBAY INC., et al.

 

STUBHUB INC.’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION SET 10

 

Date of Hearing:        March 21, 2024                                 Trial Date:       April 29, 2024

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        20VECV00691

 

Moving Party:            Defendant/Cross-Complainant Stubhub, Inc.  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff/Cross Defendant Spotlight Ticket Management, Inc.

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Kuwahara Decl. ¶10.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Spotlight Ticket Management, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) alleges that it had an agreement to serve as a member of the StubHub Affiliate Program (the “Affiliate Program”) for Defendants eBay, Inc. (“eBay”) and StubHub, Inc. (“StubHub”) (collectively “Defendants”). Plaintiff’s role was to direct consumers to Defendants’ ticket platform in exchange for commissions on orders. Plaintiff alleges that it was not being paid commissions according to the terms and conditions of the Affiliate Program because StubHub was underreporting and misrepresenting commissionable transactions owed to Plaintiff. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants interfered with Plaintiff’s contract, contractual interests, and business interests with non-party American Express (“Amex”), which hired Plaintiff to manage its own ticketing platform as part of a concierge service for Amex’s cardholders. More specifically, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants instructed Amex not to share data detailing ticket purchases facilitated through StubHub, and this data helped Plaintiff to discover the underpayment of money owed to them from Defendants.

 

On August 28, 2020, Plaintiff filed its original complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for breach of contract; breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; unjust enrichment; quantum meruit; promissory estoppel; tortious interference; and unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business practices. Plaintiff’s operative Third Amended Complaint was filed January 23, 2023.

 

On March 30, 2023, Stubhub, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Spotlight Ticket Management, Inc., Anthony Knopp, Kenneth Hanscom, Joseph Greiner, Jen-Ban Ho, and Matthew Huff for fraud, trade libel, breach of contract, and unjust enrichment.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

Stubhub Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Production Set 10 is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Stubhub, Inc. moves to compel Plaintiff Spotlight Ticket Management, Inc. to provide further discovery responses to Request for Production Set 10, specifically to produce the final settlement agreement between Spotlight and Aspire (RFP No. 151), the draft settlement agreements between Spotlight and Aspire (RFP No. 152), and the communications between Spotlight and Aspire regarding the Kevin Bruce declaration (RFP No. 153). Stubhub makes the motion on the grounds that Spotlight has no basis to withhold the documents under the mediation privilege as it claims as the documents StubHub seeks go directly to witness credibility and communications between Spotlight and Aspire cannot be protected by attorney-client privilege.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) The motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) The motion must also be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of any issue presented by it. (See CCP §§ 2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).) Finally, a motion to compel further responses to inspection demands must be accompanied by a separate document that sets forth each item to which further response is requested and the factual and legal reasons for compelling it. (CRC, rule 3.1345(a).)

 

Stubhub contends it seeks basic, critical information related to the settlement agreement that Spotlight entered into with Aspire Lifestyles (Americas) Inc.—a company Spotlight sued and accused of colluding with StubHub. Stubhub also seeks relevant communications regarding a declaration signed by an Aspire employee that Spotlight obtained as a result of the settlement. Stubhub first argues Spotlight’s say so that the mediation privilege applies does not make it true. As the party claiming the privilege, the burden is on Spotlight to establish that the mediation privilege applies. But Spotlight’s responses do no such thing. Moreover, even if the mediation privilege applied, the final settlement agreement is carved out and must be produced. (See Cassel v. Superior Ct. (2011) 51 Cal. 4th 113, 124.) Lastly, Stubhub argues the objections are meritless as the evidence is clearly relevant and these requests expressly seek documents that were shared between Spotlight and Aspire. To be protected, a document must be a “confidential communication between client and lawyer.” (Evid. Code §954.)

 

In opposition, Spotlight contends after StubHub filed the instant motion to compel, the parties met and conferred. All that remains at issue now is whether Spotlight needs to produce (1) the final Spotlight-Aspire settlement agreement, which vests jurisdiction in the mediator – and the mediator alone – as to its satisfaction, enforcement and finality, and (2) an unredacted version of a single communication between Spotlight and Aspire made outside the presence of the mediator where the mediator remained engaged and the communication related specifically to carrying out the directives of the mediator. Spotlight notes it has produced a redacted version where communications not directly related to the mediation are unredacted.

 

As for the remaining issues, Spotlight argues the final settlement agreement is by its terms confidential and is protected by Evidence Code section 1119. StubHub’s proposed interpretation of the statute would render any and all settlement agreements discoverable by third-parties to them, effectively rendering them barely more protected than government-based public records. Moreover, StubHub should not be able to conduct a fishing expedition into a highly confidential document, as it can determine whether Kevin Bruce or the Aspire PMK are biased by asking them questions at the depositions. However, if the court determines that the agreement should be produced, then only a redacted version of the agreement should be produced and designated as “highly confidential.” Lastly, the single, redacted communication at issue is not discoverable beyond what has already been produced because even though it took place after the settlement agreement was executed,  the mediator remained engaged and the redacted communications are specifically related to the mediation process itself.

 

The court finds the documents sought by RFPs No. 151 discoverable. First, as noted by Stubhub themselves, Evidence Code section 1123 provides for the disclosure of a written settlement agreement prepared and executed in the course of mediation. As such, executed settlement agreements are discoverable but not drafts. Nor has Stubhub demonstrated good cause for drafts of the settlement agreement. The court notes discovery into this specific instance would not essentially render all confidential settlement agreements to be available as if they were the subject of a FOIA request as Spotlight contends here. The discovery sought is relevant and falls within the exception under the Evidence Code. As noted by Stubhub, this agreement resolved the litigation between Spotlight and Aspire where Spotlight alleged that Aspire and StubHub engaged in a “diversion scheme” to interfere with Spotlight’s relationship with American Express and this is the same “scheme” that Spotlight is suing StubHub for in this present litigation.

 

As for RFP No. 153, Stubhub is ordered to explain how the single communication regarding the Declaration of Kevin Bruce is not materially related to the mediation as mediation confidentiality protects communications and writings if they are materially related to, and foster, the mediation, and it is to be applied where the writing, or statement would not have existed but for a mediation communication, negotiation, or settlement discussion. (Wimsatt v. Superior Court (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 137, 160.)

 

As for how the documents are produced, Spotlight is to produce the documents with the designation of “Highly Confidential” under the protective order entered in this case.

 

Accordingly, Stubhub Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Production Set 10 is GRANTED, in part.


Case Number: 20VECV01388    Hearing Date: March 21, 2024    Dept: W

MARK A. LIKER v. ELIEZER APPEL, et al.

 

demurrer to the second amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        March 22, 2024                                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        20VECV01388

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Cochran, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Liker

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Mark A. Liker alleges Defendant Eliezer Appel, in his capacity as a California licensed attorney, instructed Plaintiff to purchase certain real property for $250,000.00 to Defendant Cochran Inc. and promised Plaintiff a return on his investment as well as plaintiff would take ownership of the property or that the property would secure Plaintiff’s investment. Plaintiff further alleges Eliezer Appel concealed from Plaintiff the fact that plaintiff did not own the property or the company that took title to the property that is, the Defendant Lockhart Development, Inc., and that Eliezer Appel took Plaintiff’s investment for his own gain.

 

On November 24, 2020, Plaintiff Mark A. Liker filed a complaint against Defendants Eliezer Appel, Lockhart Development, Inc., and Cochran Inc. asserting a single cause of action for fraud. Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint on August 30, 2023, asserting new causes of action – breach of contract, negligent misrepresentation, intentional misrepresentation, conspiracy to commit fraud, and breach of attorney fiduciary duty/duty of loyalty. Liker added Knolton Inc. as Defendant Doe 1.

 

On February 11, 2021, Lockhart Development, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Mark A. Liker asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Written Contract; (2) Intentional or Negligent Interference with Contractual Relations; (3) Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (5) Declaratory Relief; (6) Implied Contractual Indemnity; and (7) Slander of Title.

 

On October 20, 2022, Appel filed a cross-complaint against Liker, the California Neurological Institute, and Avi Marciano asserting causes of action for (1) Violation of Physician’s Medical Practice Act (MPA); (2) Undue Influence (CA Welfare & Institutions Code 15610.70); (3) Negligent Infliction of Extreme Emotional Distress (NIED); (4) Intentional Infliction of Extreme Emotional Distress (IIED); (5) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; and (6) Civil Extortion.     

 

This action is related to Marc A. Larocque v. Christine Larocque Franz, et al. (LC105233).

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Cochran, Inc.’s Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

discussion

 

Defendant Cochran, Inc. demurs to the SAC on the grounds Plaintiff has not stated a claim for fraud, the court has issued a stay, and Plaintiff has not stated a claim for breach of contract.[1]

 

Fraud

 

Defendant Cochran, Inc. demurs to the fraud causes of action on the grounds Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to support a claim of intentional or negligent misrepresentation.

 

“ ‘The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or “scienter”); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638, quoting 5 Witkin, Summary of Cal. Law (9th ed. 1988) Torts, § 676, p. 778.) The elements of a claim for negligent misrepresentation are “‘[M]isrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, and with intent to induce another's reliance on the fact misrepresented; ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation by the party to whom it was directed; and resulting damage… [citation omitted]” (Hydro-Mill Co. Inc. v. Hayward, Tilton & RolappIns. Associates, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1154 [citation omitted].)  “Causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud and, therefore, each element must be pleaded with specificity.  [Citations.]”  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.) 

 

Similar to the other defendants demurrers previously ruled on, Cochran, Inc. argues Plaintiff has failed to show particular acts and elements of fraud necessary for its claim as it pertains to Cochran, Inc. and the allegations set forth by plaintiff with respect to the misrepresentation of ownership fly in the face of the public record. Moreover, other portions of the fraud causes of action are conclusory and contradictory, including there was no lien in favor of Roshmore.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the allegations of fraud read in conjunction with the alter ego, agency and conspiracy allegations in the SAC satisfy the elements for fraud as enumerated in in Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631 and elements for conspiracy to commit fraud as detailed above in Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 1105.

 

Like the demurrers brought by Appel, Broederlow and Roshmore Development Inc., the court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. The SAC sufficiently alleges facts to recover for misrepresentation under an alter ego theory against Cochran. Specifically, the SAC alleges Defendants Appel and Broederlow, as officers and/or owners, on behalf of Cochran, Lockhart, and Roshmore, are alter egos of Appel and acted in concert to execute deed transfers and record a fraudulent deed of trust. These alter ego allegations are sufficient to support a claim against Cochran.

 

Accordingly, the demurrers to the second and third causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

Stay

 

Defendant Cochran next demurs to the SAC on the grounds the court’s ruling on the motion to stay pending appeal (Exh. A) specifically limits further proceedings in the case except for a) administrative matters b) collection of attorney’s fees and c) challenging pending pleadings, i.e., the pending demurrers etc.

 

As previously noted, the court allowed the parties to perfect the pleadings while being stayed, which would include demurrers to those pleadings.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendant Cochran demurs to the complaint on the grounds Liker has not alleged the requisite elements for breach of contract. Defendant Cochran contends amongst other things, he fails to indicate who he had a contract with nor has he attached the contract to the SAC.

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. As noted above, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged alter ego allegations against Cochran. Moreover, the complaint alleges the parties entered into a contract whereby Liker agreed to invest $250,000 of Liker’s money into the Cass Property and transferred the funds and in return, would receive ownership and be made an officer of Lockhart. The Defendants are alleged to have breached that agreement by never providing ownership or making Plaintiff an officer of Lockhart.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract is OVERRULED.
