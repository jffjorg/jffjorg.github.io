DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19NWCV00577    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: C

HERNANDEZ v. KIA MOTORS AMERICA, INC.

CASE NO.: 19NWCV00577

HEARING:  December 10, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE: Raul A. Sahagun

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED, in part, in the amount of $5,982.41. Plaintiff is therefore awarded costs in the total amount of $14,118.51.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. On July 19, 2019, Plaintiff Emelin Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Kia Motors America, Inc (“Defendant”). On September 3, 2021, Plaintiff filed the Second Amended Complaint (SAC). The SAC alleges Plaintiff purchased a 2017 Kia Optima LX from Defendant. The SAC alleges the vehicle had a defective transmission and engine, and that Defendant failed to repair and repurchase the vehicle in violation of the express warranties.

 

On August 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Settlement. On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Memorandum of Costs, which seeks $20,100.92 without attorney’s fees. The Motion for Attorney’s Fees is currently pending.

 

Defendant now moves to tax costs. Defendant seeks to strike all costs sought, or in the alternative, to tax costs in the sum of $15,901.28, leaving a balance of $4,199.64.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a) sets forth the items allowable as costs while Section 1033.5, subdivision (b) details the items that are not allowable as costs. “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion.” (Acosta v. SI Corp. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1370, 1380.) Costs must be both “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation,” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5 subd. (c)(1)), and reasonable in amount. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).) 

 

Generally, the burden on a motion to tax costs depends upon whether the costs appear proper. “If the items appear to be proper charges the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the [prevailing party], and the burden of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable is upon the objecting party.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.) “The court's first determination, therefore, is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item, and whether it appears proper on its face. If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show them to be unnecessary or unreasonable.” (Ibid.) 

 

Discussion

 

          Applicability of Code of Civil Procedure section 1033, subdivision (a)

 

The Court initially discusses Defendant’s argument that the Memorandum of Costs should be stricken in its entirety because the settlement of $31,000.00 could have been recovered in a limited case under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033, subdivision (a).

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1033, subdivision (a) states the following:

 

“Costs or any portion of claimed costs shall be as determined by the court in its discretion in a case other than a limited civil case in accordance with Section 1034 where the prevailing party recovers a judgment that could have been rendered in a limited civil case.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1033, subd. (a).)

 

Plaintiff argues that Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) requires the Court to award reasonably incurred costs and expenses, and that Code of Civil Procedure section 1033, subdivision (a) does not apply. Plaintiff argues that even if section 1033, subdivision (a) was applicable, courts have held that a trial court should award costs to a plaintiff who recovers less than the jurisdictional amount for an unlimited civil case when he or she reasonably and in good faith initiated the action believing that the ultimate recovery would exceed the jurisdictional limit, citing Chavez v. City of Los Angeles, (2010) 47 Cal. 4th 970, 987. Plaintiff reasonably believed that based on the evidence, Plaintiff could recover an award over $25,000 when she filed the case in 2019. (Oliva Decl., ¶ 3.) A statutory repurchase of the vehicle would have been in the amount of $30,473.17. (Oliva Decl., ¶ 4.) Recovery of civil penalty damages would have been $60,946.34, and Plaintiff could have recovered registration payments and prejudgment interest had this case proceeded to trial. (Olivia Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.) All of these damages were requested in the prayer of the Complaint.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033, subdivision (a), the Court is not required to decline fees and costs to a party who files action as an unlimited civil case but ultimately recovers an amount that could have been recovered in limited civil case; it merely gives judge discretion to do so if the judge finds denial is appropriate. (See Cruz v. Fusion Buffet, Inc (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 221, 231-32.) (Italics added.)

 

The Court initially notes that the jurisdictional amount for a limited civil case was statutorily changed to amounts exceeding $35,000 in 2024—prior to this change, the amount was $25,000. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 85, subd. (a).) Plaintiff thus must have reasonably believed she could recover over the previous amount of $25,000 when the Complaint was filed in 2019. The Court finds that Plaintiff was reasonable in filing the action as an unlimited civil case based on her belief at the time. (Olivia Decl., ¶¶ 3-7.) The Court thus exercises its discretion in allowing Plaintiff to seek costs despite Code of Civil Procedure section 1033, subdivision (a).

 

Applicability of Civil Code section 1794 subdivision (d) and Item 16

“Other Costs”

 

As noted in the foregoing legal standard, motions to tax costs are generally governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5. Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot recover costs for postage, printing, and transportation expenses listed under Item 16 as “other” expenses because these types of costs are disallowed under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5. In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Civil Code section 1794 subdivision (d) applies instead, which allows for a much broader scope of recovery for prevailing plaintiffs in a Song-Beverly action. Plaintiff argues that these “other” costs are recoverable as “costs and expenses” under Civil Code section 1794 subdivision (d). Plaintiffs cite Jensen v. BMW (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112 to support this contention.

 

Civil Code section 1794 subdivision (d) provides the following:

 

“If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” 

 

(Civ. Code § 1794, subd. (d).)  

 

The Court finds that the Jensen court did not endorse as broad an interpretation as Plaintiff’s claim. In Jensen, “[t]he sole issue is whether the plaintiff was entitled to expert witness fees under section 1794, subdivision (d)” as an “expense” rather than a “cost.” (Jensen v. BMW (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112, 137.) The trial court had disallowed expert witness fees on the ground that they are not provided for in Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5. (Ibid.) The Court of Appeal noted that section 1033.5, which defines items allowable as “costs,” expressly precludes recovery of “[f]ees of experts not ordered by the court. . .except when expressly authorized by law.” (Ibid.; See Code Civ. Proc. 1033.5, subd. (b)(1).) The Court found that the Legislature intended the word “expenses” to cover items not included in the definitions of costs. (Ibid.) The Court then turn to the legislative history to determine the scope of the term “expenses”. (Ibid.) According to an analysis by the Assembly Committee on Labor, Employment, and Consumer Affairs, “costs and expenses” was added to Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) because “[i]ndigent consumers are often discouraged from seeking legal redress due to court costs. The addition of awards of ‘costs and expenses’ by the court to the consumer to cover such out-of-pocket expenses as filing fees, expert witness fees, marshall’s fees, etc., should open the litigation process to everyone.” (Ibid.) (Italics added.) Based on this legislative history, the Jensen court determined that “the Legislature exercised its power to permit the recovery of expert witness fees by prevailing buyers under the Act.” (Ibid.) Therefore, under Jensen, Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) does not allow entitle Plaintiff to any cost and expense reasonably incurred.

 

Here, Plaintiff has failed to cite any other authorities to show that costs for postage, printing, and transportation expenses sought are also expressly authorized by statute or that any court has interpreted Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) to provide for these costs. Accordingly, the Court finds that Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 bars recovery of “other” costs for postage, printing, and non-deposition transportation expenses. (See Code Civ. Proc. 1033.5, subd. (b)(3); see also Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn., supra, 19 Cal.App.4th at 775-76 [“Routine expenses for local travel by attorneys or other firm employees are not reasonably necessary to the conduct of litigation.”].)  

 

As to the other costs under Item 16, for CourtCall and LA Court Connect reservations, hearing transcripts, mediation, and “expert costs for attendance at vehicle inspection,” Defendant argues they should be taxed. (See Memorandum of Costs, Attachment 16a.) Although, Defendant provides no specific reasons for why these costs should be taxed. Plaintiff has neither provided reasons to justify the costs other than citing Civil Code section 1794 subdivision (d). The Court finds that the costs for the reservations, non-court ordered hearing transcripts, mediation are not recoverable under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5. The Court further finds the expert costs were not ordered by the court and not substantiated under section 8(b) of the Memorandum of Costs. The Court thus taxes all “other” costs under Item 16, in the amount of $5,982.41.

         

Item 1 “Filing and Motion Fees”

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff is only entitled to $460.52 for filing the Complaint and $12.57 for filing the notice of settlement. Defendant argues the remaining $2,782.18 in sought fees are unrecoverable because the motions and oppositions were unnecessary. Defendant identifies the following unnecessary motions and oppositions: 1) Plaintiff’s opposition to Defendant’s Demurrer, which was sustained, 2) Plaintiff’s Motions for Leave to Amend, which were unnecessary because Plaintiff filed a proper Complaint, 3) Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel deposition of a person most qualified (PMQ), which was denied as untimely, 4) Plaintiff’s earlier Motion to Compel deposition of a person most qualified (PMQ) was withdrawn, 5) Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Motions, 6) Plaintiff’s Motions in Limine, which were never heard, 5) various courtesy copy and service charges.

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant cites no authority in support of its argument. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s Demurrer and refusal to stipulate to a leave to amend required Plaintiff to file an opposition and a Motion for Leave to Amend. As to the Motions to Compel PMQ, Defendant withheld the witness, forcing Plaintiff to file the Motion. Plaintiff withdrew the earlier version of the Motion to Compel based on Defendant’s agreement to produce the witness for deposition after the Motion was filed. As to the ex parte motions, Plaintiff had to file the motions due to Defendant’s obstruction of discovery. Defendant did not oppose the ex parte motions. As to the Motions in Limine, they were necessarily filed in December 2020 on the statutory deadline in connection with the first trial date of December 30, 2020.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has provided ample justification for its filing and motion fees. Defendant has not provided authority that the Court should tax costs purely based on the fact Plaintiff was unsuccessful on some of the motions. The Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to costs for such motions where they were filed in good faith, but unsuccessful. (See Rozanova v. Uribe (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 392, 400 (affirming trial court’s decision to award costs for unsuccessful motions that were filed “in good faith.”).) The Court thus denies the motion to tax costs for  Item 1 “Filing and Motion Fees.”

 

Items 4 “Deposition Costs” and 5 “Service of Process” Costs

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff is not entitled to recover the costs of taking eight unnecessary depositions and serving unnecessary deposition subpoenas in the amount of $10,003.74. Defendant concedes that Plaintiff should recover costs for service of the Complaint, but not for service of the deposition subpoenas. Defendant argues Plaintiff could have served the deposition subpoenas Defendant electronically, avoiding the costs.

 

Plaintiff argues that three deponents were the Persons Most Qualified and custodian of records for each of the three repairing facilities, and the four other deponents were technicians that performed repair at one of the three repairing facilities. (Olivia Decl., ¶¶ 8-10.) The deponents testified about the repairs performed on the Subject Vehicle and authenticated the records from each of the dealerships.  Thus, each deposition was relevant for the case where Plaintiff alleged that Defendant and its dealership had failed to conform the Subject Vehicle to warranty within a reasonable number of repair attempts. Plaintiff argues that as non-parties, the deponents were required to have the deposition subpoenas served on them personally, unless they consented to being represented by Defendant’s counsel beforehand. Thus, Plaintiff served the initial subpoenas to the deponents, and once Defendant’s representation of the deponents was confirmed, Plaintiff served the deponents electronically.

 

The Court finds deposition costs and service of process costs are allowable under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivisions (a)(3)-(4). Defendant points to no authority to support their argument that these deposition costs were not “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation.” The Court thus denies the motion to tax costs for Items 4 “Deposition Costs” and 5 “Service of Process” costs.


Case Number: 23NWCV00830    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: C

GOMEZ-DELLEPIANE v. KAISER PERMANENTE

CASE NO.: 23NWCV00830

HEARING:  December 10, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  Raul A. Sahagun

 

#3

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Zhai’s Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Zhai to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a medical malpractice action. On March 16, 2023, Plaintiff Ornella Gomez-Dellepiane (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Kaiser Permanente, Jing Zhai C.N.M., and Does 1 to 20. The Complaint alleges Zhai incorrectly placed an intrauterine device into Plaintiff’s abdomen, causing serious pain and eventually requiring her to get it surgically removed. The Complaint alleges one cause of action for medical malpractice.

 

On October 11, 2023, Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. as Doe 1.

 

On October 6, 2023, default was entered against Defendant Zhai.

 

Defendant Zhai now moves to set aside entry of default, asserting that Plaintiff failed to effectuate service of process via substituted service. The Motion is unopposed.

 

Legal Standard

 

“The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (d).)

 

A motion to vacate a default and set aside a judgment is within the discretion of the trial court. (Strathvale Holdings v. E.B.H. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 1241, 1249.) “Lack of personal jurisdiction renders a judgment (or default) void, and the default may be directly challenged at any time.” (Id. at p. 1250.) “When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction.” (Ibid.) “‘A summons is the process by which a court acquires personal jurisdiction over a defendant in a civil action’ [citation], and a defendant has an absolute right to demand that process be issued against him in a manner prescribed by law.” (Mannesmann DeMag, Ltd. v. Superior Court¿(1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 1118, 1122.)¿“As a general rule, however, the provisions of the rules governing service of process are to be liberally construed.” (Ibid.)  

 

“A plaintiff may serve individual defendants through substitute service when they cannot be personally served with reasonable diligence.” (Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.) Substituted service is governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20, subdivision (b), which states the following: “If a copy of the summons and complaint cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered to the person to be served. . .a summons may be served by leaving a copy of  the summons and complaint at the person’s dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.”

 

“[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that . . . service was proper.” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441.) “[A] registered process server’s declaration establishes a presumption affecting the burden of producing evidence of the facts stated in the declaration.” (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff filed the Proof of Service of Summons (POS) on July 18, 2023. The POS states that Defendant Zhai was served via substituted service at her place of business. (POS, ¶ 5.) The attached Declaration of Diligence states that the first attempt of service, on April 20, 2023, was at 10820 183rd St, Cerritos, CA 90703 (“Cerritos Medical Offices”), where an unnamed employee told the server that documents must go to 9400 Rosecrans Ave, Bellflower, CA 90706 (“Bellflower Medical Offices”). (Decl. Due Diligence, ¶ 2.)  At the second attempt, on April 21, 2023, no one was authorized to accept service at the Bellflower Office. (Ibid.) At the third attempt, on April 24, 2023, Anna Munos was served as a person authorized to accept on behalf of Defendant Jing. (Ibid.) Proof of mailing was also attached.

 

Defendant Zhai argues entry of default should be set aside where substituted service was not properly effectuated. Defendant Zhai declares that the Bellflower Medical Offices was not her usual place of business. (Zhai Decl., ¶ 5.) Defendant Zhai declares that from March 15, 2022 to present, the Cerritos Medical Office has been her usual place of business. (Zhai Decl., ¶ 4.) Counsel for Defendant Kaiser Permanente Foundation Health Plan, Inc. was assigned in November 2023. (Rector Decl., ¶ 3.) Defendant Zhai declares she was not aware anyone was trying to serve her with documents at the Cerritos Medical Offices or any other location. (Zhai Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

Upon reviewing the record, counsel discovered Plaintiff entered default against Defendant Zhai, and provided her with copies of the service documents. (Zhai Decl., ¶¶ 6, 8.) Subsequently representing Defendant Zhai, counsel met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel in an attempt to set aside the default entered, but no resolution was met. (Rector Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff was not properly served via substituted service. Defendant Zhai has declared that the Bellflower Medical Offices is not her usual place of business and that she was never made aware of such lawsuit until counsel contacted her. (Zhai Decl., ¶¶ 5-8.) The trial court has a large amount of discretion to set aside a default judgment. (Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 525.) Courts favor “orders excusing defaults and permitting controversies to be heard upon their merits.” (Ibid.)  

 

The Court therefore exercises its discretion and GRANTS Defendant Zhai’s Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default.


Case Number: 23NWCV01546    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: C

CHANGYA NEWMATERIAL TECHNOLOGY, LTD v. E SOLUTIONS

CASE NO.: 23NWCV01546

HEARING:  December 10, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE: Raul A. Sahagun

 

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Amend Judgment is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a breach of contract and debt collection action. On May 18, 2023, Plaintiff Changya Newmaterial Technology, LTD. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against E Solutions and Does 1 to 10 (“E Solutions”). The Complaint alleges E Solutions buys goods from Plaintiff. E Solutions allegedly failed to pay for goods and owes Plaintiff in the amount of $173,780.72. The Complaint alleges the following four causes of action: 1) Breach of Contract, 2) Account Stated (Invoices and Goods Ordered), 3) Implied Contract (Goods Ordered), and 4) Implied Contract (Packaging Materials).

 

Default was entered against E Solutions on July 25, 2023. On October 17, 2023, default judgment was entered against E Solutions in the amount of $204,540.45. (See 10/17/23 Judgment.)

 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff obtained a Writ of Execution against E Solutions.

 

Plaintiff now moves to amend the default judgment to include an additional defendant under Code of Civil Procedure section 187. Plaintiff seeks to collect the judgment against Elias B Escobar Lupercio (“Lupercio”), who is doing business as (dba) E Solutions, because there is no separate legal entity. The motion is unopposed.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 187 states the following: “When jurisdiction is, by the Constitution or this Code, or by any other statute, conferred on a Court or judicial officer, all the means necessary to carry it into effect are also given; and in the exercise of this jurisdiction, if the course of proceeding be not specifically pointed out by this Code or the statute, any suitable process or mode of proceeding may be adopted which may appear most conformable to the spirit of this Code.”

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 187, a judge may amend a judgement to add additional judgment debtors. (Butler America, LLC v. Aviation Assurance Company, LLC (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 136, 145.) “The decision to grant an amendment in such circumstances lies in the sound discretion of the trial court. ‘The greatest liberality is to be encouraged in the allowance of such amendments in order to see that justice is done.’ [Citations.]” (Greenspan v. LADT, LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 486, 508.)

 

Discussion

 

The Court finds it has no personal jurisdiction over Lupercio because there is no proof of service indicating Plaintiffs personally served Lupercio with the Summons and Complaint. Lupercio is not a defendant in this action. Because the Court lacks personal jurisdiction over Lupercio, the Court may not amend the judgment to add Lupercio as a defendant or judgment debtor.

 

The Court thus denies Plaintiff’s Motion to Amend Judgment.


Case Number: 23NWCV03539    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: C

THEE AGULA INC., ET AL. v. RONAK PATEL, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV03539

HEARINGS:  December 10, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE: Raul A. Sahagun

 

#6

TENTATIVE ORDERS

 

Defendant Patel’s Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Order Conditionally Directing Clerk to File Entry of Default and Enter Defaults Nunc Pro Tunc is DENIED.

 

Defendant Ronak Patel to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a legal malpractice action. On November 1, 2023, Plaintiffs Thee Aguila Inc. and Rocio Rosales (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Ronak Patel, the Law Practice of Ronak Patel, and Does 1 to 50. On November 6, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC). The FAC alleges Patel negligently represented Plaintiffs in a previous lawsuit, leading to an unfavorable judgment. The FAC alleges the following causes of action: 1) Negligence (by client), 2) Breach of Contract (1) (by client), 3) Breach of Contract (2) (by client), 4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty (by payor), 5) Conversion (by payor), 6) Money Had and Received (by all Plaintiffs), 7) Conversion, 8) Breach of Fiduciary Duty (by client), 9) Unlawful Business Practices under Business and Professions Code § 17203 (Violation of California Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.16(e)(1)) (by client), 10) Accounting (by payor), 11) Unlawful Business Practices under Business and Professions Code § 17203 (Violation of California Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.15(d)(4) (by payor), and 12) Accounting (by client).

 

On February 13, 2024, defaults were entered against Defendants Ronak Patel and Law Practice of Ronak Patel.

 

Defendant Ronak Patel (“Patel”) now moves to set aside the entered defaults under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) and requests an order that the deadline for his responsive pleading shall be nine calendar days from the date of the Court’s order. Plaintiffs concurrently move for an order conditionally directing clerk to file entry of default and enter defaults nunc pro tunc. The Court will address both motions.

 

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) states the following:

 

The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant Patel’s Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default

 

Patel moves to set aside the entered defaults against himself and the Law Offices of Ronak Patel under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d). Initially, Patel notes that the Law Offices of Ronak Patel is Patel’s business name and is not a legally separate entity—thus, he seeks to set aside both entered defaults. (Patel Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiffs served Patel with the FAC on December 22, 2023, and thus, the deadline to file a responsive pleading was January 22, 2024. (Patel Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.) On January 22, 2024, Patel filed a Declaration of Demurring or Moving Party in Support of Automatic Extension under Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.41 and 435.5 (“Extension Declaration”). (Patel Decl., ¶ 8; Exh. 1.) Patel argues that the new deadline for his responsive pleading was extended to February 21, 2024, and thus, when default was entered on February 13, 2024, Patel still had 9 days to file a responsive pleading. (Patel Decl., ¶ 9.) Patel thus argues that the entered defaults are void and should be set aside as clerical error.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the defaults should not be set aside. Plaintiffs argue that Patel’s Extension Declaration was insufficient such that there was no automatic 30-day extension. Civil Code of Procedure sections 430.41(a)(2) and 435.5(a)(2) (governing demurrers and motions to strike) require that the declaration explain the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer at least 5 days prior to the due date of the responsive pleading. The Extension Declaration states that Patel expected Plaintiffs’ counsel would agree to provide a 15-day extension under California Rules of Court 3.110, and that Patel “waited to meet and confer until I was more ready to discuss the issues meaningfully.” Further, Plaintiffs argue the Extension Declaration’s statements that Patel attempted to meet and confer regarding a demurrer or motion to strike prior to the January 22, 2024 responsive pleading due date are false. Plaintiffs’ counsel was never contacted by either Patel or the Law Offices of Ronak Patel regarding filing a demurrer or motion to strike prior to January 22, 2024. (Wenyao Decl., ¶ 3-4.) Neither did either Defendant ask Plaintiffs’ counsel to provide an extension of time to file a responsive pleading at any time. (Wenyao Decl., ¶ 9.) Lastly, Plaintiffs argue the issue of the legal status of the Law Offices of Ronak Patel should be determined when default judgment is entered against both Defendants.

 

In reply, Patel argues that the facts support finding of a clerical error that warrants setting aside the entered defaults. Further, Plaintiffs’ opposition is essentially a motion for sanctions for improperly certified documents, which cannot be heard at this juncture. Patel argues that Plaintiffs should be estopped from arguing that there was no 30-day extension where they attempted to refile requests for defaults on March 3, 2024, essentially admitted there was an extension. (See Plaintiffs’ Motion.)

 

“Section 473, subdivision (d), authorizes the trial court to “set aside any void judgment or order” upon a noticed motion. [Citations.] The motion may be made at any time. [Citations.] It is immaterial how the invalidity is called to the court's attention. [Citations.] A prematurely entered default is invalid, and any judgment entered after an invalid default is also invalid. . . A default may be entered only after the defendant has been served with a summons and has failed to answer or file other responsive papers within the time prescribed in the summons, or such further time as may be allowed.” (Schwab v. Southern California Gas Co. (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1320; see also Code Civ. Proc. § 585, subds. (a)-(c), (e).)

 

The Court finds that the defaults were prematurely entered. The Extension Declaration states that Patel reasonably expected Plaintiffs to stipulate to a 15-day extension under California Rules of Court, rule 3.110. (Patel Decl., ¶ 8; Exh. 1.) The Declaration states that, because Plaintiffs’ counsel “has either refused to respond or has not had time to respond to my request for the extension,” Patel was “not able to engage in a good faith meet and confer effort regarding my contemplated demurrer and motion to strike.” (Ibid.) The Court acknowledges that Plaintiffs contest the truth of the Declaration—Plaintiffs assert that Patel did not reach out regarding a potential demurrer or motion to strike, or regarding an extension to file a responsive pleading. (Wenyao Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, 9.) Without determining the credibility of the Declaration, the Court finds that, the  Declaration was timely filed on the last day to file a responsive pleading. Patel was thus entitled to an automatic 30-day extension under Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.41 and 435.5. The defaults were thus prematurely entered and should be vacated as void.

 

Therefore, the Court grants Defendant Patel’s Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Order Conditionally Directing Clerk to File Entry of Default and Enter Defaults Nunc Pro Tunc

 

Plaintiffs request relief in the event the Court sets aside the entered defaults against Defendants. Plaintiffs argue that on Sunday, March 3, 2024, Plaintiffs submitted another set of requests for entered defaults against Ronak Patel and The Law Practice of Ronak Patel to the clerk for electronic filing with payment. (Shatford IV Decl., ¶¶ 3-5; Exhs. A-C.) The clerk rejected both documents and did not enter any defaults. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs filed the requests in the event that Patel’s declaration provided him a 30-day extension to file a responsive pleading. Plaintiffs waited until after the 30-day extension deadline on February 20, 2024, to file the new requests for the entered defaults.

 

In opposition, Patel argues a nunc pro tunc order is a mechanism in which the Court corrects a mistake in the record that was improperly recorded. Such mechanism cannot be used to correct was actually happened (i.e., the clerk rejected the March 3, 2024 requests for entry of default). A nunc pro tunc order would be unjust under these circumstances where Plaintiffs have waited 6 months to seek relief. Further, if Plaintiffs disagreed with the clerk’s rejection of the requests, Plaintiffs were required to apply for ex parte relief under LASC Local Rules, rule 3.200—Plaintiffs failed to do so. Patel suggests the Court should order sua sponte appropriate sanctions.

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that LASC Local Rules, rule 3.200 does not bar this Motion. The rule merely states that a party “may apply for ex parte relief” (LASC Local Rules, rule 3.200.) Patel is not prejudiced by this error, and the court has inherent authority to order documents filed nunc pro tunc regardless of the rule. Patel has not met his burden in showing why a nunc pro tunc order would result in injustice. Lastly, Plaintiffs argue the request for sanctions is procedurally improper.

 

“[A] nunc pro tunc order is generally limited to correcting clerical errors; ‘a nunc pro tunc order cannot declare that something was done which was not done.’”

(People v. Borja (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 481, 485.)

 

The Court finds that an issuance of a pro nunc tunc order to instate the rejected requests for defaults of entry is unwarranted. There was no clerical error here. According to the Notice of Court Rejection of Electronic Filing, the requests for were rejected by the clerk because default was previously entered on February 12, 2024. (Shatford IV Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. C.) Plaintiffs are not entitled to a pro nunc tunc order merely because they filed the requests and paid the fee. The requests were properly rejected, and the Court may not rewrite what has already been done.

 

Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiffs’ Motion for Order Conditionally Directing Clerk to File Entry of Default and Enter Defaults Nunc Pro Tunc.


Case Number: 24NWCV00247    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: C

HERNANDEZ v.  BECHTOLD, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00247

HEARING:  December 10, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE: Raul A Sahagun

 

#8

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendants Kyler Jade Bechtold and The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints’ Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

 

Defendants to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a motor vehicle negligence action. On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff Mario Alberto Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Kyler Jade Bechtold, The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints, California Los Angeles Mission, and Does 1 to 50. The Complaint alleges that on May 4, 2022, Defendant Bechtold made an illegal U-turn and collided into Plaintiff’s vehicle, causing injury and property damage. The Complaint alleges the Defendant Bechtold acted in the scope of his employment with Defendants The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints and California Los Angeles Mission (“Employer Defendants”). The Complaint alleges Employer Defendants negligently hired Defendant Bechtold. The Complaint alleges the following against of action: 1) Negligence, 2) Negligence Per Se, 3) Statutory Liability, and 4) Punitive Damages.

 

Defendants Kyler Jade Bechtold and The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints (erroneously sued as The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints and California Los Angeles Mission) (collectively, “Defendants”) now move to strike the fourth cause of action for punitive damages (paragraphs 47 to 70) and paragraphs in the prayer for relief seeking punitive damages (paragraph C, line 22) and attorney’s fees (paragraph F, line 25).

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 436 permits trial courts to strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in a pleading: 

 

 “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: 

 (a)¿Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. 

 (b)¿Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

Parties must meet and confer “in person or by telephone” before filing a motion to strike. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 435.5(a).) Defense counsel attempted to call Plaintiff’s counsel, but they were unable to speak. (Magwood Decl., ¶ 2(a).) Defense counsel asked the office assistant or paralegal to have Plaintiff’s counsel call back. (Ibid.) After not receiving a call back, Defense counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer correspondence on February 22, 2024. (Magwood Decl., ¶ 2(b); Exh. A.) Defense counsel did not receive a response from Plaintiff’s counsel thereafter. (Magwood Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has not been met, but in the interests of judicial efficiency, the Court will continue to hear the merits of the Motion.

 

Discussion

 

          Punitive Damages

 

Defendants argue that the Complaint lacks factual allegations supporting a finding of malice, oppression, or fraud to support their prayer for punitive damages. The Complaint merely contains allegations that Defendants’ actions were carried out “recklessly, intentionally, carelessly, and maliciously,” and were conducted in conscious disregard for the rights and safety of others, but provides no specific allegations in support of these conclusions.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that there is a less strict pleading standard for a motion to strike punitive damages. Only allegations of fraud require a heightened pleading standard, and here, the punitive damages claim is not based on fraud. Plaintiff argues the Complaint alleges sufficient facts to support a prayer for punitive damages. Particularly, the Complaint alleges Defendant Bechtold consciously disregarded dangerous and probably consequences when he made an illegal U-turn onto oncoming traffic. (Compl., ¶¶ 57-63.) Further, Employer Defendants made the conscious decision to ignore the unfitness of its drivers despite the high likelihood of the probable dangerous consequences of causing harm to Plaintiff and the public. (Ibid.) Plaintiff is entitled to reasonable inferences based on the allegations.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that the only factual allegations of Defendant Bechtold’s conduct—negligently operating the subject vehicle while “driving at an unsafe speed and making an illegal U-turn directly in front of Plaintiff”—at most demonstrate mere negligence.

 

“‘In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.’ [Citation.]” (Today IV’s Inc. v. Los Angeles County MTA (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) Civil Code section 3294 provides that on a non-contract cause of action, a plaintiff may recover punitive damages when they prove “by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).) In a negligence action, plaintiffs must allege facts in the complaint demonstrating malice—conclusory allegations that simply characterize defendant's conduct as “malicious” are insufficient and subject to motion to strike. (See Smith v. Sup.Ct. (Bucher) (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1036, 1042 (finding, in a legal malpractice claim, plaintiff’s conclusory allegations that attorneys' conduct was “intentional, knowing, malicious, fraudulent, false and deceitful” and action in “conscious and knowing disregard of the interest and rights of plaintiff” were deficient as a matter of law to show entitlement to punitive damages).)

 

The Court finds the Complaint contains conclusory allegations of malice and oppression, which are insufficient to support a punitive damages award. A complaint must plead facts demonstrating malice or oppression, as opposed to characterizing allegations as malicious or oppressive. (See Smith, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1036, 1042.) While the Court agrees with Plaintiff that reasonable inferences made be made from the facts pled in evaluating a complaint, the Court finds such inferences may not be made to support a punitive damages claim. The Court thus grants the Motion to Strike the fourth cause of action for punitive damages and the prayer for punitive damages.

 

          Attorney’s Fees

 

Defendants argue that attorney’s fees are not recoverable unless provided for by contract, statute, or law. Defendants argue there is no contract between Plaintiff and Defendants. The Complaint identifies no statute authorizing recovery of attorney’s fees. Plaintiff does not address the issue of attorney’s fees in the opposition. Defendants reiterate their argument in reply.

 

“Except as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties; but parties to actions or proceedings are entitled to their costs, as hereinafter provided.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1021.) “Generally, it is clear, attorneys' fees are not recoverable in personal injury or wrongful death actions.” (Krouse v. Graham (1977) 19 Cal. 3d 59, 81.) 

 

The Court finds attorney’s fees are unsupported by any statute or contract between the parties. Thus, attorney’s fees should be stricken from the prayer in the Complaint.
