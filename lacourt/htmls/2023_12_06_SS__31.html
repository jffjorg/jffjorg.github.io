[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS 

 

Dept. 31 

1:30 p.m.

I. Background  

Plaintiff Nolvia Varela (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Eureka Realty Partners, Inc. (“Eureka”) and L.J. Russell Construction for damages arising from falling into a hole due to an alleged improperly maintained construction area.  

Defendant Eureka now moves for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff to dismiss Plaintiff’s action by failing to pay monetary sanctions in compliance with the monetary sanction portion of the Court’s June 15, 2023 Order pertaining to the Defendant’s motions to compel responses discovery and request for monetary sanction. The motion is unopposed.  

 

II. Motion for Terminating Sanctions  

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030 gives the court the discretion to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in a misuse of the discovery process.  A court may impose terminating sanctions by striking pleadings of the party engaged in misuse of discovery or entering default judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(d).)  A violation of a discovery order is sufficient for the imposition of terminating sanctions.  (Collison & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1620.)  Terminating sanctions are appropriate when a party persists in disobeying the court's orders.  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 795-796.)   

A terminating sanction is a "drastic measure which should be employed with caution."  (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at 793.)  "A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction."  (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.)  While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery."  (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at 793.)  "[A] court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations."  (Ibid.)  Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information.  (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971].) 

Here, the Court’s June 15, 2023 Order ordered Plaintiff to serve verified responses, without objections to Eureka’s form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and request for production of documents. (Min. Order, June 15, 2023.) Additionally, Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, were ordered to pay $824.95 to Eureka, by and through its counsel of record. (Ibid.) Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to pay the ordered monetary sanctions. Although not expressly stated, it appears that Plaintiff did in fact serve discovery responses, which was the substantive portion and purpose of  
Eureka’s motions to compel. Eureka requests terminating sanctions to dismiss Plaintiff’s Complaint with prejudice for not mailing the payment check that Plaintiff’s counsel stated he would send.  

However, “[d]iscovery sanctions are intended to remedy discovery abuse, not to punish the offending party. Accordingly, sanctions should be tailored to serve that remedial purpose, should not put the moving party in a better position than he would otherwise have been had he obtained the requested discovery, and should be proportionate to the offending party's misconduct.” (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 1215, 1223.) To impose terminating sanctions at this time would place Eureka in a better position because Plaintiff did serve the requested discovery, and to dismiss Plaintiff’s complaint with prejudice for failure to pay a monetary sanction would be grossly disproportionate to the type of misconduct here. Eureka’s Notice and moving papers request only the ultimate sanction and did not request a lesser sanction in the alternative. Therefore, the Court will not consider imposing a lesser sanction at this time.  

 

Accordingly, Eureka’s motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED.

I. Background 

On August 17, 2022, Plaintiff Isaac Cota, a minor, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem Christopher Cota (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant California Highway Patrol (“CHP”) and Sergeant Lee for damages arising from a motor vehicle v. bicycle incident. On February 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint naming Christopher Lang as Doe 1. 

On November 8, 2022, CHP filed its Answer to Plaintiff’s complaint. 

On October 17, 2023, CHP filed the instant motion seeking leave to file a permissive cross-complaint against Plaintiff for negligent entrustment, negligent supervision, negligence per se, negligence, equitable indemnity, contribution, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief.  

Any opposition was due on or before November 21, 2023. The motion is unopposed.  

  

II. Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint  

A cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the initial complaint or cross-complaint against the cross-complainant must be filed before or at the same time as the answer to the initial complaint or cross-complaint, which answer must be filed within 30 days of service of the complaint or cross-complaint.  (CCP §§ 412.20(a)(3), 428.50(a), 432.10.)  Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a trial date.  (CCP §428.50(b).)   

If a party fails to file a cross-complaint within the time limits described above, he or she must obtain permission from the court to file the cross-complaint.  (CCP §§ 426.50, 428.50(c).)  Leave to file a mandatory cross-complaint must be granted absent bad faith. (Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 99.)  Leave to file a permissive cross-complaint need only be granted in the interest of justice. (CCP §428.50(c).) Where the proposed cross-complaint arises out of the same transaction as plaintiff’s claim, the court must grant leave to file the cross-complaint so long as defendant is acting in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50.) 

Here, CHP argues that the incident on June 27, 2021 involved the Los Angeles County Sheriff’s Department requesting assistance from CHP East Los Angeles for an unpermitted cruising event on Whittier Blvd. that included hundreds of vehicles, pedestrians, and bicyclists which caused major disruptions to traffic flow and blocked access to local business. CHP avers the lead CHP patrol vehicle was turning left when Plaintiff, on a bicycle, turned in front of the CHP patrol vehicle and contact was made between the bicycle and patrol vehicle. Plaintiff did not request any medical attention and rode away. CHP requests leave for to cross-complaint against Plaintiff for not wearing a helmet and for the bicycle not being equipped with reflective lights or reflectors.  

The Trial date is currently set for February 14, 2024. CHP had video of the incident showing Plaintiff riding with other minors without a helmet and other circumstances of the incident, yet waited over a year after filing its Answer to assert a cross-complaint against Plaintiff for information it had since the onset of this lawsuit. CHP’s counsel avers it was “[d]ue to oversight” that “prior defense counsel did not recognize the grounds for a cross-complaint against plaintiff and his parents.” (Sullinger Decl. ¶11.) Nonetheless, despite CHP’s counsel’s oversight or inadvertence, Plaintiff did not oppose the motion and there is otherwise no demonstration of bad faith for the proposed cross-complaint arising from the same transaction as Plaintiff’s claim. The late filing of the motion to file a compulsory cross-complaint absent some evidence of bad faith is insufficient evidence to support denial of the motion. (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal. App. 3d 94, 101.) 

 

The motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is therefore GRANTED. CHP is ordered to file its proposed cross-complaint within ten (10) days of the date of this Order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT 

 

Dept. 31

I. Background 

Plaintiffs Yeymi Alejandra Perez Ramirez, Gloria Leslie Reyes, and Saul Andres Reyes, by and through his guardian ad litem, Bertha Ramirez, (“Plaintiffs”), individually and as successors in interest to Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente (“Decedent”), filed this action against Defendants 1037 North Sycamore (Los Angeles) Owner, L.P., CIM Group, L.P. and CIM Holdings, LLC (“Defendants”) for wrongful death arising from work Decedent performed on Defendants’ property, in which unsecured lumber beams fell on Decedent. The complaint brings causes of action for wrongful death (negligence and premises liability) and a survival action.   

Defendants now demur to the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), arguing the Plaintiffs are barred from pursuing their claims based upon the Exclusive Remedy Provisions of the Workers’ Compensation Act under Cal. Labor Code §3600, subdivision (a), and that the FAC omits all previous references of Decedent working at the direction of Defendants. Plaintiff opposes the motion, and Defendants filed a reply. 

 

II. Procedural 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (CCP § 430.41(a).)   

The court finds Defendants fulfilled this requirement prior to filing the demurrer.  (Hamby Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.) 

 

III. Judicial Notice 

Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of Plaintiff’s Complaint. The request is granted. 

  

IV. Demurrer 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings.  It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint).  (CCP §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true.  (Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.)  No other extrinsic evidence can be considered.  (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery.  (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-72.)  The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint; or in a superseded complaint in the same action.  (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”]). 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action.  (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97,119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) 

 

A. Workers’ Compensation Act  

“ ‘The elements of a negligence cause of action are the existence of a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury.  [Citation.] The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.’ [Citation.]”  (McIntyre v. The Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)  As in any negligence action, whether a duty was owed under the facts is a question of law for the court.  (Castaneda v. Olsher (2007) 41 Cal.4th 1205, 1213.)   

 

The WCA provides the exclusive remedy for an injury sustained by an employee in the course and scope of employment. (Lab. Code, §§ 3600, subd. (a), 3602, subd. (a); Charles J. Vacanti, M.D., Inc. v. State Comp. Ins. Fund (2001) 24 Cal.4th 800, 813, 102 Cal.Rptr.2d 562, 14 P.3d 234 (Vacanti).) The workers’ compensation exclusivity rule is based on the “presumed ‘compensation bargain’ ” in which, in exchange for limitations on the amount of liability, the employer assumes liability regardless of fault for injury arising out of and in the course of employment. (Shoemaker v. Myers (1990) 52 Cal.3d 1, 16, 276 Cal.Rptr. 303, 801 P.2d 1054.) The compensation bargain encompasses both psychological and physical injury arising out of and in the course of the employment. (Lab. Code, §§ 3600, subd. (a), 3208.3.)  

(Shirvanyan v. Los Angeles Community College Dist. (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 82, 105.)   

Consequently, an injured worker's remedies against his employer or a co-employee are (absent narrow exceptions) solely under the workers' compensation law—i.e., there is no “common law” action against the employer or a co-employee because, as a general rule, workers' comp is the exclusive remedy for injury or death of an employee occurring in the course and scope of employment.  (Lab. Code §§ 3600, 3601; see generally, Jones v. Kaiser Industries Corp. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 552, 556.)   

Subject to exceptions, workers’ compensation is the¿only¿remedy¿available to injured employees¿and their dependents¿against the employer or against any fellow employee responsible for injuries “arising out of and in the course of employment.” (Lab. Code §§ 3600-3602, 5300; see¿Shoemaker v. Myers¿(1990) 52 Cal.3d 1, 18.)  There are some situations in which the injured employee may maintain a civil action against his or her employer.  These include statutory exceptions such as: 1) physical assault by the employer; 2) aggravation of injury by the employer’s fraudulent concealment; 3) products liability cases, 4) power press guards; and 5) the employer is uninsured.  (See Lab. Code §§ 3602, 3706, 4553.)  This exclusivity rule extends to co-employees, unless the co-employee proximately causes another employee's injury or death by a “willful and unprovoked physical act of aggression” or by intoxication.  (Lab. Code, § 3601(a); see Torres v. Parkhouse Tire Service, Inc. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 995, 1002.)     

Defendants argue the FAC is almost the same as the original complaint, except for the specific omission of any words stating Decedent was working at the time of the incident, and that the FAC is a sham pleading. A court has authority to strike sham pleadings.  (Ricard v. Grobstein, Goldman, Stevenson, Siegel LeVine & Mangel (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 157, 162.)  “Under the sham pleading doctrine, plaintiffs are precluded from amending complaints to omit harmful allegations, without explanation, from previous complaints to avoid attacks raised in demurrers or motions for summary judgment.”  (Deveny v. Entropin, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 408, 425.)  However, “[t]he sham pleading doctrine is not intended to prevent honest complainants from correcting erroneous allegations … or to prevent correction of ambiguous facts.... Instead, it is intended to enable courts to prevent an abuse of process.”  (Id. at 426 (internal citations and quotations omitted).)  Further, the sham pleading doctrine is not intended to prevent correction of bona fide mistaken allegations or to prevent correction of ambiguities.  (Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 336, 344.)  The doctrine only applies where prior allegations of fact are “destructive” of any of the causes of action alleged in the subsequently filed pleading.  (Karp v. Dunn (1964) 229 Cal.App.2d 186, 190.)  The sham pleading doctrine “is an exception to the general rule of pleading that prior complaints are superseded by subsequent ones. Only where an extreme situation is presented should the exception be applied.”  (Id. at 191.) 

The critical element to the applicability of the workers compensation exclusivity doctrine is whether Decedent was injured within the course and scope of his employment with Defendants for Workers’ Compensation purposes.  

 

Here, the original complaint alleged in pertinent part: 

 

10. Plaintiffs are informed and believe that on or about July 13, 2021, Plaintiffs’ decedent, Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente was employed by an unknown company within the State of California. In that capacity, decedent, Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente, was lawfully working on the Subject Property at the construction site in Los Angeles, California. 

… 

13. Plaintiffs are informed and believe that on July 13, 2021, Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente, 

decedent, was directed by Defendants to perform work at the Subject Property consisting of 

construction activities. […] 

… 

24. […] Defendant owners, OWNER, GROUP, and HOLDINGS, and each of them, directed decedent Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente to perform work at the Subject Property… 

(Compl. ¶¶ 10, 13, 24.) 

 

Defendants argue the FAC now has removed all references to Decedent “working” on the construction site, and there are no longer any allegations as to why Decedent was on the subject property and Defendants’ role in directing Decedent’s work. The FAC now alleges:  

 

10. Plaintiffs are informed and believe that on or about July 13, 2021, Plaintiffs’ decedent, Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente was employed by an unknown company within the State of California. On or about July 13, 2023, decedent, Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente, was lawfully on the Subject Property at the construction site in Los Angeles, California. 

 

… 

 

13. Plaintiffs are informed and believe that on July 13, 2021, Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente, 

decedent, was lawfully at the Subject Property. Defendants had not advised and/or warned decedent Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente of the dangers, known or which should have been known, to Defendants at the Subject Property. As a direct and proximate result of Defendants’ actions, while decedent Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente was at the Subject Property, unsecured 2” x 4” lumber beams fell from an overhead location on Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente causing severe injuries that resulted in his death. 

 

… 

 

24. Plaintiffs are informed and believe that on July 13, 2021, at Defendants’ direction, 

instruction, supervision and/or control of Defendants and their employees created, controlled, 

operated, maintained and allowed a dangerous condition… which was known or which should have been known to Defendants and each of them, to be dangerous and unsafe for all persons at the Subject Property, including, decedent Lucas Obdulio Perez Vicente, and without providing safety precautions or warnings, instruction and training… 

(FAC ¶¶ 10, 13, 24.) 

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs contend the identity of Decedent’s employer has not been established, and that the merits of Decedent’s employment should be addressed on summary judgment. Plaintiffs argue CIM Holdings, LLC rejected Plaintiffs’ workers compensation claim for reasons including “a question as to whether Lucas Vicente would qualify as a statutory employee for workers’ compensation benefits.” More particularly, as provided in Plaintiffs’ Exhibit 3, the denial of workers’ compensation benefit to CIM Holdings, LLC provided: 

 

“After careful consideration of all available information, we are denying liability for your claim of injury. Workers’ compensation benefits are being denied because you have not filed workers’ compensation claim that Lucas Vicente’s passing was due to work related injuries. We are also denying the claim because you have failed to cooperate with our investigation. Lastly, there is a question whether Lucas Vicente would qualify as a statutory employee for workers’ compensation benefits. If you comply with our investigation, we will reconsider our position.” 

(Opp. Exh. 3.) 

Plaintiffs acknowledge that Decedent was working at a construction site with a number of contractors and subcontractors at the time of the incident, but argue the Workers Compensation Act cannot bar Plaintiffs’ action because the employee-employer relationship has not been established. Plaintiffs also argue that there is no proof that Defendants maintained workers compensation insurance. In the event the demurrer is sustained, Plaintiffs request leave to amend.  

In reply, Defendants aver Plaintiffs have not explained the deliberate removal of the allegations that Decedent was working on the subject property, and that extrinsic evidence outside the four corners of the pleadings should not be considered. Defendants provide they do not deny Decedent was an employee, but the FAC does not include any allegations that Defendants denied Decedent was an employee. 

Cal. Lab. Code § 3357 provides, “Any person rendering service for another, other than as an independent contractor, or unless expressly excluded herein, is presumed to be an employee.” At the time the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint, the Court reasoned that the allegations contained in the original complaint were sufficient to indicate that Decedent was presumed to be an employee. However, leave to amend was provided for Plaintiffs to correct ambiguous facts, such as whether he was an independent contractor, or to plead any statutory exceptions for Plaintiffs to be able to maintain the action despite the workers’ compensation exclusive remedy rule. However, Plaintiffs’ only change to the operative complaint was to omit all references of Decedent working on the subject site and being directed by Defendants to perform construction work.  This is a material change that would be “destructive” of Plaintiffs’ causes of action alleged in the subsequently filed pleading. 

Here, although Defendants argue extrinsic evidence should not be considered, Defendants themselves also make use of extrinsic evidence in support of their motion. Nonetheless, workers’ compensation is the only remedy available to injured employees and their dependents against the employer if the injuries arose out of and in the course and scope of the injured worker’s employment. As provided by Plaintiffs, there does appear to be an issue of whether Decedent’s passing was due to work and whether Decedent qualified as a statutory employee, which would take this outside of the exclusivity rule for pleading purposes only. Further, Plaintiff’s opposition alleging Defendants did not maintain workers compensation insurance would also be a statutory exception to the exclusivity rule. However, none of these allegations were pled in the FAC. 

Although Plaintiffs’ FAC constitutes a sham pleading by omitting material allegations in terms of Decedent working on a construction site at the direction of Defendants, there is also a possibility for an amendment to change the legal effect of the pleading. Therefore, Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC is sustained with leave to amend.  

 

Plaintiffs are granted twenty (20) days leave to amend.¿

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT 

 

Dept. 31 

1:30 p.m.  

December 6, 2023   

  

Plaintiffs Benjamin Joseph Truitt, Jessica Truitt, and Macy Truitt (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Sea Sprite Motel and James Graham for damages arising from a motor vehicle incident. 

 

At this time, Defendants Sea Sprite Limited Partnership (Doe 1), erroneously sued as Sea Sprite Motel, and James Graham (“Defendants”) move to enforce the Settlement Agreement (“Agreement”) pursuant to CCP § 664.6 on the grounds that the settlement is contingent upon Plaintiffs filing a minor’s compromise, and Plaintiffs’ counsel has yet to pursue the minor’s compromise.  

 

The Court notes there is an Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal (Settlement) currently set for December 20, 2023. On September 25, 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed a declaration declaring his office “is in the middle of preparing the minor’s compromise for this honorable Court to review.” (Janfaza Decl. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s counsel acknowledges that, “Since the minor’s compromise has not been finalized and granted by this court, our office has yet to receive the settlement check from defense counsel.” However, since then, Plaintiff’s counsel has still not filed a petition for an order for a minor’s compromise. 

 

Because Defendants’ motion will be rendered moot upon Plaintiffs filing the minor’s compromise, and Plaintiffs’ counsel is purportedly preparing the minor’s compromise, the Court continues the Hearing on Defendants’ Motion to Enforce Settlement from December 6, 2023 to _______________. 

 

Additionally, the Court sets an Order to Show Cause Re: Status of Minor’s Compromise and Why Plaintiffs’ Counsel Should Not Be Sanctioned $1000 For Failure To File Minor’s Compromise and Failure to Proceed with Reasonable Diligence pursuant to CCP section 583.130 for _________________.  

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
