DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.   

Case Number: 22STCV10768    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

HEALTHCARE MANAGEMENT ASSOCIATES, INC., et al., 

 

         vs.

 

R.O.A.R. MANAGEMENT COMPANY, INC., et al.

 Case No.:  22STCV10768

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 28, 2024

 

Cross-Defendants Healthcare Management Associates, Inc.’s, Mary Aviles’, National Intra-Operative Monitoring, Inc.’s, and Orangewood Surgical Center LLC’s unopposed motion for a stay of all discovery pending the issuance of a Remittitur by the Court of Appeal is denied as moot.

 

Cross-Defendants Healthcare Management Associates, Inc. (“HMA”), Mary Aviles (“Aviles”), National Intra-Operative Monitoring, Inc. (“NIOM”), and Orangewood Surgical Center LLC (“Orangewood”) (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”) move for an order staying all discovery in this matter until the issuance of a Remittitur by the Court of Appeal.  (Notice Motion, pg. 2.)

 

Background

On July 30, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed the instant motion.  As of the date of this hearing no opposition has been filed.

On August 26, 2024, the Court of Appeal issued its opinion on the instant case, remanding to this Court with directions to vacate its order denying the motion to strike and to enter a new order granting the motion in part and striking references to specified portions of the first amended cross-complaint (“FAXC”), and affirming this trial court’s denial of the remainder of the challenged allegations Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike.  (See Appeal Opinion, pg. 41.)

 

Discussion

Cross-Defendants’ motion is denied as moot.  Cross-Defendants’ requested relief is no longer necessary in light of the fact that the Court of Appeal restored this Court’s jurisdiction over the instant matter.  (See Appeal Opinion, pg. 41.)

 

Conclusion

Cross-Defendants’ unopposed motion for a stay of proceedings is denied as moot. 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Dated:  October _____, 2024

Defendant Hyundai Motor America’s motion to compel arbitration is denied.

 

Defendant Hyundai Motor America (“HMA”) moves to compel Plaintiff Pablo H. Durelli (“Durelli”) (“Plaintiff”) to arbitrate his claims and stay all proceedings in this action pending completion of arbitration.  (Notice Compel Arbitration, pg. 1; 9 U.S.C. §§1-16; C.C.P. §§1281 et seq.)

 

Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff’s 10/15/24 evidentiary objection to the Declaration of Vijay Rao (“Rao”) is sustained as to No. 1.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

HMA’s 6/11/24 request for judicial notice of the Complaint filed in the instant action is denied because this Court does not need to take judicial notice of filings on the instant docket.

 

          Background

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiff his operative complaint in the instant action against HMA and Non-moving Defendant DTHLA Motors, LLC d/b/a Hyundai of Downtown Los Angeles (“DTHLA”) alleging four causes of action: (1) violation of Song-Beverly Act- breach of express warranty [against HMA]; (2) violation of Song-Beverly Act- breach of implied warranty [against HMA]; (3) violation of Song-Beverly Act §1793.2 [against HMA]; and (4) negligent repair [against DTHLA].  Plaintiff’s causes of action stem from Plaintiff’s purchase of a 2019 Hyundai Ioniq on January 13, 2020.  (Complaint ¶9.)

On June 11, 2024, HMA filed the instant motion.  Plaintiff filed his opposition on October 15, 2024.  HMA filed its reply on October 21, 2024.

         

          Motion to Compel Arbitration

Enforceability of the Arbitration Agreement

Federal law provides for enforcement of this Arbitration Agreement. The Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. §1, et seq. (“FAA”), establishes a strong federal policy in favor of arbitration of disputes where a written arbitration agreement exists. Section 2 of the FAA provides, in pertinent part that “[a] written provision . . . to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract . . .  shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable.”  (9 U.S.C. §2.)  The purpose of the FAA is to “reverse the longstanding judicial hostility to arbitration agreements.” (Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp. (1991) 500 U.S. 20, 24.)  The FAA places arbitration agreements “on an equal footing with other contracts and [requires courts] to enforce them according to their terms.”  (AT&T Mobility, LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333, 339; see also Rent-A-Center West, Inc. v. Jackson (2010) 561 U.S. 63, 67 [“The FAA reflects the fundamental principle that arbitration is a matter of contract.”].)  The FAA will preempt not only a state law that “discriminat[es] on its face against arbitration,” but also a state law that “covertly accomplishes the same objective by disfavoring contracts that (oh so coincidentally) have the defining features of arbitration agreements.”  (Kindred Nursing Centers Limited Partnership v. Clark (2017) 137 S.Ct. 1421, 1426.)

The FAA restricts a court’s inquiry related to compelling arbitration to two threshold questions: (1) whether there was an agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) whether the agreement covers the dispute.  (Howsam v. Dean Witter Reynolds, Inc. (2002) 537 U.S. 79, 84.)  Here, HMA fails to meet its burden. HMA fails to demonstrate Plaintiff entered into an agreement to arbitrate. 

HMA merely declares the process by which a customer would agree to the Connected Services Agreement (“CSA”) that contains the arbitration agreement (“Arbitration Agreement”) at issue.  Exhibit 1 to the Declaration of Vijay Rao (“Rao”) is the acknowledgment checkbox screen that Plaintiff “would have seen when [he] activated Bluelink services,” and Exhibit 2 is the Bluelink Agreement “that was in effect at that time.”  (Decl. of Rao ¶6, Exhs. 1, 2.)  These documents are generic, not specific to Plaintiff, and do not establish that they apply to Plaintiff.  

Additionally, HMA has not shown Plaintiff actually checked the box, or otherwise “signed” or agreed to the CSA.  Further, Rao’s declaration does not state, for example, that all purchasers of Hyundai vehicles must enroll in the Bluelink Agreement, or otherwise explain how he knows Plaintiff enrolled in the Bluelink service.

Indeed, Exhibit 1 attached to Mr. Rao’s declaration, entitled “Complimentary Subscription,” states: “Your Hyundai includes 3 years of complimentary Blue Link services which offers a suite of safety and convenience features. This subscription includes the Connected Care, Remote and Guidance packages.”  (See Decl. of Rao, Exh. 1.)  Exhibit 1 only demonstrates that the subscription is voluntary or optional, not that Plaintiff agreed to the terms and conditions of the CSA, including the Arbitration Agreement.

HMA also failed to meet its burden to demonstrate the causes of action in Plaintiff’s Complaint arises from the CSA and thereby implicates the Arbitration Agreement.  Here, arbitration only applies to claims related to the “connected services” that are the subject of the agreement, not the statutory warranty claims brought by Plaintiff.  (See Decl. of Rao ¶6, Exh. 2 at §15(c) [“Hyundai and you agree to arbitrate any and all disputes and claims between us arising out of or relating to this Agreement Connected Services, Connected Services Systems, Service Plans, your Vehicle, use of the sites, or products, services, or programs you purchase, enroll in or seek product/service support for, whether you are a Visitor or Customer, via the sites or through mobile application, except any disputes or claims which under governing law are not subject to arbitration, to the maximum extent permitted by applicable law. This agreement to arbitrate is intended to be broadly interpreted and to make all disputes and claims between us subject to arbitration to the fullest extent permitted by law.”].)

The CSA distinguishes between the “Connected Services” that are discussed in the CSA and other issues that may be covered by HMA’s warranty.  (See Decl. of Rao ¶6, Exh. 2 at §11 [“YOUR VEHICLE’S LIMITED WARRANTY does not cover the Connected Services or the wireless service, which is provided to you on an “as is” and “where is” basis, without any warranty of any kind, express or implied”].)  Moreover, the CSA contemplates services and systems that relate to communication/digital features, not general warrantable issues involving traditional components in the vehicle, such as the engine, transmission, etc.  (See Decl. of Rao ¶6, Exh. 2 at §1 [“Your Hyundai or Genesis vehicle, (“Vehicle”) must come with an authorized Hyundai Bluelink or Genesis Connected Services telematics system (“Connected Services System”), which is the equipment, including hardware and software, sued to provide Connected Services to your Vehicle.”]; see Exh. 2 at §8.].)

Moreover, the CSA arbitration clause specifically states that “in the unlikely event that the appropriate customer service department is unable to resolve your concerns, we each agree to resolve those disputes through binding arbitration.” (See Decl. of Rao ¶6, Exh. 2 at §15(c).)  Notably, Plaintiff did not raise any such concerns with HMA’s customer service departments such that arbitration would be invocable as to those disputes.  Plaintiff’s warranty claims are statutory in nature, and are unrelated to the services offered and disputes contemplated in the CSA.

The inclusion of “your Vehicle” in the arbitration clause (buried within an agreement specifically outlining terms and conditions for “Connected Services” that are not covered by Defendant’s warranty) does not support any argument that the parties reasonably intended such arbitration would govern disputes over warrantable defects. If they had, there would be no reason to include any of the other categories of disputes that would be subject to arbitration as they would be nothing more than surplusage, which courts are to avoid if at all possible.  (VFLA Eventco, LLC v. William Morris Endeavor Entertainment, LLC (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 287, 297.)

Here, the CSA’s arbitration clause applies only to concerns that relate to the “Connected Services” that are the subject of the agreement and that Defendant’s customer service departments (upon being notified by a consumer) are unable to resolve. To interpret the CSA’s arbitration clause as contemplating any claim that relates to Plaintiff’s “vehicle,” such an interpretation would render the rest of the specified types of disputes as surplusage, as they all relate to the vehicle as well.  A more reasonable interpretation of the CSA’s arbitration clause is that it covers only disputes arising under or relating to the “Connected Services” governed by the agreement itself, and not Plaintiff’s warranty claims.

Based on the foregoing, HMA failed to prove the existence of a valid Arbitration Agreement that is enforceable by HMA.

 

Conclusion

HMA’s motion to compel arbitration is denied.
