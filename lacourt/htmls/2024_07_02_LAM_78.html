DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV13057    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 78


Superior Court of California
County of Los Angeles
Department 78

DANIEL ANTUNEZ,
Plaintiff,
          vs.
LOS ANGELES DODGERS HOLDING COMPANY, et al.,
Defendants. Case No.: 19STCV13057
Hearing Date: July 2, 2024
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
MOTION TO TAX COSTS FILED BY DEFENDANT DODGERS  

The motion is denied.
Moving party to provide notice. 
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for negligence, premises liability, negligent hiring, assault, and battery arising from an altercation which took place in April 2018. Plaintiff alleges that he was attacked by security guards working at Dodger Stadium.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY
On April 16, 2019, Plaintiff Daniel Antunez filed a Complaint against Defendant the Los Angeles Dodgers, LLC (the Dodgers).
On April 17, 2019, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint
On August 9, 2019, Plaintiff filed Doe Amendments naming Armando Montalvo, Carlos Mitre, Judish Williams, Danny Choi, and Nicole Griffin as additional defendants in this action.
On February 28, 2024, the parties completed jury trial. The jury found in favor of Plaintiff.
DISCUSSION
The Dodgers move to tax costs from the memorandum of costs filed by Plaintiff.  
Legal Standard
Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding. (Code Civ. Proc. section 1032, subd. (b).) Under Code Civ. Proc., section 1033.5, subd. (c)(2), allowable costs “shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” Subdivision (3) requires: “Allowable costs shall be reasonable in amount.” There is no requirement that copies of bills, invoices, statements, or any other such documents be attached to the memorandum. Only if the costs have been put in issue via a motion to tax costs must supporting documentation be submitted. (Bach v. County of Butte (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 294, 308.) 
Where a cost item does not appear proper and necessary on its face, the burden of proof is on the claimant to show the cost is appropriate. (Murphy v. F.D. Cornell Co. (1930) 110 Cal. App. 452, 454.) If the items appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761.) Where items are properly objected to as not reasonable or necessary, however, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Id.) The trial court’s determination on a motion to tax or strike costs will be reversed only for an abuse of discretion. (Santantonio v. Westinghouse Broadcasting Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 102, 121.)    
“[T]he mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a ‘proper objection’ to an item, the necessity of which appears doubtful, or which does not appear to be proper on its face. … However, ‘[i]f the items appear to be proper charges the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the defendant …and the burden of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable is upon the [objecting party].” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 114.)
Analysis 
The Dodgers move to tax costs. The Dodgers dispute costs for (1) jury fees, (2) deposition costs, (3) the cost of renting a room for deposition, and (4) trial assistant fees.
1. Jury Fees
Jury fees are expressly allowable under Code Civ. Proc., section 1033.5, subd. 9(a)(1). 
Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs states jury fees totaled $3,218. The Dodgers allege Plaintiff has not paid jury fees. 
Code Civ. Proc., section 631(e) provides that the party demanding a jury trial shall deposit with the clerk or judge, at the beginning of the second and each succeeding day’s session, a sum equal to that day’s fees and mileage of the jury. 
The Court has confirmed with the Clerk’s Office that Plaintiff has now paid all jury fees. 
Therefore, the fees will not be taxed.

2. Deposition Fees
The Dodgers next object to the cost of deposition transcripts.
The Dodgers first object to the cost for expedited transcripts. Citing Hsu v. Semiconductor Systems, Inc. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 1330, the Dodgers argue that the expenses for expediting preparation of the deposition transcripts are generally not recoverable. Plaintiff argues in opposition that the expedited transcripts were necessary because the depositions were taken just 35 days before trial and that Defendants also requested expedited transcripts. Given that the depositions took place so close to trial, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that the expedited costs were necessary for litigation. These costs will not be taxed.
The Dodgers next object to the deposition costs for the deposition of Chief Phillip Lee Sanchez on the grounds that Plaintiff seeks fees for Sanchez’s expert witness fee in addition to the transcription costs. As the Dodgers point out, expert witness fees are only allowable if the expert was ordered by the court. (Code Civ. Proc., sections 1033.5(a)(8), 1033.5(b)(1).) 
Plaintiff argues in opposition that the actual cost of Sanchez’s deposition transcription and videotaping was $3,817.62 and that his expert fees were paid for separately. Plaintiff provides a statement of account from the court reporting service that transcribed the deposition stating the cost of transcribing and video taping the deposition totaled $3,817.62. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 3.) Plaintiff also provides another invoice showing Sanchez was paid separately for his expert witness fee. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 4.)  
Because it appears the fees Plaintiff incurred for Sanchez’s deposition did not include the expert witness fee, these fees will not be taxed.
The Dodgers next object to the fees for a certificate of non-appearance taken for Carlos Mitre, arguing that Plaintiff is seeking double recovery for both the cost of the later completed deposition of Mitre and the cost of an earlier certificate of non-appearance. Plaintiff argues in opposition that he incurred both of these costs when Mitre failed to appear for a deposition after Plaintiff served a fourth amended deposition notice and only appeared later after Plaintiff served a seventh amended notice of deposition. (DiDonato Decl., ¶¶9-13.) Because Plaintiff properly noticed the deposition and prepared to take Mitre’s deposition and Mitre failed to appear, the Court finds that the non-appearance fees were necessary to the litigation. These costs will not be taxed.
3. Rental of a meeting room for deposition
Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs includes a charge for a “Rented meeting room for Lucy Depo.” in the amount of $275.63. The Dodgers argue this cost is not recoverable because it is not permitted under Code Civ. Proc., section 1033.5. 
Items not mentioned in section 1033.5 may be allowed or denied at the court’s discretion. (Code Civ. Proc., section 1033.5(c)(4).)
Here, Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that he reserved a room in Lancaster for the deposition of Lucy Gonzalez because she resided in Kern County and was in her eighth month of pregnancy at the time of the deposition. (diDonato Decl., ¶14.) Because Gonzalez was required to remain near her primary care physician in case of emergency, Plaintiff’s counsel found it necessary to incur an expense to depose her at a location closer to her residence. (Id., ¶15.) 
Although not mentioned in section 1033.5, the section does not disallow this cost. Thus, the Court may allow or deny the cost in its discretion. Because the rental was necessary to depose Gonzalez and accommodate her medical condition, the Court will allow this cost in its discretion.
4. Trial Technician
The Dodgers finally argue that Plaintiff’s trial assistant fees are not allowable because the trial assistant was not necessary to the conduct of the litigation and the fees were not supported by documentation.
First, there is no requirement that a memorandum of costs be supported by a specific type of documentation.
Expenses for equipment charges and trial technician costs are not allowed or prohibited by statute. The Court has discretion to allow or deny these costs. (Code Civ. Proc., section 1033.5(c)(4).)
Here, Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that a significant portion of the evidence presented was surveillance footage of the event giving rise to this litigation. (diDonato Decl., ¶16.) The trial assistant was necessary to present these electronic exhibits. (Id.) Plaintiff provides the technician’s time entries and represents that he deducted other costs, such as the technician’s parking costs. (Id., ¶17, Exh. 12.)
The Court finds that the technician was necessary to present electronic exhibits during trial. Therefore, the Court will exercise its discretion and allow the costs.

Case Number: 23STCV10369    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 
County of Los Angeles 
Department 78 
 
JORGE ROMERO, et al.,
Plaintiffs,
vs. 
GENERAL MOTORS, LLC,
Defendant. Case No.: 23STCV10369
Hearing Date: July 2, 2024
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
DEFENDANT GENERAL MOTORS’ DEMURRER TO THE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT AND MOTION TO STRIKE PUNITIVE DAMAGES

Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.
Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.
Moving party to provide notice.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for violations of the Song-Beverly Warranty Act and fraudulent inducement and concealment. Plaintiffs allege that they purchased a 2020 Chevrolet Silverado 1500 from Defendant in 2020. The vehicle arrived with defects that Defendant failed to repair despite a reasonable number of opportunities. Plaintiffs allege that Defendant committed fraud by allowing the vehicle to be sold without disclosing that the vehicle and its 8-speed transmission were defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On May 9, 2023, Plaintiffs Jorge and Maria Romero filed their Complaint against General Motors, LLC. 
On October 2, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC).
On January 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (SAC).
On February 8, 2024, Defendant filed this demurrer and motion to strike.
On May 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed oppositions.
On May 20, 2024, Defendant filed replies.

DISCUSSION 
I. DEMURRER 
Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment on the same grounds as the demurrer which the Court overruled on December 6, 2023. The Court has already ruled on these issues and did not give Plaintiffs leave to amend the fifth cause of action. Because the Court has already ruled on these issues and there is no change to this cause of action in the SAC, the demurrer is overruled.
II. MOTION TO STRIKE
Defendant moves to strike Plaintiffs’ demand for punitive damages in their prayer for relief on the same grounds as the first motion to strike. 
In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “‘Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).) 
Here, Defendant argues that (1) Plaintiffs may not recover punitive damages because Plaintiffs cannot recover punitive damages for Song Beverly claims, (2) Plaintiffs cannot claim both a civil penalty under the Song Beverly Act and punitive damages, (3) Plaintiffs cannot recover punitive damages for his fraud cause of action because the SAC fails to allege a cause of action for fraud, and (4) because the SAC fails to state facts sufficient to support punitive damages. The Court previously found that Defendant’s first three arguments were without merit. The Court previously granted the motion to strike on the grounds that the alleged conduct did not arise to despicable conduct sufficient to support a demand for punitive damages and because the FAC did not name an officer, director, or managing agent who participated in the alleged malicious conduct.
The Court notes that the SAC and FAC are the same except for two bolded names in paragraphs 69 and 70. Because there has been no substantive change to the SAC, the motion to strike is denied as to Defendant’s first three arguments. However, upon further examination of the SAC, the Court notes that fraudulent conduct is sufficient to support a demand for punitive damages. Plaintiffs’ SAC adequately alleges that Defendant fraudulently induced Plaintiffs to purchase the subject vehicle by concealing the vehicle’s transmission defects with the intention of inducing reliance and causing Plaintiffs to purchase a defective vehicle. (See minute order dated December 6, 2023.) Because the SAC alleges fraudulent conduct consistent with the definition of fraud articulated in Civ. Code, §3294, the SAC sufficiently alleges fraudulent conduct which would support a demand for punitive damages. 
Additionally, upon further examination of the SAC, the Court notes Plaintiffs alleged that President Johan de Nysschen knew of the transmission defects and met with the authorized repair facility regarding the defects. (SAC ¶69.) However, Defendant continued to conceal the defects. (Id.) Another employee, Mark Gordon, likewise knew of the defects and stated a service solution would not be developed due to cost. (SAC ¶70.) It is reasonable to infer from these facts that Defendant’s president knew of the defects and continued authorizing the concealment of these defects. Although the SAC does not state what Mark Gordon’s position is, it is reasonable to infer from the fact that Defendant would not develop a service solution for the transmission defects that Defendant knew of the transmission defects, knew that the defects would not be fixed, and nevertheless continued to sell defective vehicles by concealing the defects. 
Because the SAC properly alleges at this pleading stage that an officer, director, or managing agent engaged in the alleged fraudulent conduct, the SAC states facts sufficient to support a demand for punitive damages. The motion to strike is denied.

ROGELIO VALENZUELA SANCHEZ,
Plaintiff,

GENERAL MOTORS, LLC,

Hearing Date: July 2, 2024 
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
PLAINITFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS 
Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Response to Request for Production, Set One is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.
Responsive documents and supplemental responses must be produced within 10 days after the date of this order. If any documents are withheld based on a claim of privilege, a privilege log must be produced. This privilege log must also be produced within 10 days after the date of this order. 
Moving party to provide notice and to file proof of service of such notice within five court days after the date of this order. 
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is a lemon law case. Plaintiffs allege that in 2019, she purchased a 2018 GMC Terrain SLE vehicle which included an express written warranty. The vehicle was delivered with defects which Defendant could not repair after a reasonable number of opportunities. 
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On June 16, 2023, Plaintiff Laura Castaneda filed her Complaint against Defendant General Motors, LLC.
On March 5, 2024, Defendant failed to appear for IDC.
On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel further responses to requests for production.
On June 20, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition.
On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.
DISCUSSION 
Plaintiff moves for an order compelling Defendant’s further responses to her RPDs.
Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the Court may order a responding party to serve a further response to a request for production when the Court finds that any of the following apply: 
1. A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; 
2. A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 
3. An objection in the response is without merit or too general. 
To prevail, the party moving for the order must first offer specific facts demonstrating “good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) If “good cause” is shown by the moving party, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) 
Here, Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s further responses to RPD numbers 5, 28, and 29.
RPD No. 5
All DOCUMENTS which refer, relate to or concern Plaintiff’s acquisition of the SUBJECT VEHICLE (this Request shall include the sales/lease file commonly referred to as the deal jacket).
Defendant objected to this RPD on the grounds that it is overbroad, vague, and ambiguous. Additionally, the documents are beyond Defendant’s possession, custody, or control because the dealership possesses the documents. Defendant agreed to produce the lease contract GM obtained from the dealership. Defendant also argues that the request seeks privileged material.
Plaintiff alleges the response was improper because Defendant has control over the requested documents and that there should be no differentiation between Defendant and its local representative. 
The RPD does not appear to be vague or overbroad because it is properly limited to documents pertaining to Plaintiff’s vehicle specifically. 
Defendant’s argument that the request seeks privileged material is also without merit. The parties stipulated to a protective order in December 2023 to exclude trade secret and confidential information from discovery. Additionally, Defendant fails to provide a privilege log identifying the documents Defendant alleges are privileged. It does not appear that Plaintiff is seeking privileged documents. To the extent that Defendant believes it would be required to produce privileged documents, Defendant must provide a privilege log. 
As for Defendant’s argument that the documents are not in its possession or control, Defendants must comply with Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.230 if they are unable to comply with the request. As Plaintiff points out, Code  Civ. Proc., section 2031.230 requires the responding party to respond with a representation of inability to comply, affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry was made, whether the document existed, was destroyed, lost, misplaced, or stolen. Here, Defendant merely states the documents are not within its control and does not contain the information required under Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.230. Thus, if Defendant cannot access the documents, Defendant must state that it made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry into the documents and is unable to comply with the request.
Plaintiff argues that Defendant has control of the documents requested because the dealership is Defendant’s agent. However, an agency relationship does not mean that Defendant has access to the requested documents. The Court declines to make a determination of credibility as to whether Defendant in fact has access to the requested documents.
Because Defendant’s response does not comply with Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.230, the motion is GRANTED with respect to RPD No. 5.
RPD No. 28-29
28. All DOCUMENTS that refer, relate to or concern YOUR policies and procedures for the issuance of recalls.
29. All DOCUMENTS that refer, relate to or concern YOUR policies and procedures for the issuance of technical service bulletins.
Defendant objected to the requests on the grounds that the terms “refer, relate to or concern” are overbroad, vague, and ambiguous. Defendant also argues that the requests are unduly burdensome and seek irrelevant documents. 
Information about whether a violation of the Song Beverly Act was willful is relevant to whether a defendant is liable for a civil penalty. (Oregel¿v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc.¿(2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094.)
Here, Plaintiff argues that the information about Defendant’s policies regarding recalls and technical service bulletins is relevant because it will show the scope of the defects and how widespread a particular defect must be to justify a recall or a technical service bulletin, as well as whether Defendant followed its own policies issuing recalls or technical service bulletins. However, this information does not appear to be relevant to a claim under the Song Beverly Act. Although evidence that there was a service bulleting or recall in effect at the time Plaintiff purchased the vehicle would be relevant to whether the alleged violation of the Song Beverly Act was willful, Defendant’s policies regarding recalls or service bulletins do not appear relevant to whether the violation was willful. 
Plaintiff also argues that the information about Defendant’s policies will show the problems were widespread enough to result in a recall or service bulletins being issued. However, Plaintiff already requested the recalls and technical service bulletins in a different RPD. Obtaining information about Defendant’s policies will not show how widespread the defects with Plaintiff’s vehicle were. Additionally, knowing how widespread a defect must be to justify a recall or technical service bulletin will not show that Defendant was aware of the defects with Plaintiff’s vehicle at the time she purchased the vehicle. Rather, the recalls and technical service bulletins regarding the subject defect would show whether Defendant knew of the alleged defects.
Because the documents requested do not appear to be relevant to Plaintiff’s claims, the motion is DENIED with respect to these RPDs.
Plaintiff does not seek sanctions.
