DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01343    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Bahereh Enayati,

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Super King Markets and Does 1 to 25

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

      23VECV01343    107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Summary Judgment

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Defendant B&V Enterprises, Inc. dba Super King Markets (“Defendant”) moves for summary judgment. This court denies Defendant’s motion.

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On January 17, 2023, Bahereh Enayati (“Plaintiff”) sustained injuries after she slipped and fell on Defendant’s property (the “Subject Property”). (Complaint p. 5.) On March 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 to 25, alleging causes of action for general negligence and premises liability. (Complaint pp. 4-5.)

 

On August 1, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed an opposition on October 3, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on October 14, 2024.

 

II.              LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in¿an¿action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c(a)(1).) The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure §¿437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿“A triable issue of material fact exists where ‘the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.’” (California Medical Assn. v. Aetna Health of California Inc. (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 660, 665, quoting Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra at 850.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the moving party must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c(p)(2).) A cause of action “cannot be established” if the undisputed facts presented by defendant prove the contrary of plaintiff's allegations¿as a matter of law. (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1597.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the moving party has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c(p)(2).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162-163.) 

 

III.            DISCUSSION

 

  1. Premises Liability Cause of Action

 

A cause of action for premises liability requires showing: (1) duty; (2) breach; (3) causation; and (4) damages. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (“Ortega”) (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.)

 

Defendant argues summary judgment should be granted in its favor because: (1) Plaintiff cannot produce admissible evidence Defendant had actual or constructive notice of any liquid or slippery substance on the floor before Plaintiff fell; (2) Plaintiff cannot produce evidence Defendant failed to exercise reasonable care; and (3) Plaintiff cannot establish that Defendant was negligent or that Defendant’s negligence caused Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

i.                 Notice

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

“[W]here the plaintiff relies on the failure to correct a dangerous condition to prove the owner’s negligence, the plaintiff has the burden of showing that the owner had notice of the defect in sufficient time to correct it.” (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1206.) The plaintiff must prove the defendant had actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition. (Id. at p. 1206.) “A defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law if the plaintiff fails to show that the dangerous condition existed for at least a sufficient time to be discovered by ordinary care and inspection.” (Id. at p. 1207.) “The owner must inspect the premises or take other proper action to ascertain their condition, and if, by the exercise of reasonable care, the owner would have discovered the condition, he is liable for failing to correct it.” (Id.)

 

b.     Discussion

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot produce admissible evidence Defendant had actual or constructive notice of any liquid or slippery substance on the floor before Plaintiff fell. (Motion p. 12.) Defendant submits evidence it visually inspects the restroom at the Subject Property twice an hour each day. (Barrow Declaration ¶ 6; Garcia Declaration ¶ 6.) Defendant maintains inspection logs showing when the restroom was cleaned. (Barrow Declaration ¶ 6.) Defendant submits a declaration by its Senior Director of Human Resources, Samuel Barrow, who states that on the day Plaintiff fell, the restroom was inspected at 1:23 pm, 1:48 pm, 2:24 pm, and 2:58 pm. (Barrow Declaration ¶ 6; Ex A; Defendant Undisputed Material Facts and Supporting Evidence No. 4.) Barrow also states that “Inspections of the bathrooms include the floors and ensuring they are clean and free of debris and any substances.” (Barrow Declaration ¶¶ 1, 5.) Finally, Barrow states that Defendant “was not put on notice of liquid on the bathroom floor at any point during the four inspections from 1:00 p.m. to 2:58 p.m. or at any point prior to the notification of Plaintiff’s fall.” (Barrow Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

This court finds that Defendant has satisfied its initial burden of showing it did not have actual notice of the dangerous condition.

 

However, this court also finds that Defendant’s inspection logs do not show, as a matter of law, that Defendant lacked constructive notice.

 

In Ortega, the plaintiff submitted evidence showing an inspection had not been made within fifteen to thirty minutes, and possibly as long as two-hours, before the incident. (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at pp. 1204, 1210.) The jury returned a verdict for the plaintiff and the Supreme Court of California affirmed. (Id. at pp. 1204, 1213.) The Court stated that a plaintiff “may demonstrate the storekeeper had constructive notice of the dangerous condition if they can show that the site had not been inspected within a reasonable period of time so that a person exercising due care would have discovered and corrected the hazard.” (Id. at p. 1212.) If a plaintiff “can show an inspection was not made within a particular period of time prior to an accident, they may raise an inference the condition did exist long enough for the owner to have discovered it.” (Id. at pp. 1212-1213.) The Court further stated that “[i]t remains a question of fact for the jury whether, under all the circumstances, the defective condition existed long enough so that it would have been discovered and remedied by an owner in the exercise of reasonable care.” (Id. at p. 1213.)

 

Similarly, in Sapp v. W. T. Grant Co. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 89, 91, a plaintiff slipped on a spool of thread. Defendant produced evidence it inspected the aisle twenty minutes before the incident. (Id. at p. 92.) The Court held that whether the “twenty minute interval between inspections [was] commensurate with the exercise of ordinary care by defendant” was “a question that was properly left with the jury to decide.” (Id.)

 

Finally, in Zipusch v. LA Workout, Inc. (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1284, a plaintiff sustained injuries after her foot became stuck to a sticky substance on a treadmill. The trial court granted summary judgment in the defendant’s favor, but the Court of Appeal reversed. (Id. at p. 1284.) The Court noted that “[c]ircumstantial evidence of a property owner’s failure to inspect the premises before an accident is sufficient to infer the risk existed long enough for the property owner, in the exercise of due care, to have discovered and removed it.” (Id. at p. 1293.) The Court held that the plaintiff raised a triable issue of material fact regarding whether the defendant “negligently inspected and maintained its exercise equipment” because no staff member had inspected or cleaned the equipment in the eighty-five minutes before the accident and the defendant’s employee testified the undersides of the treadmill belts were not routinely inspected. (Id. at p. 1293.)

 

It is undisputed that Plaintiff fell at or around 4:02 pm. (Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Undisputed Material Facts No. 18.) Defendant’s submitted declaration and Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts state it last inspected the restroom at 2:58 pm. (Barrow Declaration ¶ 6; Defendant’s Separate Statement p. 3.) If the restroom as last inspected at 2:58 pm, that would mean approximately sixty-four minutes passed between Defendant’s last recorded visual inspection of the restroom until the incident. Although this is not as long as the last inspection in Zipusch v. LA Workout, Inc., it is much longer than the fifteen to thirty minutes in Ortega and the twenty minutes in Sapp v. W. T. Grant Co. Accordingly, Defendant has not shown that the time between the inspection and Plaintiff’s fall shows it did not have, as a matter of law, constructive notice of the dangerous condition.

 

In the Separate Statement attached to its reply, Defendant states that “the log shows additional inspections of the bathroom through 3:53 p.m.” (Reply Separate Statement pp. 26-27.) This statement conflicts with the Separate Statement that Defendant submitted with the Motion. Further, Barrow’s declaration states that, “on the day of the incident[, t]he women’s restroom w[as] inspected at 1:23 p.m., 1:48 p.m., 2:24 p.m., and 2:58 p.m.” (Barrow Declaration ¶  6.) Barrow’s declaration does not mention an inspection at 3:53 p.m. (Barrow Declaration.)

 

Even if the restroom was inspected nine minutes before the incident, Defendant has not shown, as a matter of law, it lacked constructive notice. In Ortega, the inspection may have been as little as fifteen minutes before the incident. There, the Court stated “[i]t remains a question of fact for the jury whether, under all the circumstances, the defective condition existed long enough so that it would have been discovered and remedied by an owner in the exercise of reasonable care.” (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1213.) Likewise, here, it remains a question of fact for the jury to decide whether Defendant had constructive notice. Defendant argues Ortega and similar cases are distinguishable because they involved entrances and aisles, and “[n]one of the[] plaintiffs [in those cases] were injured in bathrooms.” (Reply pp. 5-6.) How difficult a condition is to detect may be relevant to whether it existed long enough such that it would have been discovered and remedied by an owner in the exercise of reasonable care, but this court finds no reason to limit cases like Ortega to dangerous conditions existing only in entrances or aisles.

 

Moreover, assuming Defendant’s inspection was sufficient to show it did not have, as a matter of law, constructive notice of the restroom’s condition, the burden would then shift to Plaintiff.  And, Plaintiff has satisfied its burden of showing triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Defendant’s inspection log is accurate and whether Defendant’s employee cleaned the restroom.

 

Plaintiff submits evidence showing Defendant uses an electronic system to log its inspections. (Ex. 2, 11:24-12:25.) Employees “use an electronic ‘pipe’ or ‘probe’ to contact a sensor placed at different areas of the store.” (Ex. 2, 11:24-12:25.) The “’pipe’ or ‘probe’ is assigned to the employee who inspects at the beginning of a shift when the employee presses the device on a button near the front desk of the store labeled with his or her name.” (Ex. 2, 18:24-21:8.) Defendant’s ‘Inspection Log’ shows that inspections were completed by “Saira” at 1:23 pm, 1:48 pm, 2:24 pm, and 2:58 pm the day of the incident. (Barrow ¶ 6; Ex. A.) Plaintiff submits a deposition by Defendant’s employee, Saira Aparicio. (Ex. 1, 1:2-14.) In her deposition, Aparicio stated that in January 2023, she did not inspect the produce or restroom areas, she did not use the “probe” at that time, she left work the day of the incident at 2:31 pm, and no other “Saira” worked at the store on January 17, 2023. (Ex. 1, 8:21-14:19.)

 

In its reply, Defendant argues its employee, Patricia Mejia, cleaned the bathroom on the day of the incident, not Saira Aparicio, and “[e]xtensive testimony from Mr. Barrow explained that the internal electronic inspection device had inadvertently not been updated, which is why Ms. Aparicio’s name appeared on the inspection logs[.] (Reply Separate Statement p. 26; Reply p. 2.) Defendant may have an explanation for its inaccurate records, but Plaintiff has met her burden of showing a triable issue of material fact exists.

 

Further, Plaintiff submits evidence showing a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant’s employee cleaned the restroom shortly before she fell and whether Defendant had actual notice of the restroom’s condition.

 

In her deposition, Plaintiff stated she entered the restroom as “the cleaning lady” came out. (Ex. 5, 21:1-11.) Plaintiff also stated that she was the only person in the restroom when she walked into the stall, tried to close the door, and then fell. (Ex. 5, 21:21-22:8.) Defendant’s employee’s presence in the restroom right before Plaintiff fell creates a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant had actual notice of the bathroom’s condition.

 

Further, according to Defendant’s employee who cleaned the restroom that afternoon, Mejia, the restroom was swept and picked up about ten minutes before Plaintiff went in. (Ex. 2, 11:2-25.) Mejia also stated that the last time the floor was mopped was “a long time, most likely an hour” before Plaintiff fell. (Ex. 2, 12:6-11.) But Plaintiff submits photographs of the restroom stall, taken while Plaintiff was still on the floor. (Ex. 2, 65:6-11.) The photographs show trash stuffed into the wall and part of the seat, a toilet seat cover on the ground, and a puddle by Plaintiff’s feet. (Ex. 2 63:24-67:9; Ex. 10.) Plaintiff also submits Barrow’s deposition testimony, where he stated that had a routine maintenance inspection been done, there would not have been trash in the stall. (Ex. 4, 23:25-25:25.) Plaintiff’s evidence shows a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant’s employee cleaned the restroom shortly before she fell.

 

In its reply, Defendant states “Plaintiff’s testimony is easily disproven by the cleaning logs.” (Reply p. 3.) However, Plaintiff has submitted sufficient evidence showing a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether the inspection log is accurate. Moreover, to the extent Plaintiff’s testimony conflicts with Defendant’s inspection log and Mejia’s testimony, those are triable issues of material fact.

 

Defendant has not shown that its inspection establishes, as a matter of law, that it did not have constructive notice of the restroom’s condition. Further, Plaintiff has submitted evidence satisfying her burden of showing triable issues of material fact exist regarding whether Defendant’s inspection log is accurate, whether Defendant’s employee cleaned the restroom shortly before Plaintiff fell, and whether Defendant had actual notice of the restroom’s condition. Thus, there are triable issues of material fact regarding whether Defendant had actual or constructive notice of the restroom’s condition.

 

ii.               Reasonable Care

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

“Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.) The property owner must “exercise ordinary care to discover dangerous conditions and to make reasonable inspections of its premises.” (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1209.)

 

“A store owner exercises ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the portions of the premises open to customers, and the care required is commensurate with the risks involved.” (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1205.) “[T]he owner must use the care required of a reasonably prudent person acting under the same circumstances.” (Id.) “As to business invitees, the owner should conduct frequent inspections.” (Id. at p. 1207.)

 

b.     Discussion

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot produce admissible evidence that it failed to exercise reasonable care. (Motion p. 14.) Defendant submits evidence showing it visually inspected the restroom twice per hour. (Barrow Declaration ¶ 5.) Barrow’s declaration states that “Inspections of the bathroom include the floors and ensuring they are clean and free of debris and any substances.” (Barrow Declaration ¶ 5.) Defendant also submits evidence showing there were no reports or complaints involving the bathroom floors reported to maintenance or management between January 17, 2023, and January 17, 2023. (Barrow Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

The adequacy of a precaution or inspection system is a question of fact for the jury to decide. (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1208 [“Was a 20-minute interval between inspections of the aisles commensurate with the exercise of ordinary care by defendant? This is a question that was properly left with the jury to decide.”]) Accordingly, Defendant’s evidence is insufficient to show that, as a matter of law, it exercised reasonable care. Further, as noted above, Plaintiff submits evidence showing Defendant’s inspection log from the day of the incident was not accurate, and evidence Defendant’s employee did not clean the bathroom shortly before Plaintiff fell, which establish triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant exercised reasonable care.

 

Accordingly, this court finds that triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Defendant exercised reasonable care.

 

iii.             Causation

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

“A plaintiff meets the causation element by showing that (1) the defendant’s breach of its duty to exercise ordinary care was a substantial factor in bringing about plaintiff’s harm, and (2) there is no rule of law relieving the defendant of liability. (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.App.4th 1200, 1205.) “These are factual questions for the jury to decide, except in cases in which the facts as to causation are undisputed.” (Id.)

 

b.     Discussion

 

The parties dispute whether Defendant’s negligence caused Plaintiff’s injuries. (Motion p. 14; Opposition p. 14.) Defendant argues “Plaintiff cannot carry her burden of proof to show any act or omission by Super King was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s injury” and “Plaintiff lacks competent admissible evidence that Super King had notice of any allegedly dangerous condition before the incident occurred. (Motion pp. 14-15.) Defendant also states that “[Defendant] submits that the plaintiff urinated herself, which is supported by witness statements attesting to the smell of urine and pictures taken immediately after the incident showing the liquid concentrated on Plaintiff’s buttock.” (Motion p. 1.)

 

Defendant submits its response to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatory No. 4, where Defendant stated “Pictures and witnesses confirm that Plaintiff urinated herself, which was the source of the liquid on the floor.” (Ex. D, pp. 6-7.) Defendant also submits a declaration by its Assistant Manager, Maria Garcia, who went to the restroom after receiving a call that Plaintiff had fallen. (Garcia Declaration ¶¶ 1-3.) Garcia’s declaration states she “noticed that Plaintiff’s pants were wet from her own urine, which was the substance she had slipped and fallen on.” (Garcia Declaration ¶ 4.) Defendant also submits an exhibit showing a picture of Plaintiff’s legs and the floor after she fell. (Ex. H.) The picture shows a wet substance on the ground. (Ex. H.)

 

Defendant does not cite to the evidence suggesting Plaintiff urinated on herself in the argument section of its Motion, but page one of Defendant’s motion states:  “Super King submits that the plaintiff urinated herself, which is supported by witness statements attesting to the smell of urine and pictures taken immediately after the incident showing the liquid concentrated on Plaintiff’s buttock. (See Hoekstra Decl., ¶ 4, Exhibit E, Super King’s responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, No. 4.)” In contrast, Defendant’s Reply Separate Statement (p. 29) states that whether Plaintiff urinated on herself is “Disputed, but irrelevant.”

 

Defendant submitted evidence showing its negligence did not cause Plaintiff’s fall, so the burden shifts to Plaintiff.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff states that Defendant’s claim Plaintiff slipped in her own urine is “[s]pecious” and “just another attempt to avoid responsibility for substandard maintenance.” (Opposition p. 14.) Plaintiff submits Plaintiff’s deposition, where she stated she went into the restroom, tried to close the stall door, and fell. (Ex. 5, 23:1-5.) Plaintiff denied slipping on her own urine. (Ex. 5, 41:11-22, 75:1-3.) Plaintiff also submits Garcia’s deposition where Garcia said she was “not sure” whether it was urine on the ground. (Ex. 2, 69:12-70:16.)

 

Accordingly, this court finds that there is a triable issue of material fact regarding whether Defendant’s negligence caused Plaintiff’s fall.

 

Thus, Defendant’s request for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s premises liability cause of action is denied.

 

  1. Negligence Cause of Action

The elements of a negligence action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917-918.) Thus, the elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for a negligence cause of action. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)

As discussed above, triable issues of material fact remain as to whether Defendant had notice of the dangerous condition, took reasonable care, and whether Defendant’s negligence caused Plaintiff’s injuries.

Thus, Defendant’s request for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s negligence cause of action is also denied.

IV.            CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is denied.

II.             MEET AND CONFER¿ 

 

"Unlike a motion to compel further responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) 

¿ 

Because no response was provided to the instant discovery requests and Defendant files motions to compel, not motions to compel further, there is no requirement for the parties to have met and conferred prior to the filing of the motions.¿

 

Regardless, Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating she sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiffs on April 30, 2024. (Cloud ¶ 10.)

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

 

a.     Interrogatories 

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [that party] waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290.) “The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)

  

b.      Demands for Production 

 

“I]f a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]…[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300.) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that . . . (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)  

 

c.      Produce Discovery After Motion is Filed

 

“If a party provides an untimely interrogatory response . . . the untimely response might completely or substantially resolve the issues raised by a motion to compel responses[.]” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at pp. 408-409; see also County of San Benito v. Superior Court (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 243, 256 [citing Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. in relation to a motion to compel further responses to requests for production and interrogatories.]) The court, however, retains authority to hear the motion. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 409.) “The trial court . . . might deny the motion to compel responses as essentially unnecessary, in whole or in part, and just impose sanctions.” (Id.)

 

d.      Sanctions

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).) 

 

Similarly, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(c).)

 

Under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a), “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even [if] . . . the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”
