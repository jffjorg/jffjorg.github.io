Dept. F47

Date: 12/4/23

Case #22CHCV00679

 

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT & DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

Motion filed on 10/10/23.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Jonathan Berdeguer and Jennie Buchelly

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Larry Tran

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order setting aside the default entered on 4/10/23 and default judgment entered in this case on 4/13/23 against Defendants Jonathan Berdeguer and Jennie Buchelly.

 

RULING: The motion is granted, in part, and denied, in part. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Defendants Jonathan Berdeguer (Berdeguer) and Jennie Buchelly’s (Buchelly) (collectively, Defendants) tenancy at premises owned by Plaintiff Larry Tran (Plaintiff).  On 8/24/22, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants for breach of contract and common counts. 

 

On 10/11/22, Buchelly was personally served with the summons and complaint.  (See Proof of Service filed 3/15/23).  On 10/21/22, Plaintiff filed an application to serve Berdeguer by publication.  On 3/15/23, proof of service by publication was filed.  On 4/10/23, default was entered against Defendants.  On 4/13/23, default judgment was entered against Defendants. 

On 6/28/23, Defendants were personally served with Applications and Order for Appearance and Examination.  (See Applications filed 5/19/23 and Proofs of Service filed 7/10/23). 

 

On 10/10/23, Defendants filed the instant motion seeking an order setting aside the default entered on 4/10/23 and default judgment entered in this case on 4/13/23.  On 11/21/23, Plaintiff filed an opposition.  There is no proof of service attached to the opposition.  On 11/27/23, Defendants filed and served a reply to the opposition noting that the opposition was filed and served 2 days late and requesting that the Court strike/not consider same.    

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants’ request that the Court strike the late opposition is denied.  Defendants have not shown that they were prejudiced by the late filing or service.  Nor have Defendants requested a continuance of the hearing so that they have more time to respond to the opposition.  Without considering the opposition, the Court’s ruling on the motion would be the same. 

 

The  Court notes that the declarations filed in support of the motion are not executed in compliance with CCP 2015.5. 

 

Buchelly requests relief from the default and default judgment under CCP 473(b) claiming that the default and default judgment were entered as a result of her mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.  Buchelly admits to being personally served on 10/11/22.  (Buchelly Decl. ¶5).  Buchelly then claims that she became sick with Covid at the end of October and/or early November 2022, she was sick for nearly three months and she forgot about being served until receiving “a notice of examination.”  (Buchelly Decl. ¶¶8-10).  Attached to Buchelly’s declaration is a photo taken on 11/10/22 of a home Covid test.  There is no medical documentation to establish that the results are actually Buchelly’s or when the test was taken.  Even if the Court accepts as true that Buchelly tested positive for Covid on or around 11/10/22, she had approximately a month to respond to the complaint before such time.  As such, it does not appear that the default was entered as a result of her illness but rather her inexcusable neglect in failing to timely respond to the complaint. 

 

Additionally, Buchelly admits that she remembered the lawsuit when she “received a notice of examination” which was personally served on her on 6/28/23.  (See Buchelly Decl. ¶9; Proof of Service filed 7/10/23).  Buchelly fails to explain why she waited approximately 3 ½ months after “remembering” being served with the summons and complaint to file the instant motion for relief from default and default judgment.  (See Buchelly Decl., generally).  An application for relief under CCP 473(b) based on a party’s own fault must be made within a reasonable time with six months being the outer most time limit.  Therefore, even if the Court found that Buchelly’s failure to timely respond to the complaint was excusable due to her claimed illness, she failed to act within a reasonable time to have the default and default judgment set aside once she learned of same.    

 

CCP 473(d) allows the Court to set aside any void judgment or order.  The default and default judgment entered against Berdeguer are void due to lack of personal jurisdiction.  There is no  evidence in the court file that Berdeguer was properly served with the summons and complaint.  Although Plaintiff filed an Application for Publication, there is no court order allowing for service by publication as required.  See CCP 415.50.  The fact that Berdeguer may have had actual notice of the action is not a substitute for proper service of process and is insufficient to confer jurisdiction.  American Express Centurion Bank (2011) 199 CA4th 383, 392.  Therefore, the default and default judgment based on such service of the summons and complaint on Berdeguer are void entitling him to have them set aside.   

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied as to Defendant Jennie Buchelly and granted as to Defendant Jonathan Berdeguer.

RULING: The motion is granted, in part, and moot or denied, in part, as set forth below. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiffs Steffanie Stelnick, Todd Stelnick, and Southern California Real Estate Management, Inc.’s (collectively, Plaintiffs) claim that Defendants Ryan House and Tamar House have breached a Settlement Agreement that ended a prior lawsuit.  Plaintiffs contend that the Houses have repeatedly breached the Settlement Agreement by engaging in competing activity in violation of a non-compete clauses in the Settlement Agreement.  Plaintiffs’ operative Second Amended Complaint contains causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Trademark Infringement (15 U.S.C. 1114(1)) against the Houses and others.

On 11/14/23, Defendants filed a request to strike the reply because it was filed late.  On the same day, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed a response to the request.  Because the reply was filed in time for the Court to review same before the original 11/17/23 hearing date and Defendants were not prejudiced by the late filing, the reply was considered in ruling on this motion.  See CRC 3.1300(d).

 

ANALYSIS

 

CCP 2030.300 provides, in relevant part:

 

“(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”

 

Ms. Stelnick contends that SCPMC’s responses to the subject Form Interrogatories contain frivolous and meritless objections and SCPMC failed to completely respond to Form Interrogatories 15.1 and 17.1.

 

The opposition to the motion contends that except for the issue of sanctions, the instant motion is moot because SCPMC served supplemental responses on 10/30/23 consistent with this Court’s 8/14/23 ruling on the motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set 1, as against Ryan House.  (See Liu Decl. ¶19, Ex.N).  SCPMC further contends that Ms. Stelnick’s counsel failed to adequately respond to meet and confer efforts to resolve the issues presented by this motion before SCPMC served its supplemental responses on 10/30/23 and filed the opposition on 11/3/23.  (Liu Decl. ¶¶9-20, Ex.G-N).  The opposition also contends that Ms. Stelnick and/or her counsel acted improperly by scheduling the hearings on the instant motion and multiple other discovery motions on different days requiring multiple appearances.  (See Opposition, p.2:10-13; Liu Decl. ¶5).

 

The reply argues that the motion is not moot as certain of the supplemental responses of SCPMC  (as to Form Interrogatories 12.2, 12.3 and 12.4) are still deficient and the issue of sanctions remains.  The reply also notes that the second supplemental responses of Mr. House (which the opposition claims were served in September 2023, but Ms. Stelnick’s counsel claims were not received until the end of October 2023) did not resolve the issues in SCPMC’s responses as they are two different defendants. 

 

Based on the opposition, including the supplemental responses served on 10/30/23, the Court’s 8/14/23 ruling and the reply, it appears that the motion is moot as to Form Interrogatories 12.1, 15.1, 16.1, 16.2, 16.3, 16.6 and 17.1 as to Requests for Admissions Nos. 4, 5 and 14-53.  (See Liu Decl., Ex.N; Reply, p.4:1-16).  To the extent Ms. Stelnick may still contend the motion is not moot as to Form Interrogatories 16.1, 16.2, 16.3 and 16.6 (the motion as to Ryan House as to these interrogatories was denied), the request to compel further responses to those interrogatories is denied for the same reasons set forth in the Court’s 8/14/23 ruling as to Ryan House.   

 

SCPMC’s supplemental responses to Form Interrogatories 12.2, 12.3 and 12.4 are still deficient as they include objections which the Court noted in its ruling on Ms. Stelnick’s motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set 1, as to Ryan House were without merit.  (See 8/14/23 Minute Order, p.3).

 

As noted in the Court’s 11/16/23 ruling, it is the Court’s reservation system which prohibits the scheduling of all of the motions to compel on the same date.  As such, it is not the fault of Plaintiffs’ counsel that the motions were originally scheduled on separate dates.  As set forth in the 8/16/23 Minute Order, the remaining discovery motions will now all be heard on 12/4/23.  (See 11/16/23 Minute Order).    

 

The Court finds that imposing sanctions on either party or their counsel under the circumstances would be unjust.  CCP 2030.300(d).  As noted above, the motion lacks merit as to Form Interrogatories 16.1, 16.2, 16.3 and 16.6.  SCPMG is not entitled to sanctions as it failed to serve further responses until after the motion was filed and as noted above, certain of those further responses are still deficient.  The Court further notes that SCPMG’s request for $16,946.00 in sanctions is confusing and excessive.  The motion indicates that the amount includes the sanctions requested in the motion heard on 11/16/23 and then indicates that if sanctions are awarded to Tamar House on that motion, such amount can be deducted from the request on this motion.  (See Opposition, p.3, fn.1; Liu Decl. ¶21).  It is SCPMG’s responsibility to clearly set forth the amount of sanctions sought in relation to each motion and not require the Court to parse out which fees relate to which motion.  Additionally, as noted in the Court’s 8/16/23 ruling, SCPMG’s counsel is attempting to recover for his time  reviewing past discovery requests, responses, and Court orders as well as researching and analyzing substantive and procedural legal issues relating to the discovery requests which he is  required to do to meet both his and his client’s discovery obligations.  (See Liu Decl. ¶23; 11/16/23 Minute Order).  If the Court found that SCPMG was entitled to recover sanctions (which it did not), it would only be able to recover fees and costs related to the motion, not fees incurred in properly responding to the discovery.  

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the Court’s 8/14/23 ruling, the supplemental responses served on 10/30/23 and the reply (which only addresses deficiencies in the supplemental responses as to Form Interrogatories 12.2, 12.3, 12.4), it appears that the motion is moot as to Form Interrogatories  12.1, 15.1, 16.1, 16.2, 16.2, 16.6 and 17.1 as to Requests for Admissions Nos. 4, 5 and 14-53.  (See Liu Decl., Ex.N; Reply, p.4:1-16). 

 

To the extent Ms. Stelnick contends the motion is not moot as to Form Interrogatories 16.1, 16.2, 16.3 and 16.6, the request to compel further responses to those interrogatories is denied for the same reasons set forth in the Court’s 8/14/23 ruling as to Ryan House.

 

The motion is granted as to Form Interrogatories 12.2, 12.3 and 12.4 as the supplemental responses served on 10/30/23 include improper objections.  Further responses are due within 30 days. 

 

Both parties’ requests for sanctions are denied.

ANALYSIS

 

CCP 2033.220 provides:

 

“(a) Each answer in a response to requests for admission shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.

CCP 2033.290, in relevant part:

 

“(a) On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”

 

Ms. Stelnick contends that Ms. House’s responses to Requests for Admissions 4, 5 and 8 are incomplete and include frivolous and meritless objections.

The reply argues that the motion is not moot as Ms. House’s supplemental responses to Requests for Admissions 4 and 5 still include improper and unmeritorious objections and the supplemental response to Request for Admission 8 is implausible.

Commercial property management and residential and commercial leasing are specifically excluded from this agreement not to compete with Defendants.’

 

To the extent required, Responding Party denies the remainder of the request.”

 

The Court finds that Ms. House’s initial response to Request for Admission 4 to be deficient as it fails to provide any explanation as to how or why the request is vague and/or ambiguous.  When the basis for these objections is explained in the supplemental response, the Court finds them to be valid.  Additionally, the Court finds the substantive response included in the supplemental response to this request to be sufficient. 

 

Request 5 asks Ms. House to “[a]dmit that the AGREEMENT included a liquidated damages provision whereby the HOUSES would become liable for $54,000 for each breach by the HOUSES of the non-compete clauses.”  Ms. House initially objected to the request “on the grounds that it is vague and ambiguous.”  In her supplemental response, Ms. House states:

 

“Responding Party objects to this request on the ground that it is vague and ambiguous, particularly as to the undefined phrases: ‘become liable,’ ‘each breach,’ and ‘non-compete clauses.’ Responding Party admits the AGREEMENT provides in relevant part: ‘In the event Plaintiffs breach their obligation of non-competing, Plaintiffs agree to pay to Defendants, as liquidated damages, the sum of $54,000 for each breach. The Parties acknowledge that this provision for liquidated damages does not constitute a penalty or forfeiture within the meaning of Civil Code §§3275 or 3369 or any other provision of California law. The Parties further acknowledge that the payment of damages pursuant to this provision does not abrogate or affect in any way the terms of this Agreement.’

 

To the extent required, Responding Party denies the remainder of this request.”    

 

As with Request 4, the Court finds that Ms. House’s initial response to Request for Admission 5 to be deficient as it fails to provide any explanation as to how or why the request is vague and/or ambiguous.  When the basis for these objections is explained in the supplemental response, the Court finds them to be valid.  Additionally, the Court finds the substantive response included in the supplemental response to this request to be sufficient. 

 

Request 8 asks Ms. House to “[a]dmit that, in or about March 2021, Defendant TEAM HOUSE REAL ESTATE, INC. sent out a paid ‘promoted’ Tweet or similar social-media message stating in part, ‘We also help with rentals so please let us know how we can help with your real estate needs!’  Ms. House initially responded to this request by stating that she was “[u]nable to admit or deny.”  Ms. House’s supplemental response to Request for Admission 8 states:

 

“Responding Party lacks sufficient information or knowledge to admit or deny this Request. A reasonable inquiry regarding the matter in this Request has been made, and the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable Responding Party to admit the matter.”

 

Ms. House’s initial response was deficient in that it did not comply with CCP 2033.220(c).  The supplemental response now complies with the statutory requirements.  Ms. Stelnick provides no authority which would allow the Court to order Ms. House to further respond because her claim of lack of information “is simply implausible here.”  (See Reply, p.4:17-22).  If Ms. Stelnick ultimately proves the truth of the matter, she has remedies available if the Court determines that the  admission was of substantial importance, Ms. House had no reasonable ground to believe that she would prevail on the matter and there was no other good reason for Ms. House’s failure to admit.  See CCP 2033.420.

 

With regard to sanctions, Ms. House did not serve supplemental responses until after the motion was filed forcing Ms. Stelnick to incur fees and costs related to filing the motion.  On the other hand, Ms. Stelnick’s argument that the supplemental responses are still inadequate is without merit.  As such, the Court finds that imposing sanctions on either party or their counsel under the circumstances would be unjust.  CCP 2033.290(d).  Even if the Court found that either party was entitled to recover sanctions, the Court finds that the amounts requested by both Ms. Stelnick ($5,828.25) and Ms. House ($6,387.50) to be excessive.    

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied.  Both parties’ requests for sanctions is denied.

“(a) On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply:

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On 3/21/23, Plaintiff Steffanie Stelnick (Ms. Stelnick) served Requests for Production, Set 1,  on Defendant Ryan House (Mr. House).  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.1).  Pursuant to an extension of time to respond, on 5/3/23, Mr. House served responses to the Requests for Production and produced documents.  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.2, 3).  On 5/18/23, Plaintiffs’ counsel emailed a meet and confer letter to defense counsel regarding what she believed to be deficiencies in the responses.  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.4).  On 5/24/23, defense counsel responded by granting a 30-day extension to file motions to compel further responses and requested further meet and confer.  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.5).  Counsel for the parties were unable to schedule a mutually agreeable time to meet and confer.  (Berkowitz Decl. ¶6).  Therefore, on 7/20/23, Ms. Stelnick filed and served the instant motion seeking an order compelling Mr. House to serve further responses to Requests for Production, Set 1.  Additionally, Ms. Stelnick requests an award of sanctions against Mr. House and his attorney in the amount of $5,828.25. 

 

Mr. House has opposed the motion and requests sanctions in the amount of $3,535.00 against Ms. Stelnick and her counsel.  Ms. Stelnick has filed a reply to the opposition. 

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

CCP 2031.210(a) provides:

 

“(a) The party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling has been directed shall respond separately to each item or category of item by any of the following:

(1) A statement that the party will comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling by the date set for the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (c) of Section 2031.030 and any related activities.

(2) A representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of a particular item or category of item.

(3) An objection to the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.”

 

CCP 2031.220 provides:

 

“A statement that the party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling has been directed will comply with the particular demand shall state that the production, inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, and related activity demanded, will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.”

 

CCP 2031.230 provides:

 

“A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”

 

CCP 2031.240 provides:

 

“(a) If only part of an item or category of item in a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is objectionable, the response shall contain a statement of compliance, or a representation of inability to comply with respect to the remainder of that item or category.

(b) If the responding party objects to the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of an item or category of item, the response shall do both of the following:

(1) Identify with particularity any document, tangible thing, land, or electronically stored information falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made.

(2) Set forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground for, the objection. If an objection is based on a claim of privilege, the particular privilege invoked shall be stated. If an objection is based on a claim that the information sought is protected work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010), that claim shall be expressly asserted.

(c)(1) If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.

(2) It is the intent of the Legislature to codify the concept of a privilege log as that term is used in California case law. Nothing in this subdivision shall be construed to constitute a substantive change in case law.”

 

CCP 2031.310 provides, in part:

 

“(a) On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with each of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

(3) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.”

 

The Court notes that Ms. Stelnick did not obtain Court permission before filing the motion to allow her to submit a “concise outline” rather than a separate statement in support of the motion.  See CCP 2031.310(b)(3).  The Court will allow the concise outline in lieu of a separate statement in this circumstance.  However, in the future, the parties must obtain prior court approval before submitting a concise outline instead of a separate statement in support of a motion to compel further responses.  

 

Ms. Stelnick takes issue with Mr. House’s responses to Requests 1, 6, 9, 12-38 and 40-68 because Mr. House has stated that he will “produce, non-privileged documents in his possession, custody, and control” leaving Ms. Stelnick to guess whether any documents are being withheld on the basis of privilege.  (Issue 1) 

 

Mr. House’s argument with regard to Issue 1 is confusing at best.  The opposition seems to argue that Mr. House has not specifically objected to each of the subject requests based on privilege or indicated that documents are being withheld on the basis of any privilege.  (See Opposition, p.6:26-p.7:3).  However, the opposition also argues that the objection is sufficient to satisfy the requirements of CCP 2031.240(c)(1) which would seem to indicate that Mr. House is specifically objecting to each of the subject requests on the basis of privilege.  The general privilege objection in Mr. House’s responses to the document requests states:

 

“Responding Party objects to these requests to the extent they seek information that is protected from disclosure by the attorney-client privilege, the attorney work product doctrine, or any other applicable privileged or protected information. To the extent that the requests may be construed as seeking such privileged or protected information, Responding Party hereby claims such privilege and invokes such privilege. The fact that Responding Party does not specifically object to an individual request on the ground that it seeks such privileged or protected information is not a waiver of the protection afforded by the attorney-client privilege, the work product doctrine, or any other applicable privilege or protection.”    

 

The language in the foregoing general objection does not satisfy the requirements of CCP 2031.240.  The general objection fails to identify the particular privilege invoked as to each of the subject requests.  CCP 2031.240(b)(2).  Rather, it leaves Ms. Stelnick and the Court to guess whether any documents are being withheld based on the attorney-client privilege, the attorney work product doctrine, or any other unidentified applicable privilege.  Additionally, contrary to the assertion in the opposition, the general privilege objection does not provide sufficient factual information for Ms. Stelnick to evaluate the merits of any privilege claim.  (See Opposition, p.6:9-p.7:3); CCP 2031.240(c)(1).

 

Ms. Stelnick has shown the requisite good cause justifying the discovery is entitled to further responses to Requests 1, 6, 9, 12-38 and 40-68.  CCP 2031.310(a)(1), (3), (b)(1).  If no documents are being withheld on the basis of privilege, further responses eliminating the portion of the responses indicating that “non-privileged documents” are being produced is warranted.  If documents are being withheld on the basis of privilege, further responses stating the particular privilege(s) invoked along with sufficient factual information for the other parties to evaluate the merits of the claim, including a privilege log, if necessary, are required.  CCP 2031.240(b)(2), (c)(1).

 

Ms. Stelnick also takes issue with the documents produced in response to Requests 57-68.  (Issue 2).  Ms. Stelnick believes that Mr. House has not produced all of the responsive documents he has with regard to these requests.  Ms. Stelnick has not made the proper motion nor cited the proper authority with regard to this issue.  Ms. Stelnick should have made a motion to compel compliance under CCP 2031.320.  Even if Ms. Stelnick had made the proper motion and/or cited the correct statute for such relief, it could not be granted by the Court based on Ms. Stelnick’s speculation that Mr. House has additional responsive documents.  The Court notes that Ms. Stelnick offers no evidence (i.e., declaration, copies of the documents) to support her claim in the reply that she has discovered additional responsive documents on her own.  (See Reply, p.3:11-16).  Rather, she merely claims that such documents are included in her pending Application for Writ of Attachment without specifying the documents or to which specific request they are responsive.  Nor has Ms. Stelnick definitively shown that Mr. House has possession, custody and control of documents she purportedly discovered on her own.     

 

The Court finds that it would be unjust to impose sanctions on either party since neither was entirely successful on the motion.  CCP 2031.310(h).  Therefore, both parties’ requests for sanctions are denied. 

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted, in part, and denied, in part. 

 

Mr. House is ordered to provide further responses to Requests 1, 6, 9, 12-38 and 40-68 within 30 days.   

 

The request to compel Mr. House to produce additional documents in response to Requests 57-68 is denied. 

 

Both parties’ requests for sanctions are denied.

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiffs Steffanie Stelnick, Todd Stelnick, and Southern California Real Estate Management, Inc.’s (collectively, Plaintiffs) claim that Defendants Ryan House and Tamar House have breached a Settlement Agreement that ended a prior lawsuit.  Plaintiffs contend that the Houses have repeatedly breached the Settlement Agreement by engaging in competing activity in violation of a non-compete clauses in the Settlement Agreement.  Plaintiffs’ operative Second Amended Complaint contains causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Trademark Infringement (15 U.S.C. 1114(1)) against the Houses and others. 

 

On 3/21/23, Plaintiff Steffanie Stelnick (Ms. Stelnick) served Requests for Production, Set 1,  on Defendant Southern California Property Management Company, Inc.  (SCPMC).  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.1).  Pursuant to an extension of time to respond, on 5/3/23, SCPMC served responses to the Requests for Production and produced documents.  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.2, 3).  On 5/18/23, Plaintiffs’ counsel emailed a meet and confer letter to defense counsel regarding what she believed to be deficiencies in the responses.  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.4).  On 5/24/23, defense counsel responded by granting a 30-day extension to file motions to compel further responses and requested further meet and confer.  (Berkowitz Decl., Ex.5).  Counsel for the parties were unable to schedule a mutually agreeable time to meet and confer.  (Berkowitz Decl. ¶6).  Therefore, on 7/20/23, Ms. Stelnick filed and served the instant motion seeking an order compelling SCPMC to serve further responses to Requests for Production, Set 1.  Additionally, Ms. Stelnick requests an award of sanctions against SCPMC and its attorney in the amount of $5,828.25. 

 

SCPMC has opposed the motion and requests sanctions in the amount of $3,250.00 against Ms. Stelnick and her counsel.  Ms. Stelnick has filed a reply to the opposition. 

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

CCP 2031.210(a) provides:

 

“(a) The party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling has been directed shall respond separately to each item or category of item by any of the following:

(1) A statement that the party will comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling by the date set for the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (c) of Section 2031.030 and any related activities.

(2) A representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of a particular item or category of item.

(3) An objection to the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.”

 

CCP 2031.220 provides:

 

“A statement that the party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling has been directed will comply with the particular demand shall state that the production, inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, and related activity demanded, will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.”

 

CCP 2031.230 provides:

 

“A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”

 

CCP 2031.240 provides:

 

“(a) If only part of an item or category of item in a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is objectionable, the response shall contain a statement of compliance, or a representation of inability to comply with respect to the remainder of that item or category.

(b) If the responding party objects to the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of an item or category of item, the response shall do both of the following:

(1) Identify with particularity any document, tangible thing, land, or electronically stored information falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made.

(2) Set forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground for, the objection. If an objection is based on a claim of privilege, the particular privilege invoked shall be stated. If an objection is based on a claim that the information sought is protected work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010), that claim shall be expressly asserted.

(c)(1) If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.

(2) It is the intent of the Legislature to codify the concept of a privilege log as that term is used in California case law. Nothing in this subdivision shall be construed to constitute a substantive change in case law.”

 

CCP 2031.310 provides, in part:

 

“(a) On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with each of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

(3) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.”

 

The Court notes that Ms. Stelnick did not obtain Court permission before filing the motion to allow her to submit a “concise outline” rather than a separate statement in support of the motion.  See CCP 2031.310(b)(3).  The Court will allow the concise outline in lieu of a separate statement in this circumstance.  However, in the future, the parties must obtain prior court approval before submitting a concise outline instead of a separate statement in support of a motion to compel further responses.
