DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV05601    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

Pejman Kohansedgh,

Plaintiff,

v.

Gonzalo Lizama, et al.,

Defendants.

Case No.:   23VECV05601       

court’s [TENTATIVEorder: motion to seal

 

I. BACKGROUND 

 

On August 25, 2019, plaintiff Pejman Kohansedgh (“Plaintiff”), the landlord of a certain residential real property entered a lease and a guaranty to the lease with defendants Gonzalo Lizama, Yasmina Monjaraz, Santiago Monjaraz, and Yesenia Cayetano (collectively “Defendants”). In June 2023, Defendants ceased rent payments but remained in possession of the Property. ¿ 

¿ 

On December 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed an unlawful detainer complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants. ¿On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”).

 

On May 29, 2024, the Court denied the Motion for Summary Judgment filed by defendants Yesenia Cayetano and Santiago Monjaraz.

 

On August 7, 2024, the Court entered Judgment against Defendants for possession and money for past due rent and holdover damages in the sum of $45,150.00.

 

On August 14, 2024, defendants Gonzalo Lizama and Yasmina Monjaraz filed their Motion to

Seal, which Plaintiff opposed on September 5, 2024. There is no reply as of September 13, 2024.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A party that requests that a record or portion of a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing it. The motion must be accompanied by a supporting memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.¿(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1); Savaglio v Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 588, 597–601.) All parties that have appeared in the case must be served with a copy of the motion or application. Unless the judge orders otherwise, a party that already possesses copies of the records to be sealed must be served with a complete, unredacted version of all papers as well as a redacted version. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(2).)¿ 

¿¿ 

Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d), a judge may order that a record be filed under seal only if the judge expressly finds facts that establish all the following:¿¿¿ 

(1)   There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; 

(2)   The overriding interest supports sealing the record; 

(3)   A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; 

(4)   The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and 

(5)   No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.¿ 

In ruling on a motion to seal, the court must weigh the competing interests and concerns.¿This process necessitates (1) identifying the specific information claimed to be entitled to protection from public disclosure, (2) identifying the nature of the harm threatened by disclosure, and (3) identifying and accounting for countervailing considerations. (H.B. Fuller Co. v. Doe (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 879, 894.) Therefore, in order to prevail on his motion, the moving party must present a specific enumeration of the facts sought to be withheld and the specific reasons for withholding them. (Id. at 904.)¿ 

¿ 

The California Supreme Court has held that the First Amendment provides “a right of access to ordinary civil trial and proceedings.” (NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1212.) The court further noted its belief that “the public has an interest, in all civil cases, in observing and assessing the performance of its public judicial system.” (Id. at 1210.) There is a presumption of openness in civil court proceedings. (Id. at 1217.) This presumption may apply to seemingly private proceedings. (In re Marriage of Burkle (2006) 135 Cal. App.4th 1045, 1052 [divorce proceedings].)  

¿ 

Before ordering filed documents sealed, courts must find compelling reasons, prejudice absent sealing and the lack of less-restrictive means. (NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc., supra, 20 Cal.4th at 1208–1209 n. 25; Hurvitz v. Hoefflin (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 1232, 1246; Champion v.¿Sup.¿Ct. (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 777, 787.) A proposed sealing must be narrowly tailored to serve the overriding interest, such as by sealing portions of pleadings or redacting particular text. (Burkle, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at 1052, 1070.) An application to seal must be accompanied by a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify sealing. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)¿¿ 

 

CCP section 1161.2 permits certain people from accessing Limited Civil case records. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1161.2(a)(1) [“The clerk shall allow access to limited civil case records filed under this chapter . . . .”].) Section 1161.2(a) provides in relevant part that records in an unlawful detainer matter will not be sealed if judgment is entered for the plaintiff within sixty days of the date on which the complaint is filed:  

 

(1) The clerk shall allow access to limited civil case records filed under this chapter, including the court file, index, and register of actions, only as follows: 

 

(F) Except as provided in subparagraph (G)1, to any other person 60 days after the complaint has been filed if judgment against all defendants has been entered for the plaintiff within 60 days of the filing of the complaint, in which case the clerk shall allow access to any court records in the action. If a default or default judgment is set aside more than 60 days after the complaint has been filed, this section shall apply as if the complaint had been filed on the date the default or default judgment is set aside. 

 

Here, moving defendants seek to have the entire record sealed pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.550, contending that the severe consequences of having this eviction action on their record must outweigh the right of public access to these records. Alternatively, moving defendants request the record be confidential from the public until further order from the Court pursuant to CCP section 1161.2.

 

Upon review of the motion and declarations in support of the motion, the Court finds the following. There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record and the overriding interest supports sealing the record as prospective landlords for affordable and habitable units have told moving defendants that an unsealed eviction judgment on their record would make them ineligible. (Motion, p. 2; Lizama Decl., ¶ 4; Monjaraz Decl., ¶ 4.) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed. (Motion, p. 2; Lizama Decl. ¶ 6; Monjaraz Decl., ¶ 6.) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored and no less restrictive means exist to preserve the overriding interest as moving defendants only ask that the matter be sealed so that they can find suitable housing. (Motion, p. 2.) Further, moving defendants contend there is no prejudice to Plaintiff in granting the motion. (Motion, p. 3.) Nor does Plaintiff raise prejudice in opposition; instead, Plaintiff merely discusses Defendants’ bad faith arguments in this action. 

 

Accordingly, the Court grants the motion pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.550.

I.                BACKGROUND

On December 29, 2023, Plaintiff Julianne Theresa Ross filed a complaint against Defendants City of Los Angeles, and 8155 Langdon Avenue, LLC, among others, alleging a cause of action for premises liability/dangerous condition of public property arising out of Plaintiff’s alleged trip and fall over an uneven portion of sidewalk.

On April 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed proof of service by substituted service as to Defendant 8155 Langdon Avenue, LLC (“Defendant”).

On May 16, 2024, Defendant filed this motion to quash service of summons and complaint. On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On July 15, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 418.10 reads in relevant part:

“(a) A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:

(1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”

““When a defendant challenges the court's personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process the burden is on the plaintiff to prove … the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 CA4th 403, 413, (internal quotes omitted); see Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 CA4th 1154, 1163).” Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2019) ¶ 4:421.5. “‘[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction.’” (Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 544; accord Renoir v. Redstar Corp. (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1152.) A filed proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper, if it complies with applicable statutory requirements. (Floveyor Internat. v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 795.)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

Defendant moves to quash service of summons and complaint.

 

According to the proof of service filed on April 15, 2024, service was made on Defendant’s appointed agent Ritesh Desai by substituted service on John Doe, a receptionist at Defendant’s headquarters. (Scott Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. A.) There is also a declaration of mailing attached, showing that the summons and complaint were mailed at the same address where Doe was served. (Id.)

 

Defendant argues that the summons and complaint were not placed in the hand of the alleged John Doe, but was left on the front doorstep of the building, outside the front door and the appointed agent did not receive a copy. (Salas Decl., ¶ 2; and Desai Decl., ¶ 5.) Additionally, Defendant argues there is no declaration of due diligence on file, attesting to whether the process server attempted personal service before serving by substituted service.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff provides a photograph which disproves Salas’ averment that the summons and complaint were left on the front doorstep of the building. The process server took a photograph of Salas, which depicts that the summons and complaint were left on his desk right in front of him. (Kocaj Decl., Exh. B: Sallee Decl. of Diligence.) A trial court is not required to accept “self-serving evidence contradicting [a] process server’s declaration.”  (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390.) In light of the photograph, the court does not accept the self-serving declarations of Salas, attesting that that the summons and complaint were left outside, and Desai, attesting that he did not receive by mail the summons and complaint. Even if the court were to accept Desai’s declaration that he did not receive the summons and complaint by mail, “service of a summons is deemed completed on the 10th day after mailing.” (CCP § 415.20(a).) Actual receipt is not a requirement.

 

The court next addresses whether reasonable diligence is required in serving a limited liability company, such as Defendant with substituted service.

 

The persons on whom service may be rendered on behalf of a limited liability company is governed by Corp. Code §17701.16.  Section 17701.16(b) provides, service on a limited liability company may be done by “[p]ersonal service…(1) to any individual designated by it as agent, or (2) if the designated agent is a corporation, to any person named in the latest certificate of the corporate agent filed pursuant to Section 1505 at the office of the corporate agent[.]” In addition to this method of service, an LLC may be served by substituted service on the designated agent pursuant to CCP §415.20(a).  (Corp. Code §17701.16(a).)   

 

CCP § 415.20(a) provides that, in lieu of personal service “to the person to be served as specified in [CCP] Section 416.10 [the registered agent],” service may be effected “by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in his or her office . . . with the person who is apparently in charge thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.” Service on a limited liability company may be made in the same manner. (See Corp. Code §§ 17701.13(a)(2) [limited liability company required to maintain agent for service of process], 17701.16(b)–(c) [manner of service on limited liability company]; Weil et al., Cal. Prac. Guide, Civil Procedure Before Trial, Ch. 4-D, ¶ 4:172 [“The designated agent may be served either by personal service (CCP § 415.10, ¶ 4:184 ff.); substitute service (CCP § 415.20(a), ¶ 4:193 ff.); or service by mail with acknowledgment of receipt (CCP § 415.30(a), ¶ 4:225 ff.).”].)  

Here, the proof of service complies with the statutory requirements as service was effected Defendant’s agent, Ritesh Desai, by substituted service on John Doe. As such, the proof of service creates a presumption that service was proper.

As noted above, Defendant argues that there is no showing that two or three attempts were made at personal service on Desai, prior to resorting to substituted service on John Doe, and thus, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has not shown reasonable diligence.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that reasonable diligence is not required for serving business entities, and it is only required for serving individuals. The court agrees.  Here, substituted service is made pursuant to CCP § 415.20(a), and not CCP § 415.20(b). While the plain terms of CCP § 415.20(b) does require reasonable diligence, CCP § 415.20(a) does not. Further, Defendant cites to federal district court decisions that California has no “reasonable diligence” requirement for substitute service on entities. (Gunnar Optiks, LLC, 23-cv-0671-AGS-VET (S.D. Cal. Jan. 16, 2024).) In the absence of a controlling California court decision, a court may consider the reasoning reflected in federal cases that apply analogous statutes or rules. (Pollock v. Tri-Modal Distribution Services, Inc. (2021) 11 Cal.5th 918, 936.)

In addition, treatises concur with this conclusion. (See, e.g., Michael Paul Thomas, California Civil Courtroom Handbook and Desktop Reference § 10.24 (2023 ed.) (“Unlike substitute service on natural persons, substitute service on entities does not require any prior attempt at personal service.”); Robert I. Weil et al., California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial ¶ 4:196 (2023 ed.) (“No such showing [of prior efforts at personal service] is necessary for substitute service on entity defendants ....”).)

Furthermore, California's Judicial Council also agrees that no such showing of prior efforts at personal service is necessary for substitute service on entity defendants. (See Judicial Council Comment to Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 415.20(a) (“If a defendant is a corporate or noncorporate entity, service may be made in the first instance, in lieu of delivery of process to a specified officer or employee of such entity personally, by leaving the papers in his office.”).)

In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cites to federal district cases, and not California appellate cases. While Plaintiff does cite to California case law, none of the cases address the issue at hand and are not on point.

As such, the court finds that reasonable diligence is not required when serving a limited liability company. Thus, the motion to quash is denied.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to quash is DENIED.

Defendant is ordered to give notice.
