DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19TRCV01062    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

WEBTOON ENTERTAINMENT, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

19TRCV01062

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

ROCKETSHIP ENTERTAINMENT, LLC, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          December 5, 2024 (continued from October 9, 2024)

 

Moving Parties:                      (1) Defendants Rocketship Entertainment, LLC and Thomas G. Akel; (2) plaintiff Quanquan (Chelsey) Han and Webtoon

Responding Party:                  (1) Plaintiffs Webtoon and Han; (2) defendants Rocketship Entertainment and Thomas Akel

(1)   Motion to Reopen Discovery

(2)   Motion to Bifurcate

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers and joint statement. The Court had continued the hearing to allow counsel to further meet and confer and to file a joint statement.

RULING

            Defendants’ motion to reopen discovery is GRANTED in part.  Discovery is reopened to allow defendants to obtain documents sufficient to show total sales of unOrdinary and a declaration as to the authenticity of those documents.

            The motion to bifurcate is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On November 25, 2019, plaintiffs Webtoon Entertainment, Inc. and Quanquan (Chelsey) Han filed a complaint against Rocketship Entertainment, LLC and Thomas G. Akel for (1) inducing breach of contract, (2) intentional interference with contractual relations, (3) intentional interference with prospective economic relations, (4) fraudulent inducement to enter contract, (5) negligent misrepresentation, (6) violation of Bus. and Prof. Code §17200, (7) common law unfair competition, and (8) declaratory relief.

            On November 3, 2020, the court overruled the demurrer to the complaint.

On December 28, 2020, Rocketship filed a FACC.

On April 5, 2021, Rocketship filed a SACC for (1) breach of contract, (2) fraud, (3) intentional interference with an existing contract, (4) intentional interference with business relations, (5) violation of Bus. and Prof. Code §17200, and (6) indemnification.

            On August 25, 2021, the court granted Rocketship’s ex parte application to effectuate service on cross-defendant Naver Webtoon.

On November 18, 2021, the court denied in part Webtoon’s motion for summary adjudication as to the complaint’s 1st and 2nd causes of action and granted it as to the second, third, and fourth causes of action in the SACC.

            On December 10, 2021, the court denied Webtoon’s motion to quash service of summons on Naver Webtoon.  The court also granted the motion to lift protective order as to Ken Kim’s deposition.

            On January 5, 2022, the court denied Rocketship’s motion to compel further responses to requests for admission.

            On February 25, 2022, the court sustained without leave to amend Webtoon’s demurrer as to the SACC’s second, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action as to cross-defendant Naver Webtoon.  The court also granted without leave to amend Webtoon and Han’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the fifth cause of action in the SACC.

            On March 3, 2022, a dismissal was entered as to the SACC.

            On March 3, 2022, Rocketship filed a notice of appeal.

            On March 14, 2022, Webtoon and Han filed the herein motion to bifurcate equitable issue.

            On April 6, 2022, the court granted Rocketship’s motion to stay proceedings pending appeal.

            On April 26, 2022, in light of the ruling on April 6, the court took the motion to bifurcate off calendar stating that it will consider the motion when the stay is lifted.

            On May 17, 2024, the appellate court issued a remittitur as to its February 28, 2024 order dismissing Rocketship’s appeal, finding neither of the court’s orders appealable.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Reopen discovery

CCP §2024.050 states: “(a) On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set.  This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.  

            (b) In exercising its discretion to grant or deny this motion, the court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to, the following:  (1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery.  (2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier.  (3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party.  (4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action. 

            (c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to extend or to reopen discovery, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

            Bifurcate

Under CCP §598, “[t]he court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case . . . .”

Under CCP §1048(b), “[t]he court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action . . . or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues.”

Under both CCP §§598 and 1048(b), the trial court has broad discretion.  See Grappo v. Coventry Financial Corp. (1991) 235 Cal. App. 3d 496, 504 (trial court has ample authority to exercise broad discretion under §§ 598 and 1048 and Evid. Code § 320 [providing court has discretion to regulate order of proof); National Electric Supply Co. v. Mount Diablo Unified School Dist. (1960) 187 Cal. App. 2d 418, 421 (trial court has necessarily broad discretion under §1048 which will not be interfered with except for abuse thereof).

DISCUSSION

            As context for the two motions, in the complaint, plaintiffs allege that this action arises out of defendants’ intentional interference with Webtoon’s economic relationships with its comic creators, and in particular, Thomas Akel’s fraud in inducing Han to breach her Webtoon Intellectual Property (“IP”) Agreement by entering into a Publishing Agreement with his competing company, Rocketship.  Complaint, ¶12.  As Webtoon’s Head of Content, in early 2018, Akel acted on Webtoon’s behalf to negotiate an IP Agreement with Han granting Webtoon joint ownership and management rights for her hit comic series, “unOrdinary.”  Id., ¶13.  The IP Agreement provided for Webtoon to have (1) 20% ownership of the intellectual property for Han’s series; (2) a right of first refusal requiring Han to notify and give Webtoon an exclusive first opportunity to negotiate a further deal with her prior to her entering into any other agreement or arrangement; and (3) the exclusive and worldwide right to represent Han with respect to selling, negotiating, and producing works related to her series.  Id., ¶14.  They entered into the IP Agreement effective January 26, 2018.  Id., ¶15.

Plaintiffs further allege that after Akel was let go by Webtoon in November 2018, he approached Han in March 2019 to negotiate a deal on behalf of his publishing company, Rocketship.  Id., ¶16.  He was fully aware that Han negotiating and entering into an agreement with Rocketship without notifying Webtoon would breach her IP Agreement with Webtoon.  Nevertheless, Akel sought to negotiate such a deal with Han to benefit himself and his company by fraudulently obtaining rights to the unOrdinary series to Han’s and Webtoon’s detriment.  Id., ¶17.  Based on Akel’s misrepresentations and manipulation of Han, she signed the Publishing Agreement with Rocketship on July 1, 2019.  Id., ¶23.  Akel has been similarly approaching other Webtoon creators whom he knows have granted Webtoon joint ownership, a right of first refusal, exclusive management rights, and/or other rights under similar IP agreements, for the express purpose of inducing those authors to enter agreements with Rocketship that violate their Webtoon agreements.  Id., ¶30.

The complaint seeks injunctive relief and damages for inducing breach of contract; intentional interference with contractual relations; intentional interference with prospective economic relations; fraudulent inducement to enter a contract; negligent misrepresentation; violation of California Business and Professions Code §17200, et seq.; and common law unfair competition, as well as declaratory relief and rescission of the Rocketship Publishing Agreement pursuant to Civil Code §1689.  Id., §34.

 

            Motion to reopen discovery

            Pursuant to CCP §2024.050(a), defendants request that the Court reopen discovery based on the release of the print version of the property at issue, unOrdinary. 

            Defendants assert that since the initiation of the appeal, “significant developments have occurred that critically impact the question of damages.”  Defendants explain that in December 2023, unOrdinary was officially released in print, which, defendants argue “transitions the potential damages from theoretical projections to tangible financial figures and market data.”  Defendants contend that the actual sales data for unOrdinary is available and that this data is critical for the defense of plaintiffs’ damages claims.  Defendants explain that the additional discovery includes financial records, marketing strategies, change in publishers, and consumer feedback, which, defendants contend, did not exist prior to the release of unordinary and management changes at Webtoon.  Defendants also asset that since the appeal, there have been substantial shifts in the management structure of Webtoon U.S., “directly affecting the company’s operations and strategic decisions, which are central to the damages claimed by Webtoon.”  Defendants argue that the request is narrowly tailored and will not cause delays or prejudice to Webtoon.

            In opposition, plaintiffs contend that defendants did not meet and confer before filing the motion.  Rather, plaintiffs argue, defendants sent a “perfunctory letter” before filing the motion and ignoring plaintiffs’ offer to stipulate to an informal information exchange.  Further, noting that discovery closed in October 2021, plaintiffs argue that the motion fails on the merits because the requested discovery is irrelevant, burdensome, and designed to cause more unwarranted delay.  Plaintiffs assert that plaintiffs’ argument “that is even colorable is that Webtoons’ sales of unOrdinary might be relevant to Rocketship’s damages on its surviving breach of contract/indemnification causes of action against” Han is unsupported because defendant Akel had testified that he could calculate defendants’ lost profits as of April 2021 and defendants did not designate a financial expert.  Plaintiffs further argue that to the extent that defendants seek discovery to test Webtoon’s damages claim, defendants have conceded that Webtoon has already produced its historical sales data for the relevant time period for other print comics.  Plaintiffs also point out that the term of Rocketship’s agreement was from June 29, 2019 through June 29, 2022, and that plaintiffs began selling unOrdinary in print in December 2023; thus, there is no overlap in time.  Plaintiffs also contend that no substantial similarity between the businesses at issue exist and Webtoon’s sales do not provide a baseline for Rocketship’s.  Plaintiffs further argue that defendants have not shown diligence in pursuing discovery, citing as an example when defendants propounded requests for production, set two related to many topics they now deem “necessary’ but did not enforce the requests for production although plaintiffs offered to meet and confer to develop an appropriate electronic search protocol.  Plaintiffs argue also that they have suffered substantial prejudice from defendants’ “bad faith delays” and would have to incur significant costs engaging in the broad discovery sought.  Plaintiffs point out that nearly three years have elapsed from the initial trial date.  Finally, plaintiffs request monetary sanctions.

            In reply, defendants reiterate their arguments that a change in circumstances creates a necessity for the sought evidence and the relevancy of such discovery.  Defendants also assert that they sufficiently attempted to meet and confer but that plaintiffs’ response was “late, paltry, and antagonistic.” 

            As noted above, the hearing was continued to allow counsel to meet and confer as to what further discovery may be needed before trial, with respect to unOrdinary being sold in print by HarperAlley starting in late 2023.  The parties filed a joint statement.  According to the joint statement, plaintiffs understand that the total number of copies of unOrdinary sold in print 2023-2024 is what both parties are relying on as a proxy for the sale success Han’s work would have had years earlier.  Thus, plaintiffs propose producing documents sufficient to allow those total sales figures and a declaration as needed to confirm the authenticity of those documents.  Defendants propose production of documents sufficient to determine Han’s net revenue from HarperAlley as it pertain to unOrdinary:  (1) payments to Han, (2) documents showing total sales of unOrdinary, and (3) the agreement between Han/Webtoon and HarperAlley.

            Thus, in comparing the parties’ positions, the parties both agree as to plaintiffs’ production of documents showing total sales of unOrdinary.  It is unclear as to the relevancy of payments to Han and the agreement between Han/Webtoon and HarperAlley. 

            The Court rules as follows:  In consideration of the factors under CCP §2024.050(b), the Court finds that reopening discovery is warranted in light of the print version of unOrdinary, but limited to only documents showing total sales of unOrdinary and a declaration as to the authenticity of those documents. 

            The motion is thus GRANTED in part.

 

            Motion to bifurcate

            Pursuant to CCP §§1048(b) and 598, plaintiff Han (and joined by plaintiff Webtoon Entertainment, Inc.) requests an order bifurcating and trying first the issue of rescission of the Rocketship Agreement, contending that it is an equitable issue. 

Plaintiffs argue that rescission is an equitable remedy and that an early determination of the issue of rescission will serve the interests of judicial economy and efficiency, and the convenience of the parties, in that such a determination could cause this matter to be resolved without the need for a separate jury trial with respect to the parties’ legal claims for damages.  Plaintiffs contend that if the court orders rescission of the Rocketship Agreement, then Rocketship’s two remaining counterclaims would be rendered moot.   Citing to Civil Code §1689, plaintiffs argue that fraud is a ground for rescission.

In opposition, defendant argues that while rescission, as a remedy, is equitable, the question of fraudulent inducement upon which plaintiffs wish to rely to seek the rescission remedy is not and that the gist of plaintiff’s action is a claim for fraudulent inducement.  Defendant asserts that recission is a remedy election that follows factual determination of the right to make the election.  Further, defendant contends, the complaint does not state that plaintiffs are seeking rescission as a remedy, including under the 4th cause of action for fraudulent inducement.  Defendant also asserts that although plaintiffs asserted an affirmative defense of fraudulent inducement, whether a party detrimentally relied on the misrepresentation of another party is properly left to a jury.

Civil Code §1689 states in part:  “(b) A party to a contract may rescind the contract in the following cases:  (1) If the consent of the party rescinding, or of any party jointly contracting with him, was given by mistake, or obtained through duress, menace, fraud, or undue influence, exercised by or with the connivance of the party as to whom he rescinds, or of any other party to the contract jointly interested with such party.”

It is the gist of the action that determines whether the action is for the enforcement of a right recognized only in equity, in which case there is no right to a jury trial.  C & K Engineering Contractors v. Amber Steel Co. (1978) 23 Cal. 3d 1, 8.  “[T]he right of the parties to a jury and the court in which the action must be brought will be determined by the nature of the substantive relief requested and not by the form of the complaint.”  Runyan v. Pac. Air Indus. (1970) 2 Cal. 3d 304, 313.  “Where no damages are sought, and a plaintiff seeks only to rescind, the action is equitable and a jury trial is not a matter of right.”  Hastings v. Matlock (1985) 171 Cal. App. 3d 826, 835.

The Court notes that plaintiffs have not sought a prayer for relief of rescission.  Rather, they seek declaratory relief, damages, an order that defendants be enjoined from printing the unOrdinary comic and any further Webtoon comics to which defendants improperly obtained printing rights, an award of punitive damages, an award of costs and disbursements, and for such other and further relief as the court deems just and property.  Further, although plaintiffs generally allege rescission at para. 34, they do not allege such a remedy under any of the causes of action.  See Lectrodryer v. Seoulbank (2000) 77 Cal. App. 4th 723, 728 (“As [the defendant] acknowledges, [the plaintiff’s] claim for restitution was ‘technically an action at law.’  Indeed, [the plaintiff] sought and received damages as its only remedy.  Such a remedy, even when equitable principles are applied, indicates the gist of the claim is legal in nature.”) (citation omitted).  Plaintiffs also have not alleged that Han rescinded the agreement or make a claim for rescission.

The motion is thus DENIED.

 

ORDER

            Defendants’ motion to reopen discovery is GRANTED in part.  Discovery is reopened to allow defendants to obtain documents sufficient to show total sales of unOrdinary and a declaration as to the authenticity of those documents.

            Plaintiffs’ motion to bifurcate is DENIED.

            Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 22TRCV01568    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: B

POURAN YAGHOUBI, AN INDIVIDUAL vs LOS ANGELES WORLD AIRPORTS, A PUBLIC ENTITY, et al.

Case No. 22TRCV01568

Ex Parte Application FOR ORDER CONTINUING TRIAL AND ALL RELATED DATES AND DEADLINES

“If at first you don't succeed, try, try again.”  The origins of this phrase are not entirely clear, having been quoted as far back as 1840 but popularised in Edward Hickson's Moral Song (1857).  Whoever originally coined it, most people would agree with its sentiment.

This is a premises liability case.

“Defendants negligently, recklessly and/or wantonly owned, maintained, managed, operated, controlled and safeguarded the premises at or near 200 World Way, Los Angeles, CA 90045, such that an unknown object and/or substance and/or liquid was allowed to exist and/or accumulate, thereby causing the area to be slippery and constituting a dangerous condition…”  (Complaint Attachment.)

When the parties failed to comply with this Court’s June 27, 2023 Order that they conduct a mediation or two hour face-to-face attempt to settle by the Post Mediation Status Conference scheduled for June 6, 2024, they submitted their Joint Stipulation to Continue Post-Mediation Status Conference.  March 6, 2024 was requested as a new date for the post mediation conference.  This date was a month after the then ordered trial date of February 5, 2025.  The Court rejected that request.  It advised the parties that all future dates remained on calendar.  The trial date of February 5, 2025 had been set on June 27, 2023.  Further, the Court advised the attorneys that there would be no continuance of the trial absent extreme good cause.

As the trial date approached, the parties on November 15, 2024 submitted a Stipulation asking that the trial be continued to May 29, 2025.  The Stipulation was wholly devoid of any explanation purporting to justify the request.  This Court wrote a 4 page Order denying that request, reciting the history of the case and reminding the parties that the matter remained on calendar for a February 5, 2025 trial.  The Court stated in that Order:

“Apparently the parties view court orders as mere suggestions.  They do not appear to take seriously the orders of this court.  They ignored the court’s order regarding mediation.  They now ignore the court’s June 6, 2024 order that there would be no continuance absent extreme good cause.  Indeed, their form stipulation (using a form was used in the Central District HUB) provides no explanation whatsoever for a requested continuance of the trial date.

The court DENIES the request for a continuance of the trial and final status conference.  The Court expects the parties to timely and fully comply with the June 27, 2023 Case Management Conference Order setting forth the trial preparation obligations.”

The parties have now scheduled this ex parte application supported by a Joint Stipulation to Continue Trial.  They have provided the Court some explanation for their inability to be prepared for the February 5, 2025 trial date, and now ask that the trial not merely be delayed until May 29, 2024 as requested merely 3 weeks ago, but now seek to have the trial delayed an additional 2 months until August 4, 2025.

The Court makes the following Order:

The Ex Parte Application is continued for hearing on December 12, 2024 at 9:00 a.m. in this Department.

The parties are ordered to meet and confer and prepare a Joint Discovery Plan to be filed no later than December 9, 2024 which:

1.  Lists all past discovery (interrogatories, document requests, request to admit) with the dates each was initiated and responded to.

2. Lists each deposition that was taken, the date when it was noticed and that date it actually took place.

3.  Lists each deposition that a party desires to take in the future, the date a Notice shall be served and that date that it shall be noticed to commence.

4.  Lists each discovery tool that shall be invoked (interrogatories, document requests, request to admit) and when it shall be served.

5.  Lists all third party discovery that the parties shall commence, the date it shall be initiated and completed.

6.  Lists all medical examinations and when Notice shall be served and the exam performed.

7.  Lists  the date by which all discovery shall be completed.

8.  Explain why the parties failed to comply with the June 6, 2024 mediation completion date set by the Court in June 2023 and why a mediation is now scheduled for April 12, 2025, a date nearly 5 months in the future and 10 months after the Court ordered date.  The parties must explain why it is not possible to conduct a mediation within approximately 45-60 days.

9.  Provide any additional information that the parties believe shall assist the Court in setting a new trial date.

The parties must be fully prepared to discuss the Joint Discovery plan, enter into appropriate stipulations intended to expedite remaining discovery, and otherwise materially advance the preparation f this case for trial.  It is exceedingly unlikely that the Court shall afford the parties nearly the amount of additional delay of the trial that they are requesting.
