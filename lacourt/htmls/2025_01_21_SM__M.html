DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV02647    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Sanchez v. Mizban, et al.

CASE NO.:                21STCV02647

MOTION:                 Defendants Kasra Mizban and Nasser Mizban’s Motion for Cost of Proof Sanctions

HEARING DATE:   01/21/2025

 

Background

 

On January 22, 2021, Plaintiff Gabriel R. Sanchez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Kasra Mizban (“K. Mizban”); Nasser Mizban (“N. Mizban”) (collectively, “Defendants”); and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive for: (1) Negligence and (2) Negligent Entrustment. The Complaint alleges Defendants negligently drove their vehicle causing it to rear-end Plaintiff’s vehicle and causing Plaintiff to sustain injuries. (Compl., ¶¶15-16.)

 

On August 31, 2022, the Court granted Defendant N. Mizban’s summary adjudication motion as to the second cause of action for negligent entrustment.

 

On March 8, 2023, Defendants filed a joint First Amended Answer to Complaint. Defendant K. Mizban also filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff Gabriel Sanchez, Veronica Garcia (“Garcia”) (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”), and ROES 1 through 50, inclusive for: (1) Negligent Entrustment; (2) Negligence – Tort of Another; (3) Equitable Indemnity; (4) Contribution; (5) Apportionment of Fault; and (6) Declaratory Relief. On June 5, 2023, Cross-Defendants filed an Answer to Cross-Complaint. On September 26, 2024, the Court dismissed the Cross-Complaint as to Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Gabriel Sanchez only.

 

On September 25, 2023, Defendant/Cross-Complainant K. Mizban filed a Motion to Deem Requests for Admission (Set One) Admitted. On October 26, 2023, the Court found the motion to be moot but imposed sanctions in the amount of $690.00.

 

            On April 11, 2024, the Court granted in part Defendants’ summary adjudication motion as to their Eleventh Affirmative Defense that Plaintiff’s damages are limited to economic damages pursuant to Civil Code Section 3333.4.

 

            On September 9, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion for Costs of Proof Sanctions Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.420 in the amount of $458,771.74 against Plaintiff Gabriel Sanchez. On January 7, 2025, Plaintiff filed an Opposition. On January 13, 2025, Defendants filed a Reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

“If a party fails to admit the genuineness of any document or the truth of any matter when requested to do so under this chapter, and if the party requesting that admission thereafter proves the genuineness of that document or the truth of that matter, the party requesting the admission may move the court for an order requiring the party to whom the request was directed to pay the reasonable expenses incurred in making that proof, including reasonable attorney’s fees.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.420, subd. (a).)

 

“The court shall make this order unless it finds any of the following: [¶](1) An objection to the request was sustained or a response to it was waived under Section 2033.290. [¶](2) The admission sought was of no substantial importance. [¶](3) The party failing to make the admission had reasonable ground to believe that that party would prevail on the matter. [¶](4) There was other good reason for the failure to admit.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.420, subd. (b).

 

Analysis

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Kasra Mizban and Defendant Nasser Mizban (hereinafter, “Defendants”) move for an order awarding costs of proof sanctions including attorneys’ fees in the amount of $458,771.74 against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Gabriel Sanchez (“Plaintiff”) on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to admit duly served Requests for Admission (“RFA”). Specifically, Defendants argue that the granting of their April 11, 2024, MSA determined various Requests for Admissions denied by Plaintiff were in fact true including that Plaintiff’s damages were limited to economic damages pursuant to Civil Code Section 3333.4 because Plaintiff cannot establish his financial responsibility. Defendants therefore argue Plaintiff had no good reason for his failure to admit the RFAs that were served on him.

 

The Court has set forth the relevant RFAs:

 

RFA No. 22: “Admit that YOU had YOUR driver’s license suspended prior to the INCIDENT (hereinafter the term “INCIDENT” shall mean or refer to the motor vehicle accident/incident that took place on or about October 6, 2019 that is the subject of this lawsuit and YOUR operative complaint).”

 

Plaintiff’s response: “Objection, vague and ambiguous as to “YOU had YOUR driver’s license suspended.” General objections incorporated herewith. Responding party has insufficient information to admit or deny at this time. Discovery and investigation are ongoing and Plaintiff reserves the right to supplement, amend, and modify this response.”

 

(Hollins Decl., ¶¶4, 11-13, Exs. B – Plf. Resp. to Def. UMF 20, C, D, and E.)

 

RFA No. 32: Admit that YOU provided a verified response to written discovery stating that YOU had a California Driver’s License at the time of the INCIDENT despite not having a California Driver’s License at the time of the INCIDENT.

 

Plaintiff’s response: “General objections incorporated herewith. Responding party has insufficient information to admit or deny at this time. Discovery and investigation are ongoing and Plaintiff reserves the right to supplement, amend, and modify this response.”

 

Amended response: “Deny.”

 

(Id. at ¶¶11-12, 14, Exs. C, D, and F.)

 

RFA No. 6: “Admit that YOUR driver’s license was suspended at the time of the INCIDENT because of a medical condition that YOU suffered from.”

 

Plaintiff’s response: “Objection. Vague and ambiguous as to “driver’s license” and “medical condition.” General objections are incorporated herein. Notwithstanding and without waiving any of the foregoing objections, Responding Party responds as follows: Deny. Responding Party reserves the right to amend and/or supplement this response, as discovery and investigation are ongoing and continuing.”

 

(Id. at ¶¶4, 15-17, Exs. B – Plf. Resp. to Def. UMF 27, 31-34, G, H, and E.)

 

RFA No. 7: “Admit that YOUR driver’s license was suspended at the time of the INCIDENT because the California Department of Motor Vehicles determined that YOU posed a threat to traffic safety and should not be driving.”

 

Plaintiff’s response: “Objection. Vague and ambiguous as to “driver’s license.” General objections are incorporated herein. Notwithstanding and without waiving any of the foregoing objections, Responding Party responds as follows: Deny. Responding Party reserves the right to amend and/or supplement this response, as discovery and investigation are ongoing and continuing.”

 

(Id., Exs. B – Plf. Resp. to Def. UMF 32, G, H.)

 

RFA No. 10: “Admit that YOU have never had a New York state driver’s license.”

 

Plaintiff’s response: “Objection. Vague and ambiguous as to “driver’s license.” General objections are incorporated herein. Notwithstanding and without waiving any of the foregoing objections, Responding Party responds as follows: Deny. Responding Party reserves the right to amend and/or supplement this response, as discovery and investigation are ongoing and continuing.”

 

(Id., Exs. B – Plf. Resp. to Def. UMF 36, G, H.)

 

RFA No. 20: “Admit that Allstate Insurance determined that there was excessive permissive use of the VEHICLE by YOU with regards to the INCIDENT.”

 

Plaintiff’s response: “Deny.”

 

(Id. at ¶¶4, 19-20, Exs. B – Plf. Resp. to Def. UMF 88, J, K.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues his denials of RFA Nos. 22, 32, 6, 7, and 10 have no bearing on liability in this matter or any defenses because the Court’s summary adjudication ruling was due to Plaintiff’s lack of insurance at the time of the incident, not Plaintiff’s license status or suspension of his license. (Opp. at 4:15-23.) Plaintiff also contends his license suspension had no causal connection to the accident or its consequences. (Id. at 5:28-6:1.) Furthermore, Plaintiff contends his inability to recover non-economic damages due to lack of insurance under Civil Code Section 3333.4 has no bearing on his right to recover economic damages. (Id. at 6:8-10.) Similarly, Plaintiff asserts his license status at the time of the accident is irrelevant to the determination of whether Defendants breached their duty of care or caused the collision. (Id. at 6:17-19.) Likewise, Plaintiff argues his denial of RFA No. 20 was made in good faith based on the information available to him at the time, i.e., that he was covered under the Allstate policy and was not excluded until after the subject collision occurred. (Id. at 7:1-8.) Finally, Plaintiff contends Defendants’ requested costs are excessive and unsupported, thus are punitive rather compensatory and in violation of Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.420.

 

Here, Defendants’ have shown that Plaintiff unreasonably denied the RFAs at issue in this present motion. Although Plaintiff contends RFA Nos. 22, 32, 6, 7, and 10 are of no substantial importance to this case, the Court disagrees. In this lawsuit, Plaintiff seeks to recover economic damages for Negligence and Defendant/Cross-Complainant K. Mizban alleges causes of action for Negligence – Tort of Another, Negligent Entrustment, and Apportionment of Fault against Plaintiff. It is reasonable to seek admission of Plaintiff’s driver’s license status for the determination of an affirmative defense to Plaintiff’s Negligence claim and/or determination of apportionment of fault. Moreover, the summary adjudication motion made several references to the suspension of Plaintiff’s driver’s license. (Def. Reply at 2:25-27.)

 

As to RFA No. 20, Plaintiff was aware of facts prior to his response to the RFAs that were sufficient to illicit a proper admission to this request due to Defendants’ Motion for Terminating Sanctions, which included documentation form Allstate Insurance indicating that there was a determination of excessive use of the vehicle by Plaintiff. (Id. at 5:12-16, Ex. B.) Thus, Plaintiff had no good faith basis to “deny” RFA No. 20 at the time he provided his response. To the extent Plaintiff claims he denied RFA No. 20 due to lack of information or belief he was insured by Allstate, one can reasonably infer that Plaintiff had access to the Allstate policy information at the time the RFAs were served on him, therefore Plaintiff had a duty to investigate such information. (See Bloxham v. Saldinger (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 729, 752 [175 Cal.Rptr.3d 650, 668], as modified on denial of reh’g (Aug. 27, 2014).)

 

With respect to the $458,771.74  in attorneys’ fees and costs requested by Defendants on this matter, the Court finds that the request is grossly unreasonable and not fully supported by the declaration of Andrew S. Hollins. In Garcia v. Hyster Co. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 724, the court held that Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.420, subdivision (a) (formerly Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033, subdivision (o)) is not operative until after the party serves their denial, thus any expenses awarded that were incurred prior to the denial are improper. (Garcia v. Hyster Co. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 724, 736-737.) Likewise, the statute only authorizes expenses incurred in proving the matters denied by the opposing party. (Id.)

 

Here, based upon the limited information contained in the declaration of Andrew Hollins, and the Court’s experience with the case, the Court believes that Defendants have sought attorney’s fees from the inception of the case.  The Hollins declaration fails to identify the specific time spent on the tasks necessary to prove Sanchez’s driver’s license was indeed suspended, including time spent preparing multiple subpoenas to the California and New York DMVs; meeting and conferring with Sanchez’s counsel and the DMVs regarding confidential information; attempting to serve and depose the medical doctors identified in the DMV records related to a medical suspension of Sanchez’s driver’s license; and filing a Motion for the Release of Unredacted DMV Records because Sanchez refused to provide an authorization for the release of the unredacted DMV records.  It is hard to conceive that counsel spent approximately 2,185 hours on these tasks. Since they have not been broken down, the Court will determine a reasonable amount of fees for these limited tasks, which in some cases, were not completed centered on the sanctions issue before the Court. 

 

Based upon the Court’s experience in general, and its experience handling this matter and the numerous motions filed by Defendants related to these issues, the Court concludes that $16,800 is a reasonable sanction based upon a reasonable attorney’s time handling these tasks at the previously identified rate of $210.00 an hour. 

 

Based on the foregoing, the Motion for Costs of Proof Sanctions is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $16,800.00.

 


Case Number: 24SMCV02388    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Tohid Naeem v. Longevity Hyperbarics, LLC

CASE NO.:                24SMCV02388

MOTION:                  Longevity Hyperbarics, LLC’s Demurrer with Motion to Strike

HEARING DATE:    1/21/2025

 

Background

Plaintiff alleges that he is a disabled veteran who brings this action in pro per for gross violations of privacy, confidentiality, and professional duty. (First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶ 1.) The complaint alleges that on or about April 26, 2024, Plaintiff received medical treatment at Defendant's facility, including hyperbaric oxygen and red-light therapy. Plaintiff sought these services to alleviate physical and mental symptoms related to his disabilities. (Id., ¶ 8.)

Plaintiff further alleges that during this session, without Plaintiff's knowledge or consent, a technician employed by Defendant accessed Plaintiffs personal belongings and photographed Plaintiffs U.S. passport using her personal phone. (FAC, ¶ 9.) Plaintiff discovered the unauthorized photographing of his passport, confronted the technician, and coerced her to delete the photographs. However, Plaintiff is uncertain whether copies of the photographs were retained, disseminated, or shared before deletion. (Id., ¶ 10.) The FAC alleges that upon information and belief, the technician involved in this egregious breach of privacy has a criminal history. Discovery is necessary to ascertain the full extent of this technician's background and whether Defendant was aware of or should have been aware of her criminal history. (Id., ¶ 11.)

Legal Standard

 

“Before filing a demurrer . . . the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading . . . for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41.) 

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint alleges facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10; Young v. Gannon (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 209, 220.  The court “may consider all material facts pleaded in the complaint and those arising by reasonable implication therefrom; it may not consider contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  (Young v. Gannon (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 209, 220 (citing Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638; Montclair Parkowners Assn. v. City of Montclair (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 784, 790).) The court treats all facts alleged in the complaint to be true.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 732.)

 

When considering demurrers, courts “are required to construe the complaint liberally to determine whether a cause of action has been stated, given the assumed truth of the facts pleaded.”  (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733 (citing Rogoff v. Grabowski (1988) 200 Cal.App.3d 624, 628.))  “The burden is on the plaintiff to demonstrate the manner in which the complaint can be amended.” (Ross v. Creel Printing & Publishing Co. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 736, 748.)

 

Where there are grounds for a demurrer and a motion to strike, they must be filed together and noticed for hearing at the same time. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435(b)(3) and CRC Rule 329.) 

 

Analysis

Demurrer

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for breach of confidentiality, third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED), fourth cause of action for negligence, fifth cause of action for Violation of Civil Code § 1798.53, sixth cause of action for negligent inflectional of emotional distress (NEID), seventh cause of action for negligent hiring, retention and supervision, eighth cause of action for violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200, and the ninth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.  Defendants argue that the FAC fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute these causes of action.

Meet and Confer

 

The Court finds that the parties complied with the meet and confer requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41.  (Drossel Decl., ¶ 2.)

 

COA 2: Breach of Confidentiality, and COA 5: Violation of Civil Code § 1798.53

 

Defendant demurs to second cause of action for breach of confidentiality and the fifth cause of action for Violation of Civil Code § 1798.53, arguing that Plaintiff does not allege disclosure of personal information. Instead, Plaintiff contends that he “coerced” the deletion of the photographs of his passport, thereby preluding Defendant from disclosing his personal information.  Civil Code §1798.53 provides: 

Any person, other than an employee of the state or of a local government agency acting solely in his or her official capacity, who intentionally discloses information, not otherwise public, which they know or should reasonably know was obtained from personal information maintained by a state agency or from ‘records’ within a ‘system of records’ (as these terms are defined in the Federal Privacy Act of 1974 (P.L. 93-579; 5 U.S.C. 552a)) maintained by a federal government agency, shall be subject to a civil action, for invasion of privacy, by the individual to whom the information pertains.

“The term ‘personal information’ means any information that is maintained by an agency that identifies or describes an individual, including, but not limited to, his or her name, social security number, physical description, home address, home telephone number, education, financial matters, and medical or employment history. It includes statements made by, or attributed to, the individual.” (Civil Code §1798.3(a).)

“The term ‘disclose’ means to disclose, release, transfer, disseminate, or otherwise communicate all or any part of any record orally, in writing, or by electronic or any other means to any person or entity.” (Civ. Code § 1798.3, subd. (c).)  No breach of confidentiality occurs absent a disclosure of confidential information. (See Sutter Health v. Superior Court (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 1546, 1557-1559 (noting that no breach of confidentiality occurs unless private information is disclosed and viewed by an unauthorized party).)

            Here, Plaintiff has failed to allege Defendant disclosed his personal information to a third party. Plaintiff only alleges that the employee photographed his passport and Plaintiff is uncertain whether copies of the photographs were retained, disseminated, or shared before deletion. As such, the demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the second and fifth causes of action for breach of confidentiality and violation of Civil Code §1798.53.

             

COA 3: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to allege extreme and outrageous conduct, and that Plaintiff simply contends that Defendant photographed his passport without his consent. Further, Defendant argues that the FAC fails to set forth allegations of severe or substantial emotional distress.

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.”  (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903, internal quotations omitted.)  For conduct to be outrageous it must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”  (See Ess v. Eskaton Properties, Inc. (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 120, 130.)  Liability does not extend to mere insults, indignities, threats, annoyances, petty oppressions, or other trivialities.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1051.)  “Severe emotional distress means . . . emotional distress of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable man in a civilized society should be expected to endure it.”  (Fletcher v. Western Nat. Life Ins. Co. (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 376.)  

“[I]t is not enough that the defendant has acted with an intent which is tortious or even criminal, or that he has intended to inflict emotional distress, or even that his conduct has been characterized by ‘malice,’ or a degree of aggravation which would entitle the plaintiff to punitive damages for another tort.”  (Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 488, 496.)  “Liability has been found only where the conduct has been so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community.”  (Id.)  While there is no bright-line as to what constitutes outrageous conduct and thus this involves a case-by-case analysis, courts can determine whether conduct was sufficiently outrageous at the demurrer stage.  (Id., at 494.) 

The court agrees that Plaintiff has not alleged extreme and outrageous conduct. Plaintiff only alleges that the employee went through his belongings and took a photograph of his passport. Without more, the Court cannot say this conduct would go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community.

Additionally, the allegations relating to suffering severe emotional distress are insufficient to meet the high bar to establish severe emotional distress.  (See e.g., Pittman v. City of Oakland (1988) 197 Cal.App.3d 1037, 1047 [mere allegation that plaintiff suffered shame, humiliation and embarrassment without further factual explanation fails to state claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress]; see also Hughes, supra, 46 Cal.4th at 1051.) Plaintiff alleges he is under the care of a psychiatrist, attending monthly sessions, and is seeking further psychological care to address the lasting emotional trauma stemming from the incident. Plaintiff alleges his trust in medical professionals has been permanently damaged, and he now feels paranoid and unsafe receiving medical treatments, particularly when required to disrobe, exacerbating his multiple disabilities. (FAC, ¶ 13.) At most, Plaintiff alleged he feels paranoid and unsafe receiving medical treatments, and he sees a psychologist once a month. As a result, the demurrer to the third cause of action for IIED is SUSTAINED.

COA 4: Negligence

Defendant demurs to the fourth cause of action for negligence, arguing that Plaintiff’s bare assertion that Defendant’s “fail[ure] to exercise reasonable care in supervising its employees . . . resulted in harm to Plaintiff” (FAC, ¶ 18) does not satisfy the “damages” element of a claim for negligence. Defendant also argues that Plaintiff’s speculative assertion that he suffers “risk of further damage through potential misuse of [his] personal information” (FAC, ¶ 32) does not serve as a basis for the fourth cause of action for negligence.

To state a claim for negligence, a plaintiff must allege: 1) a legal duty to use due care; 2) breach of this legal duty; and 3) that results in 4) plaintiff’s injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.) “Ordinarily, negligence may be alleged in general terms, without specific facts showing how the injury occurred, but there are ‘limits to the generality with which a plaintiff is permitted to state his cause of action, and ... the plaintiff must indicate the acts or omissions which are said to have been negligently performed. He may not recover upon the bare statement that the defendant’s negligence has caused him injury.’ [Citation].” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527.)  

Plaintiff has pled that he is uncertain whether copies of the photographs were retained, disseminated, or shared before deletion, and that he suffered severe emotional distress as a result of the incident. Plaintiff alleges he is under the care of a psychiatrist, attending monthly sessions, and is seeking further psychological care to address the lasting emotional trauma stemming from the incident. (FAC, ¶ 13.) As noted above, negligence, including the element of damages, may be alleged in general terms. The demurrer to the cause of action for negligence is therefore OVERRULED.

COA 6: Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

Defendant argues the third cause of action for NIED fails because it is not an independent tort. California courts have repeatedly recognized that NIED is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence such that the traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply. (See e.g., Spates v. Dameron Hospital Association (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 213; Marlene F. v. Affiliated Psychiatric Medical Clinic, Inc. (1989) 48 Cal.3d 583, 588.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff has already alleged a cause of action for negligence based on the same facts as Plaintiff’s claim for NIED.  To this extent, the negligence and NIED causes of action are duplicative and the demurrer to the NIED cause of action may be sustained on such grounds.  (Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501 (stating that a demurrer may be sustained when a cause of action is duplicative of another cause of action and “thus adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery”; see Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)  Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. 

COA 7: Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision

A cause of action for negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of an employee requires the following elements: (1) the employer hired employee; (2) the employee was/became unfit or incompetent to perform the work for which he was hired; (3) the employer knew or should have known the employee was/became unfit or incompetent and that this unfitness or incompetence created a particular risk to others; (4) the employee’s unfitness or incompetence harmed plaintiff; and (5) the employer’s negligence in hiring/supervising/ retaining the employee was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  (CACI No. 426; see also Federico v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1207, 1213-1214.)  To establish a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, or supervision, a plaintiff must show that the employer knew or should have known that hiring or retaining the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm occurs.  (See Z.V. v. County of Riverside (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 889, 902; Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1042, 1054.)  Negligent hiring, retention, or supervision is a form of direct negligence.  (Ibid.) 

Plaintiff’s FAC fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, and supervision. Namely, Plaintiff has failed to allege that Defendant knew or should have known that the employee was/became unfit or incompetent and that this unfitness or incompetence created a particular risk of harm to others, which materialized.  (Z.V., supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at p. 902 [holding to establish a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, or supervision, a plaintiff must show that the employer knew or should have known that hiring or retaining the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm occurs”].) Further, Plaintiff would have to also include facts supporting its allegation that Defendant knew or should have known that the employee was unfit or incompetent, that this incompetence created a particular risk of harm to others, and that particular harm occurs. Plaintiff only alleges that discovery is necessary to ascertain whether Defendant knew or should have been aware of the employee’s criminal history. (FAC, ¶ 11.) As a result, the demurrer to the seventh cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, and supervision is SUSTAINED.

COA 8: Violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 because Plaintiff does not allege Defendant unlawfully and/or unfairly obtained from Plaintiff money/property to which he is entitled to. Nor does Plaintiff adequately allege risk of further injury to warrant an injunction.

Unfair competition is broadly defined as “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice” as well as “unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and “any act prohibited by Chapter 1 (commencing with Section 17500) of Part 3 of Division 7 of the Business and Professions Code.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) An individual can only bring a Section 17200 claim if they have “suffered injury in fact” and have “lost money or property as a result of the unfair competition.”  (Id., § 17204.)  The remedies available under the UCL are limited to injunctive, restitutionary and related relief. (Bus. & Prof.Code, § 17203; State v. Altus Finance, S.A. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1284, 1303.) To obtain injunctive relief, the plaintiff must show that the wrongful conduct alleged in the complaint is ongoing or likely to recur. (Madrid v. Perot Systems Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 440, 464–466, 30 Cal.Rptr.3d 210.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff fails to allege that the wrongful conduct is ongoing or likely to occur or that he lost money because of Defendant’s conduct. As a result, the demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

COA 9: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

Again, Defendant argues that there was no breach of the fiduciary duty because Plaintiff fails to allege that the employee disclosed his personal information. The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)  As to the first element, the basic fiduciary obligations are twofold: undivided loyalty and confidentiality. (Pierce v. Lyman (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1102, superseded by statute on different issue, as stated in Pavicich v. Santucci (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 382, 396.) A fiduciary or confidential obligation or relationship can arise when confidence is reposed by persons in the integrity of others, and if the latter voluntarily accepts or assumes to accept the confidence, he or she may not act so as to take advantage of the other's interest without that person's knowledge or consent.  (Oates v. Lincoln (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 25, 35.)

As noted above, There is no breach of confidentiality absent a disclosure of confidential information. (See Sutter Health v. Superior Court (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 1546, 1557-1559 (noting that no breach of confidentiality occurs unless private information is disclosed and viewed by an unauthorized party).) Thus, the demurrer to the ninth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

Motion to Strike

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s requests for punitive damages are unsupported. 

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. [or] (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.)  An “immaterial allegation” means “irrelevant matter” as that term is used in Section 436. (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10(c).)  “An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following: (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense. (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10(b).) 

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.) The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Id.) A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged, if proven, would not support a finding that the defendant acted with malice, fraud or oppression. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California (2010) 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63.) 

“Malice” is defined in section 3294(c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (See, e.g., Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.) 

To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.) Rather, a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he [or she] knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.” (Id. at 90). Further, the allegations must be sufficient for a reasonable jury to conclude that Defendant’s conduct was “despicable” defined as “base, vile or contemptible.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725.)

There are no facts pled which would show any malice or oppression on Defendant’s part. The FAC alleges that Defendant’s employee photographed Plaintiff’s passport. Without more, the FAC fails to state a claim or punitive damages. It is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. (Dawes, supra, 111 Cal. App. 3d 87.) The motion to strike punitive damages is GRANTED.

Leave to Amend

            Plaintiff’s fails to meet his burden demonstrating how the FAC could be amended successfully. However, Plaintiff filed a motion for leave to file second amended complaint (SAC), which alleges additional facts as to the cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress but otherwise fails to set forth additional relevant facts. As a result, leave to amend is granted only as to the claims for intentional infliction of emotional distress and punitive damages.  As to all other claims, the demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is sustained in part and overruled in part. It is sustained as to the second cause of action for breach of confidentiality, third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, fifth cause of action for violation of Civil Code § 1798.53, sixth cause of action for negligent inflectional of emotional distress, seventh cause of action for negligent hiring, retention and supervision, eighth cause of action for violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200, and ninth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.  It is overruled as to the fourth cause of action for negligence.

 

Plaintiff has 20 days leave to amend as to the cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress only.

            The motion to strike is granted with 20 days leave to amend.
