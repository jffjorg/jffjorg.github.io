ORDER RE:

AMENDED MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER RE: DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT AARONOFF, AND FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $4,050

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

 

Defendant Vidala Aaronoff (“Aaronoff”) moves for a protective order that prohibits Plaintiff Curtis R. Olson from taking Aaronoff’s deposition.

Aaronoff’s motion will be continued to a new date as set forth below. NO HEARING WILL TAKE PLACE ON MAY 31, 2024.    

Pursuant to the Court’s power to “amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a)(8)), the Court orders the parties in this case to participate in a meet and confer with the Court (“Informal Discovery Conference” or “IDC”). Lead or other designated counsel for the parties with full authority are ordered to participate in person in an IDC. After consulting with opposing counsel regarding available dates, Aaronoff must make a prompt reservation for the IDC using the Court’s online reservation system. Aaronoff must file Dept. 50’s one-page IDC form in the department seven days prior to the IDC, and the responding parties may file the same form in the department setting forth a response three days prior to the IDC.  

Once Aaronoff has confirmed an IDC date, Aaronoff must use the Court’s online reservation system to continue the motion to a post-IDC discovery hearing date. The parties are ordered to have with them whatever materials are needed to make the IDC session productive and successful. Prior to the IDC date, lead or other designated counsel for the parties, with full authority, are to meet and confer, in person or via telephone in a further attempt to resolve as many of the issues as possible before the IDC. (See CRC Rule 3.670, subd. (f)(2).) If the parties resolve their discovery disputes before the IDC date, Aaronoff is ordered to take both the IDC and the motion off calendar as soon as possible. 

Aaronoff is ordered to provide notice of this Order. 

 

DATED:  May 31, 2024                                 ________________________________

Hon. Teresa A. Beaudet

Judge, Los Angeles Superior Court

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR AN ORDER COMPELLING DEFENDANT JOSEPH D. DAVIS TO PRODUCE DOCUMENTS AND FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST SAID DEFENDANT AND HIS ATTORNEYS OF RECORD;

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR AN ORDER COMPELLING DEFENDANT HILARY DAVIS TO PRODUCE DOCUMENTS AND FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST SAID DEFENDANT AND HER ATTORNEYS OF RECORD

 

 

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Douglas A. Bagby (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on December 22, 2022 against defendant Joseph Daniel Davis (“Joseph Davis”).  

Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on February 26, 2024 against Defendants Joseph Davis and Hilary Davis (jointly, “Defendants”). The TAC alleges four causes of action.

Plaintiff indicates that on August 31, 2023, he served a Request for Production of Documents, Set No. One, to Joseph Davis. (Bagby Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. A.) Plaintiff also indicates that on August 31, 2023, he served a Request for Production of Documents, Set No. One, to Hilary Davis. (Bagby Decl., ¶ 1, Ex. A.) Plaintiff states that “[o]n October 2, 2023…in lieu of providing any records, Mr. Davis and Hilary Davis served objections to all of the requests for production of documents served August 31, 2022 [sic].” (Bagby Decl., ¶ 8; Bagby Decl., ¶ 5.)

On March 22, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held in this matter. The Court’s March 22, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he matter is called for hearing…In open court, the Informal Discovery Conference is held. The parties have fulfilled their Informal Discovery Conference requirement regarding any motion to compel or compel further re: set one of the requests for production served by Plaintiff on August 31, 2024 [sic]. Any such motion may be filed within 45 days of today’s date.”

In his supporting declarations, Plaintiff states that “I granted Defendants until April 3, 2024, to ‘provide amended responses and produce the documents requested from your clients.’ On April 4, 2024, after the deadline had passed, I received amended responses on behalf of Defendants in which they purport to amend and add to the objections they submitted on October 2, 2023, after I denied their request for an extension of time to amend their objections.” (Bagby Decl., ¶ 19.; Bagby Decl., ¶ 11.)

Plaintiff now moves for an order “requiring defendant, Joseph D. Davis, to produce the documents sought in the pending motion and the Separate Statement filed concurrently herewith,” as well as an order requiring “Joseph D. Davis and his attorneys of record to pay sanctions to Plaintiff in the sum of $90 such [sic] other amount as the Court may deem appropriate for whatever time Plaintiff’s co-counsel may invest in the pending motion.” Joseph Davis opposes.

Plaintiff also moves for “an order requiring defendant, Hilary Davis, to produce the documents sought in the pending motion and the Separate Statement filed concurrently herewith,” as well as an order requiring “Hilary Davis and her attorneys of record to pay sanctions to Plaintiff in the sum of $90 plus such other amount as the Court may deem appropriate for whatever time Plaintiff’s co-counsel may invest in the pending motion.” Hilary Davis opposes.

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a) permits a propounding party to move for an order compelling a further response to a demand for inspection if the propounding party deems that a statement of compliance is incomplete, a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive, or an objection is without merit or too general. ((Id., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery sought and must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b).)

Discussion

As set forth above, Plaintiff moves for orders compelling Defendants to “produce the documents sought” in the instant motions.

It appears Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set No. One to Defendants. In his declaration in support of the motion pertaining to Joseph Davis, Plaintiff states, inter alia, that he “make[s] this declaration in support of [his] motion to compel Defendant, Joseph D. Davis…to produce what [he] contend[s] to be properly discoverable documents sought in the Request For Production served August 31, 2023…” (Bagby Decl., ¶ 1.) Similarly, in his declaration in support of the motion pertaining to Hilary Davis, Plaintiff states, inter alia, that he “make[s] this declaration in support of [his] motion to compel Defendant, Hilary Davis to produce what [he] contend[s] to be properly discoverable documents sought in the Request For Production served August 31, 2023…” (Bagby Decl., ¶ 1.)

Plaintiff asserts that good cause exists for the production of the records requested.

As an initial matter, Defendants assert in their oppositions that the instant motions are moot. Defendants indicate that on April 3, 2024, Defendants served Amended Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One. (Niedbalski Decls., ¶¶ 7, Exs. 4.) Defendants’ counsel states that “Plaintiff did not meet and confer on Defendant’s April 3, 2024 amended responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Set One.” (Niedbalski Decls., ¶¶ 9.) Defendants also note that “no informal discovery conference was held as to the alleged deficiency of the April 3, 2024 responses.” (Joseph Davis Opp’n at p. 2:9-10; Hilary Davis Opp’n at p. 2:7-8.)

As set forth above, Plaintiff appears to acknowledge that Defendants served amended responses to the discovery requests at issue. (See Bagby Decl., ¶ 19.; Bagby Decl., ¶ 11, “I received amended responses on behalf of Defendants in which they purport to amend and add to the objections they submitted on October 2, 2023…”) The Court notes that if Plaintiff contends that Defendants’ Amended Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One are deficient, a further IDC on this issue will be necessary. As Defendants served amended responses to the subject discovery requests, the Court agrees with Defendants that Plaintiff’s instant motions are moot.

Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against both Defendants. Plaintiff cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (h), which provides that “[e]xcept as provided in subdivision (j), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” As an initial matter, the Court does not find that Defendants unsuccessfully opposed the instant motions.

Moreover, Plaintiff cites to Kravitz v. Superior Court (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 1015, 1017, where the Court of Appeal found that “[a] pro se litigant cannot recover attorney’s fees as a discovery sanction, but he can recover the ‘reasonable expenses’ he has ‘incurred,’ including photocopying, computer-assisted legal research, and other identifiable and allocable costs.” Plaintiff acknowledges that he is “not permitted to request sanctions for the time spent by Plaintiff in drafting the subject motion[s],” but asserts that “Plaintiffs co-counsel may become involved in the pending motion if [Mr. Davis and Hilary Davis] files opposition [sic] to the motion and Plaintiff will request sanctions for any time his co-counsel invests in the matter through the hearing on the subject motion.” (Mots. at p. 6:25-26; 7:1-3.) Plaintiff does not appear to provide any evidence in connection with the motions or replies concerning purported time Plaintiff’s co-counsel spent on this matter.

In light of the foregoing, the Court denies Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions.

Lastly, Defendants seek monetary sanctions against “Bagby and his attorney” in connection with their oppositions to the instant motions. Defendants note that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following:…(h) Making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or to limit discovery. (i) Failing to confer in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning discovery, if the section governing a particular discovery motion requires the filing of a declaration stating facts showing that an attempt at informal resolution has been made.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (h)-(i).) Defendants cite to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a), which provides, inter alia, that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  

Defendants assert that “[i]nstead of…meeting and conferring in good faith, Bagby disregarded [Mr. Davis’ and Ms. Davis’] amended responses and filed this motion.” (Joseph Davis Opp’n at pp. 5:27-6:1; Hilary Davis Opp’n at p. 5:2-3.) Defendants assert that accordingly, the Court “should award sanctions against Bagby and his attorney in the amount of $1,000.00.” (Joseph Davis Opp’n at p. 6:3; Hilary Davis Opp’n at p. 5:5.) In his “reply declarations” in support of the motions, Plaintiff asserts that “[t]he amended responses on behalf of Defendants received April 4, 2024, purport to amend and add to the objections they submitted on October 2, 2023, after I denied their request for an extension of time to amend their objections. They did not respond timely and they added objections after I specifically denied their request for an extension of time to do so. Thus, I did not believe I was required to conduct a meaningless meet and confer with counsel for the defendants.” (Bagby Reply Decls., ¶ 2.)

Based on a consideration of the arguments and evidence of the parties, the Court does not find that Defendants have demonstrated that Plaintiff engaged in a “misuse of the discovery process” such that sanctions are warranted under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010 and 2023.030. Thus, the Court denies Defendants’ requests for sanctions.

            Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motions for orders compelling Joseph Davis and Hilary Davis to produce documents are denied. The parties’ requests for sanctions are denied.  

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order.
