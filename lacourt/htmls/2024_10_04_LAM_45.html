DEPARTMENT 45 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All rulings shown here are TENTATIVE ONLY, and thus oral argument WILL be heard. All Counsel are still required to attend.

Case Number: 23STCV13001    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

JEFF STEVEN EUSTICE, an individual,

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

 

AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC., and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive,

 

                              Defendants.

 
Case No.: 23STCV13001

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed: 6/07/2023

Trial Date: 4/28/2025

 

 

 

Hearing date:              10/4/2024       

Moving Party:             JEFF STEVEN EUSTICE, AN INDIVIDUAL (Plaintiff)

Responding Party:      AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC. (Defendant)

 

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production, Set One

The court has considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.  

The motion is GRANTED. Defendant American Hondo Motor Co Inc. is ordered to provide further responses to plaintiff Jeff Steven Eustice’s Request for Production, Set One Number 16, 17, 18, 29, 20, and 21 within twenty (20) days’ notice of this order. Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Background

On June 7, 2023, plaintiff Jeff Steven Eustice (plaintiff) filed suit against defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (defendant) for violations of statutory obligation s under the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Plaintiff alleged he purchased a 2022 Honda Accord (subject vehicle). The subject vehicle came with an express warranty. Upon delivery the subject vehicle presented with certain non-conformities including, but not limited to, defective body, powertrain, safety, electrical, braking and noise systems. Defendant failed to repair the subject vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts.   

Legal Standard

            In California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) “Under the discovery statutes, information is discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” (John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.) “The court shall limit discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).)

            In responding to a request for production, a party shall respond in one of the following way: (1) provide a statement that they will comply, (2) provide a representation that they lack the ability to comply, or (3) object to a particular demand. (Code Civ. Proc., §2031.210, subd. (a).) “A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand. . .shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Code Civ. Proc., §2031.230.)

Discussion

            Meet and Confer

            “The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

            The court finds plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirements. Plaintiff’s counsel Noreelie Panhwar declares that she sent a meet and confer and email on October 5, 2023, she sent another meet and confer meet and confer letter on November 14, 2023. She declares defendant did not respond to either email. This is sufficient.

            Responses

Plaintiff takes issue with defendant’s responses to Request for Production Nos. 16-21. In each case, plaintiff is requesting documents regarding the electrical defect in vehicles of the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle. The documents requested are document pertaining to fixes; failure rates; customer complaints, claims and reported failures; decisions to issue notices to consumers; communications; and internal analysis or investigation about the electrical defect in the 2022 Honda Accord. In each case defendant objected entirely to the request and refused to respond. (Panhwar Decl., ¶17, Ex. 4.)

With respect to RFP No. 16-18 and 21, defendant argues it has provided code-compliant responses, either that such documents do not exist or that it will produce responsive documents. (Opp., at pp. 4:16-24.) These could be code compliant responses, but in both cases, the court’s review the responses provided were that defendant only objected. (Panhwar Decl., ¶17, Ex. 4.)  Defendant does not attach documentation showing its supplemented its original response to provide these responses. Since defendant has conceded that further responses are appropriate by virtue of supplying what the responses would be, the court grants the request as to these numbers.

With respect to RFP No. 19 and 20, the requests are for the consumer complaints and/or failure rates of other consumers’ cars.

1.      Grouds for Discovery

Plaintiff argues that defendant has issued numerous Technical Service Bulletins (TSBs) regarding the Electrical Defect of the 2022 Honda Accord, which necessarily would have been the product of monitoring efforts, internal investigations, analysis, and consumer complaints.  As such, plaintiff seeks this discovery to obtain information from defendant regarding how it dealt with this specific defect and the ramifications of its actions (e.g., whether Defendant provided its repair facilities with sufficient literature to complete repairs etc.). Here, the court agrees that the proffered reasons support that discovery of this information is appropriate.

2.      Objections

Defendant argues that it need not provide information because the requests pertain to the private information of other consumers. This argument is unconvincing since the request is not for consumer’s identifying information. However, it is not apparent why this information would need to have consumer identifying information such that its discovery is barred. Instead, defendant can refrain from disclosing customer identifying information.

Defendant argues the discovery is overly broad and not reasonably calculated to lead to discoverable evidence since plaintiff does not need to find the source of the problem, but only establish that the subject vehicle did not conform to the warranty. However, the standard for discovery is broad. Discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 541.) The court agrees with plaintiff that information surrounding how or whether defendant dealt with complaints could support plaintiff’s argument that defendant willfully failed to repair or address the electrical defect. Defendant’s citation to Ibrahim v. Ford Motor Co (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 878 is inapt. It does not stand for the principle that a willfulness determination is only based on the repair history of the plaintiff’s vehicle. Ibrahim specifically states that willful means simply that “[defendant] knew of its obligations but intentionally declined to fulfill them.” (Id. at p. 894.) Thus the evidence may support plaintiff’s suit.

Defendant objects that the term electrical defect is unclear, however, the court finds the definition sufficient. Plaintiff defined electrical defect  “to mean such defects which result in symptoms including, but not limited to: premature failure of battery; dead/faulty battery; battery deeply discharged; vehicle not starting; TSB #22-060; TSB #22-059; TSB #89-003; and any other concern identified in the repair history for the subject 2022 Honda Accord; Vehicle Identification Number 1HGCV1F40NA034781”. (Panhwar Decl., ¶17, Ex. 4.)  The term is sufficiently clear regarding the scope of the defect and the definition provides details to defendant to appropriate narrow its searches.

Defendant also raise objections that the documents requested are confidential, commercially sensitive, and proprietary or trade secret. A trade secret means “information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique or process that: (1) Derives independent economic value. . .from not being generally known to the public. . .; and (2) is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.” (Civ. Code, §3426.`, subd. (d).) A party that is claiming the trade secret privilege under Evidence Code section 1060 has the burden of proving the party’s entitlement to that privilege. (Amgen Inc v. California Correctional Health Care Servs. (2020) 47 Cal.App.5th 716, 733.) It is unclear why document surrounding an electrical defect are subject to a trade secret privilege. Further to the extent defendant is withholding information on this basis, defendant has failed to sufficiently prove entitlement to the privilege. Defendant offers no justification for the claim of privilege.

Finally, to the extent defendant argues production would be an undue burden, defendant has failed to provide evidence supporting the claim of burden. Presently, they claim it could be a burden, but do not explain why it would be a burden. This is insufficient. (See Code Civ. Proc., §2031., subd. (e) [stating a party who claims an undue burden or expense, has the “burden of demonstrating that the information is from a source that is not reasonably accessible because of undue burden or expense”].)

The motion is granted.

Moving party is directed to give notice.

White, an individual (“Cross-Defendants”), and ROES 1-10, inclusive, alleging causes of action for  (1) Conversion; (2) Violation of CA Penal Code § 502; (3) Unfair Practices Act – CA Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq; (4) Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; and (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty.

            On February 5, 2024, Ms. Pak filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiffs; Andrew M. White, an individual; Jonathan N. White, an individual (“Cross-Defendants”), and ROES 1-10, inclusive, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Money Had and Received; (3) Common Count - Open Book Account; (4) Common Count - Account Stated; (5) Conversion; (6) Unauthorized Access to Computer Files and Documents (Violation of CA Penal Code § 502); (7) Violation of the Unfair Practices Act – CA Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq; (8) Battery; (9) Assault; (10) Sexual Battery; (11) Sexual Harassment in Violation of FEHA, Gov’t Code 12940, et seq; (12) Failure to Prevent Sexual Harassment in Violation of FEHA, Gov’t Code Sec. 12940(k); (13) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (14) Violations of the Elder And Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act) (Wel. & Inst. § 15600, et seq.).

On, August 22, 2024, Ms. Pak filed an opposition to the motion. On August 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply.  

 

Timeliness

            Notice of the motion to compel further must be provided within 45 days of service of the response, or any supplemental response, or on or before any specific later date to which the parties have agreed.  (Code Civ. Pro., § 2030.300, subd. (c).) This 45-day deadline runs from the date the verified response is served and is extended for service by mail in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure Sections 1010.6(a)(4) and 1013(a). (Ibid.) This is a jurisdictional requirement. (Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Super. Ct. (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)  

            Here, on May 14, 2024, Ms. Pak served Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Requests for Admission, Set One (“RFAs”) and Form Interrogatories, Set Two (“FROGs”), containing only objections and a lack of substantive responses. (White Decl., ¶ 10, Exhibits G-1 and G-2.) On June 6, 2024, Ms. Pak served her Further Reponses to Plaintiffs’ requests, following Plaintiff serving their Declaration for Additional Discovery and Requests for Admission, Set One. (Id. ¶ 12, Exhibit I.) Plaintiffs filed the instant motion on July 25, 2024, and the last substantive response from Ms. Pak was on June 6, 2024, which is 49 days apart.

            Thus, the timeliness requirement is met.

 

Separate Statement

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a separate statement containing the requests and the responses, verbatim, as well as reasons why a further response is warranted. (California Rule of Court, rule 3.1345(a).) The separate statement must also be complete in itself; no extrinsic materials may be incorporated by reference. (California Rule of Court, rule 3.1345(c).) 

Here, on July 23, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Separate Statement to the instant motion adhering to the requirements of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a). Thus, this requirement has been met.

 

 

 

 

Meet and Confer

The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b).)

Plaintiffs advance the declaration of their counsel of record, Jonathan N. White, attesting to the attempt to meet and confer made prior to the filing of the instant motions. On June 13, 2024, Attorney White provided a meet and confer letter to Ms. Pak’s counsel to identify the deficiencies in Ms. Pak’s responses to Plaintiffs’ RFAs. (White Decl., ¶ 13.) On June 21, 2024, Ms. Pak’s counsel provided their response to the meet and confer letter and refused to provide further responses to Plaintiffs’ discovery requests. (Id. ¶ 14, Exhibit K.) Plaintiffs’s counsel displayed a “reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)

Thus, Plaintiffs sufficiently met and conferred in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure Section 2016.04.

 

Discussion

Here, Plaintiffs are seeking to compel further responses to their RFAs, Set One, Nos. 36-104 and corresponding Form Interrogatory No. 17.1. (Motion, page 7.)

Request for Admissions

Regarding requests for admissions, the only proper responses are to 1) admit, 2) deny, or 3) state that a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter in the particular request has been made, and that the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable that party to admit the matter. (CCP § 2033.220(b).) Where any portion of the request is true the party to whom it is directed must admit that portion: “Each answer shall … [a]dmit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true, either as expressed in the request itself or as reasonably and clearly qualified by the responding party.” (CCP § 2033.220(b)(1).) Whether an admission is admitted to or denied, the admission or denial must be “as complete and straightforward” as the information available reasonably permits. (CCP. § 2033.220(a), (b)(1).) 

Where responses to requests for admissions have been served but requesting party believes that they are deficient because the answers are evasive or incomplete or because an objection is without merit, that party may move for an order compelling a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (a).)

Here, on May 14, 2024, Ms. Pak served Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Requests for Admission, Set One (“RFAs”) and Form Interrogatories, Set Two (“FROGs”), containing only objections and a lack of substantive responses. (White Decl., ¶ 10, Exhibits G-1 and G-2.) On May 21, 2024, Plaintiffs served their Declaration for Additional Discovery and Requests for Admission, Set One. (Id., Exhibit H-2.) On June 6, 2024, Ms. Pak served her Further Reponses to Plaintiffs’ requests, which contained many of the same objections as before, as well as refusing to provide responses to RFAs Nos. 36-104 because “[t]he requesting party has exceeded the numerical limit imposed by Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.030(b)” (Id., Exhibit G-1.) Ms. Pak has refused to provide responses to RFAs Nos. 36-104 on the basis that Plaintiffs served their Declaration for Additional Discovery “over one month later after they served their RFAs on Ms. Pak and after they already received Ms. Pak’s initial responses with objection as to the exceeding the limit of requests.” (Opposition, page 6.)

            According to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.030, “[u]nless a declaration as described in Section 2033.050 has been made, a party need only respond to the first 35 admission requests served that do not relate to the genuineness of documents, if that party states an objection to the balance under Section 2033.230  on the ground that the limit has been exceeded.”

Here, Ms. Pak was correct in objecting to RFAs Nos. 36-104 because Plaintiffs did not provide a Declaration for Further Discovery accompanying their initial service of RFAs, Set One. However, on May 21, 2024, Plaintiffs then served their Declaration for Further Discovery, which was followed by Ms. Pak refusing to provide responses because Plaintiffs served the Declaration after Ms. Pak provided her initial responses. (White Decl., Exhibit G-1.) There is no reason that Ms. Pak refused to provide responses to RFAs Nos. 36-104, since Plaintiffs eventually cured by serving a Declaration for Further Discovery that is compliant with Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.050. Additionally, Plaintiffs’ counsel attempted to meet and confer with Ms. Pak regarding this issue, and Ms. Pak again objected to the overlimit of the RFAs on the same grounds of Plaintiffs providing the Declaration for Further Discovery Ms. Pak served her initial responses. Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.030, subdivision (b) states that “unless a declaration . . . has been made, a party need only respond to the first 35 admission requests . . . .” In accordance with the Code, Plaintiffs provided Ms. Pak with a Declaration, so Ms. Pak could not have refused to respond to Plaintiffs’ RFAs Nos. 36-104.

Thus, Ms. Pak is compelled to provide further responses to Request for Admissions (Set One) Nos. 36-104 that are code-compliant.

           

Form Interrogatories

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.220, subdivision (a) requires that “[e]ach answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, a party may move to compel further responses to a form interrogatory if the other party’s answer is “evasive or incomplete.” A response must “represent the interrogated party's present best and complete answer.” (Fuss v. Sup. Ct. (1969) 273 Cal.App.2d 807, 816.)

Where respondents object or respond inadequately as to discovery requests, a motion lies to compel further responses, as to which respondent has the burden to justify the objections.¿ (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Sup. Ct. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)¿ Where general boilerplate objections are made, courts have authority to compel further responses (but not to find a waiver of privilege objections).¿ (Best Products, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 119 Cal.App. 4th 1181, 1189.)¿ 

Plaintiffs move to compel Ms. Pak to serve a further response to FROG No. 17.1. FROG 17.1 relates to the Requests for Admissions, Set One, which was served alongside Form Interrogatories, Set Two. (White Decl., Exhibit F-2.) The interrogatory asked Ms. Pak: “Is your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories an unqualified admission? If not, for each response that is not an unqualified admission:  

  1. state the number of the request; 
  1. state all facts upon which you base your response; 
  1. state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and 
  1. identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.” (Id.)

Ms. Pak’s provided further responses to RFAs Nos. 1-35 in her responses that were given on June 6, 2024. (White Decl., Exhibit. I-2.) Ms. Pak stated the number of the request and provided facts upon which she based her responses. (Id.) She provided the names of the parties who would have knowledge of the facts, including, Lucas Granillo, Michelle Pak, and Don Norris. (Id.) She also identified documents or other tangible things to support her responses, including text messages and an email. (Id.) Ms. Pak further stated that “all information she provided is what is currently available to her. Should Ms. Pak acquire additional information or documents, she will amend and/or supplement this response.” (Opposition, Exhibit F.) Thus, Ms. Pak is not compelled to provide further responses to FROG 17.1 as it pertains to RFAs. Nos. 1-35.

As for FROG 17.1 relating to RFAs Nos. 36-104, Ms. Pak must provide her responses because, as stated above, Ms. Pak cannot claim that refusing to respond to RFAs. Nos. 36-104 is justified on the basis that Plaintiffs did not serve a Declaration for Further Discovery. Thus, Ms. Pak is compelled to provide further responses to FROG 17.1 as it pertains to RFAs. Nos. 36-104.

 

Sanctions

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(d).)¿¿¿ CCP section 2033.290(d) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(d).)   

 Here, Plaintiffs argue that “Ms. Pak knowingly and intentionally provided meritless boilerplate objections, and withheld responsive answers to Plaintiffs' discovery requests,” and that Ms. Pak should be sanctioned $1,560.00. (Motion, page 24.) Ms. Pak argues that Plaintiffs “have filed the instant Motion to Compel without any basis” and “in bad faith,” and that Plaintiffs should be sanctioned $4,250.00. (Opposition, page 7.)

As explained above, Ms. Pak had no reason to deny responses to Plaintiffs’ RFAs Nos. 36-104 on the basis that the 35-interrogatory limit was exceeded because Plaintiffs eventually provided a Declaration for Further Discovery, compliant with Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.030, subdivision (b). Ms. Pak also refused to provide her responses after Plaintiffs sent a meet and confer letter attempting to informally resolve the issue. (Opposition, Exhibit F.) Further, Ms. Pak has failed to provide substantial justification to explain why she continuously refused to give substantive answers to Plaintiffs’ RFAs Nos. 36-104, despite Plaintiffs having cured and provided a Declaration for Further Discovery. Thus, sanctions against Ms. Pak would be proper.

Here, Plaintiffs’ counsel provides a declaration attesting to the calculation of the sanctions sought. (White Decl., ¶ 18-19.) He states that his hourly rate is $300/hour and that he spent over 15 hours on this matter, including drafting one motion, one separate statement, one proposed orders, one declaration, compiling declaration exhibits, finalizing all documents, anticipated work on the reply, and anticipated preparation and appearance for the hearings. (Id. ¶ 19.) This totals $4,500.00. However, Plaintiffs only requested $1,560.00 in expenses and attorneys’ fees in their Motion. This would amount to 5.2 hours of work on the motion, at Plaintiffs’ counsel rate of 300$/hour. The Court find this a reasonable amount of time to work on the motion, and a reasonable fee per hour, given counsel’s experience in civil litigations, transactional law and privacy law. The court will award what the Plaintiffs explicitly requested in sanctions.

 

Conclusion

Therefore, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel further responses to Requests for Admission, Set One is GRANTED.

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two is GRANTED only as it pertains to RFAs No. 36-104. 

Plaintiffs’ motion for monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,560.00 is GRANTED. Ms. Pak’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

Timeliness

            A

further response is warranted. (California Rule of Court, rule 3.1345(a).) The
separate statement must also be complete in itself; no extrinsic materials may
be incorporated by reference. (California Rule of Court, rule 3.1345(c).) 

Here, on May 23,
2024, Counsel for Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson filed a Separate
Statement to the instant motion adhering to the requirements of California
Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a). Thus, this requirement has been met.

 

Meet and Confer

The motion must
be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration in compliance with Code of Civil
Procedure Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (b); Code Civ.
Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b).)

Norris
Defendants and Taurin Robinson advance the declaration of their counsel of
record, Seungjai Oh, attesting to attempt to meet and confer made prior to the
filing of the instant motions. (Oh Decl, ¶ 10.) On April 29, 2024, Attorney Oh
sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiffs’ counsel to address the Plaintiffs’
non-code-compliant discovery responses to Norris Defendants’ and Taurin
Robinson’s RFPDs. (Id.) On May 3, 2024, the parties agreed to extend the
deadline to respond to the meet and confer to May 16, 2024, and Norris
Defendants’ and Taurin Robinson’s deadline to file motions to May 23, 2024. (Id.
¶ 11.) On May 16, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel sent a meet and confer response
letter and produced documents claimed to not be subject to a stipulated
protective order (“SPO”). (Id. ¶ 13.) On May 23, 2024, Norris Defendants
and Taurin Robinson filed the instant motions. Counsel for Norris Defendants and
Taurin Robinson displayed a “reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal
resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., §
2016.040.)

Thus, Norris
Defendants and Taurin Robinson sufficiently met and conferred in compliance
with Code of Civil Procedure Section 2016.04.

 

Discussion

Here, Norris
Defendants and Taurin Robinson are seeking to compel further responses to their
Request for Further Production, Set One.

 

Request for Production

Any party may
obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property or
electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not
privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible
evidence.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) A party can demand the production of
documents that is in the “possession, custody, or control of the party whom the
demand is made.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.010.) If a court determines the scope
of discovery imposes a burden, causes great expense or is intrusive,¿and such
outweighs the likelihood that the discoverable information sought will garner
admissible evidence, the court shall limit the scope of discovery. (Code Civ.
Proc. § 2017.020.)¿¿ 

            When
responses to an RPD are deficient, the propounding party may file a motion to
compel further responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310.) A motion to compel
further responses to RPDs may be brought when the responses contain: (1) “a
statement of compliance when the demand is incomplete;” (2) “answers that are
inadequate, incomplete or evasive;” and (3) “an objection to a particular
request is without merit or too general.” (Id.)¿¿ 

¿           A
motion to compel further responses must set forth specific facts showing good
cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., §
2031.310(b)(1).)¿It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material
sought will be admissible as¿evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify
discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for
effective trial preparation or to¿prevent surprise at trial. (Associated
Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court¿(1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-58.)

            Here,
Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson do not dispute that Plaintiffs eventually
provided Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson with Supplemental Responses
along with all requested documents. (Reply, page 2.) Norris Defendants and
Taurin Robinson concede that on September 19, 2024, Plaintiffs amended the
discovery responses to Norris Defendants’ and Taurin Robinson’s RFPDs and
“produced substantial discovery production.” (Id.) Norris Defendants
further do not argue that these supplemental responses are not code-compliant.
(Id.)

            Thus,
Norris Defendants’ and Taurin Robinson’s Motions to Compel Further Responses to
their RFPDs are moot.

 

Sanctions

Notwithstanding
the above, Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson suggest that their request for
sanctions is still at issue.

“The court shall
impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010)
against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a
motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that
the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that
other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ.
Proc. § 2030.300(d).) Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.290(d) provides for
mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who
unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it
finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification
or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 
(Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(d).)   

Here, sanctions
against Plaintiffs would not be appropriate. Norris Defendants and Taurin
Robinson argue that sanctions against Plaintiffs are warranted because of the
extensive delay in providing the discovery responses to Norris Defendants’ and
Taurin Robinson’s RFPDs. Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson suggest that Plaintiffs
did not produce any documents until May 16, 2024, when Plaintiffs provided
documents that they believed were not subject to an SPO. (White Decl., ¶ 7.) According
to Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson, Plaintiffs did not provide
code-compliant responses. (Motion, page 3.) Though, the parties were engaging
in correspondence relating to the execution an SPO when Attorney Oh rescinded
and voided his signatures on all versions of SPOs, indicating that negotiations
did not prove to be fruitful. (Oh Decl, ¶ 12.) This Court then granted
Plaintiffs a Protective Order on August 19, 2024. (Id. ¶ 8.) On
September 19, 2024, Plaintiffs then produced substantial discovery production
in compliance with the Court’s Protective Order. (Reply, page 2; see 08/19/24
Protective Order)

This Court finds
that Plaintiffs did not substantially delay discovery. Plaintiffs provided
supplemental production on the agreed extension deadline of May 16, 2024 with
documents Plaintiffs’ counsel believed to not be subject to an SPO. (Oh Decl.,
¶ 13-14.) This Court then entered a protective order on August 19, 2024 and
Plaintiffs provided Norris Defendants and Taurin Robinson with substantive
responses to their RFPDs 30 days later in compliance with the Court’s
protective order. (White Decl., ¶ 8-9.) The justification that Plaintiffs had
in refusing to provide further document production on May 16, 2024 (that some
documents should be subject to a protective order) thus proved to be accurate
given the Court’s entry of the protective order.

Therefore,
Norris Defendants’ motion for sanctions is DENIED.

 

Conclusion

Based on the
foregoing, Norris Defendants’ and Taurin Robinson’s Motions to Compel further
responses to Requests for Production, Set One are DENIED as moot.

Norris
Defendants’ and Taurin Robinson’s motions for monetary sanctions are DENIED.

 

It is so ordered.
