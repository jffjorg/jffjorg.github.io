DEPARTMENT I LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling. Please call the court no later than 4:30 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, leave a message with the court clerk at (310) 260-3629 advising her that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing, and finally, serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing.

Case Number: 21SMCV01921    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: I

The court will discuss this with counsel.  The court better understands about the mediation, but the court had issued a mediation order and one would have thought that it would have been followed.  The court is also unsure why there remains discovery to be done.  The FSC order was given long ago.  The parties knew when the trial was to take place.  The fact that they decided not to complete discovery is not really good cause to continue the trial or the cut off dates.

 

Eisner’s counsel states that her husband has a very important business event taking place shortly after the trial is set to begin (it starts the next day) and that she really ought not miss it.  The court notes that this was adverted to in the prior attempt to continue the trial, meaning that this is not an after-thought, although it was not the principal reason given.  The event will last until a few days after the trial is set to start.  The court understands the issue and will discuss it.

 

What the court cannot fathom is why the parties are seeking an 10 month continuance to April 2025.  The court’s calendar is congested, so that might be somewhat academic.  But it is an awfully long time given that the conflicting event will end a week after the trial is supposed to begin.  The court also understands that defendant is getting married and will go on a honeymoon, and the court would not want to disrupt those plans.  (The court has at least that much of a heart.)  But even that does not explain the need for a 10 month continuance.  The court does not know plaintiff’s position on the continuance or the duration.

 

The court is quite troubled by what appears to be at least in large part a problem of not completing discovery on time.  The court will discuss the matter with counsel.  The court notes that its trial calendar is congested, so it could be that the court would be more receptive to the motion than it otherwise would have been.


Case Number: 22SMCV02291    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: I

The court will discuss this with the parties.  The problem seems to be that plaintiff’s condition has not stabilized and that new providers were recently disclosed in response to supplemental discovery, one of which initially objected to producing records and now will not produce them until after the trial is set to conclude.  The court will discuss the matter with the parties.  The court is concerned about the situation, but understands what has occurred.  The parties are also willing to accept a late 2025 trial date.  Given that, the court is likely to GRANT the request, albeit reluctantly.  There will be no further continuances.

 


Case Number: 23SMCV04441    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: I

This is a Song-Beverly case.  Plaintiff served discovery and responses were due on 11/17/23.  No extensions were requested, but no responses were provided.  Plaintiff then wrote to defendant asking for the responses and giving a deadline for them, but plaintiff did insist that objections were waived.  Even with the letter, defendant failed to provide responses or even respond to the letter.  (The court uses “letter” to mean communication.  It is, of course, a bit of a dated term.)  Plaintiff then brought these motions to compel and a motion to deem admitted.  In opposition, defendant states that this was just a mistake caused by the fact that the lawyer who had been handling the case apparently messed up, but has since left the firm.  Defendant contends that this is an anomaly, and that responses were served on 3/15/24, but plaintiff would not accept them as timely.  In reply, plaintiff notes that this is no mistake because defendant does this sort of thing a lot.  Thus, the plaintiff infers that this is a tactical decision by the defense; it is the way the defense chooses to litigate.  Accordingly, plaintiff contends its motions should be granted.  Also on today’s calendar is defendant’s request for relief from the waiver.  The arguments are much the same.

 

The court is going to cut through this.  Song-Beverly cases follow a pattern.  Plaintiff tends to serve fairly broad discovery.  Defendant tends to object to providing any discovery other than about the subject vehicle.  A motion to compel is brought.  Different judges have different ways of approaching this issue.  Here is what this court does.  This court gives the defense two options.  Option A is to provide the discovery requested beyond the subject vehicle to the extent of the same make, model, year, and alleged problem.  The court is willing to relax the “year” part to the extent that there were no material changes in the vehicle’s design from one year to the next, but that has to be shown or at least asserted.  If a privilege is asserted, documents withheld must be logged.  Option B is to stipulate that if plaintiff prevails on the merits, plaintiff can recover the enhanced damages allowed by the statute.  (No defendant has ever chosen Option B.)

 

That is the court’s inclination here.  While it is true that defendant’s excuse for not responding is thin, the court will, for today’s purposes, credit the explanation and not conclude that defense counsel is acting unethically and committing perjury and thus has to be reported to the State Bar.  That said, the court admonishes defense counsel that should this sort of thing happen again in this court—in this case or any other—the court would not take such a charitable view.  Indeed, that advice might well be taken to heart beyond this court and into other courts, as at least this Minute Order is now on the record.  Defense counsel has used up its last mulligan.  Repeated behavior like this would likely lead to the conclusion that plaintiff is right: this is an unethical tactic used by defense counsel that leads to perjury, most likely directed, or at a minimum sanctioned, by the client.  That would be a bad inference for a court to draw.  It leads to crime-fraud exceptions to privilege and down other roads that the defense would prefer not to walk.  But not today.

 

If one credits defendant’s explanation, then the motion for relief from waiver would make sense.  And that is what the court will do.

 

But that said, the above discussion is what the court will order.  Therefore, defendant is ORDERED TO SERVE verified, code-compliant responses to the discovery propounded.  The responses may include objections as to privilege (but any document withheld on that basis will be logged other than communications between client and counsel and no one else) and objections to the extent the discovery seeks information beyond the subject vehicle and other vehicles of the same make, model, year, and alleged problem or problems.  There will be no general objections or any other objections.  Those responses, and the responsive documents, will be served within 30 days.  If defendant believes that it has already served responses that comply with this order, defendant may stand on those responses.  However, if a motion to compel further is brought and granted, sanctions including evidentiary, issue, or terminating will be on the table.  As to the RFA’s, they will be answered without objection within 30 days, although the 17.1 form interrogatory may have the objections set forth above.

 

Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions are GRANTED AS PRAYED.  They are assessed against defendant and defense counsel and are payable within 30 days.  If not timely paid, the court will reconsider its order (the part where it credits counsel’s explanation) and may deem all objections waived, as that would support plaintiff’s view that this is a tactical move rather than neglect.

DISCOVERY RELATED GUIDELINES FOR DEPARTMENT I

The guidelines below are not “rules” and they are not universally applicable in all cases.  Rather, they are guidelines as to the court’s general views.  Each case, of course, will be decided on its own facts and circumstances, and some of the guidelines below may not apply to particular circumstances.  That said, the court does take these views seriously.

Discovery Responses

The Court provides the following guidance concerning its general views on some common issues regarding responses to written objections.  The Court has noticed that many attorneys view objections and the manner of making them in a way different than does the Court.  To avoid these common pitfalls, the Court offers the following in addition to the provisions of the Litigation Guidelines appended to the Los Angeles County Superior Court Local Rules.

First, “General Objections” are not permitted.  The Code of Civil Procedure does not provide for them, and therefore they are disregarded by the Court.  Any response that “incorporates” the “General Objections” is improper by definition. 

Second, the Court often views boilerplate objections as being in bad faith.  A bad faith response is no response at all in the Court’s view.  Therefore, making boilerplate objections does not preserve those objections nor does it constitute a good faith response to written discovery.  A bad faith response is viewed by the Court for what it is: bad faith.  The objections will be stricken and deemed waived.  In the context of a Request for Admission, such objections may lead to a finding that the request is deemed admitted, although that will depend on the facts of the particular case and the specific discovery at hand.

Objections should be tailored to each specific request.  If a request truly is overbroad, then an objection to that effect is proper.  But such an objection is best accompanied by some reasonable limitation by the responding party that will narrow the request appropriately and (as narrowed) provide a substantive response rather than a mere statement that the request is overbroad.  The same is true as to an objection that a request is unduly burdensome.  The Court also notes that the party asserting that a request is unduly burdensome has the obligation at some point to provide an evidentiary basis for the objection, including evidence as to what the burden of compliance would be.  (West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407.)  Often such an objection will include a statement by the responding party as to a narrower request that would not be unduly burdensome and provide substantive responses as so limited.  Objections that a request is “vague and ambiguous” must set forth the vague and ambiguous term as well as the responding party’s reasonable interpretation of that term as well as a statement that, so interpreted, the response will provide the discovery requested.  If there is no reasonable interpretation possible (which is a rare situation), then the responding party must so state.  Objections as to privilege must set forth the basis explaining why the information sought is in fact privileged.  Where a privilege is asserted in the context of a document request, a privilege log must accompany the answer or be provided within a short and reasonable time after the answer.  Where the objection is made in the context of an interrogatory, it must be clear from the objection the scope of the information being withheld.  If there is no log, there should be no privilege objection to a document request (meaning that a prophylactic privilege objection is the equivalent of no objection; it preserves nothing).  There are some rare exceptions, such as where the entire point of the discovery is to get allegedly privileged information or where compliance would require a log that is in essence an index of counsel’s file.  In that situation, the log is unnecessary, but the assertion should be made that the request is in that rare context.

Third, if an objection is made to the discovery but a response is being given, it must be clear whether information or documents are in fact being withheld on the basis of the objections.  If the objections are clear and done in the manner set forth above, with statements in the objection as to a narrowing that will make the request proper, this is usually a simple task.  The objections themselves state the limit and the response will be full and complete as limited.  But where the objections are not so clear, the response must clearly state whether any information or document is being withheld on the basis of the objection and, if so, the extent of the withholding.  Accordingly, in those situations, phrases like “Notwithstanding and subject to the foregoing objections, responding party states as follows” are improper.  Those sorts of phrases make the verification useless, as the verifier can always fall back on the ”objections” as the reason why a document was not produced or information was not disclosed.  On the other hand, where the line of demarcation is clear, the verification will still serve its purpose.

Fourth, for document requests, the substantive response must conform to the Code.  There are relatively tight rules as to what the response must say, and the response must say it.  For example, where a responding party is not producing documents because they are not in the party’s possession, custody, or control, the responding party must verify that a diligent search has been made and must further provide the information set forth in the Code of Civil Procedure in such cases.  In the case of interrogatories, the responses must also conform to the Code of Civil Procedure and must be made after diligent inquiry.  It is not proper to refuse to respond because the responding party has no personal knowledge.  If the knowledge is hearsay, it must still be disclosed, although it can be qualified to make it clear that it is not based on the verifier’s personal knowledge.

Fifth, the Court frowns on responses that do not conform to the foregoing rules being served with the view that the responses will moot themselves out in the meet and confer process.  That is not how the process works.  A good faith response is required before the meet and confer process begins.  The meet and confer process will (hopefully) bridge the gaps between the parties’ respective positions.  Further, where a response to a request for documents is made and documents are to be produced subject to certain objections (with the documents withheld properly delineated), the documents should be turned over irrespective of the meet and confer.  The documents are to be produced with alacrity to the extent that there is no objection to them.

What this means is that the response to a discovery request is not a trivial undertaking.  Nabbing the response from the form file is a generally bad idea and can lead to all objections being waived.  The point is that the boilerplate often renders the remainder of the response useless.  The only exception is where it is clear that the substantive response is not in any way limited by the objections.  In that case, the objections do no harm, although they also do no good.

Meet and Confer

The Code of Civil Procedure requires that before a motion to compel further responses or a motion for a protective order is filed, the parties engage in a good faith attempt to resolve their differences.  They are to “meet and confer” for that purpose.  Only if that effort fails can a motion be brought. 

Sadly, many litigants view the meet and confer process as just another procedural hoop through which they must jump in order to bring the motion, similar to the need to include an actual demurrer with the demurrer papers.  The Code requires it, so they do it, but no one’s heart is really in it.  That is not sufficient.

Given that, the Court believes it appropriate to set forth how the Court views the meet and confer requirement.  Failure to abide by the guidelines below may well justify denial of the motion or a continuance of it to allow the process to take place.  If one party but not the other refuses to participate as set forth, more likely than not the party refusing to participate will find itself on the losing end of the motion.  The following quotation fairly sums up the Court’s own view.

“[W]e feel compelled to observe that resort to the courts easily could have been avoided here had both parties actually taken to heart Justice Stone's admonitions in Townsend that ‘the statute requires that there be a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution.’ (Townsend, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at p. 1438.) Perhaps after 11 years it is necessary to remind trial counsel and the bar once again that ‘[a]rgument is not the same as informal negotiation’ (id at p. 1437); that attempting informal resolution means more than the mere attempt by the discovery proponent ‘to persuade the objector of the error of his ways’ (id. at p. 1435); and that ‘a reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with [opposing] counsel....  Rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.’ (Id. at p. 1439.)” (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1294, emphasis in original, parallel citations omitted.) 

In practical terms, it means as follows.  It is entirely appropriate to begin the process with a letter or other correspondence.  The letter should not include a demand in the form of an ultimatum, but it can certainly include the relief that is being sought with an invitation to meet and confer.  It is also entirely appropriate for the other party to respond with a letter or other correspondence to set forth its position on the issues.  Such an initial exchange can often be helpful to narrow the dispute.  Whether further exchanges make sense will depend on each case and each dispute.

However, at some point, before impasse is reached the process must include an actual “meet.”  The letters might well suffice to “confer,” but an exchange of correspondence is not a meeting.  In the COVID-19 era, the “meeting” can be virtual or telephonic, but a meeting there must be.  The meeting must be attended by a person from each side with the authority to agree to the other side’s position without getting further permission from anyone, including the client.  If only the client can give the needed authority, then the client must be available instantaneously at all times during the meeting.  This does not mean that one side or the other must concede, but it does mean that the people meeting have the ability in real and practical terms to strike a deal then and there.

The parties should approach the meet and confer process as a problem-solving exercise.  The purpose of the meet and confer is not to convince the other side of the bankruptcy of its position; rather the purpose is to reach an agreement by which the party seeking discovery is able to obtain the information that it reasonably needs and the party providing discovery is not put to an undue burden or forced to provide unnecessary information that might infringe on a privacy interest or disclose a competitive trade secret or the like.

At the conclusion of the meet and confer, the parties should have an agreed statement as to the outcome.  If the outcome is a total impasse, then they should agree on that.  If they have resolved some or all of their differences, then they should state—in writing—what that agreement is so that there will be no confusion later.  Often, an agreement will be without prejudice to a further request by the propounding party for more information after that party receives the responses.

If a motion is still required, the declaration in support should describe with some detail the meet and confer process.  While the Court is not interested in the back and forth, the Court is interested in when the meeting took place, who was there, and how long it lasted.

All communications—in writing or oral—must be civil, polite, and professional.  Name-calling and accusations are devices that undermine the process; they do not further it.  A party engaging in such activity generally is not acting in a good faith effort to reach an agreement.

 


Case Number: 23SMCV04489    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: I

Before the court today are five motions to compel further responses.  The motions are GRANTED as requested except that fees for the hearing will only be awarded once and there will also be a reduction because the moving party was able to file an omnibus reply.  The court’s award will be $28,292.50 for all motions combined.

 

Plaintiffs filed their complaint against their carrier alleging that the carrier breached the insurance policy.  Defendant sent out written discovery, including two sets of form interrogatories.  Plaintiffs responded, but defendant did not believe that the responses were compliant.  Defense counsel sent a meet and confer effort, to which the response was “File your motions.”  Plaintiffs attempted to set an IDC, but the court took it off calendar because defendant had not agreed to it.  Parenthetically, the court will never conduct an IDC with a record like this.  The court’s view is that an IDC is what happens after the parties have met and conferred to narrow their differences as much as possible but believe that a conference with the court may enable them to narrow it even more or avoid the need for motion practice entirely.  It is not a substitute for a meet and confer, as plaintiffs seem to think.  Given the state of the meet and confer, the court reads plaintiffs as taking the position that their responses are appropriate and standing on them.  The “all-or-nothing” approach is a risky one, but it is not per se improper.

 

Initially, plaintiffs contend that the motion should be rejected for inadequate meet and confer.  The meet and confer was inadequate, but as discussed above the fault lies with plaintiffs, not the defense.  Plaintiffs told defendant to file the motions because plaintiffs had no interest in meeting and conferring.  Defendant, given that response, need do no more.

 

Plaintiffs also contend that the separate statements are inadequate because there is no summary of the documents plaintiffs produced.  To put a bit more flesh on the bones, plaintiffs responded to much of the discovery by saying that the answers could be found in the document production.  Because, plaintiffs argue, there is no summary of that production, the court cannot know whether or not the responses were accurate.  Plaintiffs are wrong.  There is nothing in the Code that requires a summary of documents produced.  Of course, Rule 3.1345(c)(6) does require a summary of an item relevant to the motion.  But that is not this case.  The motion is a motion to compel further responses, at least in large part.  The court needs no document summary for that; the court can rule on the motion by looking at the discovery and the response.  There might be a few exceptions, but the failure to summarize does not render the entire motion (or motions) improper.

 

Turning, then, to the motions themselves, the court will begin with the special interrogatories.  As to SI nos. 1-28 and 30-35, plaintiffs objected, but they never attempted to justify the objection, as is required.  (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92.)  The justification ought to be in the response itself, but it is not made even in the opposing papers.  The closest plaintiffs come is to say that their responses are adequate because they produced documents and the information is contained therein.  If so, then well and good, but the statement does not justify an objection.  The objections, then, are OVERRULED.  Some responses were objection only responses, specifically SI Nos. 22-26 and 30.  Because there were only unsupported objections, the motion is GRANTED as to those responses.  The remaining responses are all made “without waiving” the objections, followed by a statement that responsive documents will be provided.  By saying “without waiving,” the response becomes evasive.  Defendant cannot tell if any information is being withheld on the basis of any of the objections.  Accordingly, a further response is required.  Further, it is not an acceptable answer to an interrogatory to say that responsive documents “will be” provided.  The interrogatory response can advert to documents rather than provide a narrative answer in certain cases, but the specific documents must be identified in the answer.  Thus, the motion as to the special interrogatories is GRANTED IN FULL.  Plaintiffs will provide verified, code-compliant responses without objection other than privilege within 30 days.

 

Next the court deals with the requests for production.  RFP Nos. 19, 25-29, and 40-42 were responded to by objection only.  In opposition, plaintiffs state that these RFP’s are improper (except for RFP 42).  The court agrees that the requests are overbroad to the extent that they seek medical records related to body parts for which plaintiffs here claim no injury.  If the lawsuit here deals with an arm, plaintiffs need not produce medical records as to the foot.  Because in motions to compel responses to of a request for production the initial burden is on the propounding party to justify the request, to the extent that the request seeks medical records unrelated to the body part here at issue, the objection is SUSTAINED.  In all other respects, these RFP’s are proper and the objections are without merit and are OVERRULED.  Therefore, the motion is GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART as to these RFP’s.  However, production will be contingent on the parties’ agreement to a protective order, such as the LA Superior Court’s template order.  Updated verified responses, without objection (other than as set forth in this paragraph or attorney-client privilege or attorney work product), will be provided within 15 days and the documents will be provided within 45 days.  To the extent a privilege is asserted, a privilege log will be provided at the same time as the documents.  The log will be sufficient to establish a prima facie case of privilege.

 

RFP 19 seeks the retainer agreement.  Plaintiffs object saying that the retainer agreement is privileged.  Normally, a retainer agreement is protected.  But here, one thing that plaintiffs seek are Brandt fees.  Because of that, defendant is entitled to at least the fee part of the retainer agreement.  All other aspects of it would seem to be beyond what can be demanded and may be redacted.  Thus, the motion is GRANTED IN PART as to this RFP as well.  The time-line and specifics will be the same as above.

 

RFP Nos. 25-28 seek emails plaintiffs sent or received from a certain account between January and February 2023.  This is plainly pertinent to the extent that plaintiffs assert that they were never told that their policy had been cancelled or that it was in danger of being cancelled or that they owed any money.  That said, plaintiffs need not produce all of the emails sent or received during this period even as it relates solely to third parties.  This should have been dealt with had there been a reasonable meet and confer but to the extent it seeks communications not involving defendant, it is overbroad.  That said, plaintiffs take the position that the email in question was no longer being used at the relevant time.  Defendant seeks to test that by seeking all of the emails.  Defendant has established a need to know if the email address was active or dormant, but not the details of any communications.   Thus, plaintiffs should also produce the list of emails sent or received but may redact the party to whom the email was sent or by whom it was sent (if not the defendant) so that it will be a log of the emails setting forth the dates and times but not the identities.  That, too, could easily have been dealt with during a meet and confer.  The motion is thus GRANTED IN PART.  Plaintiffs will serve updated responses and produce all such communications between either of them, on the one hand, and defendants or anyone related to defendants (like an agent) on the other hand.  The time-line will be the same as above.

 

As to the remaining RFP’s, plaintiffs state that they will produce documents subject to their objections.  However, except as set forth above, the court does not believe that most of the objections have merit (and the court does believe that the RFP’s are justified except as discussed above).  Therefore, plaintiffs will file further verified responses without objection other than privilege along the same time-line as above.  Thus, the motion is GRANTED as to those RFPs. 

 

As to the RFA’s, the motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiffs objected because the number exceeds 35.  The Code allows 35 RFA’s as of right (plus authentication RFAs), but allows more if a specific declaration is filed, as it was here.  While this court has, from time to time, required more of a propounding party, that has occurred only where the number appears abusive on its face or a showing of abuse has been at least suggested in a way supported by evidence or reasonable inference.  So, for example, had defendant propounded 350 RFAs, the court would have more sympathy for the notion that one cannot exceed the statutory number by a factor of 10 by serving a generic declaration.  Even then, the better practice would be to file a motion for a protective order.  However, here, the number is far from abusive.  Further, the “undue burden” objection must be accompanied by evidence demonstrating and quantifying the burden.  (West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407.)  No such evidence is submitted here.  The motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiffs will serve verified responses without objection within 30 days.

 

Finally, there are the form interrogatories.  Sets 1 and 2 differ based on the definition of “INCIDENT.”  The first set defines the term as the handling of plaintiff’s claim and the second defines it as the September 9, 2023 accident.  Plaintiffs object but say that responsive documents are attached.  That is not sufficient and the motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiffs will file verified responses without objection other than privilege within 30 days.  If plaintiffs want to respond by adverting to documents (and assuming that the Code so permits), they may do so by adverting to specific documents and identifying them.  The documents will be produced with the supplemental responses.

 

Defendants seek a whopping $32,902.50 in sanctions for the five motions combined.  That is a pretty big number, but the Edson declaration establishes why the amount sought is reasonable.  As set forth above, there are some savings because defendant filed an omnibus reply and there is only one hearing.  But beyond that, the motion is GRANTED.  Sanctions are imposed against plaintiffs, but not counsel, in the amount of $28,292.50 for all five motions combined.  They are payable within 30 days.

The guidelines below are not “rules” and they are not universally applicable in all cases.  Rather, they are guidelines as to the court’s general views.  Each case, of course, will be decided on its own facts and circumstances, and some of the guidelines below may not apply to particular circumstances.  That said, the court does take these views seriously.

Discovery Responses

The Court provides the following guidance concerning its general views on some common issues regarding responses to written objections.  The Court has noticed that many attorneys view objections and the manner of making them in a way different than does the Court.  To avoid these common pitfalls, the Court offers the following in addition to the provisions of the Litigation Guidelines appended to the Los Angeles County Superior Court Local Rules.

First, “General Objections” are not permitted.  The Code of Civil Procedure does not provide for them, and therefore they are disregarded by the Court.  Any response that “incorporates” the “General Objections” is improper by definition.

Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407.)  Often such an objection will include a statement by the responding party as to a narrower request that would not be unduly burdensome and provide substantive responses as so limited.  Objections that a request is “vague and ambiguous” must set forth the vague and ambiguous term as well as the responding party’s reasonable interpretation of that term as well as a statement that, so interpreted, the response will provide the discovery requested.  If there is no reasonable interpretation possible (which is a rare situation), then the responding party must so state.  Objections as to privilege must set forth the basis explaining why the information sought is in fact privileged.  Where a privilege is asserted in the context of a document request, a privilege log must accompany the answer or be provided within a short and reasonable time after the answer.  Where the objection is made in the context of an interrogatory, it must be clear from the objection the scope of the information being withheld.  If there is no log, there should be no privilege objection to a document request (meaning that a prophylactic privilege objection is the equivalent of no objection; it preserves nothing).  There are some rare exceptions, such as where the entire point of the discovery is to get allegedly privileged information or where compliance would require a log that is in essence an index of counsel’s file.  In that situation, the log is unnecessary, but the assertion should be made that the request is in that rare context.

Third, if an objection is made to the discovery but a response is being given, it must be clear whether information or documents are in fact being withheld on the basis of the objections.  If the objections are clear and done in the manner set forth above, with statements in the objection as to a narrowing that will make the request proper, this is usually a simple task.  The objections themselves state the limit and the response will be full and complete as limited.  But where the objections are not so clear, the response must clearly state whether any information or document is being withheld on the basis of the objection and, if so, the extent of the withholding.  Accordingly, in those situations, phrases like “Notwithstanding and subject to the foregoing objections, responding party states as follows” are improper.  Those sorts of phrases make the verification useless, as the verifier can always fall back on the ”objections” as the reason why a document was not produced or information was not disclosed.  On the other hand, where the line of demarcation is clear, the verification will still serve its purpose.

Fourth, for document requests, the substantive response must conform to the Code.  There are relatively tight rules as to what the response must say, and the response must say it.  For example, where a responding party is not producing documents because they are not in the party’s possession, custody, or control, the responding party must verify that a diligent search has been made and must further provide the information set forth in the Code of Civil Procedure in such cases.  In the case of interrogatories, the responses must also conform to the Code of Civil Procedure and must be made after diligent inquiry.  It is not proper to refuse to respond because the responding party has no personal knowledge.  If the knowledge is hearsay, it must still be disclosed, although it can be qualified to make it clear that it is not based on the verifier’s personal knowledge.

Fifth, the Court frowns on responses that do not conform to the foregoing rules being served with the view that the responses will moot themselves out in the meet and confer process.  That is not how the process works.  A good faith response is required before the meet and confer process begins.  The meet and confer process will (hopefully) bridge the gaps between the parties’ respective positions.  Further, where a response to a request for documents is made and documents are to be produced subject to certain objections (with the documents withheld properly delineated), the documents should be turned over irrespective of the meet and confer.  The documents are to be produced with alacrity to the extent that there is no objection to them.

What this means is that the response to a discovery request is not a trivial undertaking.  Nabbing the response from the form file is a generally bad idea and can lead to all objections being waived.  The point is that the boilerplate often renders the remainder of the response useless.  The only exception is where it is clear that the substantive response is not in any way limited by the objections.  In that case, the objections do no harm, although they also do no good.

Meet and Confer

The Code of Civil Procedure requires that before a motion to compel further responses or a motion for a protective order is filed, the parties engage in a good faith attempt to resolve their differences.  They are to “meet and confer” for that purpose.  Only if that effort fails can a motion be brought. 

Sadly, many litigants view the meet and confer process as just another procedural hoop through which they must jump in order to bring the motion, similar to the need to include an actual demurrer with the demurrer papers.  The Code requires it, so they do it, but no one’s heart is really in it.  That is not sufficient.

Given that, the Court believes it appropriate to set forth how the Court views the meet and confer requirement.  Failure to abide by the guidelines below may well justify denial of the motion or a continuance of it to allow the process to take place.  If one party but not the other refuses to participate as set forth, more likely than not the party refusing to participate will find itself on the losing end of the motion.  The following quotation fairly sums up the Court’s own view.

“[W]e feel compelled to observe that resort to the courts easily could have been avoided here had both parties actually taken to heart Justice Stone's admonitions in Townsend that ‘the statute requires that there be a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution.’ (Townsend, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at p. 1438.) Perhaps after 11 years it is necessary to remind trial counsel and the bar once again that ‘[a]rgument is not the same as informal negotiation’ (id at p. 1437); that attempting informal resolution means more than the mere attempt by the discovery proponent ‘to persuade the objector of the error of his ways’ (id. at p. 1435); and that ‘a reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with [opposing] counsel....  Rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.’ (Id. at p. 1439.)” (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1294, emphasis in original, parallel citations omitted.) 

In practical terms, it means as follows.  It is entirely appropriate to begin the process with a letter or other correspondence.  The letter should not include a demand in the form of an ultimatum, but it can certainly include the relief that is being sought with an invitation to meet and confer.  It is also entirely appropriate for the other party to respond with a letter or other correspondence to set forth its position on the issues.  Such an initial exchange can often be helpful to narrow the dispute.  Whether further exchanges make sense will depend on each case and each dispute.

However, at some point, before impasse is reached the process must include an actual “meet.”  The letters might well suffice to “confer,” but an exchange of correspondence is not a meeting.  In the COVID-19 era, the “meeting” can be virtual or telephonic, but a meeting there must be.  The meeting must be attended by a person from each side with the authority to agree to the other side’s position without getting further permission from anyone, including the client.  If only the client can give the needed authority, then the client must be available instantaneously at all times during the meeting.  This does not mean that one side or the other must concede, but it does mean that the people meeting have the ability in real and practical terms to strike a deal then and there.

The parties should approach the meet and confer process as a problem-solving exercise.  The purpose of the meet and confer is not to convince the other side of the bankruptcy of its position; rather the purpose is to reach an agreement by which the party seeking discovery is able to obtain the information that it reasonably needs and the party providing discovery is not put to an undue burden or forced to provide unnecessary information that might infringe on a privacy interest or disclose a competitive trade secret or the like.

At the conclusion of the meet and confer, the parties should have an agreed statement as to the outcome.  If the outcome is a total impasse, then they should agree on that.  If they have resolved some or all of their differences, then they should state—in writing—what that agreement is so that there will be no confusion later.  Often, an agreement will be without prejudice to a further request by the propounding party for more information after that party receives the responses.

If a motion is still required, the declaration in support should describe with some detail the meet and confer process.  While the Court is not interested in the back and forth, the Court is interested in when the meeting took place, who was there, and how long it lasted.

All communications—in writing or oral—must be civil, polite, and professional.  Name-calling and accusations are devices that undermine the process; they do not further it.  A party engaging in such activity generally is not acting in a good faith effort to reach an agreement.
