DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV08440    Hearing Date: August 1, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JOSE GUADALUPE SOTO-

RODRIGUEZ, et al.

 

 

         vs.

 

AKG TRADING (USA) INC., et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV08440

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  August 1, 2024

 

Specially-appearing Defendant Cosentino Group, S.A. n/k/a Cosentino, S.A.’s motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction is GRANTED. 

 

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiffs Jose Guadalupe-Soto Rodriguez and Sara

Sanchez (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed the operative first amended complaint against

39 Defendants, alleging: (1) negligence; (2) strict liability—warning defect; (3)

strict liability—design defect; (4) fraudulent concealment; (5) breach of implied

warranties; and (6) loss of consortium.

 

            On 4/26/2024, the Court continued Cosentino’s motion to quash based on lack of jurisdiction to allow jurisdictional discovery to take place.

 

            Now, with discovery having taken place, the Court takes up the motion. 

 

Legal Standard

 

Cal Code of Civil Procedure § 418.10(a)(1) states:

 

(a) A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or

within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve

and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:

 

(1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.

(2) To stay or dismiss the action on the ground of inconvenient forum.

(3) To dismiss the action pursuant to the applicable provisions of Chapter 1.5 (commencing with Section 583.110) of Title 8.”

 

A California court can exercise personal jurisdiction over a nonresident

defendant who has “minimum contacts” with the state. (Pavlovich v. Superior Court

(2002) 29 Cal.4th 262, 268; Virtualmagic Asia, Inc. v. Fil-Cartoons, Inc. (2002) 99

Cal.App.4th 228, 238.) A defendant has minimum contacts if the quality and nature

of its activity in the forum state (referred to as “contacts”) is such that it is

reasonable and fair to require the defendant to conduct a defense in that state.

(Paviovich, supra, 29 Cal.App.4th at 268; Strasner v. Touchstone Wireless Repair & Logistics, LP (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 215, 221.)

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant Cosentino argues that it lacks the requisite contacts with California to establish personal jurisdiction over it.

 

            After review, the Court agrees.

 

The focus of the minimum contacts analysis is the defendant's contacts

with the forum. (Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court (2017) 582 U.S. 255,

260.) Depending on the extent of a defendant's contacts with California, it may be

subject to either general or specific jurisdiction. (Elkman v. National States Ins.

(2009) 14 Cal.4th 434, 445-446.)

 

A court can exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant if (1)

the defendant has purposefully availed itself of the forum's benefits and protections

and (2) the cause of action relates to or arises out of the defendant's contacts with

the forum, and (3) the exercise of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play

and substantial justice. (Pavlovich, supra, 29 Cal.4th at 269.) The contacts for

specific jurisdiction are those that existed when the plaintiff's cause of action arose.

(Strasner, supra, 5 Cal.App.5th at 226.)

 

To establish minimum contacts for specific jurisdiction, the plaintiff must

show that the defendant has purposefully availed itself of the privilege of

conducting activities in California to involve its benefits and protections. (See

Axiom Foods, Inc. v. Acerchem Int'l (9th Cir. 2017} 874 F.3d 1064, 1068; Bristol-

Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 783, 799-800.) In this

analysis, (1) only the defendant's contacts with California are considered, not the

defendant's contacts with persons who reside there, (2) the defendant's contacts

must have been purposeful rather than random, fortuitous, or attenuated, and (3)

the defendant must have sought some benefit by availing itself of the jurisdiction.

(See Walden v. Fiore (2014) 571 U.S. 277, 285-286; Burger King Corp. v.

Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 474-475; Vons v. Seabest Foods (1996), 14 Cal.4th 434, 446.)

 

            As part of jurisdictional discovery, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

Jorge Cuervo Vela, the Global Director of the Legal Department at Cosentino Group, testified in his deposition on May 6, 2024 that Cosentino SA is the registered trademark owner for Silestone, and he authenticated the trademark registrations. He further testified that Cosentino SA licensed the use of the trademark to C&C North America, creating a market for the product in the US, especially in California (see Declaration of Raphael Metzger.

 

            (Supp. 2: 6-7.)

 

            Plaintiff argues that the Cosentino trademarks are:

 

…critical evidence because they show that Cosentino S.A. owns the trademark for Silestone and registered it with the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office, whereby the product was distributed in the United States and even appears on a building in Los Angeles which Cosentino calls a “Cosentino City.” Pursuant to well-established California law, Cosentino Group is therefore in the chain of distribution of the Cosentino products and, through its ownership, use and licensing of the trademarks, caused the epidemic including plaintiff’s silicosis. Cosentino is therefore liable to Plaintiffs on their products liability claims for causing the silicosis, Kasel v. Remington Arms Company (1972) 24 Cal.App.3d 711, 721-728, whether or not is the manufacturer or the distributor of the products or claims to be a mere “holding company.

 

            (Supp. 2, 14-22.)

 

            However, in opposition, Cosentino Group explained that Cosentino Group, S.A. changed its name to Cosentino, S.A. on July 23, 2023. References to “Cosentino, S.A.” in records, filings, reports, and other documents prior to July 23, 2023 are not references to specially-appearing defendant Cosentino Group, S.A. n/k/a Cosentino, S.A.; such references are references to an entirely different and separate legal entity that is now known as Cosentino Industrial, S.A.U.

 

            Cosentino Group submitted evidence that Jorge Cuervo Vela repeatedly explained at his deposition that Cosentino Group is not the registrant or owner of the Silestone trademark. For example, Mr. Guervo testified:

 

[W]hat I can tell you with all certainty is that that Cosentino, SA, that the record shows is not the Cosentino, SA, that we are here to discuss today.

 

As I said earlier, back in -- on that date of that registration date, Cosentino, SA, name was Grupo Cosentino, SL. …

 

And you can ask me however many times you like and I can continue to answer here for hours or days, but doesn’t matter how many times you ask me the question, that society that is shown on that document next to where it says in English “Owner” that Cosentino, SA, is not the Cosentino, SA that we’re here to discuss today. That is not the Cosentino, SA that I am here representing in this deposition.

 

…

 

When we discussed the registration of this Silestone trademark earlier, I have been 100 percent clear, and I have gone out of my way to clarify that the Cosentino, SA, that that registry or that document showed it is not the same Cosentino, SA, that I’m here representing today

 

            (Metzger Decl, Exh. A, Cuervo Dep. at 60:12–61:9, 79:5–10.)

 

Mr. Cuervo also made clear Cosentino Group, S.A. n/k/a Cosentino, S.A. did not license the trademark to defendant C & C North America, Inc.:

 

Q. What is licensing contract that you worked on for C & C North America?

 

A. I don’t remember the details. What I can tell you 100 percent, is that it did not involve Cosentino, SA. …

 

…

 

Q. Sir, I was asking you in order for this trade name to be on this building, the trade name had to be licensed to C & C North America, true? [Objections omitted.]

 

THE WITNESS: I cannot speculate. I don’t know. I don’t know the details of the agreement between Cosentino Global and Cosentino North America. And respectfully I would invite you to ask these questions to their representative for Cosentino North America.

 

            (Cuervo Dep. at 44:18–22, 82:5–15.)

 

            Moreover, even assuming Cosentino Group did own the trademark, Plaintiffs do not cite any authority for the proposition that registering or owning a trademark constitutes a purposeful contact with any one state for purposes of establishing personal jurisdiction. While Plaintiffs cite Kasel, supra, 24 Cal.App.3d 711, this case deals with choice of law and enterprise liability in a situation where the defendant “made a general appearance in the case and raised no objection to the ‘forum’ jurisdiction of the court” (Id. at p. 721). As such, it is factually distinguishable and does not support the exercise of jurisdiction here.

 

A nonresident defendant’s contacts with the forum state “must be the defendant’s own choice and not ‘random, isolated, or fortuitous.’” (Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Judicial Dist. Court (2021) 141 S.Ct. 1017, 1024–1025, quoting Keeton v. Hustler Magazine, Inc. (1984) 465 U.S. 770, 774.) “They must show that the defendant deliberately ‘reached out beyond’ its home—by, for example, ‘exploi[ting] a market’ in the forum State or entering a contractual relationship centered there.” (Ford Motor, at p. 1025, original brackets, quoting Walden v. Fiore (2014) 571 U.S. 277, 285.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s evidence fails to show that Cosentino Group is the registrant or owner or the Silestone trademark, and even if it was, Plaintiff has not set forth persuasive California authority which could show that this would constitute meaningful contact for purposes of personal jurisdiction under California law.

 

Based on the foregoing, specially-appearing Defendant Cosentino Group, S.A. n/k/a Cosentino, S.A.’s motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction is granted. 

 

 

It is so ordered.

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

PROJECT 7 SECURITY GROUP, et al.

                          

         vs.

 

MICHAEL JOHNSON

 

 Case No.:  23STCV24878 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  August 1, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion to dismiss is GRANTED.

 

On 10/9/2023, Plaintiffs Project 7 Security Group and Brandon Gatewood (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed suit against Michael Johnson, alleging: (1) independent action in equity to vacate judgment for lack of personal jurisdiction; and (2) independent action in equity to vacate judgment for extrinsic fraud or mistake.

 

Now, Defendant moves to dismiss this action.

 

Discussion

 

            This case arises out of another case BC717979, in which a default judgment was entered in Defendant here’s favor.      

 

Defendant, who is representing himself in pro per, argues that this case should be dismissed because Plaintiff is attempting to improperly re-litigate the allegation of defective service which was already addressed in BC717979.

 

The Court agrees.  

 

On 7/11/2023, the Honorable Cherl J. Nellon in Department 13 denied Project 7’s motion to vacate default judgment, expressly finding that service was proper.

 

It is a well-accepted maxim that one trial court judge may not reconsider or overrule a ruling of another trial judge. (In re Marriage of Olivarez (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1248; Ziller Electronic Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1232.)

 

Because a superior court is a single entity comprised of member judges, “no member of that court cannot sit in review on the actions of another member of that same court.” (Id. at pp. 427–428.) Allowing such a practice would lead to judge-shopping—venturing from judge to judge until a favorable ruling is obtained—which “would instantly breed lack of confidence in the integrity of the courts.” (Id.) The rule against one trial judge overruling another reflects an understood notion of comity, conserve judicial resources, and prevents a judge from interfering with a case ongoing before another judge. (In re Alberto (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 421, 426-431.) The superior court’s jurisdiction to reconsider its rulings is generally to be exercised by the judge who made the original order. (Ziller Electronic Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1232.)  Indeed, the second judge, being presented with the same motion, is cautioned to direct the moving party to the original judge who ruled on the first motion. (Id.) “For one superior court judge, no matter how well intended, even if correct as a matter of law, to nullify a duly made, erroneous ruling of another superior court judge places the second judge in the role of a one-judge appellate court.” (Id. at p. 427.)

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that its claim is not barred because the judgment was obtained without personal jurisdiction over it. However, this was the precise contention which was raised, considered, and rejected in the earlier lawsuit. Plaintiff’s opposition makes no substantive mention of this fact. Plaintiff’s opposition lacks any legal authority which could suggest that a party may file a separate lawsuit to relitigate a void judgment issue which has itself already been litigated in an earlier case. A single assertion that “[t]his lawsuit is not barred by collateral estoppel or re judicata” without any accompanying analysis is insufficient to show that Plaintiff’s claim here is not barred.  Plaintiff’s arguments, which lack substantive analysis, are particularly unavailing, given that this motion was previously continued to provide Plaintiff an opportunity to file a fully considered opposition.

 

Second, while a trial court retains authority to vacate a default judgment on equitable grounds, Plaintiff has not provided any indication that any court but the trial court that granted the initial default judgment retains that power. Judge Nellon possessed this authority and did, in fact, consider the motion to vacate judgment. Judge Nellon determined that service was proper and denied the motion. As such, this Court finds that the proper mechanism for Plaintiff to raise its contention that the judgment should have been vacated was a motion for reconsideration in Judge Nellon’s Court. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to dismiss is granted.

 

 

It is so ordered.
