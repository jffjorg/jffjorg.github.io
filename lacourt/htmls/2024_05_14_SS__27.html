Case Number: 18STCV00258    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO QUASH 

Hearing Date: 5/14/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 18STCV00258 MERCED MALDONADO vs BARRETT WISSMAN

Moving Party: Defendants

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO QUASH IS DENIED

 

On October 10, 2018, Merced Maldonado ("Plaintiff") initiated a lawsuit against Barrett Wissman ("Barrett"), Nina Kokob ("Kokob"), and DOES 1 through 20 (collectively, "Defendants") citing injuries from assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. The incident in question occurred at 121 South Rossmore Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90028. On August 29, 2023, the Court approved Plaintiff's Application for Publication for both Defendants. Subsequently, on September 6, 2023, Judge Thomas Griego issued the Order for Publication concerning Defendant Nina Wissman, and Judge Mark E. Windham did the same for Defendant Barrett Wissman. The summons was published in the Los Angeles Times on September 18, September 25, October 2, and October 9, 2023. On December 6, 2023, the court heard Defendants’ fifth motion to quash the summons by publication in the Los Angeles Times. In that motion, Defendant argued that service by publication was improper and violated the Hague Convention, and also pointed out the use of an incorrect name in the publication. The court continued the hearing and requested supplemental briefing. On December 20, 2023, the Court heard the matter again and issued an order denying the motion to quash and instructed Defendant to file an answer or response within 30 days.

 

On January 10, 2024, Defendant filed a sixth motion to quash, presenting the same arguments previously made, that the service by publication was improper and violated the Hague Convention. However, the Court has already ruled on these issues on the merits concerning the service by publication in the Los Angeles Times. Thus, the present motion is DENIED.

Case Number: 21STCV08885    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

Hearing Date: 5/14/24

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV08885 ERNESTO RIVERA, AN INDIVIDUAL vs BIMBO BAKERIES USA, INC.

Moving Party: Cross Defendant Robert Health Logistics

Responding Party: Bimbo Bakeries and Logistics Academy

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS IS DENIED

 

Background

 

On March 5, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant Bimbo Bakeries USA, alleging that several large pellets fell on Plaintiff while he was transporting containers for Defendant Bimbo Bakeries USA. On March 13, 2023, Defendant Bimbo filed a cross-complaint against Logistics Academy and Robert Health Logistics for indemnity. Robert Health Logistics now moves the Court for judgment on the pleadings against Bimbo's fourth cause of action in its cross complaint for express indemnity and also seeks an order for Logistics to indemnify Defendant Robert Health Logistics in this action.

 

Legal Standard

 

A defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed. (CCP § 438(f).) A motion by a defendant may be made on the grounds that (1) the court “lacks jurisdiction of the subject of one or more of the causes of action alleged” or (2) the complaint or cross-complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (CCP § 438(c).)

 

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.) “A motion for judgment on the pleadings is akin to a general demurrer; it tests the sufficiency of the complaint to state a cause of action.” (Wise v. Pacific Gas and Elec. Co. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 725, 738.)

 

Like a general demurrer, “ordinarily, a [motion for judgment on the pleadings] does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action, and if any part of a cause of action is properly pleaded, the [motion] will be overruled.” (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether properly pled factual allegations—assumed to be true and liberally construed—are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Stone Street Capital, LLC v. Cal. State Lottery Com’n (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 109, 116.)

 

Discussion

 

Robert Health Logistics ("RHL") requests that the court issue an order for Logistics Academy to indemnify and defend RHL in the present action. However, this exceeds the scope of a motion for judgment on the pleadings. Under California law, a motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made on the grounds that (1) the court "lacks jurisdiction of the subject of one or more of the causes of action alleged," or (2) the complaint or cross-complaint "does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant" (CCP § 438(c).) RHL has failed to provide any authority indicating that a court can make an order requiring a party to indemnify and defend another party pursuant to a judgment on the pleadings.

Regarding RHL's motion against Bimbo's 4th cause of action for express indemnity in its cross-complaint, the request is appropriate for motion for judgment on the pleadings. In Bimbo’s fourth cause of action against all cross-defendants, including RHL, Bimbo alleges that "Carrier agrees to indemnify, defend and hold Broker and Customer [BIMBO BAKERIES USA, INC.], and their officers, employees, agents, and directors, harmless from and against any and all fines, penalties, costs, demands, damages (including bodily injury and property damage), losses, obligations, claims, liabilities, and expenses (including reasonable attorney's fees) of whatever type or nature arising out of or related to: (i) any act or omission by Carrier or its subcontractors, (ii) Carrier’s or any of its permitted subcontractor's performance or breach of this Agreement, and (iii) any use, operation, maintenance, or possession of Customer’s or Broker’s owned or leased equipment by Carrier or any of its subcontractors" (Bimbo’s Cross Complaint ¶ 19). Bimbo further alleges that "Cross-Defendants [RHL and Logistics] were the agents, employees, representatives, co-venturers, partners, or in some manner agents or principals, or both, for each other, and were acting in the course and scope of said agency or employment or representative capacity." (Bimbo’s Cross Complaint ¶ 10.)

Based on the four corners of the complaint, Bimbo has alleged sufficient facts to support a cause of action against RHL for express indemnity. RHL asserts that the motion is supported by a request for judicial notice; however, no such document has been filed with the Court. Even if the Court were to take judicial notice of the contract attached as Exhibit 2 to the motion, this contract is solely a signed agreement between RH and Logistics. Bimbo's cross-complaint does not state that its express indemnity claim is based on this particular contract. The existence of RHL's contract with Logistics does not preclude the possibility that Bimbo is asserting express indemnity on a different basis. Thus, RHL’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is DENIED.

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/14/24  

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV31244 MARITZA RAMIREZ vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles  

Notice: Sufficient  

Ruling:  MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION IS GRANTED; REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

 

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

Moving Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles  

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient  

Ruling: MOTION FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

MOTION FOR COMPLIANCE

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles  

Notice: Sufficient  

Ruling:  MOTION FOR COMPLIANCE IS DENIED

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

 

Background 

On September 26, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present premises liability case. On January 25, 2024, Plaintiff noticed Defendant City of Los Angeles’ PMQ deposition for February 13, 2024. On February 12, 2024, Defendant served its objection one day prior to the scheduled deposition date citing deponent’s unavailability. Plaintiff now moves the Court to compel the Defendant’s PMQ deposition.

 

Legal standard

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)  A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party or party-affiliated deponent to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).)  

  

The party served with a deposition notice waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)  In addition to serving this written objection, a party may also move for an order staying the taking of the deposition and quashing the deposition notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (c).)  

  

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production . . . of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  

 

A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:   

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.   

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040 or when the deponent fails to attend the deposition, a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. 

  

Where a motion to compel a party’s appearance and testimony at deposition is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent, unless the court finds the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)  On motion of a party who, in person or by attorney, attended at the time and place specified in the deposition notice in the expectation that the deponent’s testimony would be taken, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of that party and against the deponent.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(2).) 

 

Analysis

Defendant admits that it served the objection one day before the date of the deposition, and the objection is therefore untimely (CCP § 2025.410(a)). After Defendant's non-appearance at the deposition on February 13, 2024, Plaintiff sought to meet and confer, but the parties reached an impasse regarding the depositions and objections. Plaintiff has fulfilled the requirements under § 2025.450(b)(2). Thus, all the requirements to compel Defendant’s PMQ deposition have been met, and the present motion is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to appear for deposition within 20 days of today.

The Court is not persuaded by Defendant's multiple arguments relating to the procedural defects of the motion. First, if Defendant seeks relief from waiver of objections, it must file a fully briefed motion, rather than request relief in its opposition. Second, there is no IDC requirement for a motion to compel a deposition. Third, a separate statement is not required when no response has been provided to the request for discovery (California Rules of Court 3.1345(b)(1)).

The Court notes that the notice of deposition includes a demand for production of documents, and Plaintiff seeks an order compelling Defendant to produce all identified documents at the deposition. The moving papers fail to show good cause for the production of the documents sought, as required by §2025.450(b)(1).  Plaintiff failed to discuss good cause, and therefore, the Court declines to enter an order compelling Defendant to produce documents; nonetheless, the Court urges the parties to work together to resolve any issues concerning documents without court intervention.

Plaintiff requests $1,321.65 in sanctions.  Sanctions are appropriate here because Plaintiff brought a motion to compel that the Court is granting.  However, the Court reduces the amount of sanctions from the amount requested to $750, recognizing that Plaintiff appears to have been particularly aggressive in bringing the motion at the same time that the parties were in communication attempting to schedule a PMK deposition.  Defendant and its counsel are jointly and severally ordered to pay sanctions of $750 to Plaintiff within 20 days.

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

Defendant’s motion is untimely. The motion was filed on April 19, 2024; thus, the earliest possible hearing date with required notice is May 15, 2024, one day after the present hearing date. Defective notice deprives the Court of jurisdiction to act. (Lee v. Placer Title Co. (1994) 28 CA4th 503, 509, 511.) Counsel may have failed to account for the fact that service was completed through electronic submission and overnight mail, which adds 2 additional court days to the notice period.

MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE

This motion concerns Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production (RFP) No. 19, regarding surveillance footage of the incident.

REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO.19 ask Defendant to provide “Any and all videotapes and/or other media type surveillance footage taken of the INCIDENT, specifically two hours prior to INCIDENT and two hours after INCIDENT.”

 

Defendant’s response states: “Responding Party conducted a reasonable and diligent search and produced all responsive documents in its custody, control, or possession related to this request: video footage of the Incident. Discovery and investigation are ongoing, and Responding Party reserves the right to amend, supplement, or modify this response.”

Defendant thereafter supplemented its response indicating it would make a search and inquiry for information related to the incident for a period 5 minutes before and 5 minutes after the incident.  Defendant then states it “produces such responsive documents.” 

Plaintiff appears to contend that whatever was produced did not show the incident and, therefore, Defendant is not complying with the request.  However, one cannot infer that Defendant possesses a video of the incident or that it did not produce all responsive documents in its possession, custody, or control. First, Defendant's response did not state that it would produce the exact footage of the incident but rather that it has produced all responsive documents in its possession, custody, or control relating to the request. Responsive documents might include video of times shortly before or after the incident, as both relate to the incident and are responsive to the timeframe requested by RFP No. 19. Second, Defendant stated that it has produced all documents in its possession, custody, or control. This response does not eliminate the possibility that Defendant might not possess the exact footage of the incident. Third, Plaintiff has failed to introduce evidence showing that Defendant is in possession but withholding additional video footage. Simply, the Court has no basis to conclude that Defendant has not complied with the Request as indicated.  Of course, if Defendant attempts to use some video of the incident for its own purposes that has not been produced, Plaintiff will be able to point to Defendant’s discovery response and request an appropriate remedy.  But for now, the Court accepts Defendant’s statement that it has produced all responsive documents.  Accordingly, there is nothing to compel, and the Motion to Compel is denied.

The request for sanctions related to the Motion to Compel video is also denied.

Responding Party: Unopposed

Ruling: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL IS CONTINUED.

  

Background 

 

Attorney Colin M. Jones represents Plaintiff. Jones moves to be relieved as counsel, citing an irremediable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. No opposition has been filed. 

 

Legal Standard¿ 

¿ 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.) 

¿ 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).) 

¿ 

Analysis and Conclusion¿ 

¿ 

Jones has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Jones seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).) Jones states that Plaintiff has retained another attorney for the same cause of action arising from this incident, which has since been settled.

 

The Court notes that the next hearing, an Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal, is set for May 29, 2024, which is only 15 days away.  Trial is currently scheduled for June 10, 2024.  Based on the fact that these dates are both less than a month away, the Court is concerned that granting Jone’s motion now may disrupt the orderly process of justice.  At the same time, based on the information provided by Jones, it appears that dismissal may be appropriate.  Accordingly, the Court hereby continues this motion until May 29, 2024.
