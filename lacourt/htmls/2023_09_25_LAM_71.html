Case Number: 23STCP01914    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DR. PAUL C. BROUN, et al.,

 

         vs.

 

STATE OF GEORGIA.

 Case No.:  23STCP019141

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 25, 2023

 

Petitioners Dr. Paul C. Broun’s, Sheri Gilligan’s, and VoterGA’s motion to compel compliance with Petitioners’ second Subpoena Duces Tecum, served on Non-party Los Angeles County District Attorney’s Office, Custodian of Records and Evidence, served on Non-party on March 14, 2023, to produce and permit inspection and copying of forensically sound evidence drives containing information from Konnech, Inc. is granted. 

 

LADA is to produce the items responsive to Petitioners’ request within 30 days of this ruling.

 

          Petitioners Dr. Paul C. Broun (“Broun”), Sheri Gilligan (“Gilligan”), and VoterGA (“VoterGA”) (collectively, “Petitioners”) move for an order compelling Non-party Los Angeles County District Attorney’s Office, Custodian of Records and Evidence (“LADA”) to comply with Petitioners’ second subpoena duces tecum, served on LADA on March 14, 2023, requiring LADA to produce and permit inspection and copying of forensically sound evidence drives containing information from Konnech, Inc. (“Konnech”) (“Subpoena”).  (Notice of Petition, pgs. 1-2.)  Petitioners bring the instant petition on the grounds that the records sought from LADA are directly relevant to Petitioners’ out-of-state case, essential to a fair resolution of the lawsuit, and cannot be obtained by less intrusive means.  (Notice of Petition, pg. 2.)

 

Background

          On December 8, 2022, Petitioners served LADA with their first subpoena duces tecum, to which LADA objected and refused to produce any requested documents.  (Decl. of Cubeiro ¶¶3-4, Exh. B.)  On January 27, 2023, Petitioners’ counsel and LADA’s counsel met and conferred via telephone to discuss the subpoena, and Petitioners’ counsel offered to narrow the focus of the request.  (See Decl. of Cubeiro ¶6.)  On February 1, 2023, Petitioners’ counsel emailed LADA’s counsel a narrowed and focused request.  (Decl. of Cubeiro ¶7, Exh. C.)  Petitioners’ counsel did not receive a response to his email.  (Decl. of Cubeiro ¶8.)  Petitioners filed the Subpoena at issue on March 13, 2023.  (Decl. of Cubeiro ¶9.)  On March 31, 2023, LADA filed an objection to Petitioners’ second subpoena and refused to provide any of the requested documents.  (Decl. of Cubeiro ¶10.)

          On May 31, 2021, Petitioners filed the instant petition.  LADA filed an opposition on August 24, 2023.  Petitioners filed their reply on August 30, 2023.

 

          Request for Judicial Notice

          Petitioners’ 8/30/23 request for judicial notice is denied.

 

          Evidentiary Objection

          LADA’s 8/24/23 evidentiary objection to the Declaration of Garland Favorito (“Favorito”) are overruled as to Nos. 2, 3, 5, and 7, and sustained as to Nos. 1, 4, and 6.

 

Motion to Compel Compliance

C.C.P. §2025.480(a) provides: “If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.”  (C.C.P. §2025.480(a).)[1]

Petitioners’ subpoena requests the following items:

A copy of all forensically sound evidence drives imaged from a) computer/electronic hardware obtained from any Konnech, Inc. location or b) any and all cloud-based services used by Konnech, including the following:

 

1) From the Konnech, Inc. corporate network:

a. All PCAP traffic at the network perimeter

b. All images identifying ingress and egress TCP/IP source and

c. Destination IP addresses for all known ports or protocols in use in such communications.

 

2) From the Konnech, Inc. network mapping atoll, like NMAP

a. Enumerated topology of the network

b. Enumerated topology of network infrastructure, and

c. Enumerated topology hosts (servers, workstations) existing on the network, including all generated NMAP files and PDFs

 

3) From Konnech, Inc. data captured from all third party cloud services company sites like Jira, Microsoft Teams, or any similar services, including corporate administrative services.

 

In regards to a search warrant executed in the State of Michigan, or otherwise gathered in relation to Case No. BA 509784, People of the State of California v. Eugene Wei Yu (DOB: 11/01/1957).

 

(Petition SS, pg. 2.)

LADA fails to demonstrate that the items requested are privileged.  LADA argues that the items are privileged under Evidence Code §1040, which provides in pertinent part,

(a)  As used in this section, “official information” means information acquired in confidence by a public employee in the course of his or her duty and not open, or officially disclosed, to the public prior to the time the claim of privilege is made.

 

(b)  A public entity has a privilege to refuse to disclose official information, and to prevent another from disclosing official information, if the privilege is claimed by a person authorized by the public entity to do so and either of the following apply:

 

(1)   Disclosure is forbidden by an act of the Congress of the United States or a statute of this state.

 

(2)   Disclosure of the information is against the public interest because there is a necessity for preserving the confidentiality of the information that outweighs the necessity for disclosure in the interest of justice; but no privilege may be claimed under this paragraph if any person authorized to do so has consented that the information be disclosed in the proceeding. In determining whether disclosure of the information is against the public interest, the interest of the public entity as a party in the outcome of the proceeding may not be considered.

 

(Evid. Code §§1040(a)-(b).)

LADA argues that the items subject to the subpoena are privileged because they constitute “official information” because they were “acquired in confidence” by the DA in the course of his duty and have not yet been officially disclosed.  (Opposition, pg. 3.)  Yet, LADA makes no showing that the items were acquired in confidence.  Absent a showing that the items were acquired in confidence, no privilege applies.  (Shepherd v. Superior Court (1976) 17 Cal.3d 107, 124, overruled on other grounds by People v. Holloway (2004) 33 Cal.4th 96; Ibarra v. Superior Court (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 695, 705.)

Moreover, even if LADA were to show that the items subject to the subpoena were “acquired in confidence” and so constitute “official information,” the privilege prescribed by Evidence Code §1040 is not absolute.  Items may be withheld under the provisions of §1040(b)(2) only upon a finding that disclosure would be “against the public interest.”  (Shepherd, 17 Cal.3d at pg. 125.)  LADA has made no such showing.  While Mark Beeart declares that the Konnech matter is an open investigation (Decl. of Beaart ¶6), moving party’s evidence belies that assertion.  (Reply, pg. 2 n.1.)  Moreover, Mr. Beeart makes no showing as to why the disclosure of the subpoenaed items would impact any ongoing investigation. 

Accordingly, Petitioners’ motion is granted.

 

          Conclusion

Petitioners’ motion to compel LADA’s compliance with their March 14, 2023, subpoena duces tecum to produce and permit inspection and copying of forensically sound evidence drives containing information from Konnech, Inc. is granted. 

LADA is to produce the items responsive to Petitioners’ request within 30 days of this ruling.

Moving Party to give notice.

Case Number: 23STCV12365    Hearing Date: September 25, 2023    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CESAR CRUZ,

 

         vs.

 

TINA ODJAGHIAN LAW GROUP, et al.

 Case No.:  23STCV12365

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 21, 2023

 

Defendants Tina Odjaghian Law Group’s, Tina Odjaghian’s, Wil Fabian’s, and Elizabeth Fabian’s unopposed demurrer to pro per Plaintiff Cesar Cruz’s complaint is sustained with 20 days leave to amend as to the x causes of action.

 

          Defendants Tina Odjaghian Law Group (“Law Group”), Tina Odjaghian (“Odjaghian”), Wil Fabian (“Wil”), and Elizabeth Fabian (“Elizabeth”) (collectively, “Defendants”) demur unopposed to the complaint (“Complaint”) of Plaintiff Cesar Cruz (“Cruz”) (“Plaintiff”), in pro per.  (Notice of Demurrer, pg. 1.)

 

Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the moving party must meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading and obviate the need for filing the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41.)

Defendants’ counsel filed a declaration stating on June 9, 2023, she called and left a voicemail message for Plaintiff to discuss the deficiencies in his Complaint and also sent him an email.  (Decl. of Hak ¶4.)  Defendant’s counsel declares the parties were unable to come to an agreement as to all of the items in the demurrer.  (Decl. of Alcumbrac ¶5, Exh. 5.)  Defendants’ counsel declares that having not heard from Plaintiff since she sent him an email and called him, she called and sent him two emails on June 19, 2023.  (Decl. of Alcumbrac ¶6, Exh. 6.)  Defendants’ counsel declares Plaintiff responded to her June 19, 2023, email later that day, in which he stated that he was preparing an amended complaint and that he would send it to her on June 23, 2023.  (Decl. of Alcumbrac ¶7, Exh. 7.)  Defendants’ counsel declares she engaged in several email correspondences with Plaintiff regarding issues with his proposed amended complaint, namely that he would file the complaint in the Imperial County Superior Court.  (Decl. of Alcumbrac ¶¶9-11, Exhs. 9-11.)  Defendant’s counsel’s declaration substantially complies the requirements of C.C.P. §430.41(a) because states the issues raised in the demurrer could not be resolved out of court.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a); see Decl. of Alcumbrac ¶12.)  Therefore, the Court will consider the instant demurrer.

 

          Background

          Plaintiff filed his operative Complaint against Defendants on February 3, 2023, in the Imperial County Superior Court.  On May 1, 2023, Defendants’ motion to transfer was heard before the Imperial County Superior Court; the Imperial County Superior Court granted Defendants’ motion to transfer Plaintiff’s case to the Los Angeles County Superior Court.  Plaintiff’s case was assigned to the instant Court on June 12, 2023.  Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges four causes of action: (1) breach of contract; (2) fraud; (3) general negligence; and (4) “intentional.”

          On July 3, 2023, Defendants filed the instant demurrer.  As of the date of this hearing Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

Demurrer

Summary of Demurrer

Defendants demur on the basis that Plaintiff’s 1st through 4th causes of action are uncertain and the 2nd through 4th causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action against Defendants.  (Demurrer, pgs. 3-4; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f).)

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Uncertainty

A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

If the complaint contains enough facts to apprise defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, failure to label each cause of action is not ground for demurrer: “Although inconvenient, annoying and inconsiderate, the lack of labels . . . does not substantially impair [defendant’s] ability to understand the complaint.” (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 n.2.) 

Where a demurrer is made upon this ground, it must distinctly specify exactly how or why the pleading is uncertain, and where such uncertainty appears (by reference to page and line numbers of the complaint).  (See Fenton v. Groveland Community Services District (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 797, 809.) 

Defendants fail to specify where the uncertainty appears by reference to page line and numbers in the complaint.  (See id.)  However, Plaintiff’s Complaint as a whole is impermissibly uncertain such that Defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against them.  (Khoury, 14 Cal.App.4th at pg. 616.)

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer on the basis of uncertainty is sustained with 20 days leave to amend. 

 

Failure to State a Claim

Breach of Contract (1st COA)

A cause of action for breach of contract requires the following elements: (1) the existence of a contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or non-performance; (3) defendant’s breach of the contract; and (4) damages suffered by plaintiff.  (Reichert v. General Insurance Co. (1968) 68 Cal.2d 822, 830.)

Plaintiff must plead the contract, plaintiff’s performance or excuse for non-performance, defendant’s breach, and damage to plaintiff therefrom.  (Acoustics, Inc. v. Trepte Construction Co. (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 887, 913.)  Although a written contract is usually pleaded by alleging its making and attaching a copy which is incorporated by reference, a written contract can also be pleaded by alleging the making and the substance of the relevant terms.  (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Insurance Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199; Perry v. Robertson (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 333, 341.)  “An oral contract may be pleaded generally as to its effect, because it is rarely possible to allege the exact words. [Citation.] A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. [Citations.]”  (Khoury, 14 Cal.App.4th at pg. 616.)  That plaintiff could not allege with specificity the exact terms of the contract does not preclude statement of a cause of action.  (Id.)

Plaintiff alleges a retainer agreement was made.  (Complaint ¶BC-1.)  Plaintiff alleges criminal breach of contract.  (Complaint ¶BC-1.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant entered a binding contract with the intention to deceive and defraud.  (Complaint ¶BC-2.)  Plaintiff alleges he suffered damages proximately caused by defendant’s breach of the agreement by personal injury.  (Complaint ¶BC-4.)  Plaintiff alleges he is entitled to $100 million in attorney’s fees, punitive damages, and restitution.  (Complaint ¶BC-6.)

Plaintiff fails to plead the existence of a written or oral contract, either by attaching the contract or alleging the terms of the contract, and with whom the contract was made.  (Construction Protective Services, Inc., 29 Cal.4th at pgs. 198-199; Khoury, 14 Cal.App.4th at pg. 616.)  Plaintiff fails to allege his performance or non-performance of the contract and which defendants breached the contract, and facts regarding the breach of the contract.  (Reichert, 68 Cal.2d at pg. 830.)

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 1st cause of action for breach of contract is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Fraud (2nd COA)

          A fraud cause of action must contain the following elements: (1) defendant made representations that were false when made; (2) defendant knew the statements were false when made; (3) plaintiff justifiably relied upon the statements; and (4) as a result, plaintiff suffered damages.  (CACI 1900).  

Fraud must be pleaded with specificity rather than with general and conclusory allegations.  (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.)  The specificity requirement means a plaintiff must allege facts showing how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were made, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, the plaintiff must allege the names of the persons who made the representations, their authority to speak on behalf of the corporation, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when the representation was made.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645; West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 793.)

Plaintiff alleges intentional or negligent misrepresentation of criminal malpractice.  (Complaint ¶FR-2.)  Plaintiff alleges the representations were false and the truth was as stated in Attachment FR-2.b, which is not attached to the Complaint.  (Complaint ¶FR-2.)  Plaintiff alleges when defendant made the representations, defendant knew they were false, or defendant had no reasonable ground for believing the representations were trust.  (Complaint ¶FR-2.)  Plaintiff alleges defendant concealed or suppressed material facts as stated in Attachment FR-3.a, but does not include such an attachment with his Complaint.  (Complaint ¶FR-3.)  Plaintiff alleges defendant concealed or suppressed material facts defendant was bound to disclose or by telling plaintiff other facts to mislead plaintiff and prevent plaintiff from discovering the concealed or suppressed facts.  (Complaint ¶FR-3.)  Plaintiff alleges defendant made a promise about a material matter without any intention of performing it in the form of loss of benefits and entering a binding contract with the intention to deceive and defraud Plaintiff.  (Complaint ¶FR-4.)  Plaintiff alleges in justifiable reliance upon defendant’s conduct, Plaintiff was induced to act as state in Attachment FR-5, but no such attachment is included with the Complaint.  (Complaint ¶FR-5.)  Plaintiff alleges because of his reliance upon defendant’s conduct, Plaintiff has been damaged as stated in Attachment FR-6, but no attachment is included.  (Complaint ¶FR-6.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants colluded with the insurance company by way of concealment, harming Plaintiff through continued oppressive malicious acts in order to benefit.  (Complaint ¶FR-6.)

Plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for fraud.  Plaintiff fails to allege facts showing how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were made.  (Lazar, 12 Cal.4th at pg. 645.)

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 2nd of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

General Negligence (3rd COA)

A cause of action for negligence requires the following elements: (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiff to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff.  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318; Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.)

Plaintiff alleges Defendants Does 1 to 4 was the proximate cause of damages to Plaintiff.  (Complaint ¶IT-1.)  Plaintiff alleges by the following acts or omissions to act, defendant intentionally caused the damages to plaintiff: Plaintiff entered into a binding contract with premeditated intent to deceive and defraud Plaintiff.  (Complaint ¶IT-1.)  Plaintiff alleges the following reasons for liability: infliction of emotional distress, misrepresentation, criminal malpractice, legal malpractice, professional malpractice, breach of contract, collusion, corruption, conflict of interest, financial exploitation, discrimination, unfair business practices, exploitation/abuse, deception, fraud, defamation, discrimination, breach of duty of care, breach of fiduciary duty, malice, oppression, strict liability, assault-battery, and aggravated assault.  (Complaint ¶IT-1.) 

Plaintiff fails to allege Defendants owed him a legal duty to use due care, Defendants breached the duty, causation, and damage to plaintiff.  (County of Santa Clara, 137 Cal.App.4th at pg. 318.)

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 3rd cause of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Intentional Tort (4th COA)

          Plaintiff fails to allege a cognizable cause of action; Plaintiff must identify the particular intentional tort and allege facts that meet the required elements to allege the specific tort.  Plaintiff makes the same allegations as stated in the 3rd cause of action.  Plaintiff cannot allege an intentional tort by way of listing the names of various intentional torts.

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 4th cause of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

          Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 1st, 2ne, 3rd, and 4th causes of action in his Complaint is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.
