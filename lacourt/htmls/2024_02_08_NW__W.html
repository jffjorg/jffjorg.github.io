DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV15600    Hearing Date: February 8, 2024    Dept: W

MICHAEL COROMINAS, et al. v. WEST HILLS HOSPITAL AND MEDICAL CENTER, et al.

 

motion FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT  

 

Date of Hearing:        February 8, 2024                   Trial Date:       November 25, 2024

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        21STCV15600

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Exer Medical Corporation d/b/a Exer Urgent Care (erroneously sued and served herein as Exer Urgent Care) and Michael H. Lee, M.D.

Responding Party:     Non-Opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 26, 2023, Plaintiffs Michael Corominas and Lisa Corominas filed a complaint against Defendants West Hills Hospital and Medical Center, Martin M. Mortazavi, M.D., Jessica Thomas, M.D., Paul Diehl, M.D., Dean Wilson, Jr., P.A., Specialty Imagining Center, Tejpal Singh, M.D., Providence Cedars-Sina Tarzana Medical Center, Chris Y. Wang, M.D., Jamie Soo Moi Eng, M.D., Exer Urgent Care, Michael H. Lee, M.D., and California Institute of Neuroscience for medical negligence and loss of consortium. Plaintiffs allege that further testing and workup should have been performed during the ER visit including testing for signs and symptoms and were negligent in failing to do so.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Exer Medical Corporation d/b/a Exer Urgent Care and Michael H. Lee, M.D.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Exer Medical Corporation d/b/a Exer Urgent Care and Michael H. Lee, M.D. (collectively “Exer Urgent Care”) move this court for summary judgment on the grounds Plaintiffs’ complaint fails because Exer Urgent Care did not breach the applicable standard of care regarding Plaintiff Michael Corominas; and, Plaintiffs’ complaint fails because Exer Urgent Care did not, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, proximately cause or contribute to Plaintiffs’ injuries.

 

The elements of medical malpractice are:¿“(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.”¿¿(Simmons v. West Covina Medical Clinic¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 696, 701-02 (citations omitted).)¿ “Both the standard of care and defendants’ breach must normally be established by expert testimony in a medical malpractice case.”¿¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Thus, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”¿¿(Munro v. Regents of University of California¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).)¿ An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.¿¿(Starr v.¿Mooslin¿(1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.)¿

 

Defendants present the expert declaration of Richard Cho, M.D. Dr. Cho earned his medical degree from UCLA Medical School in 1994 and completed his internal medicine residency with UCI Medical Center with an emphasis in geriatrics in 1997. (Cho Decl. ¶3.) Dr. Cho has been actively practicing internal medicine/hospitalist medicine for more than 15 years. (Cho Decl. ¶3.) Dr. Cho testifies based on his background, education, training and experience as well as review of the materials, it is his opinion that none of the alleged actions/omissions of Defendant Exer Urgent Care or Defendant Dr. Lee proximately caused or contributed to Plaintiffs’ injuries or damages. (Cho Decl. ¶¶4-45.) Specifically, Plaintiff never presented with a fever, his spine was never tender to palpation, he did not exhibit any neurological deficits or radicular pain or motor weakness or bowel/bladder dysfunction, or paralysis at any time during his January 26, 2020 presentation to Exer Urgent Care. (Cho Decl. ¶27.) Plaintiff did not return to Exer Urgent Care in 2020 nor did he contact them regarding his back pain at any relevant time. (Cho Decl. ¶28.) Instead, Plaintiff presented to Providence Cedars Sinai Tarzana Medical Center’s Emergency Department on January 29, 2020. (Cho Decl. ¶29.) Accordingly, the standard of care was met at all times with respect to the care and treatment provided to Plaintiff by Defendants. (Cho Decl. ¶33.) Specifically, the evaluations, history, documentation, orders, care and treatment, and all related services rendered to Plaintiff were within he standard of care. (Cho Decl. ¶34.) Plaintiff presented with low back pain, which was a non-specific complaint and reported a prior history of back pain and a herniated disc. (Cho Decl. ¶35.) Based upon his presentation, Plaintiff’s complaints, and the evaluation and findings performed at Exer Urgent Care on January 26, 2020, was within the standard of care to diagnose him with local pain affecting the low back (non-traumatic), and back pain. (Cho Decl. ¶38.) It was also within the standard of care to prescribe Plaintiff with Percocet and Prednisone for treatment of his low back pain.  (Cho Decl. 39.) This was a reasonable course of action given his non-specific low back pain and lack of other, more serious signs or symptoms. (Cho Decl. 39.) Lastly, Plaintiff was properly provided discharge instructions including referring to and instructing to follow-up with his primary care provider and if his condition worsened or Plaintiff developed new symptoms, to contact Defendants or go to the hospital emergency department. (Cho Decl. ¶40.) Dr. Cho contends a diagnosis (or even a suspicion) of an epidural abscess requires more than just a complaint of low back pain. (Cho Decl. ¶44.)

 

Defendants have met their burden of showing, by expert declaration, they did not breach the standard of care or cause Plaintiff’s injury.  As Defendants are entitled to judgment on the first cause of action for negligence, Defendants are also entitled to judgment on the second cause of action for loss of consortium. (See Vanhooser v. Superior Court (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 921, 927.)

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show, by contrary expert declaration, that a triable issue of fact exists. Plaintiff instead filed a notice of non-opposition. Therefore, Defendants Exer Urgent Care is entitled to summary judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendants Exer Medical Corporation d/b/a Exer Urgent Care and Michael H. Lee, M.D.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV03809    Hearing Date: February 8, 2024    Dept: W

JUAN CARLOS ANTONIO, et al. vs MARTHA MEJIA CAMPOS, et al.

 

DEFENDANTs’ demurrer with motion to strike

 

Date of Hearing:        February 8, 2024                               Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03809

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Juan Carlos Antonio and Belen Solano

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Reyes Decl. ¶¶3-15.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 31, 2023, Plaintiffs Juan Carlos Antonio and Belen Solano filed a complaint against Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson for Violation Rent Stabilization Ordinance, Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, Unlawful and Unfair Business Practices in Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200, and Implied Warranty of Habitability. The parties entered into a rental agreement. Plaintiffs allege the unit they rented, however, was unpermitted and had several habitability issues. Plaintiffs further allege Defendants did not properly file a Declaration of Intent to Evict in Order to Comply with a Government Agency’s Order.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND, in part. Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson demur to the complaint on the grounds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to support a cause of action against Defendants. Defendants also move to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint.

 

First Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action on the grounds for Violation of Rent Stabilization Ordinance on the grounds that Plaintiffs do not have a private right of action to sue under the RSO.

 

LAMC § 151.05 provides that “no landlord shall demand or accept rent for a rental unit without first procuring and serving on the tenant or displaying in a conspicuous place a valid written registration statement from the Department or its designee. On or after April 30, 1983, no landlord shall demand or accept rent for a rental unit without first serving a copy of a valid registration or annual registration renewal statement on the tenant of that rental unit.”   

 

If a landlord fails to comply with LAMC § 151.05 and demands rent from a tenant, “[a] tenant may withhold the payment of any rent otherwise lawfully due and owing after July 1, 1979, until such time as the landlord has complied with Section 151.04 C. and Section 151.05 A. of this Chapter.  Once the landlord has complied with Sections 151.04 C. and 151.05 A. of this Chapter, the tenant becomes obligated to pay the current rent and any back rent withheld pursuant to this Subsection.”  (LAMC § 151.11(B).)   

 

Defendants argue the complaint alleges violation of the RSO, but fails to allege any specific Los Angeles Municipal Code or ordinance that Defendants allegedly violated. Plaintiffs also fail to cite any authority providing them a private right of action to sue for violation of the RSO.

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have not alleged facts sufficient to support a claim for Violation of Rent Stabilization Ordinance. Plaintiffs have a private right of action to sue for violation of LAMC § 151.11. However, the court agrees the complaint makes no reference to LAMC §151.11. The general rule is that statutory claims must be pled with particularity. (See, e.g., Covenant Care, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 790; Zipperer v. County of Santa Clara (2005) 133 Cal. App. 4th 1013, 1020.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Second Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the second cause of action for breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing on the grounds the claim fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendants.

 

“The covenant of good faith and fair dealing, implied by law in every contract, exists merely to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made. The covenant thus cannot ‘ “ ‘be endowed with an existence independent of its contractual underpinnings.’ ” ’ It cannot impose substantive duties or limits on the contracting parties beyond those incorporated in the specific terms of their agreement.” (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349-350, original italics, internal citations omitted.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ complaint fails to allege that all conditions required for Defendants’ performance had occurred or were excused and that Defendants unfairly interfered with the Plaintiffs’ right to receive benefits of the contract.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the facts are sufficiently pled establishing the contractual relationship between the parties. The court agrees. A claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing must “go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach” and not “rel[y] on the same alleged acts [or] simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action.” (Careau & Co. v. Sec. Pac. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.) Although no breach of contract claim is pled, the court considers the underlying contract reference alleged in the complaint. Here, the complaint goes beyond a mere breach of contract and alleges Defendants interfered with Plaintiffs right to receive the benefits of the contract by renting an illegal unit as well as evicting Plaintiffs in bad faith including failing to comply with LAMC.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Third Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for Violation of Business & Professions Code section 17200 on the grounds the claim fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendants.

 

Business and Professions Code section 17200 prohibits “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.” (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200; see Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 610.) “An unlawful business practice or act is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969.)  “A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1473.) 

 

Defendants argue the cause of action fails to allege any purportedly “unlawful” or “unfair” business practices with the required specificity. Moreover, Plaintiffs do not allege sufficient facts to support the relief requested.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue Defendants misrepresentation and illegal keeping of Plaintiff's property (security deposit) are unlawful. Moreover, Defendant’s demurrer overlooks the impact of California’s Proposition 64 on this statute.

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have failed to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for violation of Business and Professions Code 17200. As noted above, statutory claims must be pled with particularity. (See, e.g., Covenant Care, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 790.) While the complaint generally alleges Defendants violated LAMC, Plaintiffs must allege with particularity the ordinance violated. However, the court finds the complaint alleges Plaintiffs suffered “injury in fact” in paying rent on an illegal unit as well as being improperly evicted and loss of money as a result.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fourth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability on the grounds the claim fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendants.

 

The essential elements of a cause of action for breach of implied warranty of habitability are: (1) a materially defective condition of the property affecting habitability; (2) the defective condition was unknown to the tenant at the time of occupancy; (3) the effect on habitability of the defective condition was not apparent on reasonable inspection; (4) notice was given to the landlord within a reasonable time after the tenant discovered, or should have discovered, the breach; and (5) damages. (Quevedo v. Braga (1977) 72 Cal.App.3d Supp. 1, 7—8 (Quevedo), overruled on other grounds by Knight v. Hallsthammar (1981) 29 Cal. 3d 46, 55 [disapproving of the element that landlord must have been allowed reasonable time to repair].) 

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs fail to allege that notice was provided to Defendants about a material defective condition affecting the premises habitability, fails to allege that Plaintiffs provided notice to the Defendants of the condition within a reasonable time after Plaintiffs’ discovery of the condition, and fails to allege that Defendants were given reasonable time to correct the deficiency. Finally, Plaintiffs also fail to allege specific damages caused by the alleged breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability.

 

The court agrees. The complaint fails to allege that notice was provided to Defendants about a material defective condition affecting the premises habitability or when said notice was allegedly provided to Defendants , provides no information indicating that the complaint alleges that Plaintiffs provided notice to the Defendants of the condition within a reasonable time after Plaintiffs’ discovery of the condition, and provides no information indicating that the complaint alleges that Defendants were given reasonable time to correct the deficiency.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike Plaintiffs’ demand for past rent collected in the amount of $67,000.00 and relocation assistance in the amount of $20,850.00 as well as Plaintiffs’ request for attorney fees.

 

Plaintiff failed to file an opposition to Defendants’ motion to strike. The court agrees Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged facts to support a claim for past rent collected and relocation assistance. Moreover, the complaint does not allege facts supporting a request for attorney fees.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.
