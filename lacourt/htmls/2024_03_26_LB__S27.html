DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV19312    Hearing Date: March 26, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Claudia and Josue Morales filed this action against Defendants, Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Memorial Health Services, Praveen R. Jonnala, M.D., and Ali Khoynezhad, M.D., PhD for negligence, hospital negligence, and loss of consortium.  Plaintiff added Ivana Radeva, M.D. as Doe 1 and California Anesthesia Associates Medical Group, Inc. as Doe 5.  The complaint arises out of Defendants’ care and treatment of Claudia Morales for cardiothoracic surgery. 

 

2.     Motions for Summary Judgment

a.     Law Governing Standard of Care

The standard of care against which the acts of health care providers are to be measured is a matter within the knowledge of experts.  Elcome v. Chin (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 310, 317.  Unless the conduct required by the particular circumstances is within the common knowledge of the layman, the standard of care in a malpractice action can only be proved by an expert’s testimony.  Id.  If the “common knowledge” exception does not apply to a medical malpractice action, expert evidence is conclusive and cannot be disregarded.  Id. 

 

A medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because he chooses one medically acceptable method of treatment or diagnosis and it turns out that another medically accepted method would have been a better choice.  CACI 506.  Likewise, a medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because his efforts are unsuccessful or he makes an error that was reasonable under the circumstances.  CACI 505.

 

Whether the standard of care in the community has been breached presents the basic issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by opinion testimony unless the medical question is within the common knowledge of laypersons.  See Jambazian v. Borden (1994) 25 Cal.App4th 836, 844. “‘When a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.’“ (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985.)

 

b.     Law Governing Causation

In order to establish that defendant's negligence was a “substantial factor” in causing injury or death, the plaintiff must prove the negligence was of itself sufficient to bring about that harm.  “The law is well settled that in a personal injury action causation must be proven within a reasonable medical probability based upon competent expert testimony.  Mere possibility alone is insufficient to establish a prima facie case.  [Citations.]  That there is a distinction between a reasonable medical ‘probability’ and a medical 'possibility' needs little discussion.  There can be many possible ‘causes,’ indeed, an infinite number of circumstances which can produce an injury or disease. A possible cause only becomes ‘probable’ when, in the absence of other reasonable causal explanations, it becomes more likely than not that the injury was a result of its action.  This is the outer limit of inference upon which an issue may be submitted to the jury.” Bromme v. Pavitt (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1498; citing Jones v. Ortho Pharmaceutical Corp. (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 396, 402 403.

 

c.     Moving Burden

Defendants support their motion with the expert declaration of Dr. Eric Ontiveros, M.D.  Dr. Ontiveros sets forth his expert qualifications, states which documents he reviewed to form his expert opinion, details the moving defendants’ care and treatment of Plaintiffs, and ultimately concludes Defendants complied with the standard of care at all times and nothing they did caused or contributed to Plaintiffs’ injuries. 

 

d.     Shifted Burden

Because Defendants met their moving burden to show they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law, the burden shifts to Plaintiffs to raise a triable issue of material fact.  They failed to file timely opposition to the motion, and therefore necessarily failed to meet their shifted burden.  The motion is unopposed and granted. 

 

Moving Defendants are ordered to give notice.

2.     Motion to Compel Mental Examination

a.     Relief Sought

Defendant seeks an order compelling Plaintiff to attend a mental examination on the ground that he is claiming severe emotional distress as a result of the incident that forms the basis of the action.

 

b.     Opposition

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  He argues it should be denied because (a) Defendant erroneously noticed a physical examination instead of a mental examination, (b) Defendants failed to meet and confer prior to filing the motion, and (c) the requested examination and tests are overbroad. 

 

c.     Reply

Reply papers were due on or before Tuesday, 3/19/24.  Despite the fact that the opposition papers were timely filed and served, the Court has not received timely reply papers.  The Court will not consider late-filed reply papers. 

 

d.     Authority for Mental Examination

Except for defense physicals in personal injury cases (in which one examination is permitted as a matter of course) and exams arranged by stipulation, a court order is required for a physical or mental examination. Such order may be made only after notice and hearing, and for “good cause shown.”  (CCP §2032.320(a).) 

 

The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action.  (CCP §2032.020(a).)  This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation.  Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337.  Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.”  See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837, wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer.  Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged. 

 

Additionally, CCP §2032.310(b) requires a party moving to conduct a mental examination to specify “the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination.”  §2032.320(d) requires the Court, in any order granting a motion to compel mental examination, to specify the above information.  Pursuant to Carpenter v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 260, a court order granting a motion to compel mental examination must specify and list by name the diagnostic tests and procedures to be used in the examination.

 

e.     Physical Examination

Plaintiff notes that Defendant erroneously noticed his mental examination as a physical examination without seeking a stipulation or leave of court to conduct the examination.  The issue is moot, as Defendant has now filed this motion as a motion to conduct a mental examination. 

 

f.      Meet and Confer

Plaintiff contends the motion should be denied because Defendant failed to meet and confer.  The paragraph heading indicates meet and confer is required per CCP §2016.040, and the body of the argument indicates meet and confer is required per §2032.310 et seq. 

 

§2016.040 simply indicates what is required in a meet and confer declaration when the code requires a meet and confer declaration.  It does not specify whether and when meet and confer declarations are required. 

 

Plaintiff does not specify the code section that requires meet and confer when he cites ”§2032.010 et seq.”  The Court has reviewed the Code, and finds that CCP §2032.310(b) states the requirements for a motion to compel a mental examination, and specifies, “The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  The Court asks Plaintiff, in the future in this and other actions, to specify the code section to which he refers in his briefs.

 

In any event, the Declaration of David K. Korenfeld, Defense Counsel, in support of the moving papers, states at ¶10 that Counsel attempted to meet and confer prior to filing the motion.  Counsel declares that his meet and confer letter is attached to the moving papers as Exhibit G.  The meet and confer letter is detailed and discusses the parties’ positions and why Defendant believes a mental examination is proper.  The Court asks Plaintiff’s attorney not to include misleading information in his briefs in the future in this and other actions.

Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 839, a party claiming ongoing mental health issues “can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.”  Defendant therefore showed good cause for the examination.

Case Number: 23LBCV00491    Hearing Date: March 26, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Cary Atwood initiated this action against Defendants, Allyn Emery and Loma Law Group, APC on 3/15/23.  Plaintiff’s original complaint included causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty, conversion, unjust enrichment, breach of contract, equitable estoppel, quantum meruit, and accounting.  The crux of the original complaint was that Plaintiff agreed to generate cases for Defendants, and Defendant would pay her for those referrals.  Defendants failed to pay, resulting in a balance due in excess of $400,000.

 

Defendants intended to challenge the original complaint, and the parties’ meet and confer efforts resulted in the filing of a First Amended Complaint.  The FAC was filed on 8/18/23, and the FAC adds Lyran Consulting, Inc. as an additional plaintiff.  The same causes of action from the original complaint are pled, and the main difference from the original complaint to the FAC is that the FAC pleads the existence of two contracts, one between Atwood and Defendants, and the other between Lyran and Defendants.   

 

  1. Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares the parties met and conferred extensively before Defendants filed their demurrer.  The meet and confer requirement is satisfied. 

 

c.     Requests for Judicial Notice

Defendants seek judicial notice of various documents from the Secretary of State’s website concerning Lyran Consulting, Inc.  The RJN is granted.

 

d.     First Cause of Action, Breach of Fiduciary Duty

i.              Issue

Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is for breach of fiduciary duty.  Plaintiffs allege their relationship with Defendants was that of joint venturers, such that Defendants owe Plaintiffs a fiduciary duty.  Defendants demur to the cause of action, contending Plaintiffs failed to allege facts supporting the conclusion that the parties’ relationship was fiduciary in nature. 

 

ii.             Types of Fiduciary Relationships

The parties agree that a fiduciary relationship can arise in one of two ways.  First, there can be a specific type of relationship, such as a joint venture, that imposes a fiduciary duty.  Second, a fiduciary duty arises if one party knowingly undertakes to act on behalf of and for the benefit of the other. 

 

iii.            Joint Venture

Plaintiffs’ FAC alleges the parties were in a fiduciary relationship due to their “joint venturer” status.  Defendants contend the parties’ contracts, attached as exhibits to the FAC, make clear that the parties were not joint venturers.  The parties’ contracts, at ¶2.03, state, “Contractor enters into this agreement, and will remain throughout the term of the agreement, as an independent contractor. Contractor agrees that he or she is not and will not become an employee. partner, agent. or principal of Client while this agreement is in effect.”

 

Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, argue that the language in the contract is not controlling, and that the issue of whether the parties were in a joint venture is one for the jury to decide.  They rely on April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 820 to support their position.  In April Enterprises, the plaintiff and the defendant entered into an agreement to produce a television show, with each party receiving 50% of net profits upon syndication.  Id. at 813-814.  The defendant moved for judgment on the pleadings on the breach of fiduciary duty claim, contending the parties’ contract specifically indicated the plaintiff was an independent contractor, and the contract was fully integrated.  The Court of Appeals held that the label in the parties’ contract was not controlling, and that instead the issue was whether the elements necessary to create a joint venture had been pled.  It held that the elements had been pled, such that it was for the jury to determine whether the parties were in a joint venture or not.  Id. at 819.  Specifically, the Court held (citations omitted):

“A joint venture ... is an undertaking by two or more persons jointly to carry out a single business enterprise for profit.” (Citation) The elements necessary for its creation are: (1) joint interest in a common business; (2) with an understanding to share profits and losses; and (3) a right to joint control. (Citations)  “Such a venture or undertaking may be formed by parol agreement [citations], or it may be assumed as a reasonable deduction from the acts and declarations of the parties [citations].” (Citation) Whether a joint venture actually exists depends on the intention of the parties. (Citations)

Here, it cannot be determined as a matter of law that the complaint fails to allege facts supporting creation of a joint venture. Appellant argues that the common enterprise to seek syndication of the show after it was produced and originally telecast was a joint venture and we find that the first amended complaint sufficiently alleges such a relationship. The requisite joint interest in a common business is supplied by the allegations that the parties planned to coproduce the shows in order to exploit the market for its syndication and that each contributed its own unique talents in furtherance of this objective. The requisite joint control is supplied by the allegation that each party agreed to have equal rights to initiate syndication of the show.

 

Defendants, in reply, contend April Enterprises is not on point for two reasons.  First, they contend the parties’ contract, in this case, expressly disclaims any partnership or agency agreement, where the April Enterprises contract had no such language.  Second, they contend the elements of joint venture are not pled in this case. 

 

The Court rejects the first argument.  The crux of the April Enterprises holding is that the parties cannot contract around the existence of a joint venture relationship.  If the elements of joint venture are established, the fact that the parties defined their relationship differently in the contract does not defeat the relationship. 

 

The Court agrees, however, with the second argument.  The plaintiff in April Enterprises had pled that the parties were forming a business for the purpose of producing a television show, and that they were to split profits on a 50/50 basis.  Plaintiffs, in this case, allege they entered into essentially a referral relationship with Defendants, where they would provide referrals and Defendants would pay them for the referrals.  None of the elements of joint venture, detailed above, are pled. 

 

iv.            One Party Acts for the Benefit of Another

Plaintiffs also argue, in opposition to the demurrer, that they have sufficiently alleged that they agreed to act for the benefit of Defendants, such that the second definition of fiduciary duty is met.  The Court rejects Plaintiffs’ argument for two reasons.  First, the issue is not whether Plaintiffs were acting for the benefit of Defendants, but whether Defendants agreed to act for the benefit of Plaintiffs; no such allegations appear in the complaint.  Second, as Defendants correctly noted in the demurrer, when the parties’ contract creates a mutually beneficial relationship, there is no fiduciary relationship.  See City of Hope National Medical Center v. Genentech, Inc. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386 (mis-cited as City of Hope, 43 Cal.4th at 386 in moving papers). 

 

 

v.         Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty is sustained.  Because it does not appear further allegations could be pled to support imposition of a fiduciary relationship, leave to amend is denied.

 

e.     Second Cause of Action, Conversion

Plaintiffs, in their second cause of action, allege Defendants converted the monies due to Plaintiffs, and the monies due are in excess of $400,000.  Defendants demur to the claim on two grounds.  First, they contend breach of contract cannot support conversion.  Second, they contend Plaintiffs have failed to plead a sum certain that would support a conversion claim.

 

Plaintiffs argue the conversion claim is based on “knowing withholding of money in violation of the joint venture” and not based on breach of contract.  Because the Court finds Plaintiffs failed to plead a joint venture, this argument fails.  The claim is clearly one for breach of contract, and the demurrer to the conversion cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

f.      Third Cause of Action, Unjust Enrichment

Defendants contend the cause of action for unjust enrichment rises and falls with Plaintiffs’ breach of contract and quasi-contract. 

 

g.     Fourth Cause of Action, Breach of Contract

  1. Vague and Ambiguous

Defendants first contend the cause of action for breach of contract is uncertain and ambiguous because the cause of action purports to be one single cause of action, but it is based on the alleged breach of two different contracts. 

As will be discussed further below, the Court finds Lyran cannot maintain its claims in this suit.  Plaintiffs must file an amended complaint doing one of two things.  First, Plaintiffs may file the amended complaint on behalf of Atwood only, eliminating Lyran as a plaintiff.  Second, if Plaintiffs revive Lyran’s terminated status, they may keep Lyran as a plaintiff, but they must each plead separate causes of action for breach of contract to avoid confusion. 

 

Notably, the Court is not clear on why Lyran is attempting to act as a party to this lawsuit in light of its terminated status (below) and in light of the fact that it alleges it assigned all of its claims to Atwood.

 

ii.          Judicial Admission

Defendants next argue that the original complaint only pled a contract between Atwood and Defendants, such that Plaintiffs “admit” that was the only operative contract.  Plaintiffs, however, added Lyran as a party to the case via the First Amended Complaint, and therefore had to add the Lyran contract.  Defendants cite nothing to support their position that this is improper.  The demurrer on this ground is overruled.

 

iii.         Assignment

Defendants argue the contract between Lyran and Defendants cannot form the basis of a breach of contract claim because Plaintiffs admit Lyran assigned all rights in the contract to Atwood, and the contract includes an anti-assignment clause. 

 

The contract between Lyran and Defendants is Exhibit B to the FAC.  ¶4.07 of the contract states, “Neither this agreement nor any duties or obligations under this agreement may be assigned by Contractor without the prior written consent of Client.” 

 

Plaintiffs’ FAC, at ¶4, alleges, “Plaintiff Cary Atwood is the successor-in-interest and assignee to all right, title and interest in the contract between plaintiff Lyran Consulting and defendants.” 

 

Plaintiffs argue, in opposition to the demurrer, that the date of assignment is not pled, and that if the assignment occurred after the breach, it would not be a violation of the agreement.  neither party cites authority directly on point to this issue, but it does seem that a contract with a non-assignment clause could be assigned for purposes of collection, as opposed to purposes of performance. 

 

The demurrer on this ground is overruled.

 

iv.            Status

Defendants’ next argument is that Lyran is not a California corporation, and therefore the contract is voidable.  The Court agrees with Plaintiffs, who argue, in opposition to the demurrer, that the contention that a contract is “voidable” is an affirmative defense.  Nothing cited by Defendants states that the contract is “void,” only “voidable.”  The demurrer on this ground is therefore overruled.

 

As a related argument, Defendants argue Lyran is not a California corporation and therefore cannot maintain its lawsuit against Defendants.  Defendants cite Corporations Code §§2105 and 2203 in support of this position.  §2203(c) provides that a foreign corporation “shall not maintain any action or proceeding upon any intrastate business so transacted in any court of this state” unless it complies with certain requirements.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, argue there is no proof that Lyran is not currently in good standing.  The judicially noticeable documents, however, show that, as of 10/18/23, Lyran’s status was “terminated.”  In an abundance of caution, the Court reviewed the current Secretary of State website, and it continues to show Lyran’s status as “terminated.” 

 

The demurrer to Lyran’s cause of action for breach of contract is sustained.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiffs intend to and do revive Lyran’s status with the Secretary of State. 

 

h.     Fifth Cause of Action, Equitable Estoppel 

Defendants contend there is no cause of action for equitable estoppel, which exists solely as a defense to a complaint.  Plaintiffs argue they are pleading equitable estoppel because they anticipate Defendants will assert a defense of unenforceable fee sharing between an attorney and a non-attorney, but they are estopped from asserting such a defense. 

 

The Court finds this is not a cause of action.  If Defendants attempt to assert an improper affirmative defense, Plaintiffs may file a pleading challenge to the defense, or may otherwise make any necessary argument that the defense does not apply, but they need not assert a defense to a potential defense by way of their complaint.  The demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

 

i.      Quantum Meruit

Defendants argue the quantum meruit cause of action is barred by the two-year statute of limitations.  Specifically, Defendants contend the contract between Lyran and Defendants automatically terminated at its inception due to Lyran’s assignment of the contract to Atwood, and that date was more than two years prior to the filing of the complaint. 

 

Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, correctly note that the date of assignment is not pled.  Additionally, and more importantly, even if the statute of limitations were to bar Lyran’s claims, it would not bar Atwood’s claims.  As noted above, Lyran’s claims are barred because of its terminated status with the Secretary of State, so the real remaining issue is whether Atwood’s claims are barred.  Because they are not, the demurrer on this ground is overruled.

Defendants also argue the quantum meruit cause of action rises and falls with the breach of contract cause of action.  The Court agrees.  Lyran’s quantum meruit cause of action fails for the reasons stated above, but the demurrer to Atwood’s claim is overruled.

 

j.      Seventh Cause of Action, Accounting

Plaintiffs argue a fiduciary relationship can support a claim for accounting.  Because there is no fiduciary relationship pled, the demurrer to the accounting cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  

 

  1. Motion to Require a Bond

a.     Relief Sought

Defendants seek to have Plaintiffs filed a bond in the amount of $410,000 on the basis that Plaintiffs are not California residents.

 

b.     General Governing Law

In an action or special proceeding brought by a nonresident plaintiff, the defendant may at any time move for an order requiring the plaintiff to post security.   (CCP §1030(a).)  The stated grounds for the motion are that the plaintiff resides out of state or is a foreign corporation, and there is a reasonable possibility that the moving defendant will obtain a favorable judgment. (CCP §1030(b).)  The motion can be brought at any time.  (CCP §1030(a).) The trial court has no authority to deny the motion on the ground that it is untimely.  (Heffron v. Los Angeles Transit Lines (1959) 170 Cal.App.2d 709.)  The motion is required to be noticed.  (CCP §1030(a).) The notice of motion must be served in accordance with CCP §1005(b). The motion must be accompanied by a supporting affidavit or declaration which establishes the stated grounds for the motion and sets forth the nature and amount of the costs and attorney fees the defendant has incurred and expects to incur until the action is concluded.  (CCP §1030(b).)  It must also be accompanied by a memorandum of points and authorities.  (CCP §1030(b), CRC 313(a).)

 

The plaintiff may pursue a number of tactics in opposing a motion for security. These may include:

(1) proof that the plaintiff is not a nonresident;

(2) showing the plaintiff's indigency (CCP §995.240); and

(3) arguing the defendant's failure to make an adequate prima facie showing of a reasonable possibility of success in the action.

 

In cases where adequate grounds exist for granting the motion for security, the plaintiff can still challenge the amount of the costs and attorney fees requested by the defendant. The security can be ordered only for "reasonable" attorney fees, and the defendant must be otherwise entitled to recover those fees by contract or by another statutory provision.  (CCP §1030(a).)

 

c.     Analysis

Plaintiffs argue the motion must be denied because Defendants are not California residents.  They cite various cases holding that the purpose of §1030 is to enable a California resident sued by an out-of-state resident to secure costs in light of the difficulty of enforcing a costs judgment against a person who is not within the court’s jurisdiction. 

 

While this may be the purpose of the statute, there is nothing in the statute or in any cited case clarifying that a moving defendant must be a California resident in order to bring the motion.  The Court therefore rejects Plaintiffs’ argument in this regard.

 

d.     Reasonable Probability of Success on the Merits

Plaintiffs next argue Defendants have not shown a reasonable probability of success on the merits.  Defendants challenge Plaintiffs’ use of federal authorities in this regard.  The Court finds that, using the California standard, Defendants have not met their burden.  Their moving papers merely reiterate their arguments in their demurrer, which was discussed above.  The problem with their position is that they do not meaningfully articulate any challenge to Atwood’s breach of contract claim (as opposed to Lyran’s claim), and Atwood’s breach of contract claim appears to be the “meat” of this entire action. 

 

Defendants do not argue that there was no contract between them and Atwood, that Atwood failed to perform on the contract, or that they paid Atwood for her performance on the contract.  They fail, therefore, to show a reasonable possibility of success on the merits of the lawsuit, and therefore the motion is denied.

 

  1. Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained in part and overruled in part as set forth fully above.  Plaintiffs must file a Second Amended Complaint, in compliance with this ruling, within twenty days.  Defendants must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter. 

 

Defendants’ motion to require Plaintiffs to post bond is denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV02392    Hearing Date: March 26, 2024    Dept: S27

 

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Aireanna M. Chapman, as a designated Private Attorney General, filed this wage and hour action against Defendant, Marlora Investments LLC.  Plaintiff filed her action on 12/11/23, and Defendant has not filed an answer to the complaint. 

 

2.     Motions for Approval of PAGA Settlement

a.     Settlement

Plaintiff and Defendant participated in an arms’ length mediation, and they agreed to a settlement of the PAGA claims at the conclusion of the mediation.  The settlement affects 246 aggrieved employees and covers the employment period between 10/04/22 and 1/26/24.  The settlement requires Defendant to pay $93,500, which would be paid out as follows:

$31,166.67 attorneys’ fees

$9688.30 litigation expenses

$5500 administration expenses

$5000 representative incentive payment to Plaintiff

$42,156.03 net settlement remaining, of which 75% ($31,608.26) will go to LWDA and 25% ($10,536.26) will be paid to aggrieved employees

 

b.     Analysis

i.              Does a presumption of fairness exist? 

Was the settlement reached through arm's-length bargaining?  Yes. The parties participated in a full-day mediation with Mark LeHocky, Esq., and ultimately agreed to settle at the conclusion of the session on 12/27/23.  Young Declaration, ¶3.

Were investigation and discovery sufficient to allow counsel and the court to act intelligently?  Yes. Young provides a detailed description of all discovery conducted at ¶12 of his declaration in support of the motion.   

Is counsel experienced in similar litigation?  Yes.  Attorney Mahoney details the firm’s experience with PAGA litigation at ¶3.

What percentage of the class has objected?  n/a

 

            CONCLUSION:  The settlement is entitled to a presumption of fairness.

 

b.         Is the settlement fair, adequate, and reasonable?

Strength of Plaintiffs' case.  "The most important factor is the strength of the case for plaintiffs on the merits, balanced against the amount offered in settlement."  (Kullar v. Foot Locker Retail, Inc. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 116, 130.) Plaintiff's Counsel estimated damages in this action and concluded Defendant’s exposure is $363,750; however, Counsel also notes that this includes substantial penalties, and employers often take the position that such penalties are not available under the circumstances.  See Young Declaration, ¶¶19-20. 

Risk, expense, complexity and likely duration of further litigation.  Given the nature of the claim, the case is likely to be expensive and lengthy to try.  Procedural hurdles (e.g., motion practice and appeals) are also likely to prolong the litigation as well as any recovery by the aggrieved employees.

3.         Risk of maintaining class action status through trial.  n/a

4.         Amount offered in settlement. The Settlement Amount of $93,500 represents only a small fraction of the potential exposure, but in light of the potential for a finding in Defendant’s favor in connection with the penalty issues, the settlement is in the "ballpark" of reasonableness. After administrator, attorney, and enhancement payments are made, the remaining settlement will be in the amount of $42,156.03 will remain.  If 25% of the remaining settlement ($10,536.26) will be available for distribution to all aggrieved employees, then payments to the 246 Aggrieved Employees will average $42.83.  

5.         Extent of discovery completed and stage of the proceedings.  As indicated above, at the time of the settlement, Plaintiff's Counsel had conducted sufficient discovery.

6.         Experience and views of counsel.  The settlement was negotiated and endorsed by Plaintiff's Counsel who, as indicated above, is experienced in PAGA litigation.

 

            CONCLUSION:  The settlement can be deemed "fair, adequate, and reasonable."

 

c.         Scope of the release

Upon the approval by the Court of this Settlement, each PAGA Member (including Plaintiff), shall fully release and forever discharge Defendant, and its present and former parent companies, subsidiaries, divisions, related or affiliated companies and joint ventures, shareholders, officers, directors, employees, agents, attorneys, insurers, reinsurers, successors and assigns, and any individual or entity which could be jointly liable with Defendant ("Releasees") from the PAGA Released Claims, which include all claims known or unknown under PAGA.  ¶1.27 of settlement agreement.  The “PAGA Released Claims” are defined as all claims under PAGA that area pled in Plaintiff’s complaint or that could have been pled under PAGA based on the factual allegations in the complaint and that arose during the covered period.  ¶5 of settlement agreement.

Plaintiff will also provide a general release and CC §1542 waiver as to Defendant, as well as a release of any discrimination-based claims. ¶5.1.1 of settlement agreement.

 

 d.        Is the notice proper?

PAGA has no notice requirements for unnamed class members, nor may such employees opt out of a PAGA actions.  See Hernandez v. DMSI Staffing, LLC. (N.D. Cal. 2015) 79 F.Supp.3d 1054, 1063, aff'd sub nom. Hernandez v. DMSI Staffing, LLC (9th Cir. 2017) 677 Fed. Appx. 359.  See also Moniz v. Adecco USA, Inc. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 56. 

Within 15 calendar days after entry of judgment on this settlement, Defendant will compile a list of aggrieved employees and provide it to the Claims Administrator. ¶4.2 of settlement agreement.  Defendant must fully fund the settlement within 180 days of entry of judgment on this settlement.  ¶4.3. Within 10 calendar days of receipt of the final settlement check from Defendant, the Claim Administrator shall send notice and check to each PAGA member. ¶4.4.

 

e.         Attorney fees and costs

An aggrieved employee who prevails in a PAGA action may recover reasonable attorney's fees and costs.  Labor Code §2699(g)(1).  Whether a plaintiff established entitlement to an award of fees under PAGA is a question best decided by the trial court. San Diego Municipal Employees Association v. City of San Diego (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 906.

Counsel requests $31,166.67 in litigation fees (33% to the gross settlement).  Per Counsel’s declaration, this amount is fair and reasonable. 

Counsel requests reimbursement for costs incurred during the litigation in the sum of $9,688.30, which is also fair and reasonable. 

 

The Court is inclined to approve fees in the amount of $31,166.67 and costs in the amount of $9,688.30.

 

f.          Representative Enhancement Award

The Settlement Agreement provides for $5,000.00 for a representative payment. Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s attorney both declare this incentive is based on Plaintiff’s willing participation in every stage of the litigation.  The Court also notes that Plaintiff has agreed to a broad waiver of all future claims.  The Court finds the incentive award reasonable.   

 

g.         Claims Administration Costs

The Settlement Agreement provides for $5500.00 in Administration Costs to Phoenix.  This is fair and reasonable and in line with the normal amount sought by administrators in these cases.

 

 h.        Conclusion

The Court finds the settlement and all requested fees and costs to be reasonable, and the Court will grant the motion to approve the settlement.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
