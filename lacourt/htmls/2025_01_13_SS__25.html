ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On November 29, 2021, Plaintiff Nicollee Inguagiato (“Plaintiff”), proceeding pro se, filed this action against Defendants Juan Feldman and Ignacio Feldman alleging Breach of Contract and Negligence causes of action. (Compl., ¶¶ 1, 8.)

 

On August 31, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant Juan Feldman (“Defendant”), alleging Breach of Contract and Common Counts causes of action. (FAC, ¶¶ 1, 8.)

III.              Legal Standard

 

Discovery Requests

 

A party must respond to requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., §2031.300, subd. (a).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 94 limits discovery in limited jurisdiction actions to any combination of 35 interrogatories with no subparts, demands to produce documents, and requests for admission with no subparts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 94, subd. (a).)

 

            Discovery Responses

 

Parties responding to requests for production “shall respond separately to each item or category of item by any of the following: (1) [a] statement that the party will comply with the particular demand … [;] (2) [a] representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand … [; or] (3) [a]n objection to the particular demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210, subd. (a).)

 

Motions to Compel Further Responses

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a), provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to a demand for inspection … the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) [a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] (2) [a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] (3) [a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.” 

 

            Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

 

The motions must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)  

 

            Finally, California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3)).

 

IV.              Discussion

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that while Plaintiff moved to compel after 45 days of service of the responses, Defendant’s responses to the Requests for Production are not verified. Thus, Plaintiff, the propounding party, did not waive the right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

 

Additionally, the Court notes that, to date, Plaintiff has submitted evidence of just six discovery requests propounded on Defendant – namely, the six requests in the Requests for Documents. Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff is in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 94.

 

Accordingly, the Court addresses the merits of the Motions.

 

In California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541. “Under the discovery statutes, information is discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186. “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” Ibid. “When discovery requests are grossly overbroad on their face, and hence do not appear reasonably related to a legitimate discovery need, a reasonable inference can be drawn of an intent to harass and improperly burden.” Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.

On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection to the response is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(a)(1)-(3).) Where a party seeks to compel further responses to requests for production of documents, a showing of good cause must be established before production may be compelled. Williams v. Superior Court, supra, 3 Cal.5th 531, 550.

To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact. Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224. The specific facts must be set forth in a declaration. Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224. “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” Ibid. 

A.    Meet and Confer

The one request at issue here is Request No. 3, which seeks all documents that evidence all rental income that Defendant received for the rental properties each month from June 2018 through November 2020. (Motion, Sep. Statement.)

 

In response to Request No. 3, Defendant stated: “Objection: This request seeks private financial information and thus violates Article I, Section 1 of the California Constitution.” (Motion, Sep. Statement; Counts Decl., Exh. B, p. 2.)

 

            Defendant argues that the Motion was filed untimely after the applicable motion cut-off deadline; however, the Court addressed this issue at the Ex Parte hearing on December 18, 2024.

 

            The Court finds that Plaintiff has shown good cause. Plaintiff’s discovery request is indeed pertinent to the cause as it seeks documents evidencing, not just damages needed to calculate the unpaid property management fees owed, but also to prove the breach of damages needed to calculate the unpaid property management fees owed, as well a to prove the breach of contract claim itself by showing Defendant failed to make payments to Plaintiff as agreed. The Court agrees that the information is integral to Plaintiff’s ability to substantiate her claims in the matter and meet her burden of proof. The Court further notes that the Request for Production of Documents, lists in the definitions “RENTAL PROPERTIES” to include real estate located at 1130 Rose Avenue, Venice, CA 90291; 4268 Jackson Avenue, #A&B) Culver City, CA 90232; and 12711 Mitchell Avenue, #1, Los Angeles, CA 90066.) This description matches to the properties listed in the operative Complaint for breach of contract.  

 

            Thus, Plaintiff has presented specific facts as to why further responses should be compelled and why the documents requested will tend to prove or disprove a fact of consequence in this action or will lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove such fact. In sum, Plaintiff has established good cause.

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant to provide a response to Request for Production request No. 3 is GRANTED. Defendant is to provide a response with said documents by January 20, 2025.

III.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief.  (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure § 473, subdivision (b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) 

 

The general underlying purpose of Section 473, subdivision (b) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits.  (Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838-839.)  “Apart from any statute, courts have the inherent authority to vacate a default and default judgment on equitable grounds such as extrinsic fraud or extrinsic mistake.” (Bae v. T.D. Service Co. of Arizona (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 89, 97.) A motion seeking such equitable relief “‘may be brought on such ground even though the statutory period [for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b)] has run.’ [Citation.]”  (Ibid.) 

 

Notably, in limited civil cases, the grounds for equitable relief are extended to include “inadvertence or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 86, subd. (b).) ¿

 

To qualify for equitable relief from a default judgment on the basis of extrinsic mistake or fraud, one must satisfy three elements: “ ‘First, the defaulted party must demonstrate that it has a meritorious case. Second, the party seeking to set aside the default must articulate a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense to the original action. Last [], the moving party must demonstrate diligence in seeking to set aside the default once … discovered.’ ” (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 982 (Rappleyea).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 provides:  

 

“(a)¿When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i)¿two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii)¿180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. 

 

(b)¿A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party’s lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action. 

 

(c)¿Upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.”  

 

Here, the Motion states that the default and default judgment should be vacated/set aside because Defendant was never served with the summons and complaint. (Motion, p. 2.) Further, Defendant states the judgment was made without her knowledge and that she “received notice by Department of Motor Vehicle [(“DMV”)] that the plaintiff had requested license suspension.” (Motion, p. 1.) After receiving the documents from the DMV, Defendant states she “read the complaint and believed that [she] had a good and substantial defense to the action, and continue to believe so, as shown by [her] proposed answer to the complaint.” (Motion, p. 2.)

 

However, Defendant fails to present “a concise statement of the law, evidence and arguments relied on, and a discussion of the statutes, cases, and textbooks cited in support of the position advanced.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rules 3.1113.) Defendant’s Motion does not include any legal authority or clear discussion of the authority supporting the stated positions. Defendant does not cite any case law or statutory authority that authorizes this Court to set aside the default or default judgment.

 

More importantly, in review of the Proof of Service on file, it appears that Tameka Wells was served personally at 12951 Benson Ave. #133, Chino, CA 91710, on June 16, 2022 at 8:31 a.m., which Tameka Wells does not dispute in her Motion to be her address. 

 

Further, Defendant states she attached a proposed answer, but no such document is included with the Motion.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

III.              Legal Standard

 

“Regardless of the particular relief granted, any arbitrator's award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.” (O'Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants¿(2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.) “Once a petition to confirm an award is filed, the superior court must select one of only four courses of action: it may confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.” (EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc.¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.) “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.” (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.)

IV.              Discussion

 

A.    Subject Matter Jurisdiction

 

Opposition

 

In Opposition to the Second Amended Petition, Respondent argues the Arbitrator lacked subject matter jurisdiction because the parties did not engage in mediation prior to arbitration, which directly contravenes the Contract’s “unambiguous language demonstrate[ing] the parties’ clear intention to limit arbitration to only those disputes for which a mediation had at least been attempted.” (Opp., p. 5.) In support of this argument, Respondent predominantly relies on National Union Fire Ins. Co. v. Stites Prof. Law. Corp. (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1718 and United Firefighters of Los Angeles v. City of Los Angeles (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1576. (Opp., pp. 5-6.) Further, Respondent contends Respondent was not required to raise an objection based on subject matter jurisdiction at the arbitration or at the trial court. (Opp., p. 6.)

 

To the extent there is an argument that the Contract’s terms are ambiguous regarding unsuccessful mediation, Respondent refutes such interpretation and argues the Contract’s terms expressly state the mediation shall be conducted prior to arbitration, unless the parties mutually agree otherwise. (Opp., pp. 6-7.) Respondent argues any contrary interpretation would “reduce the mediation requirement, and all conceivable mediation requirements, to a nullity.” (Opp., pp. 7-8.) However, if the Contract is deemed ambiguous, Respondent argues the language of the Contract should be construed against Petitioner, who caused the uncertainty to exist as the Contract is a form contract typically used by Petitioner. (Opp., pp. 7-9.)

 

Respondent also attaches a copy of an email from the AAA confirming receipt of an email from Petitioner, advising she is withdrawing the request for mediation and would like to proceed with arbitration, and an email from Respondent, advising they would like to proceed with mediation with Marcia Kamine and providing scheduling considerations. (Opp., Exh. 1.) In reply to the email, the AAA states: “In light of the disagreement regarding mediation, the arbitration will proceed forward.” (Opp., Exh. 1.)

 

Reply

 

In Reply, Petitioner argues Respondents fail to meet their burden in attacking the Arbitration Award. (Reply, pp. 3-4.) Petitioner states Respondents’ opposition fails to address the requirements stated in Code of Civil Procedure section 1286.2 – namely, error and prejudice. (Reply, pp. 3-4.) Further, Petitioner argues the question of arbitrability is a question for the arbitrator, and Respondents should have raised their concerns regarding mediation at the arbitration proceeding. (Reply, pp. 4-5.)

 

Additionally, Petitioner argues Respondents waived any objection to the arbitrator’s authority by proceeding with the arbitration on the merits and failing to raise the issue of arbitrability with the Arbitrator. (Reply, pp. 5-6.) Petitioner also distinguishes National Union Fire Ins. Co., supra, 235 Cal.App.3d 1718 from the instant matter. (Reply, p. 6.)

 

Finally, Petitioner states Respondents’ Opposition is untimely as a “response requesting that an arbitration award be vacated or corrected must be filed no later than 100 days after the service of the signed copy of the award.” (Reply, p. 6, citing Code Civ. Proc. § 1288.2.) Since Petitioner filed the Original Petition on September 6, 2023 and Respondents did not file a demurrer until September 3, 2024, Petitioner argues Respondents’ Opposition to the Arbitration Award is untimely.

 

Analysis

           

            Subject to Code of Civil Procedure section 1286.4, section 1286.2 states the court shall vacate the award if the court determines “[t]he arbitrators exceeded their powers and the award cannot be corrected without affecting the merits of the decision upon the controversy submitted.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.2, subd. (a)(4).)

 

“A response requesting that an award be vacated or that an award be corrected shall be served and filed not later than 100 days after the date of service of a signed copy of the award upon: (a) The respondent if he was a party to the arbitration; or (b) The respondent's representative if the respondent was not a party to the arbitration.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1288.2.)

 

Here, the Final Arbitration Award, dated August 16, 2023 and attached to Petitioner’s Original Petition as Exhibit 1 and Amended Petition and Second Amended Petition as Exhibit B, does not include a proof of service or other document demonstrating the neutral arbitrator served a copy of the Final Arbitration Award on the parties. (Second Amended Petn., Exh. B.) In addition, the Arbitration Agreement does not appear to specify terms for service of the Award different from those set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6. However, the Court notes Petitioner’s Original Petition filed on September 6, 2023 includes a copy of an email dated August 28, 2023 from Petitioner to Respondents which purports to attach the August 16, 2023 Arbitration Award Letter. (Original Petn., Exh. 2.) While the precise date of service of the Final Arbitration Award on the parties by the arbitrator is uncertain, it appears the parties received a copy of the Final Arbitration Award sometime between August 16, 2023 and August 28, 2023.

 

Respondent does not raise issues regarding receipt of the Final Arbitration Award.

 

Given then that the parties received the Final Arbitration Award sometime between August 16, 2023 and August 28, 2023, Respondents needed to have served a request to vacate the award 100 days after, at the latest, August 28, 2023. The time for Respondents to have served a request to vacate the arbitration award had expired by the time Respondents’ initial response was filed and served.

 

Therefore, Respondents’ request to vacate the arbitration award would typically be denied on procedural grounds. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1288.2; see also Law Finance Group, LLC v. Key (2023) 14 Cal.5th 932, 959 [“in a postarbitration judicial proceeding to enforce an arbitral award, a challenger may forfeit the opportunity to raise a claim of contract illegality by failing to timely request that the arbitration award be corrected or vacated”].)

 

However, the Court may address Respondents’ claim on the merits since Respondents’ challenge is in regard to subject matter jurisdiction, which will be discussed in further detail below.

 

First, the Court finds Respondents may raise an untimely subject matter jurisdiction challenge to the arbitrators’ authority for the first time in the trial court. (See National Union Fire Ins. Co., supra, 235 Cal.App.3d). In National Union, the Second District Court of Appeal held that (1) appellant, who refused to participate in arbitration proceedings and failed to initiate a challenge to the arbitration award pursuant to the procedure found in Code of Civil Procedure section 1280, et seq., could raise the arbitrators’ purported lack of subject matter jurisdiction for the first time in the trial court, and (2) the dispute between the parties was not subject to arbitration pursuant to the pertinent statutory code. (National Union Fire Ins. Co., supra, 235 Cal.App.3d at pp. 1722, 1723, fn. 2, 1728-1729.) As a result, the Court of Appeals, in pertinent part, affirmed the denial of a petition to confirm arbitration award. (Id., at pp. 1728-1729.) Finding the instant circumstances substantially similar to those of the appellant in National Union as they pertain to the timing of the subject matter jurisdiction challenge and in light of the rule that “[l]ack of subject matter jurisdiction can be raised at any time, even for the first time on appeal,” the Court thus finds Respondents’ challenge to subject matter jurisdiction need not have been raised first during the arbitration proceedings. (Id., at p. 1724.) Thus, Respondents’ challenge to the arbitrator’s authority is appropriately before the Court.

 

            Next, the Court finds the parties’ claims were subject to arbitration pursuant to the Arbitration Agreement, so long as procedural requirements in the Agreement were properly followed. (Id., at p. 1725 [“Code of Civil Procedure section 1286.2, subdivision (d), allows a trial court to vacate an arbitrator's implied or expressed determination that she or he has the jurisdiction to determine matters when they are outside the scope of an arbitration agreement; see Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.2.) Here, Petitioner sets forth in the Petition, and files a copy of, the “Standard Form Agreement Between Owner and Architect for a Custom Residential Project” (the “Contract”) executed between the parties, which contains an arbitration provision governing disputes between the parties arising out of the Contract (“Arbitration Agreement”). (Second Amended Pet., ¶ 4, Exh. A, p. 17.) Since it is undisputed the issues between the parties arose out of the Contract, the Court thus finds the dispute between the parties was subject to arbitration pursuant to the Contract and Arbitration Agreement.

 

While the nature of the dispute was generally subject to arbitration, the Arbitration Agreement’s terms contained procedural requirements that were required to be satisfied before the parties proceeded with arbitration – namely, the parties were required to attempt mediation first. While Petitioner contends procedural requirements such as condition precedents are questions of arbitrability subject to the arbitrator’s assessment (Reply, pp. 2, 4-5), Petitioner presents no binding authority in California to that effect. Thus, for this Court to address the full nature of the mediation requirement, the Court must turn to principles of contract interpretation.

 

Here, the parties’ Contract states:

 

§ 8.2 Mediation § 8.2.1 Any claim, dispute or other matter in question arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be subject to mediation as a condition precedent to binding dispute resolution. If such matter relates to or is the subject of a lien arising out of the Architect's services, the Architect may proceed in accordance with applicable law to comply with the lien notice or filing deadlines prior to resolution of the matter by mediation or by binding dispute resolution.

 

§ 8.2.2 Mediation, unless the parties mutually agree otherwise, shall be administered by the American Arbitration Association in accordance with its Construction Industry Mediation Procedures in effect on the date of this Agreement. The parties shall share the mediator’s fee and any filing fees equally. The mediation shall be held in the place where the Project is located, unless another location is mutually agreed upon. Agreements reached in mediation shall be enforceable as settlement agreements in any court having jurisdiction thereof.

 

§ 8.2.3 If the parties do not resolve a dispute through mediation pursuant to this Section 8.2, the method of binding dispute resolution shall be the following: Arbitration pursuant to Section 8.3 of this Agreement.

 

(Second Amended Pet., Exh. A, p. 16.)

 

In Opposition, Respondents also submitted evidence of a letter from the AAA highlighting that since the parties could not come to an agreement about mediation – namely, Petitioner withdrew a request for mediation and Respondent stated a desire to proceed with mediation – the matter would proceed to arbitration. (Opp., Exh. 1.)

 

The Court finds the plain meaning of the Contract’s terms require mediation to be held as a condition precedent to arbitration unless the parties mutually agree otherwise. (Second Amended Pet., Exh. A, p. 16, §§ 8.2, 8.2.2; Code Civ. Proc., § 1638 [“The language of a contract is to govern its interpretation, if the language is clear and explicit, and does not involve an absurdity”]; see also Lange v. Schilling (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1412.) Per the terms of the Contract, only if the parties both agree to forgo mediation or a mediation attempted pursuant to section 8.2 is unsuccessful, then the parties may proceed to arbitration. (Second Amended Pet., Exh. A, p. 16, § 8.2.3.) The Court finds these terms to be reasonable and not absurd. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1638.)

 

The Court acknowledges the ruling in Howsam v. Dean Witter Reynolds, Inc. (2002) 537 U.S. 79, 84 [“ ‘procedural questions which grow out of the dispute and bear on its final disposition’ ” are for an arbitrator, not the judge, to decide” and “an ‘arbitrator shall decide whether a condition precedent to arbitrability has been fulfilled.’ ”].) However, there is no indication that the arbitrator addressed this procedural issue.

 

The letter from the AAA submitted by Respondents, and notably not disputed by Petitioner, demonstrates the parties were not in mutual agreement to forgo mediation prior to arbitration. Petitioner presents no evidence demonstrating otherwise. Further, there is no evidence presented by either party that the parties attempted mediation at any time prior to arbitration. Thus, the Court finds the evidence presented demonstrates that the parties did not mutually agree to forgo mediation or were unsuccessful at mediation prior to proceeding with arbitration as directed by the Contract’s terms. As a result, the Arbitrator lacked subject matter jurisdiction over the matter.

 

To the extent Petitioner argues Respondents have not demonstrated they suffered prejudice, the Court disagrees. Respondents state they suffered the loss of the benefits of mediation due to the Arbitrator’s error – “Respondents, as unrepresented lay persons involved in a contract dispute with a licensed professional and the architect of that contract, maintained their request for mediation throughout this process because they believed it would provide the benefits recognized by Lange and California courts generally.” (Opp., pp. 9-10; Lange, supra, 163 Cal.App.4th at p. 1417.)

 

For the foregoing reasons, Petitioner Alison Kendall’s Second Amended Petition to Confirm Contractual Arbitration Award is DENIED, and the Arbitration Award is VACATED.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Petitioner Alison Kendall’s Second Amended Petition to Confirm Contractual Arbitration Award is DENIED, and the Arbitration Award is VACATED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

II.                 Legal Standard

 

After a mechanic’s lien has been recorded, “[t]he owner of property or the owner of any interest in property subject to a claim of lien may petition the court for an order to release the property from the claim of lien if the claimant has not commenced an action to enforce the lien within the time provided in Section 8460.”  (Civ. Code, § 8480, subd. (a).)  A claimant must commence an action to enforce a lien within 90 days of recording the lien. (Civ. Code, § 8460, subd. (a).)  Section 8460 further provides that “[i]f the claimant does not commence an action to enforce the lien within that time, the claim of lien expires and is unenforceable.” (Civ. Code, § 8460, subd. (a).) Section 8460 also provides that the 90-day time limit to commence an action to enforce a lien does not apply if there was an agreement to extend credit and a notice of that fact was recorded within 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien or more than 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien but before a purchaser or encumbrancer for value and in good faith acquires rights in the property.  (Civ. Code, § 8460, subd. (b).) 

 

III.              Discussion

 

A.    Service Requirements

Code, § 8486, subd. (b).) Section 8108 provides addresses at which a respondent must be served, depending on the title of the person to be served. The petitioner bears the burden of proving he complied with service and date for hearing requirements. (Civ. Code, § 8488, subd. (a).)

(g) Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.

(h) Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien.

A property owner “may not petition the court for a release order … unless at least 10 days before filing the petition the owner gives the claimant notice demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the claim of lien.” (Civ. Code, § 8482.)

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff Timeless Construction Group Inc. (Compl., ¶ 8.)

 

On August 9, 2024, Defendant/Cross-Complainant filed an Answer and Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant, alleging causes of action for Breach of Contract and Fraud.

 

On October 28, 2024, the Court overruled in part and sustained in part Plaintiff’s Demurrer to Defendant’s Cross-Complaint. (10/28/24 Order.) Specifically, the Court overruled the demurrer as to the breach of contract cause of action but sustained the demurrer as to the fraud cause of action. Further, the Court granted in part and found as moot in part Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike. (10/28/24 Order.) Specifically, the Court granted the Motion to Strike attorneys’ fees and found the Motion to Strike punitive damages was moot in light of the ruling on the Demurrer.

 

On August 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Cross Complaint (“FACC”).

 

On November 7, 2024, Defendant filed a Second Amended Cross Complaint (“SACC”).

 

On December 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Demurrer to Defendant’s Second Amended Cross Complaint (the “Demurrer”) and Motion to Strike (the “Motion”).

 

On December 30, 2024, Defendant filed an Opposition to the Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

 

No Reply has been filed.

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Defendant was granted leave by the Court to amend the Cross Complaint; however, Defendant appears to have amended the Cross Complaint twice, without leave of court for the second amendment. However, the Court exercises its discretion to treat the SACC as the operative amended cross complaint given Plaintiff’s lack of opposition to the filing of the SACC and the filing of the Demurrer to the SACC.  

 

Additionally, the Court notes Defendant’s Opposition is untimely. The Opposition was filed one day after the statutory deadline. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005.)

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

“As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

IV.              Discussion

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).) 

The Demurrer is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a). (Demurrer, Euredjian Decl.) According to the declaration, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel “to setup a time to further meet and confer.” (Euredjian Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel never received a response. (Euredjian Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

The Court does not find that a single outreach email satisfies the meet and confer requirement. While the Court may not overrule a demurrer for failure to adequately meet and confer (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4)), the Court cautions the parties to abide by their statutory obligations in the future.

 

B.     Demurrer

 

Moving Party Position

 

Plaintiff demurs to the second cause of action for Fraud on the grounds it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Plaintiff. (Demurrer, p. 2.) While the SACC states Plaintiff made “representations that ‘were false and made with the intent to induce [Defendant] to rely on them” (Demurrer, p. 2, citing SACC, ¶ 18), Plaintiff contends the SACC fails to state how Defendant determined Plaintiff’s representations were false and made with the intent to induce her to rely on them. (Demurrer, p. 2.)

 

Additionally, Plaintiff contends the Demurrer should be sustained without leave to amend because Defendant “has already amended the cross-complaint twice, and there is no reasonable possibility of curing the defects in the SACC by yet another amendment.” (Demurrer, pp. 2-3.)

 

            Opposition

 

            In Opposition, Defendant argues the SACC contains sufficient facts to constitute a Breach of Contract cause of action. (Opp., pp. 3-6.)

 

            Next, Defendant addresses the SACC’s cause of action for Fraud. Defendant describes Plaintiff’s argument as an argument that “no duty to disclose material facts existed in this case.” (Opp., p. 6.) However, Defendant contends Plaintiff had a duty to disclose facts concerning the reasonable cost of replacing Defendant’s roof and the competency of its labor force, as that information was not readily accessible to Defendant and was material to Defendant’s decision to hire Plaintiff for the roofing project. (Opp., p. 6.)

 

            Defendant states the SACC alleges Plaintiff “intentionally concealed material facts regarding the reasonable costs of the roofing project and the competency of the labor force.” (Opp., p. 7.) Defendant further explains the concealment occurred by way of concealment of reasonable costs and concealment of labor competency, and Defendant relied on Plaintiff’s representations and was not in a position to independently verify the accuracy of Plaintiff’s “cost estimates or evaluate the skill level of the labor force. [Plaintiff], as a licensed contractor, was in a position of trust and was expected to provide accurate information.” (Opp., pp. 7-8.) Defendant then states the damages suffered due to Plaintiff’s concealment. (Opp., p. 8.)

 

            In the event the Court finds deficiencies in the SACC, Defendant requests leave to amend to provide “additional factual details regarding Contractor’s breach of contract.” (Opp., pp. 8-9.)

 

            Allegations in the Second Amended Cross-Complaint

           

            The SACC alleges Plaintiff is a corporation who Defendant believes holds a valid contractor’s license issued by California, and Defendant is an individual. (SACC, ¶¶ 2-4.)  The parties entered into a written contract (the “Contract”) on or around November 1, 2023, relating to the installation of a roof on Defendant’s property located at 121 West 47th Street, Los Angeles, California 90037 (the “Property”). (SACC, ¶¶ 1, 8.)

 

            Under the Contract, Plaintiff was to “furnish certain labor, services, equipment, and materials so as to replace the roof” of Defendant’s Property. (SACC, ¶ 8, Exh. 1.) The Contract “provided for the payment of $34,850 which exceeded the reasonable value of the services by no less than $20,000.” (SACC, ¶ 8, Exh. 1.)

           

            However, Plaintiff failed to “deliver services, materials, labor, and equipment in conformance with the terms of the Contract and in fact, assigned inebriated employees to perform the required services.” (SACC, ¶ 9.) Plaintiff commenced its alleged performance of the Contract in November 2023 but has failed and refused and continues to fail and refuse to perform its obligations under the Contract by providing defective materials and incompetent labor. (SACC, ¶¶ 10, 12.) Defendant has fully performed all required of her under the Contract except “those excused by the failure to perform the contract competently and the fraud” of Plaintiff in misrepresenting the value of their services. (SACC, ¶ 11.)

 

            Defendant alleges Plaintiff “intentionally misrepresented” to Defendant that the work would be performed in a workmanlike manner; Plaintiff would use suitable, high-quality roofing materials; Defendant’s premises would be “left in broom clean condition”; and the roof would be “free from leaks for 50 years.” (SACC, ¶ 17.) Defendant states these “representations were false and made with the intent to induce [Defendant] to rely upon them.” (SACC, ¶ 18.) Defendant “reasonably relied on these misrepresentations to her detriment” and has suffered damages as a result. (SACC, ¶¶ 19-20.)

 

            Analysis

 

            As an initial matter, the Court notes Plaintiff only demurs to the second cause of action in the SACC and not the first cause of action. Thus, the Court will only address the second cause of action.

 

                        Second Cause of Action – Fraud

 

The elements of fraud are: “ ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184, citing Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)  A cause of action for fraud must be “ ‘pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. … “ ‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which “ ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’ ” ’ ” (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184, citing Lazar, supra, 12 Cal.4th at p. 645.)

 

To properly allege fraud against a corporation, a moving party must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, “their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

Here, Defendant alleges Plaintiff misrepresented that the work would be performed in a workmanlike manner; Plaintiff would use suitable, high-quality roofing materials; Defendant’s premises would be “left in broom clean condition”; and the roof would be “free from leaks for 50 years.” (SACC, ¶ 17.) However, this description, similar to that already addressed by this Court in regard to the FACC, is not pled with specificity – Defendant must allege what Plaintiff’s agents said specifically and what authority they had to speak. (See Tarmann, supra, 2 Cal.App.4th at p.157.)

            Further, Defendant again fails to plead any facts demonstrating Plaintiff’s knowledge of falsity or Plaintiff’s intent to defraud or induce reliance. (Charnay, supra, 145 Cal.App.4th at p. 184.)

Finally, Defendant’s allegations that she “reasonably relied” on Plaintiff’s misrepresentations and suffered damages as a result of that reliance (SACC, ¶¶ 19-20) are too general to suffice for a fraud cause of action. (Small, supra, 30 Cal.4th at p. 184 [A cause of action for fraud must be “ ‘pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. … “ ‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which “ ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered’ ” ’ ”].)

            Accordingly, the Demurrer is sustained.

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman, supra, 18 Cal.3d at p. 348.)

Thus, the Court grants leave to amend one last time.

For the foregoing reasons, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the SACC’s second cause of action.

C.     Motion to Strike

 

Moving Party Position

 

Plaintiff moves to strike Defendant’s request for punitive damages on page 5, line 5 of the SACC (Notice of Motion, p. 1.; Motion, p. 1) because Defendant “fails to allege ultimate facts from which it can be reasonably inferred that [Plaintiff] acted with the requisite malice, oppression, or fraud against [Defendant] within the meaning of Civil Code section 3294.” (Motion, p. 3.)

 

                        Opposition

 

            In Opposition, Defendant contends the request for punitive damages is appropriate given Defendant’s fraud cause of action in the SACC. (Opp., pp. 1, 3.)

 

                        Analysis

 

California law authorizes a party’s motion to strike matter from an opposing party’s pleading if it is irrelevant, false, or improper. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436.) Motions may also target pleadings or parts of pleadings that are not filed or drawn in conformity with applicable laws, rules, or orders. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (b).)

 

However, motions to strike in limited jurisdiction courts may only challenge pleadings on the basis that “the damages or relief sought are not supported by the allegations of the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 92, subd. (d).) The Code of Civil Procedure also authorizes the Court to act on its own initiative to strike matters, empowering the Court to enter orders striking matter “at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.)

 

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 requires that “[b]efore filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).) (Emphasis added.)

 

Punitive damages are authorized by Civil Code section 3294 in non-contract cases “where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied . . . .”  (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

 

As an initial matter, the Court finds Plaintiff has not satisfied the meet and confer requirement of Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5. The Motion is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, but the meet and confer efforts here – namely, a single outreach email – do not meet the statutory requirements of meeting and conferring “in person or by telephone.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).)

 

The Court finds that the SACC fails to allege facts sufficient to support a punitive damages award. The Demurrer to the SACC’s fraud cause of action is sustained, and thus, there are no other facts by which it can be inferred that Plaintiff acted with malice, fraud, or oppression.

 

Since there are no alleged facts that punitive damages may be based upon, the Motion is MOOT.

 

D.    Conclusion & Order

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Timeless Construction Group Inc.’s Demurrer as to the second cause of action (Fraud) of the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s Motion to Strike Portions of Second Amended Cross-Complaint is MOOT.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
