DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV30187    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

December 16, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV30187

MOTIONS

Motion to Strike Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Marcos Soto Mendoza

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Otoniel Montano

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Marcos Soto Mendoza (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant Otoniel Montano (“Defendant”) for habitability violations.  Defendant cross-complained against Plaintiff, and Plaintiff moves to strike the cross-complaint.  Defendant opposes the motion.    

ANALYSIS

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50, a party may file a compulsory cross-complaint with the answer or with leave of Court in the interests of justice at any time during the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50.)  Defendant answered Plaintiff’s operative complaint on September 10, 2024 but filed the cross-complaint on September 11, 2024.  Defendant, therefore, should have sought leave of Court to file the cross-complaint. 

Therefore, the motion to strike is granted without prejudice to Defendant’s right to file a motion for leave to cross-complain against Plaintiff. 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 24STCV16279    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

December 16, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV16279

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff 594 Mapleton LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Mirga Phipps White

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff 594 Mapleton LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this action to quiet title on real property.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants Nicholas Phipps White, Mirga Phipps White, and their company, the Hollywood Land Development Company, LLC (“Defendants”) have wrongfully claimed title to 594 South Mapleton Drive in Los Angeles, which Plaintiff owns. 

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint.  However, Plaintiff has not “repeated, verbatim, in the separate statement of undisputed material facts” each issue on which Plaintiff seeks summary adjudication and identified “each supporting material fact claimed to be without dispute with respect to” each issue.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1350, subds. (b), (d).)  Accordingly, the Court denies the motion for summary adjudication as procedurally improper. “There is little flexibility in the procedural imperatives of [Code of Civil Procedure section 437c], and the issues raised by a motion for summary judgment (or summary adjudication) are pure questions of law.  As a result, section 437c is unforgiving; a failure to comply with any one of its myriad requirements is likely to be fatal to the offending party.”  (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1607.)  The Court considers the motion as a motion for summary judgment only. 

Defendants filed an opposition to the motion on December 12, 2024.[1]  The opposition was due 14 days before the date set for hearing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(2).  Although the Court acknowledges the late filing of the opposition have not provided Plaintiff with an opportunity to respond to it, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the late filed brief.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)            

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Quiet Title

            To prevail on a claim for quiet title, Plaintiff must show it has title to the property at issue.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.) 

            Plaintiff advances the declaration of Dennis Roach (“Roach”), an attorney who Plaintiff retained in connection with Plaintiff’s purchase of the property at issue.  Roach states Plaintiff purchased the property in July 2019 and has not sold the property or transferred its interest to it.  (Declaration of Dennis Roach, ¶¶ 3, 8.)

Plaintiff also advances the grant deed Plaintiff recorded on the property on July 2, 2019.  (Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibit A.)  Defendants filed a grant deed on June 6, 2024 “to confirm a change of name” of the owner of the property.  That deed reflects Plaintiff transferred title to Defendant Nicholas Phipps White.  (Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibit B.)   As Plaintiff advances evidence Plaintiff did not sell or otherwise transfer its interest in the property, Plaintiff’s evidence demonstrates the interest Defendants claim to the property is invalid.  Plaintiff’s evidence satisfies its burden of showing Plaintiff holds title to the property at issue and will prevail on its claim for quiet title.  Plaintiff, therefore, has shifted the burden to Defendants to raise triable issues of material fact as to the cause of action for quiet title. 

White’s opposition amounts to an attack on the pleadings Plaintiff has filed, including allegations Roach’s declaration is forged and Plaintiff’s “facts” are fake.  White has neither responded to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement nor submitted one of her own and a Declaration through which she has presented admissible evidence to counter that provided by Plaintiff.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(3) [“The opposition papers shall include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be disputed, indicating if the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed.  The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts the opposing party contends are disputed”]; see also Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163 [bare assertion moving party fabricated evidence insufficient to avoid grant of summary judgment].)

As she has not effectively opposed the motion, White has not satisfied her burden of establishing any triable issue of material fact concerning Plaintiff’s quiet title claim. 

Second Cause of Action: Cancellation of Instrument

To prevail on its cause of action for cancellation of instrument, Plaintiff must show the instrument at issue is void or voidable as against Plaintiff.  (Civ. Code, § 3412.)  As set forth above, Plaintiff’s evidence shows the grant deed Defendants filed is invalid.  Such evidence satisfies Plaintiff’s burden of showing Plaintiff will prevail on the claim for cancellation of instrument, because the deed Defendants filed is void.  Plaintiff, therefore, has shifted the burden to White to raise a triable issue of material fact as to the cause of action for cancellation of instrument. As discussed above, Defendants have not done so.

Third Cause of Action: Declaratory Relief

To prevail on the cause of action for declaratory relief, Plaintiff must show it has an interest in real property about which an actual controversy exists.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1060.)   As set forth above, Plaintiff advances evidence it did not sell or otherwise transfer its interest in the property and, therefore, Defendants’ claim to the property is invalid.  Plaintiff’s evidence meets its burden of showing it is entitled to a declaration Plaintiff holds title to the property at issue.  Having shifted the burden to Defendants to raise a triable issue as to this cause of action, White has failed to do so.

Fourth Cause of Action: Injunctive Relief

“To qualify for a permanent injunction, the plaintiff must prove (1) the elements of a cause of action involving the wrongful act sought to be enjoined . . . ; and (2) the grounds for equitable relief.”  San Diego Unif. Port Dist. v. Gallagher (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 501, 503.)  As Plaintiff advances evidence it did not sell or otherwise transfer its interest in the property, Defendants’ claim to the property is invalid.  Because real property is unique, Plaintiff’s evidence meets its burden of showing Plaintiff holds title to the property at issue and is entitled to enjoin Defendants’ claims to interests in the property.  Plaintiff has shifted the burden to White to raise a triable issue of material fact as to the cause of action for injunctive relief.  As White had essentially not opposed the motion, she cannot meet that burden. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

White fails to demonstrate triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Plaintiff is entitled to judgment in this matter.  Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file a proof of service of same.
