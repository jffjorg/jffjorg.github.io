DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02457    Hearing Date: October 26, 2023    Dept: W

AMIR EDWARDS v. ANTHONY HSIEH, et al.

 

DEFENDANTs’ demurrer to the complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        October 26, 2023                              Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV02457

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Anthony Hsieh and LoanDepot.com, LLC

Responding Party:     No opposition.

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Zuberi Decl.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff Amir Edwards filed a complaint against Defendants Anthony Hsieh and LoanDepot.com, LLC for Breach of Contract, Negligence, Fraud, and Common Counts. Plaintiff alleges Defendants concealed the fact that they used Plaintiff’s Promissory Notes to give to the seller and instead, kept “three times the amount of money required to purchase the property” to themselves. Plaintiff further alleges Defendants used Plaintiff’s Promissory Notes to clear title, which essentially purchased property from the seller. 

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Anthony Hsieh and LoanDepot.com, LLC’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request the court take judicial notice of a Deed of Trust recorded February 22, 2022, as Instrument Number 20220204890 in the Official Records of the County of Los Angeles Recorder’s Office (RJN, Exh. A). The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Misjoinder

 

Defendants first demur to the complaint on the grounds there has been a misjoinder of Defendant Hsieh.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(d) provides that a demurrer may be brought on grounds of misjoinder of parties. (See CCP § 430.10(d).) Code of Civil Procedure section 379 provides that all persons may be joined in one action as defendants if there is any right asserted against them “arising out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences and if any question of law or fact common to all these persons will arise in the action; or [a] claim, right, or interest adverse to them in the property or controversy which is the subject of the action.” (CCP § 379(a).) “[A] defendant may not make allegations of defect or misjoinder of parties in the demurrer if the pleadings do not disclose the existence of the matter relied on; such objection must be taken by plea or answer.” (Harboring Villas Homeowners Association v. Superior Court (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 426, 429.)  

 

Defendants argue the complaint contains no allegations whatsoever against Hsieh, who is the former Chief Executive Officer of LoanDepot. Accordingly, without allegations that Hsieh had personal involvement or stake in the Loan, Note, or Deed of Trust, he is not a proper party to this action. In addition, Defendants contend Plaintiff does not allege any facts that would meet the high burden for imposing alter ego liability against Hsieh.

 

The court agrees. The complaint fails to allege any facts against Defendant Hsieh, including any alter ego allegations.  To invoke the alter ego doctrine, the plaintiff must plead unity of interest and ownership and that an inequity will result if the corporate entity is treated as the sole actor. (See Vasey v. California Dance Co. (1977) 70 Cal.App.3d 742, 749.) Factors to consider in applying the doctrine include the commingling of funds and other assets, the holding out by one entity that it is liable for the debts of the other, identical equitable ownership in the two entities, inadequate capitalization, and disregard of corporate formalities. (Sonora Diamond Corp. v Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538-39.) 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Uncertainty

 

Defendants also demur to the complaint on the grounds the complaint is uncertain.

 

A demurrer based on uncertainty only applies where the complaint is so bad that a defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Though California courts take a liberal view toward in artfully drawn pleadings, it remains essential a complaint set forth the actionable facts with sufficient precision to inform the defendant of what the plaintiff is complaining about and what remedies are being sought.  (Signal Hill Aviation Co. v. Stroppe (1979) 96 Cal.App.3d 627, 636.) 

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s complaint is uncertain on its face because (1) it remains entirely unclear what role, if any, Hsieh played in the subject transaction and (2) Defendants cannot decipher what the bases for Plaintiff’s claims are, making it nearly impossible for Defendants to respond to the complaint. The court agrees. As noted above, it is unclear what claims are brought against Defendant Hsieh. Moreover, although Plaintiff has set out and labeled the causes of action, it is unclear what the facts are and what is being alleged.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendants demur to the breach of contract cause of action on the grounds Plaintiff does not set forth the terms of the agreement, what the breach of what agreement was, or that Plaintiff suffered any injury.

 

To recover damages for a breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for non-performance; (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) The pleadings must specify that the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct. (CCP § 430.10(g).) Further, “[a] written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. [Citation.] In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms. This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’  [Citation.]” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489 [citing 4 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (4th ed. 1997) Pleading, §§ 479-480, pp. 572-73].)

 

The court agrees the breach of contract cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to support a claim against Defendants. As noted above, Plaintiff must allege either the contract by its terms or its legal effect. The written agreement is not attached to the complaint nor are the terms alleged. Plaintiff also fails to allege how Defendants breached the agreement. BC-4, regarding damages, is blank. This is insufficient to support a claim for breach of contract.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action.

 

Negligence

 

Defendants demur to the second cause of action for negligence on the grounds there is no legal duty owed to Plaintiff by Defendants in the context of this case and Plaintiff does not even touch on the elements of a negligence cause of action.

 

To plead a cause of action for negligence, one must allege (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.) “In order to state a cause of action for negligence, the complaint must allege facts sufficient to show a legal duty on the part of the defendant to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Bellah v. Greenson (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 614, 619.)  In California, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 407-408.) 

 

The court agrees the negligence of action fails to state facts sufficient to support a claim against Defendants. Plaintiff’s negligence allegations include Plaintiff was led to believe money from Defendants were going to the seller, rather than Plaintiff’s Promissory Notes. As noted above, Plaintiff fails to allege the existence of a duty between Plaintiff and Defendants and moreover, Plaintiff fails to allege how Defendants breached that duty and was injured as a result of that breach.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action.

 

Fraud

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for fraud on the grounds the cause of action is a rambling hodgepodge of generalized conclusory statements and is devoid of the specificity required to state a fraud cause of action.

 

The court agrees. The elements of fraud are (1) misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damages. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.) Fraud allegations “involve a serious attack on character” and therefore are pleaded with specificity. (Hills Trans. Co. v. Southwest Forest Industries, Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 702, 707.) General and conclusory allegations are insufficient. (Lazar, supra, at p. 645.) The particularity requirement demands that a plaintiff plead facts which “ ‘ “show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” ’ ” (Ibid.) Further, when a plaintiff asserts fraud against a corporation, the plaintiff must “allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157, 2 Cal.Rptr.2d 861.) Less specificity in pleading fraud is required “when ‘it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy....’ ” [Citations.] (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)

 

Plaintiff’s allegations are insufficient as they are not alleged with the requisite specificity. Plaintiff does not allege who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. Plaintiff also fails to allege how Plaintiff was injured as a result of the alleged fraud.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action.

 

Common Counts

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for common counts on the grounds Plaintiff does not present a copy of, or any details regarding, the purported agreement under which either of the Defendants agreed to be indebted to Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s prior allegations directly contradict the claim for account stated.

 

“The essential elements of an account stated are: (1) previous transactions between the parties establishing the relationship of debtor and creditor; (2) an agreement between the parties, express or implied, on the amount due from the debtor to the creditor; (3) a promise by the debtor, express or implied, to pay the amount due.” (Zinn v. Fred R. Bright Co. (1969) 271 Cal.App.2d 597, 600.)  The elements of a cause of action for money lent are (1) that the defendant is indebted to plaintiff in a certain sum; and (2) for money lent, paid or expended to, or for, the defendant.  (Moya v. Northrup (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 276, 280.) The¿elements¿of a cause of action for¿money¿had¿and¿received¿are: (1) that the defendant received money that was intended to be used for the benefit of the plaintiff; (2) that the money was not used for the benefit of the plaintiff; and (3) that the defendant has not given the money to the plaintiff. (Avidor v. Sutter’s Place, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th1439, 1454.)

 

Here, based on the entirety of the complaint, it is unclear what money is owed from Defendants to Plaintiff. Moreover, as noted by Plaintiff, the allegations contradict Plaintiff’s earlier allegations that he executed the Note to borrow money from loanDepot. Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action with Leave to amend.
