Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Don B. Laughton v. City of Redondo Beach, et al.   

23TRCV00414

1.      City of Redondo Beach’s Motion for Summary Judgment

2.      Redondo Beach Unified School District’s Motion for Summary Adjudication  

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            City of Redondo Beach’s (“City”) Motion for Summary Judgment is denied.  

            Redondo Beach Unified School District’s (“District”) Motion for Summary Adjudication is denied.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on February 14, 2023. The operative First Amended Complaint was filed on June 6, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff slipped and fell on property owned or controlled by Defendants. Plaintiff was walking on the sidewalk when he encountered a bus stop with many students congregated around it. Plaintiff had to traverse around the stop and the students onto an area where the sidewalk abuts with a grassy slope. Plaintiff encountered a height differential which caused him to trip and fall.

Defendant’s City’s Objections to the Declaration of Henry M. Koffman

 

Objections 1 to 2 are overruled.

 

Motions for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.

 

“A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(1).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  Avivi, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467; CCP § 437c(c).

 

City of Redondo Beach

 

Defendant City moves for summary judgment as to Plaintiff’s Complaint on the grounds that Defendant did not have actual or constructive notice of the alleged defect, and that the condition resulted from natural forces and not any human activity.

 

Defendant has met its initial burden to show that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action. CCP § 437c(p)(2). A public entity is not liable for a dangerous condition of public property unless the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the condition. Martinez v. City of Beverly Hills (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 508; Gov. Code 835.2. “A public entity will be charged with constructive notice of a dangerous condition only if (1) the dangerous condition existed for a sufficient period of time before the plaintiff's injury, and (2) it was sufficiently obvious that the entity acted negligently in not discovering and repairing it.” Martinez v. City of Beverly Hills (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 508, 514. “Because it is the failure to discover and repair an obvious defect that renders the public entity negligent (and hence potentially liable for injuries caused by that defect), it becomes relevant whether (1) the entity had a “reasonably adequate” “inspection system” in place “to inform [it] whether the property was safe for the use or uses for which [it] used or intended others to use the public property and for uses that the public entity knew others were making of the public property” and (2) the entity “operated such an inspection system with due care” and still “did not discover the” defect.” Martinez v. City of Beverly Hills (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 508, 519.

 

Defendant submitted evidence to show that it did not have actual notice of the alleged dangerous condition. (Defendant’s Separate Statement of Facts and Supporting Evidence, 23-26.) Defendant submitted evidence to show that it did not have constructive notice of the alleged condition based on the location of the alleged defect – a portion where the sidewalk meets the grassy part of adjacent private property, that this defect would not have been obvious with a reasonable inspection, and that the location is not typically used for pedestrian use. (Id. at 5-18.)

 

However, Plaintiff has met his burden to provide specific facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to his cause of action.  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  Plaintiff submitted evidence to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to the reasonableness of the inspection system. First, evidence was provided that the height differential and the slope that was created was higher than acceptable standards. This differential should have been discovered by a reasonable inspection according to Plaintiff’s expert. The fact that the differential was not discovered during an apparent inspection conducted in January 2018 and during repairs that were conducted in 2020 shows the existence of a triable issue of fact as to the reasonableness of the inspection system. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts and Supporting Evidence, 62-71.)

 

Plaintiff submitted evidence that the defect was of an obvious nature that a reasonable inspection would have discovered the defect. Plaintiff presents the following facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to the obviousness of the defect: the location of the defect with its close proximity to bus benches that blocked access to the sidewalk causing pedestrians to traverse along the defect. In addition, Plaintiff presented evidence that an inspection of the area around the bus stop would be practical. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts and Supporting Evidence, 35, 56-74.)

 

Defendant’s arguments in the Reply heavily criticize the perceived emphasis by Plaintiff on the location of the bus benches. However, the location of the bus benches is significant based on the fact that the benches can foreseeably divert pedestrians directly into the path of the defective height differential and slope. It is not merely the existence of the bus benches that forms the basis of Plaintiff’s claim but, instead, of course, the actual defect itself must exist as well. Here, Plaintiff submitted evidence to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to the defect itself and that the defect should have been discovered through a reasonable inspection.

 

The case primarily relied upon by Defendant, Martinez v. City of Beverly Hills (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 508, is distinguishable on the facts namely based on the location of the defect. In Martinez, the defect occurred in an alley way which may not be typically traversed by pedestrians. Here, in the instant action, the defect occurred in a location that could have foreseeably utilized by a pedestrian based on the location of bus benches (and of course individuals who may be congregating around those benches) which may cause a pedestrian to divert away from a straight path through the sidewalk.

 

Finally, Defendant also attempts to argue that it cannot be liable due to a defect that was simply created by natural forces. However, Defendant submitted no competent evidence or authority to support this argument. Defendant’s evidence lacks competency on this point and is simply a conclusion based on no underlying evidence.

 

Thus, for the foregoing reasons, Defendant City’s motion for summary judgment is denied.

 

 

Redondo Beach Unified School District

 

Defendant/Cross-Defendant District moves for summary adjudication against Plaintiff and the City of Redondo Beach on the grounds that the Complaint and the City’s Cross-Complaint “fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a viable cause of action for money damages, as the District owed no duty to Plaintiff or City related to their respective causes of action alleged in their Complaint and Cross-Complaint against District.” (Notice of Motion, page 1, lines 12-14.) Namely, District argues that the area where Plaintiff fell was owned, controlled, and maintained solely by the City. A landowner has no right to control or manage premises held by another. Steinmetz v. Stockton City Chamber of Commerce (1985) 169 Cal. App. 3d 1142, 1147.  District submitted evidence that Plaintiff fell due to a defect located on a sidewalk maintained and controlled by the City.  (District’s Separate Statement of Facts, 4-11.)

 

However, Plaintiff and the City submitted evidence to meet their burden to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to their causes of action. CCP 437c(p)(2). “[A] landowner who exercises control over an adjoining strip of land has a duty to protect or warn others entering the adjacent land of a known hazard there. […] This duty arises even if the person does not own or exercise control over the hazard and even if the person does not own the abutting property on which the hazard is located.” Contreras v. Anderson (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 188, 197. Here, as noted in the Court’s ruling to the City’s motion, Plaintiff has identified the specific hazard – the dangerous height differential in the land abutting the grassy sloping area owned and controlled by the District and the sidewalk owned by the City.

 

Evidence was submitted by the opposing parties that free access was allowed to pedestrians including students and the Plaintiff to traverse onto the lawn area and back onto the sidewalk. There is no dispute that the lawn area was owned and controlled by moving party District. Thus, the opposing parties have identified a duty of care that was owed by the District to protect and warn of a potential hazard that existed on the land abutting the property that it controlled. (City’s Separate Statement of Facts and Supporting Evidence, 13-17).

 

For example, in Alcaraz v. Vece (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1149, the Plaintiff tripped on a meter box located on municipal party that was adjacent to private property – the apartment complex where Plaintiff lived. The California Supreme Court affirmed the Court of Appeal’s reversal of summary judgment in favor of the landlord holding that triable issues of fact existed as to the control over adjacent property and the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff.  Id. at 1154.  In Marsh v. City of Sacramento (1954) 127 Cal.App.2d 721, the Court of Appeal held that both the City and the adjacent property owned may be held liable after plaintiff fell into a ditch that abutted the sidewalk.  Similarly, in the instant action, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether the District had control over the land via its ownership of the grassy slopy walkway which immediately abutted an alleged dangerous condition. Thus, a triable issue of fact exists as to whether the District should have warned Plaintiff of the alleged dangerous condition or remedied the dangerous condition. (City’s Separate Statement of Facts and Supporting Evidence, 9-17); (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts and Supporting Evidence, 25-30; 57-71.)

 

Thus, for the foregoing reasons, the District’s motion for summary adjudication is denied.
