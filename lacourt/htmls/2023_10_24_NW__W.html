DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00714    Hearing Date: October 24, 2023    Dept: W

YS PROPERTIES, LLC vs HAYK SHISHOYAN, et al.

 

plaintiff’s MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, ISSUE SANCTIONS AND MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANTs for misuse of the discovery process

 

Date of Hearing:        October 24, 2023                              Trial Date:       November 27, 2023

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00714

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff YS Properties, LLC  

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 26, 2022, Plaintiff YS Properties, LLC filed a complaint against Hayk Shishoyan, Hats Enterprises, LLC and Hats Enterprises LLC dba Encino Tailors for breach of written contract and breach of guaranty. Plaintiff alleges Defendants have failed to pay monthly rent for their commercial lease with Plaintiff.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions, in the alternative, Issue Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions against Defendant Hats Enterprises LLC for Misuse of the Discovery Process is GRANTED, in part.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions, in the alternative, Issue Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions against Defendant Hayk Shishoyan for Misuse of the Discovery Process is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves the court for an order imposing terminating sanctions or, in the alternative, issue and monetary sanctions against Defendants and their attorney of record on the grounds Defendants continually engage in tactics to prevent legitimate discovery and fails to obey this Court’s prior order that issued sanctions against Defendants and their attorney of record. Plaintiff contends Defendants willfully failed to pay the monetary sanctions and willfully failed to obey this Court’s prior order that required Defendants to serve code-compliant supplemental responses, without objection, within 20 days.

 

Where a party fails to obey an order compelling answers to discovery, “the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c), 2023.010, subd. (c); R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.)  The Court may impose a terminating sanction against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).)  Misuse of the discovery process includes failure to respond to an authorized method of discovery or disobeying a court order to provide discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d), (g).)  A terminating sanction may be imposed by an order dismissing part or all of the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(3).)   

 

Before any sanctions may be imposed the court must make an express finding that there has been a willful failure of the party to serve the required answers.  (Fairfield v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 113, 118.)  Lack of diligence may be deemed willful where the party understood its obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply.  (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 787; Fred Howland Co. v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1966) 244 Cal.App.2d 605, 610-611.)  The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful.  (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 788; Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250; Evid. Code, §§ 500, 605.) 

 

Plaintiff notes that Defendants failed to provide further discovery responses within 20 days after the court’s order on July 13, 2023. (Xu Decl. ¶3.) The court also issued monetary sanctions against Hats Enterprises in the amount of $1,000.00, against Hayk Shishoyan in the amount of $1,750.00, and against Defense Counsel for $2,750, through joint and several liability. (Xu Decl. ¶4.) Defendants nor Defense Counsel have complied with the court order to pay sanctions despite Plaintiff following up several times. (Xu Decl. ¶¶7-11.)  On August 7, 2023, Defendants did provide supplemental responses; however, those responses contained objections, contrary to court order.  (Xu Decl., ¶4 Exh. B.) Under the totality of the circumstances, the court cannot conclude that less severe sanctions would be ineffective and that terminating or issue sanctions are warranted at this time, despite defendants failure to oppose this motion.   If Defendants still have not complied with the Court’s July 13, 2023 Order by providing responses without objections, and if Plaintiff believe they are entitled to further responses, Plaintiff may pursue other relief through the appropriate discovery motions. 

 

Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $9,520 together with the unpaid amount of the previous sanctions amount of $2,750 for a total of $12,270 against Defendants and Defense Counsel, jointly and severally. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $2,750 (prior court order) and $3,850 for the instant motion for the total of $6,600.00.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions, in the alternative, Issue Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions against Defendants Hats Enterprises LLC for Misuse of the Discovery Process is GRANTED, in part. Case Number: 22VECV01096    Hearing Date: October 24, 2023    Dept: W

TANNETTE GATES v. warner center condominiums, et al.

 

DEFENDANT TOPA INSURANCE COMPANY’S DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT AND MOTION TO STRIKE PUNITIVE DAMAGES

Date of Hearing:        October 24, 2023                              Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01096

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Topa Insurance Company

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Tannette Gates

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Martino Decl. ¶¶5-9.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 3, 2022, Plaintiff Tannette Gates filed a complaint against Warner Center Condominiums, Ross Morgan & Company, Inc., Amber Hill, Valerie Vincent, Joanne Catran, Mimi Himes, Afsaneh Safaei, Arash Safaei, and Hippo Analytics, Inc. for damages arising out of a kitchen pipe exploding in the unit upstairs and water from the upstairs unit damaging Plaintiffs. On January 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint asserting causses of action for: (1) Breach of CCRs; (2) Enforcement of Equitable Servitudes; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (4) Nuisance; (5) Negligence; (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (7) Breach of Contract and Implied Covenant of Duty of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (8) Third-Party Beneficiary Breach of Contract; (9) Professional Negligence and (Negligent Misrepresentation (10) Violation of Business and Professional Code § 17200; (11) Declaratory Relief; and (12) Unjust Enrichment against. Plaintiff also named Topa Insurance Company as a defendant.

 

On January 30, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Amber Hill, Valerie Vincent, Joanne Catran, and Mimi Himes.

 

Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint on May 11, 2023. On September 7, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Hippo Analytics, Inc.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED; Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In support of their demurrer with motion to strike, Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of several court documents (RJN, Exhs. 1-3.) The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code 452(d).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Topa Insurance Company demurs to the three causes of action concerning breach of contract and unjust enrichment as well as moves to strike Plaintiff’s claims for punitive damages.[1]

 

Seventh Cause of Action

 

Defendant Topa first demurs to the seventh cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds that although Plaintiff refers to an alleged contract being attached to the complaint, there is no contract attached to the complaint. Additionally, there is no policy language alleged in the second amended complaint.

 

To recover damages for a breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for non-performance; (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) The pleadings must specify that the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct. (CCP § 430.10(g).) Further, “[a] written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. [Citation.] In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms. This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’  [Citation.]” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489 [citing 4 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (4th ed. 1997) Pleading, §§ 479-480, pp. 572-73].)

 

Plaintiff alleges she entered into a contractual obligation with Topa. (SAC ¶101.) Plaintiff then alleges she entered into a valid and enforceable contract with Hippo and the Hippo Contract is valid and enforceable against Topa. (SAC ¶102.) Plaintiff does not allege the terms of the contract other than Topa breached the agreement by failing to properly investigate the claim and therefore, ultimately denying Plaintiff’s claim. (SAC ¶105.) Plaintiff has failed to attach the agreement to the complaint or allege the terms of the agreement either verbatim or by its legal effect.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff states they are aware of the deficiencies and request that the court allow Plaintiff leave to amend to address the issues. Plaintiff does not address how the pleading can be amended successfully. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)

 

The court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer to the seventh cause of action. At the hearing, Plaintiff must address how they plan on addressing the issues raised in the demurrer so leave to amend may be granted.

 

Eighth Cause of Action

 

Defendant also demurs to the eighth cause of action for third party beneficiary claim for breach of contract on the same grounds as the demurrer to the seventh cause of action. For reasons stated above, the court SUSTAINS the demurrer. At the hearing, Plaintiff must address how they plan on addressing the issues raised in the demurrer so leave to amend may be granted.

 

Unjust Enrichment

 

Defendant demurs to the twelfth cause of action for unjust enrichment on the grounds California does not recognize such a cause of action.

 

There is a split amongst California courts regarding whether unjust enrichment is an independent cause of action. Some courts have explicitly stated that “there is no cause of action in California for unjust enrichment.” (Melchior v. New Line Prods., Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 779, 793; McKell v. Wash. Mut., Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1490; Jogani v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 901, 911.) These courts conclude that unjust enrichment is “not a cause of action ... or even a remedy, but rather a principle, underlying various legal doctrines and remedies. It is synonymous with restitution.” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 387 (internal quotation marks and citations omitted). However, other California courts have recognized an independent cause of action for unjust enrichment. (See First Nationwide Sav. v. Perry (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1657, 1662–63; Lectrodryer v. SeoulBank (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 723, 726.)

 

The court follows the line of authority that unjust enrichment may be alleged as cause of action. (See Professional Tax Appeal v. Kennedy-Wilson Holdings, Inc. (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 230.)

 

Defendant’s demurrer on the grounds unjust enrichment is not a cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages on the ground Plaintiff has failed to allege any oppression, fraud or malice committed by Topa. The only mention of punitive damages throughout the entire complaint is in Plaintiff’s prayer for relief in which she contends that she prays for judgment in her favor on all causes of action “for general, special, direct, compensatory, and punitive damages, with the exact amount to be proven at time of trial.”

 

The court agrees that the allegations supporting a claim for punitive damages is insufficient. Plaintiff generally alleges denying the claim was done with malice, fraud, and/or oppression.  These conclusory allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

Case Number: 23VECV02860    Hearing Date: October 24, 2023    Dept: W

MICHELE ANN MEDINA, AN INDIVIDUAL v. BIRD RIDES, INC.

 

DEFENDANT BIRD RIDES, INC,’S MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION AND STAY CASE PENDING COMPLETION OF ARBITRATION

 

Date of Hearing:        October 24, 2023                              Trial Date:       None

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV02860

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Bird Rides, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Michele Ann Medina  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 30, 2023, Plaintiff Michele Ann Medina filed a complaint against Defendants Bird Rides, Inc. and Segway, Inc. for negligence. Plaintiff alleges while using a scooter rented from Defendants, the subject scooter malfunctioned causing Plaintiff to be ejected off and fall to the ground.

 

On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Segway, Inc.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Bird Rides, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Case Pending Completion of Arbitration is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Bird Rides, Inc. moves this court for an order compelling Plaintiff Medina to arbitrate her claims against Bird asserted in this lawsuit, pursuant to the arbitration provision of the “Bird Rental Agreement, Waiver of Liability and Release” (the “Rental Agreement”) Plaintiff and Bird entered into on July 22, 2022.

 

The court briefly notes Plaintiff’s opposition was untimely filed. Papers opposing a motion must be filed and served at least nine court days before the hearing. (CCP 1005(b).) Plaintiff filed their opposition on October 19th – three court days before the hearing. The court admonishes Plaintiff and expects Plaintiff to comply with all service and filing requirements imposed by the Code of Civil Procedure and Local Rules going forward. Nevertheless, the court elects to exercise its discretion to consider the untimely opposition. (CRC 3.1300(d).)

 

“[W]hen a petition to compel arbitration is filed and accompanied by prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy, the court itself must determine whether the agreement exists and, if any defense to its enforcement is raised, whether it is enforceable. Because the existence of the agreement is a statutory prerequisite to granting the petition, the petitioner bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence. If the party opposing the petition raises a defense to enforcement… that party bears the burden of producing evidence of, and proving by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to the defense.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal. 4th 394, 413.)

 

Defendant Bird has met their initial burden of showing that an arbitration agreement exists with Plaintiff. Defendant presents a copy of the arbitration agreement Plaintiff accepted and agreed to on July 22, 2022, when she first signed up as a Bird user. (Frascella Decl. ¶19, Exh. C.) As part of the arbitration agreement, Plaintiff agreed to the following: [A]ll claims arising out of or relating to use and rental of a Vehicle, this Agreement, and the parties’ relationship with each other shall be finally settled by binding arbitration… The arbitration shall be administered by JAMS, or alternatively a mutually agreed upon arbitrator or arbitration service, under the applicable rules for JAMS or the mutually agreed upon arbitration service, excluding any rules or procedures governing or permitting class actions.” (Frascella Decl., Exh. A.) Furthermore, Plaintiff’s claim she was injured while riding the Bird Scooter arises directly out of and relate to the Bird Rental Agreement itself.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff first argues the motion must be denied because the exhibits attached are unauthenticated and the declarant, Mr. Frascella, lacks the personal knowledge of the things he attests to in the declaration. The court disagrees. Mr. Frascella has properly authenticated each exhibit and moreover, Mr. Frascella has properly laid the foundation providing his personal knowledge to authenticate the documents.

 

Next, Plaintiff refers to a Hurt affidavit being insufficient to prove Plaintiff’s intent to be bound by the provisions of Bird’s arbitration clause. There is no Hurt affidavit provided by Bird. Nonetheless, the court finds in light of the evidence presented by Frascella’s declaration, Bird has met its burden of establishing that it gave Plaintiff reasonably conspicuous notice of the existence of the Arbitration Agreement. (See eyer v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2d Cir. 2017) 868 F.3d 66, 75 holding the design of the screen and language used in the interface provided reasonable notice of its Terms of Service as a matter of California law.)

 

Lastly, Plaintiff makes the argument the FAA does not apply. The court disagrees. As noted by Defendant, the Bird Rental Agreement is “a contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce,” as users like Plaintiff must use the Bird App on their smartphones to attest to the Bird Rental Agreement and rent a Bird scooter.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. The action is stayed pending arbitration.
