DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02741    Hearing Date: March 6, 2024    Dept: W

LONNI LZ INC. vs GENERAL MOTORS, LLC

 

PLAINTIFF LONNI LZ INC’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION RESPONSES FROM DEFENDANT GENERAL MOTORS, LLC, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:          March 6, 2024                                     Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         23VECV02741

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc.

Responding Party:       Defendant General Motors LLC   

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc. filed a complaint against Defendant General Motors LLC on June 23, 2023 for Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Plaintiff alleges on October 16, 2022, Plaintiff purchased a 2023 Chevrolet Suburban. However, the subject vehicle has suffered from nonconformities to warranty, including, but not limited to, the engine, pistons, check engine light, faulty valve cover gasket, and other defects.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Request for Production Responses from Defendant General Motors, LLC is GRANTED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Cameron Major submitted by Defendant General Motors, LLC in support of its Opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Request for Production Responses. The court OVERRULES the objections.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc. moves the court for an order compelling Defendant GM to produce further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production, Set One Nos. 27, 28, and 36 within ten calendar days; an order compelling the production of documents identified in GM’s responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set One; and sanctions against GM and/or its counsel of record in the amount of $3,000.00.

A party may move to compel further written response to a request for production if the responding party’s statement of compliance is incomplete, a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, or an objection is without merit. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) To prevail, the party moving for the order must first offer specific facts demonstrating “good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) If “good cause” is shown by the moving party, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) 

 

Plaintiff argues GM served inapplicable objections that are without merit to Plaintiff’s request for production of documents nos. 27, 28, and 36. Specifically, GM failed to provide any factual information for Plaintiff to work off of to determine why a privilege objection was lodged. GM also failed to provide a privilege log. Plaintiff further argues the requests and the documents sought are relevant to Plaintiff’s lemon law and civil penalty claims. Plaintiff contends GM’s policies and procedures are relevant to its compliance with the Act as these documents shed light on how Defendant purportedly repurchases vehicles promptly and in accordance with its affirmative obligation to do so. Moreover, Plaintiff’s requests are also relevant on the issue of whether GM committed a willful violation under Civil Code Section 1794(c).

 

In opposition, GM first argues Plaintiff failed to sufficiently meet and confer with GM in good faith as its emails made no effort to substantively discuss GM’s responses and objections to Plaintiff’s discovery requests.  The court disagrees. As noted at the previous hearing, Plaintiff has presented evidence of their attempts to meet and confer with Defendant regarding the issues around discovery including the instant motion.

 

Next, GM argues the objections are well founded as the requests are overbroad in scope, seeking “WRITINGS,” without limitations, regarding highly proprietary and sensitive information regarding how GM runs its operations. Moreover, the requests also repeatedly demand production of confidential, proprietary, and commercially sensitive trade secrets.

 

The court finds good cause for further responses and production of documents to Plaintiff’s request for production of documents, nos. 27, 28, and 36. As noted by Plaintiff, GM’s policies and procedures are relevant to whether GM made a good faith effort to comply with the Song-Beverly Act. Defendant GM has not shown such requests are overbroad in scope. If GM believes the production is privileged, it must produce a privilege log. The parties may also agree to a protective order as noted by Defendant GM in opposition.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff Lonni LZ Inc’s Motion to Compel Further Request for Production Responses from Defendant General Motors, LLC is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2031.310(h).)  

 

Plaintiff requests $3,000.00 in sanctions against GM and/or its counsel for GM’s failure to provide compliant responses to Plaintiff’s Requests. Plaintiff contends GM cannot show that its actions are justifiable. Defendant provided a litany of boilerplate objections that are non-compliant under the Code. It also provided non-responsive answers and refused to produce a single responsive document to the requests previously specified.

 

In opposition, Defendant GM argues Plaintiff served facially overbroad requests that exceed the scope of the parties’ claims and defenses for the sole purpose of manufacturing a discovery dispute, yet GM (i) served Code-compliant responses, and (ii) produced hundreds of pages of responsive documents concerning Plaintiff’s Suburban.

 

The court has previously sanctioned defendant for failure to adequately meet and confer.  The court declines to award redundant sanction against defendant for the same conduct.  The court otherwise finds the defendant’s position substantially justified, as the requests were quite broad, even if the court ultimately overruled them.  The request for sanctions is denied.

 


Case Number: 23VECV03197    Hearing Date: March 6, 2024    Dept: W

WEST REGIONAL CARDIO THORACIC VASCULAR SURGEONS, et al. v. HANY MALEK, et al.

 

Demurrer to the first amended cross-complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        March 6, 2024                                   Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03197

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 24, 2023, Plaintiffs West Regional Cardio Thoracic and Vascular Surgeons, West Regional Vein Center, West Regional Physicians Network, Inc. ("WRPN"), Lorena Medical Group, Inc. ("Lorena Medical Group"), Angelus Medical Clinic Multispecialty Group, Inc. ("Angelus Medical Group"), and Atlantic Surgical Center, LLC ("Atlantic") filed a complaint against Defendants Haney Malek, Bridgette Morales, James Gouda, Chelsea Malek, Joseph Marquis, Rola Moukaddem, Hanan Botros, Reda Hanna, Scandin Medical Management Enterprises, Inc., Scantlin Medical Solutions, Inc., Care Concepts, Inc., True Vine Concepts, LLC, Best Ride, Inc., Cura Hospice Care, Inc., and Trinity Healthcare Management, Inc. for trespass, conversion, intentional interference with contractual relations, and conspiracy.

 

On November 30, 2023, Hany S. Malek, D.C. filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendants Harold A. Tabaie, D.O., Ph.D., West Regional Cardio Thoracic and Vascular Surgeons, West Regional Vein Center, West Regional Physicians Network, Inc., Angelus Medical Clinic Multi Specialty Group, Inc., Lorena Medical Group, Inc., Selective Investments Channel, LLC, and West Regional Physicians IPA for 1) Breach of Written Contracts; 2) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; 3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 4) Anticipatory Breach of Contract; 5) Conversion; 6) Money Had and Received; 7) Unjust Enrichment; 8) Unfair Competition; 9) Constructive Trust and Accounting; 10) Declaratory Relief; 11) Accounting; 12) Preliminary and Permanent Injunctive Relief; 13) Malicious Prosecution; 14) Abuse of Process; 15) California Penal Code § 502; 16) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Harold A. Tabaie, D.O. Ph.D’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED, in part.

 

Nominal Cross-Defendant’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    DEMURRER OF CROSS-DEFENDANT HAROLD A. TABAIE, D.O., PH.D, TO FIRST AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

Cross-Defendant Harold A. Tabaie, D.O., Ph.D (“Dr. Tabaie”) demurs to Hany S. Malek, D.C.’s (“Dr. Malek”) first, second, third, thirteenth, fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth causes of action.[1] [2]

 

Contract Terms

 

Dr. Tabaie demurs to the cross-complaint on the grounds Dr. Malek seeks to enforce contract terms that are illegal or void.

 

i.                    Illegal Contracts

 

Dr. Tabaie first argues the FAXC’s allegations that Dr. Tabaie violated certain contract terms fails as these terms are illegal and unenforceable since they give Dr. Malek, a non-physician, veto power over Dr. Tabaie’s operation of the medical practice.

 

Section 13401.5 lists the permitted shareholders in various professional medical corporations. It provides in pertinent part as follows: “Notwithstanding subdivision (d) of Section 13401 and any other provision of law, the following licensed persons may be shareholders, officers, directors, or professional employees of the professional corporations designated in this section so long as the sum of all shares owned by those licensed persons does not exceed 49 percent of the total number of shares of the professional corporation so designated herein, and so long as the number of those licensed persons owning shares in the professional corporation so designated herein does not exceed the number of persons licensed by the governmental agency regulating the designated professional corporation[.]”

 

The complaint essentially alleges the parties entered several shareholder agreements whereby the parties agreed to amongst other things: Dr. Malek and Dr. Tabaie will have a single, equal vote on any matter; employees could not be hired or fired without agreement by both Dr. Malek and Dr. Tabaie; Dr. Malek is an additional accountholder, authorized user, and signor on company bank accounts; unanimous consent is required to fire any employee; neither Dr. Malek nor Dr. Tabaie may do any act that would make it impossible to carry out the ordinary business of the companies; unanimous consent is required to modify the Shareholder Agreements; and unanimous consent is required to assign any check writing authority. (FAXC ¶71.) Dr. Malek alleges Dr. Tabaie breached these terms when terminating other employees of the companies deemed sympathetic to Dr. Malek without Dr. Malek’s approval or consent; unilaterally removing Dr. Malek from his co-owned certain shareholder agreements (WRP IPA contracts); converting no less than $930,000.00 from the corporate bank accounts without explanation or approval of Dr. Malek; and closing company bank accounts, removing all funds, transferring said funds to reassigned accounts in the name of Dr. Tabaie. (FAXC ¶72.)

 

Dr. Malek argues these terms do not violate the Corporate Practice of Medicine Doctrine because under California law, a licensed chiropractor, like Dr. Malek, may have ownership in a professional corporation. (Bus. & Prof. Code §3600, et. seq.) The only restriction imposed on Dr. Malek pursuant to Section 13401.5 is “the sum of all shares owned by those licensed persons does not exceed 49 percent of the total number of shares of the professional corporation so designated herein.” Here, Dr. Malek’s 49 percent ownership interest in West Regional, Angelus, Lorena and the IPA is not in dispute, nor is the propriety of such an ownership structure. Regardless, Dr. Malek contends, the court should not look at the factual findings of the Evidentiary Hearing regarding two of the contracts on demurrer.

 

The court agrees that Dr. Malek cannot enforce certain terms of the agreement that violate the Corporate Practice of Medicine Doctrine. First, the court notes a general demurrer may lie where the terms of the contract alleged are illegal and thus, void. (See Beck v. American Health Group Int'l, Inc. (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1555, 1563 [superseded by statute on other grounds].) Accordingly, the court may look at the legal effect of the shareholder agreements Dr. Malek relies on. By alleging that the shareholder agreements provided that “the unanimous consent of the two shareholders [Tabaie and Malek] is required in the hiring or firing of employees” and “neither shareholder may do any act which would make it impossible to carry on the ordinary business of [the company]”, the court agrees such terms violate California’s ban on the Corporate Practice of Medicine as they allow Dr. Malek to exercise undue control over the practice. However, the court disagrees that the shareholder agreements are therefore automatically illegal and void. In California Physicians' Service v. Aoki Diabetes Research Institute (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1506, the court found although the nonprofit violated the statutory ban on the corporate practice of medicine, Blue Shield also failed to demonstrate that the violation made the contract unenforceable. The court stated “[t]he ban on the corporate practice of medicine is meant to protect patients, not health care service plans; a contract for the provision of medical services by licensed professionals is plainly not malum in se; Blue Shield would be unjustly enriched if it were allowed to retain the benefit of services bestowed on its subscribers without compensating ADRI.” (California Physicians' Service v. Aoki Diabetes Research Institute (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1506, 1517.) Similarly here, the court finds allegations including converting no less than $920,000.00 from the corporate bank accounts and transferring said funds to accounts Dr. Malek cannot access would unjustly enrich Dr. Tabaie if Dr. Malek is entitled to a portion of the funds.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. The cross-complaint should be limited to breaches of the shareholder agreements that are not in violation of the Corporate Practice of Medicine Doctrine. 

 

ii.                  Void Contracts

 

Next, Dr. Tabaie argues the contracts for West Regional PC and Angelus PC are void as a matter of law and/or conflict with new admissions in the FAXC and the contracts for Lorena PC and West Regional IPA have neither been produced nor their terms alleged in haec verba. The court notes that  none of the exhibits of the contracts are provided. They are not attached to the FAXC filed with the court (although it seems like they must have been attached to the copy served on Dr. Tabaie). Despite requesting that the parties provide the court with copies of the exhibits, they were not provided as of the date of the hearing. 

 

Dr. Tabaie contends West Regional PC’s “Operating Agreement” and Angelus PC’s “Shareholder Operating Agreement” is a document designed to govern a limited liability company. However, in California, professional medical services can only be rendered through a “professional corporation” – not a limited liability company. The court agrees. California permits licensed professionals to form professional corporations, but not limited liability companies, for the delivery of medical and certain other professional services. (Corps. Code § 13401.5.)  The court agrees that Dr. Malek cannot proceed to enforce a void agreement.  

 

As for Lorena PC and West Regional IPA, the court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. As noted by Dr. Tabaie, Dr. Malek need not attach the contracts but either then must set out the terms verbatim in the body of the complaint or allege the legal effect. (See McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) “In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must allege the substance of its relevant terms.” (Id.) The court finds the FAXC does allege the substance of the relevant terms and if Dr. Malek is unable to produce the agreements by the time of trial, then that is a consideration for the jury.

 

iii.                Selective Investments

 

Dr. Tabaie also demurs to the cross-complaint on the grounds the contract for Selective Investments does not contain the terms Dr. Malek alleges. Dr. Tabaie argues Malek fails to allege facts sufficient to show how Selective Investments – an alleged holding company of parcels of land – has been harmed. Dr. Tabaie further argues the Selective Investments Channel, LLC “Amended and Restated Limited Liability Company Agreement” does not contain the terms Dr. Malek seeks to enforce.

  

In opposition, Dr. Malek contends Dr. Tabaie does not set forth the cause or causes of action to which he takes issue and therefore believes there is nothing for him to oppose.  Because the court is allowing leave to amend, the court will sustain the demurrer as it relates to allegations pertaining to Selective Investments.  Dr. Malek is to set forth how Selective Investments has been harmed, as well as the terms of the parties’ agreement that he seeks to enforce.  

 

Thirteenth Cause of Action

 

Dr. Tabaie demurs to the thirteenth cause of action for malicious prosecution on the grounds Dr. Malek does not state facts of essential elements sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

To establish the tort of malicious prosecution, a plaintiff must plead three elements: that a lawsuit “(1) was commenced by or at the direction of the defendant and was pursued to a legal termination favorable to the plaintiff; (2) was brought without probable cause; and (3) was initiated with malice. [Citation.]” (Nunez v. Pennisi (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 861, 872-873.) 

 

Dr. Tabaie argues the basis of Malek’s malicious prosecution claim is the Elder Abuse Temporary Restraining Order (“TRO”) matter entitled Tabaie v. Malek (Case No. 23VERO0110), which was filed and adjudicated in the Family Law Division; however, it is longstanding precedent in California that Family Law proceedings cannot serve as the basis for a malicious prosecution claim. (Bidna v. Rosen (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 27, 35; Robert J. v. Catherine D. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1500, 1525.) Dr. Tabaie further argues even if the Elder Abuse TRO was not a Family Law proceeding, Dr. Malek’s malicious prosecution claim still fails as a matter of law because Malek did not prevail in that action. Dr. Tabaie states a vital element of a claim for malicious prosecution is that the prior action “resulted in a termination of the action in plaintiff's favor.” (Lanz v. Goldstone (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 441, 458.)

 

In opposition, Dr. Malek argues there is not an absolute bar of malicious prosecution claims based on any kind of family law motion or order to show cause. In Nicholson v. Fazeli (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 1091, the court concluded “that it would be improper to apply its absolute bar on malicious prosecution actions to ordinary civil actions, such as this one, that originate in a family law proceeding.” (Nicholson v. Fazeli (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 1091, 1099.) In Nicholson, the cross-complaint did not raise any family law issues nor did it involve marital status, child custody or spousal support. (Id.) The court noted “[t]hough the characterization of the property in question as Trust property might have had some impact on the division of community property, the Trust did not seek to characterize the property as community or separate property but rather as Trust property. The Trust's action was simply a civil action for possession of property alleged to be trust property and damages for the loss of trust property. Had this same pleading been separately filed in the superior court, there would be no question that it could support a malicious prosecution action[].”

 

Here, the court finds Nicholson controlling.  The Elder Abuse Act restraining order sought by Dr. Tabai arose in an ordinary business dispute.  The parties are not family members.  The fact that the case was heard in a family law court is merely an assignment decision by the Los Angeles Superior Court.  The Act itself is ordinarily enforced through civil litigation in civil courts.  As such, the court finds allowing malicious prosecution actions based on successful or unsuccessful civil harassment suits is not barred merely because the matter was heard in a court that is part of the family law division. 

 

But even if the court found that this action aligns with Nicholson, the court agrees that the elder abuse TRO did not result in Dr. Malek’s favor. Dr. Malek cites to Maleti v. Wickers (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 181 and Sycamore Ridge Apartments LLC v. Naumann (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 1385, to support his position. However, in Maleti, the court stated “[a] voluntary dismissal of the prior suit by the plaintiff—not resulting from a settlement—will, in general, constitute a favorable termination.” (Maleti v. Wickers (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 181, 205.) Here, the Judge in the Family Law proceeding reinstated the TRO in Dr. Tabaie’s favor and then Dr. Tabaie voluntarily dismissed the action after the parties entered a mutual settlement agreement. (RJN, Exhs. L-N.) Although the court in Sycamore noted the malice element of malicious prosecution included proceedings are initiated for purpose of forcing settlement which has no relation to merits of the claim, there is no indication of such in the instant action. JSJ Limited Partnership v. Mehrban (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1512, 1524.  As noted by the court in JSJ  Limited Partnership v. Mehrban (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1512, 1524: “[W]hen a dismissal results from negotiation, settlement, or consent, a favorable termination is normally not recognized” because “the dismissal reflects ambiguously on the merits of the action.”

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the thirteenth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

Fourteenth Cause of Action

 

Dr. Tabaie demurs to the fourteenth cause of action for abuse of process on the grounds Dr. Malek has failed to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for abuse of process.

 

The tort of abuse of process has two elements: (1) an “ulterior purpose,” and (2) “a willful act in the use of the process not proper in the regular conduct of the proceeding.” (Siam v. Kizilbash (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1563, 1579.) “Process is action taken pursuant to judicial authority . . . . [¶] Merely obtaining or seeking process is not enough; there must be subsequent abuse, by a misuse of the judicial process for a purpose other than that which it was intended to serve. [Citations.] The gist of the tort is the improper use of the process¿after it is issued.” (Adams v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 521, 530-531.) 

 

Dr. Tabaie contends Dr. Malek’s claim fails because the mere “naming and serving [Malek] in” the Elder Abuse TRO matter, “caus[ing] the complaint [in that matter] to be filed against and served on [Malek],” “caus[ing] the [Elder Abuse TRO] to be issued and served against [Malek]” and “voluntarily dismiss[ing]” that action after it was settled does not constitute an “abuse of process.” Moreover, Dr. Malek’s claim that Dr. Tabaie’s appeal of Judge Cotton’s order appointing receivership “knowing they had no intention of pursuing it” also cannot form the basis for an abuse of process claim.

 

In opposition, Dr. Malek argues his allegations set forth myriad improprieties, all of which were done to obtain, and obtained, unjustifiable collateral advantage, and certainly “extraneous to or of a result not within its legitimate scope.” (Ion Equipment Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal. App. 3d 868, 876.) According to Dr. Malek, this includes the Elder Abuse Action in its entirety; seeking a TRO in the way in which it was done; dismissing the Elder Abuse Action right before trial; filing an appeal of the Receivership Order in this case; and filing an ex parte application for an undertaking that would never be needed.

 

The court finds Dr. Malek has not stated grounds for an abuse of process cause of action. “The California Supreme Court stated: ‘[T]he mere filing or maintenance of a lawsuit—even for an improper purpose—is not a proper basis for an abuse of process action.’ [Citations.]” (S.A. v. Maiden (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 27, 42.) Here, Dr. Malek’s claim is based entirely on the filing of the Elder Abuse TRO as well as the Receivership Order Appeal and ex parte Application. Dr. Malek’s allegations do not actually allege any misuse of process in the course of maintaining the allegedly meritless Elder Abuse TRO proceedings or the instant matter as Dr. Malek has not alleged how the appointment of a receivership entertained an ulterior motive in using the process.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourteenth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fifteenth Cause of Action

 

Dr. Tabaie demurs to the fifteenth cause of action for California Penal Code § 502 on the grounds Dr. Malek has not alleged facts sufficient to support a cause of action.

 

The Comprehensive Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (“CDAFA”) codified in California Penal Code §502 provides that any person who commits the following acts is guilty of a public offence: “(1) Knowingly accesses and without permission… uses any data, computer, computer system, or computer network in order to… wrongfully control or obtain money, property, or data; [or] (2) Knowingly accesses and without permission takes, copies, or makes use of any data from a computer, computer system, or computer network, or takes or copies any supporting documentation, whether existing or residing internal or external to a computer, computer system, or computer network.”  (Penal Code §§502(c)(1)(B), 502(c)(2).) 

 

Dr. Tabaie argues Dr. Malek does not allege Dr. Tabaie “hacked” the companies’ systems and moreover, by Dr. Malek’s own allegations, at a minimum Dr. Tabaie is majority 51% owner and CEO of all the clinics and the only licensed physician, which entitles him access his own business and patient records. Additionally, “[s]tanding to bring a CDAFA[/Penal Code § 502] claim requires a showing of economic harm or loss,” which Malek does not allege.

 

Dr. Malek does not address this argument. The court finds Dr. Malek has failed to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for violation of Penal Code section 502. The fifteenth cause of action alleges Dr. Tabaie acted outside the scope of his position as shareholder/member of the MT Enterprise as set forth in Section 502(h)(1) and Dr. Tabaie knowingly accessed and used computers and computer systems of the MT Enterprise and the data contained therein. (FAXC ¶¶137, 138.) The court notes the FAXC also alleges Dr. Tabaie changed passwords and blocked Dr. Malek’s computer access. (See FAXC ¶72(h).) However, it is unclear from the allegations how Dr. Tabaie acted outside the scope of his employment or how Dr. Malek was harmed by Dr. Tabaie’s conduct.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifteenth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Sixteenth Cause of Action

 

Dr. Tabaie demurs to the sixteenth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds Dr. Malek fails to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action.

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) intention to cause or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) severe emotional suffering; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress. (See Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780; Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009.) To satisfy the element of extreme and outrageous conduct, defendant’s conduct “‘must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’” (Moncada, supra, 221 Cal.App.4th at 780 (quoting Trerice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883).)  

 

Dr. Tabaie argues the FAXC does not directly allege what that conduct is and Dr. Tabaie can only surmise Dr. Malek’s claim rests on the alleged conduct related to the Elder Abuse TRO and the filings in this lawsuit. The court agrees it is unclear what the outrageous conduct includes. The cross-complaint simply alleges “Tabaie’s conduct as set forth in this First Amended Cross-Complaint was extreme and outrageous.” (FAXC ¶144.) Even if the court were to assume that the allegations then include Dr. Tabaie filing the Elder Abuse TRO or violating Penal Code section 502, Dr. Malek does not allege any severe emotional suffering. The cross-complaint simply alleges he “suffered extreme emotional distress”. (FAXC ¶145.) 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixteenth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

II.                  DEMURRER OF NOMINAL CROSSDEFENDANTS TO FIRST AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

Nominal Cross-Defendants West Regional Cardio Thoracic and Vascular Surgeons, West Regional Vein Center, West Regional Physicians Network, Inc., And Angelus Medical Clinic Multispecialty Group, Inc. (collectively, “Nominal Cross-Defendants”) demur to the Third, Fifth, Sixth, Seventh, Eighth, Ninth, Tenth, Eleventh, and Twelfth Causes of Action for Shareholder Derivative Claims contained in Dr. Malek’s First Amended Cross-Complaint.[3]

 

Corporations Code §800

 

Nominal Cross-Defendants first demur to the FAXC on the grounds Corporations Code section 800 requires notice and demand by a registered shareholder before bringing a derivative action.

 

Pursuant to Corporations Code section 800, subdivision (b)(2), a plaintiff shareholder must allege with particularity (1) the demand it made on the board for action, or, alternatively, why such demand would have been futile; and (2) allege further that it informed the board in writing of the ultimate facts of each cause of action against each defendant or delivered to the corporation or board a true copy of the complaint which plaintiff proposes to file. 

 

In Shields v. Singleton (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1611, the court held that the plaintiff was required to give the board of directors the opportunity to consider and act upon the proposed litigation by presenting to the board the “ultimate facts of each cause of action” and the action that plaintiff wished the board to take to remedy the alleged wrongdoing. (Shields, supra, 15 Cal.App.4th at p. 1619.) Alternatively, a plaintiff may demonstrate that such a demand on the board would have been futile. (Ibid.) 

 

Dr. Tabaie argues every derivative claim asserted by Dr. Malek fails as a matter of law because he failed to provide the companies statutory notice and demand pursuant to Section 800(b)(2). Dr. Tabaie further argues the derivative claims Dr. Malek asserts on behalf of West Regional PC fail as a matter of law for the separate reason that Dr. Malek is not a record shareholder and has not obtained court approval after noticed motion and hearing to assert a derivative claim on that company’s behalf pursuant to Section 800(b)(1).

 

In opposition, Dr. Malek argues contrary to Nominal Cross-Defendants’ argument, Dr. Malek has set forth the necessary and appropriate allegations, contending the futility in notice and demand upon Dr. Tabaie. The court finds the allegations insufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. The FAXC alleges “a demand would have been futile because it would have required Tabaie to assert and pursue claims against himself or against entities solely controlled by him.” (FAXC ¶40.) However, the FAXC fails to allege Dr. Malek gave Nominal Cross-Defendants proper notice of the ultimate facts of each cause of action.

 

The court also notes Nominal Defendants’ contention Dr. Malek is not a record shareholder of West Regional PC. The cross-complaint and the judicially noticed documents do not support Dr. Malek’s contention he is a record shareholder. As to claims regarding West Regional PC, the court agrees Dr. Malek does not have standing.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND if Dr. Malek can sufficiently demonstrate he informed the board in writing of the ultimate facts of each cause of action against each defendant or delivered to the corporation or board a true copy of the complaint he proposed to file.

 

Fifth, Sixth, Seventh, Eighth, Ninth, Tenth, and Eleventh Causes of Action

 

Nominal Defendants demur to the fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action on the grounds the harm alleged in the FACC is direct in nature – not derivative.

 

Nominal Defendants argue each cause of action involves traditional direct shareholder claims – not derivative – because the alleged harm relates to Dr. Malek’s individual interests and purported rights as a shareholder. Nominal Defendants contend Dr. Malek does not seek to recover any monies for the benefit of West Regional PC, West Regional IPA, and Angelus PC.

 

“[A] derivative suit is one in which the shareholder seeks ‘redress of the wrong to the corporation. [Citations.]’ [Citation.] Thus, an action ‘ “is derivative, i.e., in the corporate right, if the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, or to the whole body of its stock and property without any severance or distribution among individual holders, or it seeks to recover assets for the corporation or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.” [Citations.]’ [Citation.] On the other hand, a direct or individual suit by a stockholder ‘ “is a suit to enforce a[ ]right against the corporation which the stockholder possesses as an individual.” [Citation.]’ [Citation.] A direct (as opposed to a derivative) action is maintainable ‘only if the damages [are] not incidental to an injury to the corporation. [Citation.]’ [Citation.]  And ‘ “the two actions are mutually exclusive: i.e., the right of action and recovery belongs to either the shareholders (direct action) or to the corporation (derivative action).”’ [Citation.]” (Bader v. Anderson (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 775, 793.)

 

The court agrees Dr. Malek has not alleged facts seeking redress of the wrong to the corporation. Each cause of action alleges damages incidental to the corporations.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action are SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Third and Twelfth Causes of Action

 

Nominal Defendants demur to the third and Twelfth causes of action on the grounds Dr. Malek does not specify the alleged harm per Nominal Cross-Defendant. Nominal Defendants argue without facts linking the “bank accounts” and “patients” to the corresponding nominal cross-defendant, it is uncertain what harm Dr. Malek seeks to recover on behalf of West Regional PC, West Regional IPA, and Angelus PC versus Lorena PC or Selective Investments LLC as well as uncertain which of those Nominal Cross-Defendants, or combination thereof, allegedly lost the “300 patients” amongst other things. The court finds that the allegations are sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third and twelfth causes of action is OVERRULED on the grounds of uncertainty.
