[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ORDER DEEMING MATTERS IN REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

October 11, 2024

 

On July 21, 2023, plaintiff Financial Credit Network, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Gildarda B. Martinez dba JL Screens & Doors (“Defendant”). On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff served Requests for Admission (Set One) on Defendant. (Abbott Decl., ¶ 3.) On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff sent Defendant a letter attempting to meet and confer regarding the status of Defendant’s responses. (Id., ¶ 4.) No response to the letter was received and no discovery responses have been served. (Ibid.) Plaintiff filed this motion on July 10, 2024.

Where a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants them relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (a)(1)-(2).) The court shall grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

Defendant did not oppose this motion and it does not appear that substantially compliant responses have been served before the hearing. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED.

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED and imposed against Defendant in the reduced amount of $290 for 1 hour at Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rate of $230 and $60 in filing fees, to be paid within 20 days of the date of this Order.

Judge of the Superior Court

I.            INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Francisco Teletor Sente (“Plaintiff”) moves to tax $1,305 in costs claimed by defendants Anthony Griego and Diana Griego, individually and as trustee for the Diana Griego Trust (collectively, “Defendants”) for filing fees in their memorandum of costs filed on August 20, 2024.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

The “prevailing party” is entitled to recover costs for suit in any action or proceeding. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b); Santisas v. Goodin (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 606.) “Prevailing party” includes the party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a)(4).)

Recoverable costs “shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).) “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774.) Verification of the memorandum of costs by the prevailing party’s attorney establishes a prima facie showing that the claimed costs are proper.¿(See Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1267 [“There is no requirement that copies of bills, invoices, statements, or any other such documents be attached to the memorandum.”].) Mere conclusory assertions are insufficient to rebut a prima facie showing by the prevailing party. (Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Service, Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.)  “On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. [Citations.] Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion.” (Ladas, supra, 19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774.)

 

III.        DISCUSSION

Defendants’ memorandum of costs claims 3 first appearance fees associated with their demurrer to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint. Plaintiff argues that Defendants should not be able to recover $1,305 in filing fees without submitting documentation. Plaintiff also argues that Defendants have “included the cost three times for each defendant.”

These arguments are unpersuasive because the verified memorandum is a prima facie showing of allowable costs and does not require any documentation to be attached. (See Jones, supra, 93 Cal.App.3d at p. 266.) In addition, the listed costs are for Defendants’ filing fees for their demurrer and first appearance. Filing fees are allowable as costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 10333.5, subd. (a)(1).) Moreover, the claimed total cost of $1,305 is reasonable because there are three defendants and each defendant must pay a “first paper” filing fee. (Townzen v. Cty. of El Dorado (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358 [awarding each of eleven defendants a filing fee for responsive pleading as costs].) Under the Superior Court of California’s civil fee schedule, each defendant is required to pay an initial filing fee of $435 for their Answer or other first paper filed. (Gov. Code, §§ 70611; 70602.5, subd. (a); 70602.6, subds. (a), (b); 70612.) Therefore, three first appearance fees of $435, which amount to $1,305, is not excessive and the amount will not be taxed.   

IV.        CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s motion to tax costs is DENIED.

I.      INTRODUCTION

On November 2, 2023, plaintiff Nancy Khoa (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant City of Alhambra (“Defendant”). Plaintiff alleges that at approximately 9 a.m. on August 25, 2023, she was walking on a walkway in Almansor Park in Alhambra, California, when an 8 to 10-foot-long tree branch suddenly fell from a large tree and landed directly on her head. Plaintiff asserts causes of action for: (1) dangerous condition on public property pursuant to Government Code section 835 and (2) negligence pursuant to Government Code sections 815.2, 815.4, and 820(a).

On June 24, 2024, Defendant filed this motion for summary judgment. In the alternative, Defendant seeks summary adjudication of the following issues: (1) Defendant did not have actual or constructive notice of any dangerous condition of public property, and (2) there is no negligence. (Motion, p. 2.) Defendant also asks that if the motion is denied, for the Court to find that there is “no legal basis for direct negligence against the City, solely indirect [negligence] from some alleged tort of an agent.” (Motion, p. 2.)

Defendant’s moving papers also include an argument based on Government Code section 831.4, which was withdrawn in its reply brief. (Reply, p. 3.)
          On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief, a response to Defendant’s separate statement, and evidentiary objections.

On October 4, 2024, Defendant filed a reply brief and objections to Plaintiff’s evidence.

II.     LEGAL STANDARDS

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) A motion for summary adjudication shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(2).) 

IV.    EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

          The Court rules only on objections to evidence deemed material to its disposition of the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (q).) All other objections are preserved for appeal. With this in mind, the Court rules on Defendant’s evidentiary objections as follows:

Objection Nos. 1, 3-6 to Declaration of Evin Lambert: Overruled.

Objection Nos. 1-3 to Plaintiff’s Evidence: Sustained.  

V.     DISCUSSION

Plaintiff alleges that a branch (“Subject Branch”) from a Chinese Elm tree (“Subject Tree”) in Defendant’s Almansor Park (“Park”) fell on her head and caused serious injury (“Subject Incident”). The Subject Tree and Subject Branch are alleged to be the “dangerous condition of public property”. (Compl., ¶¶ 8-11.) Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant

carelessly, negligently, and/or recklessly planted, planned, owned, built, installed, reviewed, evaluated, directed, designed, maintained, allowed, permitted, regulated, used, contracted, controlled, serviced, inspected, repaired, modified, altered, monitored, managed, repaired, occupied, improved, constructed, controlled, warned or failed to warn of, removed or failed to remove, and/or supervised the [Park], including the Subject Tree and surrounding area where the Subject Incident occurred.

 

(Compl., ¶ 23.)

A. Dangerous Condition of Public Property

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for dangerous condition of public property fails because Plaintiff cannot establish that it had actual or constructive notice that the Chinese Elm tree was dangerous or that any of its branches were going to fall. (Motion, p. 2.)

To hold a public entity liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition, a plaintiff must prove (1) that the property was a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, (2) that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, (3) that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and (4) that either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition or the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition and sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition. (Gov. Code, § 835.)

A public entity had actual notice of a dangerous condition if it had actual knowledge of the existence of the condition and knew or should have known of its dangerous character. (Gov. Code, § 835.2, subd. (a).) Here, Defendant argues that it “did not have actual notice of any problem, condition, decay or potential in need of repair or prevention of the Subject Chinese Elm [tree].” (Motion, p. 12.) Defendant contends that no defect was obvious or discernable during an inspection conducted 14 months earlier, nor during the bi-weekly ground inspections prior to and on the date of Plaintiff’s injury. Defendant argues that the defective branch was not visible from the ground because it was located at the top of the Subject Tree and hidden by the tree’s canopy.

Defendant submits the Declaration of Javier Velazquez (“Velazquez”). Velazque declares that he started working for Defendant as a Park Worker II in August 2022 and since May 2023, is currently a Park Crew Chief at Almansor Park who supervises Park Workers (Levels I and II) and part-time Park employees. (Velazquez Decl., ¶ 1.) He states that part of his duties and responsibilities involve performing “ground level inspections to look for safety hazards in the Park along the path of travel on and around the 1.1 mile trail that loops through and around the park.” (Id., ¶ 3.) These safety inspections are performed every Friday and Monday between 7 a.m. and 8 a.m., during which Velazquez checks all the trees along the trail for “visible safety hazards, such as broken branches, dry branches, tangled branches, dead limbs, hangers – damaged branches lodged and/or still hanging in the tree – and exterior signs of damage to the trees.” (Id., ¶ 4.) Specifically, Velazquez would “visually inspect[] a tree from approximately 10 feet away”, “look at the base of the tree to see if any roots are lifted or exposed”, then “look for exterior signs of an issue or defect in the trunk of the tree such as cracks around the tree”, and finally “look up for visible signs of damage to the branches.” (Id., ¶ 5.)

On August 21, 2023, Velazquez performed one of his inspections and found that a tree had been blown over during a recent storm (“Hurricane Hillary”). (Id., ¶ 6.) He reported the fallen tree to his supervisor, Vincent Hon, who contacted the tree service contractor, West Coast Arborist (“WCA”), who came and removed the tree along with fallen branches.” Hurricane Hillary continued through the next day, August 22, 2023, and on August 23, 2023, Velazquez performed an additional ground level tree safety inspection of all the trees in the Park, including the Subject Tree. (Id., ¶¶ 7-8.) He looked for broken branches, hangers, and other signs of damage; all visibly broken limbs were addressed and he did not see any concerns about the Subject Tree. (Id., ¶ 8.) On August 25, 2023, from 7:00 a.m. to 8:00 a.m., Velazquez performed a routine ground level tree safety inspection of all the trees, including the Subject Tree, and did not observe any safety hazards. (Id., ¶ 9.) Velazquez states that if he had observed anything that appeared to be a safety hazard on August 21, 23, or 25, 2023, he would have “cautioned off the area and reported it to [his] supervisor so that [his supervisor] could determine if WCA needed to be called to address the hazard.” (Id., ¶ 11.)

After Velazquez was informed of Plaintiff’s incident, he observed that the Subject Branch had fallen from the “very top center of the [Subject Tree].” (Id., ¶ 12.) He “later learned that there was a prior branch failure (“Initial Failure”)” which was not detected during his inspections that week because the Subject Branch was “tucked securely in the upper foliar crown of the Subject Tree.” (Ibid.)

The Court notes that Plaintiff does not contend that Defendant had actual notice of the Subject Tree’s dangerous nature. Instead, Plaintiff only argues that triable issues of fact exist regarding whether Defendant was on constructive notice. A public entity had constructive notice of a dangerous condition only if the plaintiff establishes that the condition had existed for such a period of time and was of such an obvious nature that the public entity, in the exercise of due care, should have discovered the condition and its dangerous character. (Gov. Code, § 835.2, subd. (b).) On the issue of due care, admissible evidence includes, but is not limited to evidence of whether the condition and its dangerous character would have been discovered by an inspection system that was reasonably adequate or whether the public entity maintained and operated an inspection system with due care and did not discover the condition. (Gov. Code, § 835.2, subds. (b)(1)-(b)(2).) “Whether the dangerous condition was obvious and whether it existed for a sufficient period of time are threshold elements to establish a claim of constructive notice.” (Heskel v. City of San Diego (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 313, 317.) “Where the plaintiff fails to present direct or circumstantial evidence as to either element, his claim is deficient as a matter of law.” (Ibid.)

Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot establish that it had constructive notice of the Subject Tree’s dangerous condition because the Subject Tree was professionally inspected and pruned by WCA on June 16, 2022, and reported to be in “good condition.” (Motion, p. 12; Def.’s Ex. 3.) In addition, the Subject Tree was not scheduled to be professionally inspected and pruned by WCA until June 16, 2024, because Defendant and WCA implemented a 2-year inspection period for its trees. (Macias Decl., ¶ 14.) The parties do not dispute that a “two to three year [sic] inspection protocol is reasonable, appropriate, and sufficient” to implement a “safety grid trimming program.” (UMF No. 7.)

Defendant also contends that the alleged dangerous condition was not obvious. Although the Subject Tree was damaged by Hurricane Hillary sometime between August 21 and August 25, 2023, its employees were unable to detect and remove the Subject Branch during the three inspections which were conducted that week (including one which was performed on the morning of Plaintiff’s incident) because the branch was “hidden by virtue of the Subject Tree’s canopy.” (Motion, p. 12.) Defendant refers to photos of the Subject Tree taken on August 25, 2023, which are attached as Exhibits 7 and 8 to Defendant’s compendium of evidence, and contend that they depict a tall, healthy, and upright tree.

Moreover, Joseph McNeil, a board certified master arborist and tree risk assessor, declares that he inspected the Subject Tree on April 4, 2024 and examined photographs of the Subject Tree taken on August 25, 2023, which show it to be “reasonably balanced.” (McNeil Decl., ¶ 12.) McNeil observed necrotic lesions on the Subject Tree, but did not see “any indicator of past failures due to this attribute.” (Id., ¶ 19.) Rather, McNeil opines that “[t]he failure was more likely than not associated with an unknowable combination of the location of the branch at the top of the Subject Tree, the limb attitude, distribution of mass along the limb, possible extension of the limb above or beyond the surrounding foliar canopy, loss of wind protection due to [a] previous failure, and/or notch stress caused by local necrotic lesions, coupled with heavy rain and wind within four days of the incident.” (McNeil Decl., ¶ 22.) McNeil also states that based on photographic evidence, he “cannot say with any certainty whether an experienced professional would have observed any potentially problematic attributes of [the] limb immediately prior to failure that would have led that professional to assign either a high or low potential for failure to that limb.” (McNeil Decl., ¶ 24.)  

          In opposition, Plaintiff argues that a reasonable inspection system would have alerted Defendant to the Subject Tree’s dangerous condition and that Defendant did not employ due care in inspecting and maintaining the Subject Tree, especially in light of the recent storm. Plaintiff argues that dead branches seen from ground level, were a “clear warning of future failures” that could be detected in any reasonable inspections, but that Defendant’s inspections were not reasonable because Velazquez had no training in tree inspection. (Opp., p. 10.) Plaintiff contends that a reasonable inspection would have included an employee arborist or WCA because Defendant knew that at least one tree and multiple branches at the Park had failed during the storm. (Opp., p. 10.)

          Plaintiff submits the declaration of Evin Lambert, a arborist consultant who has performed over 1,000 tree risk assessments. (Compendium of Evidence, Ex. 14.) Lambert opines that the Subject Tree displayed moderate to severe stem cankers causeed by anthracnosem, to which Chinese elm trees are particulary susceptible. (Lambert Decl., ¶ 8.) Lambert states that tree stem cankers are a significant indicator of adverse tree health and “signal[] structural weakness, rendering a tree vulnerable to secondary infections, and increasing the likelihood of limb failure.” (Ibid.) Lambert observes that the Subject Branch that hit Plaintiff’s head exhibited dark tissue around the cracked branch segment, likely related to anthracnose, and that this dark tissue “suggests that the [Subject] [B]ranch suffered from a history of anthracnose infections, which can severely undermine the constiuttion and structural integrity of a tree.” (Id., ¶ 9.) Lambert opines that the Subject Tree had not undergone routine periodic pruning for at least five years and exhibited an “obviously dead, hanging branch” which is a “clear sign” that the Subject Tree did not undergo reasonable inspection or maintenance. (Id., ¶ 12.)

          On reply, Defendant argues that Lambert’s declaration does not raise triable issues of fact regarding the health of the Subject Tree because Lambert fails to explain how they determined that the stem cankers were actually caused by anthracnose. (Reply, pp. 5-6.) However, Lambert sufficiently states that they “personally conducted a level two inspection of the subject Chinese Elm tree . . . on April 4, 2024, which included a 360-degree visual examination of the tree and an assssment for structural defects in the tree’s trunk, crown, and roots.” (Lambert Decl., ¶ 5.) Lambert also states that she assessed the condition of the Subject Branch based on photos. (Lambert Decl., ¶ 10.) The explanation of the grounds for an expert’s opinion in an opposing expert’s declaration “need not be as detailed or extensive as that required in expert testimony presented in support of a summary judgment motion or at trial.” (Garrett v. Howmedica Osteonics Corp. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 173, 187-189.)

Defendants also complain that Lambert does not identify any particular stem canker which caused the Subject Branch to fail. However, Lambert states that there was dark tissue around the cracked branch segment, indicating an anthracnose infection, which undermines the integrity of a tree. Even if no stem canker “caused” the Subject Branch to fall, Lambert opines that they are an “indicator” of tree health.

Defendant also rejects Plaintiff’s suggestion that it was placed on constructive notice of the dangers presented by the Subject Tree due to other fallen trees and branches which had been blown over by Hurricane Hillary. Defendant contends that it would have been forced to shut down all of the parks throughout the city until WCA could inspect each and every tree after a storm. But, given Lambert’s opinion that the Subject Tree obviously displayed health problems, whether Defendant acted with “due care” by conducting only  ground-level inspections of the Subject Tree in the wake of an uncharacteristic summer storm is a triable issue of fact.

          B. Negligence

          Defendant moves for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s negligence cause of action on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to identify a statute imposing liability. This is a misreading of Plaintiff’s complaint, which states that it is brought pursuant to Government Code sections 815.2, 815.4, and 820.

The motion for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action is also improper because Defendant is requesting that the Court
“limit the scope of liability.” A motion for summary adjudication “shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) A party’s “scope of liability” is not included in any of those categories which are appropriate for summary adjudication.

VI.    CONCLUSION

          In light of the foregoing, the motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication is DENIED.
