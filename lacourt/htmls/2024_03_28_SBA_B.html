RULING

            The motions to compel further responses to defendant’s inspection demands, set one are GRANTED.

            Nominal defendants 4223 Associates, 4229 West 101st Street Associates, and 4200 West Century Associates are ordered to serve further responses and to produce responsive documents and to provide a privilege log within 20 days.

BACKGROUND

            On December 22, 2022, plaintiff Cynthia A. Blanks, as trustee of The Cynthia A. Blanks Revocable Trust #2 and as trustee of The Seanjay Ramanand Sharma Revocable Trust filed a complaint against Devanand Sharma aka Frank Martini (and nominal defendants 4200 West Century Associates, 4229 West 101st Street Associates, and 4223 Associates) for (1) declaratory relief and (2) accounting.

            On March 1, 2023, defendant (self-represented) filed a cross-complaint against Cynthia A. Blanks, Seanjay R. Sharma, Kamelia Yagadari, and Satanand Sharma for (1) breach of fiduciary duty, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and dealing, (3) fraud, (4) conversion, (5) constructive trust, (6) accounting, (7) quiet title action, (8) methodone clinics purchased with defendant’s property, and (9) declaratory relief.

            On September 19, 2023, cross-defendant filed a FACC for (1) declaratory relief and (2) accounting.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

45-Day Rule:  This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP § 1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).

Meet-and-Confer Requirement:  The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).

Separate Statement:  Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3). 

Request for Production of Documents

On receipt of a response to an inspection demand, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  CCP § 2031.310(a).  A statement of compliance shall state that the production, inspection, and related activity demanded will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.  CCP § 2031.220.  “A representation of inability to comply with [a] particular demand for inspection . . . shall affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand.  This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.  This statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”  CCP § 2031.230.

A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).  “To establish ‘good cause,’ the burden is on the moving party to show both:  [1] Relevance to the subject matter (e.g., how the information in the documents would tend to prove or disprove some issue in the case); and [2] Specific facts justifying discovery (e.g., why such information is necessary for trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial).  The fact that there is no alternative source for the information sought is an important factor in establishing ‘good cause’ for inspection.  But it is not essential in every case.”  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1495.6 (citations omitted).  “Declarations are generally used to show the requisite ‘good cause’ for an order to compel inspection.  The declarations must contain ‘specific facts’ rather than mere conclusions.”  Id. at 8:1495.7 (citation omitted).  “The declarations may be on information and belief, if necessary.  However, in such cases, the ‘specific facts’ supporting such information and belief (the sources of the information) must also be alleged.”  Id. at 8:1495.8 (citation omitted).  “Most declarations are made by the attorney for the moving party, who is usually more familiar with the relevancy and ‘specific facts’ constituting ‘good cause’ for inspection.”  Id. at 8:1495.9.

“If ‘good cause’ is shown by the moving party, the burden is then on the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure (the same as on motions to compel answers to interrogatories or deposition questions. . . ).”  Id. at 8:1496 (citation omitted).

“Indeed, three other courts have already compelled arbitration in near identical situations, including in another matter against Legends and an HPMC entity involving similar claims filed by a plaintiff likewise represented by Plaintiff’s counsel here. Garuda Labs, Inc., Case No. 21CV379147 (Cal. Super. Ct., Santa Clara Cnty. Oct. 29, 2021) (same), attached as Exhibit B to RJN”  (Moving P&A pg 6:3-12.)

Rule of Court 8.1115 essentially provides that only published California Supreme Court or Cout of Appeal decisions may be cited.  Other cases “must not be cited or relied on by the court or a party in any other action.”  How does this rule apply to (unpublished) Superior Court rulings by other judges?  Is it proper to note how other Superior Court Judges have ruled on similar pleadings?  Perhaps the failure of the Rule to address this question leads counsel to believe that it is proper to argue to the Court that other Superior Court judges have ruled a particular way in cases similar to the one before the Court.  This bench officer does not believe that it is correct to reach such a conclusion, as to do so essentially is turning non-citable rulings into “citations.”

This Court does not make its determination by relying upon the rulings of other Superior Court judges that are not citable authority.  Nor does it think it proper to “cite” those rulings in counsel’s argument.

 

BACKGROUND

On October 27, 2023, plaintiff Latasha Hicks filed a complaint against Hollywood Park Management Company, LLC, Christopher Holland, Garuda Labs, Inc., Legends Hospitality, LLC, and Hollywood Park Management Company II, LLC for (1) harassment in violation of FEHA and (2) IIED.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Garuda Labs, Inc. dba as Instawork requests an order compelling binding arbitration as to itself and to all the defendants and to stay the proceedings.

The complaint alleges that plaintiff was employed through defendant Garuda Labs, Inc. dba Instaworks (“Instaworks”) to work as a cashier/prep cook on the premises at Sofi Stadium of defendant Hollywood Park Management Company.  Plaintiff’s duties included receiving orders, preparing customer orders, and preparing food for games.  Defendant Christopher Holland was plaintiff’s supervisor at the workplace on the premises of HPMC.  FAC, ¶11.  In October 2021, plaintiff commenced her employment with Instaworks.  Within the work environment at Sofi, plaintiff reported to Holland, her designated lead, who provided instructions regarding her assigned station for each shift.  Id., ¶22.  On November 14, 2021, while diligently attending to her daily duties at the cashier station, plaintiff had an abrupt encounter with Holland.  During this encounter, Holland repeatedly inquired about plaintiff’s sister Parvaneh Jones, who was also employed by Instawork and stationed at Sofi.  Plaintiff found Holland’s line of questioning unsettling as he sought information regarding Jones’ age and relationship status.  Despite her discomfort, plaintiff promptly dismissed Holland’s inquiries and continued to fulfill her work responsibilities.  Id., ¶23.

The FAC further alleges that on November 21, 2021, Holland approached Hicks during work and made a request for her to take explicit photographs of her sister Jones.  Id., ¶24.  On November 27, 2021, while engaged in work responsibilities, Holland approached Hicks and inquired if she harbored romantic feelings toward him.  Id., ¶25.  On November 28, 2021, Holland approached Hicks and placed his arm around her neck and shoulders and remarked that she should include explicit pictures of herself to send to him.  Id., ¶26.  On December 5, 2021, while working in the kitchen area, Holland deliberately brushed against Hicks’ buttocks as he walked past her and later in the shift, approached her from behind and startled her by shouting, “boo!”.  Id., ¶27.  In February 2022, plaintiff’s assignment at Sofi came to an end.  Id., ¶29. 

The FAC further alleges that she was misclassified as an independent contractor.  Id., ¶¶32-34.

            Existence of an Enforceable Agreement

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant contends that plaintiff entered into a binding and enforceable arbitration agreement (contained in the Contractor Services Agreement or “CSA”) with defendant Instawork, which, defendant argues, requires her to assert any claims against Instawork, Hollywood Park Management Company, LLC (“HPMC”), Legends Hospitality, LLC, and Hollywood Park Management Company II, LLC related to her relationship with Instawork and services provided through the Instawork Platform in binding arbitration.

Defendant explains that it is a technology company that develops software applications connecting businesses with hourly workers (“pros”) for short-term work on longer-term jobs.  Defendant asserts that “pros” like plaintiff can use the Instawork App to access shifts and other opportunities.  Through the app, third-party businesses select and negotiate directly with Instawork pros to set the work to be performed, as well as the schedules, locations, and other details for the work.  To book a shift, a pro downloads the Instawork App.  Once the app is downloaded, the pro creates a new account.  To do so, the pro provides their first and last name, telephone number, and email address.  Before tapping “Next” to complete the process of creating a new account, the pro is expressly informed, “[b]y tapping Next, you agree to our Privacy Policy and Terms of Use[.]”  The pro must then following the onboarding process and complete all required documentation before booking a shift.  Once the pro taps to book a shift, they are prompted to and must tap to review the CSA to continue.  At the end of the CSA, to continue, the pro must then tap “I accept” to agree to its terms.  Simon Olavarria decl.

Defendant presents evidence that on February 23, 2023, plaintiff Hicks entered into the CSA with Instawork in accordance with the process set forth above, by accessing the app, scrolling through the entirety of the CSA, and then tapping “I accept.”  Olaverria decl., ¶¶9-10, Exh. B.

Under the “Mutual Arbitration and Dispute Resolution” provision at para. 11, the CSA states, in part:  “The Parties mutually agree to resolve any and all disputes between them or between You and any of Instawork’s parents, subsidiaries, affiliates including Partners, successors, assigns, officers, directors, employees, and/or agents exclusively through final, binding, and individual arbitration instead of filing a lawsuit in court . . . .”  CSA, at 11.1. 

Defendant contends that the plain language of the CSA covers claims against co-defendants and that HPMC, Legends, and HPMC II also have standing as third-party beneficiaries and under equitable estoppel.  Legends is a “Partner” under the CSA.  Olaverria, ¶11.

The joinder by Legends asserts that it is subject to the arbitration agreement as a third-party beneficiary, agent of the signatory, and/or because the claims against defendant are covered by the arbitration agreement.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that Instawork does not have standing to argue on behalf of the other defendants.  Plaintiff contends that Instawork has not shown other defendants were third-party beneficiaries or that equitable estoppel applies as this case does not involve breach of contract.  Plaintiff also argues that the subject arbitration agreement is void per se under the FAA, 9 USC 402(a), the “Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act” (“EFAA”), which renders a pre-dispute arbitration agreement unenforceable as to cases alleging sexual harassment.  She asserts that she suffered harassment prior to the Act going into effect, but signed Instawork’s arbitration agreement after the Act’s effective date.

            In reply, defendant reiterates its arguments.  Defendant further argues that according to plaintiff’s allegations, the conduct arose and accrued before the EFAA went into effect on March 3, 2022.

 

The court rules as follows:  The court finds that an arbitration agreement exists between plaintiff and defendant Instawork, which plaintiff does not refute, that covers her claims.  Further, plaintiff agreed to resolve any and all disputes between herself and any of Instawork’s Partners, which would include defendant Legends, and thus, Legends is a third-party beneficiary.

As to the doctrine of equitable estoppel, “a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are ‘intimately founded in and intertwined’ with the underlying contract obligations.”  JSM Tuscany, LLC v. Superior Court (2011) 193 Cal. App. 4th 1222, 1237.  The doctrine applies:  (1) when the signatory must rely on the terms of the written agreement containing the arbitration clause in asserting its claims against the nonsignatory; or (2) when the signatory alleges “substantially interdependent and concerted misconduct” by the nonsignatory and a signatory and the alleged misconduct is “founded in or intimately connected with the obligations of the underlying agreement.”  Goldman v. KPMG, LLP (2009) 173 Cal. App. 4th 209, 218-219.  The first situation exists “‘when the signatory to a written agreement containing an arbitration clause “must rely on the terms of the written agreement in asserting [its] claims” against the nonsignatory.’”  Id. at 218 (citation omitted). 

The court notes that the claims against HPMC and HPMC II and Legends are based on the same facts and inherently inseparable from the arbitrable claims against signatory and moving party Instawork.  See Garcia v. Pexco, LLC (2017) 11 Cal. App. 5th 782, 786, where the plaintiff sued both the staffing agency who staffed her and the company where she was staffed, alleging the same causes of action against the staffing agency and the company without distinguishing between the defendants.  Plaintiff asserts the same causes of action against each of the defendants collectively as her “employer.”  Although disputed by Instawork, plaintiff alleges that defendants were “joint employers.”  FAC, ¶¶36-42.  The allegations against HPMC, HPMC II, and Legends are intertwined with Instawork’s and are founded in the alleged employment relationship.  Plaintiff alleges that she performed services for each and every one of defendants, and to the mutual benefit of all defendants, and all defendants shared control of plaintiff as an employee, either directly or indirectly, and the manner in which defendants’ business was and is conducted.  See id., ¶41.

As to the EFAA, the law applies “with respect to any dispute or claim that arises or accrues on or after the date of enactment of this Act,” it does not apply retroactively, and plaintiff’s causes of action accrued before it was enacted on March 3, 2022.  See Murrey v. Superior Court (2023) 87 Cal. App. 5th 1223, 1230.

The motion is thus GRANTED.

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.  The case is STAYED.

Standing

First cause of action for recognition of foreign final judgment to the UFCMJRA, CCP §1713, et seq. and second cause of action for recognition of foreign final judgment pursuant to international comity

CCP §1715 states:  “(a) Except as otherwise provided in subdivision (b), this chapter applies to a foreign-country judgment to the extent that the judgment both:  (1) Grants or denies recovery of a sum of money.  (2) Under the law of the foreign country where rendered, is final, conclusive, and enforceable.  (b) This chapter does not apply to a foreign-country judgment, even if the judgment grants or denies recovery of a sum of money, to the extent that the judgment is any of the following:  . . . . (2) A fine or other penalty. . . . (c) A party seeking recognition of a foreign-country judgment has the burden of establishing that the foreign-country judgment is entitled to recognition under this chapter.” 

Defendants argue that the August 2, 2017 “money judgment” that plaintiff seeks recognition of is not an actual judgment granting monetary recovery.  Defendant contends that as alleged, the “money judgment” ordered Li to return shares within a certain number of days.  Further, if she did not return the shares, although the judgment included the potential for monetary recovery by including a formula for calculating an amount owed per each share, it also included an unenforceable penalty—“multiplier of 2.24” and awards court costs and fees which are punitive in that there was no contractual agreement for payment of such in the underlying contracts.  Defendants further argue that the court does not have jurisdiction to enforce a judgment requiring the return of shares of a publicly listed Chinese company.

The court finds that the allegations are sufficient and as to whether the 2.23 multiplier is a “penalty” does not appear on the face of the complaint.

Third cause of action for statutory fraudulent transfer – actual intent under Civil Code 3439.04, fourth cause of action for statutory fraudulent transfer – constructive intent under Civil Code 3439.05, and fifth cause of action for common law fraudulent transfer

            Under Civil Code §3439.04, “(a) A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is voidable as to a creditor, whether the creditor’s claim arose before or after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred, if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation as follows:  (1) With actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor. (2) Without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation, and the debtor either: (A) Was engaged or was about to engage in a business or a transaction for which the remaining assets of the debtor were unreasonably small in relation to the business or transaction. (B) Intended to incur, or believed or reasonably should have believed that the debtor would incur, debts beyond the debtor’s ability to pay as they became due.

            (b) In determining actual intent . . . consideration may be given, among other factors, to any or all of the following:  (1) Whether the transfer or obligation was made to an insider.  (2) Whether the debtor retained possession or control of the property transferred after the transfer.  (3) Whether the transfer or obligation was disclosed or concealed.  (4) Whether before the transfer was made or obligation was incurred, the debtor had been sued or threatened with suit.  (5) Whether the transfer was of substantially all the debtor’s assets.  (6) Whether the debtor absconded.  (7) Whether the debtor removed or concealed assets.  (8) Whether the value of the consideration received by the debtor was reasonably equivalent to the value of the asset transferred or the amount of the obligation incurred.  (9) Whether the debtor was insolvent or became insolvent shortly after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred.  (10) Whether the transfer occurred shortly before or after a substantial debt was incurred.  (11) Whether the debtor transferred the essential assets of the business to a lienor that transferred the assets to an insider of the debtor.

(c) A creditor making a claim for relief under subdivision (a) has the burden of proving the elements of the claim for relief by a preponderance of the evidence.”

Under Civil Code §3439.05, “(a) A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is voidable as to a creditor whose claim arose before the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation and the debtor was insolvent at that time or the debtor became insolvent as a result of the transfer or obligation.

(b) A creditor making a claim for relief under subdivision (a) has the burden of proving the elements of the claim for relief by a preponderance of the evidence.”

Under Civil Code §3439.09, “A cause of action with respect to a transfer or obligation under this chapter is extinguished unless action is brought pursuant to subdivision (a) of Section 3439.07 or levy made as provided in subdivision (b) or (c) of Section 3439.07:

(a) Under paragraph (1) of subdivision (a) of Section 3439.04, not later than four years after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred or, if later, not later than one year after the transfer or obligation was or could reasonably have been discovered by the claimant.

(b) Under paragraph (2) of subdivision (a) of Section 3439.04 or Section 3439.05, not later than four years after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred.

(c) Notwithstanding any other provision of law, a cause of action under this chapter with respect to a transfer or obligation is extinguished if no action is brought or levy made within seven years after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred.”

            Defendants argue that the UVTA claims are time-barred because plaintiff allege that the judgment became “final and immediately enforceable” on December 12, 2017 and that the dates of transfer for the San Marino and Palos Verdes properties occurred on February 15, 2016 and June 27, 2017, respectively.  See Request for Judicial Notice, Exhs. A and B.  The complaint was not filed until August 18, 2023, more than 5 ½ years after December 12, 2017, and not within four years.  Defendants also argue that the 5th cause of action for common law fraudulent transfer is barred by the three-year statute of limitations under CCP §338.  Defendants contend that plaintiff has not alleged any facts to “suggest delayed discovery of the transfers of the property.”

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the fraudulent transfer claims are not time-barred under the delayed discovery doctrine. 

            The court finds that the third through fifth causes of action appear to be barred by the statute of limitations as the complaint was filed more than four years after the judgment became final and the properties were transferred to defendant Wei Lai Development.  The court also finds that plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged delayed discovery.  A plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his claim would be barred without the benefit of the discovery rule must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.  See CAMSI IV v. Hunter Technology Corp. (1991) 230 Cal. App. 3d 1525, 1536-37. “Mere conclusory assertions that delay in discovery was reasonable are insufficient and will not enable the complaint to withstand general demurrer.”  Id.

            The demurrer is thus SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the third through fifth causes of action.

 

ORDER

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action and SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the third and fifth causes of action.

Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion to expunge lis pendens is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

On August 11, 2023, plaintiff Enjoyor Technology Co., Ltd. filed a petition against Xin Li and Wei Lai Development LLC for recognition of foreign money judgment and complaint for fraudulent transfer.

            The proofs of service filed August 18, 2023 indicate that defendants were personally served on August 18.

            On August 18, 2023, plaintiff filed a notice of lis pendens.

            On September 18, 2023, defendant Xin Lu filed a declaration of demurring of moving party in support of automatic extension.

On September 18, 2023, defaults were entered against defendants.

            On October 18, 2023, defendants filed a motion to expunge lis pendens.

            On October 19, 2023, defendants Xin Li and Wei Lai Development LLC filed a demurrer.

            On November 6 and 15, 2023, defendants filed a notice of withdrawal of the motion to expunge lis pendens.

On December 22, 2023, the court took judicial notice of defense counsel’s declaration of in support of automatic extension and plaintiff’s request for entry of default filed after and found that plaintiff’s request should have been rejected.  On its own motion, the court set aside the defaults against defendants.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

‘“A lis pendens is a recorded document giving constructive notice that an action has been filed affecting title or right to possession of the real property described in the notice.”’  Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.4th 642, 647 (citation omitted).  Any time after a notice of pendency of action has been recorded, any party or nonparty with an interest in the real property affected thereby may move the court in which the action is pending to expunge the notice.  CCP §405.30.

A court must order the notice expunged if the court finds any of the following:  the pleading on which the notice is based does not contain a real property claim (CCP §405.31); the claimant has not established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim (CCP §405.32); or, the real property claim has probable validity, but adequate relief can be secured by the giving of an undertaking.  CCP §405.33.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants request an order expunging the notices of lis pendens recorded against APN #5331-023-014 and APN #7582-0001-0009.  The court notes that plaintiff filed a notice of lis pendens on August 19, 2023 against defendants’ property located at 3300 Palos Verdes Dr. W, Rancho Palos Verdes but there is no notice of lis pendens as to the San Marino property.

            Real property claim

Under CCP §405.31, the court shall order the notice expunged if the court finds that the pleading on which the notice is based does not contain a real property claim.  “In making this determination, the court must engage in a demurrer-like analysis.  Rather than analyzing whether the pleading states any claim at all, as on a general demurrer, the court must undertake the more limited analysis of whether the pleading states a real property claim.  Review involves only a review of the adequacy of the pleading and normally should not involve evidence from either side, other than possibly that which may be judicially noticed as on a demurrer.  Therefore, review of an expungement order under section 405.31 is limited to whether a real property claim has been properly pled by the claimant.”  Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.4th 642, 647- 648.

“’Real property claim’ means the cause or causes of action in a pleading which would, if meritorious, affect (a) title to, or the right to possession of, specific real property . . . .”  CCP §405.4. 

Defendants argue that plaintiff has no real property claim; rather, the complaint is a “generalized claim for money.”  Defendants assert that there is no connection between the judgment plaintiff seeks to enforce and the two California properties.  Further, “[t]he fact that someone buys property with stolen money does not make the victim the owner of that property as a matter of real property law.  It merely entitles the victim to pursue the thief and to recover a money judgment, perhaps seeking to attach the property along the way . . . .”  Lewis v. Superior Court (1994) 30 Cal. App. 4th 1850, 1863 [addressing a constructive trust cause of action].  Also, defendants contend, “[a] lis pendens is not a shortcut method of attachment for unsecured creditors.”  Deane v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal. App. 3d 292, 297 [cause of action for constructive trust].

In opposition, plaintiff argues that a fraudulent transfer of real property is a real property claim, citing to Kirkeby, supra.

Using a demurrer-like analysis, the court rules as follows:

The third through fifth causes of action are for fraudulent transfer.  Plaintiff alleges that the transfers of Li’s interest in the various “Cash and Real Properties to Defendants” are voidable under the UFTA.  See Complaint, ¶96.  Plaintiff requests an order nullifying and voiding the transfers of Li’s interest in the properties so that they “remain with Defendant Li and are entitled to be included in a judgment against Defendant Li and entities in which she controls, for the value of the asset(s) that are the subject of the transfers or the amount necessary to satisfy a judgment (e.g., the Money Judgment) against Defendant Li.”  Id., ¶97.  Plaintiff also alleges that it believes that the transfers were made to make Li appear judgment proof to prevent plaintiff from satisfying the money judgments against her.  Id., ¶92.  Under Kirkeby, “a fraudulent conveyance action seeking a avoidance of a transfer under [Civil Code 3439.07(a)(1)] clearly ‘affects title to, or the right to possession of’ real property and is therefore a real property claim for the purposes of the lis pendens statutes.”  33 Cal. 4th at 649.  The Kirkeby court found that the Lewis case (cited by defendants) was inapposite.  See also Hunting World, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal. App. 4th 67, 73 (an action to set aside a fraudulent conveyance fell within the “clear wording of the ‘real property claim’ prong of lis pendens law.”).  Thus, the court finds that the third through fifth causes of action are “real property claims.”  The court notes, however, that they have not been adequately pled.  See the ruling on demurrer to complaint.

            Probable validity of claims

“Unlike other motions, the burden is on the party opposing the motion to expunge—i.e., the claimant-plaintiff—to establish the probable validity of the underlying claim.  The claimant-plaintiff must establish the probable validity of the claim by a preponderance of the evidence.”  Howard S. Wright Constr. Co. v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal. App. 4th 314, 319 (citation omitted).

            Under Civil Code §3439.04, “(a) A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is voidable as to a creditor, whether the creditor’s claim arose before or after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred, if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation as follows:  (1) With actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor. (2) Without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation, and the debtor either: (A) Was engaged or was about to engage in a business or a transaction for which the remaining assets of the debtor were unreasonably small in relation to the business or transaction. (B) Intended to incur, or believed or reasonably should have believed that the debtor would incur, debts beyond the debtor’s ability to pay as they became due.

            (b) In determining actual intent . . . consideration may be given, among other factors, to any or all of the following:  (1) Whether the transfer or obligation was made to an insider.  (2) Whether the debtor retained possession or control of the property transferred after the transfer.  (3) Whether the transfer or obligation was disclosed or concealed.  (4) Whether before the transfer was made or obligation was incurred, the debtor had been sued or threatened with suit.  (5) Whether the transfer was of substantially all the debtor’s assets.  (6) Whether the debtor absconded.  (7) Whether the debtor removed or concealed assets.  (8) Whether the value of the consideration received by the debtor was reasonably equivalent to the value of the asset transferred or the amount of the obligation incurred.  (9) Whether the debtor was insolvent or became insolvent shortly after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred.  (10) Whether the transfer occurred shortly before or after a substantial debt was incurred.  (11) Whether the debtor transferred the essential assets of the business to a lienor that transferred the assets to an insider of the debtor.

(c) A creditor making a claim for relief under subdivision (a) has the burden of proving the elements of the claim for relief by a preponderance of the evidence.”

Under Civil Code §3439.05, “(a) A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is voidable as to a creditor whose claim arose before the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation and the debtor was insolvent at that time or the debtor became insolvent as a result of the transfer or obligation.

(b) A creditor making a claim for relief under subdivision (a) has the burden of proving the elements of the claim for relief by a preponderance of the evidence.”

 

            Defendants argue that the fraudulent transfer claims are not “probably valid” because they are not even legally sufficient as they are barred by the statute of limitations; plaintiff has not alleged delayed discovery; and plaintiff cannot meet its burden of producing any admissible evidence tending to show any of the elements.  Defendants also argue that there are no “badges of fraud” in that no fraudulent intent exists.  Defendants assert that the value of the ownership of the remaining parcel of real property remains in Li’s hands as she owns all of the membership units of the entity that owns the real property.  Also, when Li quitclaimed the title to Wei Lai, she did not receive insufficient value—by definition, Li got the exact same value as she parted with, i.e., the membership units in Wei Lai, which she has, she owns, she continues to own, and which she has no intention of transferring.  Li states in her declaration that at the time she purchased the first of two parcels of real estate, her real estate broker told her it was a good idea in the U.S. to place ownership of the property in the name of an entity that she owned.  She relied on her advice and transferred ownership of both parcels to the LLC that her broker helped her establish. 

            In opposition, plaintiff presents no evidence to support the probable validity of the claims.  Instead, plaintiff relies solely on the verified complaint.  As found in the demurrer, the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action. 

            The court rules as follows:  The court finds that plaintiff has not met its burden.  Even if the court were to consider the verified complaint, plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory under the third through fifth causes of action and do not address the factors as to “actual intent.”

The motion is thus GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion to expunge lis pendens is GRANTED.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion for Reasonable Attorney’s Fees and Costs Under CCP §405.38

            The court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On August 11, 2023, plaintiff Enjoyor Technology Co., Ltd. filed a petition against Xin Li and Wei Lai Development LLC for recognition of foreign money judgment and complaint for fraudulent transfer.

            The proofs of service filed August 18, 2023 indicate that defendants were personally served on August 18.

            On August 18, 2023, plaintiff filed a notice of lis pendens.

            On September 18, 2023, defaults were entered against defendants.

            On September 18, 2023, defendant Xin Lu filed a declaration of demurring of moving party in support of automatic extension.

            On October 18, 2023, defendants filed a motion to expunge lis pendens.

            On October 19, 2023, defendants Xin Li and Wei Lai Development LLC filed a demurrer.

            On November 6 and 15, 2023, defendants filed a notice of withdrawal of the motion to expunge lis pendens.

            On December 22, 2023, the court took judicial notice of defense counsel’s declaration of in support of automatic extension and plaintiff’s request for entry of default filed after and found that plaintiff’s request should have been rejected.  On its own motion, the court set aside the defaults against defendants.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “At any time after notice of pendency of action has been recorded, any party, or any nonparty with an interest in the real property affected thereby, may apply to the court in which the action is pending to expunge the notice.”  CCP §405.30.  In a motion to expunge a notice of lis pendens, the claimant who filed the lis pendens has the burden of proof.  Thus, that claimant, in opposing the motion to expunge the lis pendens, must demonstrate the following:  (1) the action affects title to or right of possession of the real property described in the notice; (2) in so far as the said notice is concerned, the party recording the notice has commenced the action for a proper purpose and in good faith; and (3) the probable validity of the real property claim by a preponderance of the evidence.  Hunting World, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal. App. 4th 67, 70; see also CCP §405.30, et seq.  The court must order the notice expunged if it finds the pleading on which the notice is based does not contain a real property claim (CCP §405.31) or the claimant has not established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim (CCP §405.32). 

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Enjoyor Technology Co., Ltd. requests an award of attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $10,338.75 against defendants.

            Plaintiff argues that under the “practice approach” adopted by Castro v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 1010, it is the prevailing party on defendants’ motion to expunge lis pendens (filed on October 18, 2023).  Plaintiff argues that it is the prevailing party because it “has realized its litigation objectives” as the motion was withdrawn and the lis pendens remains on defendants’ property.  Plaintiff also asserts that defendants would not have prevailed anyway.

 

            The court rules as follows:

“The court shall direct that the party prevailing on any motion under this chapter be awarded the reasonable attorney’s fees and costs of making or opposing the motion unless the court finds that the other party acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of attorney's fees and costs unjust.”  CCP §405.38.  “The statute . . . does not define prevailing party.”  Castro v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 1010, 1018. 

In Castro, the appellate court determined that “the withdrawal of an improperly recorded lis pendens, while a motion to expunge is pending, achieves the litigation objectives of the moving party by removing the cloud on the title and permitting the property’s transfer.  The moving party thus has obtained the relief that the trial court would have granted had it ruled on the motion.  That is not to say, however, that statutory fees under section 405.38 are automatically awarded, only that statutory fees should not automatically be denied.  Thus, . . . notwithstanding the withdrawal of the lis pendens, in order to award statutory attorney fees to the moving party, the trial court must determine the merits of the pending motion.”  Id.  “Because section 405.38 does not define prevailing party or expressly authorize or bar recovery of attorney fees in the event the lis pendens is withdrawn before a ruling on a motion to expunge, we adopt the practical approach to determine the prevailing party for purposes of awarding attorney fees.  Under the practical approach, a trial court must determine whether the moving party is the prevailing party under section 405.38 by analyzing the extent to which each party has realized its litigation objectives.  To determine litigation objectives . . . the court must consider and decide whether the moving party would have prevailed on the motion.”  Id. at 1022-23.

The court finds that the “practical approach” under Castro is inapplicable considering the procedural posture.  As stated above, the motion to expunge was withdrawn; thus, there is no “pending motion.”  The hearing on the motion was taken off calendar.  The court thus made no determination as to the merits, including, as to whether the complaint on which the notice to expunge is based contains a real property claim or whether plaintiff has established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim.  The court also did not make any finding as to whether defendants acted with substantial justification in filing the motion to expunge.  In any event, it appears that withdrawal of the motion had nothing to do with the merits as defendants refiled the motion when they were no longer in default.

            Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.

 

ORDER

            The motion for attorney’s fees is DENIED.

            Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

            The demurrer to the complaint, and first and second causes of action in the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 16 and prayer for punitive damages.

BACKGROUND

On September 6, 2023, Jean C. McDonald filed a complaint against Mardu Inc. for (1) elder abuse and (2) negligence.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendant Mardu Inc. demurs to the complaint on the ground that plaintiff lacks capacity to sue and to the first and second causes of action on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

The complaint alleges that plaintiff is married to Stu McDonald (“husband”).  In 2017, her husband’s daughter Kelley Avery contracted with defendant Mardu to provide senior home care services for husband.  Complaint, ¶6.  The scope of Mardu’s services was later expanded to include care for plaintiff, at around the time she started to exhibit diminished mental capacities.  Husband started to exhibit diminished mental capacities around 2017.  Id., ¶8.

            Capacity to sue

Defendant argues that plaintiff lacks capacity to sue because plaintiff alleges that she suffers from a diminished mental capacity.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that she does not lack capacity but can amend to include a legal representative. 

The court finds that it appears that plaintiff lacks capacity to sue.

 

First cause of action for elder abuse

The complaint alleges that around May 2023, husband acted violently towards plaintiff, causing emotional and physical harm, and did so in the presence of one or more caregivers employed by Mardu.  Complaint, ¶11.  The caregivers who witnessed husband’s acts of violence towards plaintiff reported such acts to Avery and Mardu.  Mardu intentionally concealed husband’s conduct from plaintiff’s daughter Debbie Martin, who was known to Mardu.  Id., ¶12.  Defendants’ conduct constitutes neglect as defined in Welf. & Inst. Code §15610.57.  Id., ¶13.  

In order to state a cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act, “a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.” Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 789.  

“[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.”  Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407 (citing Covenant Care).  “Allegations of misconduct directed against one defendant . . . do not state a cause of action against another defendant against whom the allegations of misconduct are not directed.”  Id. at 410 (citation omitted).  “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence.”  Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal. App. 4th 336, 347 (citation omitted).  Citing to the content of the definition of “neglect” in Welf. and Inst. Code §15610.57(b), the Supreme Court stated, “[w]hat they each seem to contemplate is the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder’s basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.”  Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148, 58.  “What seems beyond doubt is that the Legislature enacted a scheme distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together – claims of professional negligence and neglect.”  Id. at 159.

Defendant argues that plaintiff has failed to plead with particularity.  Further, defendant asserts, plaintiff has not alleged how Mardu violated the statute, including that defendant or any of its caregivers failed to exercise a degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.  Rather, plaintiff alleges that defendant provided assistance with personal hygiene or in the provision of food, clothing, or shelter.  Complaint, ¶6.  Plaintiff does not allege that defendant failed to provide medical care, or to protect plaintiff from health and safety hazards, or to prevent malnutrition or dehydration, or to assist plaintiff in managing finances, for example.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient.  Plaintiff also contends that “any fault for the complaint’s alleged factual deficiencies falls squarely upon Mardu, who has consistently withheld vitally relevant documents.”  Plaintiff asserts that she propounded discovery requests in November 2023 but did not receive responsive and redacted (based on husband’s HIPAA privilege) documents until end of February 2024 and on March 12, 2024.

In reply, defendant denies improperly withholding documents.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action for elder abuse.  The allegations are conclusory and are not plead with the requisite particularity and do not support reckless neglect.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first cause of action.

 

Second cause of action for negligence

Defendant argues that the allegations fail to state a claim because plaintiff alleges no facts demonstrating why or how defendant had a duty to report to plaintiff’s daughter, a non-party, the unspecified conduct of plaintiff’s husband.  Further, defendant contends, plaintiff has not alleged how defendant somehow caused or contributed to plaintiff’s injuries or what actual harm occurred.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient as to duty and causation.  The allegations do not support that defendant had a duty to report to plaintiff’s daughter (in addition to reporting to Mardu and husband’s daughter Avery) or that failure to do so caused plaintiff’s injuries or damage.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the second cause of action.

 

Motion to Strike

Defendant requests that the court strike para. 16 and prayer for punitive damages.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

Civil Code §3294(b) states: “With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

A claim for punitive damages may not be based on conclusory allegations of oppression, fraud or malice, but instead must be based on factual allegations which support such a conclusion.  See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-42 (Court of Appeal issued a peremptory writ directing trial court to issue order striking plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages because “[t]he sole basis for seeking punitive damages are . . . conclusory allegations” which were “devoid of any factual assertions supporting a conclusion [defendants] acted with oppression, fraud, or malice”).

Evidence of negligence, gross negligence, or even recklessness is not sufficient to support an award of punitive damages.  Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 1188, 1210-11.

Defendant argues that plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to support the allegations of malice, oppression, or fraud or to identify any specific factual “despicable conduct.”

In opposition, plaintiff contends that the allegations are sufficient.

The court finds that there are no allegations pled to support a claim for punitive damages, much less a claim for elder abuse, as stated above.  There are no allegations to show malice, oppression, or fraud.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ORDER

The demurrer to the complaint and first and second causes of action in the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to para. 16 and prayer for punitive damages.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

BACKGROUND

            On December 20, 2023, U.S. Bank National Association dba U.S. Bank Equipment Finance filed a complaint against Kate Holding, Inc. and Anthony John Logan for (1) breach of written agreement, (2) breach of personal guaranty, (3) indebtedness, (4) account stated, (5) unjust enrichment, (6) claim and delivery, and (7) conversion.

            On February 27, 2024, a default was entered against Kate Holding.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            CCP §512.010 sets forth the requirements in an application for writ of possession.  CCP §512.010(b) states: “The application shall be executed under oath and shall include all of the following:   (1) A showing of the basis of the plaintiff's claim and that the plaintiff is entitled to possession of the property claimed. If the basis of the plaintiff's claim is a written instrument, a copy of the instrument shall be attached.

            (2) A showing that the property is wrongfully detained by the defendant, of the manner in which the defendant came into possession of the property, and, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the reason for the detention.

            (3) A particular description of the property and a statement of its value.

            (4) A statement, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the location of the property and, if the property, or some part of it, is within a private place which may have to be entered to take possession, a showing that there is probable cause to believe that such property is located there.

            (5) A statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to a statute; or seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff; or, if so seized, that it is by statute exempt from such seizure.”

CCP §512.040(b) states:  “The writ will be issued if the court finds that the plaintiff's claim is probably valid and the other requirements for issuing the writ are established.  The hearing is not for the purpose of determining whether the claim is actually valid.  The determination of the actual validity of the claim will be made in subsequent proceedings in the action and will not be affected by the decision at the hearing on the application for the writ.”

CCP §511.090 states:  A “claim has probable validity where it is more likely than not that plaintiff will obtain judgment against defendant on that claim.”

            Further, “[a]t the hearing, the court shall make its determinations upon the basis of the pleadings and other papers in the record; but, upon good cause shown, the court may receive and consider additional evidence and authority produced at the hearing or may continue the hearing for the production of such additional evidence, oral or documentary, or the filing of other affidavits or points and authorities.”  CCP §512.050.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff seeks a writ of possession against each defendant for two 2016 Freightliner Cascadia 125 6x4 truck tractors.

            The complaint alleges under the first cause of action that on May 20, 2022, plaintiff and defendant Kate Holding, Inc. entered into an Equipment Finance Agreement for two 2016 Freightliner Cascadia 125 6x4 Sleeper Truck Tractors, VIN: 1FUJGLD56GLHF8884 and VIN: 3AKJGLBG9GSGW1773, for which defendant was to make 45 monthly payments of $2,937.15.  Complaint, ¶7, Exh.1. Defendant breached the terms of the EFA by failing to make the monthly payment due June 26, 2023 or any payment due thereafter pursuant to the EFA.  Plaintiff accelerated the balance, declaring the full amount due and owing.  Id., ¶9.  Based upon the uncured default in the EFA, Kate Holding is liable to plaintiff for the total amount outstanding of $105,036.96 together with interest at the rate of 12%.  Id., ¶10.  Under the second cause of action, plaintiff alleges that defendant Anthony John Logan breached the personal guaranty.  Id., ¶¶13-16.

            The court finds that the applications comply with the requirements set forth in CCP §512.010.  See Terrica Vorvick decl.  Plaintiff has met its burden to establish the probable validity of its claim. 

            Plaintiff is not required to post an undertaking as defendants have no positive interest in the vehicle.  There is currently due the sum of $105,036.96 plus interest, and the fair market value of the sleeper trucks is approximately $70,000.  See Vorvick decl.  Pursuant to CCP §515.020, the amount of defendants’ undertaking is set at $105,000. 

            The applications are GRANTED.
