DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV02046    Hearing Date: January 8, 2025    Dept: 73

01/08/2025

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf M. Treu, Judge presiding

 

Claire Donahue v. 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP, et al. (24STCV02046)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Grand Park Law Group, A.P.C.

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Nicolson Law Group, P.C.

 

motion to compel further responses to requests for production (filed 08/06/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Request for Production, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is hereby ordered to provide further verified responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,360.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff Claire Donahue (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendants 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP; LA Urban Living LLC; Jeclim LLC; Jeremy Miller; Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff was an occupant of real property located at 427 S

Mariposa, Unit 511 Los Angeles, CA (the “Property”) as a lessee/tenant under a written lease

with Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that on April 6, 2020, Plaintiff reported an active leak and

water damage in the bedroom ceiling to management, who acknowledged the issue but failed to

conduct repairs despite multiple follow-ups. On January 25, 2022, Plaintiff discovered additional

mold growth in the bedroom after heavy rains. Plaintiff informed the building manager and

requested repairs. During Plaintiff’s tenancy, Defendants failed to maintain the Property in a safe

and habitable condition. Plaintiff was eventually forced to vacate the Property due to health

issues while living in the Property. Defendants constructively evicted Plaintiff from the

Property—having refused to make the required repairs to the Property and ensure the Property be

made safe and habitable, and after Plaintiff told Defendants that Plaintiff suspected that the

fungal growth at the Property was making Plaintiff sick.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of the implied warranty of habitability;

2.      Nuisance;

3.      Breach of contract;

4.      Negligence;

5.      Negligent misrepresentation;

6.      Constructive eviction;

7.      Premises liability;

8.      Fraud; and

9.      Intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On August 6, 2024, Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP (“Defendant” or “Mariposa”) filed a motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to requests for production, set one, and request for monetary sanctions, arguing:

·       Defendant properly served Plaintiff electronically with its Requests for Production of Documents, Set One (“RPDs”) on March 22, 2024.

·       On April 25, 2024, Defendant sent an email to Plaintiff’s counsel to confer about Plaintiff’s failure to respond.

·       On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel claimed that Defendant failed to properly serve their written discovery responses.

·       On April 30, 2024, Defendant granted Plaintiff an extension of time to provide Defendant with her responses based on a service date of April 30, 2024, making Plaintiff’s responses due on or before June 4, 2024.

·       On June 7, 2024, after still not receiving Plaintiff’s responses, Defendant again granted Plaintiff an extension of time to provide Defendant with her responses until June 14, 2024.

·       On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff electronically served Plaintiff’s Responses to Defendant’s Requests for Production. However, these responses were not tailored specifically to Defendant’s RPDs. Further, Plaintiff produced only one document in response to Defendant’s 47 requests.

·       On July 29, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff’s counsel a formal meet and confer letter identifying the deficiencies in Plaintiff’s responses. Defendant requested code-compliant responses by Friday 2, 2024.

·       On Monday August 5, 2024, having not received any communications from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant wrote to Plaintiff’s counsel to arrange an informal discovery conference and an extension of time to file any required motions to compel further responses.

·       On Tuesday August 6, 2024, having not received any communications from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel further responses.

No opposition has been filed. On December 31, 2024, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents

 

“A party may demand that any other party produce . . . a document that is in the possession, custody, or control of the party on whom the demand is made.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010(b).) The demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete, (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive, or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).)

 

“The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand,” and “[t]he motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b).) On the other hand, the respondent has the burden to justify objections in response to a motion filed to compel further responses. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Super. Ct. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

To show good cause, the movant must demonstrate specific facts showing relevance of the requested documents. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1); TBG Ins. Servs. Corp v. Super. Ct. (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) To satisfy this fact-specific good cause inquiry, the movant must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact. (Calcor Space Facility v. Super. Ct. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224 (directing trial court to vacate its order compelling the defendant to produce records because the plaintiff failed to provide specific facts showing good cause for their production).)

 

B.      Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to RPDs

 

Here, Defendant has presented sufficient facts to demonstrate good cause justifying further responses to its RPDs, as well as demonstrating a good faith attempt to meet and confer to solve the issues informally. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(1); TBG Ins. Servs. Corp., supra, 96 Cal.App.4th at p. 448.)

 

In support of its motion, Defendant presented the Declaration of Christine R. Ghattas (“Ghattas Decl.”), Defendant’s counsel of record, which states the following facts:

 

On March 22, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff electronically with its RPDs. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s responses were due on April 24, 2024, 30 days following service, plus an additional two court days for electronic service. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.260(a) ; 1013 (a).) However, Plaintiff failed to provide responses by this date. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 3.) After communications between counsel for Defendant and Plaintiff where Defendant’s counsel granted Plaintiff multiple extensions to her deadline to provide responses, on June 7, 2024, Defendant ultimately granted Plaintiff an extension to provide her responses without objections by June 14, 2024. (Id., ¶¶ 4-9; Exhs. B.)

 

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff electronically served her responses to Defendant’s RPDs. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 10; Exh. C.) However, such responses were deficient for multiple reasons, including the following:

1.       Plaintiff’s responses listed requests that she was purportedly responding to that are completely different than the ones Defendants actually made. (Compare Id., Exh. A, at p. 4 (“REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 1: [¶] Please produce all DOCUMENTS you identified or relied on in preparing your responses to the Interrogatories served concurrently with this Request for Production.”); Id., Exh. C, at p. 5 (“REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 1 [¶] Please provide any and all documents, writings, notes, records, reports and other tangible items which support the contentions supported at paragraph 15 of your complaint.”))

2.      Plaintiff’s responses referenced exhibits that she did not actually produce. (See Id., Exh. C, at p. 6-11.)

3.      Plaintiff’s responses entirely ignored Defendant’s RPDs Nos. 27-47. (See Id., Exh. C, at p. 6-11.)

4.      Plaintiff produced only one document in response to Defendant’s 47 individual requests targeting documents related to Plaintiff’s medical treatment, lost income, specific contentions made in Plaintiff’s Complaint, and more. (See Id., Exh. A.)

 

On July 29, 2024, Defendant’s counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a formal meet and confer letter identifying Defendant’s claimed deficiencies in Plaintiff’s production and requesting that Plaintiff provide code-compliant responses to Defendant’s RPDs by Friday, August 2, 2024. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 11, Exh. D.) On August 5, 2024, having not received any communications from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant’s counsel sent an additional meet and confer correspondence requesting an informal discovery conference and an extension of time to file any motions to compel further responses. (Id., ¶ 12, Exh. E.) Again, Plaintiff’s counsel did not provide any response.

 

This evidence is sufficient to demonstrate good cause justifying Plaintiff’s further response, or in some cases initial response, to Defendant’s RPDs. Further, Plaintiff’s lack of opposition waives any potential justification for any potential objections Plaintiff may have had to any of Defendant’s RPDs.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel plaintiff’s further responses to requests for production, set one.

 

C.      Defendant’s Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

In connection with its motion, Defendant also requests the imposition of monetary sanctions on Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel for their misuse of the discovery process. Defendant’s request totals $2,140.00, representing 4.5 hours preparing this motion and related documents, 1.0 hours anticipated to prepare a reply, and 2.5 hours anticipated to prepare for and attend the hearing on this motion charged at a $260 per hour fee, as well as a $60.00 filing fee for this motion. (Ghattas Decl., ¶¶ 14-15.)

 

Code Civ. Proc., section 2031.310, subdivision (h), provides as follows: “[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Ibid.)

 

Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, defines misuses of the discovery process to include “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery[,]” “[m]aking an evasive response to discovery[,]” and “[f]ailing to confer in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning discovery, if the section governing a particular discovery motion requires to filing of a declaration stating facts showing that an attempt at informal resolution has been made.” (Id. at subds. (d), (f), and (i).) Further, Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, provides that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that the one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Id. at subd. (a).)

 

Here, the facts described above, as well as Plaintiff’s failure to oppose Defendant’s instant, justify the imposition of monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel for misuse of the discovery process. However, since the motion is unopposed, the Court will not award Defendant’s anticipated fees for preparing a Reply. Further, the Court finds that 2.5 anticipated hours to prepare for and attend the hearing on an unopposed motion is unreasonable, and that 4.5 hours to prepare a relatively simple discovery motion is not reasonable given counsel’s level of expertise. The Court awards Defendant’s attorney’s fees for 5.0 hours of attorney work and for the filing fee. Thus, monetary sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $1,360.00.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,360.00.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Request for Production, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is hereby ordered to provide further verified responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,360.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.

Writers Guild of America, West, Inc. v. Lifeguard Productions, LLC, et al. (24STCP02401)

 

Counsel for Petitioner/moving party: Dylan R. Holmes

Counsel for Respondents/opposing parties: N/A, no opposition filed

 

PETITION TO CONFIRM ARBITRATION AWARD (filed 07/31/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Writers Guild of America, West, Inc.’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is granted for a total judgment of $63,096.16, to be paid by Respondents.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 31, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (“Petition”) as to Respondents Lifeguard Productions, LLC (“LP”), Lifeguard Investments, LLC (“LI”), and Mike Landry (“Landry”).  Petitioner states that the parties entered into a Collective Bargaining Agreement (“CBA”) on June 28, 2012.  According to the CBA, the parties agreed to arbitrate certain disputes arising out of the agreement.  A dispute arose between the parties and on December 1, 2020, neutral arbitrator Paul Crost issued an Arbitration Opinion and Award in favor of Petitioner in the amount of $50,151.66.  (Pet. ¶¶ 6-8, Attach. 8(c).)  Petitioner requests that the Court confirm the arbitration award and grant interest at a rate of 18% per year from December 1, 2020, to January 8, 2025, for a total judgment of $63,096.16.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Petition to Confirm Arbitration

 

Once arbitration is concluded, “any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.”  (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.).  Any of the parties may file a petition with the court, which must then “confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1285, 1286; EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc. (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.)  “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.”  (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.)  “Neither the trial court, nor the appellate court, may ‘review the merits of the dispute, the sufficiency of the evidence, or the arbitrator’s reasoning, nor may we correct or review an award because of an arbitrator’s legal or factual error, even if it appears on the award’s face.”  (EHM Productions, supra, at p. 1063-64.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 1285.4 states: “A petition under this chapter shall:

 

(a)    Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement.

(b)   Set forth the names of the arbitrators.

(c)    Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.

 

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure, section 1283.6 provides that: “The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”  (Emphasis added.)  In addition, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by filing and serving a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1288, 1288.4.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4, the statute governing proper service of this Petition states, in pertinent part:

 

“(a) A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.

(b) If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision: ¶ (1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.

 

B.      Petition

 

Petitioner and Respondents entered into a Collective Bargaining Agreement (“CBA”) containing an arbitration provision governing certain disputes between the parties arising out of the CBA.  (Pet. ¶ 4, Attach. 4(b).)  A copy of the agreement is filed as Attachment 4(b) and presents a list of authorized arbitrators approved by the parties, including Paul E. Crost.  (Attach. 4(b) – Art. 11.C.2.e.3.)  Following a dispute between the parties regarding a breach of the CBA, on December 1, 2020, arbitrator Paul E. Crost issued an Opinion and Award in favor of Petitioner and against Respondents as follows: (1) payment of “residuals compensation…in the principal amount of $17,270.85;” (2) payment of “interest on the unpaid residuals compensation…in the amount of $17,880.81 which amount shall continue to accrue at the MBA rate of 1.5% per months until the unpaid residuals compensation is paid in full;” and (3) payment of “$15,000 as damages for their failure to comply with the financial reporting requirements and for their failure to provide a valid assumption agreement as required by the MBA,” for a total of $50,151.66.  (Attach. 8(c), p. 49.)  Arbitrator Crost served Respondents with the Opinion and Award by first class mail, postage prepaid on December 3, 2020.  (Pet. pp. 50-51.)

 

Petitioner requests that the Court confirm the arbitration award and grant additional interest on the unpaid residuals compensation at a rate of 18% per year or 1.5% per month from December 1, 2020, to January 8, 2025, for a total of $12,944.50.  (Pet. ¶ 10; Proposed Order; Holmes Decl.)

 

The Court finds that the Petition was timely filed.  Respondents were served with the Petition and Notice of Hearing by personal service on December 5, 2024, in accordance with Article 11.A.6 of the CBA.  (12-16-24 Proof of Personal Service.)  Additional papers were served on Respondents by certified or registered mail on December 16, 2024.  (12-16-24 Proof of Service by Mail.)  Moreover, Petitioner’s counsel Dylan R. Holmes has presented a calculation of the interest requested in his declaration filed on December 16, 2024.

 

Given that Petitioner has complied with all of the requirements for the instant Petition, the Court grants the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award for a total judgment of $63,096.16, to be paid by Respondents.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Petitioner Writers Guild of America, West, Inc.’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is granted for a total judgment of $63,096.16, to be paid by Respondents.

Brittany Powell v. God’s Pantry, et al. (24STCV12497)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Employees First Labor Law

Counsel for Defendant God’s Pantry/moving party: Keith A. Fink & Associates

 

[motion TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF PLAINTIFF AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS] (filed October 17, 2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

God’s Pantry’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is GRANTED.

 

God’s Pantry’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $2,440.00 within 30 days.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from Defendant God’s Pantry’s (God’s Pantry), South Bay Workforce Investment Board, Inc.’s (South Bay), and Augusto Dolce’s (Dolce) (collectively, Defendants) alleged retaliatory termination of Plaintiff Brittany Powell (Plaintiff). Plaintiff alleges that Defendants refused to properly compensate her for overtime hours and failed to provide her with required meal and rest breaks in violation of the Labor Code. (Compl., ¶¶ 24-28.) Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants were requiring her, as a paralegal, to engage in the unauthorized practice of law. (Id. ¶¶ 32-33.)

 

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants alleging causes of action for: (1) Failure to Pay Minimum Wages, (2) Failure to Furnish Wage and Hour Statements, (3) Failure to Maintain Payroll Records, (4) Failure to Provide Meal and Rest Period Compensation, (5) Failure to Pay Overtime Compensation, (6) Failure to Pay Wages in a Timely Manner, (7) Waiting Time Penalties, (8) Failure to Indemnify/Reimburse Necessary Expenditures Incurred During Discharge Work Duties, (9) Unfair Competition, (10), Whistleblower Retaliation, and (11) Wrongful Termination.

 

On October 17, 2024, Defendant God’s Pantry filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. On December 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On December 31, 2024, God’s Pantry filed a reply.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)  A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party or party-affiliated deponent to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿2025.450, subdivision (a) provides:¿“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for¿inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”¿ 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿2025.450, subdivision (b) provides:¿“A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:¿ 

 

  1. The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿ 

 

  1. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

 

B.      Discussion

 

God’s Pantry argue that Plaintiff’s deposition should be compelled because God’s Pantry has

sought to take Plaintiff’s deposition since around July 16, 2024 when the first notice of deposition was served upon Plaintiff. (Vasquez Decl., ¶ 3, Exhibit A.) The noticed deposition was set for August 22, 2024. (Id. ¶ 3, Exhibit A.) On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel then objected to the August 22, 2024 date, and according to God’s Pantry, the objection failed to provide any alternative dates. (Id. ¶ 5, Exhibit B.)

 

According to God’s Pantry, on September 25, 2024, God’s Pantry served another notice of deposition for October 14, 2024, and informed Plaintiff’s counsel that Plaintiff would have until September 27, 2024 to object to the new date. (Id. ¶ 10, Exhibit E.) On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel objected to the October 14, 2024 date, and provided no alternative dates. (Id. ¶ 11, Exhibit F.) On October 7, 2024, God’s Pantry’s counsel emailed Plaintiff stating that God’s Pantry had been trying to find dates for Plaintiff’s deposition and then provided alternative dates for the deposition for dates in October. (Id. ¶ 13, Exhibit G.)  According to God’s Pantry, from the date of filing the instant motion, Plaintiff’s counsel has not responded nor provided alternative dates. (Id. ¶ 15.)

 

Plaintiff argues that on the date of filing the instant motion, Plaintiff’s counsel provided God’s Pantry with alternative dates for Plaintiff’s deposition to be completed in early December. (LaCour Decl., ¶ 5, Exhibit B.) God’s Pantry’s counsel rejected the alternative dates, and asked for additional dates after January 7, 2025, to which Plaintiff’s counsel complied. (Id., Exhibit A.) The parties agreed that January 16, 2024 would be a workable date for taking Plaintiff’s deposition. (Id., Exhibit C.)

 

The Court finds that compelling Plaintiff’s deposition to be proper. God’s Pantry has served two notices for Plaintiff’s deposition, and Plaintiff’s counsel has objected to both without provided alternative dates. When God’s Pantry emailed Plaintiff on October 7, 2024 and provided further dates, Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond until October 17, 2024, the date the instant motion was filed. It was not until the threat of the motion that Plaintiff’s counsel cooperated and finally provided God’s Pantry with alternative dates in October. Once Plaintiff’s counsel provided these alternative October dates did that process begin to reach a consensus on when Plaintiff’s deposition can be taken. Thus, the Court will hold Plaintiff to the agreed upon date of January 16, 2024.

 

God’s Pantry’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is GRANTED for the date of January 16, 2024.

 

C.      Sanctions

 

The Court finds that sanctions against Plaintiff’s counsel is proper.

 

As explained above, Plaintiff refused to provide God’s Pantry’s counsel with alternative dates to take Plaintiff’s deposition that worked with Plaintiff’s counsel, when God’s Pantry’s counsel served two notices to take Plaintiff’s deposition. It was when the instant motion was filed that Plaintiff’s counsel began adequately participating in the discovery process and provided God’s Pantry’s counsel with alternative dates, which ultimately resulted in the agreed upon date of January 16, 2024.

 

God’s Pantry is seeking $4,522.50 in sanctions.

 

Counsel declares that his hourly rate $595.00. (Vasquez Decl., ¶ 16.) The Court finds this hourly rate to be reasonable, given the experience of counsel. Counsel further declares that he spent 3.5 hours researching and preparing the instant motion, with an anticipated 2 hours preparing the reply, and an anticipated two hours for attending the hearing. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds this excessive. The Court will decrease the amount of time spent on the motion to 1.5 hours, given the simplicity and brevity of the issues. The Court will decrease the amount of time drafting a reply to 1.5 hours. The Court will also decrease the amount spent for attending the hearing to 1.0 hour, given the option for remote appearances. Thus, the Court will aware 4.0 hours at $595.00 an hour, plus the $60.00 filing fee, which equals $2,440.00.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

 Based on the foregoing, God’s Pantry’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is GRANTED for the date of January 16, 2024. God’s Pantry’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $2,440.00 payable within 30 days.

Moskowitz and Company, Inc. v. Sync Brokerage, Inc., et al. (24STCV17905) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: The Federici Law Firm P.C.

Counsel for Defendant: N/A

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 11/26/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment is GRANTED in the total amount of $52,667.45.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that Defendants contracted for Plaintiff’s accounting services. (Complaint, ¶¶ 9-16.) Defendants and Plaintiff entered into a written promissory note on February 5, 2024, whereby Defendants would pay $45,965.00 to Plaintiff upon receiving funding and working capital for Defendants’ business. (Id., ¶¶ 17-18; Khalawafi Decl., Exh. A.) Although Defendants received such capital and were able to pay the Note, Defendants refused to pay the promised sum to Plaintiff. (Id. ¶ 22.)

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff Moskowitz and Company Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed its Complaint against Defendants Sync Brokerage, Inc. (“Sync”), Andres Hoyos (“Hoyos”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and Does 1 through 20, inclusive, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Fraud, and (3) Quantum Meruit. Plaintiff’s Complaint prays for damages on breach of the promissory note totaling $45,965.00, punitive damages on the fraud claim, reasonable litigation costs, pre-judgment interest at the legal rate, and attorneys’ fees as permitted by the promissory note.

 

On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Proof of Service of Summons as to both Defendants, indicating that a registered California process server served both Defendants by personal service at the corporate address for Sync located at 17404 Ventura Blvd, #110, Los Angeles, CA 91316. The proof of service further indicates that the summons package for each defendant was left in the care of named defendant Hoyos.

 

On September 5, 2024, the Court Clerk entered default as to both Defendants.

 

On November 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Request for Entry of Default Judgment as to both Defendants, including its default prove-up package, requesting the following damages, including: (1) damages on Defendants’ breach of the promissory note, totaling $45,965.00; (2) pre-judgment interest on Defendants’ breach of contract since February 5, 2024; (3) Plaintiff’s costs of litigation totaling $675.08; and (4) Plaintiff’s statutory attorneys’ fees, as permitted through the promissory note, totaling $1,7698.95.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.      Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.      Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                             Yes (11/26/24) 

2.      Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                      Yes (12/02/24)

3.      Declaration of non-military status for each defendant                      Yes

4.      Summary of the case                                                                                 Yes (11/26/24)

5.      585(d) declarations/admissible evidence in support                      Yes (11/26/24)

(see Khalawafi Decl.)

(see Federici Decl.)

8.      Cost memorandum                                                                                    N/A

9.      Request for attorney fees (Local Rule 3.214)                                     Yes

(see Federici Decl.)

 

Damages Summary: 

 

Damages 

$45,965.00

Punitive Damages  

N/A

Interest 

$4,255.42

Attorneys’ fees 

$1,768.95

TOTAL

Other relief requested 

 

 

Plaintiff’s exhibits presented in support of its request for default judgment indicates that Plaintiff and Defendants entered into a promissory note for “the sum of $45,965.00 upon [Defendants] receiving funding and working capital for [Sync].” (Khalawafi Decl., ¶ 1; Exh. A, p. 1.)

Plaintiff is also entitled to pre-judgement interest in its breach of contract claim because the damages can be calculated with certainty. (CCP § 3287; National Farm Workers Service Ctr., Inc. v. M. Caratan, Inc. (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 796, 808.) Although the contract does not stipulate a rate of interest, the statutory interest at 10 percent per annum after the breach applies since the date of the breach. (Civ. Code § 3289(b).) Plaintiff requested pre-judgment interest in its prayer for relief. (Complaint, at Prayer, ¶ 5.) Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to pre-judgment interest on the total amount demanded, $45,965.00, at the rate of 10 percent per year, or $4,596.50, and equaling $12.59 per day ($4,596.50 divided by 365). Plaintiff’s allegations of the Complaint indicate that Defendants breached the promissory note on the day it was entered, February 5, 2024, because they never intended to perform their obligations and simply intended to induce Plaintiff to perform for free. (Complaint, ¶¶ 11-22.) Because this judgment is entered on January 8, 2025, Plaintiff is entitled to 338 days of pre-judgment interest accrual, totaling 4,255.42 (338 days multiplied by $12.59.)

 

As the prevailing party, and per the express terms of the promissory note, Plaintiff is also entitled to recover its reasonable litigation costs as permitted by statute. (CCP § 1032(b); Complaint, ¶ 28; Khalawafi Decl., Exh. A, p. 1 at ¶ IV, “Collection Costs.”) Plaintiff incurred a total of $675.08 in recoverable litigation costs, including $435 in initial filing fees, $87.51 in process server fees, and $152.57 in electronic filing fees through One Legal to file each of the documents in this action. (Federici Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Per the express terms of the written promissory note, Plaintiff is also entitled to recover his attorney’s fees. (Complaint, ¶ 28; Khalawafi Decl., Exh. A, p. 1 at ¶ IV, “Collection Costs.”) As an uncontested default case, Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees are governed by L.A. Superior Court Local Rule 3.214(a). The damages sought are $45,965.00, so Plaintiff is therefore entitled to $690 plus 3 percent of the excess over $10,000.00. The excess over $10,000.00 is $35,695.00. 3 percent of $35,965.00 is $1,078.95. Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to $1,768.95 in recoverable attorneys’ fees ($690.00 + $1,078.95). (Federici Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment is GRANTED in the total amount of $52,667.45.
