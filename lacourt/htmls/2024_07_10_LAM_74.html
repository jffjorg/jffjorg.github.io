DEPARTMENT 74 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 



 




Case Number: 22STCV13373    Hearing Date: July 10, 2024    Dept: 74

Jerome S. Greene, et al. v. Dollar Tree Stores, Inc., et al.

 

Defendant Rubin Pachulski Properties 36 LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

 

            Defendant Rubin Pachulski Properties 36, LLC (“Pachulski Properties”) partly owns the shopping plaza known as La Cienega-Sawyer Plaza. A property owner owes a duty of care – the existence of which the court decides as a legal question – to keep the property safe. It is a triable issue of material fact whether the security measures taken at the property satisfied Defendant’s duty of care or breached it. If the duty was breached, a triable issue of material fact exists whether that breach caused plaintiff’s injuries because a triable issue of material fact exists whether the criminal act was foreseeable or, alternatively, an intervening superseding cause of plaintiff’s injuries. Summary judgment and adjudication are improper.

 

BACKGROUND

            On April 21, 2022, Plaintiff Jerome Greene filed the instant action for negligence and premises liability by and through his guardian ad litem Francine Greene, as a result of injuries sustained following an assault on the premises allegedly owned, leased, controlled, managed, secured, and/or maintained by Defendants.  On September 1, 2022, Plaintiff’s counsel informed the Court that Plaintiff Jerome Greene had passed away as a result of his injuries and filed a First Amended Complaint adding Plaintiff’s sister, Francine Greene, as his successor in interest.   The First Amended Complaint names the following defendants: Dollar Tree Stores, Inc. (“Dollar Tree”), RP Realty Partners, LLC (“RP Realty”), RPD Catalyst, LLC (“RPD”), RPD Catalyst II, LLC (“RPD II), and Security 1 (“Security”).  Subsequently, the following defendants were added to the case: RPD Property Management Company, LLC (“RPD Management”) and Rubin Pachulski Properties 36, LLC (“Pachulski Properties”), while Defendants RPD and RPD II were dismissed without prejudice.  Throughout the pendency of the lawsuit, several additional cross-complaints have been filed between the parties.

            On April 26, 2024, Defendant Pachulski Properties filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication.  Plaintiff filed an Opposition on June 27, 2024, and Defendant filed a Reply on July 5, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle him/her to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The moving party must make an affirmative showing that he/she is entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition.  (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742-743.)  Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)

When a plaintiff seeks summary judgment, he/she must produce admissible evidence on each element of each cause of action on which judgment is sought.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)  When a defendant seeks summary judgment, he/she has the “burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets his or her initial movant’s burden.  (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.)  Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p).)  The opposing party may not simply rely on his/her allegations to show a triable issue but must present evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162.)  Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

As to any alternative request for summary adjudication of issues, such alternative relief must be clearly set forth in the Notice of Motion and the general burden-shifting rules apply but the issues upon which summary adjudication may be sought are limited by statute.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(f)(1).)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Ibid.)

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Pachulski Properties moves for summary judgment as to the causes of action alleged in the First Amended Complaint on the basis that there are no triable issues of material fact as to Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant and, thus, Defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  Alternatively, Defendant requests summary adjudication as to the following matters: Defendant Pachulski Properties cannot be held liable for any of Plaintiff’s negligence-based claims because there is no triable issue of material fact as to Defendant’s (1) duty of care, (2) breach of any duty of care, (3) any alleged act or omission that proximately caused the criminal assault at issue, and (4) the criminal assault at issue was an intervening and superseding cause of Plaintiff’s damages.

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.  (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp¿(2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)  While those who own, possess, or control property are not insurers of the safety of their patrons, they generally have a duty to exercise reasonable and ordinary care in keeping the premises reasonably safe and in managing the property¿to¿avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.  (Ortega v. Kmart Corp.¿(2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Annocki¿v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC¿(2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  The existence and scope of duty are legal questions for the court.  (Annocki, 232 Cal.App.4th.¿at¿36.)  “A store owner exercises ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the portions of the premises open to customers, and the care required is commensurate with the risks involved.”¿ (Ibid.)  If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition¿reasonably safe for their¿[customers’]¿use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.”  (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc.¿(1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)  The party asserting the cause of action has the burden to prove that the owner had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to correct it.  (Ortega, 26 Cal.4th at 1203, 1206.)  Once duty is established, the plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached this duty by failing to exercise ordinary care and the breach was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  (Ortega, 26 Cal.4th at 1205.)

“The elements of a cause of action for wrongful death are a tort, such as negligence, and resulting death.”  (Lopez v. City of Los Angeles (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 675, 685.)

The following facts are undisputed.  On November 21, 2021, Jerome Greene (“Decedent”) was shopping at the Dollar Tree located at 1837 South La Cienega Boulevard in Los Angeles, located within a larger shopping plaza known as La Cienega-Sawyer Plaza (“Plaza”).  (Opposition – Undisputed Facts (“UF”) Nos. 1, 7.)  When he approached one of the checkout counters, he inadvertently cut in front of Defendant Gill.  (UF No. 2.)  In response, Gill approached Decedent and began physically attacking Decedent.  (UF Nos. 3-4.) Approximately nine months after the incident, Decedent passed away from the injuries.  (UF No. 5.)  Gill was criminally charged for the incident in LA County Case No. SA10569, The People of the State of California v. Nicholas Wayne Gill.  (UF No. 6.)  Since June 1, 2021, and on the date of the incident, Defendant RPD Management was the property manager of the Plaza.  (UF Nos. 8-9.)  Pachulski Properties did not manage the property on the date of the incident; however, it did have an interest in the subject property.  (UF Nos. 10-11.). Defendant Security 1, Inc. is a third-party security company contracted to provide roaming security for the common areas of the Plaza, including on the date of the incident.  (UF No. 12.)  No one associated with Defendants RPD, RP Realty, or Pachulski Properties knew of, or had any interactions with Defendant Gill prior to the incident and they did not learn about the incident until served with the instant lawsuit.  (UF Nos. 17-18.)

Defendant Pachulski Properties argues that it cannot be held liable for any of the negligence-based claims because there is no triable issue of material fact as to its duty of care, breach of duty, any alleged act or omission which proximately caused the criminal assault at issue.  Furthermore, according to Defendant, the criminal assault at issue was an intervening, superseding cause of Plaintiff’s damages.  In support of its Motion, Defendant presents the following evidence: excerpts from the deposition testimonies of (1) Alexis Munguia, (2) Rosalba Alvarez, (3) Maria Vasquez, and (4) Scott Dew, RPD Realty’s person most knowledgeable, (5) Declaration of Rosalba Alvarez, (6) Exhibit C to Defendant Dollar Tree’s Compendium of Evidence filed in support of Dollar Tree’s Motion for Summary Judgment, containing a link to the video footage of the incident, and (7) copy of felony complaint in The People of the State of California v. Nicholas Wayne Gill, LA County Superior Court Case No. SA 105659.  (Ball Decl. ¶¶ 5-11, Exs. 3-9.)

Having reviewed the evidence submitted in support of the Defendant’s Motion, the Court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of presenting evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation. Defendant argues that it did not owe a duty to protect the Decedent from what Defendant characterizes as the “random, unforeseen, third-party criminal violence of Gill that occurred inside the Dollar Tree.” (Mot. p. 12.) Defendant adds that even if a security guard had been inside the store that day, there is no evidence that Gill would have been prevented from entering the store, as Dollar Tree was responsible for determining who was allowed to enter or asked to leave, and, according to Defendant, the assault would have still occurred.  (Ibid.)  Finally, Defendant argues that Gill’s attack “was so extraordinary that it was not reasonably foreseeable to Pachulski Properties” and was thus, “an intervening cause of Decedent’s alleged injuries that superseded any negligence on the part of Pachulski Properties.”  (Mot. p. 19.) Each of these assertions is a triable issue of material fact which the court cannot decide as a matter of law. Accordingly, the Court denies Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, as well as the Motion for Summary Adjudication.

CONCLUSION

The Court Defendant Rubin Pachulski Properties 36, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication.  Defendant shall give notice.
