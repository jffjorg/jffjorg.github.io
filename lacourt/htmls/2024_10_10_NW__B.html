Case Number: 20STCV06902    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: B

Hearing: 10/10/2024

Re: 20STCV06902 Girik Issaian vs Gonzalez Logistics, Inc., et al.

Plaintiff Girik Issaian’s Motion for Relief from Potential Waiver of Trial by Jury

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff’s Motion for Relief from Potential Waiver of Trial by Jury is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

On February 20, 2020, Plaintiff Girik Issaian (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Gonzalez Logistics, Inc. (“Gonzalez”), Mario Miranda, Sr. (“Miranda”), and DOES 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Miranda, acting in the scope of their employment under Defendant Gonzalez, was driving a tractor trailer when they negligently collided with Plaintiff’s tractor trailer, causing Plaintiff to suffer personal injuries and property damage.

On March 25, 2020, Defendants Gonzalez and Miranda filed a demand for jury trial and posted the requisite fees.

On February 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed a notice of posting of jury fees.

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective as of June 24, 2024.

On July 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a demand for jury trial.

On August 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for order granting relief from potential waiver of trial by jury.

A jury trial is set for February 10, 2025.

LEGAL STANDARD

“The right to a trial by jury as declared by Section 16 of Article I of the California Constitution shall be preserved to the parties inviolate.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 631(a).) “In civil cases, a jury may only be waived pursuant to subdivision (f).” (Ibid.) CCP section 631(f) provides that a party waives trial by jury by (1) failing to appear at the trial; (2) written consent filed with the clerk or judge; (3) oral consent, in open court, entered in the minutes; (4) failing

to announce that a jury is required, at the time the cause is first set for trial, if it is set upon notice of stipulation, or within five days after notice of setting if it is set without notice or stipulation; (5) failing to timely pay the fee described in subdivision (b), unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee; or (6) failing to deposit with the clerk or judge, at the beginning of the second and each succeeding day’s session, the sum provided in subdivision (e). (Ibid., § 631(f).) “The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.” (Ibid., § 631(g).) “The trial court should grant a motion for relief of a jury waiver ‘unless, and except, where granting such a motion would work serious hardship to the objecting party.’” (Mackovska v. Viewcrest Road Properties LLC (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1, 10 [quoting Boal v. Price Waterhouse & Co. (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 806, 809].) “When there is doubt about whether to grant relief from a jury trial waiver, the court must resolve that doubt in favor of the party seeking a jury trial.” (Ibid.)

“In a motion for relief from waiver of a jury trial, the crucial question is whether the party opposing relief will suffer any prejudice if the court grants relief.” (Mackovska, supra, 40 Cal.App.5th at 10.) “‘The prejudice which must be shown from granting relief from the waiver is prejudice from the granting of relief and not prejudice from the jury trial.’” (Ibid. [quoting Massie v. AAR Western Skyways, Inc. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 405, 411].) “‘The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.’” (Ibid. [quoting Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 808, 811].) “Denying relief where the party opposing the motion for relief has not shown prejudice is an abuse of discretion.” (Ibid.)

ANALYSIS

This action was commenced on February 20, 2020. Therefore, jury fees were required to be posted no later than February 19, 2021. (CCP § 631(c)(2))(requiring that, if no initial case management conference is scheduled in a civil action, fees are due no later than 365 days after filing the initial complaint.) Here, Plaintiff posted jury fees on February 15, 2023, nearly two years after the statutory deadline.

Plaintiff argues that the late posting of jury fees was inadvertent, as Plaintiff’s counsel believed that both a demand for a jury trial and payment of fees were timely completed. (Dordick Decl. ¶ 3.) Counsel only discovered the error during a review of the Court’s register of actions earlier this year. (Ibid.) Counsel asserts that, after this realization, they promptly posted and served notice of the jury fees. (Ibid.)

Additionally, Plaintiff argues that granting this motion will not pose undue hardship on Defendants. Plaintiff notes that both Defendants and Plaintiffs have made jury demands and posted jury fees. (Motion p. 5, lines 21-22.) Therefore, Plaintiff argues, all parties have anticipated a jury trial in preparing for litigation. (Ibid. at lines 22-23.) Plaintiff further notes that trial remains months away. (Ibid. at line 21.)

The Court also notes that this motion is unopposed.

CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s motion for relief from potential waiver of trial by jury is GRANTED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 22STCV10620    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: B

ANTONIAS ROMERO V. BERNICE STEWDART-DONALDSON, ET AL.

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET THREE, REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, SET THREE, REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION, SET THREE, AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

Date of Hearing: October 10, 2024 Trial Date: November 12, 2024

Department: B Case No.: 22STCV10620

Moving Party: Plaintiff Antonias Romero

Responding Party: Defendant County of Los Angeles

BACKGROUND

On March 28, 2022, Plaintiff Antonias Romero (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Bernice Stewdart-Donaldson (“Donaldson”), County of Los Angeles (“County”), City of Los Angeles (“City”), and DOES 1 to 20 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) motor vehicle negligence; and (2) general negligence. Plaintiff alleges that, on May 1, 2021, Plaintiff was a pedestrian crossing an intersection when Donaldson, acting within his scope of employment under the County and City, negligently struck Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges serious injuries as a result.

On September 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed three motions to compel further responses against County, as to the following discovery documents: (1) Special Interrogatories, Set Three; (2) Requests for Production, Set Three; and (3) Requests for Admission, Set Three (“Discovery Motions”). Plaintiff also requests sanctions in the total amount of $3,180 ($1,060 per motion).

On June 6, 2024, the action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective as of June 24, 2024.

On September 30, 2024, the County filed oppositions to all three motions.

[TENTATIVE] RULING

Plaintiff’s Discovery Motions are GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 provides, in pertinent part, that “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the

option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. (CCP § 2030.300(a).)

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP §§ 2030.300(c).)

Under California law, the objecting party has the burden of justifying its objections when the propounding party requests that the Court order further responses. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.) Further, under CCP § 2023.010, it is a misuse of discovery to make unmerited objections. (CCP § 2023.010(e).)

DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer Requirement

A motion to compel further responses shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP § 2030.300(b).) Here, Plaintiff’s counsel provides a declaration demonstrating diligent efforts to meet and confer with opposing counsel. (Anderson Decl. ¶¶ 5-7.) On July 25, 2024, counsel sent a Meet and Confer Letter to opposing counsel, outlining the deficiencies in the County’s responses and offering fifteen additional days for further response. (Ibid. at ¶ 5; Exh. C [July 25, 2024 Meet and Confer Letter].) On August 27, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a second Meet and Confer Letter to opposing counsel outlining remaining deficiencies. (Ibid. at ¶ 6; Exh. E [August 27, 2024 Meet and Confer Letter].) Plaintiff declares that, as of September 3, 2024, the County’s discovery responses remain deficient. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) Therefore, the Court finds Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts to be sufficient pursuant to CCP § 2030.300(b).)

Special Interrogatories, Set Three

Plaintiff moves to compel the County’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Three Nos. 36-42, 49-53

The motion is denied as to Special Interrogatories, Set Three, Nos. 36, 37, 38, 39, 41, 42, 50, 51, 52, and 53. These pertain to the July 12, 2015, Los Angeles Times article titled “Walking in L.A.: Times Analysis Finds the County’s 817 Most Dangerous Intersections.” This article is inadmissible hearsay and therefore irrelevant to the litigation. (Cal. Evid. Code § 1200.) The motion is also denied as to No. 40. No. 40 contains multiple inquiries, thus making the question compound, overbroad and confusing.

The motion is granted as to No. 49. Training or instruction provided to the County’s agents regarding dangerous intersection conditions are relevant to the pending litigation. The County is ordered to provide a verified, objection-free, supplemental response to Special Interrogatories, Set Three, No. 49 within 15 days of this order.

Requests for Production, Set Three

Plaintiff moves to compel the County’s further responses to Requests for Production, Set Three, Nos. 45-48, 50, and 52-55.

The motion is denied as to Requests for Production, Set Three, Nos. 45, 46, 47, and 48. These pertain to the July 12, 2015, Los Angeles Times article titled “Walking in L.A.: Times Analysis Finds the County’s 817 Most Dangerous Intersections.” This article is inadmissible hearsay and therefore irrelevant to the litigation. (Cal. Evid. Code § 1200.)

The motion is granted as to Nos. 50, 52, 54, and 55. These production requests articulate clear inquiries as to the County’s safety procedures regarding the intersection where Plaintiff was struck and are therefore relevant to the litigation. The County is ordered to provide verified, objection-free, supplemental responses to Requests for Production, Set Three, Nos. 50, 52, 54, and 55 within 15 days of this order.

Requests for Admission, Set Three

Plaintiff moves to compel the County’s further responses to Requests for Admission, Set Three, Nos. 40-43, 45-47, 49, 51-55, and 57.

The motion is denied as to Requests for Admission, Set Three, Nos. 40, 41, 42, 43, 45, 46, 47, 49, 51, 52, 53, 54, and 57. These pertain to the July 12, 2015, Los Angeles Times article titled “Walking in L.A.: Times Analysis Finds the County’s 817 Most Dangerous Intersections.” This article is inadmissible hearsay and therefore irrelevant to the litigation. (Cal. Evid. Code § 1200.)

The motion is denied as to No. 55 as it calls for speculation.

Monetary Sanctions

“The court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct” under CCP § 2023.030(a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

Here, the County’s objections were largely meritorious since the majority of Plaintiff’s discovery requests pertained to inadmissible hearsay. Therefore, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff's Discovery Motions are GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions are DENIED.

The Court ORDERS that the County provide verified, complete and code-compliant responses, without objections, to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set Three, Nos. 50, 52, 54, and 55, and Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set Three, No. 49 within 15 days of this order.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
