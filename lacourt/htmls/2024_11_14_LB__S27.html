DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV01086    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: S27

Plaintiff, Martha Valdez filed this complaint against her daughter, Defendant, Martha Diaz on 12/29/22, arising out of Defendant’s alleged fraudulent transfer of Plaintiff’s property to Defendant.

Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint on 12/18/23. The SAC includes causes of action for quiet title, declaratory and injunctive relief, intentional misrepresentation (fraud), willful concealment, elder abuse, negligence, and civil conspiracy to commit fraud.

Fredric W. Trestor, and Charles A. Mollis, attorneys of record for Defendant, Martha Diaz, seek to be relieved as counsel, declaring there has been a breakdown in communication and cooperation with the client and that continued representation is not possible. Counsel declares he served the moving papers on Plaintiff at a confirmed address. Counsel also gave proper notice of the hearing date on this motion, as well as the FSC and trial dates. The court notes that trial in this matter is in two months, which provides Defendant sufficient time to obtain new counsel, and thus, Defendant will not be prejudiced.

As such, the unopposed motion to be relieved as counsel is granted, effective upon filing proof of service on the client of the signed order.

Counsel is ordered to give notice.

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org. If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar. If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative.


Case Number: 22STCV19600    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: S27

 

Plaintiffs, Leslie Ungar and Sophia Ungar filed this habitability action against Defendants, Smart Real Estate, and Westland Real Estate Group, and arising out of Plaintiffs’ claims that there was mold in their unit.  Plaintiffs later amended the complaint to also name as Defendants, Albert Yamada, as trustee for the Yamada Albert H 2016 Trust, and Albert Yamada. Sophia is a minor and is appearing by and through her mother and guardian ad litem, Leslie Ungar.  Plaintiffs filed their complaint on 6/16/22.

 

At this time, the parties have agreed to settle the entire action.  The settlement provides for payments to Plaintiffs in the following amounts:

Leslie Ungar:                 $30,900

Sophia:                         $10,000

If the settlement is approved, Sophia’s settlement amount will be distributed as follows: $0 for medical expenses, $0 for attorneys’ fees, and $3,318 for costs, with $2,529.32 of the net balance to be deposited in a blocked account, and $4,152 of the net balance to be held on the conditions that the court determines to the in the best interest of the minor. The proposed conditions are: Petitioner and Minor wish to direct a portion of settlement funds towards insurance premiums for the purchase of a one-year health insurance plan to provide Sophia Ungar with options under a PPO medical plan, instead of relying on the current DHCS/Medi-Cal plan.

Item 12b(5) of the petition indicates the reason for the apportionment of the settlement payments between the Claimant and Plaintiff is specified in Attachment 12. However, Attachment 12 does not explain the reason that the apportionment of the settlement payments; it only states that the amount of costs are split between the two plaintiffs. 

Further, there is no explanation provided as to why Plaintiffs wish to purchase insurance premiums with part of the net settlement for Sophia, when they have a Medi-Cal plan. Moreover, were the court agree to this proposal, the court requires an affidavit attesting that $4,152 of the settlement amount for Sophia would be used for this purpose.

Lastly, the court notes that there is no proposed order granting the petition, nor is there an order to deposit into the blocked account.

3.     Motion to Enforce Interlocutory Judgment

a.         Legal Standard

“The pendency of another action growing out of the same transaction is ground for abatement of the second action.” (Lawyers Title Ins. Corp. v. Superior Court (1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 455, 458.) “The purpose of [an] interlocutory judgment . . . is to permit the trial court to retain jurisdiction over the subsequent action so that when a final determination is had in the prior pending action the court will be empowered to determine the issues in the subsequent suit.” (Lord v. Garland (1946) 27 Cal.2d 840, 851.) “An order of abatement issues as a matter of right not as a matter of discretion where the conditions for its issuance exist.” (Lawyers Title Ins. Corp. v. Superior Court, supra, 151 Cal.App.3 597 d 455, 460.) CCP § 597 provides that “where the defense of another action pending or a demurrer based upon subdivision (c) of Section 430.10 is sustained (and no other special defense is sustained) an interlocutory judgment shall be entered in favor of the defendant pleading the same to the effect that no trial of other issues shall be had until the final determination of that other action, and the plaintiff may appeal from the interlocutory judgment in the same manner and within the same time as is now or may be hereafter provided by law for appeals from judgments.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 597.) “Under Code of Civil Procedure section 597, when such a judgment is entered, no trial of other issues shall be had until the final determination of the other action.” (Leadford v. Leadford (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 571, 574.)

 

b.         Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice and the Court takes judicial notice of the complaint in 21LBCV00329. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.)

 

            c.         Analysis

Defendants contend that their motion should be granted because: (1) the Court properly abated this matter pending final resolution of 21LBCV00329 and an interlocutory judgment issued; and (2) the Court has jurisdiction to enforce the interlocutory judgment. Plaintiff does not oppose the granting of the instant motion.

 

Defendants’ counsel, Jeffrey R. Klein (“Klein”), provides a declaration. Mr. Klein states the following: trial in 21LBCV00329 was held on April 29, 2024. (Klein Decl. ¶ 4) The initial proposed judgment filed in 21LBCV00329 was not entered until July 8, 2024, and notice was served on July 9, 2024. (Klein Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. B.) The time for Defendants in 21LBCV00329 to file their notice of appeal will not expire until September 9, 2024, and a notice of appeal was filed by Defendant Phan on July 14, 2024. (Klein Decl. ¶ 6, Ex. C.) Trial in this action is set to commence on September 23, 2024, and should an appeal be filed, a final determination will not be made until a remittitur issues from the Court of Appeal. (Klein Decl. ¶ 7.) The judgment entered in 21LBCV00329 is not final as required by CCP § 597. (Klein Decl. ¶ 8.) Counsel states that “issues of res judicata, claim preclusion (collateral estoppel), the single judgment rule or the prohibition of Plaintiff’s splitting of a cause of action, will not be settled until long after the date of trial in this matter.” (Klein Decl. ¶ 8.) A trial in this action prior to a final judgment in 21LBCV00329 would create a substantial risk of irreparable harm to Defendants given the overlap of issues between the matters. (Klein Decl. ¶ 9.) This action merely expands upon those presented in 21LBCV00329 to include additional theories of liability and is, in fact, premised on a nearly identical set of facts. (Klein Decl. ¶ 9.) The lifting of the stay prematurely places Defendants in undue jeopardy for a double recovery and the parties of conflicting judgments. (Klein Decl. ¶ 8.) Mr. Klein attests that “Defendants will be irreparably harmed if the issue presented is not resolved prior to the commencement of trial in this [a]ction.” (Klein Decl. ¶ 10.)

 

The Court finds that the judgment in case number 21LBCV00329 is not final given that Defendant Phan is currently appealing such judgment. The appeal has not been ruled upon by the Court of Appeal. Defendants request that this action be stayed pending a final judgment in 21LBCV00329. (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 8:12-15.) Given that 21LBCV00329 is still pending and is related to this action as both actions involve overlapping issues, the Court finds that enforcement of the Court’s March 21, 2024 order is appropriate. The Court finds that this action should be stayed pending a final resolution of 21LBCV00329. Moreover, Plaintiff consents to the granting of the instant motion.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for enforcement of the interlocutory judgment entered on March 21, 2024, pursuant to CCP § 597. The Court therefore STAYS the instant action—Case No. 23STCV08230—until such time as 21LBCV00329 is completely resolved.

 

4.     Application for Determination of Good Faith Settlement

a.         Parties’ Positions

Plaintiff asserts that (1) there is a good faith basis for settlement and (2) any party opposing the application has the burden of showing that the settlement was not made in good faith.

 

In opposition to the application, Defendants raise the following arguments: (1) the pending settlement is not in good faith; (2) the instant action and 21LBCV00329 are premised upon the same nucleus of operative facts an the same alleged misappropriation of vehicles and equipment; and (3) there has been an interlocutory judgment and this action—including the application for determination of good faith settlement—should be stayed.

 

 

 

b.         Legal Standard

Any party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors, upon giving notice in the manner provided by subdivision (b) of Section 1005.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (a)(1).)

 

“In the alternative, a settling party may give notice of settlement to all parties and to the court, together with an application for determination of good faith settlement and a proposed order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (a)(2).) “The application shall indicate the settling parties, and the basis, terms, and amount of the settlement.” (Ibid.) “The notice, application, and proposed order shall be given by certified mail, return receipt requested, or by personal service.” (Ibid.) “Proof of service shall be filed with the court. Within 25 days of the mailing of the notice, application, and proposed order, or within 20 days of personal service, a nonsettling party may file a notice of motion to contest the good faith settlement.” (Ibid.) “If none of the nonsettling parties files a motion within 25 days of mailing of the notice, application, and proposed order, or within 20 days of personal service, the court may approve the settlement.” (Ibid.)

 

“The issue of the good faith of a settlement may be determined by the court on the basis of the affidavits served with the notice of hearing, and any counteraffidavits filed in response, or the court may, in its discretion, receive other evidence at the hearing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (b).) “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (c).)

 

When determining whether a settlement was made in good faith, the following factors are considered: (1) a rough approximation of plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability; (2) the amount paid in settlement; (3) the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs; (4) a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial; (5) the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants; and (6) the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of the non-settling defendants. (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.)

 

“The ultimate determinant of good faith is whether the settlement is grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person at the time of settlement would estimate the settlor’s liability to be.” (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1262 [citation omitted].) “[A] good faith settlement does not call for perfect or even nearly perfect apportionment of liability.” (Abbott Ford, Inc. v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 858, 875 [internal quotations omitted].) “[O]nly when the good faith nature of a settlement is disputed, it is incumbent upon the trial court to consider and weigh the Tech-Bilt factors.” (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court, supra, 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.)

 

c.        Judicial Notice

The Court therefore takes judicial notice of its March 21, 2024 minute order concerning Defendants’ demurrer to the complaint, as well as the complaint filed in 21LBCV00329.

1. Background

Plaintiffs Michael Wagih Mena Saad and Mariamgeregeszari Labib (“Plaintiffs”) filed this lemon law action against defendants Tesla, Inc. and Tesla Long Beach on April 23, 2024. Plaintiffs allege they purchased a new 2023 Tesla Model X (“the Vehicle”), manufactured by defendant Tesla, Inc., from defendant Tesla Long Beach, a Tesla dealership, on March 10, 2023; the Vehicle exhibited serious defects and noncomforties that could not be fixed after a reasonable number of attempts, and Defendants failed to replace the Vehicle or make restitution.

On May 24, 2024, defendant Tesla, Inc. (“Defendant”) moved for an order compelling Plaintiffs to pursue their claim in binding arbitration rather than litigation.

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition. On November 6, 2024, Defendant replied.

 

/ / /

 

2. Legal Standard

Under California and federal law, public policy favors arbitration as an efficient and less expensive means of resolving private disputes. (Moncharsh v. Heily & Blasé (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 8-9 (Moncharsh); AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333, 339.) Accordingly, whether an agreement is governed by the California Arbitration Act or the Federal Arbitration Act, courts resolve doubt about an arbitration agreement’s scope in favor of arbitration. (Moncharsh, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 9; Comedy Club, Inc. v. Improv West Associates (9th Cir. 2009) 553 F.3d 1277, 1284; see also Engalla v. Permanente Med. Grp., Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72 [“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability [citation] and a requirement that an arbitration agreement must be enforced on the basis of state law standards that apply to contracts in general [citation].”].) “[U]nder both the FAA and California law, ‘arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.’” (Higgins v. Superior Court (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1247.)

The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.  (Engalla, supra, 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.)  A petition to compel arbitration must allege both a “written agreement to arbitrate” the controversy, and that a party to that agreement “refuses to arbitrate” the controversy. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) It then becomes plaintiff’s burden, in opposing the motion, to prove by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to her opposition. (Ibid.)

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 requires a trial court to grant a petition to compel arbitration ‘if the court determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists.’”  (Avery v. Integrated Healthcare Holdings, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 50, 59 (Avery), quoting Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2).) Accordingly, “when presented with a petition to compel arbitration, the court’s first task is to determine whether the parties have in fact agreed to arbitrate the dispute.” (Id., at p. 59.) Generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting rulings on common issues. (Ibid.; see also Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218-219.) “In these summary proceedings the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Avery, supra, 218 Cal.App.4th at p. 59; accord Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)

 

            3. Request for Judicial Notice

            Defendant requests judicial notice of Plaintiffs’ complaint. The request is granted.

 

            4. Discussion

a. Defendant carries its threshold burden to show an arbitration agreement exists.

            In order to carry its threshold burden under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, Defendant must demonstrate the parties have a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy in question and that Plaintiffs refuse to do so.

                                    i. Defendant shows Plaintiffs signed an arbitration agreement.

            Defendant offers both the Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”) and Retail Installment Sales Contract (“RISC”) that Plaintiffs executed during the course of their purchase of the Vehicle. (Kim Decl., Exh. 1 [MVOA} and Exh 2 [RISC].) Both contain arbitration clauses. (Id., ¶¶ 6, 12, Exhs. 1-2.)

            Defendant claims it made the MVOA available to Plaintiffs via hyperlink during the sales process, and Plaintiffs were required to attest they agreed to its terms before placing their order. (Id., ¶ 4.)

            The RISC carries Plaintiffs’ electronic signatures, and Defendant attests they signed it as a condition to the Vehicle’s purchase. (Id., ¶ 13 and Exh. 2.)

            Plaintiffs do not deny they confirmed they agreed to the MVOA during purchase. They state their order “simply required [plaintiff Saad] to enter personal information and place a nominal deposit”, but they concede “[t]here was a large ‘Place Order’ button”, and they do not deny they clicked it in the course of their purchase. (Saad Decl., ¶¶ 4-5.) And although plaintiff Saad protests he “did not see a hyperlink” as Defendant contends, he does not expressly deny it was there. (Id., ¶ 6.)

Plaintiffs also suggest the RISC cannot be enforced because they “never provided any digital signatures.” (Saad Decl., ¶ 14.) But they concede “Tesla staff … had [Plaintiffs’] typed name[s] inserted into the agreement in various places”, and they imply these electronic signatures were present when they reviewed the RISC and they did not protest. (Id., ¶¶ 11-12, 14.) Plaintiffs’ entire claim depends on their assertion that they bought the Vehicle, and they do not deny they signed the RISC in order to do so.

The RISC is counter-signed by defendant Tesla, Inc. as seller of the Vehicle. (Kim Decl., Exh. 2, pp. 1, 6.)

Defendantas have carried their threshold burden to show an arbitration agreement exists.

                        ii. The RISC covers the dispute in question.

            Because of the dispute regarding the presence of an MVOA hyperlink, the Court focuses on the arbitration provision in the RISC. The RISC states, in relevant part:

“Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, any allegation of waiver of rights under this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this Vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action.”

(Kim Decl., Exh. 2, p. 5.)

            This is a statutory claim between Plaintiffs and Defendants (“you and us”), which arises out of the purchase or condition of the Vehicle. The dispute falls within the scope of the arbitration agreement.

                                    iii. Plaintiffs refuse to arbitrate.

            Defendant’s counsel attests, uncontradicted, that Plaintiffs refused his request to stipulate to arbitration. (Ameripuour Decl., ¶ 3.) Their opposition to the instant motion confirms their refusal. The final element of section 1281.2 is satisfied.

                       

b. Plaintiffs do not carry their burden in opposition.

            Because Defendant carried its threshold burden, it falls to Plaintiffs to show why the agreement should not be enforced.

                                    i. The Court disregards the MVOA for purposes of this ruling.

            Plaintiffs focus a considerable portion of their opposition on the enforceability of the MVOA. (See Opp., pp. 3-9.) As noted above, the Court declines to take up the applicability of the MVOA here, because the RISC carries Defendant’s burden. Plaintiffs also argue Defendant “fails to demonstrate that the Song-Beverly statutory violation claims against [Defendant] … are related in any way to the [MVOA] and its arbitration clause.” (Opp., 7:15-17.) Again, the arbitration agreement in question for purposes of this motion is the one that appears in the RISC. For clarity of analysis, the Court disregards the MVOA and makes no ruling on its enforceability. All the arguments in Section III of Plaintiffs’ opposition are irrelevant to this ruling.

                                    ii. Defendant is not equitably estopped from enforcing the RISC.

            Plaintiffs contend Defendant is equitably estopped from enforcing the parties’ agreement. Plaintiffs’ case law is irrelevant, because it refers to circumstances where a third party executed a sales agreement with a lemon law plaintiff. Here, the RISC directly designates Defendant as the seller of the vehicle, and it counter-signed the contract.

/ / /

                                    iii. The RISC arbitration agreement is not unconscionable.

            The unconscionability analysis focuses on two elements: the procedural and the substantive. “ ‘[U]nconscionability has both a “procedural” and a “substantive” element,’ the former focusing on ‘oppression’ or ‘surprise’ due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on ‘overly harsh’ or ‘one-sided’ results. ... ‘The prevailing view is that [procedural and substantive unconscionability] must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability.’ ... But [procedural and substantive unconscionability] need not be present in the same degree. ‘Essentially a sliding scale is invoked which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves.’ ... In other words, the more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 114, citations omitted; see also A&M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486-488.)

            Procedural unconscionability depends on two factors: oppression and surprise. (Crippen v. Central Valley RV Outlet (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1159, 1164-1165.) Plaintiffs do not demonstrate oppression. Oppression depends on “no real negotiation and the absence of meaningful choice[.]” (Olsen v. Breeze, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 608, 621.) Plaintiffs protest they did not negotiate the terms of their agreement, but nothing suggests they were prevented from doing so. No one forced them to buy their Tesla. Plaintiffs have demonstrated minimal surprise at best. “Surprise involves the extent to which the terms of the bargain are hidden in a ‘prolix printed form’ drafted by a party in a superior bargaining position.” (Ibid.) Even assuming the arbitration agreement in the RISC qualifies as a hidden term in a prolix form – and the Court is not convinced it does – nothing suggests Defendant was in a superior bargaining position. As with oppression, however rushed Plaintiffs felt to buy their car, there is nothing in the record that shows why. Defendant was not in such a superior bargaining position as to establish surprise for purposes of procedural unconscionability.

Plaintiffs have at best demonstrated a modicum of procedural unconscionability. Plaintiffs must demonstrate considerable substantive unconscionability to prevail on their opposition.

            But Plaintiffs have also shown, at best, a modicum of substantive unconscionability, not enough to carry their opposition. Two of Plaintiffs’ three arguments on this point, as directed to the RISC, refer to procedure and not substance. (See Opp., 12:11-14 [Plaintiffs “not informed of th[e arbitration] provision” and Defendant “pushed through [the] contract”].) Plaintiffs’ only colorable claim to substantive unconscionability is their assertion that Defendant only agrees to pay the first $5,000.00 of arbitration fees. That is insufficient by itself. “A consumer seeking to avoid an arbitration agreement must make ‘a showing that … fees and costs in fact would be unaffordable or would have a substantial deterrent effect’ on his or decision to seek relief, which the court must determine ‘on a case-by-case basis … .’ ” (Swain v. LaserAway Medical Group, Inc. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 59, 73-74.) Plaintiffs offer no evidence that the cost of arbitration would be unreasonable in their case. They only recite the point as a conclusion in their opposition, without support.

            Defendant carries its burden to produce an enforceable arbitration agreement, and Plaintiffs do not carry their burden to show why it should not be enforced.

 

            5. Conclusion.

The motion is granted. The parties are ordered to submit their dispute to binding arbitration according to the terms of the RISC. The Court stays this action in its entirety pending completion of arbitration, or until further order of the Court. The Court sets an OSC Re: Status of Arbitration for February 19, 2025.
