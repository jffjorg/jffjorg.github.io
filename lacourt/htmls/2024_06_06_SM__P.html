DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV01370    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Steiger v. Nine Eighteen Twenty-First Street HOA, Case No. 23SMCV01370

Hearing date June 6, 2024

Plaintiff Steiger’s Demurrer to Defendants Nine Eighteen Twenty-First Street HOA and Mortazavi’s Second Amended Answer

Plaintiff Steiger alleges the HOA defendants improperly restricted his ability to renovate his residential unit. On December 6, 2023 the court sustained Steiger’s demurrer to the third through twenty-fourth affirmative defenses raised in the HOA defendants’ first amended answer, with twenty days leave to amend. Defendants filed their second amended answer (SAA) January 23, 2024.

Steiger now demurs to the following affirmative defenses raised in the SAA: third (comparative fault), fourth (comparative fault), fifth (unclean hands), sixth (excuse of performance or prevention), seventh (mitigation), eighth (good faith), ninth (waiver/estoppel), tenth (lack of causation), eleventh (one who seeks equity must do equity), twelfth (offset), and fourteenth (business judgment rule).

Affirmative defenses are subject to demurrer when they consist only of legal conclusions without factual support.  FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384.

Steiger first argues that many of the affirmative defenses in the SAA continue to plead legal conclusions, not facts. Additional facts have been alleged beyond what appeared in prior pleadings. Steiger contends these facts are not connected with the various affirmative defenses they are alleged under.

In additional to further allegations regarding the HOA’s CC&Rs and Steiger’s knowledge and acceptance of them at escrow, the following allegations are included:

Additionally, with respect to the washer and dryer installation, the HOA provided specifications on October 25, 2021 to Plaintiff. On October 27, 2021, Plaintiff agreed to the specifications but requested that Unit D pay half the installation costs. As the HOA cannot require a unit owner to pay for installation costs or another unit’s project, the HOA could not agree or approve Plaintiff’s proposal. Plaintiff chose to not proceed with the installation of the washer/dryer as proposed and as agreed to.

Defendants complied with the applicable governing documents, applicable statutes and case law. Defendants acted reasonably and in good faith, and in a manner in which they believed was in the best interests of the entire Association taking into account current and future liability. Defendants, by and through its Board members, met the standard of care for directors of a non-profit community association, acting as ordinary prudent people. They exercised sound business judgment and common sense. The Board members considered the advice and guidance of its management agent, and of other professional individuals including the Defendants’ legal counsel. Defendants, by and through its Board members, did not breach any fiduciary duty and were reasonable in the dealings with Plaintiff and management of the Association and board resolutions.

See SAA at pp. 3-17.

In opposition, the HOA defendants argues that the allegations in the SAA are sufficient because they plead ultimate facts.  They assert the SAA identifies the steps taken and the communications made that support the various affirmative defenses alleged therein.

Upon review of the SAA, the allegations support the contested affirmative defenses. “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)

For instance, with the affirmative defenses of comparative fault, the allegations are that Steiger had been aware of the terms of the CC&Rs and the HOA defendants complied with the governing documents when providing specifications with respect to the washer and dryer installation. It is for this reason that is also alleged that any loss incurred by Plaintiff was due to his own negligence.

For the affirmative defense of unclean hands, based on these same facts, it is alleged that Steiger did not act in good faith during the negotiation process and tried to take advantage of the situation by required the unit owner below Steiger to pay for half of the installation. For the affirmative defense of the business judgment rule, the SAA alleges the HOA defendants complied with the governing documents and acted reasonably and in good faith in their decision making. 

For pleading purposes, these alleges are sufficient to support the contested affirmative defense because it gives sufficient notice for Steiger to prepare his case.  Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240.  If any uncertainty remains, this can easily be resolved during discovery.  Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616. The demurrer is OVERRULED on this ground.

Steiger argues the seventh, eighth, tenth, twelfth and fourteenth affirmative defenses operate as mere denials. However, this is not persuasive because “under a general denial, the defendant[] may urge any defenses tending to show that plaintiff has no right to recover or no right to recover to the extent that he or she claims.” Advantec Group, Inc. v. Edwin's Plumbing Co., Inc. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 624, 627. The demurrer is OVERRULED on this ground.

Lastly, Steiger argues that the HOA defendants’ equitable defenses must be alleged with specificity. This is unpersuasive because the cases cites do not support the proposition of a heightening pleading standard for equitable defenses. The demurrer is OVERRULED on this ground.

OVERRULED.

Tentative Ruling

Williams, et al. v. BMW of North America, et al., Case No. 23SMCV03039

Hearing date June 6, 2024

Plaintiffs Williams’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One.

Plaintiffs allege various violations of the Song-Beverly Act. On October 3, 2023, plaintiff electronically served initial discovery responses on BMW, including Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. Cohen Decl. ¶ 6, Exhs. A-B. On November 8, 2023, BMW served its responses two days beyond the deadline. (Id. at ¶ 8, Exhs. C-D.)

The parties agreed to appear at an Informal Discovery Conference on April 25, 2024, but BMW failed to appear. Id. at ¶¶12-13. The Court reserved a hearing date for this motions on June 6, 2024. Thereafter, BMW stipulated to extend Williams’ deadline to file the instant motions to May 17, 2024. Id. at ¶ 14, Exh. J.

Ultimately, on May 13, 2024, Williams filed and served the instant motions to compel further responses to her Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Production of Documents, Set One.

In opposition, BMW’s counsel asserts she was never served with the moving papers, despite what is claimed in the proofs of service. Dao Decl. ¶ 4-5. BMW requests the Court to reschedule the hearing on the instant motions.

Williams argues BMW was properly served and reasons that BMW’s counsel’s credibility is questionable based on BMW’s failure to provide supplemental responses and not appearing at the informal discovery conference.

The court accepts counsel’s assertion of never being served with the moving papers. Furthe, BMW would be prejudiced if it were not given an opportunity to respond to the arguments presented in the moving papers.

Accordingly, the motions will be continued, with opposition and reply to be filed and served per code.
