DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02618    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: W

JANE DOE 1, et al. vs LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT

 

motions to compel further discovery

 

Date of Hearing:        October 16, 2024                              Trial Date:       None set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02618

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, and Jane Doe 4

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 30, 2022, Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, And Jane Doe 4 filed a lawsuit against Defendants Los Angeles Unified School District for negligence. Plaintiffs were minors and students at elementary schools in LAUSD where their perpetrator was employed. Plaintiffs allege the perpetrator groomed and sexually abused Plaintiffs and LAUSD breached their duty by failing to protect Plaintiffs from sexual abuse.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED in part.

II.                  Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents, Set Three is GRANTED, in part.

III.               Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents, Set Four is GRANTED.

 

discusion

 

I.                    MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFFS’ REQUEST FOR SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,810.00.

 

Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, and Jane Doe 4 move for an order compelling Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District to provide further responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Special Interrogatories Set 1, Nos. 1-23. Plaintiffs make the motion on the grounds Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Special Interrogatories, Set One, contained unmerited objections and assertions as a basis for withholding relevant, informative witness information. Defendant LAUSD provided objection only responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Special Interrogatories, Set One, Numbers 1-23 and as such, good cause exists for an Order compelling Defendant to further respond to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Special Interrogatories by producing such information.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, a court may order a party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the court finds that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. 

 

Plaintiffs argue the special interrogatories seek the identification and last known contact information for LAUSD employees including aides and after school program leaders who worked at all school sites that Rene Tenas was assigned to. Plaintiffs contend they seek this information to investigate what employees may have witnessed, heard of or complained of Rene Tenas’ sexual abuse of students and inappropriate conduct with students. However, LAUSD has made unmeritorious objections and claiming that it does not have said information. It is difficult to believe that Defendant cannot identify individuals who they have hired to work at its school sites and interacted with its minor students.

 

In opposition, LAUSD argues Plaintiffs misrepresent the facts as LAUSD did in fact provide substantive responses to SROG Nos 1-13 and intends to supplement those responses prior to the date of the hearing. As for SROG Nos. 14-23, LAUSD argues Plaintiffs are not entitled to the private personal information and last known addresses of afterschool program leaders for five different elementary schools spanning 17 years. This is so because all alleged incidents occurred only during school instructional hours. Accordingly, such requests are extremely overbroad and violate constitutionally protected privacy rights.

 

The court finds the names of the program leaders may lead to admissible evidence. As noted by Plaintiffs, there is reason to believe Rene Tenas did regularly have students in his classroom afterschool and program leaders are reasonably likely to have been witnesses to Tenas interacting with students, having students in his classroom afterschool and engaging in inappropriate conduct with his students. To the extent that defendant has provided verified responses that it does not know the names of certain former employees or aides, those answers are code compliant and do not need to be supplemented.  If they know the name of after school program leaders, the district is ordered to provide the name and last known address and phone number.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED in part.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs seek $1,810.00 against Defendant and their counsel on the grounds LAUSD’s misuse of discovery is without substantial justification, as LAUSD’s initial responses to 33 requests were incomplete or wholly deficient.

 

In opposition, LAUSD argues sanctions aren’t warranted against LAUSD because Plaintiffs failed to properly meet and confer. As a result, LAUSD should pay sanctions for its failure to properly meet and confer. LAUSD seeks $2,025.00 in reasonable attorney's fees.

 

The court denies both requests for sanctions. The court finds Plaintiffs attempted to properly meet and confer with LAUSD. However, the court finds LAUSD acted with substantial justification in objecting to the SROGS.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFFS’ REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET THREE AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,860

 

Plaintiffs move for an order compelling Defendant LAUSD to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set Three on the grounds LAUSD’s responses are improper, deficient, and not code compliant.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the Court may order a responding to serve a further response to a request for production when the Court finds that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

 

Plaintiffs argue the requested discovery seeks LAUSD documents addressed to Curtis Johnson (former Hart Elementary Principal) and documents regarding tutoring programs and identification of administrators, afterschool program leaders, and campus aides at all school sites Tenas was assigned to. Plaintiffs contend LAUSD provided boilerplate objections and LAUSD’s contention they do not have any responsive documents in their possession, is nothing more than a specious argument as it is highly unlikely that LAUSD does not maintain a list of its employees.

 

In opposition, LAUSD asserts it will supplement its responses to RPD, Set 3 Nos. 159, 163-166, 169, 171, 174, 179, 181, 184, 186, 189, and 191 prior to the hearing date on this motion. As for the remaining responses (RPD Set 3, Nos. 161, 162, 167, 168, 170, 172, 173, 175, 177, 178, 180, 182, 183, 185, 187, 188 and 190) Plaintiffs are either not entitled to the documents sought, i.e. Curtis Johnson’s employee personnel file or the documents do not exist as already stated in the District’s responses.

 

The court OVERRULES to the objections to RPD Set 3, No. 160. Plaintiff is entitled to all documents sent to Curtis explaining the basis for his assignments and reassignments, if they exist.   The court finds request for production 159 overbroad and burdensome as written and potentially invasive of the privacy rights of others.  No further response is required by the LAUSD.  As for RPDs where LAUSD claims they do not have the documents (RPD Set 3 Nos. 167, 168, 172, 173, 175, 177, 178, 180, 182, 183, 185, 187, 188, and 190), the responses are sufficient. LAUSD cannot produce what they do not have. As for RPD No. 170, the court compels further responses if a list of aides exists. 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents, Set Three is GRANTED, in part.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request $2,860.00 in sanctions against LAUSD for LAUSD’s misuse of discovery. LAUSD also requests sanctions in the amount of $1,575.00 for Plaintiffs’ failure to properly meet and confer and failure to tailor its discovery to the scope of this litigation.

 

The court DENIES the request for sanctions for reasons stated above.

 

III.                PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFFS’ REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET FOUR AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,860

 

Plaintiffs move for an order compelling Defendant LAUSD to provide further responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents, Set Four on the grounds LAUSD’s response is improper, deficient, and not code compliant.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the Court may order a responding to serve a further response to a request for production when the Court finds that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

 

Plaintiffs seek unredacted letters sent to LAUSD students who were previously in Tenas’ class, notifying them of the release of their name and contact information and the opportunity for them to object at a hearing on May 7, 2024. Plaintiffs contend LAUSD gave redacted copies only. However, it is critical that Plaintiffs have access to information regarding witness information. Students who were previously in Tenas’ class are reasonably likely to have firsthand knowledge regarding Tenas’ abuse of Plaintiffs and other minor students.

 

LAUSD opposes the motion on the grounds Plaintiffs have not made any showing that they are entitled to each student’s home address. LAUSD contends Plaintiffs have been provided with the student names because the names were located in Tenas’ personnel file. Moreover, to now seek the student’s home addresses from letters that were created in response to a court order for discovery is overreaching and an invasion of these students’ privacy. However, should the court grant Plaintiffs’ Motion and compel the unredacted letters, LAUSD contends it will again have to follow the FERPA procedure in notifying each student of this disclosure of their private information to allow them the opportunity to object.

 

The court finds good cause for production of the unredacted letters. LAUSD has not provided any authority that it needed to give notice yet again to provide copies of these unredacted letters.  At the May 7, 2024, hearing no students appeared to object to the release of their information.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents, Set Four is GRANTED.   Defendant is ordered to provide copies of these letters, without any redactions, within 30 days.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request $1,860.00 in sanctions against LAUSD and their counsel. In opposition, LAUSD also requests sanctions in the amount of $1,350.00 against Plaintiff.

 

Sanctions are denied to both parties, as substantial justification exists for both of their positions.


Case Number: 23VECV04833    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: W

J. A. vs BLUE CROSS OF CALIFORNIA DBA ANTHEM BLUE CROSS

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel pmk deposition

 

Date of Hearing:        October 16, 2024                              Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04833

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff J.A., a minor, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Michael Allen

Responding Party:     Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Glovsky Decl.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff J.A., a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, and Michael Allen filed a complaint against Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross for 1. Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; 2. Breach of Contract; and 3. Violations of Business & Professions Code Section 17200. Plaintiff alleges this action arises out of Anthem’s repeated wrongful and bad faith denials of inpatient hospital admission so that J.A. can have medically necessary brachial plexus (nerve repair) surgery. Plaintiff alleges J.A.’s surgery was cancelled because Anthem denied the required inpatient hospital admission in the intensive care unit (ICU) required for his surgery. As of the date of this complaint, J.A. is still waiting to have surgery.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel PMK Deposition and Production of Documents is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff J.A., a minor, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Michael Allen moves this court for an order compelling Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross to produce for deposition the person(s) most knowledgeable (“PMK”) for a deposition within fourteen (14) days on the following designated Topic Nos. 1 and 2 and to produce all documents responsive to Request for Production Nos. 1 and 2 in Plaintiff’s Notice of PMK Deposition dated July 12, 2024.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2025.450, if after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action, without having served a valid objection, fails to appear for examination, or proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document. (CCP § 2025.450(a).) A motion to compel the deposition of a party to the action must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, or, when the deponent failed to attend the deposition, a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(2).)

 

Plaintiff argues Anthem’s relevance objections to both the topics for deposition and requests for production in the PMK Depo Notice lack merit. In the complaint, J.A. alleges that Anthem financially incentivizes its reviewers to encourage unreasonable denials of care. (Compl. ¶¶ 6, 34, 50.)  Plaintiff contends by intentionally designing and implementing compensation packages that incentivize reviewers to deny claims, or at a minimum create a bias against approving medically necessary treatment, Anthem has employed policies and practices to further its own financial interests at the expense of its members’ health and wellbeing. (Compl. ¶¶ 34(e), 50.) Moreover, this court has previously stated that “Plaintiff can ask at deposition of the person most knowledgeable or other person with knowledge as to whether there are financial or other incentives for denial of coverage and how such incentives work”. (05/23/2024 Minute Order at 4.) Plaintiff further argues the privacy objections do not show how or why the PMK Depo Notice implicates any individual’s privacy rights, especially given the language included in each request that “Plaintiff is not seeking the employment agreements or contracts for these individuals.”

 

In opposition, Anthem argues Plaintiff is seeking beyond what this court might have suggested as appropriate. Anthem contends not only does Plaintiff want a PMK deposition regarding compensation and bonus plans for those Anthem employees involved in the handling of Plaintiff’s pre-service request for an inpatient admission but also all documents and communications relating thereto. Moreover, the Elevance Health Incentive Compensation Plan that Plaintiff points to, Anthem argue, expressly provides that: “The Performance Measures shall not include or derive from the number or frequency of (i) denials of authorization for benefit coverage; or (ii) reductions or limitations on hospital lengths of stay, medical services or charges. Performance Measures shall not be designed, construed or used to incentivize the withholding of medically necessary services or the denial of benefits to which members are entitled.” (Glovsky Decl. Exh. 6.) Lastly, Anthem has and will continue to allow Plaintiff to ask its employee witnesses whether they are aware of any “financial or other incentives for denial of coverage” and, if so, “how such incentives work.”

 

The court finds Topics 1 and 2 relevant only as to the general basis for compensation and potential bonus compensation of Anthem. The court finds any inquiry into individuals’ actual compensation  to be an invasion of their privacy rights and overbroad. Accordingly, the court finds Request for Production Nos 1 and 2 to be overbroad as framed. The PMQ must provide documents that set forth the basis for compensation and any potential bonus compensation structure but not documents that may include individual information such as any W2s or 1099s.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests sanctions against Anthem in the amount of $1,000.00 for its obstructionist tactics which have required plaintiff to bring this motion despite this discovery’s direct relevance to the issues in this case and the Court’s prior statements in this regard.

 

The court DENIES the request for sanctions.
