DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23CECV01876    Hearing Date: February 21, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Mahesh C. Sood,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   vs.

 

Teresita N. Sood, Trustee Service Corporation, Citibank N.A., Ally Bank, MERS, Executive Trustee Services, Inc., and Does 1–100, inclusive

Defendant.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   23VECV01876                   107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

(1)  Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories,

(2)  Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, and

(3)  Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production of Documents

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE:]

 

The Motions to Compel are GRANTED. Teresita’s request for sanctions against Mahesh is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $992.00.

 

BACKGROUND

 

I.         Factual

 

The following factual background is drawn from the Complaint (as defined below.)

 

Plaintiff Mahesh C. Sood (“Mahesh”) and defendant Teresita N. Sood (“Teresita”) are co-owners of a certain real property (the “Property”). The remaining named defendants (“Lenders” and, together with Teresita, “Defendants”) are lenders or other parties who may have an interest in the Property.

 

During his ownership of the Property, Mahesh spent over $100,000.00 on the Property, including on maintenance, improvements, and taxes. Mahesh has demanded Teresita pay “[her] share of such costs and expenses paid and advanced by [Mahesh],” but she has refused to do so. Mahesh desires and has demanded that the Property be partitioned, but Teresita has refused to consent to a partition.

 

Mahesh now seeks partition by sale of the Property, restitution of 50% of the money he spent on the Property, attorney’s fees, and costs.

 

II.        Procedural

 

On April 28, 2023, Mahesh filed a complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants with causes of action for (1) partition of real property by sale and restitution as deduction against defendant’s interest in proceeds of sale, and (2) restitution based on unjust enrichment.

 

On November 8, 2023, Teresita propounded on Mahesh a set of special interrogatories, a set of form interrogatories, and a set of requests for production. (Miller Decl. ¶ 2.) Mahesh did not respond to any of the discovery requests by their response deadlines. (Id., ¶ 3.) Teresita’s counsel then sought to meet and confer with Mahesh, but Mahesh again did not respond. (Id., ¶¶ 4–5.)

 

On January 22, 2024, Teresita filed the three instant motions to compel (the “Motions”) against Mahesh. The Motions are unopposed. [NOTE: The filing deadline for any opposition was February 6, 2024.]

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.         “Meet and Confer”

 

“Unlike a motion to compel further responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) Because no response has been provided to the instant discovery requests and Plaintiff files motions to compel, not motions to compel further, there is no requirement for parties to meet and confer prior to the filing of the Motions.

 

II.        Responses to Discovery Requests

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response,…[t]he party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Cal. C. Civ. Proc. § 2030.290.) And, “a party's failure to object to interrogatories within 30 days, or such additional time as extended by the court, constitutes a waiver of any right to object.” (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Similarly, “[i]f a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]…[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.” (Cal. C. Civ. Proc. § 2031.300.) In the absence of any response to the discovery request, all that a motion to compel need show is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See id., 905–06.)

 

Teresita attaches to the Motions evidence that each discovery request was properly served on Mahesh’s attorney by mail at his correct address on November 8, 2023. (Motions Exh. “1.”) Mahesh’s responses thereto were therefore due 35 days later, on December 13, 2023. (Cal. C. Civ. Proc. §§ 1013(a), 2016.050, 2030.260(a), 2031.260(a).) Teresita attaches to the Motions evidence that no response of any kind to any of the three discovery requests was received by that response due date or thereafter. (Miller Decl. ¶ 5.) Thus, Teresita has met her burden of showing that the Motions are necessary.

 

Accordingly, Teresita’s motion to compel responses to special interrogatories is GRANTED; Teresita’s motion to compel responses to form interrogatories is GRANTED; and Teresita’s motion to compel responses to requests for production of documents is GRANTED.

 

III.      Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. CCP § 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) (the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed). “As to the amount of sanctions, Cal. C. Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) authorizes the trial court to impose an amount representing ‘the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result’ of a party's misuse of the discovery process. (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer United States, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.)

 

Each of the Motions request sanctions in the amount of $1,510.00 for a total of $4,530.00. This is based upon five hours of attorney time for each Motion at a rate of $290.00 per hour plus a $60.00 expense to file each Motion. Teresita attaches a declaration by her attorney to each Motion, however, that explains that the five-hour estimates were reached by summing 0.3 hours for drafting each meet and confer letter, 1.5 hours to prepare each Motion and assemble the Motion’s exhibits, and 3.2 hours to review Mahesh’s opposition, prepare a reply, and appear at the hearing. (Miller Decl. ¶¶ 6–8.)

 

These calculations reveal some duplication in time. First, we note that no oppositions were ever filed, thus Teresita’s attorney never needed to review such oppositions or prepare replies. Second, the Motions are now being heard together, meaning Teresita’s attorney only needs to appear at one hearing. He notes that the hearing will take “additional time since [his] office is in Van Nuys,” but we note that the courthouse in this case is conveniently also located in Van Nuys and is, in fact, less than four miles away from his office. We believe that an hour would cover both his travel time to and from the courthouse and appearance time at the hearing on the Motions. Thirdly, we notice that the three meet and confer letters are nearly identical and were all emailed on the same day. We therefore believe Teresita’s attorney should only be permitted to bill 0.3 hours once for all three letters. Lastly, we notice that the Motions are nearly identical and were all filed on the same day. We therefore believe Teresita’s attorney should only be permitted to bill 1.5 hours once for all three Motions. Thus, we believe that the reasonable amount of attorney time spent on the Motions is the sum of one hour, 0.3 hours, and 1.5 hours, or 2.8 hours. 2.8 hours billed at a rate of $290.00 per hour sums to $812.00. Adding the $180.00 expense to file the Motions, we believe Teresita is entitled to recover $992.00 in sanctions.

 

Accordingly, Teresita’s request for sanctions against Mahesh is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $992.00. Sanctions are payable within 30 days of service of this order.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:    

(1) Demurrer to Complaint

(2) Request for Judicial Notice

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE:]

 

The Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED. The Request for Judicial Notice is GRANTED.

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

A. Factual

 

The following factual background is drawn from the Complaint (as defined below).

 

On February 1, 2019, plaintiff Ani Yapunjyan (“Plaintiff”) was involved in a motor vehicle collision with another driver, Maria Martinez (“Martinez”). At the time, Plaintiff carried an automobile insurance policy issued by defendant Wawanesa General Insurance Company (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”). Plaintiff reported the collision to Defendant, who issued a claim number and placed the claim under investigation.

 

In November 2019, Defendant denied Plaintiff’s claim and notified Plaintiff of such denial (via the “Denial Letter”).

 

In January 2021, Plaintiff litigated her personal injury claim against Martinez, and in May 2023, Plaintiff recovered Martinez’s policy limits in exchange for a release of her claims against Martinez and Martinez’s insurance carrier.

 

On May 8, 2023, Plaintiff emailed an underinsured policy limit demand to Defendant, which was denied by Defendant on May 19, 2023.

 

 

B. Procedural

 

On November 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) breach of insurance contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) violations of California Insurance Code § 790.03; (4) negligence, and (5) violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200, et seq.

 

On December 14, 2024, Parties met and conferred over the telephone, and Plaintiff agreed to dismiss certain causes of action. (Edson Decl. ¶ 2.) Parties agreed to extend the deadline for Defendant to file the demurrer from January 6, 2024 to January 16, 2024 to give Plaintiff time to do so. (Id., ¶ 3.) On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff voluntarily requested dismissal, without prejudice, of her third, fourth, and fifth causes of action. The dismissal was entered. Thus, the only remaining causes of action are for (1) breach of insurance contract and (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

On January 17, 2024, Defendant demurred (via the “Demurrer”) to the Complaint in its entirety and to both remaining causes of action for failing to state facts sufficient to constitute the causes of action against Defendant. Concurrently, Defendant filed a Request for Judicial Notice, requesting this court take judicial notice of the Denial Letter.

 

On February 8, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the Demurrer (via the “Opposition”). Defendants did not file a timely reply.

 

II. PARTY ARGUMENTS

           

            The Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to both causes of action in the Complaint on the grounds that each cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Defendant contends that both causes of action are time barred and that California “favors the enforcement of statutes of limitations.” (Demurrer p. 6.) Defendant argues that the statutes of limitations for all of Plaintiff’s claims began running on November 20, 2019, the date Defendant denied her insurance claim. (Id., 8.) Thus, the two-year statute of limitations that Defendant applies to the “bad faith” tort cause of action expired on November 20, 2021, and the four-year statute of limitations that Defendant applies to the breach of contract cause of action expired on November 20, 2023, seven days before Plaintiff filed the Complaint. (Id., 8–9.) Defendant denies that the statutes of limitation were tolled by either Plaintiff’s May 8, 2023 demand for reconsideration or by the COVID-19 pandemic’s emergency rules. (Id., 9–11.)

 

            The Opposition

 

Plaintiff agrees that the statutes of limitations for all of Plaintiff’s claims began running on November 20, 2019 but rebuts that California’s Emergency Rule 9 tolled both statutes of limitations between April 6, 2020 and October 1, 2020, thereby extending Plaintiff’s deadline to file her claims by 178 days. (Opposition p. 3.) As both parties agree it has a four-year statute of limitations, this extension would clearly make Plaintiff’s breach of contract cause of action timely. Plaintiff further argues that her cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing also has a four-year statute of limitations and is similarly timely because it arises in contract, not tort. (Id., p. 5–6.) Finally, Plaintiff seeks leave to amend if the demurrer is sustained. (Id., p. 6.)

 

            The Reply

 

No reply was timely filed.

 

III. ANALYSIS

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.)

 

A. First Cause of Action for Breach of Insurance Contract

 

Parties agree that a four-year statute of limitations apply to the first cause of action for breach of insurance contract. They disagree as to when the statute of limitations expires on this cause of action, which depends on whether the statute of limitations was tolled by California’s Emergency Rule 9.

 

Emergency rule 9 was adopted by the Judicial Council during the COVID-19 pandemic and, as amended, reads: “(a) Tolling statutes of limitations over 180 days [¶] Notwithstanding any other law, the statutes of limitations and repose for civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.” (People v. Financial Casualty & Surety, Inc. (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 879, 885.) “The Advisory Committee comment explains the application of the rule: ‘Emergency rule 9 is intended to apply broadly to toll any statute of limitations on the filing of a pleading in court asserting a civil cause of action.’” (Ibid.) “The Judicial Council [further] explained the background of Emergency rule 9, and the reasons for amending it, in a Circulating Order.” (People v. Financial Casualty & Surety, Inc. (2021) 73 Cal.App.5th 33, 39.)

 

The Circulating Order begins with a sentence that explains that Emergency Rule 9 was adopted “[t]o protect parties who have a civil cause of action that accrued before or during the state of emergency related to the COVID-19 pandemic.” (Circulating Order No: CO-20-09, available at https://www.alameda.courts.ca.gov/system/files?file=co-20-09.pdf p. 1.) The Circulating Order defends the necessity of emergency rule 9 by clarifying that it “provided parties and attorneys additional time to investigate, gather information and evidence, and determine whether to file an action.” (Id., p. 4.) It defends the amendment shortening the tolling period by explaining that the California stay-at-home order was being lifted and courts were beginning to open and accept filings. (Id., pp. 4–5.) And, it defends the amendment’s longer tolling period for statutes of limitations longer than 180 days by saying that “the matters with longer statutes of limitations require investigations and information gathering—actions difficult to complete swiftly in the current chaotic business environment.” (Id., p. 5.) Finally, under a heading about clarifying the scope of the rule, the Circulating Order declares that “the rule applies to all statutory limitation periods for a civil cause of action.” (Id., p. 6.) Thus, the Circulating Order implies that all litigants whose claims accrued before or during the COVID-19 pandemic were harmed thereby because of their inability to investigate or gather information about their claims before filing, because of the stay-at-home order, and because of court closures. The Circulating Order further clarifies that the application of Emergency Rule 9 is not meant to be limited to only some litigants but rather that it should “broadly apply” to all litigants who had not yet made their initial filings on claims for which the statute of limitations was running during the COVID-19 pandemic.

 

Defendant relies on Shubin v. Universal Vacation Club to establish that “courts determining the application of Emergency Rule 9 have considered whether a plaintiff faced any extraordinary circumstances due to COVID-19 that prevented the timely filing of their lawsuit.” (Shubin v. Universal Vacation Club (2022) 622 F.Supp.3d 849, 853.) However, Shubin v. Universal Vacation Club deals with “a diversity case in federal court,” where there has been hesitancy to apply Emergency Rule 9, “at least in cases exercising federal question jurisdiction,” “in part [because] Emergency Rule 9 was issued by the California Judicial Council, and not any federal court or body.” (Ibid.) The federal court in that case believed it to be “unclear” if Emergency Rule 9 applied specifically to federal diversity cases. (Id., n. 1.) And, even Shubin v. Universal Vacation Club reiterates that Emergency Rule 9 clearly applies in California state courts. (Id., 852–53.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff is not required to plead any hardship due to the COVID-19 pandemic, and we find that Emergency Rule 9 did toll the statutes of limitations for her causes of action between April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020, affording her 178 extra days to file her causes of action. Therefore, her cause of action for breach of insurance contract is timely so long as it is filed within 4 years and 178 days of November 20, 2019, or by May 16, 2024, assuming that Plaintiff filed her claim with Defendant on the same day as the auto collision. As Plaintiff filed the Complaint before that date, this cause of action is timely.

 

Accordingly, because Plaintiff’s cause of action for breach of insurance contract is not time barred, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer to the first cause of action.

 

B. Second Cause of Action for Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Parties disagree as to what statute of limitations applies to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Defendant applies a two-year statute of limitations for tort remedies thereunder whereas Plaintiff would apply a four-year statute of limitations for contract remedies.

 

An insurer’s breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, commonly referred to as “bad faith,” “sounds in both contract and tort.” (Crisci v. Security Ins. Co. of New Haven, Conn. (1967) 66 Cal.2d 425, 432; Archdale v. American Internat. Specialty Lines Ins. Co. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 449, n. 19; Frazier v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 90, 101.) “[W]here a case sounds both in contract and tort the plaintiff will ordinarily have freedom of election between an action of tort and one of contract.” (Crisci v. Security Ins. Co. of New Haven, Conn., supra at 432.) While Defendant cites a number of cases to support its application of a two-year statute of limitations, these cases all refer to tort actions for bad faith and Defendant does not address contract actions for the same. “There is a significant difference, however, in the available remedies.” (Archdale v. American Internat. Specialty Lines Ins. Co., supra at n. 19.) “Whether the insured's remedy will be in contract or tort will depend on the nature of the relief or recovery sought by the insured.” (Id., 466.) An insured’s claim brought in tort will have broader remedies available, but a shorter statute of limitations, where the same claim brought in contract will have a longer statute of limitations but “then recovery is limited to those damages recoverable in contract.” (Id., n. 19.) Moreover, a plaintiff’s election to initially proceed on a tort rather than a contract theory does not later preclude that plaintiff from changing her election to proceed on a contract theory. (Frazier v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co., supra, 101–02.)

 

Here, Plaintiff is free to elect to proceed on her cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing on a contract theory rather than a tort theory. This is true even though the Complaint specifies that the cause of action is for “tortious” breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing because, following Frazier v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co., supra, 101–02, Plaintiff may change her election after filing her initial pleadings. This election extends to Plaintiff a four-year statute of limitations, though it also limits her remedies. Once we find that the four-year statute of limitations applies, we may further find that Emergency Rule 9 tolled the statute of limitations between April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020 and that this cause of action is timely brought so long as it is filed before May 16, 2024, assuming that Plaintiff filed her claim with Defendant on the same day as the auto collision.

 

Accordingly, because Plaintiff’s cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is not time barred, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer to the second cause of action.

 

C. The Request for Judicial Notice

 

“Judicial notice is the recognition and acceptance by the court, for use by the trier of fact or by the court, of the existence of a matter of law or fact that is relevant to an issue in the action without requiring formal proof of the matter.” (Unruh-Haxton v. Regents of University of California (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 343, 364.) “Judicial notice may not be taken of any matter unless authorized or required by law.” (Cal. Evid. C. § 450.) Matters subject to judicial notice are listed in Cal. Evid. C. §§ 451–452. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113.) “The underlying theory of judicial notice is that the matter being judicially noticed is a law or fact that is not reasonably subject to dispute.” (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 (emphasis original).) “Although the existence of a document may be judicially noticeable, the truth of statements contained in the document and its proper interpretation are not subject to judicial notice if those matters are reasonably disputable.” (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp., supra, 113 (emphasis original); Tenet Healthsystem Desert, Inc. v. Blue Cross of California (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 821, 836.)

 

Courts may take judicial notice of the existence of a document. (Unruh-Haxton v. Regents of University of California, supra, 364–65.) Courts may take judicial notice of the existence of a “legally operative document—like a contract”—and of “facts that clearly derive from its legal effect.” (Scott v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 754 (emphasis removed).) “When a court is asked to take judicial notice of a document, the propriety of the court's action depends upon the nature of the facts of which the court takes notice from the document.” (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 265.) “[A] court may take judicial notice of the fact of a document's recordation, the date the document was recorded and executed, the parties to the transaction reflected in a recorded document, and the document's legally operative language, assuming there is no genuine dispute regarding the document's authenticity.” (Ibid.)

 

Defendant seeks judicial notice of a document, the Denial Letter. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.) First, we note that “[s]trictly speaking, a court takes judicial notice of facts, not documents.” (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A., supra, 265.) Defendant clarifies in a footnote that it “is not seeking judicial notice of any contents of the letter besides its calendar date,” presumably to establish the date on which the statutes of limitations on Plaintiff’s claims began to run. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.) Effectively, Defendant asks this court to take judicial notice of the existence of the Denial Letter, the date of the Denial Letter, the names in the Denial Letter, and the Denial Letter’s effect, which was to deny Plaintiff’s insurance claim. This is permissible. (See Julian Volunteer Fire Co. Assn. v. Julian-Cuyamaca Fire Protection Dist. (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 583, 600 (“In taking judicial notice of an official document, a court may take notice not only of the fact of the document but also facts that can be deduced, and/or clearly derived from, its legal effect, such as the names and dates contained in the document, and the legal consequences of the document.”).)

 

We note that the Denial Letter differs from the documents of which judicial notice is taken in the above cited cases because the Denial Letter is not an official document that has been either generated or authenticated by a government entity nor is it a recorded document like a deed of trust. However, we do not believe this difference prevents the Denial Letter from being judicially noticed. Rather, these cases emphasize the official nature of the documents because that nature creates the statutory authorization for judicial notice to be taken thereof under Cal. Evid. C. § 452(c). Defendant relies on a footnote in Ingram v. Flippo (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1280, n. 3 for its assertion that “[c]ourts may also take judicial notice of any document referred to in the complaint where that document forms the basis for allegations set forth in the complaint.” (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.) However, Cal. Evid. C. § 452 also provides authorization for judicial notice to be taken, including “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Cal. Evid. C. § 452(h).) We believe that the existence of, date of, names on, and effect of the Denial Letter are capable of immediate and accurate determination by looking to the text of the Denial Letter without attempting to interpret its contents. Moreover, these facts and propositions are not subject to dispute, as Plaintiff does not dispute them.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the request for judicial notice of the existence of the Denial Letter, the date on the Denial Letter, the names in the Denial Letter, and the effect of the Denial Letter.

 

IV. CONCLUSION

 

Overrule the Demurrer in its entirety. Grant the request for judicial notice.
