DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies). 


Case Number: 23CHCV02198    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: F49

Dept. F49

Date: 10/10/24

Case Name: S&R5K, LLC v. Yahaira F. Kolb, Valley Goddess, Inc. and Does 1 to 25

Case No. 23CHCV02198

 

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT F49

 

OCTOBER 10, 2024

 

MOTION TO DEEM THE TRUTH OF MATTERS SPECIFIED IN REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION, SET ONE, ADMITTED AND CONCLUSIVELY ESTABLISHED; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS [7004]

Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23CHCV02198

 

Motion filed: 5/31/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff S&R5K, LLC

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Yahaira F. Kolb

NOTICE: OK.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order from this Court to deem admitted the truth specified in the Requests for Admission, Set One, and imposing monetary sanctions against Defendant Yahaira F. Kolb in the amount of $1,186.65.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is DENIED. The request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED IN PART.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from disputes over a commercial lease for the property at 10249 ½ Sepulveda Boulevard, Mission Hills, California 91345 (the “Leased Premises”).

 

On July 24, 2023, Plaintiff S&R5k, LLC (“Plaintiff” or “S&R5K”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Yahaira F. Kolb (“Kolb”), Valley Goddess, Inc. (“Valley Goddess”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and Does 1 to 25, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Lease – Failure to Timely Pay Rent, (2) Breach of Lease – Failure to Maintain Good Condition, (3) Negligent Failure to Maintain Good Condition of Property, and (4) Conversion. Subsequently, on September 8, 2023, Defendants filed their Answer to the Complaint.

 

On May 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted and Conclusively Established (the “Motion”). Subsequently, Defendant Kolb filed her Opposition to the Motion on October 1, 2024.

 

No Reply papers have been received by the Court.

 

ANALYSIS

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)¿

 

If a party to whom request for admissions is served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)¿

 

A.    Motion to Deem Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted

 

Plaintiff’s counsel, Ramshin Daneshi (“Daneshi”), attests that the Requests for Admission (“RFA”), Set One, was served on Defendant Kolb, via USPS first-class mail with return receipt requested, on March 11, 2024. (Daneshi Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. “A.”) Responses to the RFA was due on April 15, 2024; however, no responses were received on the due date. (Id. ¶ 7.)

 

In the Opposition, Kolb’s counsel, Gabriela Gevorkian (“Gevorkian”), declares that on September 25, 2024, code-compliant responses to the RFA, without objection and with verifications, were mailed to Plaintiff’s counsel’s office. (Gevorkian Decl. ¶ 16, Ex. “H.”) Gevorkian further claims that her firm was retained by Kolb and substituted in as counsel of record on May 31, 2024 (Id. ¶ 6.), after the responses became due. The Motion was served upon Defendant on June 3, 2024. (Id. ¶ 7.) Gevorkian has worked with Defendants diligently to gather facts and prepare discovery responses. (Id. ¶ 8.)   

 

Based on the above records, the Court determines that Defendant Kolb failed to serve a timely response by the April 14, 2024, due date. However, since responses to the RFA were subsequently served, without objection, prior to the hearing, and Plaintiff did not file a Reply to contest this claim, the Court deems the responses to be in substantial compliance with code requirements, absent any evidence from Plaintiff to the contrary.    

 

Accordingly, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c), the Court DENIES the Motion. The only remaining issue is the matter of sanctions.

 

A.    Monetary Sanctions

 

“It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

Here, Gevorkian asserts that both parties reached an agreement to resolve the discovery dispute, with her client paying $1,186.65 in attorney’s fees in exchange for Plaintiff’s withdrawal of all pending motions to compel discovery responses. (Gevorkian Decl. ¶ 13.) A check for $1,186.65 was mailed on behalf of Defendant to Plaintiff’s counsel’s office on September 25, 2024. (Id. ¶ 16.) However, on September 27, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel, in an email to Defendants’ counsel, claimed that the sanction amount was not agreed upon. (Id. ¶ 18, Ex. “G.”) Subsequently, Plaintiff’s counsel declined Defendant’s another offer of $2,000.00 to resolve the matter. (Ibid.)  

 

The Court emphasizes that monetary sanctions are mandatory under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c). Utilizing the lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the Motion is $511.65, calculated based on a reasonable hourly rate of $450.00 for one hour reasonably spent plus a $61.65 filing fee.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS IN PART Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff S&R5K LLC’s Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted and Conclusively Established is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff S&R5K LLC’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED IN PART.

 

Defendant Yahaira F. Kolb is ordered to pay $511.65 in sanctions to Plaintiff’s counsel within 20 days.

TENTATIVE RULING: The demurrer is OVERRULED. The motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from alleged harassment, discrimination, retaliation, and multiple Labor Code and Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”) violations by the employer Defendants.

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff Summer Hopkins (“Hopkins” or “Plaintiff”) initiated this action by filing a Complaint against Defendants Omid Vesal, M.D. (“Dr. Vesal”) d/b/a Irvine Urgent Care (“IUC”) and Carlos Ernesto Monge (“Monge”) (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

On August 7, 2024, the Court sustained a demurrer with motion to strike, filed by Defendants on July 24, 2024, and granted Plaintiff 30 days leave to amend. On the same day, August 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Hostile Work Environment Sexual Harassment, (2) Failure to Prevent and Remedy Harassment, (3) Disability Discrimination in Violation of FEHA, (4) Failure to Accommodate in Violation of FEHA, (5) Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process in Violation of FEHA, (6) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA, (7) Assault & Battery, (8) Violation of Labor Code §§ 233, 234, and 246.5, (9) Violation of Labor Code §§ 226.7 and 512 (Failure to Provide Adequate Meal & Rest Breaks), (10) Violation of Labor Code § 226(a) (Inaccurate Wage Statements), (11) Violation of Labor Code §§ 200-204 (Waiting Time Penalties), and (12) Violation of Labor Code §§ 1102.5 and 98.6.

 

On September 9, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Demurrer with Motion to Strike the FAC. Subsequently, on September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition, and Defendants replied on the same day.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal 3d 311, 318.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered.

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.) 

 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Stafford v. Shultz, 42 Cal. 2d 767, 782 (1954) [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].) An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

A.    Demurrer

 

1)      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A party filing a demurrer “shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a).) A failure to meet and confer does not constitute grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., sections 430.41 (a)(4).)

 

            Here, Defendants have satisfied this requirement (Hodges Decl. ¶¶ 2-4.)

 

2)      Third Cause of Action – Disability Discrimination in Violation of FEHA

 

An employer is liable under Government Code sections 12940 and 12926.1 (FEHA) for discrimination because of an employee’s disability when: (1) the plaintiff has a disability or medical condition or was regarded as suffering from a disability; (2) the plaintiff could perform the essential duties of the job with or without reasonable accommodations; (3) the defendant made an adverse employment decision; and (4) the decision was because of plaintiff’s actual or perceived disability or medical condition. ((Faust v. Cal. Portland Cement Co.  (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 864, 886; King v. United Parcel Service, Inc.  (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 426, 432; Jensen v. Wells Fargo Bank (2000) 85 Cal. App. 4th 245, 254.)

 

A physical disability under FEHA includes “[h]aving any physiological disease, disorder, condition, cosmetic disfigurement, or anatomical loss that” both affects one or more of the body's major systems and “[l]imits a major life activity.” (Gov. Code § 12926, subd. (m)(1).)  Major life activity is “broadly construed” and includes working. (Gov. Code § 12926, subd. (m)(1)(B)(iii).) FEHA protects individuals not only from discrimination based on an existing physical disability, but also from discrimination based on a potential disability or the employer's perception that the individual has an existing or potential disability. (Gov. Code §§ 12926, subd. (m)(4), (5), 12926.1, subd. (b).)

           

To show disability, the plaintiff must demonstrate condition makes “difficult” work or some major life activity; and, to show one is regarded as having a disability, one must be regarded as having a condition making major life activity difficult, or as having a condition that may become disabling (Gelfo v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 34, 46-47). FEHA’s definition of disability does not exclude all temporary, nonchronic impairments. (See Diaz v. Federal Express Corp. (C.D.Cal.2005) 373 F.Supp.2d 1034, 1046-1053.) However, a “disability” does not include conditions with “little or no residual effects, such as the common cold; seasonal or common influenza; minor cuts, sprains, muscle aches, soreness, bruises, or abrasions; non-migraine headaches, and minor and non-chronic gastrointestinal disorders.” (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 2, § 11065, subd. (d)(9)(B).)

 

Previously, the Court sustained Defendants’ first demurrer, finding that Plaintiff’s allegations of her medical conditions in the Complaint were inadequate to qualify as a protected disability under FEHA. In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges, “On or about February 1, 2024, a cyst in Plaintiff’s ovaries, which had caused Plaintiff pain for multiple months, ruptured and caused Plaintiff very severe pain. Plaintiff texted MONGE, Bryan, and the owner of Defendant IRVINE URGENT CARE, Dr. Vesal, informing them that she is in severe pain and is unable to work due to the serious medical condition, her ruptured ovarian cyst. This text message from Plaintiff to Dr. Vesal placed Defendants on notice of Plaintiff’s need for an accommodation of at least one day off work, and possibly more due to the residual effects of Plaintiff’s ruptured ovarian cyst and her abnormally strong menstrual flow. An emergency room physician later confirmed that Plaintiff’s ovarian cyst had ruptured on February 1, 2024.” (FAC ¶ 22) (Underlines added).

 

            Furthermore, the FAC alleges that “[the ruptured ovarian cyst] made it more difficult for Plaintiff to work, and prevented Plaintiff from working, sitting, walking, and standing without severe pain.” (FAC ¶ 64.)

 

            The Court finds that Plaintiff’s amended specific allegations are sufficient to state a “physical disability” within FEHA’s definition as set forth in Government Code section 12926 subdivision m, which provides, “‘Physical disability’” includes ... any physiological disease, disorder, condition, cosmetic disfigurement, or anatomical loss that does both of the following: (A) Affects one or more of the following body systems: ... reproductive [] (B) Limits a major life activity ... includ[ing] physical, mental, and social activities and working.” (Gov. Code § 12926, subd. (m)(1).)

 

            In the Demurrer, Defendants argue that “if the disability is the ruptured cyst (FAC ¶ 64), the disability did not exist until February 1, 2024. If the disability is plaintiff s vaginal blood issue or the cyst before it ruptured[,] the FAC has no allegations about how this issue affected her ‘throughout her employment’ or her major life activities. (FAC ¶ 65).” Defendants maintain that “[e]ither way, the FAC does not adequately identify an actual disability for which FEHA protections exist.” (Dem. at p. 9.)

 

              The Court notes that FEHA’s definition of disability does not exclude all temporary, nonchronic impairments. (See Diaz v. Federal Express Corp. (C.D.Cal.2005) 373 F.Supp.2d 1034, 1046-1053.) Additionally, as outlined in Government Code section 12926.1, the Legislature finds and declares that the law contains broad definitions of physical disability, mental disability, and medical condition, and the intent of the Legislature is to protect applicants and employees from discrimination due to an actual or perceived physical or mental impairment that is disabling, potentially disabling, or perceived as disabling or potentially disabling. (Gov. Code § 12926.1, subd. (b).)

 

Consequently, the Court does not need to determine whether a specific disabling condition is a short-term or long-term disability in order to trigger the FEHA protection, rejecting Defendants’ argument that Plaintiff is excluded the protection under FEHA due to the duration of the disability, specifically because her ovarian cyst rupture occurred only one day before an adverse employment decision was made against her.

 

Furthermore, applying a liberal interpretation of the allegations for the purpose of ruling on a demurrer, the Court also finds that disabling residual effects that substantially limit major life activities can be reasonably inferred from the allegation where Plaintiff, in responding to Dr. Vesal’s inquiry, stated, “I’ll call you in a second I’m crying in pain right now.” (FAC ¶ 24.)

 

Therefore, the Court finds the alleged medical condition – specifically the ruptured ovarian cyst – constitutes a qualifying disability under FEHA. For the purpose of reviewing the Demurrer, the Court does not need to find whether other physical conditions alleged by Plaintiff qualifies as additional disabilities or whether a disability existed throughout Plaintiff’s employment. At this stage of proceedings, the FAC would survive the demurrer challenge if the cause of action is stated under any possible legal theory. (See Franklin v. The Monadnock Co. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 252, 257.)

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that the FAC has addressed the Complaint’s deficiencies by sufficiently pleading a qualifying disability.         The Court hereby OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Third Cause of Action.

 

3)      Fourth Cause of Action – Failure to Accommodate in Violation of FEHA

 

A cause of action for failure to accommodate in the interactive process under FEHA requires (1) plaintiff has a disability or medical condition or was regarded as suffering from a disability, (2) plaintiff could perform the essential duties of a sought reassigned with or without reasonable accommodations, and (3) the employer failed to reasonably accommodate. (Wilson v. County of Orange (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1185, 1193 [reasonable accommodations include job restructuring, modified work schedules, reassignments, providing readers or interpreters, and paid or unpaid leave])

 

            The Demurrer challenges the first element, arguing that a qualifying disability or medical condition has not been sufficiently established. However, as previously analyzed, the Court finds that the FAC now adequately states elements of the FEHA causes of action, specifically, that Plaintiff’s medical condition – an ovarian cyst rupture – constitutes a qualifying disability.

 

Furthermore, Defendants’ knowledge of Plaintiff’s disabling condition is sufficiently pleaded. The FAC alleges that Plaintiff provided notice via text message to Dr. Vesal, Bryan, and Defendant Monge on February 1, 2024, prior to Plaintiff’s termination. (FAC ¶¶ 22, 61, 65, 78.)

 

            Consequently, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Fourth Cause of Action.

 

4)      Fifth Cause of Action – Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process in Violation of FEHA

 

Similar to the Fourth Cause of Action, Government Code section 12940, subdivision (n), requires an employer who receives a request for an accommodation to “engage in a timely, good faith, interactive process” with an employee “with a known” physical disability.

 

For the same reasons stated in the previous analysis, the Court finds elements of the claim are sufficiently stated.

 

Therefore, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Fifth Cause of Action.

 

5)      Sixth Cause of Action – Retaliation in Violation of FEHA

 

Government Code section 12940, subdivision (h), provides that it is unlawful employment practice for any employer or person to discharge, expel, or otherwise discriminate against any person because the person has opposed any practices forbidden or because the person has filed a complaint, testified, or assisted in any proceeding under FEHA.

 

“The elements of a claim for retaliation in violation of [the FEHA], are: ‘(1) the employee's engagement in a protected activity ...; (2) retaliatory animus on the part of the employer; (3) an adverse action by the employer; (4) a causal link between the retaliatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damages; and (6) causation.” (Le Mere v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 237, 243 (Le Mere).)

 

i)                   Protected Activities

 

            The FAC alleges that on January 10, 2024, Plaintiff opposed sexual harassment and assault by Defendant Monge and reported his behavior to her staffing manager, Bryan. (FAC ¶¶ 17, 38, 54, 106, 122, 174.) Additionally, Plaintiff claims that on February 1, 2024, she used a protected sick day and subsequently opposed Defendant’s unlawful denial of this sick leave. (Id. ¶ 25.) Later that same day, Bryan allegedly sent Plaintiff a text message stating that Dr. Vesal had instructed him to terminate Plaintiff’s employment. (Id. ¶ 26.)

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that the Sixth Cause of Action is based on two protected activities under FEHA – namely her request for an accommodation for an alleged disability and her complaint about Monge’s sexual harassment.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s argument persuasive. First, requesting reasonable accommodation for a disability is an activity protected from retaliation (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (m)(2).)       Second, Plaintiff states specific allegations regarding Defendant Monge’s sexual harassment and alleges that she reported to unlawful harassment to staff manager Bryan on January 10, 2024. It is undisputed that opposing to sexual harassment is a protected activity against employer’s retaliation under FEHA. (Gov. Code, § 12940, subdivision (j)(1), [the section sets forth, in relevant part, that “it is an unlawful employment practice] for an employer ... to harass an employee;”) see also, Gov. Code § 12940, subd. (j)(4)(C) [“For the purpose of this subdivision, “harassment” because of sex includes sexual harassment, gender harassment[.]])

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s allegations fall within the protected activities identified in subdivision (h) of Government Code section 12940, upon which her retaliation causes of actions was premised.

 

ii)                 Retaliatory Animus and Causal Link

 

            Defendants argue that the Six Cause of Action fails to allege facts to support retaliatory intent. In her response, Plaintiff contends that the retaliatory motive can be shown by demonstrating that she engaged in protected activities and that the adverse action followed within a relatively short time thereafter. Plaintiff argues that the causal link here may be established by an inference derived from circumstantial evidence.

 

            Established case law outlines that, even in the absent of direct evidence of retaliatory animus, close temporal proximity between a plaintiff’s protected activity and the alleged retaliatory conduct against the plaintiff has been found sufficient to support a prima facie case of causation. (Le Mere, supra, 35 Cal.App.5th a p. 243.) Circumstantial evidence typically relates to factors such as the plaintiff’s job performance, the timing of events, and how the plaintiff was treated in comparison to other workers. (See Iwekaogwu v. City of Los Angeles (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 803, 816; Flait v. North American Watch Corp. (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 467, 479.) Several federal cases hold that intervals of more than a few months were too long to support causation. (See, e.g., Cornwell v. Electra Cent. Credit Union (9th Cir. 2006) 439 F.3d 1018, 1036 [the court hold that seven-month gap was too long to support causation.])

 

As to the protected activity of requesting accommodations, there is close temporal proximity, as the alleged retaliatory conduct – termination of employment – occurred on the same day that Plaintiff requested sick leave. This proximity supports a prima facie case of retaliatory animus and causation.

 

Defendants argue that there is no direct evidence to show a nexus between the alleged harassment and plaintiff’s termination. However, the Court notes that both direct and circumstantial evidence can be used to show an employer’s intent to retaliate.

 

Here, Plaintiff’s termination occurred less than one month after her complaint about alleged sexual harassment. Whether this circumstantial evidence will ultimately establish the elements of Defendant’s retaliatory animus and causal link is a matter of fact, not law, and therefore cannot form a basis for sustaining the Demurrer. As such, the Court finds Defendants argument unpersuasive.

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Sixth Cause of Action.

 

6)      Twelfth Cause of Action – Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code Sections §§ 98.6 and 1102.5

 

Labor Code section 98.6 prohibits retaliation against an employee for exercising rights protected by the Labor Code. (Lab. Code § 98.6; Grinzi v. San Diego Hospice Corp. (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 72, 87.)

 

The prima facie showing of retaliation under Labor Code section 1102.5(b) (Whistleblowing) requires the following elements: (1) the plaintiff engaged in a protected activity, (2) the employer subjected the plaintiff to adverse employment action, and (3) there is a causal link between the two. (Patten v. Grant Joint Union High School Dist.  (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1384; Hansen v. Cal. Dept. of Corrections and Rehab. (2008) 171 Cal.App.4th 1537, 1546.)

 

Liability under labor code whistleblower provision requires plaintiffs to show that retaliation was a “contributing factor” in their termination, demotion, or other adverse action. This means plaintiffs may satisfy their burden of proving unlawful retaliation even when other, legitimate factors also contributed to the adverse action. (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 713; see, e.g., State Comp. Ins. Fund v. Ind. Acc. Com. (1959) 176 Cal.App.2d 10, 17.)

 

Protected activity involves the disclosure of, or opposition to, “a violation of state or federal statute, or a violation or noncompliance with a state or federal rule or regulation, not just local employer policies. (Mueller v. County of L. A. (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 809, 821-822.)

 

Defendants argue that the Twelfth Cause of Action suffers the same defects as its previous version, specifically the lack of the required causal link between Plaintiff’s protected activity and the termination. However, upon reviewing the FAC, the Court identifies two distinct activities – the involuntary act of being harassed and affirmative disclosure or opposition to the sexual harassment. The latter constitutes a protected activity under the retaliation provision of the Labor Code.  As analyzed in relation to the Sixth Cause of Action, the Court finds that the FAC adequately alleges the element required to state a claim.

 

            Therefore, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Twelfth Cause of Action.

 

B.     Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike (the “Motion”) the following portions of Plaintiff’s FAC:

 

1.      “Said actions also justify the imposition of punitive damages in that Defendant committed the acts alleged herein maliciously, fraudulently, and oppressively with the wrongful intention of injuring Plaintiff, from an improper and evil motive amounting to malice, and in conscious disregard of Plaintiff s rights. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to recover punitive damages from Defendants, and each of them, in an amount according to proof.” (FAC ¶ 72 [as to the Third Cause of Action].)

 

2.      “Said discrimination and/or refusal to accommodate/engage in the interactive process was wrongful and justifies the imposition of punitive damages since decision to terminate Plaintiff s employment because she needed one (1) day off to recover from the pain of her disability was against public policy. Defendant intentionally failed to accommodate Plaintiff, acted maliciously, fraudulently, and oppressively with the wrongful intention of injuring Plaintiff. Defendant acted with an evil purpose, in an intentional and deliberate manner, in violation of Plaintiff s civil rights, and/or with conscious disregard of Plaintiff s rights. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to recover punitive damages from Defendants, and each of them, in an amount according to proof.” (FAC ¶ 84 [as to the Fourth Cause of Action].)

 

3.      “Said discrimination and/or refusal to accommodate/engage in the interactive process was wrongful and justifies the imposition of punitive damages since the failure to accommodate Plaintiff s disability and engage in the interactive process was against public policy. Defendant intentionally failed to engage in the interactive process, with the wrongful intention of injuring Plaintiff. Defendant acted with an evil purpose, in an intentional and deliberate manner, in violation of Plaintiff s civil rights, and/or with conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to recover punitive damages &om Defendants, and each of them, in an amount according to proof.” (FAC ¶ 97 [as to the Fifth Cause of Action])

 

4.      "... Said retaliation was wrongful and justifies the imposition of punitive damages since it was against public policy. Defendant intentionally retaliated against Plaintiff on account of her protected activity, and in doing so, Defendant acted maliciously, fraudulently, and oppressively with the wrongful intention of injuring Plaintiff. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to recover punitive damages from Defendants, and each of them, in an amount according to proof.” (FAC ¶ 183 [as to the Twelfth Cause of Action])

 

1)      Meet and Confer

 

“Before filing a motion to strike ... the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).) If no agreement is reached, the moving party shall file and serve with the motion to strike a declaration stating either: (1) the means by which the parties met and conferred and that the parties did not reach an agreement, or (2) that the party who filed the pleading failed to respond to the meet and confer request or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a)(3).)

 

Here, Defendants have satisfied the meet and confer requirements. (Hodge Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.)

 

2)      Motion to Strike Punitive Damages

 

A motion to strike punitive damages is properly granted where a plaintiff does not state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, including allegations that defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.)

 

“Malice” is defined as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “‘Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.’ [Citation.]” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210.)

 

“Oppression” is defined in as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (See, e.g., Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.)

 

The allegations supporting a request for punitive damages must be alleged with specificity; conclusory allegations without sufficient facts are not enough. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.)

 

            Furthermore, “[a]n employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (b).)

 

Defendants essentially argue that the FAC does not sufficiently state the Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Twelfth Causes of Action, and therefore, the relief for punitive damages is unavailable without valid underlying claims. Plaintiff contends that punitive damages are not limited to a showing of malice and can be awarded when employers consciously disregard the rights of employees. The Court, however, finds neither party’s arguments persuasive.

 

First, the Court has previously overruled the Demurrer to the FAC, concluding that these causes of action are supported by adequate allegations. Accordingly, punitive damages are not excluded based on the absence of valid underlying claims.

 

Second, as to Plaintiff’s argument, the Court emphasizes that even when causes of action are properly pleaded, specific factual allegations supporting a conclusion that Defendants acted with oppression, fraud or malice are required to state a claim for punitive damages. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) As noted in American Airlines, Inc. v. Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 1017, 1050-1051, “Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.”

 

Here, the allegations Plaintiff relies upon do not include specific facts showing a level of “extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights.” Rather, they consist of general allegations and legal conclusions.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to strike in its entirety. Should Plaintiff later determine, through the course of discovery, that additional facts exist which would support a claim for punitive damages, Plaintiff may file a motion for leave to amend.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendants’ Demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendants Omid Vesal, M.D. d/b/a Irvine Urgent Care (“IUC”) and Carlos Ernesto Monge are ordered to serve and file their Answers to the First Amended Complaint within 30 days.

 

Defendants’ Motion to Strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

 

This is a breach of contract and tort case between a canned beverage manufacturer, Plaintiff DrinkPAK LLC (“Plaintiff” or “DrinkPAK”), and a payroll services provider, Defendant Paylocity Corp. (“Defendant” or “Paylocity”).

 

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Complaint against Defendant and Does 1 through 10, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Implied Indemnity, (4) Equitable Indemnity, (5) Contribution, and (6) Declaratory Relief. Subsequently, Defendant filed a demurrer with motion to strike to the Complaint on September 9, 2024.

 

On September 12, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Application for Brian Mead to be Admitted as Counsel Pro Hac Vice (the “Application”).

 

No Opposition or Reply papers have been received.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 9.40(a), “[a] person who is not a licensee of the State Bar of California but who is an attorney in good standing of and eligible to practice before the bar of any United States court or the highest court in any state, territory, or insular possession of the United States, and who has been retained to appear in a particular cause pending in a court of this state, may in the discretion of such court be permitted upon written application to appear as counsel pro hac vice, provided that an active licensee of the State Bar of California is associated as attorney of record.”

 

Additionally, “No person is eligible to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule if the person is: (1) A resident of the State of California; (2) Regularly employed in the State of California; or (3) Regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.” (Cal. Rules of Court rule 9.40(a).)

A.    Application to be Admitted Pro Hac Vice

 

Brian Mead (“Mead”) applies to appear as counsel pro hac vice for Defendant Paylocity. This Application is unopposed.

 

Mead is a member in good standing of the following: (1) Supreme Court of Illinois, (2) U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Illinois, (3) U.S. District Court for the Central District of Illinois, (4) U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Illinois , (5) U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Michigan, (6) U.S. District Court for the Western District of Wisconsin, (7) U.S. District Court for the District of Colorado, (8) U.S. Court of Appeals for the First Circuit, (9) U.S. Court of Appeal for the Second Circuit, and (10) U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit. (Mead Decl. ¶¶ 5-6.) In addition, Mead states that he is not a resident of California, is not regularly engaged in practice of law in the State of California and is not regularly employed or does a substantial amount of business in the State of California (Id. ¶ 4.)

 

Pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 9.40(d), the application for pro hac vice admission must state, in pertinent part, “[t]he title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted[.]” Additionally, “Absent special circumstances, repeated appearances by any person under this rule is a cause for denial of an application.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 9.40(b).)

Mead submits that he has previously applied for admission pro hac vice into California courts. (Id. ¶ 7.) These applications include the following cases: Vozzcom, Inc. v. Paylocity Corp., 2:19-cv-00014 and Drinkpak, LLC v. Paylocity Corporation, 2:24-cv-06369, both in the U.S. District Court for the Central District of California; Gill v. Marsh USA, Inc., 3:24-cv-02366 in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California; Safa v. Lerner New York, Inc., Case No. BC614196, in the Superior Court of the State of California, County of Los Angeles; and Saxco Intl, LLC v. Jeff Oberti, Case No. CGC-17-558458, in the Superior Court of the State of California, City & County of San Francisco. (Ibid.)

However, Mead’s statements do not satisfy the reequipments of California Rules of Court Rule 9.40(d), which also require “the date of each [previous] application, and whether or not it was granted.” (Cal. Rules of Court rule 9.40(d).) Additionally, the Application has failed to demonstrate the existence of any special circumstance to warrant not denying the application on account of Mead’s repeated appearance under the Rule 9.40(b).

 

Furthermore, Mead has failed to submit proof that the $50.00 fee was paid to the State Bar of California, pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 9.40(e).

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES the Application.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Paylocity Corp.’s Application for Admission of Brian Mead as Counsel Pro Hac Vice is DENIED without prejudice.
