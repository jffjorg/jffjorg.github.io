Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 18STCV08570    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

anthony aiello , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

sommer ray beaty , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

18STCV08570

 

 

Hearing Date:

October 9, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion to compel responses to requests for production of documents and for sanctions

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Sommer Ray Beaty          

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Plaintiffs Anthony Aiello and Anthony’s Worldwide Promotions, LLC

Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production of Documents and for Sanctions[1]

The court considered the moving papers and opposing declaration filed in connection with this motion.  No replay papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Sommer Ray Beaty (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling plaintiffs Anthony Aiello and Anthony’s Worldwide Productions, LLC (“Plaintiffs”) to serve responses to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, without objections, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiffs in the amount of $3,230.

If a party to whom a demand for inspection is directed fails to serve a timely response, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2031.300, subd. (b).)¿¿ 

Defendant asserts that she served Plaintiffs with her Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, on April 25, 2024 by email.  The court notes that the proof of service of this discovery, however, is incomplete and therefore defective because (1) it states that the Requests for Production of Documents were served on counsel for Plaintiffs by email, but (2) it does not state the email address to which the discovery was served.  (Paine Decl., Ex. 1, p. 8 [proof of service of discovery by email].)  However, Plaintiffs do not dispute, in the declaration of their counsel filed in support of their opposition, that they received Defendant’s discovery on this date. 

Defendant asserts in her motion that Plaintiffs did not serve timely responses to this discovery.  (Mot., p. 5:25-26.)  Plaintiffs do not appear to dispute that they did not serve timely responses, and instead state that they responded to this discovery “on July 8, 2024.”  (Green Decl., ¶ 3.)  While Plaintiffs attached a copy of an email purporting to attach the responses, Plaintiffs did not submit a copy of the responses, such that the court can verify that responses have been served to each document demand.  Moreover, Defendant has not filed a reply to this motion confirming that it is moot.

Thus, the court finds that Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Plaintiffs to serve responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and therefore grants Defendant’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b).)

The court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).)  The court finds that $850 (2 hours x $425 hourly rate) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Plaintiffs in connection with this motion.  (Paine Decl., ¶ 6.)

ORDER

            The court grants defendant Sommer Ray Beaty’s motion to compel responses to requests for production of documents and for sanctions as follows.

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300, the court orders plaintiffs Anthony Aiello and Anthony’s Worldwide Promotions, LLC (1) to serve on defendant Sommer Ray Beaty full and complete verified responses, without objections, to defendant Sommer Ray Beaty’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, that comply with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.210-2031.250, and (2) to produce to defendant Sommer Ray Beaty all documents and things in plaintiffs Anthony Aiello and Anthony’s Worldwide Promotions, LLC’s possession, custody, or control which are responsive to those requests, within 20 days of the date of service of this order.

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Tammy Davis

Motion to Compel Mental Examination of Plaintiff Tammy Davis

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court sustains plaintiff Tammy Davis’s evidentiary objection to the declaration of Dr. Marc A. Cohen, filed by defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation on October 2, 2024 in support of its reply to the motion to compel the mental examination of plaintiff Tammy Davis, in its entirety, because new evidence is not permitted with reply papers.  (City of Rocklin v. Legacy Family Adventures-Rocklin, LLC (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 713, 730.)

DISCUSSION

Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order compelling plaintiff Tammy Davis (“Plaintiff”) to submit to a mental examination.

“If any party desires to obtain discovery . . . by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).)  “A motion for examination . . . shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b).)  The motion must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration and be served on the person to be examined and all other parties who have appeared in the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subds. (b), (c).)  The court shall grant a motion for a mental examination “for good cause shown.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).)

As a threshold matter, the court notes that Plaintiff does not appear to dispute that she has placed her mental state in controversy and does not oppose sitting for a mental examination.  (Opp., p. 3:2-3; Harings Decl., ¶ 2; 4AC ¶¶ 99, 112.)  Further, based on the arguments and evidence presented by the parties, the court finds that Defendant (1) has submitted a meet and confer declaration that substantially complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040, and (2) has shown good cause to conduct a mental examination of Plaintiff, limited in part as follows.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subds. (a), (b); Stern Decl., ¶¶ 5-7, 9.)

First, the court notes that Defendant has agreed to exclude examination (1) relating to Plaintiff’s family medical history based on Plaintiff’s representation that her family medical history is not at issue and will not be the subject of her testimony or the testimony of any of her expert(s) at trial in this matter, (2) regarding the specific terms of any prior settlements between Plaintiff and Defendant, and (3) that would require Plaintiff to describe her attorney/client communications with her attorneys.  (Reply, p. 8:3-12.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff is not required to answer questions on those topics at her examination.

Second, the court finds that there is good cause to ask Plaintiff, during the mental examination of Plaintiff, about (1) her substance use, (2) her criminal history, (3) her psychiatric and mental health treatment history, and (4) her medical history and current medications.  (Notice of Mot. p. 2:19-23; Opp., pp. 6:19-20, 7:4 [objecting to topics relating to substance use, criminal history, and psychiatric treatment other than prescribed medications].)  However, the court finds that Defendant has not shown good cause to examine Plaintiff regarding her sexual history and her sexual conduct with any person other than defendant Tony Rositas, and therefore denies Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s mental examination as to those subjects.  

Third, the court denies Plaintiff’s request that she not be asked “[q]uestions about facts and circumstances already covered in [Defendant’s] deposition of” Plaintiff and in her interrogatory responses.  (Opp., p. 7:5-6.)

Fourth, the court finds that Defendant’s motion has identified, with the requisite particularity, the scope and nature of the examination, including by setting forth the names of the tests and procedures to be performed.  (Notice of Mot., pp. 2:25-3:5.)  The court exercises its discretion to deny Plaintiff’s request that the court order Defendant to produce to her “the written questions and answers” from her mental examination.[1]  (Opp., p. 10:24-28; Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b); Randy’s Trucking, Inc. v. Superior Court of Kern County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 818, 834 [trial court has the discretion to order the disclosure of written test questions and answers].)

Fifth, the court finds that Defendant has shown good cause to conduct an eight-hour mental examination to ensure that the examiner has enough time to examine and diagnose Plaintiff.  The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s request that the court limit her mental examination to four hours.

Sixth, the court notes that Plaintiff requests an order permitting her to audio record the examination.  However, because Plaintiff is already authorized to record the examination by audio technology, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that such a court order is necessary.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.530, subd. (a) [“The examiner and the examinee shall have the right to record a mental examination by audio technology”].)  Moreover, Defendant does not appear to oppose Plaintiff’s recording the mental examination.  (Reply, p. 12:14-17 [noting that Plaintiff has a statutory right to record the examination and asserting that it “has never taken any position to the contrary”].)  The court further denies Plaintiff’s request that the court order Defendant to produce any and all audio recordings within two business days of the examination and will instead order Defendant to produce any and all audio recordings of her examination within 15 court days.

The court, however, finds that Defendant has not shown good cause justifying, or cited law authorizing, the video recording of the mental examination of Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.530, subd. (a) [authorizing the right to record the mental examination only “by audio technology”] [emphasis added].)  The court therefore grants Plaintiff’s request to preclude the video recording of her mental examination.

Seventh, the court finds that Plaintiff did not present authority and adequate argument establishing that the audio recordings and the test results should be subject to a protective order that they be viewed only by counsel and Plaintiff, and therefore denies that request.

Finally, the court denies (1) Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $7,560, made in her opposition, and (2) Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the amount of $1,000, made in its reply.

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s demurrer to second amended complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation)           

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe

Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this demurrer.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court grants defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation)’s request for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (c), (d).)  The court, however, notes that it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the matters stated therein.  (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe, as trustee of the Nubian Village Trust (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative Second Amended Complaint in this action on March 6, 2024, against defendants Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing and NewRez LLC, alleging seven causes of action for (1) breach of contract; (2) promissory estoppel; (3) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (4) violations of the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (“RESPA”); (5) negligence; (6) accounting; and (7) unfair business practices.

Defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation) (“Defendant”) now moves the court for an order sustaining its demurrer to each cause of action alleged in Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint.  

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint in its entirety on the ground that Plaintiff does not have standing because Defendant has not met its burden to show that Plaintiff does not have standing to bring this action.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.10, subd. (e), 367 [“Every action must be prosecuted in the name of the real party in interest, except as otherwise provided by statute”].)

Plaintiff’s causes of action are based on the alleged breach of the Loan Modification Agreement entered into by and between Ditech, on the one hand, and Plaintiff – as trustee of the Nubian Village Trust – on the other hand, on December 11, 2018.  (SAC ¶¶ 59, 62, 65, 70, 80, 85-86, 91, 97.)  The court acknowledges that Tonja J. Taylor, an unmarried woman (“Taylor”), was the only trustor to the Deed of Trust securing the original December 17, 2007 promissory note.  (RJN Ex. 1, p. 2.)  But Plaintiff has expressly alleged that (1) she brings this action as the trustee of the Nubian Village Trust, which owns the subject property, and (2) she, as trustee of the Nubian Village Trust, executed the subject Loan Modification Agreement presented by Ditech.  (SAC p. 1:23-24; SAC ¶¶ 6, 7 [after Taylor passed away on August 27, 2017, Plaintiff became the executor of the estate and trustee of the Nubian Village Trust], 17; SAC Ex. C, Trust Transfer Deed, p. 1 [stating that Taylor, as Grantor, “transfers her interest in the property . . . to Grantor’s revocable inter vivos trust” and listing as the Grantee Taylor, as the trustee of the Nubian Village Trust dated July 31, 2008].)  

Thus, although Defendant has argued that the Nubian Village Trust is not the borrower of the original loan, Defendant did not (1) cite any authority establishing that Plaintiff could not, as the trustee of the trust that obtained Taylor’s interest in the subject property, execute the Loan Modification Agreement, or (2) present argument establishing that Plaintiff’s causes of action are based on the alleged breach of the December 17, 2007 agreement, rather than the 2018 Loan Modification Agreement.  The court therefore finds that Defendant has not shown that Plaintiff does not have standing to bring this action.

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action as barred by issue preclusion because Defendant did not meet its burden to prove each element of issue preclusion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Williams v. Doctors Medical Center of Modesto, Inc. (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 1117, 1132 [“The party asserting issue preclusion has the burden of establishing the above elements [of issue preclusion]”].)  Specifically, Defendant did not set forth adequate argument showing that (1) the issues sought to be precluded from relitigation in this action are identical to those decided in Case No. 22STCV33786, (2) those issues were actually litigated in the former proceeding, (3) the issues were necessarily decided in the former proceeding, and (4) the decision(s) in the former proceeding was final and on the merits.  (Williams, supra, 100 Cal.App.5th at p. 1131.)  Instead, Defendant has stated, in conclusory fashion and without adequate citation to the record, that “the exact same issues, such as whether a subsequent servicer and beneficiary could be held liable for failing to honor the purported modification with Ditech brought by” Plaintiff was decided on the merits.  (Dem., p. 14:21-27 [citing generally to RJN Exhibits 2, the 51-page First Amended Complaint in Case No. 22STCV33786, and 7, Order of Judgment of Dismissal].)  This is insufficient to establish that each cause of action alleged by Plaintiff is barred by issue preclusion.

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has not alleged facts establishing the existence of a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402 [elements of cause of action for breach of contract].)

The court notes that Plaintiff has alleged that (1) she executed the Loan Modification Agreement, which contained inaccuracies but was presented on a “‘take it or leave it’ basis with foreclosure as the only alternative” (SAC ¶ 17), and (2) as the successor loan servicer, Defendant was obligated to honor the Loan Modification Agreement pursuant to Civil Code section 2924.11 (SAC ¶ 61).  The court also acknowledges that, pursuant to Civil Code section 2924.11, subdivision (g), “[i]f a borrower has been approved in writing for a first lien loan modification or other foreclosure prevention alternative, and the servicing of that borrower's loan is transferred or sold to another mortgage servicer, the subsequent mortgage servicer shall continue to honor any previously approved first lien loan modification or other foreclosure prevention alternative, in accordance with the provisions of the act that added this section.”  (Civ. Code, § 2924.11, subd. (g).)  However, section 2924.11 applies only to a first lien mortgage or deed of trust that is either secured by owner-occupied residential property (i.e., property that is the principal residence of the borrower and is security for a loan made for personal, family, or household purposes) containing no more than four dwelling units, or is secured by residential real property occupied by a tenant and contains no more than four dwelling units and meets other statutorily-defined characteristics.  (Civ. Code, § 2924.14, subd. (a)(1)(A), (a)(1)(B), (a)(2).)  Plaintiff did not allege facts establishing that the Loan Modification Agreement meets those characteristics and therefore has not alleged facts establishing that, under section 2924.11, Defendant was required to honor the terms thereof, such that it may be held liable for its breach.

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action for promissory estoppel because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant promised her “that they would accurately service the Property loan by, among other things, maintaining accurate records of payments and balances and sending accurate monthly statements” (SAC ¶ 65), which is a promise that is clear and unambiguous in its terms, and (2) Defendant has not shown that this cause of action is necessarily barred by the statute of limitations because, although Defendant asserts that a two-year statute of limitations applies for oral promises, Defendant did not cite any allegations in the Second Amended Complaint establishing that the subject promise was made orally, such that it has not shown that this claim is barred.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 4301.10, subd. (e); Jones v. Wachovia Bank (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 935, 945 [elements of claim for promissory estoppel]; Raja Development Co., Inc. v. Napa Sanitary District (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 85, 92 [“A demurrer based on a statute of limitations will not lie where the action may be, but is not necessarily, barred”] [internal quotation marks and citations omitted].)

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) the court has found, for the reasons set forth above, that Plaintiff did not allege the existence of a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant, and (2) “[a] claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing requires the existence of a contract, whether express or implied.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Alameda Health System v. Alameda County Employees’ Retirement Assn. (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 1159, 1190.)  

            The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action for violation of RESPA because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) under RESPA, a servicer must respond to a notice of error by either (i) correcting the error(s) identified by the borrower, or (ii) conducting a reasonable investigation and providing the borrower with a written notification therefore that includes a statement that the servicer has determined that no error occurred and other information required by statute, and (2) Plaintiff has alleged only that Defendant did not correct the error that she identified (SAC ¶ 81), which does not, alone, constitute a violation of RESPA since a loan servicer may elect to respond by conducting a reasonable investigation and notifying the borrower that the servicer has determined that no error occurred.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); 12 C.F.R. 1024.35, subd. (e)(1) [response to notice of error must either (A) correct the errors or (B) provide the borrower with notification including a statement that the servicer has determined no error has occurred].)

 

            The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action for negligence because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant had, and breached, a duty to accurately service the subject loan (i.e., appearing to reference the Loan Modification Agreement), and therefore this cause of action (1) sounds in contract and, (2) as such, is barred by the economic loss rule.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 923 [claims for monetary losses are barred when they arise from, or are not independent of, underlying contracts].)

            The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action for accounting because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has alleged that, while Defendant accepted the Keep Your Home California program funds, it did not apply them to Plaintiff’s account (SAC ¶ 92), such that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the existence of a relationship between the parties that requires an accounting.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Sass v. Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861, 869 [elements of accounting].)

            The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the seventh cause of action for unfair competition because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts establishing that (1) Defendant violated the unlawful, unfair, and fraudulent prongs of the unfair competition law by generating unwarranted fees, demanding and accepting payments for nonexistent debts, failing to apply payments on the subject loan to the principal balance, and inflating the amounts due on the subject loan (SAC ¶¶ 97, subds. (a)-(e), 104), and (2) Plaintiff has lost money or property as a result of Defendant’s conduct (SAC ¶ 102).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Beverage v. Apple, Inc. (2024) 101 Cal.App.5th 736, 735 [“Functionally, [t]he UCL operates as a three-pronged statute: Each of these three adjectives [unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent] creates a separate and distinct theory of liability”] [internal quotation marks and citations omitted].) 

ORDER

            The court sustains in part and overrules in part defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation)’s demurrer to plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe’s Second Amended Complaint as follows.

The court sustains defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation)’s demurrer to plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe’s first, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action.

The court overrules defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation)’s demurrer to plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe’s second, sixth, and seventh causes of action.

The court grants plaintiff Aysia A. Rowe 20 days leave to file a Third Amended Complaint that cures the defects in the first, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action set forth in this ruling.

            The court orders defendant NewRez LLC, d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (on behalf of itself and erroneously sued as Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing, a Delaware corporation)  to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendants’ motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Defendants Grab Holdings Limited, Grab Holdings Inc., and Grabtaxi Holdings PTE LTD                  

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Global Pond USA Corporation

Motion to Quash Service of Summons for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Specially appearing defendants Grab Holdings Limited (“Grab Limited”), Grab Holdings Inc. (“Grab Inc.”), and Grabtaxi Holdings PTE LTD (“Grabtaxi”) (collectively, “Defendants”) move the court for an order quashing the service of summons by plaintiff Global Pond USA Corporation (“Plaintiff”) on the ground that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over Defendants.  (Code Civ. Proc.,¿§ 418.10, subd. (a)(1) [“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion . . . [¶] (1)  To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her”].)  

“A court of this state may exercise jurisdiction on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §¿410.10.)  “Under those Constitutions, jurisdiction is proper if a defendant has minimum contacts with California such that a suit in the state does not offend traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.”  (Farina v. SAVWCL III, LLC (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 286, 294.)  “‘Personal jurisdiction may be either general or specific.  A nonresident defendant may be subject to the general jurisdiction of the forum if his or her contacts in the forum state are “substantial . . . continuous and systematic.”’”  (Jayone Foods, Inc. v. Aekyung Industrial Co. Ltd. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 543, 553.)  Alternatively, to determine whether specific jurisdiction exists, “‘courts consider the “ ‘relationship among the defendant, the forum, and the litigation.’ ”  [Citation.]’”  (Ibid.)  

“‘When a defendant moves to quash service of process on jurisdictional grounds, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction.  [Citation.]  Once facts showing minimum contacts with the forum state are established, however, it becomes the defendant’s burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.  [Citation.]’”  (Burdick v. Superior Court (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 8, 17.)   

1.     General Jurisdiction

            Plaintiff does not appear to argue, and therefore has not met its burden, to establish that the court has general jurisdiction over Defendants.  (Burdick, supra, 233 Cal.App.4th at p. 17 [“‘the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction’”]; Opp., pp. 1:10-11 [asserting that the court has specific jurisdiction over Defendants], 6:9 [same].)

            “A court has all-purpose jurisdiction over defendants who are at home in the court’s forum.”  (Farina, supra, 50 Cal.App.5th at p. 294.)  Thus, “[a] defendant is subject to general jurisdiction when it has substantial, continuous and systematic contacts in the forum state, i.e., its contacts with the forum are so wide-ranging that they take the place of a physical presence in the state.”  (Strasner v. Touchstone Wireless Repair & Logistics, LP (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 215, 222.)  “For a corporation, its domicile, place of incorporation, and/or principal place of business within a state constitute the paradigm bases for establishing general jurisdiction.”  (Ibid.)  Defendants have submitted evidence showing that (1) defendant Grab Limited is organized under the laws of the Cayman Islands and has registered offices in the Cayman Islands; (2) defendant Grab Inc. is organized under the laws of the Cayman Islands and has registered offices in the Cayman Islands; and (3) defendant Grabtaxi is organized under the laws of Singapore and has its principal executive offices in Singapore.  (Iyer Decl., ¶¶ 13-14, 24-25, 35-36.)  Moreover, Defendants do not have offices in California, are not registered and have never been registered to do business in California, do not conduct and have never conducted business operations in California, do not own and have never owned bank accounts or real property in California, and do not have and have never had employees in California.  (Iyer Decl., ¶¶ 18-22, 29-33, 38-43.)

Thus, the court finds that Defendants’ contacts with California are not “substantial, continuous and systematic” so as to render them subject to general jurisdiction.  (Strasner, supra, 5 Cal.App.5th at p. 222.)

2.     Specific Jurisdiction

“‘A court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant only if: (1) “the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits” [citation]; (2) “the ‘controversy is related to or “arises out of” [the] defendant’s contacts with the forum’ ” [citation]; and (3) “ ‘the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with “fair play and substantial justice” ’ ” [citation].’”  (Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 553.) 

Plaintiff contends that Defendants purposefully availed itself of California’s benefits as a result of the following conduct: (1) promising a business partnership with Plaintiff, a California corporation; (2) Plaintiff’s creation, in reliance on Defendants’ promises, of a prototype software product platform in California; (3) stealing from Plaintiff, a California resident, its app, technology, and intellectual property; (4) Grab Inc.’s raising of $53,150,016.93 from California investors; (5) Grab Inc.’s signing and filing with the State of California, Department of Business Oversight, multiple Consent to Service of Process forms, in which Grab Inc. has authorized California’s Commissioner of Business Oversight receive service on its behalf; (6) Grab Limited’s listing of itself on an American stock exchange, including the NASDAQ, which lists its shares for sale in California; (7) Grab Limited’s going public with a market capitalization of over $14 billion, based on Defendants’ app, which includes the allegedly stolen intellectual property from Plaintiff; (8) Defendants’ making available their app to download in California; and (9) Defendants’ targeting of residents of California to download their app.

            The court finds that Plaintiff has not met its burden to show that (1) defendants Grab Limited and Grabtaxi have purposefully availed themselves of the benefits of California, and   (2) this action arises out of defendant Grab Inc.’s stock market activity in California.  The court therefore finds that Plaintiff has not shown that the court may exercise specific jurisdiction over Defendants.  (Burdick, supra, 233 Cal.App.4th at p. 17 [“‘the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction’”].)

“A defendant purposefully avails itself of a forum’s benefits if it intentionally directs its activities at a forum such that, by virtue of the benefits the defendant has received, it should reasonably expect to be haled into the forum’s courts.”  (Farina, supra, 50 Cal.App.5th at p. 294 [emphasis in original].)   “Thus, purposeful availment occurs where a nonresident defendant purposefully direct[s] [its] activities at residents of the forum [citations], purposefully derive[s] benefits from its activities in the forum [citation], create[s] a substantial connection with the forum [citation], deliberately has engaged in significant activities within the forum [citation], or has created continuing obligations between itself and residents of the forum [citation].”  (Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 554 [internal quotation marks and citations omitted].)

            First, Plaintiff has submitted the declaration of its CEO, Chris Au (“Au”), in which Au asserts that (1) during various meetings, the senior management of Defendants requested Plaintiff to design and develop a software prototype for their product, with discussions of a joint venture with Plaintiff to the launch the software; (2) Plaintiff, in reliance on those promises of business, created the prototype; (3) Au presented the prototype to the senior product design team in March and May of 2019; and (4) in mid-2019, Defendants terminated their meetings with Au and Plaintiff, stating that they did not want to pursue the project.  (Au Decl., ¶¶ 6-7, 9, 12.)  

            As a threshold matter, the court notes that it appears undisputed that the parties never executed a written agreement.  (Iyer Decl., ¶ 52.)  However, it appears that Plaintiff has taken the position that, as a result of the conduct described above, the parties formed an oral or implied contract.  The court has therefore evaluated whether the formation of a contract between Defendants, non-residents of California, and Plaintiff, a California corporation, shows that Defendants purposely availed themselves of California’s benefits.  The court finds that, based on the facts presented in opposition to this motion, it does not.

            “[A] contract with a forum resident, alone, is insufficient to establish jurisdiction.  [Citation.]  Courts must scrutinize the underlying business transaction—past negotiations, contemplated future consequences, contract terms, and the parties’ actual course of dealing—to determine whether the defendant purposefully established minimum contacts with the forum.”  (Farina, supra, 50 Cal.App.5th at p. 299 [internal citation omitted].)

            As set forth above, Plaintiff has presented evidence describing the parties’ relationship and formation of an agreement in which Plaintiff was to create a prototype of Defendants’ super app.  However, the evidence shows that (1) the “contemplated future consequences” of development of the app would not concern this forum, because Defendants have shown, and Plaintiff does not dispute, that their services are available only in specific cities in Southeast Asia, and (2) in discussing and performing this agreement, while Plaintiff performed much of the work in California, Au “traveled back and forth from Southeast Asia to California to perform this work[,]” Defendants did not “attend[] any meetings . . . with [Plaintiff] in the United States, or in California specifically.”  (Iyer Decl., ¶¶ 7, 50; Au Decl., ¶¶ 10-11;  Farina, supra, 50 Cal.App.5th at p. 299.)

Moreover, Plaintiff did not present evidence showing that the agreement (before it was allegedly breached) contemplated an “ongoing contractual relationship[]” between the parties, and instead appears to have contemplated a one-time creation of a super app and branding campaign by Plaintiff.   (Rivelli v. Hemm (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 380, 397 [finding that the ongoing contractual relationships supported a finding of purposeful availment]; Jacqueline B. v. Rawls Law Group, P.C. (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 243, 255 [“it is well settled that a discrete, shorter-term contract between an out-of-state defendant and a forum resident ‘does not automatically establish purposeful availment in the resident’s ‘home forum’”]; Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 554 [purposeful availment occurs where a nonresident defendant creates continuing obligations between itself and forum residents]; Au Decl., ¶ 3.)  The court also notes that Plaintiff did not present evidence that the parties orally, or by writing, agreed to litigate any resulting disputes in California.  (Farina, supra, 50 Cal.App.5th at p. 299 [“Choice of law provisions are relevant to this inquiry”].)

            Second, Plaintiff has submitted Au’s declaration, in which Au states that Defendants’ GrabMart Super App “is practically identical to the GrabMart Super App” that Plaintiff previously developed for Defendants, such that Defendants copied the idea, technology, and design of the prototype that Plaintiff created.  (Au Decl., ¶¶ 15-16.)  The court finds that Plaintiff did not present adequate argument to show that this assertion of its CEO establishes that Defendants purposefully directed its activities to California.  (Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 554.)  

            Although Plaintiff did not refer to the effects test, it appears that Plaintiff is attempting to argue that Defendants have purposefully availed themselves of the forum because the effect of their wrongful conduct occurred in California, i.e., pursuant to the effects test.  However, because Plaintiff did not expressly assert that the effects test governs this motion, Plaintiff therefore has not met its burden to show that it applies.  Moreover, the court notes that, even if Plaintiff had expressly raised application of this test, Plaintiff’s evidence establishes only that Defendants engaged in conduct directed to Plaintiff, and not California.  But “the effects test requires express aiming at the forum (not necessarily the plaintiff).”  (Gilmore Bank v. AsiaTrust New Zealand Ltd. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1558, 1570 [emphasis in original].)  Plaintiff did not present argument or evidence establishing that Defendants’ conduct was targeted at California or that their conduct created a substantial connection with California.  (David L. v. Superior Court (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 359, 373 [finding that the correct test to apply is the Ninth Circuit’s test, which provides that, “[e]ven if a defendant has individually targeted conduct at a plaintiff he knows resides in the forum state, specific jurisdiction now lies under the Ninth Circuit’s effects test only if his suit-related conduct creates a substantial connection with that state”] [emphasis in original].)

            Third, Plaintiff has submitted six forms entitled “Limited Offering Exemption Notices,” filed by defendant Grab Inc. with the State of California, Department of Business Oversight, showing that Grab Inc. sold securities in California.  (Mazda Decl., Ex. B, p. 1 [sale of security in California with total value of $10,000,001.66], Ex. C, p. 1 [sale of security in California with total value of $23,375,004.57], Ex. D, p. 1 [sale of security in California with total value of $6,500,000], Ex. E, p. 1 [sale of security in California with total value of $11,625,004.32], Ex. F, p. 1 [sale of security in California with total value of $100,005.38], Ex. G, p. 1 [sale of security in California with total value of $1,550,014].)

            The court finds that this evidence (1) is insufficient to show that defendants Grab Limited and Grabtaxi purposefully derived benefits from activities in California because those defendants did not sell stock and therefore receive money from investors in California, and (2) is sufficient to show that defendant Grab Inc. has purposefully availed itself of forum benefits since it has engaged in the sale of stock in California and therefore has received money (i.e., derived a benefit from) California investors.  (Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 554.) 

However, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that this action is related to or arises out of Grab Inc.’s contacts with California (i.e., with its sale of stock in California).  (Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 554.)  Plaintiff has argued that Grab Inc. has raised a significant amount of money from California investors, most of which “was subsequent to” Defendants’ alleged theft of Plaintiff’s intellectual property, such that this action arises from its conduct.  (Opp., pp.7:1-4, 8:13-15.)  The court disagrees.  Plaintiff has not presented adequate argument establishing that its action arises from Grab Inc.’s sale of stock.  Instead, the wrongful acts complained of are based on Grab Inc.’s alleged theft of Plaintiff’s intellectual property, which is separate and independent of any sales of security by Grab Inc. in California.  (Compl., ¶¶ 27, 34-35, 45, 52, 59, 66, 73, 83, 85-86, 93.)  The court further finds that Plaintiff has not presented competent evidence establishing that Grab Inc.’s sale of stock is related to the theft of Plaintiff’s property.  (Mazda Decl., ¶ 20 [stating, in conclusory fashion, that Grab Inc.’s sale of stock “is related to the technology and intellectual property that Plaintiff developed and that Defendants stole from Plaintiff”].)

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not met its burden to establish the existence of “a substantial nexus or connection between” Grab Inc.’s activity in the stock market in California and Plaintiff’s claims relating to Grab Inc.’s alleged theft of its property.  (Jayone Foods, Inc., supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 559-560 [internal quotation marks and citation omitted].)

            Fourth, Plaintiff again relies on Grab Inc.’s filing of the “Limited Offering Exemption Notices” forms with the State of California, Department of Business Oversight, in which Grab Inc. authorized the California Commissioner of Business Oversight of the State of California, the Commissioner’s successor in office, or its attorney “to receive service of any lawful process in any noncriminal suit, action or proceeding against [Grab Inc.], or its successor, executor, or administrator which arises under the California Corporate Securities Law of 1968 or any rule or order there under [sic] after this consent has been filed, with the same force and validity as if served personally on Grab Holdings Inc..”  (Mazda Decl., Ex. B, p. 2, Ex. C, p. 2, Ex. D, p. 2, Ex. E, p. 2, Ex. F, p. 2, Ex. G, p. 3.)  Plaintiff has not presented argument explaining how Grab Inc.’s authorization to receive service in certain actions arising under the Corporate Securities Law establishes that Grab Inc. purposefully availed itself of California’s benefits.  Moreover, to the extent that Plaintiff has argued that these filings show purposeful availment on the ground that Grab Inc. has sold securities in this state, the court has concluded, for the reasons set forth above, that Plaintiff did not meet its burden to show that its action arises from such activity.

            Fifth, Plaintiff asserts that Grab Limited has listed itself on an American stock exchange, which lists shares for sale in California, and sells its shares to investors in California.  In support of that assertion, Plaintiff cites, generally, to exhibits J, K, and L, which consist of, respectively, a screenshot of a page from Yahoo Finance, Grab Limited’s Form 20-F, filed with the United States Securities and Exchange Commission for the fiscal year ending on December 31, 2022, and Grab Limited’s Form 20-F, filed with the United States Securities and Exchange Commission for the fiscal year ending on December 31, 2023.  (Mazda Decl., Exs. J-L.)  However,  Plaintiff did not present adequate argument establishing how Grab Limited’s listing itself on an American stock exchange shows that it has purposefully availed itself of the benefits of California, specifically.  The court notes that counsel for Plaintiff has stated that “[o]bviously, the NASDAQ is a stock exchange that sells shares to California residents” and that, with Grab Limited’s market capitalization of $14.013 billion, “obviously there are stockholders of Grab Holdings Limited that are California residents.”  (Mazda Decl., ¶¶ 13-14.)  But (1) Plaintiff did not submit, or cite to, any evidence showing that Grab Limited has in fact sold shares to California residents, and (2) counsel for Plaintiff did not lay a foundation for those conclusory assertions. 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Grab Limited purposefully availed itself of California’s benefits by listing itself on an American stock exchange.

            Sixth, Au has stated, in their declaration, that Defendants’ “app is available to download in California.”  (Au Decl., ¶¶ 18, 24.)  But Plaintiff did not present evidence disputing, just because the app may be available to download in California, that Defendants have not offered their services to California residents.  Specifically, Defendants presented evidence showing that “[t]he services procured using the Grab app are not available, and have never been available, in the United States, or in the State of California specifically.”  (Iyer Decl., ¶ 9.)  Plaintiff did not establish, even if the app is able to be downloaded while in California, that California residents are able to use the services offered by Defendants with their app.  Moreover, Plaintiff did not show that, by making the app available for download in California (without making available the use of their services), Defendants have targeted California residents.  (Jacqueline B., supra, 68 Cal.App.5th at p. 254.)

            Finally, Plaintiff asserts that Defendants have targeted residents of California who will be traveling to Southeast Asia by advertising their app to them, relying on two online articles in support of that contention.[1]  The court finds that these articles do not establish that Defendants have targeted California residents.

            “In assessing whether an out-of-state defendant’s operation of a website constitutes purposeful availment, [courts] examine (1) whether the website targets California residents [citations], and, if it does not, (2) where the website falls on a ‘sliding scale’ of ‘interactivity’ with Internet users, with websites allowing out-of-state defendants to conduct business with California residents at one end of the scale and websites that passively make information available at the other end [citations].”  (Jacqueline B., supra, 68 Cal.App.5th at p. 254 [internal citations omitted].)

            Neither article mentions California, California residents, or the availability of Defendants’ services to use in California.  (Au Decl., Exs. B, C.)  At most, the first article provides that (1) the top source markets for travelers using their app include North America, and (2) travelers may download and register for an account while in their home country to help them plan ahead for their trips (including by exploring a city in the app as if they were at the destination).  (Au Decl., Ex. B, pp. 3, 4.)  The second article does not mention North America or the availability of the app for download to plan ahead for trips, and instead recites general information about Defendants’ services and information about travel to Southeast Asia.  (Au Decl., Ex. C.)  Plaintiff did not present other evidence or argument establishing that the articles target California residents (e.g., that the articles are accessible only in California) or that they allow Defendants to conduct business with California residents. 

The court finds that the language of these articles, without any further evidence and standing on their own, does not show that Defendants have (1) targeted California residents, or   (2) used the articles to conduct business with California residents.  (Jacqueline B., supra, 68 Cal.App.5th at p. 254 [internal citations omitted].)

3.     Jurisdictional Discovery

Plaintiff has requested, if the court is inclined to grant Defendants’ motion, that the court continue the hearing on this motion to permit Plaintiff to conduct jurisdictional discovery.

“‘A trial court has the discretion to continue the hearing on a motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction to allow the plaintiff to conduct discovery on jurisdictional issues.’  [Citation.]  ‘In order to prevail on a motion for a continuance for jurisdictional discovery, the plaintiff should demonstrate that discovery is likely to lead to the production of evidence of facts establishing jurisdiction.’”  (Preciado v. Freightliner Custom Chassis Corporation (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 964, 972 [internal citations omitted].)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not demonstrated that discovery is likely to lead to the production of evidence of facts establishing the court’s jurisdiction over Defendants.  Plaintiff has not specified the type of discovery that it intends to take and the likelihood that it will establish jurisdiction.  Instead, Plaintiff has merely requested leave to conduct discovery in conclusory fashion.  The court therefore exercises its discretion to deny Plaintiff’s request for a continuance to conduct jurisdictional discovery.

4.     Conclusion

For the reasons set forth above, the court finds that Plaintiff has not met its burden to prove personal jurisdiction over Defendants by a preponderance of the evidence.  (Farina, supra, 50 Cal.App.5th at p. 293.)  The court therefore grants Defendants’ motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)

ORDER

            The court grants specially appearing defendants Grab Holdings Limited, Grab Holdings Inc., and Grabtaxi Holdings PTE LTD’s motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction.

            The court orders that service of plaintiff Global Pond USA Corporation’s summons and complaint in this action, on specially appearing defendants Grab Holdings Limited, Grab Holdings Inc., and Grabtaxi Holdings PTE LTD, is quashed.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)

            The court orders specially appearing defendants Grab Holdings Limited, Grab Holdings Inc., and Grabtaxi Holdings PTE LTD to prepare, serve, and lodge a proposed judgment of dismissal within 10 days of the date of this order.    

            The court sets an Order to Show Cause re entry of judgment of dismissal for hearing on February 4, 2025, at 8:30 a.m., in Department 53.

 

            The court orders specially appearing defendants Grab Holdings Limited, Grab Holdings Inc., and Grabtaxi Holdings PTE LTD to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
