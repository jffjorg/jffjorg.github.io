DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV01059    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Chelato Chacon,

 

         Plaintiff,

   v.

 

 

 Zeyad Elalami, et al. 

         Defendants.

   Case Number             Department

      21VECV01059       107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

(1) Demurrer

(2) Motion to Strike

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:       

Defendants Zeyad Elalami and Luxor Funding Group demur to Plaintiff Chelato Chacon’s first amended complaint. This court overrules Defendants’ demurrer. Defendants move to strike punitive damages and certain lines from the first amended complaint. This court denies the motion to strike as to the punitive damages and the sentence alleging reports to government agencies and grants the motion to strike as to the phrase “and sexual advances of Defendants,” without leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

This is a landlord-tenant case. The following factual allegations are drawn from the Complaint (as defined below). 

 

  1. Factual 

 

In November 2014, plaintiff Chelato Chacon (“Plaintiff”) entered a lease agreement (the “Lease”) regarding a certain residential real property (the “Property”) with its prior owner (the “Original Owner”). (Complaint p. 2.) Between then and 2021, Plaintiff and the Original Owner made verbal alterations to the Lease, including rent increases; Plaintiff made timely rent payments, performed under the Lease, and made repairs to the Property without any rent offsets; and many habitability issues arose at the Property, including pest infestations and problems with heating. (Id. at pp. 2–3.)  

 

On January 20, 2021, Plaintiff learned that defendant Zeyad Elalami (“Elalami”) and the corporation in which Elalami holds multiple officer positions, defendant Luxor Funding Group, Inc. (“Luxor” and, together with Elalami, “Defendants”), had acquired the Property from the Original Owner, making Defendants the new owners of the Property. (Complaint pp. 1, 3.) The same day, Elalami informed Plaintiff that he was immediately increasing Plaintiff’s monthly rent by approximately 47.14% although Elalami did not provide Plaintiff with proper notice of any rent increase. (Complaint p. 3.) Plaintiff protested that such a rent increase violated rent control and stabilization laws governing the Property and continued to timely pay rent at his previous rate. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami accepted such payment for the months of February 2021, March 2021, and April 2021, but thereafter returned Plaintiff’s attempted payments to him. (Ibid.) Elalami made verbal and written implied threats to damage Plaintiff’s FICO credit score. (Id. at p. 3.) On February 1, 2021, Elalami attempted to issue Plaintiff a three-day notice to pay or quit via text message, alleging that Plaintiff had failed to make timely rent payments. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami told Plaintiff that he has “no lease for the Property” and Plaintiff received notice that an unlawful detainer action had been filed. (Id. at p. 4.)  

 

  1. Procedural 

 

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff initiated this action by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”). On September 20, 2021, Defendants answered the Complaint. On April 8, 2024, this court granted Plaintiff’s leave to amend the Complaint and file a first amended complaint (the “FAC”), which Plaintiff filed on May 8, 2024.

 

On May 20, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and a motion to strike (the “Motion”). On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions (the “Oppositions”) to the Demurrer and the Motion, to which Plaintiff replied on June 18, 2024.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Similarly, per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” which is 30 days. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿412.20, subd. (a)(3).)¿ 

¿¿¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.)¿ 

 

 

Plaintiff has not filed a Proof of Service of the FAC in this case. We therefore cannot determine if the Demurrer and the Motion are timely brought. The Demurrer, the Motion, the Oppositions, and both replies were timely filed as against the hearing date.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) and §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. Each section requires that such meet and confer be done “in person or by telephone.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§¿430.1, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).)¿ 

 

Defendants submit with the Demurrer and the Motion a declaration by their counsel that he and Plaintiff’s counsel met and conferred by phone but were unable to resolve the issues raised by the Demurrer and the Motion. (Aly Decl. ¶¶ 2–3.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met.

 

  1. Page Limits

 

Per California Rules of Court rule 3.1113(d), “no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 15 pages,” which limit “does not include the caption page, the notice of motion and motion, exhibits, declarations, attachments, the table of contents, the table of authorities, or the proof of service.”

 

Although Plaintiff alleges that the Demurrer exceeds the permitted page limits, the Demurrer’s memorandum of points and authorities is 15 pages in length and therefore meets the page limit requirement.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10, subd. (a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿¿ 

 

“If the party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed fails to object to the pleading, either by demurrer or answer, that party is deemed to have waived the objection unless it is an objection that the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading or an objection that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.80, subd. (a).)

 

When Defendants answered the Complaint, they waived objections to the causes of action pled therein other than any objections related to a lack of subject-matter jurisdiction or objections that the Complaint did not state facts sufficient to constitute the causes of action. Defendants argue that the FAC significantly changes each cause of action from the Complaint by adding factual allegations such that they cannot be said to have waived any objections to the causes of action in the FAC. (Demurrer pp. 16–17.) However, we disagree that the new factual allegations, which we previously held to “not change the nature of the legal obligations being brought against Defendants” (4/8/24 Minute Order p. 8), significantly change the causes of action in the FAC that were previously brought in the Complaint. We therefore find that Defendants waived objections to the first seven causes of action in the FAC, except for their objections raised in the Demurrer that the FAC fails to plead facts sufficient to state any of its causes of action. We therefore do not address any grounds on which the Demurrer is brought against the first seven causes of action except the Demurrer’s objection that the FAC fails to plead facts sufficient to state those causes of action.

 

1.      The First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 

“A cause of action for breach of contract requires pleading of a contract, plaintiff's performance or excuse for failure to perform, defendant's breach and damage to plaintiff resulting therefrom.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1031.)

 

The FAC pleads that Plaintiff entered the Lease with the Original Owner. (FAC ¶ 6.) It also alleges that “Plaintiff performed each and every one of the obligations he had under the terms of the Lease, except to the extent that such obligations were waived or were otherwise not enforceable as a result of Defendant’s prior material breaches.” (Id. at ¶ 32.) Finally, it alleges that “Defendants breached the material terms of the Lease” and that “[e]ach of the breaches resulted in Plaintiff suffering both direct and consequential damages.” (Id. at ¶¶ 33, 35.) Thus, the FAC alleges each of the elements of a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

2.      The Second Cause of Action for Breach of the Warranty of Habitability

 

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability are the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises' habitability, notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant's discovery of the condition, the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency, and resulting damages.” (Peviani v. Arbors at California Oaks Property Owner, LLC (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 874, 891.)

 

The FAC alleges that “Plaintiff [] notif[ied] Defendants, both verbally and in writing, of habitability issues on the Property, such as leaks, holes on the ceiling and floors that allowed for insets and vermin to enter the Property, faulty toilets, and bathrooms, etc.” (FAC ¶ 25.) The FAC further alleges that “[t]o the date of the drafting of the [Complaint], habitability issues remain on the Property that have yet to be addressed and remediated” and “[a]s a direct and proximate result of Defendants’ failure to correct the defective conditions, Plaintiff suffered property damage and economic loss as special damages.” (Id. at ¶ 26, 43.) Thus, the FAC alleges each of the elements of a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the second cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability.

 

3.      The Third Cause of Action for Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

 

The elements of a cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment are (1) a lease agreement between plaintiff and defendant; (2) the absence of language contrary to the implied covenant that tenant shall have quiet enjoyment and possession; (3) an act or omission of the landlord, or anyone claiming under the landlord, which substantially interferes with a tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for purposes contemplated; and (4) an applicable remedy. (See Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 588–91.)

 

The FAC pleads that Plaintiff entered the Lease with the Original Owner and that the Lease continues with Defendants, in which the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment was “inherent.” (FAC ¶¶ 6, 45.) The FAC alleges that “[a]s a result of the issues Plaintiff repaired and paid for over the years (and the construction/remediation relating thereto), the harassment to pay an illicit rent increase, and threats to tarnish Plaintiff’s credit score, Defendants breached the covenant of quiet enjoyment of Plaintiff” and that “[a]s a result of the breach of the Lease, Plaintiff has been damaged in a sum the exact amount of which has not yet been ascertained.” (Id. at ¶¶ 46, 48.) Thus, the FAC alleges each of the elements of a cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the third cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment.

 

4.      The Fourth Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are “(1) defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct (conduct so extreme as to exceed all bounds of decency in a civilized community) with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard to the probability of causing, emotional distress; and (2) as a result, plaintiff suffered extreme or severe emotional distress.” (Berry v. Frazier (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1258, 1273.) “Outrageous conduct is conduct that is intentional or reckless and so extreme as to exceed all bounds of decency in a civilized community. The defendant's conduct must be directed to the plaintiff, but malicious or evil purpose is not essential to liability. Whether conduct is outrageous is usually a question of fact.” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 203, citing Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001.) “‘Recklessness’¿means¿the deliberate disregard of the high degree of probability that an injury will occur.¿It rises to the level of a conscious choice of a course of action with knowledge of the serious danger to others.” (Samantha B. v. Aurora Vista Del Mar, LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 85, 99.) “In order to avoid a¿demurrer, the plaintiff must allege with ‘great specificity’ the acts which he or she believes are so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.)  

 

In Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 912–13, 922, a residential tenant stated a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress against her landlord sufficient to overcome a demurrer when she alleged that the landlord was aware of substandard habitability conditions, including leaking sewage, defective electrical wiring, and a “heavy” cockroach infestation; that these conditions had caused the county health department to issue a notice to vacate and demolish the property; and that the landlord failed to correct the conditions after a reasonable time. The court in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, at 922, stated that whether “the landlord’s acts are extreme and outrageous and result in severe mental distress” “presents a factual question [so that] it cannot be said as a matter of law that appellant has not stated a cause of action.”  

 

Here, the FAC alleges that Plaintiff is a residential tenant of Defendants’, that the Property contained “habitability issues,” and that although “Defendants had actual or constructive knowledge of each defective condition,” they “failed to correct them.” (FAC ¶¶ 12, 14, 37, 39.) The FAC also alleges that Defendants threatened Plaintiff and attempted to retaliatorily evict him. (Id. at ¶¶ 17–23.) The FAC alleges that “[t]hrough th[is] outrageous conduct [], Defendants, and each of them, acted with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing Plaintiff to suffer severe emotional distress,” that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of Defendants' actions, by intentionally failing to inform [Plaintiff] of the issues and the illegal rent increase and subsequent retaliation following threats of eviction, or responding to Plaintiff’s contention relating to the substantial rent increase in a timely manner, or providing Plaintiff with alternative living arrangements during the repairs, even after knowing that these conditions and defects effects were felt in the Property, and by intentionally refusing to take action and intentionally retaliating against Plaintiff trying to force him to involuntarily vacate, the Defendants’ action and inaction with regards to these issues directly and proximately led to the injuries of Plaintiff,” and that “Plaintiff has suffered and will continue to suffer pain and suffering, and extreme and severe mental anguish and emotional distress.” (Id. at ¶¶ 50–51, 53.) Like in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 912–13, 922, this adequately states a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress to overcome a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

5.      The Fifth Cause of Action for Retaliatory Eviction

 

The elements of a cause of action for retaliatory eviction are (1) lessor acts to, or threatens to, bring an action to evict the tenant (2) because the tenant exercised a cognizable right, including (a) having complained to an appropriate agency regarding the habitability of a dwelling or (b) having exercised any right under the chapter (see Civil Code § 1940, et seq.). (Feldman v. 1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1493; Civil Code § 1942.5, subd. (a), (c).) In Schweiger v. Superior Court (1970) 3 Cal.3d 507, 517, the Supreme Court held that the landlord of a month-to-month tenancy was not entitled to raise a tenant's rent, bring an action for unlawful detainer, and obtain the tenant's eviction in retaliation for tenant's exercise of statutory right to obtain the repair of two broken windows and a broken back door that could not be locked.

 

The FAC brings the retaliatory eviction cause of action “pursuant to both common law and statute.” (FAC ¶ 59.) The FAC alleges that Plaintiff made complaints to Defendants “regarding the state of the Property, and in fact had to make necessary repairs and paid for costs/supplies out of Plaintiff’s own pocket. Further, Plaintiff made these complaints to other parties and government agencies, causing Defendants to incur costs and leading Defendants to actually seek information whether false or not from any party to try and evict Plaintiff.” (Id. at ¶ 55.) The FAC also alleges, “[b]ecause of these legal complaints, Defendants retaliated against Plaintiff by serving a Notice to Pay or Quit and/or Cure or Quit after raising the rent almost nine times over what is allowable under relevant California law” and “[a]fter Plaintiff’s complaints about habitability issues on the Property and Plaintiff’s assertion of his rights on the Property, such as Plaintiff’s requests to be free from unlawful rent increases and unlawful harassment and retaliation, Defendant, using an agent of theirs called Jenny Casco, filed an unlawful detainer against Plaintiff.” (Id. at ¶¶ 23, 56.) Thus, the FAC alleges that after Plaintiff complained to Defendants and government agencies about the Property’s habitability, Defendants retaliated by serving Plaintiff with a three-day pay-or-quit notice and by filing an unlawful detainer action against him, both of which are actions done to evict a tenant. The FAC thereby adequately states a claim for retaliatory eviction.

 

Although Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s claim for retaliatory eviction must fail because “Plaintiff has remained in the property for over six years and has not been evicted,” actual complete eviction is not a required element of this cause of action. (Demurrer p. 21.) So long as Defendants acted to, or threatened to, bring an action to evict Plaintiff, he may bring a cause of action for retaliatory eviction. Defendants also argue that “[t]he reference or reports to ‘government agencies’ is unsupported by any fact in the FAC.” (Ibid.) However, this allegation itself is an alleged fact, although not in the “background facts” section of the FAC, and is also not the only grounds on which Plaintiff alleges he asserted his rights and for which he alleges Defendants retaliated against him.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the fifth cause of action for retaliatory eviction.

 

6.      The Sixth Cause of Action for Declaratory Judgment

 

The FAC states, “Plaintiff seeks a declaration pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure §1060 that the February 1, 2021 [notice to pay or quit] is illegal, invalid and unenforceable.”

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1060 provides, “[a]ny person interested under a written instrument… or under a contract, or who desires a declaration of his or her rights or duties with respect to another, or in respect to, in, over or upon property… may, in cases of actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties, bring an original action or cross-complaint in the superior court for a declaration of his or her rights and duties in the premises, including a determination of any question of construction or validity arising under the instrument or contract. He or she may ask for a declaration of rights or duties, either alone or with other relief; and the court may make a binding declaration of these rights or duties, whether or not further relief is or could be claimed at the time. The declaration may be either affirmative or negative in form and effect, and the declaration shall have the force of a final judgment. The declaration may be had before there has been any breach of the obligation in respect to which said declaration is sought.”

 

In Meyer v. Sprint Spectrum L.P. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 634, 647, the California Supreme Court explained, “[t]he purpose of a declaratory judgment is to serve some practical end in quieting or stabilizing an uncertain or disputed jural relation. Another purpose is to liquidate doubts with respect to uncertainties or controversies which might otherwise result in subsequent litigation. One test of the right to institute proceedings for declaratory judgment is the necessity of present adjudication as a guide for plaintiff's future conduct in order to preserve his legal rights.” (Internal citations omitted.)

 

Defendants argue that “Plaintiff has no standing to seek declaratory relief concerning a hypothetical unlawful detainer action that is not being litigated” and that “[s]hould such an action arise, Plaintiff can mount his defense.” (Demurrer p. 22.) However, an unlawful detainer action does not have to have already been filed in order for an “actual controversy” to exist. As the Court in Meyer v. Sprint Spectrum L.P., supra, 45 Cal.4th 634 at 647 explained, all that is needed is the existence of “doubts with respect to uncertainties or controversies which might otherwise result in subsequent litigation,” which exists here given that the notice in issue in a pay-or-quit notice that includes a disputed rent amount. We find that an actual controversy with respect to the validity and legality of the notice in question exists.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the sixth cause of action for declaratory judgment.

 

7.      The Seventh Cause of Action for Injunctive Relief

 

Plaintiff seeks “a permanent injunction against Defendants to enjoin them from retaliating against Plaintiff by illegal rent increases and/or evicting Plaintiff from the Property or attempting the same without a hearing on the merits and Court order allowing the same.” (FAC ¶ 69.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed “to meet his burden on either of these prongs [that courts consider when considering whether to issue a preliminary injunction]” and consequently that “injunctive relief must be denied.” (Demurrer pp. 22–23.) Defendants also argue that “[t]he FAC fails to specify the injunctive relief sought nor identify any interim harm that Plaintiff may suffer. As explained above, Plaintiff has adequate remedies at law. The FAC fails to allege impending injury that could only be averted by injunction.” (Id. at p. 23.) However, Plaintiff is not required to meet his burden of showing that injunctive relief should be granted at this stage. He must only show that he may seek injunctive relief. The FAC specifies that Plaintiff seeks “a permanent injunction against Defendants to enjoin them from retaliating against Plaintiff by illegal rent increases and/or evicting Plaintiff from the Property or attempting the same without a hearing on the merits and Court order allowing the same.” He may seek such.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the seventh cause of action for injunctive relief.

 

8.      The Eighth Cause of Action for Violation of Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance

 

The Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance went into effect on August 6, 2021, a few days before the Complaint was filed, and permits a tenant to bring civil proceedings against a landlord who engages in any “knowing and willful course of conduct directed at a specific tenant or tenants that causes detriment and harm, and that serves no lawful purpose.” (L.A. Municipal Code Ord. No. 187109, available at https://housing2.lacity.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/08/Tenant-Anti-Harassment-Ordinance-TAHO-187109-8.6.21.pdf.) Examples of harassment for purposes of the Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance include (1) “[f]ailing to perform and timely complete necessary repairs and maintenance required by Federal, State, County, or local housing, health, or safety laws,” (2) “[a]buse of the right of access into a rental unit as established and limited by California Civil Code Section 1954, including entering or photographing portions of a rental unit that are beyond the scope of a lawful entry or inspection,” and (3) “[t]hreatening or taking action to terminate any tenancy including service of any notice to quit or other eviction notice or bringing action to recover possession of a rental unit based on facts which the landlord has no reasonable cause to believe to be true.” (Ibid.)

 

The FAC alleges that “[after August 10, 2021], Plaintiff continued to notify Defendants, both verbally and in writing, of habitability issues on the Property, such as leaks, holes on the ceiling and floors that allowed for insets and vermin to enter the Property, faulty toilets, and bathrooms, etc.,” that “[t]o the date of the drafting of the [complaint], habitability issues remain on the Property that have yet to be addressed and remediated,” that “[o]n August 3, 2023, on the afternoon of [] Elalami’s deposition on this matter, [] Elalami engaged in further retaliation and harassment, serving on Plaintiff an unlawful and inaccurate notice to pay rent or quit, a 24 hour notice to enter Property without specifying an adequate time or reason for entry, a form requesting responses to questions that should be sought through the party’s written discovery, and an untimely notice of change of ownership which fails to detail how Plaintiff could pay rent, to who rent can be paid, and through what method Plaintiff could pay rent,” and that “[o]n or about August 4, 2023, Defendant arrived to Plaintiff’s Property, unannounced, demanding entry into Property. Despite Plaintiff’s objections and Plaintiff not being home at that time, [] Elalami entered the Property with a pest control technician who was there to attempt to finally remediate the rats and insects entering the Property through large holes on the ceiling and on the floor under the sink.” (FAC ¶¶ 25–26, 28–29.) Thus, the FAC alleges that after the Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance became effective, Defendants failed to perform or timely complete needed repairs and maintenance, entered the Property beyond the scope of a lawful entry or inspection, and served a notice to quit despite there being no reasonable grounds to do so. This sufficiently pleads harassment as defined by the Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance that took place after it went into effect.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the eighth cause of action for violations of the Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance.

 

  1. The Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)¿“[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)¿ 

 

1.      Punitive Damages

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.) 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”  (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive¿damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “A claim for exemplary damage may be supported by pleading that the wrong was committed willfully or with a design to injure.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1055.) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice.¿However, malice does not require actual intent to harm.¿Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)  

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,” within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra, at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].) 

 

“[P]unitive¿damages¿may be awarded in an action by a¿residential¿tenant¿based on the landlord's interference with peaceful possession. Punitive¿damages¿likewise are recoverable for… the infliction of emotional distress.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th 1004 at 1055, internal citations omitted.) In Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 920, a residential tenant “pleaded sufficient facts to support her prayer for exemplary damages” when her complaint both “alleged that defendant had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises including leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions” and “alleged that defendants ‘In maintaining said nuisance,… acted with full knowledge of the consequences thereof and the damage being caused to plaintiff, and their conduct was willful, oppressive and malicious.’” 

 

Defendants argue that “Plaintiff’s factual allegations fall drastically short of the requisite level of malice required for punitive damages” because “[t]he FAC contains no specific facts regarding any outrageous conduct by the Defendants.” (Motion p. 7.) However, the FAC pleads specific factual allegations about habitability issues, Defendants’ interference with Plaintiff’s peaceful possession of the Property—for example, the FAC includes specific allegations including date about instances when Elalami entered the Property without proper notice to Plaintiff or Plaintiff’s permission—and threats or improper eviction attempts that Defendants made to Plaintiff. The FAC also alleges that Defendants “had actual or constructive knowledge of each defective condition and failed to correct them” but rather responded in a “malicious and reckless manner.” (FAC ¶¶ 39, 51.) Following Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 920, these allegations are sufficient to maintain a claim for punitive damages against a landlord who has interfered with a tenant’s peaceful possession or intentionally inflicted emotional distress, as the FAC alleges Defendants have done to Plaintiff.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to strike punitive damages.

 

2.      “…And Sexual Advances of Defendants…”

 

Defendants move to strike the phrase “and sexual advances of Defendants” from paragraph 52 of the FAC. (Motion p. 2.) Plaintiff describes this phrase as a “scrivener’s error” and agrees that it should be struck. (Opposition p. 4.)

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion to strike the phrase “and sexual advances of Defendants” from paragraph 52 of the FAC. Because Parties agreed to the striking of this phrase, this court does not grant leave to amend.

 

3.      Sentence Alleging Complaints to Government Agencies

 

Defendants move to strike a sentence in paragraph 55 of the FAC which reads, “[f]urther, Plaintiff made these complaints to other parties and government agencies, causing Defendants to incur costs and leading Defendants to actually seek information whether false or not from any party to try and evict Plaintiff.” (Motion p. 2.) Defendant argues the sentence appears to be an “outlier[] from the remainder of the FAC and lack[s] any reference or support from the other allegations.” (Id. at p. 5.) Defendant suggests the sentence is “inadvertently inserted boilerplate language” as “Plaintiff does not elsewhere contend that such reports occurred.” (Id. at p. 7.) Plaintiff does not concede that the sentence alleging complaints to government agencies was inserted in the FAC by mistake and argues that it was “properly pled.” (Opposition p. 4.) That an allegation is not repeated or referred to elsewhere in the pleading is not a ground on which a motion to strike may be granted.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to strike the sentence alleging complaints to government agencies in paragraph 55 of the FAC.
