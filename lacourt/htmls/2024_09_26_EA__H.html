DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV01282    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: H

Ross, et al. v. Hernandez, et al., Case No. 23PSCV01282

ORDER ON MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendants Bryan Perez Rodriguez and Neri Rodriguez’s Motion to Strike the “Punitive” Attachment from Plaintiffs’ Second Amended Complaint is GRANTED. The Court will hear from Plaintiffs’ counsel whether leave to amend is requested and if so, will require an offer of proof.


Background

Plaintiffs Sheldon Ross (“Ross”) and Pamela Jones Calcote (“Calcote”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) brought this lawsuit after allegedly sustaining injuries in a May 2, 2021 motor vehicle accident. On November 21, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint”) against Bryan Perez Rodriguez (“Bryan”), Neri Rodriguez (together, “Defendants”), and Does 1-25, where they allege that Defendants negligently collided into Plaintiffs’ vehicle and caused severe injuries and damages to Plaintiffs.

Pending before the Court is Defendants’ motion for an order striking out the “Punitive” attachment to the Second Amended Complaint.


Legal Standard

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

Discussion

Defendants move the Court for an order striking the attachment labeled “Punitive” at page seven of the Operative Complaint on the grounds that the allegations therein do not support a claim for punitive damages.

At the outset, the Court addresses Defendants’ request for judicial notice. Defendants request, on page five of their memorandum of points and authorities, that the Court take judicial notice of excerpts Ross’ and Calcote’s respective depositions. The Court declines to do so for two reasons.

First, Defendants have failed to comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113(l) (i.e., “[A]ny request for judicial notice must be made in a separate document listing the specific items for which notice is requested.”).

Second, the Court must not turn a hearing on a motion to strike “into a contested evidentiary hearing through the guise of having the court take judicial notice of affidavits, declarations, depositions, and other such material which was filed on behalf of the adverse party and which purports to contradict the allegations and contentions of the plaintiff.” (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604-05) [“The court will take judicial notice of records such as admissions, answers to interrogatories, affidavits, and the like, when considering a demurrer, only where they contain statements of the plaintiff or his agent which are inconsistent with the allegations of the pleading before the court.” (Emphasis added.])

Turning to the merits of Defendants’ motion, Defendants contend that the allegations of the Operative Complaint do not support a claim for punitive damages.  The Court agrees.

Punitive damages may be awarded in an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract upon clear and convincing evidence that a defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)

“Malice” is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

A “conclusory characterization of defendant’s conduct as intentional, willful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of ‘oppression, fraud, or malice.’” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.)

The California Supreme Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 892 determined that “the act of operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated may constitute an act of ‘malice’ under § 3294 if performed under circumstances which disclose a conscious disregard of the probable dangerous consequences.” In Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89, the Fourth District Court of Appeal noted that driving while intoxicated does not always give rise to a claim for punitive damages: “[W]e do not agree that the risk created generally by one who becomes intoxicated and decides nevertheless to drive a vehicle on the public streets is the same as the risk created by an intoxicated driver’s decision to zigzag in and out of traffic at 65 miles per hour in a crowded beach recreation area at 1:30 in the afternoon on a Sunday in June. The risk of injury to others from ordinary driving while intoxicated is certainly foreseeable, but it is not necessarily probable.” Notably, both Taylor and Dawes were decided prior to 1987, when the Legislature added the requirement to Civil Code § 3294 that conduct be “despicable” in order to support imposition of punitive damages under a malice/willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others standard.

Plaintiffs have alleged, in relevant part, that before the collision, “Defendant voluntarily began drinking and did in fact consume, a large quantity of alcohol with the intention and expectation of operating a motor vehicle shortly thereafter”; that “Defendant knew and was fully aware that he was going to be required to drive his vehicle when he first began drinking alcohol and nevertheless consumed a large quantity of alcohol knowing the risk that he would be intoxicated as a result thereof’; that “[a]t the time of the collision, defendant was driving his vehicle under the influence of alcoholic beverage and/or drugs, or under the combined influence of alcoholic beverage and drugs.” Plaintiffs proceed to make additional allegations that are expressly made on “information and belief.” Specifically, Plaintiffs allege, on information and belief, that Defendant B. Rodriguez was “recklessly traveling at a high rate of speed estimated at 100 to 120 mph” and that he “made unsafe lane changes while traveling at a high rate of speed and lost control of his vehicle.”

“A plaintiff may allege on information and belief any matters that are not within his personal knowledge, if he has information leading to believe that the allegations are true, and thus a pleading made on information and belief is insufficient if it merely assert[s]] the facts so alleged without alleging such information that leads [the plaintiff] to believe that the allegations are true.” (Gomes v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1158-1159 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) Plaintiffs have not alleged what information they have which led them to believe that the foregoing allegations are true; as such, these allegations are disregarded as insufficient.

The motion is granted. 

Case Number: 23PSCV01695    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: H

Puente Hills Business Center, L.P. v. Fan (23PSCV01695)

ORDER ON CROSS-DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER TO CROSS-COMPLAINT

Cross-Defendant Xu Fan’s Demurrer to Cross-Complainants Sherman Liu’s and Connie Sheng’s Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED, with 20 days’ leave to amend.

Background

Plaintiff Puente Hills Business Center, L.P. (“Puente Hills”) alleges as follows:

On or about December 5, 2012, Puente Hills, as landlord, and FCC Group International (U.S.A.), Inc. (“FCC”), as tenant, entered into a written lease agreement (the “lease”) wherein FCC leased the property located at 17870 Castleton Street, Suite 126, in the City of Industry (the “original premises”) for a three-year term commencing January 18, 2013. Xu Yan Sherry Fan aka Xuyan Sherry Fan (“Fan”) executed a written Guaranty of Lease (“Guaranty”). 

The term of the lease was subsequently extended. Amendment No. 1 added additional square footage to the lease (i.e., Suites 132 and 134) (together with Suite 126, the “expanded premises”) and extended the lease term through January 31, 2017. A second amendment to the lease, Amendment No. 2, reduced the square footage to the lease (i.e., omitting Suite 126; the “reduced premises”) and extended the lease term through March 31, 2019. The third amendment to the lease, Amendment No. 3, extended the lease term through March 31, 2022, increased base rent, and provided for scheduled increases in base rent. Fan reaffirmed her guarantee of the lease by executing all three amendments. FCC subleased or assigned the Lease to M Capital Global California without Puente Hill’s knowledge or consent. FCC also failed to pay all rent due.

On June 6, 2023, Puente Hills filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Fan and Does 1-10 for (1) breach of written guaranty and (2) common count.

On March 14, 2024, Fan filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint, asserting causes of action against Domain Technology & Economy Development, Inc., Sherman Liu (“Liu”), Connie Sheng (erroneously sued as “Connie Tseng”) (“Sheng”), Puente Hills, and Roes 1-10 for (1) indemnity, (2) fraud, (3) negligence, (4) appropriation of name and likeness, and (5) identity theft. 

On April 11, 2024, Liu and Sheng filed a cross-complaint, asserting a cause of action against Fan and Voes 1-10 for breach of contract. 

Pending before the Court is Cross-Defendant Fan’s Demurrer to Liu and Sheng’s Cross-Complaint.

Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

Discussion

Fan demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f)[FN1], to the sole cause of action (i.e., for Breach of Contract) in Liu’s and Sheng’s cross-complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) 

A written contract may be pleaded either by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must allege the substance of its relevant terms.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489 [internal quotations and citations omitted].) 

Liu and Sheng have not attached a copy of any contract to their cross-complaint, nor have they set forth any contract verbatim. Liu and Sheng have alleged, without more, that they loaned Fan $30,000.00 in or about November/December 2019, at Feng’s request; that Fan reaffirmed the loan in front of mutual friends and acquaintances on many occasions, expressing that she owed them $30,000 for a loan they had given her and that she would pay them the money back; that the most recent affirmation of the loan was on or about September 1, 2022; and that Fan has failed to pay back the $30,000, or any amount thereof, as of the complaint filing date, despite their numerous requests and Fan’s numerous promises that she would do so. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 5-7 and 9-12.) 

The foregoing allegations fail to identify whether the loan was oral or written, the parties’ respective obligations, and the terms of repayment. The Court thus sustains the demurrer, with 20 days’ leave to amend.


[FN1] The Court notes that Fan also cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(g) on page 5 of her memorandum of points and authorities (i.e., a demurrer may be made on the basis that “[i]n an action founded upon a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct”). Although this ground was not identified in the notice of motion, the Court has elected to consider it, inasmuch as it was also included in counsel’s May 16, 2024 meet and confer correspondence to Liu and Sheng. (Ma Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. B.)

Case Number: 24PSCV00710    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: H

Luevano-Macias v. Starbucks Corporation, et al. (24PSCV00710)

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

Defendant Starbucks Corporation’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED. The Court is not inclined to grant leave to amend, but will hear from counsel for Plaintiff at the hearing; if Plaintiff requests leave to amend, the Court will require an offer of proof.


Background

On or about January 23, 2024, Plaintiff Crystal Luevano-Macias (“Plaintiff”) sustained severe burns after hot coffee spilled onto her hand and lap while patronizing the Starbucks drive-through in West Covina.

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Starbucks Corporation (“Starbucks”), Eugene Weiss and Does 1-50 for:

1.       Negligence—Premises;

2.       Negligence—Supervision.

Starbucks has demurred to the first cause of action on the basis that it fails to state a claim for premises liability. The Court agrees and thus sustains the demurrer.

 

Legal Standard

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

 

Discussion

Starbucks demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e) and (f), to the first cause of action in Plaintiff’s complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. 

“The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (McIntyre v. The Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671 [quotations and citation omitted].)

“Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158 [internal quotations and citation omitted]; Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37 [“Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm”]; CACI No. 1000. Premises Liability—Essential Factual Elements [To establish a premises liability claim, plaintiff “must prove all of the following: 1. That [name of defendant] [owned/leased/occupied/controlled] the property; 2. That [name of defendant] was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property; 3. That [name of plaintiff] was harmed; and 4. That [name of defendant]’s negligence was a substantial factor in causing [name of plaintiff]’s harm”].)

Plaintiff alleges that on January 23, 2024, she suffered severe burns to her thigh and groin area when she visited Starbucks in West Covina. (Complaint, ¶¶ 2 and 9-12.) Plaintiff alleges that on the date of the incident, she chose to use the drive-through, placed her order, and drove around to the window to pay for and receive her coffee. (Id., ¶ 10.) Plaintiff alleges that because Starbucks’ employee did not properly secure the lid, kept his hand on the sleeve, and “otherwise failed to hand the coffee off properly and reasonably to [her],” the hot coffee spilled on her hand and lap. (Id., ¶¶ 10-12.)

Plaintiff’s own allegations reflect that the spill arose from Starbucks’ employee’s purported mishandling of the coffee lid and sleeve when the coffee was handed to Plaintiff who was sitting in her vehicle at the drive-through window. Plaintiff has not alleged any facts supporting or relating to Starbucks’ use or maintenance of the property or premises to support a cause of action for premises liability and there is nothing about the property or premises to implicate liability.

Starbucks’ demurrer is sustained. If Plaintiff seeks leave to amend, the Court will require an offer of proof.
