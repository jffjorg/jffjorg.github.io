DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV00255    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

COLIN CAREW, an individual,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

23TRCV00255

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted in part/denied in part

 

JOSE ALFREDO MILLAN GUZMAN, an individual; EXTREME TRANSPORTATION, INC.; and DOES 1-50 INCLUSIVE,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             January 22, 2025

 

Moving Parties:          Plaintiff Colin Carew

 

Responding Party:      Defendant Extreme Transportation Inc.

 

HEARING: MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT’S PERSON MOST QUALIFIED AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

RULING

 

            The Court grants the Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendants PMQ and Denies the Motion to Compel the Request for the Production of Documents.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On January 30, 2023, Plaintiff Colin Carew filed the Complaint against Defendants Jose Alfredo Millan Guzman and Extreme Transportation Inc., alleging a single cause of action for negligent operation of a motor vehicle arising from a vehicle collision.

 

            On August 28, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent its first notice of Deposition to Defendants’

PMQ.

 

            On September 10, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a deposition subpoena for Vince Ichihara.

 

On September 16, 2024, and September 23, 2024, Parties met and conferred about a

deposition date.

 

            On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an amended notice to depose Defendant’s PMQ.

 

            On October 3, 2024, Defense counsel named Vince Echevarria as their responsive PMQ.

 

            On October 18, 2024, Defendants’ PMQ, Mr. Ichihara, was deposed.

           

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd. (a).) 

 

“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450 subd. (b)(2).)  “Although the statute appears to apply only if the deponent fails to both appear¿and¿produce documents, it has been held to apply simply on a deponent's failure to appear.” (Leko v. Cornerstone Building Inspection Service (2001) 86 Cal.App. 4th 1109, 1124.)   

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiff requests the Court order Defendant to produce a PMQ and produce the documents outlined in the deposition notice.

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion to compel must be “accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450.)

 

Here, the parties have met and conferred regarding that deposition numerous times before the filing of this motion. Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

Separate Statement

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s motion is procedurally defective because it fails to include a separate statement as required by California Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(a) for motions involving the content of discovery requests or responses. This requirement does not apply only when no response has been provided, but Plaintiff’s motion clearly involves disputes over the sufficiency of Defendant’s responses and objections, not the absence of responses. Plaintiff acknowledged these responses and detailed meet and confer efforts in their motion and supporting declaration, further confirming the necessity of a separate statement.

 

Defendant asserts that attaching the responses to the motion is insufficient, as Rule 3.1345(c) expressly prohibits this practice. The court has the discretion to deny the motion solely on this procedural ground. (Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893.)

 

Additionally, Defendant contends that Plaintiff has failed to show good cause for the requested production of documents. Although Plaintiff quotes the statutory language, the motion does not substantively address or demonstrate good cause. Preparing a proper separate statement might have allowed Plaintiff to argue good cause, but the omission undermines their argument. For these reasons, Defendant asserts that the court should deny Plaintiff’s motion.

 

As to the requests for the production of documents, since Defendant has objected to the requests, Plaintiff cannot compel their production under Code Civil Procedure section 2025.450 subdivision (a). Thus, good cause must be shown under Code Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (b)(1). Without a separate statement laying out Defendant's response to each request, the Court cannot determine whether Defendant's objections are valid.

 

Therefore, the Court denies the Motion to Compel the Production of Documents.

 

Deposition

 

At the deposition of Mr. Ichihara, Defendant stated that Mr. Ichihara was not Defendant’s PMQ and instructed Mr. Ichihara not to answer questions regarding the matters for examination and stated that all Mr. Ichihara has to do is state that the company does not have a PMQ for the particular matter for examination.

 

Defendant argues that they have no one responsive to the matters of examination outlined in the deposition notice. When served with a deposition notice for a PMQ, a Defendant’s duty is limited to “to producing the most knowledgeable person currently in its employ and making sure that that person has access to information and documents reasonably available within the corporation.” (Maldonado v. Superior Ct. (2002) 94 Cal.App.4th 1390, 1398.) Here, Defendant instructed Mr. Ichihara not to search for records or prepare for the deposition. Whether Mr. Ichihara was Designated as Defendant’s PMQ is immaterial. Defendant has a duty to prepare a PMQ with documents regarding the categories for examination and has failed to do so.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant’s PMQ.

 

 

Sanctions

 

Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery is a sanctionable misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d).) If a motion to compel a party to respond to discovery is successful, the court must impose a monetary sanction against that party unless the sanction would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc § 2030.290 subd. (c), 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The monetary sanction includes the moving party’s reasonable expenses, such as attorney’s fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanction be imposed on Defendant. Defendant has requested monetary sanctions be placed on Plaintiff. The Court denies the request to sanction Plaintiff as their motion was granted. However, the Court denies imposing sanctions on Defendant because Defendant showed good cause for opposing the motion as the Court denied the request to compel the production of documents.
