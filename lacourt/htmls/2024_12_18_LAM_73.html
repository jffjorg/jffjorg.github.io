DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV38374    Hearing Date: December 18, 2024    Dept: 73

12/19/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

SHAHNAZ AMIRTALESH v. ROCHELLE STERLING, et al. (19STCV38374)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Shahnaz Amirtalesh (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Krista Dawkins (Pyka Lenhardt Schnaider Dawkins)

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE REGARDING CONTEMPT (filed 11/15/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendants’ Motion for an Order to Show Cause regarding Contempt is GRANTED.

The court sets an Order to Show Cause Hearing Re: Contempt as to why Plaintiff should not be held in contempt for violating the Court’s August 6, 2024 Order for January 30, 2025.   

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 23, 2019, Plaintiff Shahnaz Amirtalesh (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Rochelle H. Sterling (“Sterling”), Beverly Hills Properties (“BHP”), The Sterling Family Trust, Susan Robman and Does 1-50 (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff’s tenancy at 240 N. Crescent Drive, Apt. 107, Beverly Hills, CA 90210 (the “Apartment”).  In her complaint, Plaintiff alleges that the Apartment landlord, BHP, failed to maintain sewage pipes in the building which caused a leak in her shower drain in October 24, 2018; that BHP delayed in granting her request to replace flood-damaged carpeting in her Apartment with laminate floors as a reasonable accommodation for her disability stemming from lung cancer; that BHP did not properly enforce COVID-19 restrictions regarding the use of the Apartment swimming pool; and further, that Apartment manager, Susan Robman, harassed and discriminated against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s religious beliefs.   

 

In the summer of 2019, Plaintiff filed complaints with the County of Los Angeles Department of Public Health (“DPH”) and the City of Beverly Hills regarding her apartment.  On September 5, 2019, the City of Beverly Hills issued a notice of violations to BHP.  On September 10, 2019, DPH issued an Official Inspection Report for violations regarding wall/ceiling floor and faucet fixtures. 

  

On October 23, 2019, Plaintiff filed her complaint against Defendants. Sterling is the owner of BHP and the Apartment and the trustee of the Sterling Family Trust.  On August 7, 2020, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging eight causes of action.  On or about October 20, 2021, the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action in the SAC without leave to amend.  The remaining causes of action are as follows: 

C/A 1: Breach of Warranty of Habitability 
C/A 2: Negligence  
C/A 3: Nuisance  
C/A 4: Violation of Civil Code § 1942.4 
C/A 6: Violation of the California Fair Employment and Housing Act  
C/A 7: Violation of the California Unruh Civil Rights Act 
C/A 8: Breach of Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment 

 

On March 17, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Defendant Susan Robman from the case.

 

On January 24, 2023, the Court granted Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth, sixth and eighth causes of action. At the same hearing, plaintiff dismissed, with prejudice, the fifth and seventh causes of action.

 

On November 15, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion for Order to Show Cause regarding Contempt, arguing:

·       On August 6, 2024, this Court granted Defendants' motion to compel Plaintiff to pay Defendants’ experts’ deposition fees and ordered Plaintiff to pay $3,990 to Defendants on or before September 5, 2024.

·       To date, Plaintiff has not satisfied the Court's order, even partially. (Decl. Dawkins ¶ 13.)

·       Defendants have satisfied the procedural requirements for an OSC

o   Plaintiff had knowledge of the Court’s order

§  Plaintiff was present in court when the Court issued its ruling granting Defendants motion to compel Plaintiff to pay Defendants’ experts’ deposition fees. (Decl. Dawkins ¶ 10.)

§  Plaintiff was also served with the Court’s ruling.

o   Defense counsel is not aware of any reason why Plaintiff would be unable to comply with the Court's order. (Decl. Dawkins ¶ 14.)

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendants’ motion is defective and does not establish the required elements.

·       The Court order was imposed unlawfully on Plaintiff.

·       Plaintiff has sustained an injury from a trip and fall incident making it difficult for Plaintiff to appear at hearings and prepare legal writings.

 

Defendants filed a reply, stating:

·       In Plaintiff’s Opposition, Plaintiff fails to rebut any of the required elements and appears to concede to most of them.

·       Plaintiff concedes her knowledge of the Court order. Thus, she has conceded to the making of the order and her willful disobedience.

·       Plaintiff does not establish any facts suggesting an inability to comply with the Court’s August 6, 2024 order.

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Contempt is any act, in or out of court, “which tends to impede, embarrass or obstruct the court in the discharge of its duties.” (In re Shortridge (1893) 99 Cal. 526, 532.) Particular acts constituting contempt are enumerated by statute, and include “[d]isobedience¿of any lawful judgment, order, or process of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1209.)¿“The power of the court to punish for contempt is indeed broad, but it is not unlimited. It is a drastic remedy, to be employed only when necessary to the proper and orderly conduct of judicial proceedings. A charge of contempt of court must be considered judicially as the question of guilt of any criminal offense must be, and a judge may not punish for contempt merely because he has suffered annoyance through the failure of some order he has made to receive instant observance.” (Chapman v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1968) 261 Cal.App.2d 194, 201.)¿¿ 

 

“A contempt proceeding is commenced by the filing of an affidavit and a request for an order to show cause. [Citation.] After notice to the opposing party's lawyer, the court (if satisfied with the sufficiency of the affidavit) must sign an order to show cause re contempt in which the date and time for a hearing are set forth. [Citations.] The order to show cause acts as a summons to appear in court on a certain day and, as its name suggests, to show cause why a certain thing should not be done. [Citation.] Unless the¿citee¿has concealed himself from the court, he must be personally served with the affidavit and the order to show cause; otherwise, the court lacks jurisdiction to proceed.” (Cedars-Sinai Imaging Med. Grp. v. Superior Court¿(2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1286-1287 (emphasis in original).)¿¿¿ 

 

“As a general rule, the elements of contempt include (1) a valid order, (2) knowledge of the order, (3) ability to comply with the order, and (4) willful failure to comply with the order.” (In re Ivey (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 793, 798.)¿¿ 

 

B.      Discussion

 

Defendants move for an order setting an OSC hearing regarding contempt against Plaintiff for violating the Court’s August 6, 2024 order.

 

On August 6, 2024, this Court granted Defendants' motion to compel Plaintiff to pay Defendants’ experts’ deposition fees and ordered Plaintiff to pay $3,990 to Defendants on or before September 5, 2024. (Court’s 08/06/24 Minute Order.) Defendants contend that to date, Plaintiff has not satisfied the Court’s order. (Dawkins Decl., ¶ 14.)

 

The Court finds that Defendants have satisfied the procedural requirements for the issuance of an OSC. As discussed above, the elements of contempt include (1) a valid order, (2) knowledge of the order, (3) ability to comply with the order, and (4) willful failure to comply with the order.” (In re Ivey (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 793, 798.)

 

First, Defendants have established the Court issued a valid order. The Court’s August 6, 2024 order was in writing and entered in the minutes as required under Jablon v. Henneberger (1949) 33 Cal.2d 773, 775 (“It is the general rule that an order is ineffective unless filed with the clerk or entered in the minutes”). Second, Defendants contend that Plaintiff had knowledge of the order because: (1) she was present in court at the August 6, 2024 hearing when the Court issued its ruling; (2) the notice of the ruling was served on Plaintiff; (3) defense counsel sent Plaintiff emails reminding her of the Court’s order to pay Defendants’ experts’ fees by September 5, 2024. (Dawkins Decl., ¶ 5, 10, 11, Exs. A, B, E, G.) In Plaintiff’s opposition, she does not appear to dispute her knowledge of the Court’s order. (Oppos., p. 8.) Third, Defendants assert they are not aware of any reason that Plaintiff would be unable to comply with the Court’s order. (Dawkins Decl., ¶ 14.) Plaintiff fails to establish her inability to comply with the order. While Plaintiff argues in her opposition that she suffered an injury that makes it difficult to attend court hearings and to prepare legal writings, Plaintiff fails to show how this would impact her ability to comply with the Court’s order to pay for Defendant’s experts’ fees. Fourth, Defendants have sufficiently established willful disobedience since Plaintiff has not complied with the Court’s order despite being aware of the order. (Dawkins Decl., ¶ 14.)

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ Motion for an Order to Show Cause regarding Contempt is GRANTED.

CONCLUSION

Defendants’ Motion for an Order to Show Cause regarding Contempt is GRANTED.

The court sets an Order to Show Cause Hearing Re: Contempt as to why Plaintiff should not be held in contempt for violating the Court’s August 6, 2024 Order for January 30, 2025.

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.)

Kuntsevich orally and in writing communicated to Chernova various issues: (a) Misrepresentation in KopenTech Partners' K-1 Information for 2019-2021 tax returns, potentially constituting tax fraud; (b) Non-compliance of KopenTech's financial statements with Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), including critical discrepancies affecting Gross Margin, unaccounted intangible assets, opaque capitalization approach for Software and missing intrinsic value expense of employee stock options; (c) Unrealistic valuation of KopenTech, obstructing attempts at rectification; (d) Incorrect processing of the Small Business Administration's PPP application, resulting in a lower forgiveness amount, inaccurately reflected in the 2022 financial statements; (e) State tax registration errors, incurring penalties that KopenTech needed to but wasn't addressing; and (f) Unauthorized legal expenses accrued by Chernova. Despite making some progress in addressing the issues, the overall situation did not improve sufficiently. In early November 2023, Kuntsevich requested written permission via email to speak with the Board about these matters. Chernova did not respond to Kuntsevich's request. On November 21, 2023, Chernova terminated Kuntsevich's employment at KopenTech, stating the company wished to "pursue a different direction." The next day, Kuntsevich wrote to the Board and informed the Board of the financial fraud and related issues. Kuntsevich did not receive the 2 percent equity Chernova promised.

 

On August 13, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike, arguing:

·       The FAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Kopentech Capital Markets, LLC (“KCM”)

o   Throughout the FAC, Plaintiff refers generally to KopenTech, an all-encompassing term for both KopenTech, LLC and KCM, and fails to provide any details regarding what exactly is being alleged against whom. Plaintiff does acknowledge and alleges that the two entities KCM and KopenTech are different. (FAC ¶¶ 2-3.) As such, the FAC is vague as to its supposed allegations against KCM.

o   Plaintiff does not allege any sufficient facts to establish that KCM employed him. In particular, Plaintiff has failed to (1) plead a unity of interest and ownership between Defendants sufficiently; and (2) plead any supporting facts of the elements of alter ego liability other than conclusory statements. Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2003) 83 Cal. App. 4th 523, 538.

·       The FAC broadly groups all named Defendants as “Defendants,” and refers to both KCM and KopenTech as “KopenTech” therefore rendering the FAC uncertain.

·       Plaintiff’s intentional and negligent misrepresentation claims fail

o   To support Plaintiff’s causes of action for misrepresentation, Plaintiff must allege that Defendants either intentionally or negligently made a misrepresentation as to a past or existing fact.

o   Plaintiff was allegedly due to receive this stock in July 2023, a future event, as he alleges, he was interviewed in December 2022. (FAC, ¶¶ 12, 13).

o   This alleged promise does not constitute a representation regarding existing or past material facts which could serve as the basis for a misrepresentation cause of action.

o   Further it is ambiguous as to what a “2 percent equity stake” is. Was he to be provided 2 percent of the private stock in the company? Was he to be provided with a 2 percent profit sharing plan? Without these essential details outlining the terms of said alleged agreement, the FAC is uncertain and fails to state facts sufficient to sustain a misrepresentation action as to the “2 percent stake” in “KopenTech.”

·       Motion to strike

o   Defendants move to strike all references to punitive damages in the FAC

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff refers to both KCM and KT as KopenTech throughout his FAC because he is alleging that both of the parties are his former employers.

o   Plaintiff refers to KCM as his employer. Plaintiff states in his FAC that “Defendants KopenTech, LLC and KopenTech Capital Markets, LLC, are collectively referred to herein as “KopenTech.” (FAC ¶4) Therefore, any reference, including reference to KopenTech being his employer, throughout the FAC is also implicating KCM.

o   The FAC states that KCM and KT make up an integrated enterprise. (FAC ¶4:6-8) And to support this allegation, Plaintiff puts forward that both “companies” share the same board of directors, and that Plaintiff was responsible for operations of both companies, inferring that the KCM and KT were one and the same and the Plaintiff was their employee. (FAC ¶5:12-13)

o   Plaintiff pled in in paragraph nine on information and belief that Defendants were “joint employers” directly alleging that both KCM and KT were her employer. (FAC ¶9:28) Second, in paragraph 25 of the FAC Plaintiff alleges that KopenTech was Plaintiff’s employer. (FAC ¶25:2-4)

·       Plaintiff’s FAC is not uncertain, because the factual allegations in the complaint are sufficient for the Defendants to understand Plaintiff’s claims against Defendants.

·       Plaintiff sufficiently alleged his misrepresentation claims because he stated that he was promised equity in Defendants KopenTech by Defendant Chernova and relied on that to continue to work for Defendants but never received the payment.

o   Plaintiff provided sufficient facts stating Chernova made an oral promise around December 2022 to pay Plaintiff as part of his compensation package a 2% equity in Defendants KopenTech, that Defendants failed to pay him.

o   Plaintiff’s allegation that he was promised equity stake in Defendants is not ambiguous because it is generally understood that it means ownership in the company.

·       Defendant’s motion to strike should be denied because the allegations in the First Amended Complaint sufficiently allege outrageous conduct and fraud, malice, and oppression.

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff failed to plead facts to constitute a cause of action, that the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Kopentech Capital Markets, LLC and that Plaintiff has not pled facts warranting punitive damages.  

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating they sent a correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel identifying the alleged defects in the FAC. (Dulloo Decl., ¶ 3.) The letter asked Plaintiff’s counsel to contact Defendants’ counsel’s office to discuss the issues raised in the meet and confer letter. (Ibid.) Defendants’ counsel states Plaintiff’s counsel failed to respond to the letter. (Dulloo Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendants’ counsel called Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss the deficiencies in the FAC, however, he was not able to reach Plaintiff’s counsel and left a voicemail requesting a call back. (Ibid.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

B.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.      All Causes of Action against Defendant Kopentech Capital Markets, LLC

 

Defendants demur to all causes of action against Defendant Kopentech Capital Markets, LLC (“KCM”). Defendant argues that no specific conduct has been alleged to have been committed by KCM and Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged that KCM was the alter ego of KopenTech, LLC.

 

In Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 235-36, the Court of Appeal held the following was sufficient to allege alter ego liability:  

 

Rutherford alleged that Caswell dominated and controlled PDR; that a unity of interest and ownership existed between Caswell and PDR; that PDR was a mere shell and conduit for Caswell's affairs; that PDR was inadequately capitalized; that PDR failed to abide by the formalities of corporate existence; that Caswell used PDR assets as her own; and that recognizing the separate existence of PDR would promote injustice. These allegations mirror those held to pass muster in First Western Bank & Trust Co. v. Bookasta (1968) 267 Cal.App.2d 910, 915–916, 73 Cal.Rptr. 657. As in First Western, “[a]ssuming these facts can be proved, [Caswell] ... may be held liable ... under the alter ego principle.” (Id. at p. 916, 73 Cal.Rptr. 657.) 

 

Defendants argue that Rutherford failed to allege specific facts to support an alter ego theory, but Rutherford was required to allege only “ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550, 67 Cal.Rptr.3d 330, 169 P.3d 559.) Moreover, the “less particularity [of pleading] is required where the defendant may be assumed to possess knowledge of the facts at least equal, if not superior, to that possessed by the plaintiff,” which certainly is the case here. (Burks v. Poppy Construction Co. (1962) 57 Cal.2d 463, 474, 20 Cal.Rptr. 609, 370 P.2d 313.) Therefore, we affirm the trial court's ruling that Rutherford sufficiently pled an alter ego theory of liability. 

 

Here, the FAC alleges the “Defendants KopenTech, LLC and KopenTech Capital Markets, LLC are owned and controlled by Defendant Olga Chernova and//or Kenneth King. Both entity defendants were incorporated on the same day. The same Board of Directors oversees both companies. Agreements signed by Plaintiff were applicable to both companies. Plaintiff was responsible for operations of both of companies and also three other related companies.” (FAC, ¶ 5.) The FAC further alleges “Defendants KopenTech, LLC and KopenTech Capital Markets, LLC, are collectively referred to herein as "KopenTech." KopenTech is an integrated enterprise that have common management, interrelation between operations, centralized control of labor relations, and common ownership.” (FAC, ¶ 4.) The FAC also alleges “Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereon alleges that, at all relevant times mentioned herein, Defendants, and each of them, were the agents, servants, employees, partners, joint-venturers, joint-employers or co-conspirators of each other defendant, and that each defendant was acting within the course, scope and authority of such agency, employment, partnership, joint venture or conspiracy, and that each defendant, directly or indirectly, authorized, ratified and approved the acts of the remaining defendants, and each of them.”  (FAC, ¶ 9.)

 

The Court finds these allegations insufficient to plead alter ego liability as to KCM. Plaintiff fails to plead any facts that could support an alter legal liability theory, such as KCM and KopenTech, LLC having any unity of interest and ownership or that KCM was a mere shell and conduit for KopenTech LLC's affairs.

 

Plaintiff argues in opposition that he has alleged KCM is a joint employer. 

 

To determine whether an organization is a joint employer, courts look to the facts surrounding the employment relationship. (Vernon v. State of California (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 114, 124-125). Factors considered include payment of salary to the employee, the location where the employee performed the work, the employer’s authority to hire and discharge the employee, and the employer’s overall control over the means and manner of the employee’s performance. (Id. at p. 125.) Further, “a person can be a joint employer without exercising direct control over the employee. If the putative joint employer instead exercises enough control over the intermediary entity to indirectly dictate the wages, hours, or working conditions of the employee, that is a sufficient showing of joint employment.” (Medina v. Equilon Enterprises, LLC (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 868, 879).

 

However, the Court finds the FAC’s allegations are not adequate to support joint employer liability. The FAC states in a conclusory manner that “Defendants, and each of them, were the agents, servants, employees, partners, joint-venturers, joint-employers or co-conspirators of each other defendant.” (FAC, ¶ 9.) However, there are no facts alleged as to KCM’s employment relationship with Plaintiff, such as KCM’s control over any aspects of the employment relationship, including control over wages, hours, or working conditions. Therefore, Plaintiff fails to plead liability on behalf of KCM.

 

Thus, the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against KCM. As such, the Demurrer to the FAC against Defendant KCM is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. 

 

D.     Third and Fourth Causes of Action: Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation

 

Defendants demur to the third and fourth causes of action for intentional misrepresentation and negligent misrepresentation arguing that Plaintiff has failed to allege Defendants made a misrepresentation as to a past or existing fact.

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation include “[m]isrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, and with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented; ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation by the party to whom it was directed; and resulting damage.”  (Hydro-Mill Co., Inc. v. Hayward, Tilton & Rolapp Ins. Associates, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1154, cleaned up.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiff must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

“California courts have recognized a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, i.e., a duty to communicate accurate information, in two circumstances.  The first situation arises where providing false information poses a risk of and results in physical harm to person or property.  The second situation arises where information is conveyed in a commercial setting for a business purpose.”  (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 477.)

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges “On or about December 2022, Chernova interviewed Kuntsevich for the Chief Financial Officer / Chief of Staff role at KopenTech. At the interview, Kuntsevich orally proposed a compensation package of a $200,000 annual salary base plus a 2 percent equity stake.” (FAC, ¶ 12.) The FAC further alleges “Prior to accepting and beginning on January 11, 2023, Chernova represented, confirming in writing Kuntsevich would receive the equity award in July 2023.” (FAC, ¶ 13.) The FAC also alleges “In February 2023, [Kuntsevich] resigned but was persuaded by Chernova, when she returned, to remain employed. Chernova reiterated Kuntsevich would receive the equity award in July 2023.” (FAC, ¶ 16.) The FAC alleges “Kuntsevich continued to rely on Chernova’s representations he would receive a 2 percent equity stake.” (FAC, ¶ 17.)

 

Plaintiff argues that the proposed 2 percent equity stake was a promise and a promise can support a claim for misrepresentation. “A promise to do something necessarily implies the intention to perform; hence, where a promise is made without such intention, there is an implied misrepresentation of fact that may be actionable fraud. [Citations.]” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638, (Lazar).) “A representation as to the future is a promise, unless it is merely a statement of a belief or an expectation.” (Cal. Ins. Code § 353)

 

However, the FAC fails to specify whether Plaintiff was promised a 2 percent equity stake in KCM or Kopentech LLC. The FAC alleges Chernova was Kopentech LLC’s Chief Executive Officer. (FAC, ¶ 11.) If the 2 percent equity stake was in KCM, the FAC is ambiguous as to Chernova’s authority to offer Plaintiff 2 percent equity in KCM as the FAC only states KCM was owned and controlled by Chernova “and/or Kenneth King.” (FAC, ¶ 5.) Therefore, Plaintiff has not alleged the misrepresentation with the requisite specificity.

 

Thus, the Demurrer to the FAC as to the third and fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. 

 

E.      Uncertainty

 

Defendants also demur to the entire Complaint on the basis of uncertainty.

 

CCP section 430.10(f) provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous and unintelligible. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.” (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.) “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)

 

Here, the Court does not find the rest of the Complaint is so uncertain that it is ambiguous and unintelligible. The Court overrules the demurrer to the entire Complaint on this basis.

 

F.       Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendants request the Court to strike all allegations of punitive damages as follows:

1.      FAC, paragraph 28, p. 5 lines 11-12;

2.      FAC, paragraph 32, p. 5 lines 24-25;

3.      FAC, paragraph 37, p. 6 lines 13-14; and

4.      FAC’s Prayer for Relief, paragraph 2, p. 7 line 4.

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b), an entity employer cannot be liable for punitive damages based on the acts of its employee unless an officer, director or managing agent of the corporation “was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,” or “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety or others or authorized or ratified the [unfit employee’s] wrongful conduct.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294(b); see also Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721, 726.) A managing agent is not merely a supervisory employee, but rather is limited to those “who exercise[] substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal. 4th 563, 577.) Corporate policies are “the general principles which guide a corporation, or rules intended to be followed consistently over time in corporate operations” and a managing agent is “one with substantial authority over decisions that set these general principles and rules.” (Cruz v. Homebase (2000) 83 Cal. App. 4th 160, 167-68.)

 

Plaintiff argues Defendants acted with malice and oppression by terminating Plaintiff in retaliation for complaining of illegal conduct, including, tax fraud, false statements on PPP loan application, and misrepresentation of corporate finances.

 

Reading the allegations in the Complaint as a whole, the Court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged deliberate intent on the part of Defendants to retaliate against Plaintiff for complaining of illegal conduct. The court in G.D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 27–32, it was pointed out that there exists an uncertainty in the case law as to just what terms adequately describe the necessary elements of “oppression, fraud or malice” under Civil Code section 3294. Searle suggests that different types of injurious conduct allow different formulations in pleading oppression or malice, but that the critical element is an “evil motive” of the defendant. (Id., at pp. 29–31.) Here, Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient to warrant punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s causes of action against Defendant Kopentech Capital Markets, LLC is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend. 

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s first and second causes of action is OVERRULED. Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED.

BORSCA v. O’GARA COACH COMPANY, LLC (24STCV1998)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Michael Geller (Law Office of Michael Geller Inc.)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Elizabeth Kolar (Kolar & Associates)

 

demurrer to complaint (filed on 10/02/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 28, 2024, Plaintiff Vendel Borsca filed this action against Defendant O’Gara Coach.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Unfair Business Practices

2.      Violation of Consumers Legal Remedies Act

3.      Negligent Misrepresentation

4.      Intentional Misrepresentation

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or around September 2, 2023, Plaintiff went to Defendant, O’Gara Coach Company, LLC, to purchase a new 2023 Rolls-Royce Cullinan Black Badge VIN SLATV8C01PU220220. The purchase price of the vehicle was $515,000 and Plaintiff traded in several vehicles as part of the down payment. Plaintiff alleges that the salesperson and other members of the sales staff told Plaintiff that if he formed a Montana LLC and had the vehicle "sold" to the LLC instead of him personally and have it registered in Montana instead of California, then Plaintiff could avoid sales tax and license fees. Thus, Plaintiff alleges that he formed “Vendy’s Memories LLC”, a Montana LLC. The vehicle was sold to the Montana LLC and the vehicle registered in Montana. Plaintiff alleges that he was subsequently contacted by agents for the DMV and was told that he must have his car registered in California and that he needed to process the transfer, pay the full amount of use-tax on the purchase and all registration fees and penalties.

 

On October 2, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer to the Complaint.

·       Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s entire complaint due to a misjoinder of parties

o   The subject vehicle was purchased by third party Vendy’s Memories, LLC (“VML”).

o   Borsca’s Complaint is fatally defective because he failed to join the primary Plaintiff in this matter, VML, who is the only purchaser on the Retail Installment Sales Contract (“RISC”).

·       Plaintiff’s causes of action also fail because: (1) Borsca has obliquely admitted that VML is the purchaser not him (Complaint, ¶¶ 5, 10, 11; supported by Abramson Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. 1); (2) Borsca / VML actually relied on the advice of Montana counsel, not O’GARA (Complaint, ¶ 10); (3) there was no coercion here and Borsca / VML could have walked away or changed the terms of the deal at any time; (4) neither Borsca or VML are a “Consumer” under the CLRA (Complaint, ¶¶ 5, 10, 11); and (5) Borsca and VML cannot use the CLRA given that VML purchased the Vehicle for the business purpose of being a tax shelter (Complaint, ¶¶ 7-9, 20). As such the laws and contract relevant here entirely exclude this situation.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition to Defendant’s demurrer arguing: 

·       Adding VML as a party would add nothing to the Complaint. The RISC is not at issue. An LLC that is simply the alter-ego of the buyer is not needed and the car is no longer registered in the name of the LLC so it has nothing to do with the transaction other than that is how it started.

·       Plaintiff has pled that he purchased the car for personal, family or household purposes. He still is a consumer regardless of the alter-ego LLC formed at the behest of O'Gara.

·       Plaintiff alleges that Defendant illegally convinced him to form an LLC in Montana.

 

Defendant filed a reply:

·       Defendant maintains that the Complaint is fatally defective because it fails to join VML, the actual purchaser of the vehicle at issue, as a plaintiff.

·       The RISC for the Vehicle is the very contract Plaintiff is trying to void in this lawsuit. Yet he failed to include it.

·       The Complaint fails to allege Plaintiff was harmed or has damages, as Plaintiff was not involved with the purchase.

·       Defendant opposes Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice. Borsca has done nothing to authenticate this document as an official act or final regulation. Instead this is merely a Department’s form which is not required to go under the scrutiny and finalization process of a Legislative Act or a full Regulation.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

B.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of a California Department of Tax and Fee Administration form under Evid. Code § 452(b).

 

The court may take judicial notice of regulations and legislative enactments issued by or under the authority of the United States or any public entity in the United States. (Evid. Code § 452(b).)

 

Here, the form does not appear to be a regulation or legislative enactment under Evid. Code § 452(b). Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.   

 

C.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating the parties telephonically met and conferred regarding the issues in the demurrer. (Abramson Decl., ¶¶ 4-7.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its obligations to meet and confer and the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

D.     Misjoinder

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s entire Complaint on the grounds of misjoinder of parties.

 

CCP section 430.10(d) provides that a demurrer may be brought on grounds of misjoinder of parties. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(d).) But “a defendant may not make allegations of defect or misjoinder of parties in the demurrer if the pleadings do not disclose the existence of the matter relied on; such objection must be taken by plea or answer.” (Harboring Villas Homeowners Association v. Superior Court (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 426, 429.)

 

Defendant argues the Complaint is fatally defective because it fails to join Vendy’s Memories, LLC (“VML”), the actual purchaser of the vehicle at issue, and thus, a necessary party, as a plaintiff in this matter. In support, Defendant attached a copy of the Retail Installment Sales Contract (“RISC”). (Demurrer, Ex. 1.) The RISC states that the buyer is VML. (Demurrer, Ex. 1, p. 1.) Thus, Defendant argues that VML is the actual real party in interest, and not Plaintiff Borsca. Defendant argues that Plaintiff Borsca has no standing and was not involved in the purchase. “Every action must be prosecuted in the name of the real party in interest, except as otherwise provided by statute.” (CCP § 367; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 1004.)

 

In Plaintiff’s opposition, he does not dispute that the vehicle was sold to VML. (Oppos., p. 2.) However, Plaintiff argues that VML is an LLC that is simply the alter-ego of the Plaintiff. The Complaint alleges “[Plaintiff] formed [VML] through the O'Gara attorney in Montana, solely for the purpose of registering the Rolls-Royce there.” (Compl., ¶ 9.) The Complaint also alleges Plaintiff relied on Defendant’s representations that he could avoid sales tax and license fees on the purchase of the vehicle if it was sold to a Montana LLC that he formed, and not sold to Plaintiff personally. (Compl., ¶ 7.) The Complaint further alleges that after Plaintiff was contacted by DMV agents, he had to re-register “the car properly and had to pay the State of California $54,652 due to the intentional and negligent acts of O'Gara.” (Compl., ¶ 20.) Thus, the Complaint alleges Plaintiff’s involvement in the purchase and his alleged damages. The Court finds these allegations are sufficient to show Plaintiff’s standing as a real party in interest. Defendant does not cite to any authority showing that Plaintiff is required to also join the LLC as a plaintiff.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that the demurrer to the entire Complaint should be sustained on these grounds. Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the entire Complaint due to misjoinder is overruled on this basis.

 

E.      Second Cause of Action: Consumer Legal Remedies Act

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s second cause of action fails because the Consumers Legal Remedies Act (“CLRA”) is limited to consumers.

 

Under Civil Code § 1750, to establish a claim under the CLRA the Plaintiff must allege that (1) the Plaintiff acquired, or sought to acquire, by purchase or lease, a vehicle for personal, family, or household purposes; (2) that the Defendant engaged in one or more prohibited practices under Civ. Code §1770(a), or represented that the product or service had characteristics or uses, or benefits that it did not have; (3) That the Plaintiff was harmed; and (4) That the Plaintiff's harm resulted from the Defendant's conduct. (See Consumers Legal Remedies Act; Rubenstein v. The Gap, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.4th 870, 880-881).  

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff is a consumer because despite following Defendant’s illegal advice, the vehicle was in fact purchased for personal, family or household purposes. (Compl., ¶ 22.) While Defendant argues that VML was the purchaser of the vehicle, and that the RISC lists the primary use of the vehicle as business or commercial, Plaintiff alleges that he had relied on Defendant’s advice regarding the purchase and registration of the vehicle. The Court finds that at this stage, Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient. It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) 

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

F.       Third and Fourth Causes of Action: Negligent and Intentional Misrepresentation

 

Defendant demurs to the third and fourth causes of action.

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation include “[m]isrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, and with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented; ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation by the party to whom it was directed; and resulting damage.”  (Hydro-Mill Co., Inc. v. Hayward, Tilton & Rolapp Ins. Associates, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1154, cleaned up.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiff must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

“California courts have recognized a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, i.e., a duty to communicate accurate information, in two circumstances.  The first situation arises where providing false information poses a risk of and results in physical harm to person or property.  The second situation arises where information is conveyed in a commercial setting for a business purpose.”  (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 477.)

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff Borsca was not involved in the transaction and does not have standing. However, as discussed above, the Court does not find Defendant’s argument persuasive. Defendant also argues that Plaintiff has not alleged reliance. Defendant argues that he was guided by the knowledge and advice of a Montana attorney in establishing VML, and not on the advice of Defendant.

 

The Complaint alleges “Using the attorney in Montana facilitated by O'Gara, Borsca formed “Vendy's Memories LLC" though the O'Gara attorney in Montana, solely for the purpose of registering the Rolls-Royce there.” (Compl., ¶ 10.)

 

Thus, the Complaint alleges that Plaintiff used the attorney in Montana that was facilitated by Defendant O’Gara to form VML. However, the Complaint does not allege that Plaintiff relied on the attorney’s representations. Instead, the Complaint alleges that Plaintiff relied on Defendant’s representations. The Complaint alleges the following:

 

“Defendants misrepresented the fact that Plaintiff could legally avoid paying California sales tax and California registration by selling the car to a Montana entity. Given the licensing requirements as a new-car dealer, along with the numerous sales tax training guides and training, Defendants had no reasonable ground for believing that by simply registering a car in Montana, a California resident and driver, can avoid California registration fees and California Sales Tax. Defendants had a full system in place, including a Montana lawyer on speed-dial, that was intended to induce buyers to believe this misrepresentation and Plaintiff reasonably relied on the statements and representations of the staff of the Rolls Royce dealer that sells many very high-end, expensive cars. Plaintiff relied on these statements and but-for the reduction in the sales price, he would not have purchased the car.” (Compl., ¶¶ 36-39.)

 

The Court finds these allegations sufficient at this stage. Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

G.     First Cause of Action: Unfair Business Practices

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action arguing that it is derivative of the other causes of action and thus, should also fail.

 

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.)  A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.”  (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

 

As discussed above, Defendant argues that Plaintiff Borsca was not involved in the transaction and does not have standing. However, the Court did not find Defendant’s argument persuasive. Further, this cause of action is not derivative of the other causes of action because Plaintiff also alleges Defendant violated the Automobile Sales Finance Act by failing to properly disclose the full terms of the deal. (Compl., ¶ 19.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.
