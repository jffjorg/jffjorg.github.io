DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV12444    Hearing Date: June 11, 2024    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 
County of Los Angeles 
Department 78 
 
ENCINO NEWCASTLE HOMEOWNERS ASSOCIATION,
Plaintiff, 
vs. 
DEL SOL PROPERTY MANAGEMENT, INC., et al.,
Defendants. Case No.: 21STCV12444
Hearing Date: June 11, 2024 
 
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:  
MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS FILED BY CROSS-DEFENDANT ENCINO NEWCASTLE HOMEOWNERS ASSOCIATION, KOUROSH YAGHOOBIAN, GIV BASTANIELAHABADI, AND ALLEN TABIBI

Cross-Defendants’ motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED. 
Cross-Defendants’ request for evidentiary sanctions is DENIED.
Cross-Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED as set forth below.
Moving party to provide notice.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND  
This is an action for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, breach of fiduciary duty, an account, conversion and theft, and fraud. Plaintiff Encino Newcastle Homeowner’s Association (“Encino Newcastle”) hired Defendant Del Sol Property Management (“Del Sol”) as its property manager in January 2010. Del Sol is owned and operated by Defendant Sinitsin. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants used their position to siphon money from Plaintiff, convert its property, enter improper contracts and agreements, and have refused to return Plaintiff’s books and records. Plaintiff also seeks specific performance and declaratory relief. Defendant Del Sol in turn brought a cross-complaint against the moving party and others.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On April 1, 2021, Plaintiff filed its Complaint against Del Sol and Sinitsin.
On April 27, 2022, Defendants Answered and filed a Cross-Complaint against Encino Newcastle, Kourosh Yaghoobian, Giv Bastanielahabadi, and Allen Tabibi.
On June 13, 2022, Cross-Defendants dismissed their cause of action for defamation.
On September 28, 2022, Cross-Complainants filed their First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”).
On December 20, 202, December 26, 2023, December 27, 203, and January 4, 2024, the Court granted motions to compel Del Sol’s further responses to requests for production (RPDs) and special interrogatories (SROGs).
On April 17, 2024, Cross-Defendants Encino Newcastle, Kourosh Yaghoobian, and Giv Bastanielahabadi filed this motion for terminating sanctions.
On May 29, 2024, Del Sol filed an opposition and dismissed Giv Bastanielahabadi as a Cross-Defendant.
DISCUSSION 
Cross-Defendants move for terminating sanctions against Del Sol on the grounds that Del Sol failed to comply with the Court’s prior discovery orders. In the alternative, Cross-Defendants seek evidentiary sanctions. Cross-Defendants also seek monetary sanctions.
“If a party…fails to obey an order compelling further response to interrogatories, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300(e).) In lieu of or in addition to these sanctions, the court may impose a monetary sanction. (Id.)
“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390, quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal. App. 4th 1225, 1246.)  
“Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal. App. 4th at p. 390 [citation omitted].)   
“Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 citing Lang, supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1244-1246 [discussing cases]; see, e.g., Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1617-1622 [terminating sanctions imposed (by striking the defendant’s Answer and subsequently granting default judgment) after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery]; Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 481, 491, disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCutchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, n. 4 [terminating sanctions imposed against the plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes].) 
An evidence sanction prohibits a party from introducing designated matters in evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030(c).)
“Discovery sanctions must be tailored in order to remedy the offending party's discovery abuse, should not give the aggrieved party more than what it is entitled to, and should not be used to punish the offending party.” (Karlsson v. Ford Motor Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1217.)¿ “Although the court has discretion in choosing a sanction, this discretion must be exercised in a manner consistent with the basic purposes of such sanctions, e.g., to compel disclosure of discoverable information.”  (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1193 (citation omitted).)  “Furthermore, the sanction chosen should not provide a windfall to the other party, by putting the prevailing party in a better position than if he or she had obtained the discovery sought and it had been favorable.”  (Ibid. (citations omitted).) 
Here, Cross-Defendant’s counsel testifies that he originally propounded SROGs and RPDs on Del Sol in December 2022. (Erakat Decl., ¶2.) After the Court granted Cross-Defendants’ motions to compel Del Sol’s further responses to these discovery requests, Del Sol served supplemental responses to RPDs and requested additional time to serve supplemental responses to SROGs. (Id., ¶4.) As of the date this motion was filed, Cross-Defendants have not served supplemental responses to SROGs. (Id., ¶5.) Del Sol’s counsel also had not discussed sanctions with Del Sol’s manager, stating sanctions should instead be included in the final judgment in this matter. (Id., ¶6.) 
The Court’s orders granting Cross-Defendant’s motions for further responses to SROGs and RPDs required Del Sol to serve further responses and pay sanctions within 20 days after the date of the order. The sanctions imposed against Del Sol were $1,720, $860, $860, $860, and $860.
In opposition, Del Sol’s counsel, Ilya Alekseyeff, argues extensively that he discovered Cross-Defendants Yaghoobian and Tabibi stole from the Newcastle Homeowners Association. However, this discovery is not relevant to this motion, nor does it justify Del Sol’s failure to comply with the Court’s orders issued in December 2023 and January 2024. Alekseyeff withheld the supplemental responses to SROGs because he had not received compliance with his subpoenas and believes sanctions are not warranted because Cross-Defendants suffered no prejudice by the untimely responses. (Opp. pp.9-10.) Del Sol served supplemental responses to SROGs on May 27, 2024, about three months after the deadline set in the Court’s orders. (Opp., Exh. AA.) Alekseyeff represents that he “decided to complete…[his] independent investigation…and then provide a comprehensive response,” meaning he deliberately served late supplemental responses in violation of the Court’s orders. (Alekseyeff Decl., ¶68.) With respect to the delinquent sanctions, Alekseyeff believes that it makes “more sense to wait until the verdict in the case to resolve any financial disputes between them” and that Cross-Defendants should pursue payment of sanctions through execution. (Opp. p.4.) 
The Court finds that terminating and evidence sanctions are not appropriate here because Del Sol has served the missing supplemental responses to SROGs, though late. However, Alekseyeff has engaged in discovery abuse by deliberately delaying the supplemental responses and refusing to pay sanctions as ordered. The Court’s December 2023 and January 2024 orders were not ambiguous or up for interpretation. The responses and sanctions were due 20 days after those orders were issued. Alekseyeff had no reasonable basis for delaying the response and refusing to pay sanctions. 
The Court awards Cross-Defendants additional sanctions of $2,760 for 6.9 hours of attorney time at a rate of $400 per hour and filing fees. These sanctions are imposed jointly and severally against Del Sol and Alekseyeff. These sanctions are to be paid within five days after the date of this order. 
As for the outstanding sanctions, the orders imposing sanctions have the force and effect of money judgments which are immediately enforceable through a writ of execution. (Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615.)

MOTION TO VACATE DISMISSAL FILED BY PLAINTIFF JOHN BROSNAN
 

The motion to vacate dismissal filed by Plaintiff John Brosnan DENIED.
The Court notes that no proof of service was filed with the motion.
Moving party to provide notice.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND  
This is an action for conversion, fraud, and tort of another. Plaintiff John Brosnan alleges that he rented a commercial space in Los Angeles which was part of a larger property with multiple units. In May 2019, Plaintiff parked his trailer at the property. Plaintiff’s neighbor, Salima Surani, entered his trailer and caused it to be moved to an adjacent parking lot owned by a different entity. The trailer was towed and its contents emptied. 
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On November 17, 2022, Plaintiff John Brosnan filed his Complaint against Defendants RHS Surani Inc., dba Popsicle Studio LA, and Salima Surani.
On May 18, 2023, the Court granted Surani’s Anti-SLAPP motion to strike as to the second cause of action, sustained Surani’s demurrer to the first cause of action with leave to amend, and sustained Surani’s demurrer to the third cause of action without leave to amend.
On August 9, 2023, the Court granted Defendants’ ex parte application to dismiss following Plaintiff’s failure to amend.
On August 9, 2023, the Court dismissed this action with prejudice.
On October 20, 2023, the Court entered judgment in favor of Defendants.
On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to vacate order of dismissal.
DISCUSSION 
Plaintiff moves to vacate dismissal on the grounds that the case was dismissed due to his counsel’s failure to properly respond to Defendants’ motions.
Per Code of Civil Procedure, section 473, subdivision (b), a court may relieve a party or his counsel from a judgment, dismissal, or order against him because of his “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” A party seeking relief must file a motion to set aside dismissal “no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. section 473) The six-month time limit is jurisdictional, and a court has no authority to excuse a party’s noncompliance with the time limit. (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 981-982; Arambula v. Union Carbide Corp. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 333, 340.)
Here, the court entered judgment in favor of Defendants on October 20, 2023. Plaintiff did not file this motion until May 3, 2024, six months and thirteen days after the Court entered judgment in favor of Defendants. The Court no longer has jurisdiction to grant relief under Code Civ. Proc., section 473. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to vacate dismissal is denied.

DEMURRER TO THE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT FILED BY DEFENDANTS THE NICE GUY, LLC AND THE HWOOD GROUP, LLC

The demurrer to the second amended complaint filed by Movants The Nice Guy, LLC and The Hwood Group, LLC. is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for negligence, negligent hiring, supervision and retention, assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligence and premises liability. Plaintiffs allege that, in the early morning hours of February 12, 2022, they were shot and injured by Doe Defendants at the “Subject Location,” which the complaint defines as “inside, at, near, or on the sidewalk, street, and/or property adjacent to near 401 North La Cienega Boulevard, Los Angeles County, California 90048.” The complaint alleges Moving Defendants owned, operated, managed, and controlled the Subject Location, including a restaurant/lounge on the property. At the time of the shooting, an “after-party” was taking place at the restaurant/lounge. The complaint alleges Moving Defendants anticipated or should have reasonably anticipated that criminal conduct, including gun violence, would take place during the after-party. 
PROCEDURAL HISTORY
On February 10, 2023, Plaintiffs Mark Schaefer and Adam Rahman filed this action against Defendants The Nice Guy, LLC, The Hwood Group, LLC, Justin Bieber, Bill K. Kapri aka Kodak Black aka Dieuson Octave, Revolve Group, Inc., Leslie A. Glick, Leslie Glick 2021 Irrevocable Gift Trust, City of Los Angeles, City of West Hollywood, County of Los Angeles, and Does 1-20
On July 31, 2023, the Court sustained a demurrer filed by Defendants The Nice Guy, LLC and The Hwood Group, LLC with leave to amend.
On September 1, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint (FAC).
On November 2, 2023, Plaintiffs dismissed Defendant County of Los Angeles from this action.
On December 19, 2023, Plaintiffs dismissed the City of West Hollywood from this action.
On March 13 and 27, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed Defendants Leslie Glick and Revolve Group, Inc. from this action.
On April 22, 2024, the Court sustained demurrers filed by Defendants Justin Bieber, The Nice Guy, LLC, and The Hwood Group, LLC with leave to amend.
On May 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint. 
On May 13, 2024, Defendants The Nice Guy, LLC and The Hwood Group, LLC (Movants) filed a third demurrer.
On May 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition.
On June 4, 2024, Movants filed a reply.

DISCUSSION
Movants demur to Plaintiffs’ SAC on the grounds that the SAC fails to state facts sufficient to support causes of action for negligence, negligent hiring, and public nuisance against Movants. Plaintiffs only allege three causes of action against Movants.
A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 (internal citations omitted).)
a. Meet and Confer
A party filing a demurrer “shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a).) “The parties shall meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. If the parties are not able to meet and confer at least five days prior to the date the responsive pleading is due, the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension of time within which to file a responsive pleading, by filing and serving, on or before the date on which a demurrer would be due, a declaration stating under penalty of perjury that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made and explaining the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a)(2).) A failure to meet and confer does not constitute grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., sections 430.41 (a)(4).)
Here, Movants’ counsel testifies that he met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel via telephone on May 8, 2024 and the parties could not resolve their dispute over the FAC. (Real Decl., ¶5.) Movants satisfy meet and confer requirements.
b. Discussion
i. First Cause of Action -- Negligence
Movants first demur to the cause of action for negligence on the grounds that the SAC fails to state facts sufficient to support a cause of action for negligence.
“The elements of a cause of action for negligence are well established. They are (a) a legal duty to use due care; (b) a breach of such legal duty; [and] (c) the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917, internal quotations omitted.) “The first element, duty, may be imposed by law, be assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship.” (Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1128, internal quotations omitted.) “[T]he existence of a duty is a question of law for the court.” (Ky. Fried Chicken of Cal. v. Superior Court (1997) 14 Cal.4th 814, 819.)
“[W]hile negligence is ordinarily a question of fact, the existence of a duty is generally a question of law that may be addressed by demurrer. (Paul v. Patton (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1096.) Whether one owes a duty to another must be decided on a case-by-case basis. (Weirum v. RKO General, Inc. (1975) 15 Cal.3d 40, 46.) Every case is governed by the rule of general application that all persons are required to use ordinary care to prevent others from being harmed as a result of their conduct. (Id.)
Civ. Code, section 1714(a) provides that everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.
Here, as before, the SAC alleges that on February 12, 2022, Plaintiffs were severely injured during a violent incident at a restaurant, “The Nice Guy,” in Los Angeles, California, where Defendant Bieber was hosting an after party. (SAC ¶¶19, 24.) Plaintiff Rahman was a patron of the restaurant and exited shortly before gunfire began. (SAC ¶25a.) Plaintiff Schaefer was an onlooker at the event collecting autographs on the property. (SAC ¶25b.) Plaintiffs were both shot by Does 3 through 20. (SAC ¶24.) The restaurant is owned and operated by Movants and other entities. (SAC ¶21.) Defendants’ promotion and marketing of the event attracted more people than the venue’s capacity, causing people to occupy the sidewalk, street, and properties adjacent to the restaurant. (Id.) 
Defendants anticipated or should have anticipated that criminal conduct, including gun violence, in the area would take place during the event. (SAC ¶22.) Defendants should have known that Defendant Kodak Black was involved in an altercation during the event. (Id.) Defendants knew or should have known that Defendant Kodak Black was involved in an altercation during the event that further increased the risk and foreseeability of violent conduct. (Id.) 
Plaintiffs now also allege that The Hwood Group, LLC previously operated another venue which shut down after neighbors made complaints about the noise and patrons, causing the venue to be declared a public nuisance. (SAC ¶22a.) There were prior incidents of violence on and around the subject property, including that in April 2015, four patrons leaving the premises beat a photographer. (SAC ¶22b.) There were other violent incidents at other locations operated by The Hwood Group, LLC in 2016 and 2018. (SAC ¶22c.) Defendants knew Kodak Black would be attending the event and knew he and his entourage to cause gun violence, be a draw for gun violence, and had a history of gun violence. (SAC ¶22d.) Defendants’ employees had engaged in or exacerbated violence at past events. (SAC ¶22e.) Before the incident, Defendants became aware of a violent altercation involving Black at the event which worsened over the course of the night. (SAC ¶22f.) Each Defendant caused or contributed to severe overcrowding and over-serving of action to individuals with a known history of gun violence. (SAC ¶22g.)
Although Defendants undertook to provide security for the event, Defendants failed to exercise reasonable care in the rendering of these services, instigated violence, escalated the violence of others, and failed to use reasonable care rendering security services. (SAC ¶23.) Security personnel were to perform duties, including maintaining crowd control, discouraging altercations, providing security and protection to guests, employees, and property, and restraining and/or ejecting intoxicated, disorderly, or insubordinate guests. (SAC ¶23b.) Defendants’ security personnel used excessive force, exacerbating the violence, and failed to deescalate the conflict. (Id.) The violence moved from within the restaurant to a broader area around the restaurant. (SAC ¶24.) Defendants entered into contracts with each other to provide security for the protection of patrons, onlookers, and passersby, including Plaintiffs, during the event. (SAC ¶30b.)
Foreseeability
Movants first argue that the SAC fails to state facts showing they owed a duty to protect the public at large for a third party’s conduct occurring off the premises on nearby land. 
Businesses such as shopping centers, restaurants, and bars have an affirmative duty to take reasonable steps to secure their premises, as well as adjacent common areas within their control, against reasonably foreseeable criminal acts of third parties. (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 249. “A proprietor’s general duty of maintenance, which is owed to tenants and patrons, includes the duty to take reasonable steps to secure common areas against foreseeable criminal acts of third parties that are likely to occur in the absence of such precautionary measures.” (Id. at p. 236.) “Duty in such circumstances is determined by a balancing of foreseeability of the criminal acts against the burdensomeness, vagueness, and efficacy of the proposed security measures.” (Id. at p. 238.) Due to the high social costs of imposing a duty on landowners to hire private police forces, “a high degree of foreseeability is required in order to find that the scope of a landlord’s duty of care includes the hiring of security guards.” (Ibid.) This “requisite degree of foreseeability rarely, if ever, can be proven in the absence of prior similar incidents of violent crime on the landowner’s premises.” (Ibid; see also Wiener v. Southcoast Childcare Centers, Inc. (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1138, 1149-1150 [finding a heightened standard necessary due to the difficulty or impossibility of predicting when a criminal will strike, and the difficulty of removing all means available to a criminal intent on a particular victim or goal].) 
Where the burden of preventing future harm caused by third party criminal conduct is great or onerous, heightened foreseeability shown by prior similar incidents or other indications of reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults in that location will be required. (Tan v. Arnel Management Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 1087, 1097.)
Here, Plaintiffs are essentially arguing that Defendants had a duty to provide security services to protect patrons, onlookers, staff, and the public both inside and in the area surrounding the subject property. To impose such a duty would be particularly onerous due to the cost of providing security both in and outside the restaurant. Thus, Plaintiffs must make a showing of heightened foreseeability that the subject shooting would take place.
As before, the facts are insufficient to show that the subject incident was foreseeable. First, the incident resulting in Plaintiffs’ injuries was a shooting. Although Plaintiffs now allege there were other similar incidents at the subject property, the incident described in the SAC was a beating, which is not similar to the subject shooting. Additionally, the incident described took place in 2015, seven years before the subject incident. The fact that a beating took place seven years ago at this location is insufficient to show that the subject shooting was likely to take place. Although Plaintiffs now allege that there were prior incidents at other locations operated by The Hwood Group in 2016 and 2018, LLC, these took place in other locations long before the subject incident. Thus, they are too remote to have put Movants on notice that the subject incident was likely to take place. 
The facts regarding overcrowding remain largely unchanged in the SAC. As before, even if Defendants marketed the event in a manner which invited more guests to attend than the venue could hold, it would not be reasonable to infer that the shooting was foreseeable due to overcrowding alone. Although other injuries may be foreseeable due to overcrowding, the facts are insufficient to show how a shooting was a foreseeable danger of the overcrowding. Even with allegations that the crowd was over-served alcohol and that attendees had a known history of gun violence, there are no allegations of past incidents involving shootings or that Movants knew any attendees were armed. 
The SAC now alleges that Movants’ employees used excessive force while performing duties within the scope of their employment, that the violence was instigated by the employees, and the employees had instigated violence in the past. However, there are no facts that the employees’ use of excessive force had resulted in similar shootings in the past. Thus, the facts do not support the assertion that the shooting was foreseeable. 
The SAC now alleges that Defendants knew that Kodiak Black and his entourage had a history of gun violence, were known to commit gun violence, and would be a draw for gun violence. However, even if Kodiak Black and his entourage had a propensity to commit gun violence, Black’s presence at the event alone is insufficient to show that the subject incident was foreseeable. There are no facts which would show Defendants knew Black and his entourage would engage in gun violence during the event. Even if Defendants knew of an altercation during the event involving Defendant Kodiak Black, there are no facts showing how the altercation is related to the alleged shooting. 
The facts alleged in the SAC are insufficient to show that the alleged shooting was foreseeable, and that Movants had a duty to protect Plaintiffs from the criminal acts of third parties outside the restaurant. Given the heightened foreseeability requirement, the facts alleged are insufficient to show the shooting was foreseeable such that Movants had a legal duty to implement security measures to prevent a shooting from breaking out and injuring onlookers and patrons such as Plaintiffs outside of the restaurant.
Special Relationship
Movants argue that there was no special relationship which imposed a duty on Movants to protect Plaintiffs. The parties dispute whether Movants had a special relationship with Plaintiffs by contract or due to Plaintiffs’ status as invitees.
A special relationship may arise out of a voluntary assumption of a duty upon which a person reasonably relies. (Seo v. All-Makes Overhead Doors (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1203.) If a special relationship arises out of a contractual duty, the duty is owed not only to the parties to the contract but also to those persons intended to be benefited by the performance of the contract. (Id.) A special relationship arises by contract only if the contract itself imposes a duty to protect. (McHenry v. Asylum Entertainment Delaware, LLC (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 469, 485–486.) Such a special relationship requires a specific promise in the contract to provide specific protection. (Bom v. Superior Court (2020) 44 Cal.App.5th 1, 16.)
A volunteer who, having no initial duty to do so, undertakes to provide protective services to another, will be found to have a duty to exercise due care in the performance of that undertaking if either (a) the volunteer’s failure to exercise such care increases the risk of harm to the other person or (b) the other person reasonably relies on the volunteer’s undertaking and suffers injury as a result. (Delgado, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 249.) The scope of the duty depends upon the nature of the undertaking. (Id.) Merely because an enterprise chooses to have security does not mean that the enterprise assumed a duty to protect invitees from third party violence. (Id.) Even if a duty is established, foreseeability remains relevant to the factfinder’s determination of breach and causation. (Id. at p. 250.)
Here, as before, the SAC alleges that Defendants entered into contracts with each other to provide security for the protection of patrons, onlookers, and passersby. (SAC ¶30b.) Although the SAC states Defendants entered into this contract in anticipation of gun violence at the event, there are no facts showing the contract imposed a duty to protect these groups of people from gun violence. (Id.) The SAC also alleges that Defendants had a duty to provide adequate security, not to escalate violence, to de-escalate violence, and to protect Plaintiffs from the harm they suffered. (SAC ¶32(a)(iv).) However, again, this is a legal conclusion unsupported by facts. 
Again, there are no facts to show that gun violence in or around the restaurant was foreseeable. Because the SAC does not allege that there was a foreseeable risk of gun violence in and around the restaurant, there are no facts showing that Plaintiffs’ risk of harm would have increased if Movants failed to exercise due care in providing security. Although Plaintiffs allege that they relied on express and implied representations that Defendants would provide security at the venue, this statement is again conclusory and unsupported by facts. (SAC ¶30a.) The SAC alleges Rahman had exited the venue and Schaefer was collecting autographs outside the venue. (SAC ¶25.) It is not reasonable to infer from these facts alone that Plaintiffs knew that Movants had contracted with each other to provide security services and intended their security guards to protect passersby and onlookers. Again, there are no facts that Plaintiffs knew in advance about security at the venue or that they approached the venue because they knew they would be protected even if they were invitees to the event. Thus, Plaintiffs could not have reasonably relied on Movants’ undertaking to provide security at the venue. Therefore, Movants did not owe a duty of due care to provide security despite volunteering to provide private security. 
Even if Movants volunteered to provide security, foreseeability is still important to other elements of negligence, including breach and causation. As discussed above, the SAC fails to allege facts showing the alleged shooting was foreseeable to the heightened standard required here. Even if Movants had a duty to protect Plaintiffs and the security guards Movants hired were incompetent to provide security services, there are no facts that the alleged shooting was foreseeable. Because the facts fail to show the shooting was foreseeable, the SAC fails to state that Movants’ conduct caused Plaintiffs’ injuries.
In any case, the SAC again fails to state facts sufficient to establish that Movants owed Plaintiffs a duty of due care to provide security. 
Negligence per se
As with their first demurrer, Movants allege the SAC is insufficient to establish a duty of care under the doctrine of negligence per se. The facts supporting the negligence per se theory of liability remain unchanged. Because the facts remain unchanged, this theory fails for the same reasons as Movants’ earlier demurrer.
The demurrer is sustained as to the cause of action for negligence.
ii. Second Cause of Action -- Negligent hiring
Movants next argue that the facts are insufficient to support a cause of action for negligent hiring.
“California case law recognizes the theory that an employer can be liable to a third person for negligently hiring, supervising, or retaining an unfit employee…Liability is based upon the facts that the employer knew or should have known that hiring the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm materializes.” (McKenna v. Beesley (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 552.) To be liable for negligent supervision, there must be a connection between the employment and injury to a third person.  (Mendoza v. City of Los Angeles (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1341.)  “Liability for negligent hiring and supervision is based upon the reasoning that if an enterprise hires individuals with characteristics which might pose a danger to customers or other employees, the enterprise should bear the loss caused by the wrongdoing of its incompetent or unfit employees.”  (Id. at p. 1339.) Failing to require a connection between the employment and the injured party would result in the employer becoming an insurer of the safety of every person with whom the employees come into contact, regardless of their relationship to the employer (Id at p.1341.)
Here, the SAC alleges that the security personnel hired were unfit to perform security work, did not act reasonably as a security professional would, failed to refrain from violence, failed to de-escalate violence, and negligently jeopardized Plaintiffs’ safety. (SAC ¶41.) The SAC now also alleges that Movants’ employees had instigated violence at previous events. (SAC ¶22e.) However, as discussed above, there are no facts which would show Movants knew retaining their security personnel would create a particular risk of gun violence. Although the facts alleged would show retaining the personnel increased the risk of violence breaking out at the subject property, there are no facts that these past incidents involved a shooting or that the security personnel increased the risk of the alleged shooting. The SAC fails to allege other facts which would show Movants knew any security personnel created a particular risk for gun violence. Therefore, the SAC fails to state facts sufficient to support a cause of action for negligent hiring.
Plaintiffs in opposition allege that how ultimate facts might be proven are uniquely within Defendants’ knowledge and that Plaintiffs were only required to allege ultimate facts. Although Plaintiffs are correct, the ultimate facts still fail to state that Movants’ security personnel increased the particular risk that a shooting would take place. 
The demurrer is sustained on this ground.
iii. Eighth Cause of Action -- Public Nuisance
Movants demur to the cause of action for public nuisance on the grounds that the SAC fails to allege that Movants created a condition which was harmful to health, indecent and offensive, or obstructed free passage and use of public streets and sidewalks. 
“A public nuisance is one which affects at the same time an entire community or neighborhood, or any considerable number of persons, although the extent of the annoyance of damage inflicted upon individuals may be unequal.” (Civ. Code, section 3480; see Birke v. Oakwood Worldwide (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1540, 1548.) “A private person may maintain an action for a public nuisance, if it is specially injurious to himself, but not otherwise.” (Civ. Code, section 3493; see Birke, supra, 169 Cal.App.4th at 1548.) 
Here, the SAC states Civ. Code, section 3479 provides that “Anything which is injurious to health, including, but not limited to, the illegal sale of controlled substances, or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property, or unlawfully obstructs the free passage or use, in the customary manner, of any navigable lake, or river, bay, stream, canal, or basin, or any public park, square, street, or highway, is a nuisance.” (SAC ¶94.) Specifically, Defendants caused an excessive number of people who had been over-served alcohol and individuals with a known history of gun violence to impinge on the usage of the streets and sidewalks and rendered foreseeable violent acts including gun violence. (SAC ¶97a.)
The SAC now alleges that another location operated by The Hwood Group was declared a public nuisance and shut down. (SAC ¶22a.) However, allegations that a different location was a public nuisance do not show that the conditions at the subject property constituted a nuisance. 
The allegations that Defendants created a nuisance in the form of an increased risk for gun violence are conclusory. As discussed above, the SAC does not state facts sufficient to show the subject shooting was foreseeable. 
The allegations that an excessive number of people who had been over-served alcohol were obstructing use of public streets are sufficient to show Defendants created a public nuisance. However, there are no facts that the overcrowding was specially injurious to Plaintiffs. There are no allegations that the overcrowding contributed to Plaintiffs’ injuries resultant from the alleged shooting. Therefore, the SAC does not state facts sufficient to support a cause of action for nuisance.
iv. Leave to Amend
Plaintiffs seek leave to amend. However, because this is the second time Movants are demurring to the same issues, leave to amend is denied.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This motion pertains to a Cross-Complainant filed by Goodson Real Estate Co. (GRE) and HIMR Normandie JV, LLC (HIMR) against Cross-Defendants Express Remodeling & Plumbing Inc. (Express), Jacom Mullen, The North River Insurance Company (North River), Hudson Insurance Company (Hudson), and Hajoca Corporation (Hajoca). Cross-Complainants bring causes of action for breach of contract, negligence, fraud, conversion, contractors license bond, violation of Bus. & Prof. Code, section 17200, et seq., tortious interference, and an accounting. Cross-Complainants allege that in 2021, GRE entered into a subcontracting agreement with Express to do plumbing work on an property owned by HIMR in Koreatown. Jacob Mullen is the principal of Express. GRE paid Express more than $194,000 for the work. Express and Mullen failed to perform the work in a timely manner, staff the property, and supply and install lines. The work they did perform was done in a negligent manner. Express and Mullen held up construction on the property, causing Cross-Complainants to spend a significant sum to find a new subcontractor and correct the defective work. 
Hajoca furnished materials to Express and Mullen without GRE’s authorization and opened an unauthorized charge account to GRE. Although GRE demanded that the unauthorized charges be removed and charged to Express and Mullen, Hajoca issued a Preliminary Notice for $150,000 and fabricated invoices and other information to circumvent California law on Preliminary Notices.

DISCUSSION 
I. Demurrer
Hajoca demurs to the FACC on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against it and because it is uncertain. 
Hajoca is named in the following causes of action: (1) negligence (second cause of action); (2) fraud (third cause of action); (3) unfair business practices (sixth cause of action); (4) interference with contractual relations (seventh cause of action); and (5) accounting (eighth cause of action).
1. Uncertainty
Hajoca demurs to each cause of action against it on the grounds that the FACC is uncertain.
“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.” (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.) “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)  
Here, the FACC is not so bad that Hajoca cannot reasonably respond to the allegations against it because it alleges Hajoca furnished materials to Express and Mullen without GRE’s authorization and improperly charged GRE for those materials. Therefore, the facts are sufficient to apprise Hajoca of the issues it is to meet. The demurrer is overruled on this ground.
2. Second Cause of Action – Negligence
Hajoca argues that the FACC fails to state facts to support a cause of action for negligence on the grounds that (1) the FACC only alleges economic loss and (2) the FACC fails to allege that Hajoca owed Cross-Complainants a duty of care or that Hajoca’s actions caused their damages.
“The elements of a cause of action for negligence are well established. They are (a) a legal duty to use due care; (b) a breach of such legal duty; [and] (c) the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917, internal quotations omitted.) “The first element, duty, may be imposed by law, be assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship.” (Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1128, internal quotations omitted.) “[T]he existence of a duty is a question of law for the court.” (Ky. Fried Chicken of Cal. v. Superior Court (1997) 14 Cal.4th 814, 819.)
Economic Loss Rule
The economic loss rule posits that a purchaser of a product that does not live up to the buyer’s expectations can only recover in contract and not tort, “unless [the purchaser] can demonstrate harm above and beyond a broken contractual promise.” (Food Safety Net Services v. Eco Safe Systems USA, Inc. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1118, 1130 (quoting Robinson Helicopter Company, Inc. v. Dana Corporation (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 988).) A tort claim for fraud must be sufficiently independent from a breach of contract claim for which the plaintiff suffered economic loss. (Robinson Helicopter, supra, 34 Cal.4th at 991.) Thus, in order to plead around the economic loss rule, a party must plead the existence of a duty that arises independent of any contractual duty and independent injury, other than economic loss, that arises from the breach of that duty. (Robinson Helicopter, supra, 34 Cal.4th at 988-91.)  
Here, the FACC alleges that in December 2021, GRE entered into a subcontracting agreement with Express to do plumbing work on the property. (FACC ¶16.) Express did not complete the work on the property and performed the work in a negligent manner. (FACC ¶¶21-23.) 
During the project, Hajoca was furnishing materials to Express and opened an unauthorized charge account to GRE. (FACC ¶¶30-31.) Hajoca supplied these materials and billed GRE without GRE’s knowledge or authorization. (FACC ¶32.) 
The FACC alleges that GRE had a contract with Express regarding the plumbing work on the property. GRE was unaware that Hajoca was furnishing materials to Express and that Hajoca had opened a charge account to GRE. 
It is reasonable to infer from the allegations that GRE had no contract with Hajoca. Because there was no contract between GRE and Hajoca, GRE cannot recover in contract against Hajoca. Therefore, the cause of action is not barred under the contractual strand of the economic loss rule. (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 923.) 
The moving party has not convinced the Court that the economic loss rule applies here. The rule has been applied in situations where courts are “concerned about imposing liability in an indeterminate amount for an indeterminate time to an indeterminate class.” (Id. at 922.) 
The cases cited by the moving party do not appear to support the granting of a demurrer here. The FACC alleges that Hajoca purposely billed GRE without GRE’s knowledge or authorization. Under these circumstances, the Court does not find that the economic loss rule bars this cause of action. 
The demurrer is overruled on this ground.
Duty and Causation
“[W]hile negligence is ordinarily a question of fact, the existence of a duty is generally a question of law that may be addressed by demurrer. (Paul v. Patton (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1096.) Whether one owes a duty to another must be decided on a case-by-case basis. (Weirum v. RKO General, Inc. (1975) 15 Cal.3d 40, 46.) Every case is governed by the rule of general application that all persons are required to use ordinary care to prevent others from being harmed as a result of their conduct. (Id.)
Civ. Code, section 1714(a) provides that everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.
Here, the FACC alleges Hajoca breached a duty of care to Cross-Complainants by providing more than a hundred thousand dollars of materials to Express and Mullen without asking for GRE’s authorization, without ensuring the items were used on behalf of GRE, and improperly seeking to hold GRE and HIMR responsible for payment. (FACC ¶59.) In March 2023, Hajoca issued a preliminary notice in the amount of $150,000 with regard to the unauthorized materials. (FACC ¶35.) Hajoca then fabricated and doctored invoices, delivery, and shipment dates to circumvent California laws governing preliminary notices. (FACC ¶36.) 
The FACC does not allege Hajoca owed any particular duty. Therefore, the duty of ordinary care under Civ. Code, section 1714(a) applies here.
The Court cannot say as a matter of law that Hajoca did not owe a duty of care given the facts as alleged. Moreover, the Court cannot say that GRE and HIMR have not suffered damages as a matter of law since Hajoca contends that GRE and HIMR owe it money for the product it provided.
The demurrer is overruled as to this cause of action.
3. Third Cause of Action – Fraud 
Hajoca next argues that the FACC fails to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action for fraud because Cross-Complainants failed to meet the heightened pleading standard for fraud. 
A claim for fraud must plead all of the following elements: (1) misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. (Odorizzi v. Bloomfield School Dist. (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 123, 128; Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1332.) 
In California, fraud, including negligent misrepresentation, must be pled with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)  
The specificity standard is less stringent when the defendant already has “‘full information concerning the facts of the controversy.’” (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 217, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in Californians for Disability Rights v. Mervyn's, LLC (2006) 39 Cal.4th 223, 227.) Relaxation of the specificity requirement is particularly appropriate in a¿concealment¿case.  Unlike intentional misrepresentation, which requires some affirmative representation or promise, a fraudulent¿concealment¿is the absence of something, the suppression of a fact. (Civ. Code, section 1710.)  
Here, the FACC alleges that Hajoca defrauded Cross-Complainants by providing fraudulent and doctored invoices and delivery dates for the materials it allegedly supplied to Express. (FACC ¶71.) 
Although the FACC alleges Hajoca made misrepresentations about the materials provided to Express by doctoring its records and knew the records were false, there are no allegations in the FACC stating Cross-Complainants reasonably relied on these false records to their detriment. Rather, Cross-Complainants informed Hajoca that the charges were unauthorized and demanded that they be removed. (FACC ¶34.) It is reasonable to infer from this fact that Cross-Complainants were aware Hajoca’s records were doctored. Thus, Cross-Complainants could not have relied on the doctored records to their detriment. 
Because the FACC fails to plead facts to support each element of fraud, the demurrer is sustained as to this cause of action.
4. Sixth Cause of Action – Violation of Business and Professions Code, section 17200, et seq.
Hajoca demurs to the cause of action for unfair business practices on the grounds that (1) it is based on allegations that Hajoca recorded a mechanic’s lien and (2) Cross-Complainants allegations of fraud do not survive demurrer.
To successfully plead a UCL claim for unfair business practices, a plaintiff must allege facts justifying relief in the form of protecting the public from unfair business practices or deceptive advertising. (Day v. AT&T Corp. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 325, 331-332.) A plaintiff must plead and prove that the defendant engaged in a business practice that was either unlawful (i.e., is forbidden by law) or unfair (i.e., harm to victim outweighs any benefit) or fraudulent (i.e., is likely to deceive members of the public). (Albillo v. Intermodal Container Services, Inc. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 190, 206.) 
“[T]he ‘practice’ requirement envisions something more than a single transaction . . . ; it contemplates a ‘pattern of conduct’ [citation], ‘on-going . . . conduct’, ‘a pattern of behavior’ [citation], or ‘a course of conduct’ . . . .” (Hewlett v. Squaw Valley Ski Corp. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 499, 519 (quoting State of California ex rel. Van de Kamp v. Texaco, Inc. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 1147, 1169-70).)
The remedies for UCL claims brought by private individuals are limited to (1) injunctive relief to prevent practices of unfair competition and (2) restitution. (See, e.g., Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb and Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1133; Bus. & Prof. Code, section 17203.) 
Here, the FACC alleges that Hajoca allowed Express and Mullen to open a charge account to GRE without ever asking for or receiving authorization from GRE. (FACC ¶86.) Additionally, Hajoca doctored invoices and other records to collect funds from GRE for materials GRE never received. (Id.) Additionally, Hajoca filed legally unsupported liens against the subject property. (Id.) 
As discussed above, the FACC failed to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action for fraud. Therefore, the FACC does not allege Hajoca engaged in a fraudulent practice. 
Although the FACC alleges that Hajoca doctored records to circumvent California laws with regard to Preliminary Notices, the FACC does not allege Hajoca violated any laws governing preliminary notices. Thus, the FACC does not allege Hajoca engaged in an unlawful practice.
The FACC does allege that Hajoca doctored records concerning the materials supplied to Express despite Cross-Complainants never receiving the benefit of the materials. It is reasonable to infer from this fact that Hajoca engaged in an unfair business practice. However, there are no facts alleged in the FACC which would show this was a pattern of behavior. Therefore, the FACC fails to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action for unfair business practices.
The demurrer is sustained as to this cause of action.
5. Seventh Cause of Action – Interference with Contractual Relations
Hajoca demurs to the seventh cause of action for interference with contractual relations on the grounds that (1) Cross-Complainants failed to allege that GRE was in a contractual relationship with a third party or HIMR, (2) Cross-Complainants failed to identify what conduct disrupted performance of a contract, and (3) Hajoca had an economic interest in the contract and would not have interfered with Cross-Complainants’ performance of a contract in connection with the renovations on the subject property.
The elements for the tort of intentional interference with the performance of a contract are: “(1) a valid contract between plaintiff and another party; (2) defendant’s knowledge of the contract; (3) defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.” (Asahi Kasei Pharma Corporation v. Actelion Ltd. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 945, 958.)  
 
A cause of action for interference with contractual relations does not lie against a party to the contract. (See Dryden v. Tri-Valley Growers (1977) 65 Cal.App.3d 990, 999.)
Here, Hajoca argues that the FACC fails to allege that GRE was in a contractual relationship with a third party. However, the FACC Alleges that GRE had a contract with HIMR to develop the subject property. (FACC ¶92.) Additionally, HIMR had a contractual relationship with the lender for the property. (FACC ¶93.) Although Hajoca argues this is conclusory, Cross-Complainants were only required to state the ultimate fact that a valid contract existed between them. Court finds the FACC sufficiently alleges a contract existed.
Hajoca next argues the Cross-Complaint is conclusory and does not apprise Hajoca of the wrongful acts it is purported to have committed. However, the FACC does allege that Hajoca opened a charge account without Cross-Complainants’ knowledge or authorization, failed to timely issue a preliminary notice, and fabricated invoices to collect the fabricated charges from Cross-Complainants. (FACC ¶¶34-37.) As a result of Hajoca’s actions, construction on the property has been delayed because Cross-Complainants incurred costs dealing with Hajoca’s collection efforts. (FACC ¶39.) Because the Cross-Complaint describes the conduct which disrupted construction on the subject property, the facts sufficiently state Hajoca’s actions disrupted Cross-Complainants’ agreement to develop the property. 
Finally, Hajoca argues that it does not make sense for Hajoca to interfere with Cross-Complainants’ contract because Hajoca was economically interested in the contract. Specifically, Hajoca argues it has no interest in interfering with the contract. Hajoca cites Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 514 (Applied Equipment) in support of this argument. However, the cause of action for interference with contractual relations protects “the expectations of contracting parties against frustrations by outsiders who have no legitimate social or economic interest in the contractual relationship, the tort cause of action for interference with a contract does not lie against a party to the contract.” (Applied Equipment at p.514.) 
Thus, Applied Equipment does not state that anyone with any economic interest is shielded from a cause of action for interference with economic relations. Rather, the cause of action only applies to strangers to the contract who have no legitimate economic interest under the contract. Here, because Hajoca is not a party to the contract, Cross-Complainants may bring a cause of action for interference with economic relations against it. 
The demurrer is overruled as to this cause of action. 
6. Eighth Cause of Action – Accounting
Finally, Hajoca demurs to the eighth cause of action for accounting on the grounds that (1) Cross-Complainants failed to state facts sufficient to constitute any claim against Hajoca and (2) the FACC does not allege Cross-Complainants are owed anything from Hajoca.
A cause of action for an accounting requires “a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.”  (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179.) But “[a]n action for accounting is not available where the plaintiff alleges the right to recover a sum certain or a sum that can be made certain by calculation.” (Id.) 
An accounting is an equitable proceeding that must be supported by some underlying misconduct which allows the plaintiff to invoke the right to an equitable remedy for accounting. (Green Valley Landowners Assn. v. City of Vallejo (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 425, 442, citing Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1137.) 
Although, as Hajoca points out, an accounting is generally a remedy, it may be a cause of action in some cases, such as when one party owes another a fiduciary duty that requires an accounting, and some balance is due to a plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting. (Pantoja v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2009) 640 F.Supp.2d 1177, 1191; Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 593.) 
Here, Hajoca does not owe money to Cross-Complainants. Rather, this is a dispute over whether Hajoca’s charges to GRE were proper. Where a defendant owes no money to a plaintiff, the plaintiff has no right to an accounting. (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 594.) A complicated accounting relationship between the parties by itself is not sufficient to permit the maintenance of a claim for an accounting. (Id.) Here, because Hajoca does not owe Cross-Complainants money, Cross-Complainants are not entitled to an accounting.
The demurrer is sustained as to this cause of action.
7. Leave to Amend
The burden is on Cross-Complainants to show in what manner the FACC may be amended and how the amendment would change the legal effect of his pleading. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335.) Here, Cross-Complainants do not discuss how the FACC may be amended to cure the above deficiencies and must be prepared to discuss this issue at the hearing on this matter.
II. Motion to Strike
Hajoca moves to strike portions of the FACC related to punitive damages. 
In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) 
“Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725 [examining Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(1)].) “A conscious disregard of the safety of others may constitute malice within the meaning of section 3294 of the Civil Code. In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895-896.) 
“As amended to include [despicable], the statute plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests. The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.” (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.) The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.” (Ibid.) Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’” (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.) 
“[T]he imposition of punitive damages upon a corporation is based upon its own fault. It is not imposed vicariously by virtue of the fault of others.” (City Products Corp. v. Globe Indemnity Co. (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 31, 36.) “Corporations are legal entities which do not have minds capable of recklessness, wickedness, or intent to injure or deceive. An award of punitive damages against a corporation therefore must rest on the malice of the corporation’s employees. But the law does not impute every employee’s malice to the corporation. Instead, the punitive damages statute requires proof of malice among corporate leaders: the ‘officer[s], director[s], or managing agent[s].’” (Cruz v. Home Base (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 167 [citation omitted].) As to ratification, “[a] corporation cannot confirm and accept that which it does not actually know about.’” (Ibid. [citing College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 726 [for ratification sufficient to justify punitive damages against corporation, there must be proof that officers, directors, or managing agents had actual knowledge of the malicious conduct and its outrageous character]].) 
Here, Hajoca argues that Cross-Complainants’ demand for punitive damages must be stricken because the FACC fails to plead facts sufficient to show any conduct which would warrant a claim for punitive damages. As discussed above, the FACC alleges that Hajoca opened a charge account for GRE without GRE’s knowledge, made $150,000 in unauthorized charges to GRE, and falsified invoices to collect the money from GRE. GRE never received the benefit of the materials Hajoca charged it for.
The facts alleged show that Hajoca acted with a willful disregard of Cross-Complainants’ interests because Hajoca made unauthorized charges to GRE. The Court also finds the FACC alleges Hajoca engaged in despicable conduct because Hajoca thereafter used false invoices to collect the charges despite GRE notifying Hajoca of the unauthorized charges. Because the facts show Hajoca engaged in conduct which is both despicable and willfully disregards Cross-Complainants’ interests, the Court finds that these facts are sufficient to show Hajoca engaged in malicious conduct. However, the FACC fails to name an officer, director, or managing agent who ratified this conduct. Therefore, the FACC fails to state sufficient facts to support a demand for punitive damages against Hajoca, a corporation. 
The motion to strike is GRANTED.
