DEPARTMENT 15 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23GDCV00516    Hearing Date: December 4, 2023    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTIONS IN LIMINE

Plaintiffs’ MIL No. 1

            Plaintiffs move to preclude evidence that Lorenzo Ruiz’ adult children threatened Plaintiffs and are gang members.  This evidence is irrelevant and more prejudicial than probative.  The motion is granted.

Plaintiffs’ MIL No. 2

            Plaintiffs move to exclude evidence of Lorenzo Ruiz’s exposure to Agent Orange when he served in the military on the ground that it is irrelevant, did not cause his mesothelioma, and is prejudicial because the U.S.’s use of Agent Orange in Vietnam is controversial.  This evidence could be relevant to life expectancy.  It can be mentioned briefly at trial without delving into U.S. military policy and actions in Vietnam.  The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL Nos. 1-7

            No motions were filed.

Defendants’ MIL No. 8

Defendant Pneumo Abex LLC seeks to exclude evidence from the Saranac experiments.  Pursuant to the July 8, 2022 CMO, this motion is deemed made and granted.  Plaintiffs did not show good cause to depart from that order.

            The motion is granted.

Defendants’ MIL No. 9

Defendant Autozone West moves to prevent experts from telling the jury the contents or titles of hearsay documents.  The motion does not specify any titles or publications to be excluded and therefore is too vague.  Also, some of the documents upon which the experts rely may be otherwise admissible.  And if an expert establishes the hearsay is general background information of the type relied upon by experts in the field, the hearsay may be admissible.  (People v. Veamatahau (2020) 9 Cal.5th 16, 22.) 

            The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 10

            No motion was filed.

Defendants’ MIL No. 11

Defendant Autozone West moves to exclude the use of the term “victim” contending the use of the term is more prejudicial than probative.  Plaintiffs do not identify any need to use the term “victim.”  The use of “victim” suggests someone at fault for harming the victim.  Thus, while the prejudice might not be great, the relevance is negligible.  The motion is granted.

Defendants’ MIL No. 12

            No motion was filed.

Defendants’ MIL No. 13

Autozone West moves to exclude the Don’t Blow It video and any evidence referring to the video as hearsay, unscientific, and prejudicial.  The video may be admissible at trial via an expert if the expert establishes that it is general background information of the type relied upon by experts in the field.  The claim that the video is unscientific can be the subject of cross-examination and goes to the weight to be given the video.  Defendant contends the EPA updated its guidance in 2007 so the video is outdated.  That too can be the subject of cross-examination.

Defendant argues the video’s references to an article and wives and children lack foundation.  That section of the video is irrelevant because there is no claim in this case about injury to wives and children.  Similarly, smoking does not seem to be an issue in this case.  If Plaintiffs establish that the video is admissible, the portion of the video about the magazine article, smoking, and wives and children are to be redacted.

            The motion is granted in part and denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 14

            Defendant Autozone West seeks to exclude evidence of a 1986 document prepared by the EPA referred to as the Gold Book because a 2007 EPA publication replaced it and it is hearsay.  The document could be relevant to notice of the dangers of asbestos pre-2007.  If Plaintiffs seek to use the document to show notice or knowledge, the documents may not be hearsay if it is not offered for the truth of the matter asserted.  Also, the contents of the document may be admissible at trial via an expert if the expert establishes that it is general background information of the type relied upon by experts in the field.  That the document was based on unscientific sources can be the subject of cross-examination and goes to the weight to be given the document and whether an expert can establish it is the type of information relied upon by experts.

            The motion is denied without prejudice to a contemporaneous objection at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 15

Autozone West seeks to exclude references to a 1989 proposed EPA ban that never went into effect because it was invalidated by the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals as not supported by sufficient evidence.  If Defendant contends it did not know about the hazards of asbestos by 1989, this evidence may go to notice or knowledge.  Defendant can cross-examine the witness about the fact that the proposed ban was never effective and was invalidated by the court for lack of evidence.  That cross-examination can be done efficiently, which just a few questions and does not need to consume an undue amount of time.

The motion is denied without prejudice to contemporaneous objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 16

            No motion was filed.

Defendants’ MIL No. 17

            Defendant Western Auto Supply moves exclude a Friction Product Fiber Release Studies (MVA 1810) and a MVA Scientific Consultants Report as unreliable because they use SEM and TEM technology, which Defendant contends are new technologies and therefore subject to a Kelly/Frye analysis.  (Motion at p. 3.)  However, Defendant cites no evidence this is new technology that has not been generally accepted.  Indeed, the publications Defendant seeks to exclude are from 1997 and 2008, strongly suggesting they do not include new technology.  Further, whether TEM is an appropriate method “goes to the weight and not the admissibility of the opinions” based on that technology.  (Strobel v. Johnson & Johnson (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 796, 811-812.)  That case discusses the various competing expert views on whether the methodology is generally accepted, something Defendant’s motion does not do.  (Id. at pp. 806, 811.)

            Defendant argues the reports fail to address chain of custody and therefore are not reliable.  That is a subject for cross-examination. 

            Defendant objects the reports are hearsay.  Experts may rely on hearsay in forming their opinions, and if an expert establishes the hearsay is general background information of the type relied upon by experts in the field, the hearsay may be admissible.  (People v. Veamatahau (2020) 9 Cal.5th 16, 22.)  The court cannot determine at this time whether an expert may be able to establish the admissibility and relevance of the reports. 

            The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 18

            Defendant Western Auto Supply moves to exclude studies and videos from Material Analytical Sciences because the work simulations are not similar to Plaintiff’s exposure.  Defendant argues the simulations took place in small sealed chambers, used improper testing methods such as TEM analysis not recognized by OSHA, and will be confusing to the jury because the conditions are so different.  Whether TEM is an appropriate method “goes to the weight and not the admissibility of the opinions.”  (Strobel v. Johnson & Johnson (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 796, 811-812.)

            At trial, Defendant can cross-examine the experts about the similarities and differences of Plaintiff’s exposure to a small, sealed chamber and argue the jury should give the videos no weight because of the different conditions.  The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 19

            Western Auto Supply moves to exclude photographs and documents related to Wizard Guardian brakes because Plaintiffs have no evidence Lorenzo Ruiz was exposed to Wizard brand brakes.  Plaintiffs appear to concede that Ruiz did not use Wizard brand brakes.  Instead, Plaintiffs argue the evidence is relevant because it shows Defendant should have been aware of the hazards of asbestos in brakes. 

At this point, the motion is too vague because it does not identify the specific evidence to be excluded.  Evidence about brakes sold in the 1950s is probably irrelevant because that was long before Ruiz was exposed to brakes.  Evidence of Defendant’s sales of asbestos-containing brakes closer to the time Ruiz was exposed to Defendant’s brakes may be relevant.  For example, if Defendant contends it had no knowledge that its brakes contained asbestos or that the asbestos in brakes was hazardous, evidence of Wizard brand brakes containing asbestos or containing asbestos warnings could be relevant.

            The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 20

            Defendant Wyeth Holdings moves to exclude documents dated after Lorenzo Ruiz’s last date of exposure, including a 1992 document listing suppliers of asbestos-containing products, because Ruiz worked on planes 20 years before that date.  However, the document does not state that it listed only current (1992) suppliers.  Rather, dates in the document’s attachment suggests it listed suppliers of products from decades earlier.  If Plaintiffs can show that the document included suppliers from the time period when Ruiz was working on planes, the document may be relevant.

            Defendant argues the document is not relevant because it includes some planes Ruiz never worked on.  However, Defendant did not show the document addressed only types of planes Ruiz never worked on.  If Plaintiffs can establish at trial that the document included the types of planes Ruiz did work on, it may be relevant.

            Defendant argues the document does not identify the exact plane parts where adhesives were used, does not mention 3M, and only mentions a few American Cyanamid products.  Those are matters for cross-examination and for arguing to the jury that the jury should give the document little weight.

            The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 21

            Defendant Wyeth Holdings moves to exclude evidence of American Cyanamid products because Plaintiffs have no evidence Lorenzo Ruiz worked with American Cyanamid Products.  This is a summary judgment motion in disguise.  The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 22

            Defendant The Pep Boys seeks to exclude evidence about its compliance with 1972 OSHA labeling regulations because the regulations do not require warning labels on its products.  To the extent this motion seeks a ruling on Plaintiffs’ failure to warn claims, it is an improper motion for summary adjudication.  Otherwise, the motion is too vague.  The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 23

Defendant The Pep Boys seeks to exclude evidence about dust levels based on witnesses’ visual observations.  A witness has personal knowledge of what the witness observed firsthand.  Evidence that Plaintiffs or witnesses observed dust may be relevant to the existence of asbestos fibers in the air and how Plaintiff was exposed to asbestos. 

            The motion is denied without prejudice to objection at trial.

Defendants’ MIL No. 24

            Defendant Genuine Parts Company moves to exclude undated photographs used in a deposition because they lack foundation and cannot be authenticated.  Plaintiffs contend they have witnesses who can authenticate the photographs.  If at trial, Plaintiffs seek to introduce the photographs without first establishing their authenticity and foundation, Defendant should object then.  Defendant objects the photographs are hearsay.  Photographs are not a statement of a person; they are made by the camera.  Defendant cites no law establishing that a photograph is hearsay, and Defendant did not show that the photographs depict statements made by a person (for example, that the photograph depicts a letter or email or document written by a person).  Defendant objects the photographs are irrelevant because Plaintiffs have no evidence that Plaintiff ever worked with the products depicted in the photograph.  If at trial Plaintiffs try to use the photographs without first establishing their relevancy, Defendant should object then.  The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

            The moving party is to give notice.

Defendants’ MIL No. 1

            Defendant Union Carbide moves to exclude evidence of its relationship with Dow Chemical.  This motion is too vague.  If at trial Plaintiff asks questions about Dow Chemical that are irrelevant, Defendant can object at that time.  The motion is denied without prejudice to objections at trial.

Defendant Union Carbide moves to exclude evidence of working conditions at its facilities as irrelevant and unduly prejudicial.  This motion is too vague.  If “working conditions” refers to something like wage and hour violations, that would be irrelevant.  However, if it refers to something like workers being exposed to asbestos, the evidence could be relevant.  For example, the evidence of asbestos exposure at the facilities could be relevant to knowledge or notice.

Union Carbide moves to exclude any reference to incidents where people died or were injured as a result of Union Carbide’s chemicals other than asbestos.  Incidents not involving asbestos are irrelevant, more prejudicial than probative, and would be unduly time consuming.
