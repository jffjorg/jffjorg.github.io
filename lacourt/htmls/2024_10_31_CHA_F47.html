DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 21CHCV00022    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 10/31/24

Case #21CHCV00022

 

MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT AGREEMENT & ENTER JUDGMENT

 

Motion filed on 7/29/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff United States Senate Federal Credit Union

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Michelle H. Borrows

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order enforcing the settlement agreement entered into between Plaintiff and Defendant for entry of judgment pursuant to CCP 664.6. 

 

RULING: The motion is denied without prejudice. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Defendant Michelle H. Burrows’ (Defendant) default on a Home Equity and Homeowner Loan (Loan) from Plaintiff United States Senate Federal Credit Union (Plaintiff).  On 1/11/21, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendant for breach of contract seeking damages in the amount of $78,938.31 for the remaining principal balance of the Loan.

 

In July of 2021, Plaintiff and Defendant entered a written settlement agreement which resulted in the filing of a Stipulation and Order Re: Settlement, Retention of Jurisdiction, and Dismissal on 8/19/21.  (Kostelidis Decl., Ex.A).  The settlement agreement required Defendant to pay the total amount of $84,039.29 with one payment of $3,500.00 due on or before 7/29/21, and additional payments of $500.00 beginning 8/23/21, and continuing to be paid monthly on the 23rd of each month thereafter until 7/23/22.  Id.  And lastly, payments of $1,000.00 beginning 8/23/22 to be paid monthly thereafter until full payment of $84,039.29 is satisfied.  Id.  Plaintiff contends that Defendant has failed to comply with the terms of the settlement agreement by failing to make the agreed scheduled payments.  (Kostelidis Decl. ¶3(2)).

 

Therefore, on 7/29/24, Plaintiff filed the instant motion seeking an order enforcing the settlement agreement entered into between Plaintiff and Defendant for entry of judgment pursuant to CCP 664.6.  On 8/19/24, Plaintiff filed proofs of service indicating that the motion was served on Defendant by mailing the documents to Defendant at 27621 Skylark Lane, Santa Clarita, CA 91350.  Defendant has not opposed or otherwise responded to the motion. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff makes this motion pursuant to CCP 664.6(a) which provides:

 

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.”

 

The following deficiencies in the motion preclude the granting of same: 

 

First, the Court cannot confirm that Defendant had proper notice of the hearing on the instant motion.  As noted above, the motion was served on Defendant by mail.  (See Proofs of Service filed on 8/19/24).  However, Defendant never appeared in this action; therefore, there is no address of record for Defendant in the court file.  Also, as noted above, there is no response to the motion by Defendant to cure the defect in notice. 

 

Second, Plaintiff has failed to provide sufficient evidence/information regarding the requested judgment to be entered against Defendant.  See Rooney (1973) 10 C3d 368-369.  The declaration submitted in support of the motion indicates that Plaintiff is requesting “that the Court award Plaintiff the full amount demanded in the complaint, plus interest according to proof, reasonable attorney’s fees and costs incurred in bringing this motion, less any payments made prior to breach, as provided for in the Settlement Agreement.”  (Kostelidis Decl. ¶4(2)).  However, Plaintiff does not provide any information/evidence regarding whether Defendant made any payments under the settlement agreement and/or the specific amount of the judgment Plaintiff seeks to have entered.  (See Motion, generally; Kostelidis Decl.; proposed Order).  Rather, Plaintiff merely indicates that Defendant “has failed to comply with the terms of the Settlement Agreement by failing to make the required payments.”  (Kostelidis Decl. ¶3(2)).      

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied without prejudice.    

 


Case Number: 23CHCV03185    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Case #23CHCV03185

 

DEMURRER TO THE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Demurrer filed on 9/20/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Yusheng Shew and Jill J. Van

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs Gevork Voskanian; Mary Keryan and KSJV3, Inc.

NOTICE: ok

 

Demurrer is to the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 5th, 8th and 9th causes of action in the Second Amended Complaint:

            1.  Breach of Contract

            2.  Fraud and Deceit

            3.  Negligent Misrepresentation

            4.  Accounting

            5.  Declaratory Relief

            6.  Intentional Interference

            7.  Negligent Interference

            8.  Assault

            9. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

RULING: The demurrer is overruled.  Answer is due within 30 days.    

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of the sale of a fencing company, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant KSJV3, Inc. dba Five Star Fence (Five Star), from Defendants/Cross-Complainants Yusheng Shew (Shew) and Jill Van (Van) to Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants Gevork Voskanian (Voskanian) and Mary Keryan (Keryan). 

 

On 10/20/23, Voskanian, Keryan and Five Star (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed this action against Shew and Van (collectively, Defendants) for: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Accounting, (6) Declaratory Relief, (7) Unjust Enrichment, (8) Conspiracy, (9) Intentional Interference and (10) Negligent Interference.

 

On 12/18/23, Shew and Van filed and served a cross-complaint against Voskanian, Keryan and Five Star for: (1) Fraud – Promise Without Intent to Perform, (2) Breach of Stock Purchase Agreement, (3) Breach of Secured Promissory Note, (4) Breach of Guaranty, (5) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (6) Judicial Foreclosure of UCC-1 Lien, (7) Interference With Contract, (8) Accounting and (9) Declaratory Relief.   

 

On 12/26/23, Shew and Van/Defendants filed and served a demurrer, with a 3/8/24 hearing date, to the 1st – 5th and 7th and 8th causes of action in the complaint.  On 2/22/24, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint for: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Accounting, (6) Declaratory Relief, (7) Intentional Interference, (8) Negligent Interference, (9) Assault and (10) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

After meet and confer efforts failed to resolve the issues Defendants had with the First Amended Complaint, on 4/29/24, Defendants served and filed a demurrer to the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 6th, 9th, and 10th causes of action in the First Amended Complaint.  On 6/21/24, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the First Amended Complaint with 30 days leave to amend making a Second Amended Complaint due 7/22/24 (since 7/21/24 was a Sunday).

 

Although Plaintiffs served the Second Amended Complaint on 7/22/24, it was not filed until 7/24/24 due to the ransomware attack experienced by the Los Angeles Superior Court.  Additionally, as confirmed with court staff, due to problems caused by the ransomware attack, the Second Amended Complaint did not appear in eCourt as being filed on 7/24/24 until early September (sometime between 9/4/24 and 9/6/24).

 

On 9/10/24 and 9/11/24, Plaintiffs’ and Defendants’ counsel exchanged emails regarding a response to the Second Amended Complaint with Plaintiffs taking the position that a demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint at that point would be untimely and, therefore, refusing to meet and confer regarding issues Defendants’ had with the Second Amended Complaint.  (See Chien Decl. ¶¶8-10, Ex.D, Ex.F).  On 9/12/24, Defendants’ counsel filed a “Declaration of Demurring Party In Support of Automatic Extension and on 9/13/24, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed a declaration in response thereto reaffirming Plaintiffs’ position that the demurrer is untimely.  On 9/20/24, Defendants filed and served the instant demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint.  Plaintiffs have opposed the demurrer and Defendants have filed a reply to the opposition. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Timeliness of the Demurrer

 

CCP 430.40(a) provides:

 

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (emphasis added)

 

The proof of service attached to the Second Amended Complaint shows that it was electronically served on defense counsel on 7/22/24 and defense counsel does not refute this in his declaration filed in support of the demurrer or claim that the Second Amended Complaint was not received on 7/22/24.  (See Chien Decl.).  Additionally, defense counsel admits that on 8/5/24, Plaintiffs’ counsel forwarded him a filing receipt indicating that the Second Amended Complaint was accepted for filing on 7/24/24.  (Chien Decl. ¶4, Ex.C).  Despite the foregoing, defense counsel did not begin meet and confer efforts regarding the Second Amended Complaint until 9/10/24, when the statutory deadline for filing a demurrer is 30 days after service of the subject complaint/cross-complaint, not the filing of same.  See CCP 430.40(a).  Defense counsel also did not file a declaration regarding automatic extension until well beyond such deadline. 

Defendants also claim that “the very first opportunity that Defendants would have been capable of filing a responsive pleading to the SAC would have been September 5, 2024… .”  (See Demurrer, p.7:25-27).  However, Defendants provide no evidence that they attempted to file a demurrer earlier and it was rejected.  Even if a demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint would not have been accepted for filing until the Second Amended Complaint appeared in eCourt sometime between 9/4/24 and 9/6/24, Defendants have failed to adequately explain their failure to meet and confer regarding Second Amended Complaint and have a demurrer ready for filing as soon as the Second Amended Complaint appeared in the court system, rather than waiting until 9/20/24 to file and serve the instant demurrer. 

 

Despite the foregoing, since the Court considered Plaintiffs’ opposition to the demurrer to the First Amended Complaint, which was filed and served 5 days late, it has also considered Defendants’ late demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint on its merits.  (See 6/21/24 Minute Order, p.2); CRC 3.1300(d).  Once again, the parties are warned that failure to timely file and/or serve papers in the future may result in the late papers not being considered by the Court.  (See 6/21/24 Minute Order, p.6).    

   

Grounds for Demurrer

 

Among others, a demurrer may be based on the grounds that the complaint fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action; the complaint fails to allege whether the contracts sued on are written, oral or implied by conduct and/or that the pleading is uncertain and ambiguous.  CCP 430.10(e), (f), (g).

 

1st cause of action – Breach of Contract

 

To state a claim for breach of written contract, the plaintiff must plead the existence of a contract, its terms which establish the obligation at issue, the occurrence of any conditions precedent to enforcement of the obligation, and the breach of that obligation.  FPI Development, Inc. (1991) 231 CA3d 367, 383.  A written contract may be pled by setting forth the terms, verbatim, in the complaint, by attaching a copy of the contract to the complaint and incorporating it by reference, or by pleading its legal effect (i.e., allege the substance of its relevant terms).  See McKell (2006) 142 CA4th 1457, 1489.

 

Here, Plaintiffs have alleged that they and Defendants entered into a written “Stock Purchase Agreement” (SPA) and various ancillary written agreements.  (SAC ¶7).  The Second Amended Complaint also makes clear that Plaintiffs are claiming that Defendants breached the SPA.  (SAC ¶32).  Plaintiffs have sufficiently pled the legal effect of the SPA, Plaintiffs’ performance under the SPA, the conduct of Defendants which Plaintiffs claim breached the SPA and damages resulting therefrom.  (See SAC ¶¶7-13, 29-33). 

 

2nd cause of action – Fraud and Deceit

 

The elements of a fraud cause of action are: (1) false representation by defendant, (2) intent to deceive or induce reliance, (3) justifiable reliance by plaintiff, (4) resulting damages.  Service by Medallion, Inc. (1996) 44 CA4th 1807, 1816.  Fraud claims must be pled with specific facts showing how, when, where, to whom and by what means the misrepresentations were made.  Lazar (1996) 12 C4th 631, 645; Stansfield (1990) 220 CA3d 59, 73. 

 

The allegations in the Second Amended Complaint sufficiently meet the pleading standards for a fraud cause of action.  (SAC ¶¶14-22, 27-28, 34-42).

 

3rd cause of action – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

A claim for negligent misrepresentation has the same specific pleading requirements as a fraud claim.  Small (2003) 30 C4th 167, 184.   As with the fraud and deceit claim, Plaintiffs have sufficiently pled the negligent misrepresentation cause of action.  (SAC ¶¶14-22, 27-28, 43-49). 

 

5th cause of action – Declaratory Relief

 

In a declaratory relief cause of action, a plaintiff may seek a judicial declaration of the plaintiff’s rights or duties with respect to another in cases of actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties.  Alborzian (2015) 235 CA4th 29, 40.  Declaratory relief is appropriate when a party seeks a judicial determination of the parties’ respective rights under a contract or other agreement.  See CCP 1060.

 

The Second Amended Complaint sufficiently alleges that Plaintiffs are seeking a judicial determination regarding the rights and obligations of the parties under the SPA and related ancillary agreements.  For example, Plaintiffs contend that the Promissory Note and Security Agreement should be offset based on the alleged fraudulent conduct of Defendants.  (See SAC ¶¶7-22, 53-57).

 

8th cause of action – Assault

 

The elements of an assault cause of action are: (1) defendant acted, intending to cause harmful or offensive contact, (2) plaintiff reasonably believed that plaintiff was about to be touched in a harmful or an offensive manner or that defendant threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; that it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; that plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct, (4) plaintiff was harmed and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  CACI 1301. 

 

This cause of action is alleged solely by Voskanian against Shew and incorporates all of  the previous and subsequent paragraphs.  Based on the allegations in the Second Amended Complaint, it is clear that the assault claim is based on Shew’s alleged threatening Voskanian with a 9-millimeter gun and demanding that they meet at a park to “hash stuff out.”  (SAC ¶¶23, 68, 69).  Contrary to Defendants’ assertion, it cannot be determined from the face of the pleading that there was a lack of immediacy and proximity component to the alleged threats.  It cannot be determined from the face of the pleading whether or not Voskanian and Shew were together at the time of the alleged threat, whether Shew pointed the gun at Voskanian, etc.,  especially in the face of the allegation that Voskanian was aware of the caliber of gun with which the alleged threat was made.    

 

9th cause of action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) defendant’s conduct was outrageous, (2) defendant intended to cause plaintiff emotional distress or defendant acted with reckless disregard of the probability that plaintiff would suffer emotional distress, knowing that plaintiff was present when the conduct occurred, (3) plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress and (4) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s severe emotional distress.  CACI 1600. 

 

This cause of action is also alleged solely by Voskanian against Shew and incorporates all of  the previous and subsequent paragraphs.  The intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action is also based on the allegations that Shew allegedly threatened Voskanian with a 9-millimeter gun and demanded that they meet at a park to “hash stuff out” among other things  (SAC ¶¶23, 25, 26, 74-80).  Contrary to Defendants’ assertion, the Court finds that threatening physical violence against someone over a business dispute is sufficiently outrageous to support the cause of action.    

 

CONCLUSION

 

The demurrer is overruled.  Answer is due within 30 days. 

 

Once again, both parties are reminded to comply with the filing deadlines and are warned that failure to timely file and/or serve papers in the future may result in the late papers not being considered by the Court.
