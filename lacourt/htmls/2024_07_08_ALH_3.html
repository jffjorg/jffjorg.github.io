[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANT/CROSS-COMPLAINANT EASTERN HORIZON, LLC’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF PAULINE YIP’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET TWO

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

July 8, 2024

 

)

 

 

          There are two document requests at issue in this motion brought by defendant/cross-complainant Eastern Horizon, LLC (“Eastern Horizon”). Eastern Horizon moves for an order compelling plaintiff Pauline Yip (“Yip”) to serve further responses to RFP Nos. 38 and 39, which state:

-      RFP No. 38: All DOCUMENTS RELATING TO YOUR federal and state income tax filings for the years 2010 to 2021, including, without limitation, federal and state income tax returns.

-      RPP No. 39: All DOCUMENTS RELATING TO any bank accounts held or used by YOU from January 1, 2020 to the present, including, without limitation, all bank statements.

Eastern Horizon also seeks sanctions in the amount of $2,885 against Yip and her counsel of record, while Yip seeks sanctions in the amount of $1,600 against Eastern Horizon and its counsel of record.

Yip objected to these requests on the grounds that they were overbroad in time and improperly including her attorneys in the definition of “You”, irrelevant, and invasive of her (and her attorneys’) privacy.

A party seeking to compel further responses may file a motion which “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).)

The Court addresses each document request in further detail below.

          RFP No. 38

          Eastern Horizon requests all federal and state income tax filings from 2010 to 2021 because they are “critical to Eastern Horizon’s defense to [Yip]’s sixth cause of action for equitable indemnity.” Yip alleges in her complaint that she was notified by the Internal Revenue Service and California Franchise Tax Board that she owed over $1 million in taxes as a result of Eastern Horizon’s incorrect capital gains reporting. (Compl., ¶¶ 66-68.) She states she “is being pursued by the Tax Authorities and has incurred a significant amount of fees in trying to resolve the tax issues.” (Compl., ¶ 69.)

Yip argues that her tax returns implicate her right to privacy regarding her personal financial information. Such right, however, must be balanced against Yip’s own claims which necessarily implicate her financial information insofar as it is probative of her claimed damages.

Nevertheless, the Court notes that Eastern Horizon fails to explain how 11 years of tax returns is necessary. For instance, Eastern Horizon does not set forth specific facts or evidence that tax authorities are claiming that Yip owes taxes from 2010 to 2021, even though Yip has already produced an IRS notice and a notice to suspend her driver’s license and real estate license. Accordingly, the motion is DENIED and the Court sustains Yip’s objections on the grounds that RFP No. 38 is overbroad.

RFP No. 39

Eastern Horizon argues that Yip’s bank records from January 1, 2020, to the present are necessary to evaluate the merits of Yip’s third cause of action for quiet title. Yip’s quiet title claim alleges that Eastern Horizon and Defendants obtained legal title by fraudulently inducing her to sell the property as part of a sale/leaseback agreement with an option to repurchase. Eastern Horizon claims that these records would show whether Yip was financially able to exercise her option to purchase the property at 37 Twiggs, Irvine, California 92620 when she purportedly attempted to do so in 2021.

Yip opposes the motion and argues that whether she had $1.67 million to pay for the entire repurchase price is irrelevant. Yip states that she has already produced letters of pre-approvals from lenders from 2021 to 2022, which is sufficient evidence that she was able to obtain a loan to exercise her option to repurchase. Eastern Horizons counters the sufficiency of these pre-approval letters by arguing that the letters themselves are not loan commitments, but conditional and subject to further documentation of income and expenses. But there is no recitation of any qualifying language and there is no description of the documents upon which the loans would be conditioned. While these supporting documents would be discoverable, Eastern Horizon fails to show specific facts setting forth good cause for the discovery of all of Yip’s bank records from 2020 to the present. The motion is DENIED as to RFP No. 39 and the Court sustains the objection on the grounds that it is overbroad.

Both parties’ requests for sanctions are DENIED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION FOR AN ORDER THAT THE GENUINENESS OF DOCUMENTS AND THE TRUTH OF MATTERS SPECIFIED IN REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION BE DEEMED ADMITTED, AND FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT ABEGAIL ALMA COOK

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

July 8, 2024

 

Plaintiff EBF Holdings, LLC dba Everest Business Funding (“Plaintiff”) moves for an order deeming admitted the genuineness of documents and truth of matters specified in its First Set of Requests for Admission served on defendant Abegail Alma Cook (“Defendant”) on October 16, 2023. The motion is unopposed.

Where a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

          The motion is unopposed and there is no dispute that Defendant failed to serve responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests. It also does not appear that Defendant has served substantially compliant proposed responses. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $2,015 for this simple motion (and a pending motion against co-defendant Corinthian Hospice, Inc.) is excessive. Plaintiff’s counsel, who charges a billing rate of $650 per hour, should have been able to draft these two relatively simple motions in less than 3.1 hours. Accordingly, sanctions are imposed against Defendant in the reduced amount of $385, consisting of 0.5 hours at Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rate and a $60 filing fee, payable within 20 days of the date of this Order.
