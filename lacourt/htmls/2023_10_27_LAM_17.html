DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV21696    Hearing Date: October 27, 2023    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DANIEL SIMONI, et al.

                          

         vs.

 

STEVE LYNCH

 

 Case No.:  22STCV21696

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 27, 2023

 

Plaintiff’s motion for a protective order is GRANTED.

 

On 7/5/2022, Plaintiffs Daniel Simoni and Marc Wieland (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed suit against Steve Lynch (Defendant) seeking declaratory relief.

 

            Now, Plaintiff Bigg Time Entertainment, Inc. (Plaintiff) moves for a protective order providing that John Doe’s 3 personal identifying information or likeness, including but not limited to a videotape of John Doe’s deposition, (1) not be disclosed to anyone other than the attorneys in this matter, and (2) not be disclosed by the attorneys publicly.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff seeks a protective order on behalf of “John Doe”, an employee of Bigg Time Entertainment, Inc. Plaintiff argues that John Does is entitled to accommodation pursuant to CCP section 2025.420 and California Rules of Court (CRC) rule 1.100.

 

            After reviewing Plaintiff’s unredacted filings, the Court finds good cause exists to grant the protective order. Notably, Plaintiff does not object to: (1) Defendant’s counsel possessing a copy of the video deposition; (2) Defendant viewing the videotaped deposition with counsel; or (3) Plaintiff possessing a copy of the deposition transcript. Rather, the scope of the protective order only provides that Defendant not be allowed to possess his own copy of the videotaped deposition.

 

            In light of Plaintiff’s privacy concerns and medical condition (which were disclosed under seal and thus are not discussed here), and given the evidence presented of harassment and the risk of harassment related to this litigation, the Court finds good cause to grant Plaintiff’s request for a protective order. Importantly, there is no indication that Defendant will suffer any prejudice as a result of this order.  

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for a protective order is granted.

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  October    , 2023

                                                                                                                                                          

   Hon. Jon R. Takasugi
   Judge of the Superior Court

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

JEFFREY A. PARIS

 

         vs.

 

ESTATE OF REYNOLD F. PARIS 

 

 Case No.:  22STCV28181 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 27, 2023

 

Chabad of Ojai Valley (COV)’s motion to vacate the judgment and dismiss based on failure to join an indispensable party is CONTINUED, to allow the parties to file supplemental briefing consistent with the ruling set forth below.  

 

            On 8/29/2022, Plaintiff Jeffrey A. Paris (Plaintiff) filed suit against the Estate of Reynold F. Paris, trustee of the Paris Family Trust, alleging: (1) rescission; (2) complaint in equity to set aside; (3) fraud; (4) fraud; (5) common counts; (6) common counts; (7) constructive trust; and (8) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

 

            Now, Chabad of Ojai Valley (COV) moves to set aside judgment and dismiss this case for lack of jurisdiction over the internal affairs of trusts.

 

Discussion

 

            This action arises out of a Stipulated Judgment entered 2/6/2023, which struck, in part, a $75,000 charitable gift to COV by the Third Amendment to the Paris Family Trust.

 

A person is an indispensable party to litigation if his or her rights must necessarily be affected by the judgment. Stated differently, ‘Where the plaintiff seeks some type of affirmative relief which, if granted, would injure or affect the interest of a third person not joined, that third person is an indispensable party…’[a] judgment in an action not naming an indispensable party ... is subject to later collateral attack by the non-joined indispensable party.” (Washington Mutual Bank v. Blechman (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 662, 667, at p. 668, quoting Save of Bay, Inc. v. San Diego Unified Port Dist. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 686.)

 

            Here, COV is a beneficiary of the Third Amendment to the Paris Family Trust. (Nemtzov Decl., Exh. 1.) COV contends that it and Rabbi Nemtzov were never parties to this case and were never served and had no notice of this proceeding until the Stipulated Judgment was received. (RJN No. 1)

 

            After review, the Court finds that supplemental briefing is necessary to address several issues.  

 

            As a preliminary matter, Plaintiff argues that COV cannot seek relief under CCP section 473(d) because it is not a party. However, a nonparty “that is aggrieved by a judgment or order may become a party of record and obtain a right to appeal by moving to vacate the judgment.” (People v. Hernandez (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 715, 720.) “A motion to vacate in the trial court provides a means by which such a nonparty may become a party to the litigation with a right of appeal without the need to formally intervene in the action.” (Henry M. Lee Law Corp. v. Superior Court (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 1375, 1382; 139 Cal.Rptr.3d 712.) As such, a motion to vacate is a means by which a non-party can seek to become a party to litigation.

 

            However, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that COV has not filed any proposed pleading which it would file if the judgment were set aside. “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted" CCP §473(b). In this case, COV, as a non-party, can only move to set aside the judgment. A request to dismiss the claim, which concerns the merits of the case, can only be properly considered once a motion to set aside has been granted, and COV has been made a party of record. As such, COV’s request to dismiss is premature, and should have been filed as a separate proposed pleading.

 

            Second, COV argues that the judgment is void. However, by COV’s own citation, “[t]he failure to join an indispensable party invalidates the portions of the judgment that purport to adjudicate that party's rights.” (Carpenters 46 N. Cal. Ctys. Conf. Bd. v. Zweigle (1982) 130 Cal.App.3d 337, emphasis added.) Here, COV’s motion concerns only the $75,000.00 charitable gift, while the judgment concerned the entire third amendment of the Paris Family Trust.  COV has not set forth any basis as to why the Court would or should set aside the entire judgment or dismiss the entire action, when only the $75,000 gift to COV was impacted by the entry of judgment, and only that specific portion of the judgment would be unenforceable for a failure to join COV as an indispensable party. COV is to submit briefing on the question.

 

            Third, COV argues that the probate court has exclusive jurisdiction over this case and argues that “[t]his case, which essentially contests the Trust, required Notice to Chabad of Ojai. This certainly would have been required in Probate Court.” (Motion, 8: 23-24.) However, in opposition, Plaintiff claims that COV was served on 7/11/2022 with a probate petition, and that COV never responded to that petition. Moreover, Plaintiff contends that this case can no longer proceed in probate.

 

            Both parties are to submit further briefing clarifying whether or not this case can proceed in probate, whether or not COV had notice of the probate claim, and why COV did not file any pleadings or seek any relief if it did. Moreover, Plaintiff is to clarify why it notified COV of its impacted rights as part of the probate process, but did not notify COV that it was filing this action. In other words, Plaintiff is to clarify why he believed COV was an impacted party for probate, but did not consider COV an indispensable party in this civil action. While Plaintiff clarifies that he did not serve COV in this case because he was not seeking specific damages from COV, Plaintiff has not advanced any substantive argument, supported by case law, that COV was not, in fact, an indispensable party because of its interest in property impacted by Plaintiff’s claims.

 

Based on the foregoing, COV’s motion to vacate the judgment and dismiss based on failure to join an indispensable party is continued to allow supplemental briefing, consistent with the ruling set forth above.

 

 

It is so ordered.

MICHAEL CHANG

                          

         vs.

Plaintiff’s motion for a new trial is DENIED.

 

            On 8/1/2017, Plaintiff Michael Chang filed suit against Francis Seongjin Ryu alleging extortion.

 

Now, Plaintiff moves for a new trial.

 

Legal Standard

 

A verdict may be vacated and any other decision may be modified or vacated, in whole or in part, and a new or further trial granted on all or part of the issues on the application of the party aggrieved, for any of the following causes, materially affecting the substantial rights of such party:¿ 

¿ 

(1)¿Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury¿or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial;¿ 

¿ 

(2)¿Misconduct of the jury;¿and whenever any one or more of the jurors have been induced to assent to any general or special verdict, or to a finding on any question submitted to them by the court, by are sort to the determination of chance, such misconduct may be proved by the affidavit of any one of the jurors;¿ 

¿ 

(3) Accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against;¿ 

¿ 

(4) Newly discovered evidence, material for the party making the application, which he could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered and produced at the trial;¿ 

¿ 

(5) Excessive or inadequate damages;¿ 

¿ 

(6) Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law;¿ 

¿ 

(7) Error in¿law, occurring at the trial and¿excepted to by the party making the application.¿ 

¿ 

(See CCP § 657.)¿ 

¿ 

When ruling on an application for a new trial, the court sits as an independent trier of fact.¿(Lane v. Hughes Aircraft Co. (2000) 22 Cal.4th¿405, 412.)¿The court, therefore, has broad discretion to order new trials, limited only by the obligation to state its reasons for granting a new trial and the existence of substantial evidence in the record to support those reasons.¿(Id.)¿¿In assessing¿the need for a new trial,¿the court must rely on its¿view of the overall record,¿taking into account such factors, among others, as the nature and seriousness of the¿alleged¿misconduct, the general¿atmosphere, including the judge’s control, of the trial, the likelihood of prejudicing the jury, and the efficacy of objection or admonition under all the circumstances.¿(Dominguez v. Pantalone¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d¿201, 211.)¿ 

 

Procedural Background

 

Plaintiff’s extortion claims arises from a settlement communication which took place between Defendant, while representing his client Johnny Choi, and Plaintiff’s attorney Shi Young Lim, Esq. Trial commenced on 6/6/2023 (with motions in limine having been heard on 6/2/2023), and on 6/9/2023, the jury rendered its Verdict, awarding no damages to Plaintiff. (See Verdict, Teel Decl., Exh. A. ) On 8/24/2023, the court entered Judgment in favor of Defendant, awarding Defendant costs “per memo of costs.”

 

Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that a new trial is warranted because: (1) the Court failed to give Plaintiff’s requested jury instruction regarding nominal damages; (2) the Court erroneously granted Defendant’s Motions in Limine Nos. 3 and 4; (3) the jury erred in not awarding any emotional distress damages; and (4) there were irregularities in the proceedings caused by Defendant’s closing statement.

 

As to the first point, Plaintiff argues that the Court improperly denied his proposed jury instruction allowing the jury to award nominal damages. However, as noted in Defendant’s opposition, the Court extensively conferred with the parties over this proposed instruction during the trial, and concluded the instruction was not proper after Plaintiff could not produce any authority which indicated that nominal damages were recoverable in an extortion action. (See June 8, 2023 Reporter’s Transcript, 102:14:28; 103:1-13, Exhibit A.)

 

However, setting this aside, the jury concluded that there were no actual damages suffered. Plaintiff was provided the opportunity to prove actual damage and the jury concluded that there were zero actual damages. Plaintiff has not presented any evidence or argument to show that the lack of a nominal damages instruction had any impact on the jury’s award of zero actual damages. As such, nominal damages cannot be awarded on retrial because the underlying tort – extortion – was not established. In any action sounding in tort (such as extortion), “nominal damages to vindicate a technical right cannot be recovered … where no actual loss has occurred,” because without actual damage – a vital element to any cause of action sounding in tort – no liability for the underlying tort is proved. (See Engle v. City of Oroville (1965) 238 Cal.App.2d 266, 274.)

 

There is also no indication that the jury would have awarded nominal damages. Indeed, both of Plaintiff’s proposed jury instructions on the issue of nominal damages (CACI 360, re: breach of contract and Civil Code 3660) provided only that the jury “may” award nominal damages in certain situations, not that the jury “shall” award those damages.

 

Finally, a new trial to hear nominal damages would require a re-trial of the entire case. As noted by Defendant, this would be improper for two reasons. First, it would present issues for trying this case in an unlimited jurisdiction, given the $25,000 jurisdictional limit for unlimited cases. Second, California Courts have held that a unless reversal would assure costs for the moving party (thus permitting Plaintiff a recovery for more than a “trifle”), a verdict failing to award nominal damages where they are recoverable is alone not grounds for a new trial. (Sweet v. Johnson (1959) 169 Cal.App.2d 630; see Sill Properties, Inc. v. CMAG, Inc. (1963) 219 Cal.App.2d 42, 55-56, and Walker v. Pacific Indemnity Co. (1960) 183 Cal.App.2d 513, 517.)

 

As to the second contention, Plaintiff argues that he is entitled to a new trial because the Court incorrectly granted Defendant’s Motions in Limine excluding Plaintiff’s reference to attorneys fees. In support, Plaintiff (just as in his Motions in Limine) cites to Brandt v Superior Court (1985) 37 Cal.3d. 813, 817-819 and Nelson v Kellog (1912) 162 Cal. 621, 623-624. Plaintiff also references the “Avenatti case” but does not cite the actual decision or attach a copy of the decision.

 

            As noted in its ruling on the Motions in Limine, all ll of Plaintiff’s cases are distinguishable.  In Fuhrman, the Court there expressly explained why the Brandt decision did not support an attorneys fees claim in an extortion action:

 

The fatal flaw in plaintiff's action is that she apparently never paid the money defendants demanded in their letters. The only actual damages plaintiff alleges are her emotional distress and attorney fees.

 

Attorney fees are not recoverable here, as there is no agreement for the payment of fees and no applicable statutory provision for the recovery of fees. (Code Civ.Proc., § 1021.) In Brandt v. Superior Court (1985) 37 Cal.3d 813, 210 Cal.Rptr. 211, 693 P.2d 796, the court suggests, in somewhat broad language, that whenever attorney fees are incurred as a result of the defendant's tortious conduct, such fees are an economic loss and therefore recoverable as damages. However, we construe such language to be limited to the factual setting of Brandt, supra, and the particular conduct at issue. The precise issue in Brandt was articulated at the beginning of the opinion: “When an insurer tortiously withholds benefits, are attorney's fees, reasonably incurred to compel payment of the policy benefits, recoverable as an element of the damages resulting from such tortious conduct? We hold they are....” (37 Cal.3d at p. 815, 210 Cal.Rptr. 211, 693 P.2d 796; fn. omitted.) “When an insurer's tortious conduct reasonably compels the insured to retain an attorney to obtain the benefits due under a policy, it follows that the insurer should be liable in a tort action for that expense. The attorney's fees are an economic loss—damages—proximately caused by the tort....” (37 Cal.3d at p. 817, 210 Cal.Rptr. 211, 693 P.2d 796.)

 

            (Fuhrman v. Satellite Systems (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 408, 426-27 (overruled on other grounds as stated in Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205.)

 

            In other words, the Fuhrman court explained that in the facts presented in Brandt, the plaintiff was entitled to recover attorneys fees because the plaintiff, unlike here, was forced to retain an attorney in order to enforce a contractual right and recover contractual insurance benefits due under an insurance policy which had been withheld by the bad faith of the insurer, and that it was that contractual claim which entitled the Plaintiff to attorneys fees.

 

            The Nelson case is similarly distinguishable, as it did not involve a civil extortion claim. Rather, in Nelson, the plaintiff there was arrested due to a civil warrant that was caused to be issued by the defendant, and had to hire an attorney in order to obtain a discharge from the unlawful arrest. The Nelson court permitted the recovery of attorneys fees, as Plaintiff was forced to incur that expense in order to obtain a discharge of the arrest.

 

            Finally, as for the Avenatti case, Plaintiff presumably refers to the Second Circuit decision entitled People v. Avenatti, issued in August of 2023, 2023 WL5597835. As a preliminary matter, a Second Circuit case is not binding on this Court. Second, “[t]hat decision does not support Plaintiff’s argument. Mr. Avenatti was convicted of various crimes, including extortion and wire fraud. That court addressed, among other things, the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3663A), requiring a court to order full restitution to victims of certain crimes after a defendant is sentenced. Avenatti has no relevance to the present action, as there is no evidence that Defendant was ever convicted of the crime of extortion, and there has been no finding that Plaintiff is a crime victim entitled to restitution under 18 U.S.C. § 3663A.” (Opp., 16: 11-16.)

 

            In sum, Plaintiff has failed to present authority to show that he was entitled to introduce “of-out-pocket expenses” as recoverable damages, such that the Court erred in its rulings on the Motions in Limine.

 

            As to the third contention, Plaintiff claims that the jury clearly erred in not awarding any emotional distress because there was uncontroverted evidence that Plaintiff suffered emotional distress.

 

            A jury is the sole judge of the credibility of a witness, and is free to disbelieve the witness and disregard the witness’s testimony, even where that testimony was uncontradicted, as long as there was any rational ground for doing do. (See e.g. In re Jessica C (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 1027, 1043; Schmidt v. Superior Court (2020) 44 Cal.App.5th 570, 582; Foreman & Clark Corp. v. Fallon (1971) 3 Cal.3d 875, 890.)

 

At trial, Plaintiff “testified that due to Defendant’s alleged conduct, [] he lost weight, does not talk to friends or anyone except his current attorney Chad Biggins, that he got held up for 2 hours probably 4 times he went through US Customs; that he does not travel as he does not want to deal with US Customs, has a financial hardship with getting a line of credit from Chinese manufacturers, and that he is scared of police officers or law enforcement, all of which he described as “really tough”. He claims these damages have occurred for seven years.” (Opp., 17: 11-16; Reporter’s Transcript, 128:10-25; 129:1-8; 134:3-18; 135:8-12; 147:4-21, attached as Exh. G.)

 

            However, Plaintiff’s testimony also “revealed that he has never consulted with any doctors or other medical care providers, nor has he ever taken medication, despite his testimony that his emotional distress has gone on for years, which he attempted to explain away by stating he does not like hospitals. (See June 7, 2023 Reporter’s Transcript, 147:16-28; 148:1-3, 18-23, attached as Exhibit “G”.). Additionally, Plaintiff did not present any evidence at trial that US Customs ever stopped him due to any conduct of Defendant, or that any alleged, unspecified financial issues with Chinese manufacturers were the result of any conduct of Defendants, including any evidence that Defendant had any communications with US Customs or any Chinese manufacturers. Defendant testified that he never contacted US Customs or any other authority with regarding to Plaintiff. (June 7, 2023 Reporter’s Transcript, 98:1-11.” (Opp., 17: 18-27.)

 

            As such, there was a rational basis for the jury’s unanimous decision that Plaintiff had not suffered any emotional distress damages.

 

            As for the final contention, Plaintiff argues that defense counsel showed the jury “pictures” which were not admitted into evidence, nor previously shown to Plaintiff’s counsel, and that this was highly prejudicial and contributed to decision to not award emotional distress damages.

 

            However, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence with respect to what pictures were shown to the jury, or described their content, or provided any other information about those pictures. As such, the Court has no basis from which to determine if the photos, in fact, caused prejudice requiring retrial. Second, Plaintiff does not offer any substantive argument as to how the photos were prejudicial. Even in reply, Plaintiff did not take the opportunity to address or compensate for this issue. Rather, Plaintiff’s response to this issue in full was “[f]inally, regarding emotional distress, there was no evidence controverting Mr. Chang’s testimony. The jury clearly erred in not awarding any emotional distress, caused largely in part due to defense counsel’s pictures shown at closing.” (Reply, 2: 23-25.)

 

            As such, the Court has no basis for concluding that pictures were presented which were so prejudicial that they in any way impacted the jury’s verdict.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for a new trial is denied.

 

 

It is so ordered.
