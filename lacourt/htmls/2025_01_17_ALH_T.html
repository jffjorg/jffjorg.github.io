DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV11599    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: T

Samuel Torres Chavez vs Anza Owners, LLC, et al.  


Motion to Bifurcate the Trial  

Moving Party:                         Plaintiffs Moises Lopez, Walter Lopez, and Beatriz Marin Sanchez

Responding Party:                   Defendants Anza Owner LLC and Coastline Real Estate Advisors, Inc.

Tentative Ruling:                    Grant

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a consolidated action involving two cases based on injuries from an alleged gas explosion at a construction site.

 

On March 30, 2020, Plaintiffs Moises Lopez, Walter Lopez, and Beatriz Marin Sanchez (“Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint for negligence under Labor Code section 3706 et seq., general negligence, premises liability, and loss of consortium. Plaintiffs allege that Moises Lopez and Walter Lopez were hired to do remodeling construction work at the subject premises owned by Anza Owner, LLC when they were injured from a gas explosion on April 8, 2018. (Complaint ¶ 23.)

 

Plaintiffs allege they were hired by co-defendant Fernando Garcia, doing business as Mathis Construction, who subcontracted to work on the project and was unlicensed. (Complaint ¶ 6.)

 

Plaintiffs now move to bifurcate the trial to litigate the issues of employer/employee status, lack of workers’ compensation coverage and the resulting applications of law pursuant to Labor Code sections 2750.5, 3706 and 3708.

 

Defendants Anza Owner LLC and Coastline Real Estate Advisors, Inc. (“Defendants”) oppose, and Plaintiffs reply.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Plaintiffs argue the trial should be bifurcated because they were statutory employees under Labor Code section 2750.5, and therefore, Labor Code section 3706 will allow Plaintiffs to continue this action if it is shown that the defendants did not provide workers’ compensation coverage. Furthermore, Plaintiffs argue defendants in this case must meet a different burden under Labor Code section 3708. Because this burden does not apply to Plaintiff Samuel Torres Chavez (“Chavez”) in the other consolidated case, and Chavez has not alleged he was a statutory employee, the issue of employment status and workers’ compensation coverage should be tried separately. Plaintiffs contend the relevant facts on this issue will be undisputed and can be decided as questions of law. (Motion, at p. 9.)

 

Plaintiffs contend that not bifurcating the issue will confuse the jury, given the different burden under section 3708.  

 

Plaintiffs propose bifurcating this issue into two phases: (1) deciding employee status and workers’ compensation coverage; and (2) proceeding to a jury to decide remaining issues of fact such as if defendants have overcome the section 3708 presumption of negligence. 

 

OPPOSITION

           

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ motion fails to show that bifurcation would lead to economy and efficiency, promote the convenience of witnesses, and avoid delay or prejudice.

 

Defendants also assert that Moises Lopez caused the incident in this case.

 

REPLY

 

Plaintiffs again assert that the trial must be bifurcated for the Court to make findings of law and argue they will be prejudiced if the motion is denied.  

 

ANALYSIS

I.                   Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 1048 provides, “The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial . . . of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (b).)

 “The court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 598.) “The authority of the court to bifurcate a trial of the issues in a case is conferred and limited by the provisions of” section 598. (Cohn v. Bugas (1974) 42 Cal.App.3d 381, 386.)

II.        Discussion

Labor Code section 2750.5 provides “[t]here is a rebuttable presumption affecting the burden of proof that a worker performing services for which a license is required . . . or who is performing such services for a person who is required to obtain such a license is an employee rather than an independent contractor.”¿Additionally, “any person performing any function or activity for which a license is required pursuant to Chapter 9 [commencing with Section 7000] of Division 3 of the Business and Professions Code shall hold a valid contractors’ license as a condition of having independent contractor status.” (Lab. Code, § 2750.5, subd (c).)  

“The language of section 2750.5 could not be clearer. The plain meaning of the penultimate paragraph of section 2750.5 conditions a finding of independent contractor status as to a person performing services for which a contractor's license is required upon possession by that person of a valid license. By stating that a license is a condition of the status, the Legislature has unequivocally stated that the person lacking the requisite license may not be an independent contractor. Thus, if one performs work on a construction job for which a license is required, without holding a valid license, one is by definition an employee, not an independent contractor, pursuant to section 2750.5.”¿

(Neighbours v. Buzz Oates Enterprises (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 325, 330 [internal citations and quotations omitted].)

Further, “[u]nder the plain meaning of section 2750.5, any unlicensed subcontractor is the employee of the general contractor; consequently, as a matter of law, the employee of an unlicensed subcontractor is the employee of the principal contractor.” (Neighbours, supra, 217 Cal.App.3d at 330.) “Since the employee of an unlicensed contractor is the employee of the principal contractor as a matter of law, workers' compensation is, with limited exceptions, this employee's exclusive remedy against the principal contractor when the employee is injured on the job.” (Id. [emphasis added].)

Here, Plaintiffs allege, and argue that Garcia, who was doing business as Mathis Construction, hired them to work on the project, and was unlicensed. (Complaint ¶ 6.) Plaintiffs also show they have specifically alleged negligence under Labor Code sections 3706 et seq. (Complaint ¶ 33, 42-43.) Labor Code section 3706 states: “If any employer fails to secure the payment of compensation, any injured employee or his dependents may bring an action at law against such employer for damages, as if this division did not apply.” (Lab. Code, § 3706.)

Section 3708 states in relevant part:

“In such action it is presumed that the injury to the employee was a direct result and grew out of the negligence of the employer, and the burden of proof is upon the employer, to rebut the presumption of negligence. It is not a defense to the employer that the employee was guilty of contributory negligence, or assumed the risk of the hazard complained of, or that the injury was caused by the negligence of a fellow servant. No contract or regulation shall restore to the employer any of the foregoing defenses.”

(Lab. Code, § 3708.)

Given the allegations in this case, the Court concludes that the trial should be bifurcated on the limited issue of determining whether Plaintiffs are statutory employees under Labor Code section 2750.5, and if so, whether the workers’ compensation exclusivity applies. Doing so would promote efficiency since these issues could preclude the necessity for a trial on the remaining issues in this case. As a result, contrary to Defendants’ assertions in opposition, it does not appear a bifurcation would inconvenience witnesses, create unnecessary costs, or prejudice the parties, since the inquiry would be limited; neither would this likely extend the trial for several days or weeks.

Therefore, the motion to bifurcate is granted.           

RULING

            Plaintiffs Moises Lopez, Walter Lopez, and Beatriz Marin Sanchez’s motion to Bifurcate the Trial is GRANTED.


Case Number: 21STCV25236    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: T

Bijan Torabi, et al. vs Raisa Zilberman


Motion to Set Aside Dismissal

Moving Party:                         Plaintiffs Bijan Torabi and Farideh Khorrami

Responding Party:                   None

Tentative Ruling:                    Grant

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 9, 2021, Plaintiffs Bijan Torabi and Farideh Khorrami (“Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint asserting negligence based on a motor vehicle accident. Trial was initially set for January 6, 2023.

 

At the trial on January 6, 2023, Plaintiffs did not appear, and no proofs of service of the summons and complaint were filed. As a result, the Court dismissed the complaint without prejudice under Code of Civil Procedure section 581(b)(3). (Min. Order, 1/6/23.)

 

On July 3, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a motion to set aside the dismissal which was granted on March 15, 2024. The Court also set an Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal for Failure to Serve Summons and Complaint for May 14, 2024. (Min. Order, 3/15/24.)

 

On May 14, 2024, the Court held an Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal for Failure to Serve Summons and Complaint. Plaintiffs did not appear, and seeing no proof of service of the summons and complaint, the Court dismissed the complaint without prejudice. (Min. Order, 5/14/24.)

 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion to set aside the dismissal under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b). No opposition has been filed.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Plaintiffs seek relief from the dismissal arguing attorney mistake and inadvertence.  

 

OPPOSITION


 No opposition was filed.

 

REPLY


No reply was filed.    

 

ANALYSIS

I.                   Legal Standard

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b), the Court may relieve a party from a dismissal taken against him through his mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  This application must be filed no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought, and must contain an affidavit of fault demonstrating the moving party’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)   

 

A mistake is a basis for relief under section 473 when by reason of the mistake a party failed to make a timely response.¿ Surprise occurs when a party is unexpectedly placed in a position to his injury without any negligence of his own. Excusable neglect is a basis for relief when the party has shown some reasonable excuse for the default.¿ (Credit Managers Association of California v. National Independent Business Alliance (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 1166, 1173; Davis v. Thayer (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 892, 905.)¿ Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, the moving party bears the burden of demonstrating an excusable ground, such as fraud or mistake, justifying a court’s vacating a judgment.¿ (Basinger v. Roger & Wells (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 16, 23–24.)¿¿¿ 

 

Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) However mandatory relief is only available when a party fails to oppose a dismissal motion (“which are procedurally equivalent to a default”). (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc.¿(2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 620.) The mandatory relief provision does not apply to dismissals for “failure to prosecute [citations omitted], dismissals for failure to serve a complaint within three years [citations omitted], dismissals based on running of the statute of limitations [citations omitted], and voluntary dismissals entered pursuant to settlement [citations omitted].” (Id.)  

 

II.        Discussion

            As an initial matter, this motion is timely because it was filed within six months after the complaint was dismissed.           

Here, Plaintiffs’ counsel Russel F. Behjatnia, declares the following: “[o]ur failure to appear was due to my office's inadvertence and mistake in calendaring the date of the matter. This mis-calendaring was not discovered until my office received notice of the court's order for dismissal. I apologize to the court for the inconvenience caused by this non-appearance and ask this court to excuse Plaintiff and I for this mishap.” (Behjatnia Decl. ¶ 4-5.)

            Based on this declaration, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have set forth sufficient grounds for relief based on mistake. Accordingly, the motion to set aside is granted.

RULING

             Plaintiffs Bijan Torabi and Farideh Khorrami’s motion to set aside dismissal is GRANTED.  The Court sets an OSC re Entry of Default for February 24, 2025 at 8:30 a.m.

Case Number: 21STCV37991    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: T

Zenya Dennis vs Adam and Lilith


Motion to Strike Portions of the Complaint

Moving Party:                         Defendant Omead Lavassani (Doe 5)

Responding Party:                   None

Tentative Ruling:                    Grant Without Leave to Amend

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 14, 2021, Plaintiff Zenya Dennis (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Adam and Lilith and Does 1 to 50 for negligence and premises liability. Plaintiff alleges that on October 16, 2019, she entered an Adam and Lilith store and was stabbed by a store employee. Plaintiff claims exemplary damages.

 

On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the complaint and substituted Omead Lavassani as Doe 5.

 

Defendant Omead Lavassani (“Defendant”) now moves to strike the allegations related to the exemplary damages claim.

 

On January 2, 2025, Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition to this motion.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Defendant asks the Court to strike the exemplary damages claim and various portions of the complaint. Defendant argues the complaint fails to allege sufficient facts to warrant punitive actions based on the acts of Defendant’s employee.

 

OPPOSITION

           

Plaintiff does not oppose this motion.

 

REPLY

 

            No reply has been filed.  

 

ANALYSIS

I.                   Legal Standard

A.        Motion to Strike

California law authorizes a party’s motion to strike matter from an opposing party’s pleading if it is irrelevant, false, or improper.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435; 436(a).)  Motions may also target pleadings or parts of pleadings which are not filed or drawn in conformity with applicable laws, rules or orders.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).)  A motion to strike is used to address defects that appear on the face of a pleading or from judicially noticed matter but that are not grounds for a demurrer.  (Pierson v Sharp Memorial Hospital (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 340, 342; see also City & County of San Francisco v Strahlendorf (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1911, 1913 (motion may not be based on a party's declaration or factual representations made by counsel in the motion papers).)  In particular, a motion to strike can be used to attack the entire pleading or any part thereof – in other words, a motion may target single words or phrases, unlike demurrers.  (Warren v. Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe Railway Co. (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 24, 40.)  The Code of Civil Procedure also authorizes the Court to act on its own initiative to strike matters, empowering the Court to enter orders striking matter “at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) 

B.        Striking Punitive Damages

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice” is conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).)  “‘Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.’  [Citation.]”  (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210.)    

“As amended to include [despicable], the [Civil Code section 3294] plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests.  The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.”  (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.) (emphasis added.)  The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.”  (Ibid.)  Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.  Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’”  (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.)  Further, “[t]here must be evidence that defendant acted with knowledge of the probable dangerous consequences to plaintiff’s interests and deliberately failed to avoid these consequences.”  (Flyer’s Body Shop Profit Sharing Plan v. Ticor Title Ins. Co. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 1149, 1155.) 

A motion to strike punitive damages is properly granted where a plaintiff does not state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, including allegations that defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.  (Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.)  “Mere negligence, even gross negligence, is not sufficient to justify such an award” for punitive damages.  (Kendall Yacht Corp. v. United California Bank (1975) 50 Cal.App.3d 949, 958.) Moreover, conclusory allegations are not sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages.  (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) Lastly, “[t]he mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.)

 

            C.        Punitive Damages from an Employer

 

When seeking damages from an employer, an employer is not liable for punitive damages arising from an employee’s actions pursuant to Civil Code section 3294(a) unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (b).) With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation. (Ibid.)

 

II.        Meet and Confer

According to the declaration of Kari A. Keidser, Defendant’s counsel sent an email inviting Plaintiff to meet and confer on this motion but received no response. (Keidser Decl. ¶ 8-9.)

III.       Discussion

            Here, the exemplary damages attachment to the complaint alleges that Defendants Adam & Lilith and Does 1 to 50,

“failed to train their employees how to handle altercations with a customer at the SUBJECT PREMISES. When said Defendants knew, or in the exercise of reasonable care should have known, that as a retail store there will be situations that pose risk of harm to customers on the premises; failed to warn Plaintiff Zenya Dennis, of the danger presented by the presence of the disagreements she would have with a vicious employee; failed to properly supervise, retrain, and hire employees who could adequately perform essential job duties including monitoring for disputes and how to handle them in a proper manner without the use of a deadly weapon (i.e. a knife); and failed to otherwise exercise due care with respect to the matters alleged in this complaint.”

            (Complaint, at p. 6.)

            The complaint fails to state sufficient facts to warrant punitive damages because it does not state facts that Defendant had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee who attacked Plaintiff and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct.

Therefore, due to the foregoing, the motion to strike is granted.

RULING

            Defendant Omead Lavassani’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND. The exemplary damages claim as to Omead Lavassani is stricken.
