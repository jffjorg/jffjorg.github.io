County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Plaintiffs,

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

FCA US LLC, a Delaware Limited Liability Company; and DOES 1-10, inclusive,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             December 20, 2024

 

Moving Parties:          Defendant FCA US LLC

 

Responding Party:      Plaintiffs Giuseppe Sanzone and Caroline Peterson

 

HEARING: Motion to Set Aside Order Striking Answer and Entering Default

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

RULING

 

            The Court grants the Motion to Set Aside the Order Striking the Answer and Entering Default.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On April 3, 2023, Plaintiffs Giuseppe Sanzone and Caroline Peterson filed the Complaint against Defendant FCA US LLC, alleging causes of action for violations of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

            On January 16, 2024, the Court ordered monetary sanctions of $2,500 against Defendant’s Counsel, payable within 20 days. The Court also ordered Defendant to provide verified amended or further responses without objection within 20 days. Neither of these orders were followed.

 

            On March 29, 2024, the Court ordered further monetary sanctions of $2,500 against Defendant’s Counsel, payable by April 19, 2024.

 

            On August 26, 2024, the Court granted additional monetary sanctions of $3,660 against counsel for Defendant, payable by September 13, 2024.

 

            On October 4, 2024, this Court struck the Answer of Defendant and issued an Order to Show Cause.

 

            On November 20, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion.

 

            On December 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On December 20, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

                       

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The court is authorized, after notice and opportunity for hearing, to impose the following sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process: monetary sanctions, issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, terminating sanctions, and contempt. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2023.030 subd. (a)-(e).) A terminating sanction may be imposed by an order dismissing the action against whom the sanction is imposed. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2023.030 subd. (d)(3).)  

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 2023.010, subdivision (g), provides that a misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.”  

 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc.¿(2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992; see J.W. v. Watchtower Bible and Tract Society of New York, Inc.¿(2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1142, 1169.) If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted. (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.) Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed what is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery. (Ibid.) “But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid., quoting Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280; Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar¿(2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702 (quoting same).)  

 

Because terminating sanctions are the most “drastic” penalty, they are typically a “last resort” to be “used sparingly”; however, they may still be appropriate “as a first measure” in “extreme cases” where a litigant violates a court order and “persists in the outright refusal to comply with [its] discovery obligations.” (Siry Investment, L.P. v. Farkhondehpour¿(2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 1098, 1118,¿as modified on denial of reh’g (Mar. 23, 2020).)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

             

On October 4, 2024, the Court granted a Motion to Strike the Answer and Enter Default (terminating sanctions.) An OSC for contempt and default judgment was set for November 15, 2024. At that hearing, the Court ordered any motion for relief to be heard on December 20, 2024. Defendant requests the Court vacate the prior order and reinstate Defendant’s answer.

 

Defendants argue that the Court's order imposing terminating sanctions against Defendant was improper because the sanctions arose from the attorneys’ failure to pay monetary sanctions. Defendant fully complied with the court's discovery orders, and the court never ordered Defendant to pay sanctions. Defendants contend that terminating sanctions cannot be imposed on Defendant for the fault of its attorneys, that dismissal is never an appropriate remedy for an attorney's failure to pay monetary sanctions, and that such an order would exceed the court's jurisdiction. (Jones v. Otero (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 755-756.)

 

Defendants further argue that the proper remedy for unpaid attorney sanctions is not terminating sanctions. Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subdivision (b) mandates relief when an attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect causes a default or dismissal. This provision protects innocent clients from the consequences of their attorneys' errors and shifts the burden to the attorney to seek relief. (Zamora v. Clayborn Contracting Group, Inc. (2002) 28 Cal.4th 249, 257.) Here, Defendant satisfies all the statutory requirements for mandatory relief because the sanctions arose solely from the attorney’s failure to act, and there is no evidence of Defendant's misconduct.

 

Finally, Defendants highlight that none of the two exceptions to mandatory relief under §473(b) apply here. The default was not caused by Defendant, but solely by the attorneys’ failure to pay sanctions, and there is no dispute about the attorneys' fault. Given the strong policy favoring relief for innocent clients, Defendants argue the order striking Defendant’s answer and entering default must be vacated, as it was issued in excess of the court’s jurisdiction.

 

Plaintiff argues that terminating sanctions against Defendant are justified because both Defendant and its attorneys are responsible for the discovery failures and noncompliance with court orders. While monetary sanctions were imposed specifically on Defendant’s counsel, Plaintiff emphasizes that Defendant itself failed to respond to written discovery and comply with multiple court orders. Plaintiff asserts that an attorney’s actions or inactions are imputed to the client, and a party cannot avoid sanctions by shifting blame to their attorney. (Carroll v. Abbott Laboratories, Inc. (1982) 32 Cal.3d 892, 898.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Jones is distinguishable because the court did not dismiss the case but struck Defendant’s Answer as a terminating sanction, which is permissible under California Code of Civil Procedure §2023.030(d). (Jones, supra, 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 759.) This section explicitly allows courts to impose terminating sanctions, including striking pleadings, when a party engages in misuse of the discovery process. Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s repeated failures to provide verified discovery responses and its violations of court orders constitute clear misuse of the discovery process.

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff highlights that this Court has already acknowledged Defendant’s failures independent of its counsel’s conduct. The January 16, 2024, Order explicitly required Defendant to provide verified responses, which it failed to do. The March 29, 2024, Order noted Defendant’s repeated failure to meet deadlines. Plaintiff argues that these failures demonstrate Defendant’s own culpability, not just its counsel’s.

 

In reply, Defendant highlights that the Court had previously denied two motions for terminating sanctions, recognizing that Defendant had complied with all discovery orders, albeit tardily. This is evident from the Court’s March 29, 2024, minute order, which explicitly stated that Defendant “did ultimately comply with the Court-ordered discovery.”(March 29, 2024, Minute Order.)

 

Defendant emphasizes that the basis for the terminating sanctions was counsel’s failure to pay the sanctions, not any misconduct by Defendant. The Court’s prior rulings consistently rejected terminating sanctions against Defendant, and the subsequent order striking Defendant’s answer was predicated solely on the actions of its attorneys.

 

In Jones, the Court found that “dismissal can never be an appropriate remedy for an attorney's noncompliance with an order imposing monetary sanctions.” (Jones, supra, 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 759.)

 

The Court’s March 29, 2024, Order notes that Defendant failed to abide by multiple deadlines and that while Defendant’s discovery abuse does not warrant terminating sanctions at the moment, it may in the future. (March 29, 2024, Minute Order.)

 

The Court’s August 2024 order notes that Defendant failed to pay the sanctions imposed at the order on March 29, 2024. (August 26, 2024, Minute Order.) The Order also required Defendant’s Counsel to pay further sanctions and set a compliance hearing for September 20, 2024. (August 26, 2024, Minute Order.) At the September 20, 2024, Compliance Hearing, the Court was informed that Defendant’s counsel had not paid the imposed sanctions, and thus, the Court scheduled the October 4, 2024, Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to Pay Monetary Sanctions; Why Court Should Not Terminate Sanctions, including striking the Answer; Contempt of Court hearing. (September 20, 2024, Minute Order) On October 4, 2024, Defendant failed to appear or pay the imposed sanctions, and thus, the Answer was struck, and Defendant entered default. (October 4, 2024.)

 

            Plaintiff argues that Jones is distinguishable because the Court has not ordered a dismissal, which is a terminating sanctions against plaintiffs. Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has also failed to follow past Court orders. However, the Court finds that Jones is controlling. The Court is not convinced by Plaintiffs argument regarding dismissal. It would be absurd to find that Jones is only applicable to terminating sanctions directed at plaintiffs and not terminating sanctions directed at defendants. Additionally, while Defendant has been late in responding to court-ordered discovery, the Court struck the Answer because Defendant's Counsel failed to pay the imposed sanctions or appear at the September 20, 2024, hearing not based on Defendant’s discovery abuses. Thus, the Court finds that the terminating sanctions were imposed for Defendant’s Counsel’s failure to pay sanctions, which Defendant has shown is beyond the power of the Court under Jones. (Jones, supra, 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 759.)

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Set Aside the Order Striking the Answer and Entering Default.

BACKGROUND

            On October 25, 2023, Plaintiffs propounded on Defendant the First Set of Form and Special Interrogatories.

            On December 28, 2023, Plaintiffs sent Defendant a meet and confer letter.

 

            On July 10, 2024, Defendant served its further responses to Plaintiffs’ Form and Special Interrogatories. 

 

            On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motions to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Form and Special Interrogatories.

 

            On October 7, 2024, Defendant filed their Opposition.

 

            On October 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Reply.

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs satisfied the meet and confer requirements as evidenced by Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration, wherein he states that he met and conferred with Defendant on July 29, 2024, regarding the Request for Documents, Set One, and received no response. (Thomas ¶ 16.)

 

B.    Procedural Defects

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ motion is procedurally defective and should be denied.

 

            Notice

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiffs' Notice of Motion fails to meet the procedural requirements. Specifically, it asserts that the notice does not adequately specify the grounds for the motion, nor does it identify the specific interrogatories in dispute or explain the deficiencies in Defendant’s responses. However, the Court finds that the notice requirement is satisfied because Plaintiffs' memorandum of points and authorities provides the necessary grounds for the motion. The memorandum outlines the alleged deficiencies in Defendant’s responses, including the failure to provide Code-compliant responses as required by the California Code of Civil Procedure, and identifies the specific requests in question. This provides sufficient notice to both the Defendant and the Court regarding the issues being raised.

 

            California Rules of Court 3.1113 (b)

 

            Defendant argues that California Rules of Court 3.1113(b) requires that a memorandum supporting a motion include a statement of facts, a concise statement of the law, evidence, arguments, and a discussion of the legal authorities cited. (Cal Rules of Court 3.1113(b).) Plaintiffs bear the burden of proving good cause and justifying the discovery requests with specific facts. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (Guess?, Inc.) (2002) 95 Cal.App. 92, 98.)

 

Defendant argues that instead of addressing the merits of their motion and specific interrogatories (Nos. 2-12, 15-27, 29-30, 40-41, and 43-44), Plaintiffs merely assert that Defendant has not provided complete responses without further elaboration. Thus, the motion should be denied.

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s assertion, that their Memorandum does not comply with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1113(b), is unfounded. Rule 3.1113(b) requires a memorandum to contain a statement of facts, legal arguments, and supporting citations, all of which Plaintiffs assert they provided. They contend that Defendant’s supplemental responses to various Special Interrogatories and Form Interrogatory 15.1 referenced unspecified documents, which Plaintiffs argue does not meet the Code requirements for specificity. Plaintiffs addressed this briefly in their motion, as they believed it was a straightforward compliance issue. Additionally, Plaintiffs assert that Defendant failed to produce promised documents for several Special Interrogatories and did not provide the ordered supplemental responses to specific Interrogatories, despite assurances to the Court on multiple occasions and an order following the June 21, 2024, IDC. Plaintiffs maintain that these issues were sufficiently detailed in their original Memorandum, underscoring that Defendant did not fulfill its commitments.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have complied with Rules of Court 3.1113(b). Plaintiffs’ memo contains a statement of facts, a brief statement of the law, arguments, evidence, and analysis. (Motion to Compel further Responses to Special Interrogatories p. 1-5.)

 

            Separate Statement

 

            Defendants argue that the Plaintiffs' Separate Statement fails to meet the requirements of California Rules of Court rule 3.1345(c), for several reasons. First, Plaintiffs did not include the verbatim responses or objections from Defendant, as required. Instead, they provided summaries or paraphrases, which do not fully inform the court of the actual content of the objections and responses. Second, Plaintiffs failed to offer a sufficient explanation as to why Defendant’s objections were invalid or why their responses were incomplete. Their reasoning is deemed conclusory and lacks the specific analysis needed to show how the responses fail to comply with legal requirements. Third, Plaintiffs omitted the necessary definitions, instructions, or contextual details to help the court understand the scope of the discovery requests and responses, thereby undermining the clarity of their arguments. Lastly, Plaintiffs improperly referenced prior responses by Defendant rather than setting them out verbatim, in violation of the rule prohibiting incorporation by reference from other documents. According to Defendants, these procedural deficiencies constitute violations of CRC 3.1345(c) and provide sufficient grounds for the court to deny the Plaintiffs' motion.

 

Plaintiffs argue that they submitted a concise outline in lieu of a separate statement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300 subdivision (b)(2). Furthermore, Plaintiffs state that they do not take issue with any of Defendants objections.

 

“The Court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., 2030.300 subd. (b)(2).) Here, Plaintiff has provided a concise outline that lists every response in dispute. (SROG Concise Outline p. 2-5.) The Court finds this to be sufficient and will not deny the motion on this procedural ground.

 

Merits

 

            Form Interrogatories

 

            In response to the form interrogatories Defendant served an 853-page pdf of documents. In response to Form Interrogatory 15.1, which requests factual information, witness contacts, and supporting documents for each denial, special defense, and affirmative defense asserted by Defendant, Defendant merely referenced unspecified documents in the 853-page PDF. Plaintiff requests that the Court compel Defendant to provide a code-compliant, verified supplemental response to Form Interrogatory 15.1.

 

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendant has failed to properly respond to Form Interrogatory 15.1, which seeks detailed factual information, witness contacts, and supporting documents for each denial, special defense, and affirmative defense asserted by Defendant. Plaintiffs contend that Defendant misunderstood or ignored the requirements of Form Interrogatories, drafted by the Judicial Council, by giving a single, broad response for all 34 affirmative defenses without specifying facts, witness information, or documents. Plaintiffs assert that Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.220(a) requires responses to be complete and specific, and Defendant’s approach violates this requirement.

 

            Defendant argues that its response to Interrogatory No. 15.1 is both appropriate and sufficient, especially given the current stage of discovery and the broad scope of the interrogatory. Defendant states that Form Interrogatory No. 15.1 seeks detailed information on all affirmative defenses, including associated facts, documents, and witnesses, which is overly broad and vague. Defendant further contends that it provided a substantive response, addressing key aspects of its defenses, such as the lack of defect in the vehicle, the adequacy of repair attempts, and Plaintiff's rejection of a repurchase offer.

 

Defendant emphasizes that discovery is ongoing, with depositions and vehicle inspections yet to occur, which limits the ability to give a complete response at this time. Defendant also invoked Code of Civil Procedure Section 2030.230, allowing reference to already-produced documents containing the requested information, which they argue fulfills the legal requirement for specificity.

 

Defendant concludes that the response provided is detailed enough for the current stage of discovery and will be supplemented as discovery continues, arguing that Plaintiffs’ demand for further responses is premature and does not acknowledge the progressive nature of the discovery process.

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.220 subdivision (a) states that “Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.220, subd. (a).) Here, Defendant's response is not compliant with Section 2030.220(a) as Defendant has failed to give complete and straightforward answers regarding the facts, witnesses, and documents related to Defendant's affirmative defenses. Furthermore, while Defendant cites Code of Civil Procedure Section 2030.230 as justification for its response, however, Section 2030.230 requires that specification of already produced documents “be in sufficient detail to permit the propounding party to locate and to identify, as readily as the responding party can, the documents from which the answer may be ascertained.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.) The Court finds that merely referring to an 852-page PDF does not satisfy Section 2030.230.

 

            Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatory 15.1

           

            Special Interrogatories

 

            In response to Special Interrogatories 2-7, 10-12, 15, and 16, Defendant referenced unspecified documents it claimed to have produced along with its response to Form Interrogatory 15.1.  In response to Special Interrogatories 8, 17-23, 25-27, 43, and 44, Defendant promised to produce certain documents subject to a protective order.  Defendant provided no response at all to Special Interrogatories 24, 29-30, or 40-41. Plaintiff requests Code-compliant responses to Special Interrogatories 2-12, 15-27, 29-30, 40-41, and 43-44.

 

            SROGs 2-7, 10-12, and 15-16

           

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s responses to Special Interrogatories 2-7, 10-12, 15, and 16 all reference unspecified documents Defendant may have produced, which does not comply with Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.230

 

            Defendants argue that their responses are complete as they provide all facts necessary and point to the documents produced as part of the 853-page PDF. However, Defendant has not provided in sufficient detail the documents from which the answer may be ascertained as to each Special Interrogatory. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Further Responses to SROG’s 2-7, 10-12, and 15-16.

 

            SROGs 8, 17-23, 25-27, and 43-44.

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant promised to produce SROGs 8, 17-23, 25-27, and 43-44 under a protective order but has failed to do so. However, Defendants produced the documents on September 25, 2024. (Hassantash Decl. ¶ 11.)

 

            Therefore, the Court Denies the Motion to Compel Further Responses to SROG’s 8, 17-23, 25-27, and 43-44 as moot.

 

            SROGs 4, 29-30 40-41

 

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendant failed to respond to Special Interrogatories 24, 29-30, and 40-41, thereby waiving its objections under Section 2030.290(a). Plaintiffs anticipate that Defendant will claim its November 28, 2023, responses are sufficient but assert that Defendant’s counsel committed to providing supplemental responses to all discovery in court appearances on June 21 and July 9, 2024, deadlines that Defendant did not meet. Plaintiffs contend that Defendant should be held accountable for these representations and omissions and be ordered to produce responses to the specified interrogatories without objections.

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ motion is procedurally improper and untimely, as it was filed on August 26, 2024, 229 days after the extended deadline of April 19, 2024, to compel further responses. Under Code Civil Procedure section 2030.300, any motion to compel must be filed within 45 days of receiving responses, which Plaintiffs missed. Defendant contends that its initial objection-only responses, served on November 28, 2023, were legitimate and that supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories 24, 29-30, and 40-41 were provided on July 10, 2024, consistent with courtroom discussions. Defendant asserts that it never agreed to provide further responses for every discovery request, nor did it extend any response deadlines beyond April 19, 2024.

 

Defendant also highlights that the scheduling of an Informal Discovery Conference does not extend the filing deadline for a motion to compel, per courtroom rules and Local Rule Appendix 3A. Defendant contends Plaintiffs waived their right to compel further responses by missing the April deadline, making the current motion baseless. The deadline to file this motion is mandatory, and Defendant asserts that the Court lacks authority to extend this deadline, warranting denial of Plaintiffs' motion regarding the specified interrogatories.

 

In the reply, Plaintiffs argue that the Court should grant the Motion despite its untimeliness because Defendant allegedly lied to Plaintiffs and the Court regarding Defendant’s willingness to provide further responses to all special interrogatories. In the alternative, the Plaintiffs request the Court construe the current motion as one to compel compliance with the Court’s June 21, 2024, Order. However, Plaintiff provides no authority under which the Court can grant either of their requests.

 

Therefore, the Court denies the Motion to Compel Further Responses as to SROGs 4, 29-30, and 40-41 as untimely.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.300(d) and 2031.310(h) state that the court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories or demands for production unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (d); Code Civ. Proc., 2031.310 subd. (h).)

 

Plaintiffs request sanctions in the amount of $3,975.00 regarding the SROGs and $3,390.00 regarding the form interrogatories. Defendants request sanctions in the amount of $900 for opposing the motion regarding the SROGs and $900 for opposing the motion regarding the form interrogatories.

 

The Motion to Compel Further Responses to SROGs was granted in part and denied in part, and thus, the court refuses to award sanctions for either party, as the motion was partially successful, but the partial denial shows that Defendant was justified in opposing the motion.

 

As to the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, the Motion to Compel was granted, and thus sanctions are warranted. However, the Court finds Plaintiffs request for sanctions excessive. Plaintiff's counsel states that they spent 2.9 hours drafting the motion, 3 hours drafting the reply, and 1.5 hours preparing for the hearing, billed at 450$ an hour plus a $60 filing fee totaling to $3,390. Plaintiffs Motion only regarded one form interrogatory. Therefore, the Court lowers the time spent to .5 hours on the motion, .5 hours on the reply, and .5 hours on the hearing, plus the filing fee, equaling sanctions in the amount of $735.

 

Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiffs request for sanctions in the amount of $735.

RULING

 

The Court denies the Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admission Admitted.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On May 3, 2024, Plaintiffs Serina Reed and Mitchell Reed filed the Complaint alleging a single cause of action for Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Beach of Express Warranty.

 

            On October 25, 2023, Plaintiffs propounded on Defendant a First Set of Requests for Admissions.

 

            On November 28, 2023, Defendant served responses that objected to all of Plaintiffs’ Requests for Admissions.

 

            On December 28, 2023, Plaintiffs sent Defendant a meet and confer letter.

 

            On June 21, 2024, Defendant promised to serve supplemental discovery responses by July 3, 2024.

 

            On July 10, 2024, Defendant served its further responses to Plaintiffs’ Requests for Admissions. Defendant provided no response at all to RFAs 23, 33, or 34.

 

            On July 29, 2024, Plaintiffs sent Defendant a meet and confer letter.

 

            On August 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion.

 

            On October 3, 2024, Defendant filed the Opposition.

 

            On October 10, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

 

            Trial is set for April 7, 2025.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)

If a party to whom a request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including those based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted and for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance before the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

           

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiffs request that the Court deem RFAs 23, 33, and 34 admitted.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the Discovery Act entitles them to obtain discovery responses to all RFAs. If a party fails to respond within 30 days, they waive any objections, including those based on privilege. (Code Civ Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) Plaintiffs assert that if a party fails to respond timely, the requesting party can move for an order deeming the matters admitted, without a time limit on filing or a requirement to meet and confer before doing so. (Code Civ Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) Plaintiffs expect Defendant to argue that its responses from November 28, 2023, are sufficient, but they contend that Defendant’s counsel previously represented in court appearances that it would serve supplemental responses to all discovery by specific deadlines, which were not met. Plaintiffs argue that Defendant should be held accountable for its counsel's representations and ordered to respond to RFAs 23, 33, and 34.

 

However, a Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admission Admitted may only be granted if the opposing party fails to file a response to a request for admission. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) Here, Defendant served responses on November 28, 2023. (Thomas Decl. ¶ 7, Exhibit. B.) While Plaintiff argues that Defendant should be held accountable for its promises, the Court does not have the power to grant the instant motion based on Defendant’s alleged representations. Plaintiffs also argue that Defendant likely violated their Duty of Candor toward the Tribunal under Rule of Professional Conduct 3.3; however, even if Defendant breached these duties, the Court would not be able to grant the instant motion.

 

Therefore, because Defendant served responses to Plaintiff's RFAs, the Court denies the Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admission Admitted.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.300(d) and 2031.310(h) state that the court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories or demands for production unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (d); Code Civ. Proc., 2031.310 subd. (h).)

 

Here, both parties request that sanctions be imposed. However, the Court exercises its discretion and refuses to impose any sanctions. Sanctions cannot be imposed against Defendant because they were successful in their opposition to Plaintiff’s motion. Furthermore, the Court refuses to impose sanctions on Plaintiff based on Defendant’s representation that it would provide further responses.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            The Court may take judicial notice of records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States. (Evid. Code § 452 subd.             Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of the Substitution of Trustee recorded on November 17, 2021, in the Los Angeles County Recorder's Office, the Substitution of Trustee recorded on July 24, 2023, in the Los Angeles County Recorder's Office, the eligibility guidelines for the California Housing Finance Agency's Mortgage Relief Program as published by the U.S. Treasury, and the 2019 national conforming loan limit values by county for mortgages that finance single-family one-unit properties as published by the Federal Housing Finance Agency.

 

            The Court takes judicial notice of the Substitution of Trustee documents pursuant to Evidence Code section 452 subdivision (c). The Court also takes judicial notice of the eligibility guidelines for the California Housing Finance Agency's Mortgage Relief Program and the 2019 national conforming loan limit values by county for mortgages that finance single-family one-unit properties, pursuant to Evidence Code section 452 subdivision (h). (See, Ampex Corp. v. Cargle (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1569, 1573.)

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Defendant NewRez LLC dba Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing demurs to the Complaint.

 

On May 7, 2019, Plaintiff obtained a $1,060,000 loan secured by a Deed of Trust on a property located at 9633 Crenshaw Blvd., Inglewood, CA. On December 13, 2020, the beneficial interest in the Deed of Trust was assigned to Great Lake Funding I Trust, U.S. Bank Trust National Association as trustee.

 

In December 2021, Plaintiff defaulted on her loan obligations, and a Notice of Default was recorded by Quality Loan Service Corporation on December 14, listing arrearages of $127,600. This Notice of Default was rescinded on December 22, 2022.

 

In July 2023, ZBS Law LLP was substituted as the foreclosure trustee, and on October 16, 2023, ZBS recorded a new Notice of Default indicating arrearages of $62,636.61. When Plaintiff did not cure the default, a Notice of Trustee's Sale was recorded on February 29, 2024, with a scheduled sale date of March 21, 2024.

 

In June 2023, Plaintiff learned of the California Mortgage Relief Program, which aids homeowners by covering past-due mortgage payments if arrearages are under $80,000 and the unpaid principal balance at loan origination is below the conforming loan limit of $726,525.

 

Plaintiff alleges that her arrearages were around $30,000 in June 2023. She applied to the program in July 2023, and her application was approved. Plaintiff claims Defendant needed to approve the application for the program, delayed in doing so, and that by the time they responded, her arrearages had risen to $80,000, leading her to assert, that Defendant ultimately denied the application.

 

Meet and Confer 

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) 

 

            On September 16, 2024, Defendants met and conferred Plaintiff via phone. (Jindal Decl. ¶ 4.) The Court finds this is sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement.

 

Cause of Action 1 – Violation of Civil Code Section 2924(a)(6)

           

Civil Code Section 2924 subdivision (a)(6) reads, “An entity shall not record or cause a notice of default to be recorded or otherwise initiate the foreclosure process unless it is the holder of the beneficial interest under the mortgage or deed of trust, the original trustee or the substituted trustee under the deed of trust, or the designated agent of the holder of the beneficial interest. An agent of the holder of the beneficial interest under the mortgage or deed of trust, original trustee, or substituted trustee under the deed of trust shall not record a notice of default or otherwise commence the foreclosure process except when acting within the scope of authority designated by the holder of the beneficial interest.” (Civ. Code § 2924.12 subd. (a)(6).)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff's claim alleging a violation of Civil Code section 2924 subdivision (a)(6) fails because there is no private right of action under that section. The Legislature specifically outlined which sections of the Homeowner Bill of Rights (HBOR) allow homeowners to seek remedies for violations. Civil Code section 2924.12(b) permits homeowners to recover damages for violations of seven specific HBOR sections after a foreclosure sale: Civil Code sections 2923.55, 2923.6, 2923.7, 2924.9, 2924.10, 2924.11, and 2924.17. (Civ. Code § 2924.12 subd. (b).) Furthermore, Civil Code section 2924.12(a) allows homeowners to seek injunctive relief for violations of the same seven sections if a foreclosure sale has not yet occurred. (Civ. Code § 2924.12 subd. (a).) Section 2924(a)(6) is notably absent from this list, indicating that the Legislature did not intend for it to be enforceable through a private cause of action. Defendant contends that the Legislature’s omission of section 2924(a)(6) from the enforceable sections means that no action can be brought under it. (Gomes v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1155.)  Therefore, Defendant argues that the demurrer to this claim should be sustained without leave to amend.

           

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Courts have consistently allowed claims that challenge whether the entity initiating foreclosure has the proper authority under the HBOR. However, Plaintiff does not provide any such cases. Last, Plaintiff argues that this is a substantive issue and not a procedural issue, as it directly impacts the legality of the foreclosure proceedings.

 

            In Gomes, the Court of Appeal found that Civil Code Section 2924 subdivision (a) does not provide for a private cause of action because “nowhere does the statute provide for a judicial action to determine whether the person initiating the foreclosure process is indeed authorized, and we see no ground for implying such an action.” (Gomes, supra, 192 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1155.) In her opposition, Plaintiff provides no rebuttal to Gomes. Thus, the Court finds that there is no private right of action under Civil Code section 2924 subdivision (a)(6). Subsequently, Plaintiff’s claim fails as a matter of law.

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the first cause of action without leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 2 – Violation of Civil Code Section 2924.17

 

            Civil Code Section 2924.17 requires that a notice of default, notice of sale, assignment of a deed of trust, or substitution of trustee be accurate, complete, and supported by reliable evidence. (Civ. Code § 2924.17)

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s second claim, alleging a violation of Section 2924.17, is unfounded. Plaintiff claims that the 2023 Notice of Default and 2024 Notice of Trustee's Sale were improperly recorded by ZBS as the foreclosure trustee, asserting that ZBS had not been legally substituted as trustee under the Deed of Trust at that time. However, Defendant points out that a Substitution of Trustee was recorded on July 24, 2023, designating ZBS as the foreclosure trustee. Therefore, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s claim is invalid and should be dismissed, as the recorded documents confirm ZBS’s authority to act as trustee.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the second cause of action for violation of Civil Code 2924.17 should not be dismissed, asserting that the foreclosure documents were filed without the necessary due diligence by Defendant.  Plaintiff’s complaint claims that the 2023 Notice of Default and 2024 Notice of Trustee's Sale was recorded without proper verification, as required by Civil Code 2924.17, because ZBS was not legally substituted as a trustee at the time of filing. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends that the earlier Notice of Default filed by Quality Loan Service Corporation was invalid for the same reason.

 

            The Court has taken notice of the Substitution of Trustee dated November 5, 2021, showing that Quality Loan Service Corporation was transferred trusteeship from Fidelity National Title Company and Great Lake Funding I Trust. (Request for Judicial Notice Exhibit 1.) The Court has also taken notice of the Substitution of Trustee dated July 18, 2023, showing that ZBS was transferred trusteeship. (Request for Judicial Notice Exhibit 2.)

 

            A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar, supra, 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) Here, the judicially noticed documents show that there was a valid transfer of trusteeship to Quality Loan Service Corporation and, later, to ZBS. Thus, Plaintiff’s contentions that both Notices of Default and Notices of Trustee Sale were improperly filed without authorization are unfounded.

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the second cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 3 – Tortious Interference with a Contractual Relationship

 

            To establish liability for tortious interference with contractual relations, a plaintiff must prove "(1) it has a valid and existing contract with a third party; (2) defendants had knowledge of the contract; (3) defendants committed an intentional act designed to induce a breach or disrupt the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contract relationship occurred; and (5) damages were suffered as a result." (Quelimane Co. Inc. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 55.)

 

            Defendant asserts that Plaintiff fails to establish a claim of tortious interference with a contractual relationship because she does not allege a valid and enforceable contract with CalHFA that Defendant interfered with.

 

While Plaintiff claims she was "approved" by CalHFA for assistance in June 2023, she also states that Defendant later denied her application when her arrearages exceeded $80,000, which contradicts the existence of an enforceable contract. (Comp. ¶ 28.) Furthermore, Defendant highlights that Plaintiff could not have qualified for a contract with CalHFA since the program’s guidelines require the original loan balance to be under the conforming loan limit, which was $726,525 in 2019. Plaintiff’s loan was over $1 million at origination, making her ineligible for the program. Therefore, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claim for interference with contractual relations lacks the necessary foundation of a valid contract and should be dismissed.

Plaintiff argues that her third cause of action for tortious interference with contractual relations against Defendant should not be dismissed. Defendant claims Plaintiff lacks a valid, enforceable contract with CalHFA; however, Plaintiff contends that her approval for assistance under the California Mortgage Relief Program by CalHFA created a legitimate expectation of performance, even without a formal contract.

 

Plaintiff argues that interference with prospective contractual relations can exist if there is a probable economic benefit, even if the contract is not finalized. (Quelimane Co. Inc, supra, 19 Cal.4th 26; Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. Bear Stearns & Co. (1990) 50 Cal.3d 1118, 1126.) Plaintiff’s approval by CalHFA represented a prospective contract, and Defendant’s alleged interference disrupted her ability to benefit from the program, fulfilling the elements of tortious interference.

 

Defendant’s argument about Plaintiff's loan exceeding the program’s conforming limit does not bar her claim. Plaintiff argues that interference may still apply even if a final agreement was not reached or eligibility requirements were unmet. (Crown Imports, LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1405.) Plaintiff states she was initially approved, and any issues related to the loan limit only arose after Defendant's interference, which caused her arrearages to exceed the program's limit. Plaintiff claims Defendant’s actions disrupted her ability to complete the program and obtain mortgage relief, supporting her claim for tortious interference.

 

Intentional interference with a contractual relationship requires a valid and existing contract with a third party. (Quelimane Co. Inc, supra, 19 Cal. 4th 26, 55.) While Plaintiff cites Quelimane and Pacific Gas in asserting that interference with prospective contractual relationships can occur even if the contract is not finalized, neither case supports this assertion. In Quelimane, the Court found that there was an existing contract between the parties, leading to the successful pleading of tortious interference with contractual relations, and the Court in Pacific Gas was analyzing the tort of interference with prospective economic advantage, not tortious interference with a contractual relationship. (Quelimane Co. Inc, supra, 19 Cal. 4th 26, 56; Pacific Gas & Electric Co., supra, 50 Cal.3d 1118, 1126.)

 

Here, the Complaint fails to plead any contract between Plaintiff and CalHFA, and thus, the cause of action for tortious interference with contractual relations fails as a matter of law,

 

Therefore, the Demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 4 – Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200

 

            California Business and Professions Code section 17200 prohibits “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.” (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200; see Clark v. Superior Court (2010) 50 Cal.4th 605, 610.)  A violation of other laws is deemed independently actionable under the UCL. (See Law Offices of Mathew Higbee v. Expungement Assistance Services (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 544, 554.)  “‘Virtually any law, federal, state or local, can serve as a predicate for a section 17200 action.’” (Id. (quoting Troyk v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1335).)  To establish a fraudulent practice under the UCL, the plaintiff must show that members of the public are likely to be deceived.  (See West v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 806.)  “A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.”  (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1473.)  Further, Statutory claims must be pled with particularity.  (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 410.)

 

            The Complaint pleads that “the conduct and business practices of Defendants, and their agents, alleges[sic] herein, constituted unfair and unlawful business practices within the provisions of California Business & Professions Code § 17200 et seq” (Complaint ¶ 84.)

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff lacks standing under the Unfair Competition Law because she has not demonstrated an "injury in fact" involving a loss of money or property directly caused by Defendant's actions, as required by Business and Professions Code § 17204. Plaintiff only alleges increased loan arrears and incurred costs without clarifying how these damages were caused by Defendant's conduct. Defendant points out that Plaintiff's financial losses stem from her own loan default, not Defendant’s actions. Defendant argues that borrowers who default prior to the alleged wrongful acts lack standing to sue under Section 17200, as the default, rather than any alleged misconduct, initiates fees and foreclosure proceedings. (Jenkins v JP Morgan Chase Bank N.A (2013) 216 Cal.App. 4th 497, 522-523.) Consequently, Plaintiff’s failure to meet the UCL standing requirement renders her claim legally insufficient.

 

            Furthermore, under the "fraudulent" prong, Plaintiff must show that Defendant’s practices were likely to deceive the public, which Plaintiff has not alleged, nor has she demonstrated actual reliance on any alleged fraudulent conduct. (In re Tobacco II Cases, 46 Ca1.4th 298, 312.) Regarding the "unfair" prong, Defendant’s conduct must violate a public policy connected to specific legal provisions. Plaintiff has not successfully linked her UCL claim to any HBOR violation, and, according to Defendant, she lacks grounds to maintain any HBOR claim. For the "unlawful" prong, Plaintiff must establish a violation of another law as a basis for the UCL claim, but she has not provided facts supporting any statutory violation. (Berryman v. Merit Prop. Mgmt., Inc., 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1554.) Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s failure to establish unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent conduct justifies dismissal of her UCL claim.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that she has standing under the UCL, as the statute grants individuals standing if they suffered injury in fact, and lost money or property due to an unfair practice. Plaintiff alleges that Quality Loan Service Corporation and ZBS improperly recorded foreclosure notices without proper authority, violating California Civil Code sections 2924 and 2924.17.

 

Although Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s financial issues stem from her default, Plaintiff clarifies she is not disputing her default but rather the legality of Defendant’s foreclosure procedures, including the unlawful recording of Notice of Trustee Sale. Plaintiff argues that foreclosure irregularities tied to improper assignments or substitutions provide grounds for her UCL claim. Plaintiff concludes that the alleged foreclosure misconduct caused her significant financial harm, precisely the type of injury that UCL seeks to address, and therefore requests the court deny Defendant's Demurrer.

 

“Violation of another law is a predicate for stating a cause of action under the UCL's unlawful prong." (Berryman, supra, 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1554.)

 

            The Court has found that Plaintiff failed to state a cause of action under California Civil Code section 2924 or 2924.17, as the Court has taken judicial notice of valid Substitution of Trustee documents relating to both Quality Loan Servicing and ZBS. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to plead facts showing violation of another law as required to state a cause of action under Business and Professions Code § 17200. (Berryman, supra, 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1554.)

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the fourth cause of action with leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

 

            On May 21, 2024, Plaintiff Brasie Maria Thomas filed the Complaint against Defendants Specialized Loan Servicing LLC and ZBS Law LLP, alleging causes of action for violation of Civil Code § 2924(a)(6), violation of Civil Code § 2924.17, tortious interference with contractual relationships, and violation of Business and Professional Code § 17200 arising from an alleged unlawful foreclosure.

 

            On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff requested the Court grant a preliminary injunction restraining Defendants from foreclosing on Plaintiff's property located at 9633 Crenshaw Blvd, Inglewood, California.

 

            On November 4, 2024, Defendant NewRez LLC dba Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing LLC (erroneously sued as Specialized Loan Savings LLC) filed their opposition. The same day Defendant RF Mortgage Services Corporation (Doe 1) filed their opposition.

 

            Plaintiff has not filed a reply.

 

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The purpose of a preliminary injunction is to preserve the status quo pending a decision on the merits.  (Major v. Miraverde Homeowners Ass’n. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 618, 623.)  In deciding whether or not to grant a preliminary injunction, the court looks to two factors, including “(1) the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits, and (2) the relative balance of harms that is likely to result from the granting or denial of interim injunctive relief.”  (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 553-54.) The factors are interrelated, with a greater showing on one permitting a lesser showing on the other.  (Dodge, Warren & Peters Ins. Services, Inc. v. Riley (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1414, 1420.)  However, the party seeking an injunction must demonstrate at least a reasonable probability of success on the merits.  (IT Corp. v. County of Imperial (1983) 35 Cal.3d 63, 73-74.)  The party seeking the injunction bears the burden of demonstrating both a likelihood of success on the merits and the occurrence of irreparable harm.  (Savage v. Trammell Crow Co. (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 1562, 1571.) Irreparable harm may exist if the plaintiff can show an inadequate remedy at law.  (CCP § 526(a).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

             Plaintiff requests the Court grant a preliminary injunction restraining Defendants from foreclosing on Plaintiff's property located at 9633 Crenshaw Blvd, Inglewood, California.

 

On May 7, 2019, Plaintiff obtained a $1,060,000 loan secured by a Deed of Trust on property located at 9633 Crenshaw Blvd., Inglewood, CA. On December 13, 2020, the beneficial interest in the Deed of Trust was assigned to Great Lake Funding I Trust, U.S. Bank Trust National Association as trustee.

 

In December 2021, Plaintiff defaulted on her loan obligations, and a Notice of Default was recorded by Quality Loan Service Corporation on December 14, listing arrearages of $127,600. This Notice of Default was rescinded on December 22, 2022.

 

In July 2023, ZBS Law LLP was substituted as the foreclosure trustee, and on October 16, 2023, ZBS recorded a new Notice of Default indicating arrearages of $62,636.61. When Plaintiff did not cure the default, a Notice of Trustee's Sale was recorded on February 29, 2024, with a scheduled sale date of March 21, 2024.

 

In June 2023, Plaintiff learned of the California Mortgage Relief Program, which aids homeowners by covering past-due mortgage payments if arrearages are under $80,000 and the unpaid principal balance at loan origination is below the conforming loan limit of $726,525.

 

Plaintiff alleges that her arrearages were around $30,000 in June 2023. She applied to the program in July 2023, and her application was approved. Plaintiff claims Defendant needed to approve the application for the program, delayed in doing so, and that by the time they responded, her arrearages had risen to $80,000, leading her to assert, that Defendant ultimately denied the application.

 

Likelihood to Succeed on the Merits

 

            The party seeking an injunction must demonstrate at least a reasonable probability of success on the merits.  (IT Corp., supra, 35 Cal.3d 63, 73-74.)

 

            Cause of Action 1 - Violation of Civil Code Section 2924(a)(6)

 

            Plaintiff argues she will succeed on her claim for violation of California Civil Code 2924(a)(6), which states that only the holder of the beneficial interest, the original trustee, the substituted trustee, or a designated agent may record a notice of default or initiate foreclosure. Plaintiff claims that Defendants failed to comply with this code and wrongfully initiated foreclosure proceedings against her property, putting her and her family at risk of an imminent trustee sale. (Civ. Code § 2924 subd. (a)(6).)

 

Plaintiff explains that the Deed of Trust identifies Fidelity as the trustee, and on February 4, 2021, a substitution assigned the Deed of Trust’s beneficial interest to Great Lake Funding I Trust, U.S. Bank Trust National Association. However, Plaintiff alleges that ZBS Law LLP (ZBS), on behalf of Specialized Loan Servicing, recorded a Notice of Default on October 18, 2023, and a Notice of Trustee’s Sale on February 29, 2024, without authority. Plaintiff claims that Fidelity, not ZBS, was the only entity authorized to record foreclosure notices and that ZBS acted without valid authority.

 

As a result, Plaintiff argues she was prejudiced because she could not engage with the proper trustee to seek a postponement of the sale or loan reinstatement. Plaintiff claims further harm, including damage to her credit, imminent eviction, and a lost opportunity to modify her loan to save her home from foreclosure.

 

Plaintiff requests the court enjoin Defendants' scheduled foreclosure sale until they demonstrate compliance with Civil Code 2923.5, which allows injunctive relief when foreclosure procedures are improperly followed.

 

            Defendants have provided a Substitution of Trustee dated November 5, 2021, showing that Quality Loan Service Corporation was transferred trusteeship from Fidelity National Title Company and Great Lake Funding I Trust, and a later Substitution of Trustee dated July 18, 2023, showing that ZBS was transferred trusteeship. (Request for Judicial Notice Exhibit 1, 2.) Thus, Defendant has shown that ZBS was the trustee at the time of the Notice of Default and the Notice of Trustee Sale. Furthermore, the Court has found that Civil Code Section 2924(a)(6) does not grant a private right of action. (Gomes v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1155.)

 

            Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not shown a reasonable likelihood to succeed on the merits as to the first cause of action.

 

Cause of Action 2 – Violation of Civil Code Section 2924.17

 

Civil Code section 2924.17 requires that a notice of default, notice of sale, assignment of a deed of trust, or substitution of trustee be accurate, complete, and supported by reliable evidence. (Civ. Code § 2924.17)

 

Plaintiff argues she will succeed on her claim for violation of Civil Code section 2924.17. Plaintiff contends that Defendants failed to comply with the requirements of Civil Code section 2924.17, which has unlawfully jeopardized her interest in her home by initiating foreclosure without proper authority or evidence. She claims that Defendants, specifically ZBS, acting without proper authority, recorded a Notice of Default and Notice of Trustee’s Sale despite the substitution rights being assigned to Great Lake Funding I Trust, rendering the foreclosure notices void.

 

Plaintiff argues that, due to these violations and since her property has not yet been sold, she is entitled to injunctive relief to prevent Defendants from proceeding with the foreclosure sale while the action is pending.

 

            Defendants have provided a Substitution of Trustee dated November 5, 2021, showing that Quality Loan Service Corporation was transferred trusteeship from Fidelity National Title Company and Great Lake Funding I Trust, and a later Substitution of Trustee dated July 18, 2023, showing that ZBS was transferred trusteeship. (Request for Judicial Notice Exhibit 1, 2.) Thus, Defendant has shown that ZBS was the trustee at the time of the Notice of Default and the Notice of Trustee Sale.

           

            Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not shown a reasonable likelihood to succeed on the merits as to the second cause of action.

 

Cause of Action 3 – Tortious Interference with a Contractual Relationship

 

            To establish liability for tortious interference with contractual relations, a plaintiff must prove "(1) it has a valid and existing contract with a third party; (2) defendants had knowledge of the contract; (3) defendants committed an intentional act designed to induce a breach or disrupt the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contract relationship occurred; and (5) damages were suffered as a result." (Quelimane Co. Inc. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 55.)

 

            Plaintiff argues that she will prevail on her claim for tortious interference with contractual relations.  Plaintiff contends that specific intent is not required for this tort; instead, it suffices if Defendants knew that their actions were substantially certain to interfere with Plaintiff’s economic advantage. (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1154.)

 

Additionally, according to Pacific Gas & Electric Co., Plaintiff does not need to show an actual breach of contract, only that Defendants’ interference made her performance more burdensome. (Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. Bear Stearns & Co. (1990) 50 Cal.3d 1118, 1126.)

 

In late June 2023, Plaintiff discovered the California Mortgage Relief Program (CMRP), which provides mortgage relief up to $80,000. At the time, her past-due balance was about $30,000, making her eligible. She was informed by an agent from Defendant that she could either apply for a loan modification directly with Defendant or apply to the CMRP, but not both. Relying on assurances from Defendant’s agent, Plaintiff applied for CMRP and was approved. However, Defendant delayed responding to CMRP until Plaintiff’s arrears reached $80,000, resulting in her application’s denial. Plaintiff claims this disrupted her ability to pay the past due balance, sell her home, and avoid foreclosure.

 

Intentional interference with a contractual relationship requires a valid and existing contract with a third party. (Quelimane Co. Inc, supra, 19 Cal. 4th 26, 55.)  Here, Plaintiff has failed to show that a contract was ever formed between Plaintiff and the CMRP, as Plaintiff was never approved for the program.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not a reasonable likelihood to succeed on the merits as to the third cause of action.

 

            Cause of Action 4 – Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200

 

            Plaintiff argues that the trial court has broad authority to enjoin conduct violating Business and Professions Code 17200 (UCL), which prohibits any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts or practices. Under Section 17200, "unlawful" conduct includes practices forbidden by any law, civil, criminal, or regulatory. A business practice may be considered "unfair" if it violates the spirit or policy of established law. (People v. Casa Blanca Convalescent Homes, Inc. (1948) 159 Cal.App.3rd 509, 530.) Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s actions constitute unlawful conduct, as they have engaged in statutory violations directly causing the harm Plaintiff faces, unlawful foreclosure. Plaintiff asserts that Defendant’s foreclosure activities were inherently unfair, aiming to wrongfully foreclose on Plaintiff's property. Therefore, Plaintiff urges the Court to enjoin Defendant’s unlawful and unfair business practices associated with the foreclosure of her home under the UCL.

 

            “Violation of another law is a predicate for stating a cause of action under the UCL's unlawful prong." (Berryman v. Merit Prop. Mgmt., Inc., 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1554.)

 

            The Court has found that Plaintiff was unlikely to succeed on the causes of action it bases its UCL cause of action on. Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiff has failed to provide a violation of another law as required for a successful UCL claim. (Berryman, supra, 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1554.)

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not a reasonable likelihood to succeed on the merits as to the fourth cause of action.

 

            Subsequently, Plaintiff has not shown a “reasonable probability of success on the merits.  (IT Corp., supra, 35 Cal.3d 63, 73-74.) Therefore, the Court denies the Motion for a Preliminary Injunction.

The Court sustains the Plea in Abatement and stays the action pending disposition of MARQUISE LEWIS vs KELLY SERVICES GLOBAL, LLC. (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782.)

 

BACKGROUND

            On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff Orion Serup filed the Complaint against Defendant Canoo INC. alleging a PAGA claim for Violation of California Labor Code § 2698 arising from Plaintiff's time as an employee of Defendant.

 

            On October 11, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Special Demurrer to/Plea in Abatement of Plaintiffs Complaint.

 

            On October 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code Civil Procedure section 430.10(c) expressly provides that a party may demur where “there is another cause of action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action.”  (See People ex rel. Garamendi v. American Autoplan, Inc., (1993) 20 Cal. App. 4th 760, 770.)  The pendency of another action is classified as a plea in abatement and, as such, is “not favored.”  This disfavor is expressed in a number of rules: (1) The objection itself is strictly limited so that, whether raised by demurrer or answer, the defendant must show that the parties, the cause of action, and issues are identical and that the same evidence would support the judgment in each case. . . (2) demurrer will not be sustained if the complaint also shows that the former action is no longer pending. . . (3) even if objection is good demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend and the action should not be dismissed.”  (California Union Ins. Co. v Trinity River Land Co. (1980) 105 Cal App 3d 104, 108.)

 

In determining whether another action is pending between the same parties on the same cause of action, the facts in the two causes of action are compared to determine what primary right has been invaded. California follows the "primary right theory," which holds that every judicial action must involve the following elements: a primary right possessed by the plaintiff, and a corresponding primary duty devolving upon the defendant; a wrong done by the defendant that consisted of a breach of such primary right and duty; a remedial right in favor of the plaintiff, and a remedial duty resting on the defendant springing from this wrong; and the remedy or relief itself. Of these elements, the combined primary right, duty, and wrong constitute the cause of action. (Bush v Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1374, 1384.)

DISCUSSION

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (emphasis added, CCP § 430.41(a).)

 

On October 7, 2024, the parties conferred over teams regarding the Demurrer. The Court finds this sufficient to meet the meet and confer requirements.

 

Cause of Action 1 – Violation of California Labor Code § 2698

 

            Marquise Lewis filed a complaint, including a PAGA claim, in Los Angeles Superior Court on July 8, 2024, against Canoo Inc., Canoo Sales, LLC, Canoo Technologies, Inc., and Kelly Services. (Case No. 24STCV16782) Despite removal to federal court, the state court has scheduled a non-appearance review for January 10, 2025. Lewis’s complaint alleges employment with all defendants, claiming Labor Code violations, unfair competition, and a PAGA claim for civil penalties on behalf of Canoo employees from May 2023 to judgment. Each cause of action applies to all defendants.

 

            Plaintiff Orion Serup filed the instant representative PAGA claim against Canoo Inc. in Los Angeles Superior Court on August 15, 2024. The claim seeks civil penalties for Labor Code violations on behalf of all Canoo employees from May 29, 2023, to the final judgment. Plaintiff’s allegations largely mirror those in the Lewis Case.

 

Defendant requests that the Court abate or stay all further proceedings in the instant action pending the termination of the Lewis Case.

 

Abatement

 

            Defendant argues that the Court should stay proceedings until the termination of the Lewis Case because the two cases involve the same parties and the same claim.

 

            Defendant argues that both the Lewis and instant PAGA cases involve the same essential parties, the State of California and Defendant, as PAGA actions are brought on behalf of the state to enforce labor law, regardless of the individual plaintiff. (Iskanian v. CLS Transp. Los Angeles (2014) LLC, 59 Cal. 4th 348, 386.) As California is the real party in interest, any PAGA case binds all affected employees and serves to protect their rights under state labor law. Since the Lewis case already represents all allegedly aggrieved employees, including those in the instant case, covering violations from May 2023 onward, the instant case is redundant.

Furthermore, Defendant argues that the Lewis case and the instant case involve identical representative PAGA claims, as both plaintiffs seek to enforce the same primary right: California’s right to impose penalties on employers for Labor Code violations. Since PAGA claims are brought on behalf of the state, the rights asserted by each plaintiff are fundamentally the same. (See Howitson v. EvansHotels, LLC (2022) 81 Cal. App. 5th 475, 488.)  The instant case, solely focused on a PAGA claim, is thus entirely encompassed by the Lewis case, which also includes individual and class claims but asserts the same representative right under PAGA.

Plaintiff argues that the instant PAGA case should not be abated, as it seeks penalties for Labor Code violations distinct from those in the Lewis case, which includes both class action claims and additional defendants. Additionally, Lewis brings claims against Defendant for wages while the instant case is seeking penalties under PAGA.  Plaintiff asserts that staying the instant case would unfairly delay its resolution due to procedural requirements in the Lewis class action, such as class certification, which are irrelevant to PAGA claims. Plaintiff further emphasizes that PAGA’s purpose is to facilitate enforcement of labor laws, given limited state resources, and that California law supports a liberal interpretation of PAGA to ensure timely protection of employee rights. Consequently, Plaintiff requests that the court deny the motion to abate the instant case to ensure efficient enforcement of the California Labor Code.

 

The Court finds that the parties in the instant case and the parties in the Lewis Case are identical because both cases involve PAGA claims, and thus are brought on behalf of the State of California against Defendant. (Iskanian, supra, 59 Cal. 4th 348, 386.)

When addressing whether two causes of action are identical, the Court compares the facts alleged in the two actions. The causes of action are identical if both causes of action allege invasion of the same primary right. (Bush, supra, 10 Cal. App. 4th 1374, 1384.) A “primary right” is based on the harm suffered and not the particular theory of recovery asserted by the Plaintiff. (Bay Cities Paving & Grading, Inc. v. Lawyers' Mutual Ins. Co. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 854, 860.)

 The Lewis case asserts class action claims and a representative PAGA claim arising out of Defendant’s failure to provide meal and rest periods, failure to pay wages, failure to provide wage statements, failure to indemnify, and unlawful business practices. (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782 Complaint.) In the instant case, Plaintiff alleges a representative PAGA claim arising out of Defendant’s failure to provide employment records, failure to pay wages, failure to provide meal and rest breaks, failure to provide wage statements, and failure to reimburse. (Complaint p. 3.) Thus, both cases allege the same harm based on Defendant’s failure to follow California labor law. While Plaintiff argues that the causes of action are distinguishable because different claims and different remedies are sought, the primary right of a cause of action is based on the harm suffered and not the particular theory of recovery. (See, Bush, supra, 10 Cal. App. 4th 1374, 1384.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Plea in Abatement and Stays the action pending disposition of the Lewis Case. (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782.)

 

Exclusive Concurrent Jurisdiction

 

The exclusive concurrent jurisdiction doctrine provides that a second-in-time action should be stayed even in the absence of absolute identity of parties and causes of action if doing so would avoid potentially conflicting results or a waste of judicial resources. (See People ex rel. Garamendi v. Am. Autoplan, Inc. (1993) 20 Cal. App. 4th 760, 769-70.)

 

Defendant argues that the instant case should be stayed under the doctrine of exclusive concurrent jurisdiction, this doctrine prevents conflicting results and judicial resource waste by giving priority to the first court to assume jurisdiction. Here, the federal court handling the Lewis case has the authority to resolve all PAGA-related claims and provide any relief that could be granted in the instant case. (Huff v. Securitas Servs. USA, Inc. (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 745, 750.) Multiple courts addressing overlapping PAGA suits against Canoo could duplicate efforts and lead to inconsistent outcomes. Consequently, Defendant requests a stay of the instant case until the Lewis case is resolved.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s concerns about inconsistent rulings and judicial waste are unfounded, as concurrent PAGA actions are commonly permitted. Plaintiff asserts that there is no risk of conflicting outcomes regarding specific claims. Under PAGA, separate actions by different employees against the same employer are allowed (see Tan v. GrubHub, Inc. (N.D.Cal. 2016) 171 F.Supp.3d 998, 1012–1013.), and collateral estoppel would prevent any redundant suits if an unfavorable judgment is reached first. California precedent also supports parallel proceedings, as res judicata would address any future duplicative judgments. Plaintiff urges the court to prioritize the legislative intent of PAGA for prompt labor law enforcement, noting that distinct claims and parties, in the instant case, minimize any potential duplication with Lewis.

 

The Court denies Defendant’s request to Stay proceedings under the Exclusive Concurrent Jurisdiction doctrine as moot.
