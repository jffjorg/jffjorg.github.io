DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV37687    Hearing Date: October 11, 2023    Dept: 32

 

LENNY & LARRY’S, LLC,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

A&B INGREDIENTS, INC.,

                        Defendant.

 

  Case No.:  19STCV37687

  Hearing Date:  October 11, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion in limine no. 5

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 21, 2019, Lenny & Larry’s, LLC filed this action against Defendant A&B Ingredients, Inc. The operative Second Amended Complaint, filed June 6, 2022, alleges (1) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability, (2) breach of the implied warranty of fitness, (3) negligent misrepresentation, (4) fraudulent misrepresentation, and (5) unfair competition. The complaint arises from the following facts.

            Plaintiff is a manufacturer of health and fitness oriented food products, such as cookies. Defendant sells a variety of ingredients to food manufacturers, including Plaintiff. The particular ingredient at issue in this case is the antioxidant CytoGuard. CytoGuard is a cultured dextrose powder (“CDP”). Plaintiff alleges that the inclusion of CDP in its cookies caused the products to have an “off flavor” and “soapy” taste, leading to customer complaints and forcing Plaintiff to discontinue sales. Plaintiff contends that the cause of the flavor issue was the presence of lipase in the CDP.  

            On September 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant motion in limine no. 5 to exclude agreements between Plaintiff and its partner bakeries (co-packers), including indemnification and limitation of liability provisions therein. Defendant filed its opposition on September 28, 2023. Plaintiff filed its reply on October 4, 2023.

LEGAL STANDARD

            The Court has the inherent power and responsibility to grant a motion in limine to exclude “any kind of evidence which could be objected to at trial, either as irrelevant or subject to discretionary exclusion as unduly prejudicial.” (Clemens v. American Warranty Corp. (1987) 193 Cal.App.3d 444, 451.) “The advantage of such motions is to avoid the obviously futile attempt to ‘unring the bell’ in the event a motion to strike is granted in the proceedings before the jury.” (Hyatt v. Sierra Boat Co. (1978) 79 Cal.App.3d 325, 337.)

DISCUSSION

I. Relevance

“No evidence is admissible except relevant evidence.” (Evid. Code, § 350.) “‘Relevant evidence’ means evidence, including evidence relevant to the credibility of a witness or hearsay declarant, having any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.” (Id., § 210.)

Plaintiff argues that the co-packer agreements are irrelevant because the co-packers are not parties to the action and the agreements are not at issue. Plaintiff contends that the co-packer agreements do not tend to prove or disprove any fact material to the action.

However, the agreements spell out the obligations of the bakeries with respect to, among other things, obtaining and testing the ingredients, and the manufacture and testing of the final product. (See, e.g., Choi Decl., Ex. 5.) Because the bakeries were responsible for certain aspects of the cookie production that could have affected flavor, the agreements are relevant to identifying potential alternative causes of the flavor issue. While Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s CDP caused the flavor issue, Defendant is entitled to proffer alternative causes.

II. Prejudice

“The court in its discretion may exclude evidence if its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will (a) necessitate undue consumption of time or (b) create substantial danger of undue prejudice, of confusing the issues, or of misleading the jury.” (Evid. Code, § 352.)

Plaintiff argues that the jury is likely to be misled by evidence that Plaintiff has been indemnified by the co-packers on matters unrelated to this action. Specifically, Plaintiff contends that the indemnification provisions in the co-packer agreements may incorrectly suggest that Plaintiff has been made whole. The Court does not find that the risk of prejudice described by Plaintiff outweighs the probative value of the agreements, as discussed above. The indemnity provisions may be redacted. This is not a reason to exclude the contracts entirely.      

Plaintiff also argues that the jury will be misled by focus on the liability of a nonparty instead of the causes of action and affirmative defenses at issue. However, potential alternative causes are related to the causes of action at issue. The fact that the bakeries are not parties to the action does not preclude admission of evidence about them. Defendant is entitled to rebut Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendant’s CDP caused the flavor issue by introducing evidence that something else besides CDP caused the flavor issue. Furthermore, Defendant’s fourth and eighth affirmative defenses relate to third-party liability. Therefore, the evidence is relevant to an affirmative defense. Defendant is not attempting to distract the jury with evidence of some unrelated issue, and the jury is not likely to be confused just because the evidence concerns a nonparty. As the factfinder, the jury is entitled to consider conflicting evidence regarding the cause of the flavor issue.

 

III. Collateral Source Rule

“The collateral source rule states that ‘if an injured party receives some compensation for his injuries from a source wholly independent of the tortfeasor, such payment should not be deducted from the damages which the plaintiff would otherwise collect from the tortfeasor.’” (Moore v. Mercer (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 424, 437, quoting Helfend v. Southern California Rapid Transit Dist. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 1, 6.) “The collateral source rule operates both as a substantive rule of damages and as a rule of evidence. As a rule of evidence, it precludes the introduction of evidence of the plaintiff being compensated by a collateral source unless there is a ‘persuasive showing’ that such evidence is of ‘substantial probative value’ for purposes other than reducing damages.” (Arambula v. Wells (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1015, quoting Hrnjak v. Graymar, Inc. (1971) 4 Cal. 3d 725, 733.)

Plaintiff argues that the co-packer agreements are inadmissible to show that Plaintiff will be compensated through indemnification, as that constitutes a collateral source independent of Defendant. However, Defendant does not seek to admit the agreements for that purpose. As discussed above, the agreements are relevant to showing that the bakeries were responsible for the flavor issue rather than Defendant. The bakeries would be proffered as an alternative cause, not a collateral source.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion in limine no. 5 is DENIED. However, Plaintiff may redact the indemnification provision of the contracts.











 


LENNY & LARRY’S,
LLC,


                        Plaintiff,


            v.


 


A&B INGREDIENTS,
INC.,


                        Defendant.

 


  Case No.:  19STCV37687


  Hearing Date:  October 11, 2023


 


     [TENTATIVE]
order RE:


plaintiff’s motion in limine no. 4



 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 21, 2019, Lenny &
Larry’s, LLC filed this action against Defendant A&B Ingredients, Inc. The
operative Second Amended Complaint, filed June 6, 2022, alleges (1) breach of
the implied warranty of merchantability, (2) breach of the implied warranty of
fitness, (3) negligent misrepresentation, (4) fraudulent misrepresentation, and
(5) unfair competition. The complaint arises from the following facts.

            Plaintiff is a manufacturer of
health and fitness oriented food products, such as cookies. Defendant sells a
variety of ingredients to food manufacturers, including Plaintiff. The
particular ingredient at issue in this case is the antioxidant CytoGuard.
CytoGuard is a cultured dextrose powder (“CDP”). Plaintiff alleges that the inclusion
of CDP in its cookies caused the products to have an “off flavor” and “soapy”
taste, leading to customer complaints and forcing Plaintiff to discontinue
sales. Plaintiff contends that the cause of the flavor issue was the presence
of lipase in the CDP.  

            On September 14, 2023, Plaintiff
filed the instant motion in limine no. 4 to exclude evidence of
settlement communications. Defendant filed its opposition on September 28,
2023. Plaintiff filed its reply on October 4, 2023.

LEGAL STANDARD

            The Court has the inherent power and
responsibility to grant a motion in limine to exclude “any kind of
evidence which could be objected to at trial, either as irrelevant or subject
to discretionary exclusion as unduly prejudicial.” (Clemens v. American Warranty
Corp. (1987) 193 Cal.App.3d 444, 451.) “The advantage of such motions is to
avoid the obviously futile attempt to ‘unring the bell’ in the event a motion to
strike is granted in the proceedings before the jury.” (Hyatt v. Sierra Boat
Co. (1978) 79 Cal.App.3d 325, 337.)

DISCUSSION

“Evidence that a person has accepted or
offered or promised to accept a sum of money or any other thing, act, or
service in satisfaction of a claim, as well as any conduct or statements made
in negotiation thereof, is inadmissible to prove the invalidity of the claim or
any part of it.” (Evid. Code, § 1154.) “The rule prevents parties from being
deterred from making offers of settlement and facilitates the type of candid
discussion that may lead to settlement. The public policy in favor of
settlement of disputes makes it inadvisable to penalize the person who seeks
settlement by allowing his unaccepted offer to be used as an admission.” (Carney
v. Santa Cruz Women Against Rape (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 1009, 1023, internal
citations omitted.) Section 1154 “broadly precludes the introduction of
statements made in the context of settlement negotiations.” (Zhou v.
Unisource Worldwide (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 1471, 1478.)

Here, Plaintiff seeks to exclude evidence
of its correspondence with Defendant and Western Pacific Foods, which Plaintiff
contends are settlement offers or related communications falling under Section
1154. Western Pacific is not a party to this action, but Plaintiff has claimed
that Western Pacific’s margarine ingredient caused the flavor issue. (Katel
Decl., Ex. 3.) In this case, Plaintiff claims that Defendant A&B’s CytoGuard
CDP caused the flavor issue. Defendant argues that the motion should be denied because
the communications were not offers of compromise, nor will they be used to
prove the invalidity of Plaintiff’s claims.

Plaintiff has not identified any
particular evidence that it seeks to exclude, beyond a November 2018 email sent
to Defendant A&B. (See Choi Decl., TE 58.)

            a. Offer of Compromise

            “[A] letter itemizing what the
sender thinks the recipient owes him or her and demanding payment, even under
threat of legal action, is, in effect, a bill and not an offer in settlement or
a document in settlement negotiations.” (Zhou v. Unisource Worldwide
(2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 1471, 1477.) “A statement by a claimant concerning the extent
of his injuries or disease, or concerning the amount of damages he or she
claims to have suffered, if ‘not connected with an offer of compromise,’ may
well constitute an admissible admission.” (Volkswagen of America, Inc. v.
Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1494.) With that being said, “the
question which statements are ‘connected’ and which statements are ‘independent’
. . . must be answered in light of the strong policy in favor of promoting
candor during settlement negotiations embodied in the statute.” (Caira v.
Offner (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 12, 36.) Section 1154 covers “not only
settlement offers and demands but also ‘any conduct or statements made in negotiation
thereof.’” (Zhou, supra, 157 Cal.App.4th at p. 1478.)

The correspondence listed as Trial Exhibit
58 is a November 2018 email from Plaintiff’s former employee, Robert Rhodes, to
Defendant titled “CytoGuard Loss Claim.” (Choi Decl., TE 58.1.) The email
informs Defendant that Plaintiff’s investigation has revealed that Defendant’s
CDP caused the soapy flavor. (Ibid.) The email states that Plaintiff is “seeking
recovery of our financial damages which are in excess of $1.2MM.” (Ibid.)
The email invites Defendant to a phone call for Plaintiff to provide more
detail regarding its findings and to answer Defendant’s questions, and plans a
follow-up visit to Defendant’s facility to discuss Plaintiff’s recovery for its
losses. (Ibid.) The email ends by expressing a desire “to bring[] this
matter to a rapid closure.” (Ibid.) The email attaches a spreadsheet,
titled “Loss Summary,” detailing costs totaling around $1.2 million. (Id.,
TE 58.2.) The email also attaches the Rothamel Report that Plaintiff relied on
to determine the cause of the soapy flavor. (Id., TE 58.3-58.14.)

Defendant argues that because this
communication contains a full itemization (TE 58.2), and Rhodes testified that
he sought “full compensation” (Katel Decl., Ex. 5 at 110:22-111:3), the communication
is nothing more than a bill. However, the correspondence suggests that
Plaintiff was not seeking full compensation for all damages. (See TE
58.13 [“we are seeking recovery of our direct financial losses” but not “commercial
and market damage that has already occurred”].) Rhodes also testified that “there
are other market costs that we did not include in here.” (Katel Decl., Ex. 5 at
111:11-14.) Therefore, the communication is not a mere bill of all damages.  

Zhou, the case upon which Defendant
relies, does not stand for the proposition that a communication cannot be
related to settlement merely because it contains a precise itemization. Instead,
Zhou acknowledged that courts broadly interpret what constitutes a
settlement-related communication. (Zhou, supra, 157 Cal.App.4th at pp.
1477-78.) Hence, the letters at issue in Zhou were considered
settlement-related even though they were “arguably preliminary to the actual
negotiations,” and “neither letter included a specific offer to discount [the plaintiff’s]
claims or suggested in what way he might be prepared to compromise his demands.”
(Ibid.)

Here, Plaintiff offered to accept $1.2
million to resolve the matter and invited Defendant to further discuss the
claim. (TE 58.1.) The email is not a bare demand for money. The communication is
sufficiently related to settlement to fall under Section 1154.

b. Admissibility

A communication that constitutes an offer
of compromise is still admissible if it is not used “to prove the invalidity of
the claim or any part of it.” (See Evid. Code, § 1154.) “Section 1154 makes an
offer of compromise inadmissible to show invalidity of the claim to which the
offer related.” (Zhou, supra, 157 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1478.) It does not
bar evidence “to show the invalidity of a different claim.” (Ibid.)

Western Pacific is not a party to this
action. This action alleges that Defendant A&B’s CytoGuard CDP caused the
soapy flavor. Plaintiff’s claim against Western Pacific is based on the theory
that Western’s aged margarine caused the soapy flavor. (Katel Decl., Ex. 3.) Therefore,
while settlement communications to Western Pacific may be inadmissible to prove
the invalidity of the claim against Western Pacific, they are admissible to
show the invalidity of a different claim, i.e., Plaintiff’s claim
against Defendant A&B. Defendant A&B is entitled to introduce evidence
of an alternative cause, such as Western Pacific’s margarine.    

Defendant argues that communications between
Plaintiff and Defendant are also admissible because they would be used to
challenge Plaintiff’s credibility and demonstrate bias and motive rather than the
validity of the claim. Specifically, Defendant points out that the Rothamel
Report sent to Defendant contradicted the one sent to Western Pacific and left
out information harmful to Plaintiff’s theory. Defendant claims that it seeks
to show that Plaintiff selectively targeted Defendant rather than going after the
logical causes of the flavor issue, such as the bakeries.

However, Defendant only needs the Rothamel
Report itself and other evidence of causation for this purpose. The correspondence
that Plaintiff sent to Defendant (TE 58) is still a protected settlement
communication.

            c. Waiver

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff has
waived the protections of Section 1154 by allowing Rhodes to testify about the
settlement offers. Defendant cites to Evidence Code section 912(a), which
waives the privileges enumerated in Sections 954, 966, 980, 994, 1014, 1033,
1034, 1035.8, 1037.5, and 1038 if “any holder of the privilege, without
coercion, has disclosed a significant part of the communication or has
consented to disclosure made by anyone.” (Evid. Code, § 912(a).) Section 912(a)
does not waive the protection enumerated in Section 1154.

            Defendant also relies on People
v. Hayes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 1211, 1265, which recites Section 912 and
affirms that “[l]egal opinion and advice given the client by counsel is a
confidential communication,” which if disclosed to a third party, waives the
privilege over the communication. But Section 1154 does not concern
confidential attorney-client communications. Rather, it precludes the
introduction of settlement-related evidence at trial for the purpose of proving
the invalidity of a claim.

The fact that a party testified during
discovery about settlement communications has no bearing on whether their opponent
is allowed to introduce the communications at trial to prove the invalidity of
a claim. Assuming, arguendo, that Plaintiff has waived the privilege
over certain communications, they would at most be discoverable, not admissible.
Even Section 912 does not go so far as to make communications admissible.
Communications falling under Section 912 are still subject to the rules of
admissibility, including Section 1154, if a party seeks to introduce them at
trial.  

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion in limine
no. 4 is GRANTED as to Trial Exhibit 58. 
However, Defendant may use the Rothamel Report that was attached to
Exhibit 58.

























































Case Number: 22STCV00517    Hearing Date: October 11, 2023    Dept: 32

 

HERMENGILDO AHUMADA-BURGARA,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

M.S. AEROSPACE INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV00517

  Hearing Date:  October 11, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees and sanctions

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 6, 2022, Plaintiff Hermenegildo Ahumada-Burgara filed this employment discrimination action against Defendants M.S. Aerospace Inc. and Harry Smith. Plaintiff alleges that he is a disabled Latino male over 40 years old who was terminated while on medical leave and replaced by a younger, less qualified employee. Defendant M.S. Aerospace was allegedly Plaintiff’s employer, and Defendant Smith is allegedly a manager of M.S. Aerospace.

            On October 31, 2022, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration. Arbitration was thereafter initiated, and an arbitrator was appointed. However, on May 17, 2023, JAMS issued an invoice for $86,000 that was due upon receipt. Defendant did not pay the invoice on the due date or within thirty days. On July 31, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to withdraw from arbitration.

            On September 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for attorneys’ fees and sanctions pursuant to the withdrawal from arbitration. Defendant filed its opposition on September 28, 2023. Plaintiff filed his reply on October 4, 2023.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

“If the employee or consumer withdraws the claim from arbitration and proceeds in a court . . . [t]he employee or consumer may bring a motion, or a separate action, to recover all attorney’s fees and all costs associated with the abandoned arbitration proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(c)(1).) Additionally, “[t]he court shall impose sanctions on the drafting party in accordance with Section 1281.99.” (Id., § 1281.98(c)(2).) Sanctions under Section 1281.99 include “reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and costs, incurred by the employee or consumer as a result of the material breach,” as well as evidence, terminating, and contempt sanctions. (Id., § 1281.99.)

DISCUSSION

I. Fees and Costs

            a. Plaintiff’s Entitlement to Costs

Plaintiff is entitled to recover the costs of the abandoned arbitration proceeding. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(c)(1).) Additionally, monetary sanctions are mandatory after a material breach. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.99(a) [“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . .”].)  

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff is only entitled to costs associated with the material breach, which means only costs incurred after the breach. However, Section 1281.98 expressly allows recovery of “all costs associated with the abandoned arbitration proceeding,” not just the material breach. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(c)(1).) While Defendant is correct that sanctions under Section 1281.99 are limited to those incurred as a result of the material breach, those sanctions are in addition to the costs of the abandoned arbitration. (See id., § 1281.98(c) [“If the employee or consumer withdraws the claim from arbitration and proceeds in a court . . . both of the following apply . . .”].) The statute allows recovery of both the costs of the abandoned arbitration and sanctions under Section 1281.99.

            Defendant argues that Section 1281.97 limits sanctions to those enumerated in Section 1281.99. However, Section 1281.97 does not govern here. Section 1281.97 applies when “the drafting party [fails] to pay certain fees and costs before the arbitration can proceed, [and] the fees or costs to initiate an arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.97(a)(1).) By contrast, Section 1281.98 applies when “the drafting party [fails to] pay certain fees and costs during the pendency of an arbitration proceeding, [and] the fees or costs required to continue the arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days . . . .” (Id., § 1281.98(a)(1).)

            Section 1281.98 applies here because the $86,000 fee that Defendant failed to pay was assessed during the pendency of the arbitration and was required for the arbitration to continue. Section 1281.98 allows recovery of all costs associated with the abandoned arbitration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(c)(1).)      

            b. Reasonable Amount

Plaintiff’s counsel claims to have spent 175 hours at a rate of $450 per hour and requests a 1.5 times lodestar multiplier, resulting in $118,125 in attorneys’ fees. (Rand-Lewis Decl. ¶ 77.) Plaintiff additionally claims $435.24 in filing fees and $1,350 in reporter fees. (Id., ¶¶ 78, 81.) In total, Plaintiff requests $119,910.24 in fees and costs. (Id., ¶ 82.)   

Defendant argues that 105 of the hours claimed were incurred before the material breach and are therefore unrecoverable. As discussed above, Plaintiff is entitled to recover the costs of the abandoned arbitration. Nonetheless, the Court finds the hours to be exaggerated when taking into account the complexity of the issues involved and counsel’s experience. (See Rand-Lewis Decl. ¶ 70.) The Court finds that Plaintiff reasonably spent 50 hours associated with the arbitration and breach. Defendant does not contest the hourly rate of $450, which the Court finds reasonable. The Court does not find a lodestar multiplier to be appropriate. This results in attorneys’ fees of $22,500. The Court further finds that Plaintiff reasonably incurred court reporter fees and filing fees totaling $1,785.24. Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to monetary recovery of $24,285.24.  

II. Nonmonetary Sanctions

The court “may order” evidence, terminating, or contempt sanctions, “unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.99(b).)

Plaintiff requests terminating and contempt sanctions on the grounds that Defendant engaged in bad faith conduct by failing to pay arbitration fees, misrepresenting facts, and maintaining positions clearly contradicted by legal authority. Plaintiff argues that Defendant intentionally sought to drag out the case and increase the costs to Plaintiff.  

Nonmonetary sanctions are discretionary. Here, the Court does not find a sufficient showing to warrant terminating or contempt sanctions. Defendant argued in good faith and was not frivolous.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions is GRANTED in part. The Court awards Plaintiff monetary sanctions in the amount of $24,285.24. The Court declines to impose terminating or contempt sanctions.

 


Case Number: 22STCV19601    Hearing Date: October 11, 2023    Dept: 32

 

AREXY RIOS,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

CALIFORNIA EYE SPECIALISTS MEDICAL GROUP, INC., et al.,

                       

                       Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV19601

  Hearing Date:  October 11, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to requests for admission

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On June 15, 2022, Plaintiff filed this employment discrimination action against Defendants California Eye Specialists Medical Group, Inc.; California Eye Care, Managed Eye Care Specialist, A Medical Group, Inc.; and California Eye Management Services, LP. Plaintiff alleges discrimination based on her pregnancy and various wage violations.  

            On September 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant three motions to compel further responses from each of the Defendants as to Requests for Admission Set One. Defendants filed their oppositions on September 27, 2023. Plaintiff filed replies on October 4, 2023.

LEGAL STANDARD

“On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete. (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)

MEET AND CONFER

            A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Roshanian Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. RFA Nos. 3-5

            RFA Nos. 3-5 ask Defendants to admit that Plaintiff was pregnant during her employment and that Plaintiff informed Defendants or Defendants’ managers that she was pregnant. Defendants objected and then responded that “Defendant is not currently in possession of sufficient information to either admit or deny this request.”

            “If a responding party gives lack of information or knowledge as a reason for a failure to admit all or part of a request for admission, that party shall state in the answer that a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter in the particular request has been made, and that the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable that party to admit the matter.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220(c).) Defendants’ response does not satisfy this requirement.

            Defendants argue that they have no personal knowledge of whether Plaintiff was pregnant and claim that their primary defense is that they did not know of Plaintiff’s pregnancy. Defendants also contend that the information is more readily obtainable from Plaintiff herself. However, as explained above, the Code requires Defendants to conduct a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter and then verify that they lack sufficient information to respond despite that inquiry. It is not a proper response to simply assert a lack of knowledge.

            Furthermore, the purpose of a request for admission is “to limit the triable issues and spare the parties the burden and expense of litigating undisputed issues.” (Brigante v. Huang (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 1569, 1576.) The purpose of an RFA is not to uncover evidence, but to eliminate the need for proof. (Id. at p. 1577.) Therefore, it is immaterial that Plaintiff herself has knowledge of certain matters. The purpose of the RFAs is to have Defendants admit certain facts that they do not plan on contesting, so that the parties do not waste time litigating them at trial. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.420 [requesting party may be entitled to costs of proof if responding party fails to admit a matter without good reason].) A further response is required for RFA Nos. 3-5.

II. RFA Nos. 6, 7, 10

            RFA Nos. 6, 7, and 10 ask Defendants to admit that Plaintiff requested an accommodation for her pregnancy, that Defendants denied Plaintiff’s request, and that Defendants failed to engage in an interactive process. In response to RFA No. 6, Defendants stated that they are not currently in possession of sufficient information to determine whether Plaintiff requested an accommodation. In response to RFA Nos. 7 and 10, Defendants stated that the requests assume facts not in evidence because Defendants have not admitted that Plaintiff requested an accommodation.

As discussed above, responses based on lack of information must comply with Section 2033.220(c). Defendants’ responses to RFA Nos. 6, 7, and 10 are based on a purported lack of knowledge regarding whether Plaintiff requested an accommodation. Therefore, a further response is required for RFA Nos. 6, 7, and 10.  

III. RFA Nos. 17, 19-24

            RFA Nos. 17 and 19-24 ask Defendants to admit that Plaintiff complained about staffing issues, that Plaintiff was required to travel between offices, and that Plaintiff was not reimbursed for various business expenses. As with above, Defendants asserted a lack of sufficient information. Again, responses based on lack of information must comply with Section 2033.220(c). A further response is required for RFA Nos. 17 and 19-24.   

IV. RFA No. 33

            RFA No. 33 asks Defendant California Eye Specialists Medical Group, Inc. to admit that the wage statements Defendant issued to Plaintiff did not accurately reflect all the wages Plaintiff earned during her employment. Defendant responded that it could not answer because the request is predicated on the assumption that Plaintiff earned wages from Defendant.

Defendant cites no authority for the proposition that every foundational fact implied in an RFA must be independently established before a party is required to respond. “Each answer in a response to requests for admission shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220(a).) In other words, Defendant must respond if it has sufficient information to do so. If Defendant knows that Plaintiff earned wages, it cannot refuse to answer the RFA simply because Plaintiff has not independently proven that fact.    

            A response to an RFA must “[a]dmit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true” and “[d]eny so much of the matter involved in the request as is untrue.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220(b).) If Defendant contends that it is untrue Plaintiff earned any wages, it must deny that part of the request. Or if Defendant does not know whether Plaintiff earned wages, its response must comply with Section 2033.220(c). A further response is required for RFA No. 33.

V. Objections

            Lastly, Defendants provide no valid explanation in support of their boilerplate objections. Therefore, none of the asserted objections are reasons not to answer the RFAs. Defendants must provide substantive responses to the RFAs at issue. As discussed above, even if Defendants lack the knowledge to admit or deny, their response must at least be code-compliant.  

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses are GRANTED. Defendants shall provide further responses to the subject RFAs within 15 days. Sanctions are denied as the parties acted with substantial justification.
