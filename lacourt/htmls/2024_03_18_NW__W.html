DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00714    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: W

YS PROPERTIES, LLC vs HAYK SHISHOYAN, et al.

 

plaintiff’s MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES AND COSTS PURSUANT TO CIVIL CODE § 1717

 

Date of Hearing:        March 18, 2024                                 Trial Date:       N/A  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00714

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff YS Properties, LLC  

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 26, 2022, Plaintiff YS Properties, LLC filed a complaint against Hayk Shishoyan, Hats Enterprises, LLC and Hats Enterprises LLC dba Encino Tailors for breach of written contract and breach of guaranty. Plaintiff alleges Defendants have failed to pay monthly rent for their commercial lease with Plaintiff.

 

Trial was held November 27, 2023. The court entered judgment for Plaintiff against Defendants, jointly and severally.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney Fees and Costs is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff YS Properties LLC moves the court for an award of attorney fees and costs pursuant to Civil Code section 1717 as the prevailing party pursuant to a judgment rendered in Plaintiff’s favor. Plaintiff requests an award of reasonable attorney fees in the amount of $103,868 and verifiable costs in the amount of $11,221.43, for a total of $115,343.43.

 

As a general rule, each party to litigation must bear its own attorney fees, unless otherwise provided by statute or contract. (CCP §1021.) However, Civil Code section 1717, subdivision (a) provides in pertinent part: “In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” Contractual attorney's fees must ordinarily be sought by noticed motion, unless the parties stipulate otherwise. (See CCP § 1033.5(c)(5); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702(a).) A motion is necessary to determine both entitlement to, and the amount of, any fees. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702(a) [motion procedure applies when court determines entitlement to fees, amount of fees, or both]; P R Burke Corp. v Victor Valley Wastewater Reclamation Auth. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1052.) 

 

Plaintiff first argues it is entitled to recover attorney fees and costs pursuant to the Lease agreement. Plaintiff sued Hats Enterprises LLC and Hayk Shishoyan for breach of the commercial lease agreement and for breach of the Guaranty of Lease, respectively. (Xu Decl. ¶2, Exh. A.) The Lease provides:

 

Attorneys' Fees. If any Party or Broker brings an action or proceeding involving the Premises whether founded in tort, contract or equity, or to declare rights hereunder, the Prevailing Party (as hereafter defined) in any such proceeding, action, or appeal thereon, shall be entitled to reasonable attorneys' fees. Such fees may be awarded in the same suit or recovered in a separate suit, whether or not such action or proceeding is pursued to decision or judgment. The term, "Prevailing Party" shall include, without limitation, a Party or Broker who substantially obtains or defeats the relief sought, as the case may be, whether by compromise, settlement, judgment, or the abandonment by the other Party or Broker of its claim or defense. The attorneys' fees award shall not be computed in accordance with any court fee schedule, but shall be such as to fully reimburse all attorneys' fees reasonably incurred. In addition, Lessor shall be entitled to attorneys' fees, costs and expenses incurred in the preparation and service of notices of Default and consultations in connection therewith, whether or not a legal action is subsequently commenced in connection with such Default or resulting Breach ($200 is a reasonable minimum per occurrence for such services and consultation). (Xu Decl., Exh. A, ¶31.)

 

Here, Plaintiff is the prevailing party. On November 27, 2023, the court ruled: “against Defendants Hats Enterprises, LLC and Hayk Shishoyan, jointly and severally, and in favor of YS Properties, LLC in the amount of $65,392.47.” (December 8, 2023 Judgment.)

 

Plaintiff further contends the fees and costs reflect the substantial work necessary for Plaintiff to file and prosecute the action all the way through trial, including 1) file the complaint, 2) prepare for default, 3) conduct discovery, 4) file motions, 5) attend all hearings, 6) prepare for trial, and 7) attend trial. Specifically, Plaintiff spent much of the attorney work and time on addressing bad faith strategy and delay tactics employed by Mr. Shishoyan and

his counsel, including but not limited to: (1) initial failure to produce responses to written discovery resulting in meet and confer (more than 25 hours), (2) multiple failures to produce responses after agreeing to multiple deadlines, resulting further meet and confer and discovery motions (more than 40 hours), (3) bad faith agreement to agree to participate in mediation and offering unreasonable settlement amounts (more than 10 hours), (4) falsely presenting intent to proceed to trial without a real commitment to do so, requiring Plaintiff to spend a significant amount of time to prepare a fully contested trial in this matter, including witness list, exhibit list, trial brief, statement of the case, and exhibit binders (more than 20 hours). (Xu Decl., ¶3.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff’s attorney’s fees are reasonable given the circumstances of the case. “Even after determining that a party is entitled to fees because it ‘prevailed,’ the trial court must still determine what amount of fees would be ‘reasonable’ in light of the relative extent or degree of the party's success in obtaining the results sought.” (Sokolow v. County of San Mateo (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 231, 247.)¿Plaintiff has set forth the attorney’s fees claimed by Plaintiff in an itemized detailed billing summary of each attorney who rendered professional services as well as the expenses in representing Plaintiff to successfully prosecute the action on the contract. (Xu Decl., Exh. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel billed 275.900 hours in prosecuting this matter through trial. (Xu Decl. Exh. B.)  Moreover, the hourly rates are fair and reasonable. Plaintiff’s counsel bills $500 for Jay Chung and Peter Lee; $295 for Minely Grigorian and Loho Angilbert (who later billed $350) and $350 for Jared Xu. (Xu Decl. ¶5.) These rates are reasonable based on each attorneys years of experience and education.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney Fees is GRANTED.

 

Costs

 

Plaintiff is also entitled to recover reasonable costs for this action. “Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (CCP §1032(b).) Here, Plaintiff seeks costs of $11,475.43, which is itemized in Exhibit B. However, Plaintiff is not entitled to research costs. Civil Code Section 1033.5(b)(2) prohibits the recovery of “Investigation expenses”. Based on this provision, the Court of Appeals held in Ladas v. California State Automobile Association that “[f]ees for legal research, computer or otherwise, may not be recovered under section 1033.5.” (Ladas v. California State Automobile Association (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 776.)  Accordingly, the court strikes $4,889.50 from the costs.

 


Case Number: 22VECV01242    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: W

DAVID FASSNACHT v. JOHN WORDIN, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        March 18, 2024                           Trial Date:      December 9, 2024

Department:              W                                                   Case No.:      22VECV01242

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff David Fassnacht  

Responding Party:     No opposition  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On Plaintiff August 29, 2022, Plaintiff David Fassnacht filed a complaint against Defendants John Wordin, John Wordin dba Life Aid Holdings, Inc., Life Aid Holdings, Inc., Life Aid Research Institute dba Life Aid Holdings, Inc. for fraudulent inducement, fraud (intentional misrepresentation), fraud (deceit by concealment), and fraud (negligent misrepresentation). Plaintiff alleges Defendant misrepresented an investment opportunity in regards to the app, LifeScore App.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves the court for an order granting leave to amend, or in the alternative, supplemental complaint, on the grounds that the amended (or, if applicable, supplemental) complaint is necessary to clarify some of the alleged terms and allege facts material to this case which have occurred subsequent to the filing of the former complaint. (Mot. p. 2:3-10.)

 

A complainant may obtain leave from the trial court to amend his pleading beyond the number of amendments allowed under Code of Civil Procedure section 472 (a) by filing a noticed motion.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1324.)¿ The motion must be accompanied by a declaration stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) why the request was not made earlier.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1324 (b).)¿¿¿¿

¿¿¿ 

“Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”¿ (CCP § 576.)  In the absence of a showing of prejudice from the opposing side, the trial court ordinarily lacks discretion to deny a motion to amend a pleading.¿ (Honig v. Financial Corp. of America (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 960, 965.)

 

Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) and section 576. The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified. . ..” “Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . .. [citation]. A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)  

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (CRC Rule 3.1324(b).) The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, and (2) specifications by reference to pages, paragraphs, and lines of the allegations that would be deleted and/or added. (CRC Rule 3.1324(a).) 

 

Plaintiff seeks to correct potential ambiguities and insufficiencies in the original complaint, including clarifying the three agreements Defendants allegedly breached as well as actions allegedly done by Defendant Wordin as an individual. Plaintiff also seeks to plead two new breach of contract causes of action that were inadvertently left out.

 

The court finds Plaintiff has fulfilled the requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a) and (b). Plaintiff submits the declaration of Counsel Kymberleigh Korpus who provides the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Korpus Decl. ¶¶4—7.) Plaintiff also attaches a copy of the proposed amendment which tracks changes. (Korpus Decl., Exh. 2.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File the First Amended Complaint is GRANTED.


Case Number: 23VECV01917    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: W

HARGES FAMILY PARTNERSHIP, LP, et al. v. CHARTWELL ESCROW, INC.

 

defendant chartwell escrow, inc.’s demurrer to plaintiff’s complaint with motion to strike  

 

Date of Hearing:        March 18, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01917

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Chartwell Escrow, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Harges Family Partnership, LP and Lorelei Harges, Trustee of the Harges Community Property Trust

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Davis Decl. ¶¶3-5.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a breach of fiduciary duty action. Plaintiffs Harges Family Partnership, LP and Lorelei Harges, Trustee of the Harges Community Property Trust allege Plaintiffs entered into multiple agreements to sell seven pieces of real property to several buyers. Chartwell agreed to act as the escrow company for the transactions. Plaintiffs allege per Section C of the Addendum, the escrow holder, Chartwell, was supposed to release the deposits to the plaintiffs one business day after their receipt. However, Plaintiffs allege Chartwell never released any of the deposits to the plaintiffs and instead, Chartwell returned the deposits to the buyers.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Chartwell Escrow, Inc.’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Defendant Chartwell Escrow, Inc.’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Chartwell Escrow Inc. demurs to the complaint for breach of fiduciary duty on the grounds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant and is impermissibly vague and uncertain.

 

The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)  “‘[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.’” (Hasso v. Hapke (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 107, 140 (quoting Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 221).) “A fiduciary duty under common law may arise ‘when one person enters into a confidential relationship with another.’” (Id.) 

 

Defendant argues given the complaint’s absence of written instructions signed by the Seller, the Buyers, or Chartwell Escrow in any of the seven separate agreements to sell real property, it’s unclear what, if any, duty was owed or breached. Specifically, an escrow holder is not liable for failure to do something not required by the terms of the escrow or for losses of a party when the escrow holder was obediently following the escrow instructions. (See Lee v. Title Ins. & Trust Co. (1968)264 Cal.App.2d 160, 162-163; Schaefer v. Manufacturers Bank (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 70, 77-78.) Instead, the scope of an escrow holder’s duties is limited to compliance with the escrow instructions of the parties. (See Markowitz v. Fidelity Nat. Title Co. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 508, 526.) As such, Plaintiffs’ allegations “Chartwell was plaintiffs’ fiduciary and owed plaintiffs the same duties as a trustee owes to a beneficiary of a trust. In that regard, Chartwell was required to look out for the best interest of plaintiffs as if... [it] stood in plaintiffs’ shoes[]” is the wrong standard.

 

Plaintiff argues there is an exception to the general rule an escrow holder’s obligations are limited to compliance with the parties’ instructions – fraud. (See Summit Financial Holdings, Ltd. v. Continental Lawyers Title Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 705, 711.) While true, Plaintiff has not alleged such colluding as they maintain in their opposition. As noted above, “[a]n escrow holder is an agent and fiduciary of the parties to the escrow.”  (Summit Financial Holdings, Ltd. v. Continental Lawyers Title Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 705, 711.)  “The agency created by the escrow is limited—limited to the obligation of the escrow holder to carry out the instructions of each of the parties to the escrow.”  (Id.)  With respect to the scope of an escrow holder’s fiduciary duties, “[a]bsent clear evidence of fraud, an escrow holder’s obligations are limited to compliance with the parties’ instructions.”  (Id.)  “[A]n escrow holder ‘has no general duty to police the affairs of its depositors.’”  (Id.)  The complaint fails to allege such.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Plaintiffs contend they should be granted at least sixty days leave to amend in order to give Plaintiffs sufficient time to obtain new counsel, for new counsel to be familiar with the file and prepare an amended complaint. Based on the discussion below, the court will sustain the demurrer with leave to amend the complaint in 20 days.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint including Paragraph 15 of the complaint as well as Plaintiffs’ claims for punitive damages.

 

Defendant first argues Paragraph 15 of the Complaint improperly alleges “Chartwell was plaintiffs’ fiduciary and owed plaintiffs the duties as a trustee owes to a beneficiary of a trust. In that regard, Chartwell was required to look out for the best interest of plaintiffs as if the defendant stood in plaintiffs’ shoes.” This allegation misstates the law, as there is no such duty imposed by law on any escrow holder. To the contrary, the only obligations assumed by an escrow holder are those found in the written escrow instructions between the parties. The court agrees this is a mischaracterization of the law. Escrow agents do not owe a fiduciary duty as a trustee.

 

Defendant also argues Plaintiffs demand for punitive damages is alleged in conclusory fashion without any facts, let alone a factual showing of willful, malicious, fraudulent, oppressive or consciously indifferent conduct. The court agrees. Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff's rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate. (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210; quoting Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.) Plaintiffs have failed to allege such.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. 

Case Number: 23VECV04097    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: W

JOSE PEREZ, et al. v. GENERAL MOTORS LLC

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:        March 18, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04097

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Jose Perez and Loren Aguirre   

Responding Party:     Defendant General Motors, LLC

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises in the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Plaintiffs Jose Perez and Loren Aguirre purchased a 2023 Chevrolet Silverado. Plaintiffs allege defects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves. On September 18, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendant General Motors LLC for 1. Violation of Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Express Warranty and 2. Violation of Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Implied Warranty.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents is GRANTED

 

discussion

 

Plaintiffs move the court for an order compelling further responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Requests for Production of Documents and monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,310.00 against Defendant General Motors, LLC and its attorneys of record, The Erskine Law Group, PC. Plaintiffs make the motion on the grounds Defendant has failed and refused to provide Code-compliant discovery responses pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 2031.310.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the court may order a responding to serve a further response to a request for production when the court finds that any of the following apply: (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; and (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.

 

To date, Defendant has failed to provide code-compliant responses and documents responsive to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents (“RPD”), including but not limited to Numbers 16, 19-32 and 37-41. Plaintiffs argue their RPDs are highly relevant to their underlying Song-Beverly Act claims. Specifically, RPD Nos. 16, 19-32 seek documents relating to policies and procedures for handling vehicle replacement and repurchase requests and RPD Nos. 37-41 seek documents relating to Defendant’s vehicle repair and warranty codes issued for the Subject Vehicle. Plaintiff contends these documents are relevant to the case at bar because they will likely shed light on whether Defendant has a policy in place that results in systemic violations of the Song-Beverly Act. Furthermore, understanding what these codes mean is necessary to understanding the documents in which they are used.

 

In opposition, GM first argues Plaintiffs did not meet and confer in good faith. A motion to compel further responses “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.”  (CCP §2031.310(b).) The declaration must state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented in the motion.  (CCP § 2016.040.) The court finds Plaintiff provided an adequate declaration that they met and conferred with Defendant in their motion. However, the court encourages the parties to continue to make reasonable and good faith attempts at resolving discovery matters before bringing motions to the court.

 

GM next argues GM has already agreed to produce its Warranty Policy & Procedure Manual as well as the policies and procedures used to evaluate lemon law claims and repurchase requests along with its California Customer Engagement Center training materials in response to Request for Production Nos. 16 and 19-32. Thus, there is nothing for this court to compel.

 

Plaintiff, in reply, argues GM has not done so. The court orders GM to produce documents reasonably requested by RPD Nos. 16 and 19-32 (pursuant to a protective order) if it has not done so by the time of the hearing on this motion.

 

As for 37-41, GM argues Plaintiffs’ requests are vague and overbroad and have no bearing on the repairs of Plaintiffs’ Silverado. Moreover, Plaintiffs also demand production of confidential, proprietary, and commercially sensitive information that contains trade secret material. The court agrees, in part. The court agrees the requests should be limited to all codes pertaining to the defects and nonconformities at issue. However, it is up to Defendant to seek a protective order regarding any discovery they believe is privileged as well as to provide a privilege log.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request this court award $2,310.00 as a sanction against Defendant and its counsel of record, The Erskine Law Group, PC for Defendant’s failure to provide properly compliant responses to Plaintiffs’ RPDs.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for the imposition of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes such a motion unless the court finds that the one subject to sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.  

 

There being substantial justification, with merit to both sides, the court denies the request for sanctions.¿¿¿
