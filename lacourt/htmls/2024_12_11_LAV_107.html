DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV00763    Hearing Date: December 11, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Kourosh Izadpanahi,

 

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Nahid Hosseimzadeh Dolkhani,

Defendant.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

          24VECV00763         107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Demurrer with Motion to Strike

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

1) Sustain without leave to amend as to the res judicata defense;

(2) Overrule as to the collateral estoppel defense;

(3) Sustain with leave to amend as to the unclean hands defense.

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

Plaintiff Kourosh Izadpanahi (“Plaintiff”) and Defendant Nahid Hosseinzadeh Dolkhani (“Defendant”) married in 2004 and divorced in 2021. (Second Amended Complaint [“SAC”] ¶ 8.) The parties share custody of one minor child. (SAC ¶ 8.)

 

On February 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for: (1) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (2) domestic violence. On April 10, 2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint. (Demurrer filed 04/10/24.) This court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend. (05/28/24 Minute Order p. 12.) 

 

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint. On July 29, 2024, Plaintiff made an oral motion for leave to amend the first amended complaint, which the court granted (07/29/24 Minute Order.) 

 

On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the second amended complaint against Defendant and Does 1 through 30, alleging causes of action for: (1) intentional interference with consortium; (2) conspiracy to interfere with consortium; (3) domestic violence; (4) battery; (5) assault; and (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress. (SAC p. 1.)

 

On October 9, 2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s second amended complaint. On November 4, 2024, this court overruled the demurrer.

 

On November 15, 2024, Defendant filed an answer to Plaintiff’s second amended complaint.

 

On November 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a demurrer with motion to strike Defendant’s answer. No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              DEMURRER

 

a.     Timeliness

 

“A party who has filed a complaint or cross-complaint may, within 10 days after service of the answer to his pleading, demur to the answer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.40(b).)¿ The deadline is extended by five calendar days if the answer being demurred to was served by mail and the place of the address and the place of mailing is within California.¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 1013(a).)

 

Defendant filed the answer with this court on November 15, 2024, and served Plaintiff with the answer by mail that same day. (Answer pdf p. 27.) Plaintiff filed the demurrer on November 19, 2024. Thus, the demurrer was timely filed.

 

b.     Meet and Confer

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to meet and confer before filing a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer, despite having met and conferred, or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).) 

 

Plaintiff submits no declaration showing Plaintiff met and conferred with Defendant prior to filing the demurrer.

 

However, “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(4).)

 

c.      Legal Standard

 

Plaintiff may demur to an answer¿on the grounds¿that¿the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense or the answer is uncertain.¿¿(Code Civ. Proc.¿§¿430.20(a)-(b).)¿ “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc.¿§¿430.20(b).) Under¿Code of Civil Procedure¿section 431.30, subdivision¿(g), every affirmative defense “. . .¿shall be separately stated, and the several defenses shall refer to the causes of action which they are intended to answer, in a manner by which they may be intelligibly distinguished.”¿

 

“In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)

d.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s affirmative defenses fail to state sufficient facts to constitute defenses and are uncertain. (Motion p. 3.)

 

1.     Res Judicata

 

“’Res judicata’ describes the preclusive effect of a final judgment on the merits” and “prevents relitigation of the same cause of action in a second suit between the same parties or parties in privity with them.” (Association of Irritated Residents v. Department of Conservation (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1202, 1218.) “[I]f a plaintiff prevails in an action, the cause is merged into the judgment and may not be asserted in a subsequent lawsuit; a judgment for the defendant serves as a bar to further litigation of the same cause of action.”   (Id. at pp. 1218-1219.) The elements of res judicata are: “(1) the decision in the prior proceeding is final and on the merits; (2) the present proceeding is on the same cause of action as the prior proceeding; and (3) the parties in the present proceeding or parties in privity with them were parties to the prior proceeding.”  (Id. at p. 1219.) Put otherwise, res judicata “arises if a second suit involves (1) the same cause of action (2) between the same parties [or their privies] (3) after a final judgment on the merits in the first suit.” (Id.)

 

“Given finite family law jurisdiction, a tort action claiming damages cannot be joined with or pleaded in a dissolution proceeding.” (Boblitt v. Boblitt (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 603, 613.) Because a family court judge has “no power to award damages, [their] refusal to do so has no preclusive effect.” (Id.)

 

In Boblitt v. Boblitt, supra, 190 Cal.App.4th at p. 606, the Court of Appeal held that the trial court erred by granting judgment on the pleadings because the family court judgment was not final, and a martial dissolution proceeding was “not based on the same primary right as a tort action based on domestic violence[.]” The Court stated:

 

Because the same primary right does not underlie a request for spousal support in a marital dissolution proceeding and a tort action for damages, the doctrine of claim preclusion does not preclude a person from offering evidence of domestic violence in connection with a request for spousal support in a dissolution proceeding, then suing for damages for domestic violence, or vice versa. Accordingly, a judgment in a dissolution proceeding where claims of domestic violence were (or could have been) litigated with relation to a claim for spousal support does not preclude a later tort action for domestic violence.

 

(Id. at p. 613.)

 

Defendant asserts that all of Plaintiff’s causes of action are barred by res judicata. (Answer p. 1.) Defendant attaches two exhibits to the answer: (1) a judgment entering dissolution of the marriage between Defendant and Plaintiff; and (2) a sanctions order from the same case ordering Plaintiff to pay $10,705.00 in financial sanctions and $900.00 in attorneys’ fees. (Ex. A; Ex. B.)

 

Defendant states that “Plaintiff made all of these allegations in Family Court and Orders were made”, “there is identity of parties or their privies between the prior and current lawsuits” and “[a] final judgment on the merits in a prior action is attached.” (Answer pp. 2-3, 5-6, 8-9, 10-11, 13-14, 16-17.) Defendant further states that “[i]f a prior lawsuit involved the same parties or their privies and the same issues, and a final judgment was rendered on the merits, res judicata bars the plaintiff from relitigating the IIED claim in the current action.” (See e.g., Answer p. 3.)

 

The dissolution order references entering a judgment of dissolution, child and family support, child custody and visitation, property division, and attorneys’ fees. (Ex. A.) The order for financial sanctions shows that the family court ordered Plaintiff to pay $10,705.00 in financial sanctions, and states that “[t]he court finds that the explanation offered by [Plaintiff] to explain his failure to cooperate in the preparation of the proposed judgment and other matters leading to delays in the case, which made it necessary for [Defendant’s] counsel to spend additional time and to seek additional court orders, is not credible on the issue.” (Ex. B.) However, these orders are silent as to whether the family court resolved claims involving intentional interference with consortium, conspiracy to interfere with consortium, domestic violence, battery, assault, or intentional infliction of emotional distress.  Finally, as noted by the authority cited above, tort actions cannot be joined with or pleaded in a dissolution proceeding and the family court has no authority to award damages for tort actions.

 

Accordingly, Defendant has not asserted facts sufficient to constitute a res judicata defense, and this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

2.     Collateral Estoppel

 

“Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, ‘precludes relitigation of issues argued and decided in prior proceedings.”  (Boblitt v. Boblitt, supra, 190 Cal.App.4th at p. 610.) “[A] judgment in a dissolution proceeding where claims . . . were, in fact, litigated . . . may have preclusive effect under the doctrine of issue preclusion, which ‘precludes relitigation of issues argued and decided in prior proceedings.” (Id. at p. 614.) The party raising the defense must show “that the parties litigated in the dissolution proceeding all of the allegations [the other party] seeks to raise in the [present] case . . . or that [the judge] resolved all of those allegations against [them].” (Id.)

 

Defendant asserts that all of Plaintiff’s causes of action are barred by collateral estoppel. (Answer pp. 1, 4, 6, 9, 12, 14, 17.)

 

Defendant cites the standard for collateral estoppel, asserts that the judgment was final, and asserts that Plaintiff “made all of these allegations in Family Court and Orders were made.” (Answer pp. 2, 4-6, 8-10, 12-14, 16-17.) Defendant further states that “Plaintiff made all of these false allegations of being hurt and Defendant Mother breaking his belongings during the divorce and it was already litigated.” (Answer p. 1.) Defendant states “Plaintiff already made the allegations of being verbally abused and belittled during the marriage, with the family court finding no truth and granting the remedy of divorce. Plaintiff was sanctioned extensively for his over litigiousness with extensive attorney fees sanctions.” (Answer p. 1.) Finally, Defendant states that “All issues were decided and adjudged in Family Court final orders.”

 

This court finds that Defendant’s answer sufficiently raises the collateral estoppel defense and overrules the demurrer on this ground.

 

This court further finds that Defendant’s answer sufficiently acquaints Plaintiff with the nature, source, and extent of Defendant’s collateral estoppel defense and overrules the demurrer on the ground this defense is uncertain.

 

3.     Unclean Hands

 

“Although the venerable unclean-hands defense has been described in many iterations, courts have developed and now consistently apply a ‘three-pronged test to determine the effect to be given to the plaintiff’s unclean hands conduct. Whether the particular misconduct is a bar to the alleged claim for relief depends on (1) analogous case law, (2) the nature of the misconduct, and (3) the relationship of the misconduct to the claimed injuries.” (Padideh v. Moradi (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 418, 426.)  “Any conduct that violates good conscience, or good faith, or other equitable standards of conduct is sufficient to invoke the doctrine.” (Id.) As to the third prong, the conduct “must relate directly to the transaction concerning which the . . . complaint is made. It must infect the cause of action involved and affect the equitable relations between the litigants.” (Id.)

 

Defendant asserts that “Plaintiff lost custody of his daughter due to his continuing behavior not in the best interest of the minor and being obsessed with over litigating in multiple courts, rather than investing his time and energy in re-unification with their daughter.” (Answer p. 1.)

 

It is unclear to this court what wrongful acts Defendant is asserting are attached to which causes of action. Defendant raises the unclean hands doctrine at the beginning of the answer (Answer p.. 1.) and states that each cause of action is barred by the unclean hands doctrine (Answer pp. 5, 8, 11, 13, 16.) It is not clear to the court what alleged wrongful conduct Defendant is asserting that might allow the unclean hands doctrine to be applied to each cause of action. And this court fails to see how any of the wrongful acts Defendant asserts Plaintiff engaged in directly relate to the causes of action for domestic violence, battery, or assault. Accordingly, this court finds that the unclean hands defense is uncertain and sustains the demurrer as to this defense.

 

III.            MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Plaintiff states Plaintiff filed a “Demurrer and Motion to Strike.” (Demurrer p. 2.) However, the motion Plaintiff filed raises issues relating only to a demurrer. Defendant filed no separate motion to strike.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff filed no motion to strike for this court to consider.

 

IV.            LEAVE TO AMEND

 

“A plaintiff against whom a demurrer is sustained is entitled to leave to amend the defective complaint if [they] can ‘prove a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.’” (Shaeffer v. Califia Farms, LLC (2020) 44 Cal.App.5th 1125, 1145.) “The onus is on the plaintiff to articulate the ‘specific ways’ to cure the identified defect, and absent such an articulation, a trial . . . court may grant leave to amend ‘only if a potentially effective amendment is both apparent and consistent with the plaintiff’s theory of the case.’” (Id.) When a Plaintiff fails to describe “what additional facts she would plead . . . [they] ha[ve] not satisfied her burden of showing that [they] can amend the complaint to cure its defects.” (Mohler v. County of Santa Clara (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 418, 428.) 

 

Defendant submitted no opposition showing Defendant can allege additional facts supporting the res judicata defense. Further, a family court has no authority to award damages for tort actions. (See Boblitt v. Boblitt (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 603, 613.)  Accordingly, this court finds there is no reasonable possibility that Defendant can successfully amend the res judicata affirmative defense.

 

This court finds a reasonable probability Defendant can cure the defects relating to the unclean hands affirmative defense and grants Defendant leave to amend that defense.

 

V.              CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains Plaintiff’s demurrer as to Defendant’s res judicata defense without leave to amend. This court overrules the demurrer as to Defendant’s collateral estoppel defense. Finally, this court sustains the demurrer as to Defendant’s unclean hands defense with leave to amend.

I.                BACKGROUND¿ 

 

On January 9, 2024, Plaintiff Orlando Garcia (“Plaintiff”) visited Syndicate Outfitters (the “Store”) and discovered the Store’s door handles and transaction counters were not compliant with the American with Disabilities Act (“ADA”). (Complaint ¶¶ 4, 10, 14-15.) Defendant Reseda Blvd, LLC owns the property where the Store is located. (Complaint ¶ 3.)

 

On May 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants 6000 Reseda Blvd, LLC, The Syndicate Apparel Store, Inc., and Does 1 through 50, alleging two causes of action for: (1) violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act; and (2) violation of the California Disabled Persons Act.

 

On September 30, 2024, Defendants 6000 Reseda Blvd, LLC and The Syndicate Apparel Store, Inc. (“Defendants”) filed a motion to strike (the “Motion”). On November 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On December 4, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

 

II.             JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request this court take judicial notice of legislative history relating to the Unruh Civil Rights Act (“UCRA”) and Disabled Persons Act (“DPA”) and various amendments to the UCRA and DPA. (Request for Judicial Notice pp. 1-2.)

 

“Legislative committee reports and analyses, including statements pertaining to the bill’s purpose, are properly the subject of judicial notice.” (People v. Superior Court (Ferguson) (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1532.) “Also appropriate for judicial notice are prior versions of a bill as well as analyses of such earlier versions, which shed light on how the Legislature arrived at the ultimately enacted statute.” (Id.)

 

This court grants Defendants’ request and takes judicial notice of Defendants’ Exhibits 1 through 7.

¿ 

A. Timeliness of Filings¿ 

¿

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion to consider untimely pleadings.¿ (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281-282.)

 

On August 29, 2024, the parties entered a stipulation agreeing to set aside default and allow Defendants to file a responsive pleading within thirty (30) days. (Joint Stipulation filed 09/05/24.) Defendants timely filed the Motion on September 30, 2024.

 

Defendants timely filed the Motion more than 16 court days before the hearing. Defendants also timely filed the reply five court days before the hearing.

 

Plaintiff filed the opposition on November 27, 2024. Nine court days before the hearing was November 26, 2024. Thus, Plaintiff’s opposition was untimely. Defendants argue Plaintiff’s failure to timely file the opposition hampered Defendants’ ability to respond. (Reply p. 2.) This court exercises its discretion and considers the opposition.

¿ 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” before the filing of a motion to strike to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the motion to strike.  

 

Defendants’ counsel submits a declaration showing they satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (Mohammadi Declaration ¶¶ 1-6.)

IV.           LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿ 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike “any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. V.              DISCUSSION

¿ 

Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s request for injunctive relief under the UCRA which seeks to compel Defendants: (1) “to remove all presently existing architectural barriers”; (2) to “obtain biennial Certified Access Specialist (“CASP”) architectural inspections of the subject facility to verify on-going ADA compliance and follow those inspection’s recommendations of all readily achievable barrier removal”; (3) to implement “accessibility policies and requiring annual employee training on providing full and equal access to clients or customers with disabilities”; and (4) to prohibit “Defendants from violating the provisions of the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Unruh Civil Rights Act.” (Motion p. 2.) Defendants argue Plaintiff’s prayer for injunctive relief is improper because the UCRA does not allow a Plaintiff to seek “affirmative relief compelling defendant to remediate property.” (Motion p. 8.)

 

Plaintiff alleges two causes of action in the complaint: (1) violation of the UCRA; and (2) violation of the California DPA. (Complaint pp. 6-7.) However, in a footnote to Plaintiff’s prayer for relief, Plaintiff states: “Plaintiff is not invoking Section 55 of the California Civil Code and is not seeking injunctive relief under the [DPA] at all.” (Complaint p. 8 fn. 2.) Accordingly, if Plaintiff is entitled to injunctive relief, it must be authorized under the UCRA.

 

Defendants bring Thurston v. Midvale Corp. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 634, 636, to this court’s attention, but argue it did not address the issue raised in this case. (Motion p. 19.) This court agrees. 

 

In Thurston v. Midvale Corp., supra, 39 Cal.App.5th at p. 638, 655, the trial court granted an injunction requiring the defendant to redesign its website. On appeal, the Court of Appeal considered “whether title III of the ADA applie[d] to th[e] website.” (Id. at p. 636.) The Court held that “including websites connected to a physical place of public accommodation is not only consistent with the plain language of Title III, but it is also consistent with Congress’s mandate that the ADA keep pace with changing technology to effectuate the intent of the statute.” (Id. at p. 644.) The Court also stated:

 

Appellant has not cited any cases discussing the requirements for an injunction under the Unruh Civil Rights Act and suggests no such case exists. If that is true, appellant makes no argument for what those requirements should be: appellant does not discuss the language, legislative intent or purpose of the Unruh Civil Rights Act or Civil Code section 52, which authorizes “any person aggrieved” to seek an injunction. (Id., subd. (c).) . . . Appellant's only reference to section 52 takes a short phrase out of context. Appellant argues that “preventative relief cannot be ‘deemed necessary’ to ensure [Thurston] is afforded a right of access” to the restaurant. In fact, the language of section 52, subdivision (c)(3) authorizes a complainant to seek preventative relief “as the complainant deems necessary to ensure the full enjoyment of the rights described in this section.”7 Thus, appellant has failed to demonstrate error. 

 

(Id. at p. 652.) Accordingly, Thurston did not address whether the UCRA authorizes an injunction requiring a party to take affirmative action.

 

The parties cite no other published cases discussing whether a plaintiff may seek an injunction requiring defendants take affirmative actions under the UCRA, and this court has found none. Accordingly, this court considers the statutory language.

 

Statutory interpretation “begin[s] [with] examining the statute’s words, giving them a plain and commonsense meaning.” (People v. Thompson (2024) 106 Cal.App.5th 101, 112.) “If the language is clear and unambiguous there is no need for construction, and [the court need not] resort to indicia of the intent of the Legislature.” (Id.) “If there is no ambiguity in the language, [the court] presume[s] the Legislature meant what it said and the plain meaning of the statute governs.” (Id.)

 

Civil Code section 52 contains the remedies that are authorized under the UCRA. Subdivision (c) states that Plaintiffs may seek:

 

A request for preventive relief, including an application for a permanent or temporary injunction, restraining order, or other order against the person or persons responsible for the conduct, as the complainant deems necessary to ensure the full enjoyment of the rights described in this section.  

 

(Civ. Code § 52(c).)

 

Section 52 does not define “preventive relief.”  However, the Civil Code title referencing specific and preventative relief states: “Preventive relief is given by prohibiting a party from doing that which ought not to be done.”  (Civ. Code § 3368.) Similarly., in Ohaver v. Fenech (1928) 206 Cal.118, 122, the California Supreme Court stated, “As a general rule, we think we may say that when the injunction merely grants preventative relief it is prohibitive, but when it directly or directly grants affirmative relief it is mandatory.” And, in Davenport v. Blue Cross (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 435, 446, the Court stated: “[A]n injunction is prohibitory if it requires a person to refrain from a particular act and mandatory if it compels performance of an affirmative act that changes the position of the parties.”

 

“[A]bsent indications to the contrary, ‘a word or phrase . . . accorded a particular meaning in one part or portion of the law, should be accorded the same meaning in other parts or portions of the law.” (North American Title Company v. Superior Court of Fresno County (2024) 17 Cal.5th 155, 590.)   Thus, with no indication to the contrary, this court presumes that the term “preventive relief” in section 52 should be accorded the same meaning as other portions of the Civil Code and prior judicial decisions. Accordingly, Civil Code section 52, subdivision (c) authorizes only injunctions that prohibits a party from doing that which ought not to be done and does not authorize relief requiring a party take affirmative action.

 

This conclusion is also supported by other portions of the UCRA. Subdivision (g) of Civil Code section 52, states that:

 

This section does not require any construction, alteration, repair, structural or otherwise, or modification of any sort whatsoever, beyond that construction, alteration, repair, or modification that is otherwise required by other provisions of law, to any new or existing establishment, facility, building, improvement, or any other structure, nor does this section augment, restrict, or alter in any way the authority of the State Architect to require construction, alteration, repair, or modifications that the State Architect otherwise possesses pursuant to other laws.

 

Further, the general UCRA statute, Civil Code section 51, subdivision (d), states that:

 

Nothing in this section shall be construed to require any construction, alteration, repair, structural or otherwise, or modification of any sort whatsoever, beyond that construction, alteration, repair, or modification that is otherwise required by other provisions of law, to any new or existing establishment, facility, building, improvement, or any other structure, nor shall anything in this section be construed to augment, restrict, or alter in any way the authority of the State Architect to require construction, alteration, repair, or modifications that the State Architect otherwise possesses pursuant to other laws.

 

Accordingly, by its plain language, the UCRA does not require modifications to existing establishments.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff raises various arguments.

 

Plaintiff argues Plaintiff sufficiently plead the causes of action in the complaint. (Opposition pp. 3-8.) Defendants are only challenging the prayer for injunctive relief and have not argued that Plaintiff failed to sufficiently plead the causes of action. Thus, this court does not address this argument.

 

Plaintiff argues the request for injunctive relief is proper because it is “based on other provisions of law.” (Opposition pp. 8-9.) Plaintiff cites portions of the ADA and federal regulations and argues that “[s]ince Plaintiff’s prayer of modifications, if any, are derived from the ADA standards and Federal Regulations, which will squarely fall under the category of ‘other provisions of law’ mentioned in [Civil Code] Sections 51(d) and 52(g) . . . Plaintiff’s prayers are . . . well within the scope of Sections § 52(c)(3).” (Opposition p. 9.)

 

However, section 52, subdivision (c) only authorizes preventive relief. And, based on the language cited above, preventive relief does not require a party take affirmative action.  In contrast, Civil Code section 55 does not limit the injunctive relief to “preventive” but rather states: “Any person who is aggrieved or potentially aggrieved by a violation of Section 54 or 54.1 of this code, Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 4450) of Division 5 of Title 1 of the Government Code, or Part 5.5 (commencing with Section 19955) of Division 13 of the Health and Safety Code may bring an action to enjoin the violation. The prevailing party in the action shall be entitled to recover reasonable attorney's fees.” (Emphasis added.) Thus, the legislature limited injunctive relief to “preventive injunctions” in section 52 but did not similarly limit section 55. However, Plaintiff explicitly states they are not invoking section 55 to seek injunctive relief. (Complaint p. 8 fn. 8.) Defendants suggest Plaintiff is not seeking relief under section 55 because Defendants would then be entitled to attorneys’ fees under that section if Defendants prevail. (Reply p. 2.) Moreover, Plaintiff has not asserted a cause of action under the ADA.

 

Plaintiff also argues he is “not seeking mandatory injunctive relief, but rather seeks relief that ensures the full enjoyment of their rights under the Act.” (Opposition p. 9.) However, “[t]he substance of the injunction, not the form, determines whether it is mandatory or prohibitory.” (Davenport v. Blue Cross, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 447.) In Davenport, Blue Cross argued the preliminary injunction was mandatory because it “required Blue Cross to perform affirmative acts of authorizing and paying for the treatment, thereby changing the status quo” while “Plaintiff argue[d] it was merely a prohibitory injunction because it enjoined Blue Cross from persisting in an anticipatory breach of its contract, which threatened plaintiff’s ‘very existence,’ and her health was the ‘status quo’ which required preservation.” (Id.) The Court agreed with Blue Cross’ interpretation because “[t]he injunction plainly ordered Blue Cross to perform affirmative acts which would change the position of the parties, by compelling Blue Cross to authorize and pay for plaintiff’s treatment.” (Id.)

 

Plaintiff is seeking to compel Defendants to remove “all presently existing architectural barriers”, to “obtain biennial Certified Access Specialist (‘CASp’) architectural inspections”, and to implement accessibility policies and annual employee training. (Complaint Prayer p. 8.) Like Davenport, these requests would require Defendants take affirmative action.

 

However, this court finds that the Plaintiff’s prayer for relief seeking to prohibit “Defendants from violating the provisions of the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Unruh Civil Rights Act” could be interpreted as “prohibiting a party from doing that which ought not to be done.”  (Civ. Code § 3368.) Accordingly, this court declines to strike that provision from the complaint.

 

Next, Plaintiff argues that the relief is “consistent with the statute’s purpose of preventing future harm” and “even if for the sake of an argument we consider the prayed injunctions as ‘mandatory,’ the Unruh Act does not restrict injunctive relief to prohibitory forms, allowing courts the discretion to issue mandatory injunctions when appropriate.” (Opposition p. 10.)

 

Plaintiff cites no authority supporting these arguments and this court finds they are meritless. The UCRA “’must be construed liberally in order to carry out its purpose’ to ‘create and preserve a nondiscriminatory environment in California business establishments by “banishing” or “eradicating” arbitrary, invidious discrimination by such establishments.” (Munson v. Del Taco, Inc. (2009) 46 Cal.4th 661, 666.) However, “[t]his court has no power to rewrite the statute so as to make it conform to a presumed intention which is not expressed.” (California Teachers Assn. v. Governing Bd. Of Rialto Unified School District (1997) 14 Cal.4th 627, 633.) The UCRA specifies the relief available. (See Civ. Code § 52.) This court cannot rewrite the statute to provide for additional forms of relief not authorized by the UCRA.

 

Finally, Plaintiff argues that a motion to strike is inappropriate “the complaint is well-pleaded and warrants a decision on [the] merits.” (Opposition p. 10.) Plaintiff further argues that Defendants have not shown the allegations are “irrelevant, false, or improper” or “not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Opposition p. 10.)

 

This court disagrees. Motions to strike are used to challenge prayers for relief that are not authorized under the complaint’s allegations, such as punitive damages. (See (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164 [“The adequacy of the punitive damages allegations, could, however, have ben tested by a motion to strike.”])

 

Thus, injunctions requiring a party to take affirmative action are not available under the UCRA.

 

VI.           LEAVE TO AMEND

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, ‘leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.’” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.)¿

 

This court finds there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiff can cure the defect and grants Plaintiff leave to amend the complaint.

 

VII.         RECLASSIFICATION

 

Defendants state that should be Motion be granted, “the only remaining claim for relief will be damages in an amount less than the jurisdictional limit of this Court” and “[t]he case should then be transferred to limited jurisdiction.” (Motion p. 20.)

 

This court granted Plaintiff leave to amend the complaint. Thus, reclassification is premature.

 

VIII.      CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants the Motion to strike the prayer for relief seeking to compel Defendants to remove “all presently existing architectural barriers”, to “obtain biennial Certified Access Specialist (‘CASp’) architectural inspections”, and to implement accessibility policies and annual employee training. (Complaint Prayer p. 8.)

 

This court denies the Motion to strike the prayer for relief seeking to prohibit “Defendants from violating the provisions of the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Unruh Civil Rights Act.” (Complaint Prayer p. 8.)

 

Finally, this court grants Plaintiff leave to amend the complaint.
