ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On November 29, 2018, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Justin Alan Newsome (“Defendant”) and Does 1-5 alleging property damage following a vehicle collision. (Compl., ¶ 7.)

 

On March 8, 2019, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On January 29, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for Entry of Judgment in the Event of Default (the “Stipulation”).

 

On March 2, 2021, the Hon. James E. Blancarte signed an order dismissing the action but retaining jurisdiction under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. (3/2/21 Order.)

 

On July 16, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment Against Defendant.

 

On September 10, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Withdrawal informing all parties Plaintiff was withdrawing the Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment Against Defendant.

 

On September 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment Against Defendant (the “Motion”).

 

No Opposition was filed.  

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6: 

 
(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following: 

 

(1) The party. 

(2) An attorney who represents the party. 

(3) If an insurer is defending and indemnifying a party to the action, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the party’s behalf. This paragraph does not apply if the party whom the insurer is defending would be liable under the terms of the settlement for any amount above the policy limits.” 

 

The Stipulation filed by Plaintiff was signed by Plaintiff’s authorized representative Caryl A. Holthouse on January 15, 2021, by Plaintiff’s counsel on January 7, 2021, and by Defendant on an unknown date. (1/29/21 Stipulation, p. 4.) The Court notes there are also some discrepancies in the date markers on the Stipulation form as the “Dated” lines end in “2020”; however, the dates on the line are complete in and of themselves and end in “2021” or “21.”  

 

The Stipulation provides the matter would be settled for a total sum of $13,370.15 plus costs of suit and prejudgment interest from the date of loss. (1/29/21 Stipulation, ¶ 1.) The Stipulation then states Defendant is to pay a total sum of $7,000, reflecting $5,000 paid by Defendant’s insurance carrier and $2,000 to be paid by Defendant at a rate of $50 per month, beginning March 15, 2021 and continuing on the fifteenth day of each month thereafter until paid in full. (1/29/21 Stipulation, ¶ 2.)

 

The Stipulation further states that failure to make a timely payment constitutes a default and, in the event of default, there is a 15-day grace period for Defendant to make payment, Plaintiff shall give written notice to Defendant regarding the default, and Defendant shall be allowed an additional ten days from the date of written notice to cure the particular default. (1/29/21 Stipulation, ¶¶ 5, 7.) If Defendant fails to cure the default, the Stipulation states “Plaintiff may immediately cause Judgment to be entered pursuant to the terms set forth in this Stipulation for the full amount of the agreed upon judgment as set forth in Paragraph 1 less any monies paid to date of the breach; and shall also file a partial Satisfaction of Judgment for all sums previously paid pursuant to this Stipulation.”  (1/29/21 Stipulation, ¶ 5.)

 

The Court finds the Stipulation to be valid and enforceable under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. Plaintiff provides evidence that Defendant made partial payment – Defendant’s insurance carrier paid a total of $5,000 and Defendant made payments in the sum of $750.25. (Benson Decl., ¶ 5.) As of the date this Motion was filed, however, Plaintiff had received no further payments from Defendant since the Notice of Default. (Benson Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff also presents evidence of the default payment letter sent to Defendant on June 12, 2024 advising Defendant of the default and the balance then due in the sum of $1,249.75. (Benson Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 2.)

 

Plaintiff requests that a judgment of $8,083.40, based on the principal amount of $13,370.15, less payments received in the amount of $5,750.25 plus suit costs in the amount of $463.50 ($388.00 lawsuit filing fee plus $75.50 service costs), be entered against Defendant. (Benson Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

Plaintiff’s unopposed Motion is GRANTED.

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Insurance Company’s unopposed Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment Against Defendant is GRANTED.

 

Judgment on Stipulation is entered in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant Justin Alan Newson in the Total amount of $8,083.40.

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On July 8, 2024, Petitioners Iehab A. Ibrahim and Christina M. Ibrahim (“Petitioners”) filed the instant verified Petition for Release of Property from Lien (the “Petition”) against Universal Floor Source, Inc. (“Respondent”). The Petition seeks an order releasing property commonly known as 1921 Voorhees Avenue, Redondo Beach, California 90278 (the “Subject Property”) from a mechanic’s lien recorded on November 16, 2023 by Respondent. (Petn., ¶¶ 1, 2.)

 

Petitioners filed a proof of service demonstrating Respondent was substitute served with the Petition and notice of hearing on August 16, 2024 at 24520 Crenshaw Boulevard, Unit A, Torrance, CA 90505 (the “Crenshaw Address”). (8/27/24 Proof of Service.) The proof of service also includes a declaration of due diligence and a declaration of mailing demonstrating the documents were mailed to Respondent to the Crenshaw Address on August 19, 2024. (8/27/24 Proof of Service.)

 

On September 4, 2024, Petitioners filed proof of service demonstrating Respondent was served by mail with the notice of hearing on August 20, 2024. (9/4/24 Proof of Service.)

 

On November 4, 2024, Petitioners filed a Proposed Order and proof of service demonstrating Respondent was served by mail with the Proposed Order on November 4, 2024. (11/4/24 Proof of Service.)

 

No Response has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

After a mechanic’s lien has been recorded, “[t]he owner of property or the owner of any interest in property subject to a claim of lien may petition the court for an order to release the property from the claim of lien if the claimant has not commenced an action to enforce the lien within the time provided in Section 8460.”  (Civ. Code, § 8480, subd. (a).)  A claimant must commence an action to enforce a lien within 90 days of recording the lien. (Civ. Code, § 8460, subd. (a).)  Section 8460 further provides that “[i]f the claimant does not commence an action to enforce the lien within that time, the claim of lien expires and is unenforceable.” (Civ. Code, § 8460, subd. (a).) Section 8460 also provides that the 90-day time limit to commence an action to enforce a lien does not apply if there was an agreement to extend credit and a notice of that fact was recorded within 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien or more than 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien but before a purchaser or encumbrancer for value and in good faith acquires rights in the property.  (Civ. Code, § 8460, subd. (b).) 

 

III.              Discussion

 

Petition Requirements

            Civil Code section 8484 requires that the petition for release order be verified by the petitioner and allege the following:

 

(a) The date of recordation of the claim of lien. A certified copy of the claim of lien shall be attached to the petition.

(b) The county in which the claim of lien is recorded.

(c) The book and page or series number of the place in the official records where the claim of lien is recorded.

(d) The legal description of the property subject to the claim of lien.

(e) Whether an extension of credit has been granted under Section 8460, if so to what date, and that the time for commencement of an action to enforce the lien has expired.

(f) That the owner has given the claimant notice under Section 8482 demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien and that the claimant is unable or unwilling to do so or cannot with reasonable diligence be found.

(g) Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.

(h) Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien.

 

            (Civ. Code, § 8484.)

 

A property owner “may not petition the court for a release order … unless at least 10 days before filing the petition the owner gives the claimant notice demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the claim of lien.” (Civ. Code, § 8482.)

 

The instant Petition is verified and is accompanied by a certified copy of the Claim of Mechanic’s Lien, Document No. 20230794418, filed with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office on November 16, 2023. (Petn., Exh. A.) The Petition states the legal description of the subject property, that no extension of credit has been granted, that the 90-day time period to enforce the lien has expired, that Petitioners gave notice under Section 8482 of their demand and Respondent did not respond, that Petitioners have not filed for bankruptcy, and that no other restraint exists preventing Respondent from filing an action to enforce the lien. (Petn., at pp. 1-2.)

 

Petitioners also include a copy of an April 9, 2024 letter that was mailed to Respondent at 24520 S. Crenshaw Boulevard, Suite A, Torrance, California 90505 – the same address listed on the Claim of Mechanic’s Lien filed with the County Recorder’s Office – demanding that the mechanic’s lien be released. (Petn., Exh. B.)

 

Service Requirements

 

“The petitioner shall serve a copy of the petition and a notice of hearing on the claimant at least 15 days before the hearing.  Service shall be made in the same manner as service of summons, or by certified or registered mail, postage prepaid, return receipt requested, addressed to the claimant as provided in Section 8108.”  (Civ. Code, § 8486, subd. (b).) Section 8108 provides addresses at which a respondent must be served, depending on the title of the person to be served. The petitioner bears the burden of proving he complied with service and date for hearing requirements. (Civ. Code, § 8488, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Respondent was served by substitute service on August 16, 2024 at the Crenshaw Address. While the Crenshaw Address is substantially similar to the address that Respondent listed on the Claim of Mechanic’s Lien filed with the County Recorder’s Office (Petn., Exh. A), the Court notes there is a discrepancy between the two addresses – namely, the Crenshaw Address does not contain the “South” demarcation present in the address listed on the Claim of Mechanic’s Lien.

 

Further, Petitioners failed to serve Respondent with the Proposed Order until November 4, 2024, just five court days before the instant hearing.

 

Accordingly, Petitioners are ordered to file a new proof of service or clarify the discrepancy at the next hearing.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On April 10, 2009, judgment was entered in this action in favor of Plaintiff and Judgment Creditor James Rodriquez (“Plaintiff”) and against Defendant and Judgment Debtor Bonami, Inc. (“Defendant”) in the amount of $3,105. (Motion, p. 3; 4/10/09 Minute Order; 8/24/18 Application For and Renewal of Judgment.)

 

Subsequently, Plaintiff assigned the judgment to Ex Parte Collection Services, LLC (“Ex Parte Collection”). (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 2.)

 

On August 24, 2018, Ex Parte Collection, claiming to be assignee of record, filed an Application For and Renewal of Judgment in the amount of $8,215.02, reflecting the $3,105 total judgment, $2,250 for costs after judgment, $2,830.02 interest after judgment, and a $30 filing fee. (8/24/18 Application For and Renewal of Judgment.)

 

On September 17, 2018, the Court approved Ex Parte Collection’s Application for and Renewal of Judgment. (9/17/18 Notice of Renewal of Judgment.)

 

On October 1, 2020, the California Secretary of State suspended Ex Parte Collection’s powers and privileges for nonpayment of taxes to the Franchise Tax Board. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 3, Exhs. 1, 2.)

 

On September 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Notice of Motion and Motion for Order Reassigning Judgment Back to James Rodriguez (the “Motion”).

 

No Opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Plaintiff asserts that he filed an “Acknowledgment of Assignment of Judgment” on May 17, 2011 assigning the judgment to Ex Parte Collection and now seeks to reassign the judgment back to Plaintiff. Plaintiff contends reassignment is necessary because Ex Parte Collection is now a suspended entity that cannot legally collect or enforce the judgment on Plaintiff’s behalf and refuses to reassign the judgment back to Plaintiff despite Plaintiff’s multiple demands. (Motion, p. 3; Rodriguez Decl., Exhs. 1, 2, 3.)

 

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 681.020, “An assignee of a judgment is not entitled to enforce the judgment under this title unless an acknowledgment of assignment of judgment to that assignee has been filed or the assignee has otherwise become an assignee of record under Section 673.”

 

            The Court’s records indicate that Plaintiff filed an acknowledgment of assignment of judgment to Ex Parte Collection on May 17, 2011.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 128, subdivision (a), “Every court shall have the power to… compel obedience to its judgments, orders, and process, and to the orders of a judge out of court, in an action or proceeding pending therein.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a); see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 177, 187; Yolanda’s, Inc. v. Kahl & Goveia Comm’l Real Estate (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 509, 514.)

 

“[T]he relationship between a collection agency and a judgment creditor is fiduciary in nature.” (Cross v. Bonded Adjustment Bureau (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 266, 276 (Cross).) “ ‘An assignment for collection vests legal title in the assignee which is sufficient to enable him to maintain an action in his own name, but the assignor retains the equitable interest in the thing assigned…. Such an assignee has been referred to as the trustee or agent of the assignor…, and a fiduciary relationship exists between them.” (Id., at pp. 276-277.) Such a relationship necessitates an obligation for the collection agency and judgment creditor to “ ‘act in the highest good faith by reason of such relationship.’ ” (See id., at p. 276; Wolf v. Superior Court (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 25, 29.)

 

            Ultimately, “ ‘[w]hether the defendant breached [a fiduciary duty] towards the plaintiff is a question of fact.’ ” (Marzec v. Cal. Public Employees Retirement System (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 889, 915, citation omitted.)

 

Here, Plaintiff assigned the judgment to Ex Parte Collections and thus created a fiduciary relationship between them. (See Cross, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 276.) After assignment, Plaintiff still retained equitable interest in the judgment, and Ex Parte Collections had a duty to act in the highest good faith regarding its obligations to Plaintiff given the fiduciary nature of their relationship. (Id., at pp. 276-277; Wolf, supra, 107 Cal.App.4th at p. 29.)

 

However, Plaintiff’s evidence demonstrates that Ex Parte Collections failed to fulfill its duties as assignee through its suspension by the California Secretary of State and refusal to respond to Plaintiff’s repeated requests for reassignment. (Rodriguez Decl. ¶¶ 3-5, 8, Exhs. 1-3; see Marzec, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at p. 915.) Due to these failures, the Court agrees its prior judgment has been rendered “null and void in effect” since there is now no party with the legal right to collect the judgment. (Motion, p. 4; Code Civ. Proc., §§ 126, 177, 187.)  

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion and orders reassignment of the judgment back to Plaintiff.

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

Plaintiff James Rodriguez’s Motion for Order Reassigning Judgment is GRANTED.
