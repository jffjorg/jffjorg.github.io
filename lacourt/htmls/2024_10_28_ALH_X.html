DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 23AHCV02923    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: X


DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE/VACATE DEFAULT

 

Defendant Cheng has filed a motion to set aside and vacate the default entered against him on July 17, 2024.  

 

This lawsuit arises from a motor vehicle accident that occurred on December 17, 2021.  Plaintiff filed his Complaint on December 19, 2023.

 

Tentative Ruling

 

Defendant’s motion to set aside the default is GRANTED.  

 

The Order to Show Cause re: Default Judgment set for October 31, 2024 is advanced to this date and discharged as is the December 23, 2024 OSC hearing.

 

Defendant’s November 21, 2024 Demurrer remains on calendar.

 

Moving Party is to give notice. 

 

Legal Standard and Analysis

 

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief. [Citation.]”  (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.)  The discretionary relief provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) provide in relevant part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” 

 

Defendant moves to set aside and vacate the default entered against him on July 17, 2024.  The motion is grounded on claims of excusable neglect, including a lack of understanding due to limited English proficiency, a belief that his insurer, State Farm, would handle the legal proceedings, and not being served with a Statement of Damages.  Plaintiff opposes the motion, arguing that Defendant was properly served, had ample opportunity to respond, and that the default was correctly entered.

 

Defendant was personally served on February 19, 2024.  (Cheng Decl., ¶ 2.)  Despite acknowledging the receipt of the lawsuit documents, Defendant did not file a responsive pleading by the March 22, 2024 deadline.  Defendant attributes this failure to a reliance on State Farm to manage his defense.  (Cheng Decl., ¶ 8.)  Gabriella Garcia, a State Farm claims representative, declares that the State Farm agent who received Defendant’s paperwork on March 5, 2024 uploaded only the summons, and not the complaint, to the system, and thus State Farm did not know a complaint was filed.  (Garcia Decl., ¶ 2.)  Thus, the State Farm Claims Department was not aware of the lawsuit until Defendant’s agent called to follow up on the summons and complaint on June 4, 2024.  (Garcia Decl., ¶ 5.)  Default was entered against Defendant on July 17, 2024.

 

Defendant moves to set aside the judgment on two grounds.  First, Defendant asserts he was never served with the Statement of Damages.  (Cheng Decl., ¶ 4.)  Second, Defendant argues the motion should be set aside based on his and his insurer State Farm’s mistake, inadvertence, and excusable neglect which led to delays in realizing Defendant was served in this lawsuit.  (Garcia Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s failure to respond does not constitute excusable neglect under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).  Plaintiff argues that Defendant, having been properly served, had a responsibility to act within the statutory timeframe and that reliance on State Farm does not absolve him of this responsibility.  Furthermore, Plaintiff asserts that the absence of a Statement of Damages at this stage is irrelevant and does not void the default.

 

In reply, Defendant emphasizes the legal requirement for a plaintiff in a personal injury action to serve a Statement of Damages before a default can be entered.  Defendant argues that the default is void as a matter of law due to this failure.  Additionally, Defendant asserts that the motion to set aside was filed within the six-month statutory period.

 

Plaintiff argues that setting aside the default would cause significant prejudice by prolonging the litigation and increasing costs.  Defendant counters that Plaintiff will not suffer prejudice if the default is set aside, as the case is in the early stages with only a recent default.  Plaintiff has not sought a default judgment yet.

 

Here, the Court finds the failure to serve a Statement of Damages is a critical procedural misstep that voids the default as a matter of law.  The proof of service only shows service of the Summons, Complaint, ADR package, Civil Case Cover Sheet, and Notice of Case Management on Defendant, not a Statement of Damages.  Further, Plaintiff’s opposition concedes that there was no Statement of Damages served.  (Opposition, p. 3:5-6.)  Without service of a statement of damages on Defendant, it would be improper to enter default against him.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11, subds. (c) and (d).)  Moreover, the circumstances described by Defendant Cheng support his argument that the failure to respond could be considered excusable neglect.  The law favors resolving cases on their merits rather than on procedural technicalities, especially when there is no significant prejudice to the plaintiff.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to set aside the default is GRANTED.  The court will set aside the default entered against Defendant Dan Que Q. Cheng on July 17, 2024.

Case Number: 24NNCV00586    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: X


PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PRODUCTION DEMANDS

 

Plaintiff moves for an order to overrule Defendant objections and compel further objection-free responses and production to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One), Nos. 1-3, 7-9, 14-21, 30, and 31.

 

This is a lemon-law action.  Plaintiff purchased a 2023 Mercedes-Benz GLE-Class vehicle on January 6, 2023, which is alleged to have serious defects and nonconformities.  The complaint, filed on March 29, 2024, has two causes of action for violations of the Song-Beverly Act for breach express warranty and breach of implied warranty.

 

Tentative Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel is DENIED in part and GRANTED in part.  The motion is denied as to Request Nos. 1-3, 9, 15-21, and 31.

 

Defendant is ordered to serve a supplemental Code-compliant response to Request Nos. 7, 8, 14, and 30 within 30 days of this ruling. 

 

Analysis

 

The motion to compel a further response is denied as to Request Nos. 1, 9, 15, and 16.  Defendant’s original responses state “[o]n information and belief . . . no such documents exist” or that Defendant is unable to locate responsive documents.  Notwithstanding Defendant’s objections, Defendant indicates that there are no responsive documents.  These responses are sufficiently Code-compliant.

 

As to Request Nos. 2 and 3, Defendant has stated that it responded to the request “in whole” and by producing all documents.  This is sufficiently Code-compliant.  The motion is also denied as to Request Nos. 17, 18, 19, 20, 21, and 31, where Defendant has stated that it answered in full, as the original response said “despite a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, MBUSA has been unable to locate any documents responsive to” that request.  These are sufficiently Code-compliant responses.

 

Regarding Request Nos. 7, 8, and 14, Defendant’s responses include the phrase “and to which it has no objection.”  It is unclear from this response whether documents were withheld and on what basis.  To the extent that documents were, in fact, withheld, a further response and privilege log must be provided.

 

Regarding Request No. 30, the motion is granted.  Defendant is ordered to provide a Code-compliant response.  The Court limits the request to customer complaints relating to defects alleged in the plaintiff’s complaint in vehicles purchased in California for the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle.
