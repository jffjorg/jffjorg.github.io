DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02589    Hearing Date: December 31, 2024    Dept: W

FED REPORTER, INC. v. LESTER SNYDER

 

defendant/cross-complainant lester snyder’s motion to strike portions of plaintiff/cross-defendant’s complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        December 31, 2024              Trial Date:       March 24, 2025

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV02589

 

Moving Party:            Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lester Snyder

Responding Party:     Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Fed Reporter Inc.

Meet and Confer:      See footnote[1]

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 28, 2022, Plaintiff Fed Reporter, Inc. filed a complaint against Lester Snyder for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and breach of implied duty to perform with reasonable care. In October 2016, Fed Reporter engaged Mr. Snyder as an independent contractor to serve as a consultant in connection with Fed Reporter’s development and marketing of a software program. Plaintiff alleges after lengthy prior negotiations, the parties memorialized the terms of their agreement by executing a detailed written contract titled “Consulting Service Agreement”; however, Defendant Snyder repeatedly failed to perform his consulting services in a satisfactory manner and as contracted for by Fed Reporter. Plaintiff further alleges thereafter Defendant Snyder repudiated the express terms of the Consulting Agreement by falsely asserting: that he was the owner of the Product’s intellectual property; that he had licensed said Product to Fed Reporter; and that he was entitled to lifetime or “evergreen” royalty payments for Fed Reporter’s licensing of the Product. Following extensive settlement discussions, Defendant Snyder accepted a settlement offer that was made by Fed Reporter. However, Defendant Snyder later reneged on the settlement agreement, thereby forcing plaintiff to file suit.

 

On Marh 7, 2023, Snyder filed a cross-complaint against Fed Reporter, Inc. for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, breach of implied duty to perform with reasonable care, unjust enrichment, and declaratory relief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lester Snyder’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s Complaint is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lester Snyder moves to strike portions of the complaint filed by Fed Reporter, Inc. on the grounds portions of the complaint maintain improper and irrelevant matter. Specifically, Defendant Snyder requests that the court strike all references to purported settlement discussions and offers, including settlement discussions that occurred on November 10, 2022.

 

The court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (CCP § 436(a).)  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).)  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  (CCP § 436.)  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.)

 

Evidence Code section 1152(a) states “Evidence that a person has, in compromise or from humanitarian motives, furnished or offered or promised to furnish money or any other thing, act, or service to another who has sustained or will sustain or claims that he or she has sustained or will sustain loss or damage, as well as any conduct or statements made in negotiation thereof, is inadmissible to prove his or her liability for the loss or damage or any part of it.”¿¿ 

 

Defendant Snyder argues the court should strike the portions of the complaint that improperly include allegations related to November 10, 2022 settlement negotiations between Defendant Snyder and Fed Reporter, Inc. (see Compl. ¶¶18, 22, 23, 25, 26, 29 and 30). Defendant Snyder contends such references are clearly barred by Evidence Code § 1152, which aims to promote the settlement of disputes by ensuring that discussions and offers made during settlement negotiations cannot be used against a party in subsequent litigation. (See Caira v. Offner (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 12.) Moreover, the allegations attempt to use statements made during settlement discussions as evidence of Mr. Snyder’s alleged breach of the Consulting Agreement. Defendant Snyder contends this is not only irrelevant but also constitutes an improper attempt to leverage privileged information.

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion to strike.[2] In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant Snyder’s motion is untimely. Plaintiff contends a motion to strike any pleading must be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading” (e.g., 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint unless extended by court order or stipulation). (CCP § 435(b)(1); see also CRC Rule 3.1322.) In the instant matter, the operative complaint was filed on December 28, 2022, Defendant was served on January 25, 2023, and Defendant filed his answer on March 7, 2023. Accordingly, the deadline for Mr. Snyder to file and serve his present motion to strike was back on or about March 7, 2023—almost two (2) full years ago.

 

Defendant Snyder maintains this timeliness argument is flawed because the allegations Defendant seeks to strike pertain to newly discovered improprieties identified during discovery and Plaintiff fails to account for the court’s broad discretion under Code of Civil Procedure section 436, which allows the striking of irrelevant or improper matter from pleadings “at any time.”

 

The court disagrees. First, Defendant Snyder does not provide how he became aware that the complaint improperly referenced the settlement discussions during the discovery process and is unclear to the court how Defendant Snyder did not know the allegations in the complaint were regarding the settlement negotiations until two years later. Second, “at any time” is made at the court’s discretion. Plaintiff made this motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 435, which as noted by Plaintiff, must be made within the time allowed to respond to the pleading. Even so, nothing in the complaint asserts that the conduct or statements alleged were made during settlement negotiations. Paragraph 18 alleges Defendant Snyder reneged on the settlement agreement and thereby forcing Plaintiff to file suit; however, the remaining allegations Defendant Snyder seeks to strike are all alleged conduct prior to the settlement negotiations on November 10, 2022. Moreover, Defendant Snyder has not demonstrated that Evidence Code 1152 is applicable to pleadings. Evidence Code 1152 relates to admissibility. As such, the court finds the motion to strike an improper vehicle. If allegations truly involve the settlement negotiations, the court finds the issue would be better resolved via motion in limine.

 

Accordingly, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lester Snyder’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s Complaint is DENIED.



Case Number: 23VECV00938    Hearing Date: December 31, 2024    Dept: W

MARIA PELMI CALDERON v. JORDAN JAMES KELLY, et al.

 

demurrer to the complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        December 31, 2024                          Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV00938

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Bagrad Petrosian, Armine Z. Petrosian, and Bagrad Petrosian & Armine Z. Petrosian, Trustees of the Bud & Armine Petrosian Trust U/D/T Dated 7/17/06

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Maria Pelmi Calderon

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Jenkins Decl. ¶4-6.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action involves a dog bite incident. On March 1, 2023, Plaintiff Maria Pelmi Calderon filed a complaint against Defendants Jordan James Kelly, Bagrad Petrosian, Armine Z. Petrosian, and Bagrad Petrosian & Armine Z. Petrosian, Trustees of the Bud & Armine Petrosian Trust U/D/T Dated 7/17/06 for general negligence and premises liability. Plaintiff alleges while working as a cleaning lady in Defendant’s house, she was attacked and bitten by a dog that was walking in and out of the defendants' house.

 

On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff amended Defendant Kelly’s name to the true name of Defendant, Jordyn Jones.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Bagrad Petrosian, Armine Z. Petrosian, and Bagrad Petrosian & Armine Z. Petrosian, Trustees of the Bud & Armine Petrosian Trust U/D/T Dated 7/17/06 Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Bagrad Petrosian, Armine Z. Petrosian, and Bagrad Petrosian & Armine Z. Petrosian, Trustees of the Bud & Armine Petrosian Trust U/D/T Dated 7/17/06 demur to the complaint of Plaintiff Maria Pelmi Calderon on the grounds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendants.

 

Civil Code section 3342 imposes strict liability on the owner of any dog who has bitten a person while “in a public place or lawfully in a private place . . . regardless of the former viciousness of the dog or the owner's knowledge of such viciousness.”  (Civ. Code, § 3342(a).)¿However, a non-owner, such as a landlord, is not automatically liable. (Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1360, 1369-1370.) For a non-owner to be liable, they must have known of the dog’s vicious propensities and had the ability to remove the dog.

 

Defendants first argue the complaint is void of allegations whether Defendants had previous knowledge of the dog's alleged dangerous and vicious nature. The court agrees. The complaint is unclear whether Moving Defendants had prior knowledge of the dog’s vicious nature and failed to control their property. The opposition maintains by allowing a dangerous and vicious dog to remain free on the premises implies knowledge on the part of the homeowner; however, this is not alleged and even so, a demurrer tests the factual allegations, not implications.

 

Defendants further argue the complaint is devoid of ultimate, material, or any facts which show that Defendants owned, monitored, controlled, restrained, walked, or otherwise handled the subject dog. There are multiple defendants in this action and Moving Defendants maintain Plaintiff fails to allege which Defendants owned the dog, owned the property and who allowed the dog to roam free outside the house. When alleging a negligence cause of action against a landlord, there must be allegations of duty to take reasonable care to control the dog's behavior, failure to meet that duty and that the negligence caused the harm. (See Uccello v. Laudenslayer (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 504, 514.) Again, the court agrees. Plaintiff seems to agree that Defendants are the property owners. As such, Plaintiff must allege the duty that exists and how that duty was breached. Plaintiff’s current allegations group together all defendants.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Bagrad Petrosian, Armine Z. Petrosian, and Bagrad Petrosian & Armine Z. Petrosian, Trustees of the Bud & Armine Petrosian Trust U/D/T Dated 7/17/06 Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 


Case Number: 24VECV00215    Hearing Date: December 31, 2024    Dept: W

WALTER BANEGGAS v. JAGUAR LAND ROVER NORTH AMERICA, LLC, et al

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel compliance with the court’s order dated september 5, 2024 and request for monetary sanctions

 

Date of Hearing:        December 31, 2024                           Trial Date:       August 26, 2025

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        24VECV00215

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Walter Baneggas

Responding Party:     Defendant Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC and Galpin Jaguar Lincoln-Mercury Inc. dba Jaguar Van Nuys/Land Rover Van Nuys

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On January 17, 2023, Plaintiff Walter Baneggas filed a complaint against Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC, Galpin Jaguar Lincoln-Mercury, Inc. dba Jaguar Van Nuys/Land Rover Van Nuys for Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty. Plaintiff purchased a 2018 Lan Rover Range Rover Velar. Plaintiff alleges they delivered the vehicle to an authorized repair facility for nonconformities, but Defendants were unable to conform the subject vehicle to the applicable express warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance with the Court’s Order Dated September 5, 2024 and Request for Monetary Sanctions is granted.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff Waler Baneggas moves the court for an order compelling Defendant Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC (“JLRNA”) to comply with this court’s September 5, 2024 Order ordering Defendant JLRNA to produce (1) the Franchise Agreement between itself and Land Rover Encino in effect on January 28, 2021 (only as to sections that relate to handling of warranties, repairs and customer complaints); and (2) documents responsive to RFP No. 45 (but only as to California, same make, model and defect). Plaintiff also moves this court for an order awarding monetary sanctions against Defendant and its law firm for Defendant’s willful and intentional defiance of the Court’s September 5, 2024 Order.

 

If a party fails to obey a court order compelling it to provide a discovery response, “the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction . . . In lieu of or in addition to this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction….” (CCP § 2031.300(c).) Misuse of the discovery process, including disobeying a court order compelling discovery, is grounds for sanctions. (CCP § 2023.010(g).)  Moreover, every court has the power “[t]o compel obedience to its judgments, orders, and process, and to the orders of a judge out of court, in an action or proceeding pending therein.” (CCP § 128(a)(4).)

 

Plaintiff argues on September 5, 2024, this court issued a final order granting Plaintiff’s Discovery Motion in part, including ordering Defendant to provide further verified responses and responsive documents to RFPs 18 (only as to sections that relate to handling of warranties, repairs and customer complaints) and 45 (only as to California, same make, model and defect), no later than 30 days after the date of the Order. (Thomas Decl. ¶9.) On October 16, 2024, Plaintiff emailed Defendant asking whether they were going to comply with the Court’s September 5, 2024 Order, to which Defendant stated that it would provide its responses on October 23, 2024. (Thomas Decl. ¶10, Exh. C.) On October 23, 2024, Defendant provided supplemental responses to Plaintiffs’ RFPs 18 and 45, stating that it would produce documents subject to a protective order. (Thomas Decl. ¶11.) However, Defendant failed to include the protective order. (Thomas Decl. ¶11.) A few days later, Plaintiff confirmed receipt and stated it was willing to sign a protective order but Defendant never did. (Thomas Decl. ¶12, Exh. D.) 

 

In opposition, Defendant claims this motion is now moot. Defendant did provide the further responses on October 23, 2024 and on December 17, 2024, the parties agreed to stipulate to the Los Angeles Superior Court Model Protective Order and produce the outstanding documents by December 31, 2024. (Cho Decl. ¶3, Exh. 5.) The parties also agreed to change the current hearing date on this matter. (Cho Decl. ¶3, Exh. 5.) However, Defendant states they could not change the hearing date from December 31, 2024 because it is only the moving party who can change the hearing date through the court reservation system. (Cho Decl. ¶4.) When Defendant advised Plaintiff counsel of the issue, Plaintiff’s counsel advised the earliest the system would let them change the date to was February 14, 2025, which they were unwilling to do so. (Cho Decl. ¶4.) 

 

At the hearing, the parties are to advise this court whether Defendant has produced the documents pursuant to the signed protective order. If Defendant has failed to provide the requested documents by the hearing date, the court will grant the instant motion.

 

Plaintiff still seeks sanctions in the requested amount of $2,242.50 as Defendant’s non-compliance forced Plaintiff to file this motion, and its continued non-compliance forced Plaintiff to file this Reply and to prepare for and appear at the hearing on December 31, 2024. The request is based on 2.7 hours drafting the motion, 0.3 reviewing the motion, 2.5 reviewing the opposition and drafting the reply and 1 hour attending the hearing at counsel’s hourly rate of $450. They also request the $60 filing fee. The court finds there was needless delay on the part of defendant, warranting sanctions of 3 hours as reasonable attorney fees for this simple motion, plus filing fees, for a total of $1410, payable within 30 days.


Case Number: 24VECV00851    Hearing Date: December 31, 2024    Dept: W

NICOLAS STEELE v. BMW OF NORTH AMERICA, LLC

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to plf’s request for production, set one

 

Date of Hearing:        December 31, 2024                                       Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        24VECV00851

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Nicolas Steele

Responding Party:     Defendant BMW of North America, LLC

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Inscoe Decl.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On February 26, 2024, Plaintiff Nicolas Steele filed a complaint against Defendant BMW of North America, LLC for violations of the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Plaintiff alleges on November 19, 2021, Plaintiff purchased a new 2022 BMW M5 CS motor vehicle. The purchase agreement included express warranties; however, Plaintiff alleges since purchasing the vehicle and during the warranty period, Plaintiff has experienced a variety of substantial nonconformities.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set One is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff Nicolas Steele moves the court for an order compelling Defendant BMW of North America, LLC to serve further responses and documents responsive to Requests for Production Set One, Nos. 3, 7, 10 and 14. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds Defendant’s responses were insufficient, including waived objections and withholding discoverable documents. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against Defendant and/or its counsel in the amount of $6,285.00.

 

On receipt of a response to an inspection demand, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  (CCP § 2031.310(a).) A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.” (CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).)

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production indicates the existence of certain documents that Plaintiff requested but was not produced, including Documentation of Repairs/Inspections, Media re Subject Vehicle, Inspection Evidence/Data/Reports, and Inspection Documents. When new counsel for Plaintiff followed up regarding the insufficient responses, Defendant indicated they would not supplement. Plaintiff argues Defendant is withholding documents directly relevant to its violations of the Song-Beverly Act, including  (1) whether the brake disc failure was a warrantable defect; (2) whether Defendant willfully refused coverage; (3) whether the Vehicle would pass without objection in the trade; (4) whether the vehicle was fit for its ordinary purpose; (5) whether Defendant conformed the Vehicle to warranty within 30 days.  Plaintiff also argues BMW improperly redacted information from the warranty vehicle inquiry. The information redacted would be relevant to whether the Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with defects breaching both the implied and express warranties. Moreover, BMW has made no legally viable objections, rather BMW only made ineffective “General Objections.”

 

In opposition, Defendant argues there are no possible further responses to provide as full responses and documents have already been produced and, to the extent any documents were not produced, they are outside the scope of permissible discovery. Specifically, RPD Nos. 3 and 7 are grossly overbroad as they are not limited in time, scope or geography and Plaintiff’s insistence that BMW provide “all” makes the requests impossibly overbroad. Moreover, the information redacted in the Warranty Vehicle Inquiry is regarding information before Plaintiff took possession and irrelevant. As for the rest, BMW argues it has already produced all responsive documents.

 

The court agrees the general objections have been waived. As noted by Plaintiff, Code of Civil Procedure does not allow for “General Objections” to requests for production of documents. (See CCP §2031.210(1)(3).)  Because each response is ‘subject to and without waiving its objections’, the court orders further responses without objections.  Moreover, the court finds that each of the requests is proper and all responsive documents must be produced, not only the limited subset offered by defendant.   If defendant has in its possession, custody or control records of other repairs made to the vehicle prior to or after the plaintiff took possession of the vehicle, such records could lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.   As for the redaction of information in the Warranty Vehicle Inquiry regarding prior history before Plaintiff took possession of the vehicle, the court finds the information relevant under the Song-Beverly Act.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set One is GRANTED.

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff also requests sanctions in the amount of $6,285.00 against BWM for its failure to compromise, supplement or produce responsive documents in light of Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts.

 

Defendant BMW also requests this court impose sanctions for Plaintiff and their counsel’s misuse of the discovery process.

 

The court DENIES both requests for monetary sanctions as neither party’s position was without substantial justification.
