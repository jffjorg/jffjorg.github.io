DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19PSCV00420    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: H

Capital One Bank (USA), N.A. v. Lu, Case No. 19PSCV00420


Plaintiff Capital One Bank (USA), Inc.’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment Under Terms of Stipulated Settlement is DENIED without prejudice. 



Background 

This is a credit card collection case. On May 6, 2019, Plaintiff Capital One Bank (USA), Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Jiazheng Lu (“Lu”) for breach of contract and common counts. 

On October 15, 2019, Plaintiff filed a “Settlement Agreement.” On October 16, 2019, Plaintiff filed a conditional “Notice of Settlement of Entire Case;” that day, an “Order on Settlement Agreement and Stipulation for Judgment” was filed, and the case was dismissed, without prejudice.

Legal Standard

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order, per Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6, vacating the dismissal in this case and entering judgment against Lu.

As a preliminary matter, the Court grants Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice as to the existence and filing date (i.e., October 15, 2019) of the Settlement Agreement.

Turning to the merits, the Court notes at the outset that the notice of motion references a “settlement sum of $35,400.00,”[FN1] whereas the proposed judgment is for $89,677.83 (i.e., principal sum of $88,668.33 plus costs of $1,009.50). The notice of motion is deficient in this regard.

On or about August 23, 2019, the parties entered into the Settlement Agreement, wherein Lu stipulated to entry of judgment in Plaintiff’s favor in the principal sum of $118,828.33 plus court costs pursuant to a memorandum of costs, less credit for payments made before the time of default. (Langedyk Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. B, ¶ 1.) It was also agreed therein that Plaintiff would not request that the above judgment be entered so long as Lu was not in default with a payment plan wherein he would pay Plaintiff $118,828.33 payable at $590.00/month on or before the 13th day of each month commencing in September 2019, followed by a final payment of $238.33 due on or before June 13, 2036. (Id., ¶ 2, Exh. B, ¶ 3.)

It was agreed that, if Lu defaulted in making any payment due to Plaintiff, Plaintiff would provide Lu with 10 days written notice, served to Lu via mail at 4158 14th Street Riverside, CA 92501 (“or as corrected by [Lu] at the time of signing this stipulation”), of its intent to apply to the Court to have the dismissal set aside and vacated and to have judgment entered under the terms of the stipulation. (Id., ¶ 2, Exh. B, ¶ 11). It was agreed that said notice would not give Lu a right to cure the default, but would “provide[] only an opportunity for Defendant to appear and offer evidence that there has been no default.” (Id., ¶ 2, Exh. B, ¶ 12.) The Settlement Agreement contained a section 664.6 clause. (Id., ¶ 2, Exh. B, ¶ 7.)

On October 16, 2019, the case was dismissed, without prejudice. (Id., ¶ 4.) Lu has defaulted on the payment arrangement set forth in the Settlement Agreement; the last payment from Lu was received on December 13, 2023. (Id., ¶ 5.) Lu has paid a total of $30,160.00. (Id., ¶ 7.)

The motion is denied without prejudice. Although Langedyk represents that Lu “was provided with 10-days written notice of the failure to make agreed upon payments and Plaintiff’s intention to request entry of judgment under terms of the Stipulation Agreement, a copy of which is attached hereto and marked Exhibit ‘A’” (Id., ¶ 6), Exhibit A reflects that it was mailed to Lu at 17585 Railroad St., City of Industry, CA 91748-1125,” rather than to 4158 14th Street Riverside, CA 92501, as set forth in Paragraph 11 of the Settlement Agreement. Further, there is no indication that Lu made any “correct[ion]” to this address at the time of signing the Settlement Agreement, as provided for in Paragraph 11.

Case Number: 22PSCV01609    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: H

B.R. Building Resources Company v. SA Hospital Acquisition Group, LLC, et al., Case No. 22PSCV01609 

ORDER ON DEMURRERS

          1.       Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant B.R. Building Resources Company’s Demurrer to Cross-Complaint filed by SA Hospital Acquisition Group, LLC, et al. is SUSTAINED, with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

          2.       Cross-Defendant Juan Banos’ Demurrer to Cross-Complaint filed by SA Hospital Acquisition Group, LLC, et al. is SUSTAINED, with 20 days’ leave to amend.

I.        Background 

This case involves a dispute regarding the alleged diversion of funds that had been designated for various construction projects to improve South City Hospital in St. Louis, Missouri.

Plaintiff B.R. Building Resources Company (“B.R.”) alleges in a First Amended Complaint that defendants Lawrence Feigen and Jeffrey Ahlholm—the owners and agents of the entities that own and operate South City Hospital, SA Hospital Acquisition Group, LLC, SA Hospital Real Estate Holdings, LLC, and SA Hospital Real Estate Holdings (the “SA Hospital Entities”)—requested on more than one occasion that B.R. transfer portions of the funds designated for the construction projects in order to cover hospital expenses, including payroll expenses. B.R. alleges that it acquiesced to these requests only to later learn that contrary to Feigen and Ahlholm’s representations, the entity providing the funding for the construction, Twain Financial Partners (“Twain”), had not approved of using the funds in this way, leading Twain to stop funding the projects altogether. As a result of the lack of funding, B.R. claims, among other things, that it has been unable to pay rental fees on equipment it leased in connection with the construction projects.[FN] 

The SA Hospital Entities, Feigen, and Ahlholm (together “Defendants”) allege in a cross-complaint (the “Cross-Complaint”) that B.R. and B.R.’s Chief Executive Officer, Juan Banos, among other things, mishandled and misallocated the construction funds intended for hospital improvement projects. 

Pending before the Court are: (1) B.R.’s demurrer to the second through fifth causes of action in Defendants’ cross-complaint and (2) Banos’ demurrer to the second through fourth causes of action. For the reasons stated herein, the demurrers are sustained, with 20 days’ leave to amend.

 

II.      Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).)

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

 

III.     Discussion

 

          A.       Request to Strike the Cross-Complaint

At the outset, B.R. and Banos each separately request that the Court strike the Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 436 on the basis that it was not filed until September 23, 2024, in violation of the Court’s order directing Defendants “to file the Cross-Complaint by no later than September 18, 2024.” (Notice, 2:22-25). The Court declines to do so.

 

          B.       B.R’s Demurrer

B.R. demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(e), to the second through fifth causes of action in the Cross-Complaint, on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action. B.R. also demurs, per subsection (f), to the second, third and fifth causes of action on the basis of uncertainty. 

B.R. further contends that the Cross-Complaint fails to state any facts sufficient to allege any theory of liability against Banos. The Court declines to entertain this argument, on the basis that Banos is not a demurring party here. Banos has filed his own separate demurrer, which the Court will address below. 

 

Second and Third Causes of Action (i.e., Fraud in the Concealment and Fraud in the Deceit, Respectively)

B.R. contends that the second and third causes of action have not been pled with the requisite specificity. The Court agrees. “Fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) “Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant to understand fully the nature of the charge made.” (Roberts v. Ball, Hunt, Hart, Brown & Baerwitz (1976) 57 Cal.App.3d 104, 109.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Lazar, supra, 12 Cal.4th at 645 [emphasis theirs].)

Defendants have alleged “on information and belief” that B.R. “submitted inaccurate invoices that served as cover for B.R.’s impermissible uses of monies from the Construction Fund,” including “cover[ing] its own payroll expenses and corporate expenses” and “pay[ing] for projects unrelated to Improvements at South City Hospital.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 28, 53, 54.) They allege that they received an “itemization” from B.R. on September 26, 2022 that “demonstrated that B.R. was misappropriating funds and allocating funds to uses which had not been approved.” (Id. at ¶¶ 34 and 40.) Defendants, however, fail to provide any specificity as to what the itemization included that would “demonstrate” B.R.’s alleged misappropriation of funds and wrongful allocation. 

Defendants further allege, again “on information and belief,” that the itemization “contained illegitimate surcharges and suspicious payments to B.R.’s affiliated vendors.” (Id., ¶¶ 40 and 41.) They do not, however, provide any facts in support of these conclusory statements or in support of their conclusory statement that B.R.’s itemization “failed to account for all of the funds that it received from the Construction Fund.” (Id., ¶ 41.) 

Defendants base their fraud claims on the above itemization, and allege that it evidences specific instances of fraud, but fail to offer any specificity describing those allegations of fraud. They also allege that they requested “bridge financing” in the total amount of $600,000.00, which they “understood that they were receiving . . . from BR Capital,” but that “[u]nbeknownst to [them], Banos and/or B.R. provided them with the sum of $600,000 from funds B.R. [Building] received from the Construction Funds[,]” which information was somehow “concealed.” (Id., ¶ 32.) No further information is provided. 

The Court sustains B.R.’s demurrer to the second and third causes of action on this basis. 

 

Fourth Cause of Action (i.e., An Accounting) 

B.R. next argues that the fourth cause of action has not been sufficiently pled. The Court agrees. “A cause of action for an accounting requires a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.” (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179.) “An action for accounting is not available where the plaintiff alleges the right to recover a sum certain or a sum that can be made certain by calculation.” (Id.) “Equitable principles govern, and the plaintiff must show the legal remedy is inadequate. Thus, where the books and records are so complicated that an action demanding a fixed sum is impracticable, an accounting is appropriate. If an ascertainable sum is owed, an action for an accounting is not proper.” (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1137.) 

B.R. has failed to allege that the books and records are so complicated that a legal remedy is inadequate. The Court sustains B.R.’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action on this basis. 

 

Fifth Cause of Action (i.e., Conversion) 

B.R. further argues that the fifth cause of action has not been sufficiently pled. The Court agrees. “Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) 

“Money may be the subject of conversion if the claim involves a specific, identifiable sum. . .” (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 209.) “[A]ctions for the conversion of money have not been permitted when the amount of money involved is not a definite sum.” (PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 396.) 

In Vu v. California Commerce Club (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 229, the court affirmed a summary judgment on a conversion claim against two gamblers who lost “approximately $1.4 million” and “approximately $120,000,” respectively, stating that “neither by pleading nor responsive proof did plaintiffs identify any specific, identifiable sums that the club took from them. That rendered the generalized claim for money not actionable as conversion.” (Id. at 235.) 

Likewise, Defendants here fail to specifically identify the amount of money converted, instead alleging that B.R. “absconded with approximately $1,996,644.56 worth of monies.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 74 [emphasis added].) 

The Court sustains B.R.’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action on this basis. 


          C.       Banos’ Demurrer 

Banos demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(e) and (f), to the second through fourth causes of action in the Cross-Complaint, on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and are uncertain. 

Banos argues that the Cross-Complaint fails to allege any facts sufficient to state a claim against him on the basis of alter ego. Defendants, in turn, argue that they have not asserted an alter ego theory of liability as against Banos but seek to impose personal liability against him. (Opp., 4:13-15 and 19-20.) In any case, the claims against Banos fail for the same reasons identified above in connection with the discussion of B.R.’s demurrer. 

The Court thus sustains Banos’ demurrer to the second through fourth causes of action.

 

IV.     Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, the demurrers to the Cross-Complaint are sustained. The Court grants Defendants 20 days’ leave to amend. (City of Stockton v. Superior Ct. (2007) 42 Cal. 4th 730, 747 [“[L]eave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the [pleading] shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.”].)  


[FN] B.R.’s failure to pay rental fees is the subject of the related lawsuit brought by Trane U.S. Inc. (Case No. 23PSCV03948), which is also pending before this Court.
