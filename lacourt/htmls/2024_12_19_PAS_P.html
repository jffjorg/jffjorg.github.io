DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19GDCV00188    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING CROSS-DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR APPOINTMENT OR RECEIVER

I.         INTRODUCTION

This case arises from a contract dispute over construction work. Cross-Complainant Business Alliance Insurance Company (“BAIC”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendant LSI General Engineering & Construction Co. and Levon Isadzhanyan (“Isadzhanyan”) for indemnity and reimbursement related to a contractor’s license bond.

On August 2, 2023, the Court entered a default judgment in favor of BAIC and against Isadzhanyan in the amount of $18,199.46.

On November 21, 2024, Isadzhanyan, appearing in pro per, filed a motion for the appointment of a receiver. On December 6, 2024, BAIC filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion arguing potential relief under CCP 437(b) is unwarranted.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Receivers are agents of the court and may be appointed only where authorized by statute.  (Marsch v. Williams (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 238, 246.)  A judge may appoint a receiver for an action pending before that judge when a corporation is dissolved.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 564, subd. (b)(5).)  The appointment of a receiver is a drastic remedy to be utilized only in “exceptional cases.”  (City & County of San Francisco v. Daley (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 734, 744.)  As such, a receiver should not be appointed unless absolutely essential and because no other remedy will serve its purpose.  (Ibid.)  A party who seeks appointment of a receiver of certain property has the burden to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that the receivership is necessary.  (See Alhambra-Shumway Mines, Inc. v. Alhambra Gold Mine Corp. (1953) 116 Cal.App.2d 869, 873.) 

III.      ANALYSIS

On November 21, 2024, Isadzhanyan, in pro per, filed a motion for the appointment of a receiver. The Court finds that the motion does not cite any legal authority or provide any substantive basis for the request. Instead, Isadzhanyan appears to request a meeting with the Court to discuss payment obligations stemming from the default judgment, which they claim they were unaware of.

BAIC filed an opposition addressing potential relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) (set aside), arguing that such relief is not warranted. However, Isadzhanyan has neither cited section 473(b) nor framed the motion as one seeking relief from the default judgment. The motion focuses solely on the appointment of a receiver, without articulating any legal basis for such a request.

As the moving party, Isadzhanyan bears the burden of demonstrating that the statutory requirements for the appointment of a receiver are met. The Court finds that this burden has not been met. The motion fails to cite any legal authority or present any argument demonstrating why the appointment of a receiver is warranted under Code of Civil Procedure section 564 or any other statutory provision. Without such authority or argument, the Court cannot evaluate the appropriateness of the request.

Additionally, while BAIC’s opposition addresses relief under section 473(b), Isadzhanyan has not raised or argued for such relief. As a result, the Court declines to address BAIC’s opposition in detail, as it does not pertain to the request currently before the Court.

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court DENIES Isadzhanyan motion for appointment of receiver.

BAIC is to give notice. 

 

Dated: December 19, 2024

 

 

                                                                       

                                                                                                  JARED D. MOSES
JUDGE OF THE SUPERIOR COURT

                                                                                 


Case Number: 23AHCV01361    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

I.         INTRODUCTION

This action arises from an incident that occurred on April 19, 2023, at an LA Fitness facility in Arcadia, California. Plaintiff, Hee Sung Kwon (“Plaintiff”), alleges he sustained injuries due to a malfunctioning treadmill. On June 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed claims against Defendants, Fitness International, LLC (erroneously sued as “LA Fitness”) and Francesca Levy (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging 1) Negligence and 2) Premise Liability.

On November 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for leave to amend the complaint to include allegations of gross negligence. On December 6, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) provides: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.¿ The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)¿¿¿ ¿ 

Permissible amendments pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) include amendments which add causes of action.¿ A plaintiff who fails to plead a cause of action arising out of the transaction or occurrence that gave rise to the complaint may apply to the court for leave to amend the complaint to assert the cause of action, whether that failure was the result of oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50; see Code Civ. Proc., § 426.10, subd. (c).)¿ The motion to amend may be filed at any time during the action, on notice to the adverse party.¿ (Id., § 426.50.)¿ ¿ 

The court has broad discretion to permit amendments to pleadings, and “the court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.”¿ (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.)¿ “The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend . . ..”¿¿ (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)¿ Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.”¿ (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿ ¿ 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1).)¿ A motion to amend a pleading must also be supported by a declaration which specifies the following: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)¿

III.      ANALYSIS

Plaintiff moves for leave to amend the complaint, asserting that new information from discovery justifies adding allegations of gross negligence against Defendants. Plaintiff relies on depositions of Francesca Levy and Omar Castro, contending that Defendants' failure to properly maintain the treadmill’s PC Boards demonstrates gross negligence. (Motion, p. 3; Roesler Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.) Plaintiff argues that the proposed amendment does not change the underlying facts or causes of action, as Defendants had notice of the allegations through discovery. Plaintiff also claims that denial would necessitate filing a separate action, burdening judicial resources. (Motion, p. 6) Trial is set for April 8, 2025, which Plaintiff asserts provides sufficient time for additional discovery. (Id.)

Defendants oppose the motion, arguing Plaintiff failed to comply with Rule 3.1324(b), as the supporting declaration does not sufficiently explain why the amendment is necessary and proper. (Opposition, p. 3.) Defendants contend the proposed amendments merely elaborate on existing claims by specifying the PC Boards, which Plaintiff can address in opposition to Defendants’ pending summary judgment motion. (Opposition, pp. 3-4.) Defendants further argue that allowing the amendment would delay resolution of the case, as their summary judgment motion, based on the original complaint, is scheduled for hearing on January 16, 2025. (Opposition, pp. 5-6.)

The Court finds Defendants' arguments persuasive. While California law generally favors granting leave to amend pleadings, such motions must comply with procedural requirements. Plaintiff’s declaration fails to sufficiently explain why the amendment is necessary and proper, as required by Rule 3.1324(b). Additionally, Defendants’ contention that the proposed amendment merely refines existing claims by specifying the PC Boards is well-taken, as Plaintiff has not demonstrated that the additional allegations are essential to their case.

Furthermore, the Court notes that Plaintiff has not filed a reply to Defendants' opposition, leaving Defendants' arguments uncontested. This failure to address the procedural deficiencies raised by Defendants further supports denying the motion. Based on the procedural defects and the lack of necessity for the proposed amendment, the Court DENIES Plaintiff's request for leave to amend.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion for leave to to file the FAC.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV02326    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA TO HUNTINGTON MEMORIAL HOSPITAL and DENYING SANCTIONS

I.         INTRODUCTION

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff Vaneza Alfaro (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Liliana Deluca (“Defendant”), alleging injuries arising from an automobile collision.

On October 31, 2023, Defendant filed instant motion to quash subpoena seeking her medical records from Huntington Memorial Hospital. Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion on December 4, 2023. On December 12, 2023, Defendant filed a reply. 

 

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

            Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1 states, “When a subpoena requires…the production of [documents], the court, upon motion reasonably made by the party, the witness or any consumer described in Section 1985.3…may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon such terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.”  “The court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the parties, the witness, or the consumer from unreasonable or oppressive demands including unreasonable violations of a witness’s or consumer’s right of privacy.”  (Id.) 

As a general rule, all unprivileged information that is relevant to the subject matter of the action is discoverable if it would itself be admissible evidence at trial or if it appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010; Schnabel v. Superior Court. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 711.) When the information sought to be discovered impacts a person’s constitutional right to privacy, limited protections come into play for that person. (Shaffer v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 993, 999.) The protections cover both a person’s personal and financial matters. (Id.) The court must balance competing rights — the right of a litigant to discover relevant facts and the right of an individual to maintain reasonable privacy — in determining whether the information is discoverable. (Id.) 

            For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.) 

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendant seeks to quash arguing that the subpoena violates her constitutional right to privacy. Defendant contends that her medical condition is not at issue in this litigation, as she has not asserted any injuries or damages related to the accident. She further argues that Plaintiff’s subpoena constitutes an improper "fishing expedition" intended to harass and intimidate, rather than to obtain relevant evidence. (Motion, pp. 3-6)

Plaintiff opposes the motion to quash, asserting that Defendant waived her privacy rights by voluntarily testifying about her condition during her deposition. Specifically, Plaintiff argues that Defendant repeatedly emphasized her lack of injuries and downplayed the force of the impact to undermine the credibility of Plaintiff’s claimed injuries. Plaintiff contends that this testimony opened the door to limited discovery of Defendant’s medical records, as they are directly relevant to evaluating the force of the impact of the incident and challenging Defendant’s credibility. (Opposition, pp. 2-6)

The Court must¿carefully balance¿the right of privacy against the need for disclosure.¿¿(Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-56; see also¿John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1199.) ¿The¿ascertainment of the truth¿in connection with legal proceedings is a compelling state interest.¿¿(Britt v. Superior Court¿(1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 857.)¿ In balancing the divergent interests, the court considers the¿purpose of the information sought, the¿effect that disclosure will have¿on the affected persons and parties, the¿nature of the objections¿urged by the party resisting disclosure, and the¿availability of the alternative, less intrusive means¿for obtaining the requested information.¿¿(Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1005.)¿ The more sensitive the nature of the personal information that is sought, the more substantial the showing of the need for the discovery that will be required before disclosure will be permitted.¿¿(Id. at 1004.) 

Here, the Court finds that Defendant’s constitutional right to privacy outweighs Plaintiff’s need for discovery. Defendant has not placed her medical condition at issue, as she has made no claims for injuries or damages in connection with this case. While Defendant did testify during her deposition that she was "fine" and "not injured," the Court concludes that this testimony does not waive her privacy rights.

Further, on balance, the competing interests favor Defendant’s privacy rights over Plaintiff’s need to discover information for impeachment or credibility purposes. Defendant’s medical records are not essential to resolving the core issue in this case, which is Plaintiff’s injuries and damages, not Defendant’s condition.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to quash the subpoena for her medical records.

Sanctions

Defendant moves the Court for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel, arguing that Plaintiff’s subpoena constitutes a misuse of the discovery process by seeking irrelevant and private information that is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Motion, p.7)

Plaintiff also moves the Court for monetary sanctions, contending that Defendant’s motion to quash is meritless and that Defendant failed to comply with statutory procedural requirements, including the timely service of required documents. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s failure to withdraw the motion despite multiple meet-and-confer efforts forced Plaintiff to incur unnecessary attorney’s fees and costs, warranting sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010(e) and 2025.420(h). (Opposition, pp. 6-8)

The Court finds that neither party is entitled to sanctions. While Defendant’s motion to quash is ultimately granted, the Court determines that Plaintiff’s opposition was substantially justified, given the disputed nature of Defendant’s deposition testimony and Plaintiff’s reasonable belief that the testimony placed Defendant’s medical condition at issue. Similarly, while Plaintiff’s subpoena may have been overly broad, the Court finds no evidence that it was issued in bad faith or with the intent to harass Defendant.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES requests for monetary sanctions.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Quash Subpoena to Huntington Memorial Hospital.

Defendant is to give notice.

Case Number: 24NNCV01373    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is a breach of guaranty case. Plaintiff JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (“Plaintiff”) is the lender, and Defendant Tristam A. Steinberg (“Defendant”) is the guarantor of a $500,000 line of credit issued to Design Medium LLC (“Design Medium”). Plaintiff filed the action on May 3, 2024.

On June 27, 2024, Defendant filed a general denial. On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment on its sole cause of action for breach of guaranty, seeking judgment in the total amount of $510,017.01, which includes principal, late fees, and attorneys’ fees and costs. The motion is unopposed.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.) 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  

To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿

III.      ANALYSIS

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on its sole cause of action for breach of guaranty against Defendant. Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $510,017.01, which includes $499,926.02 in principal, $1,500.00 in late fees and costs, and $8,590.99 in attorneys’ fees and costs. Plaintiff contends that no triable issue of material fact exists and that it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

The elements of a breach of guaranty are: (1) the defendant guaranteed payment of the indebtedness of the primary obligor to the plaintiff; (2) a default on the indebtedness occurred; (3) the plaintiff notified the guarantor of the default; and (4) the guarantor failed to remit funds to the plaintiff under the guaranty agreement. (Torrey Pines Bank v. Sup. Ct. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 813, 819.) Additionally, "a guarantor may validly waive rights and defenses in the guaranty contract." (Central Bldg., LLC v. Cooper (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 1053, 1059.)

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(c), summary judgment is proper if all evidence and reasonable inferences drawn from it establish that no triable issue of material fact exists and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. The moving party bears the initial burden of producing admissible evidence demonstrating entitlement to judgment, after which the burden shifts to the opposing party to raise a triable issue of fact.

Plaintiff has satisfied its burden of demonstrating each element of its breach of guaranty cause of action, supported by uncontroverted evidence. Plaintiff provides the declaration of Barbra J. Lopez, an employee of Plaintiff serving as a Special Credits Analyst II with a corporate title of Associate, who attests to the relevant facts. Plaintiff presents evidence that Defendant executed a Continuing Guaranty on July 25, 2019, unconditionally guaranteeing payment of all obligations of Design Medium under a $500,000 line of credit. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff further establishes that Design Medium defaulted under the loan agreement by failing to make monthly payments beginning in November 2023. (Lopez Decl., ¶¶ 5, 7, Exh. 3.) On January 22, 2024, Plaintiff notified Defendant of the default and demanded payment pursuant to the terms of the Guaranty. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 4.) Despite receiving notice, Defendant has failed to cure the default or remit any payments under the Guaranty. (Lopez Decl., ¶¶ 6-8.) The evidence provided establishes that Plaintiff has met its burden of proof on all elements of the claim.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has presented sufficient evidence to establish all elements of its breach of guaranty claim. Moreover, Defendant’s failure to file an opposition to the motion for summary judgment further supports Plaintiff's entitlement to judgment. 

Plaintiff also seeks $8,590.99 in attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in pursuit of remedies under the Guaranty. These fees are supported by the Declaration of Joshua K. Partington and are reasonable given the scope and nature of the case. (Partington Decl., ¶ 2.)

For the foregoing reasons, the Court finds that Plaintiff has established its entitlement to judgment as a matter of law. Defendant failed to raise a triable issue of material fact or oppose the motion. Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment. Judgment shall be entered in favor of Plaintiff JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A., and against Defendant Tristam A. Steinberg in the total amount of $510,017.01.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment.

Plaintiff is to give notice.
