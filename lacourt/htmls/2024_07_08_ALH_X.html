DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20GDCV00722    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: X

   Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

 

 

HEARING DATE:      July 8, 2024                                        TRIAL DATE: Disposed (3/22/24)

                                                          

CASE:                         Leafire, Inc., et al. v. CADM, Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20GDCV00722

 

 

MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants CADM, Inc., Jianqiang Gu, Caxyh, LLC, Cayfd, LLC, Camyg, LLC, Camg, LLC, and Cajyong, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      Plaintiffs Leafire, Inc. and Llulllu, LLC

 

SERVICE:                              Filed on May 13, 2024

 

OPPOSITION:                       Filed on June 18, 2024

 

REPLY:                                   None filed

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

             Defendants CADM, Inc., Jianqiang Gu, Caxyh, LLC, Cayfd, LLC, Camyg, LLC, Camg, LLC, and Cajyong, LLC seek an order vacating or correcting the May 1, 2024 in part as to the ruling that this Court has in rem jurisdiction of the two real estate properties located in the county of Riverside.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On April 3, 2023, Plaintiffs LEAFIRE, INC. and LLULLLU, LLC (“Plaintiffs”) filed the operative Fifth Amended Complaint (“5AC”) against Defendants CADM, INC.,  JIANQIANG GU, CAXYH, LLC, CAYFD, LLC, CAMYG, LLC, CAMG, LLC, CAJYONG, LLC, HFE, LLC, WEE HIN, ROBERT CHEN, VANESSA LE, YING LIU, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive. The 5AC asserted eight causes of action for:

 

            1. Fraud

            2. Investment Fraud under Corp. Code §§ 25000-25707

            3. Breach of Written Contract

            4. Breach of Oral Contract

            5. Money Had and Received

            6. Damage to Personal Property

            7. Infliction of Emotional Distress

            8. Civil Extortion

            9. Unfair Competition

            10. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            11. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            12. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            13. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            14. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            15. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            16. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            17. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

            18. Avoidance of Fraudulent Transfer

 

On March 22, 2024, this Court entered Judgment in favor of Plaintiffs on the first, third through fifth, tenth through fifteenth, and seventeenth causes of action and awarded compensatory and punitive damages, prejudgment interest of ten percent, ad litigation related costs.

 

On April 8, 2024, Defendants CADM, Inc., Jianqiang Gu, Caxyh, LLC, Cayfd, LLC, Camyg, LLC, Camg, LLC, and Cajyong, LLC (“Defendants”) filed a Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict. The matter was fully briefed and came for hearing before the Court on May 1, 2024. This Court denied the motion on both procedurally and substantive grounds.

 

On May 13, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion for reconsideration of the May 1, 2024 order. On June 18, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition. No reply has been filed.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

             Defendants CADM, Inc., Jianqiang Gu, Caxyh, LLC, Cayfd, LLC, Camyg, LLC, Camg, LLC, and Cajyong, LLC’s Motion for Reconsideration of May 1, 2024 Order is DENIED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008, subdivision (a), “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a).)¿ 

 

As it relates to new or different facts, circumstances, or law under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008, subdivision (a), “the moving party must provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.”¿ (Shiffer v. CBS Corp. (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 246, 255.) Furthermore, “facts of which the party seeking reconsideration was aware at the time of the original ruling are not “new or different.” (In re Marriage of Herr (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1468.)¿ 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Defendants CADM, Inc., Jianqiang Gu, Caxyh, LLC, Cayfd, LLC, Camyg, LLC, Camg, LLC, and Cajyong, LLC (“Defendants”) move for this Court to reconsider and vacate in part the May 1, 2024 order as to the ruling that this Court has in rem jurisdiction of the two real estate properties located in the county of Riverside because venue was proper and conclude in part this ruling was made in error.

 

            “Subject to subdivision (b), if a defendant has contracted to perform an obligation in a particular county, the superior court in the county where the obligation is to be performed, where the contract in fact was entered into, or where the defendant or any defendant resides at the commencement of the action is a proper court for the trial of an action founded on that obligation, and the county where the obligation is incurred is the county where it is to be performed, unless there is a special contract in writing to the contrary.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 395(a).) However, “[t]he [main relief] rule comes into operation when the court is required to select between inconsistent venue rules because the complaint prays for two distinct types of relief, one local and one transitory.” (Dow AgroSciences LLC v. Superior Court (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 1067, 1078 (Dow AgroSciences LLC).) “[I]f the main relief requested is personal, fraud damages as an example, and a real property right is only incidentally involved such that it does not change the transitory character of the action, the section 392(a) exception will not be applied.” (Id.)

 

            Here, the May 1, 2024 order denying Defendants Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict and Alternatively, Motion for New Trial were denied on two grounds. First, this Court found that the underlying motions were untimely. The Court notes that Defendants did not move for reconsideration on the grounds related to the timeliness of the underlying motions, thus present no new or different facts to remedy this defect. Second, this Court found that jurisdiction over this action was proper and the judgment was supported  by sufficient evidence, which constituted substantive grounds for denial of the underlying motions. Now, Defendants contend that this Court made an error in finding that jurisdiction over the action was proper because two real properties are located in Riverside, CA and Defendant Gu has been a resident of Riverside County since commencement of this lawsuit. However, like in Dow AgroSciences LLC and as noted previously by this Court the main relief sought in this action was more transitory in nature than local because most of Plaintiffs’ claims sought personal relief for fraud and breach of contract. Also, Plaintiff asserts only two out of six parcels of real property that were subject to dispute in this case were located in Riverside County, whereas the majority of the real property were located in Los Angeles County. Defendants provide no response or evidence refuting this argument. As such, the venue rule set forth in Code of Civil Procedure Section 395(a) applied and provided this Court with jurisdiction over the entire action. Moreover, Defendants fail to meet their burden under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008(a) because they admit that it was known to at least Defendant Gu that he was a resident of Riverside County since the filing of the lawsuit, so it is not a new or different fact for purposes of reconsideration. Even if it did constitute a new of different fact, Defendants provide no explanation as to why this was not disclose at an earlier time including during the filing of the underlying motions.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants CADM, Inc., Jianqiang Gu, Caxyh, LLC, Cayfd, LLC, Camyg, LLC, Camg, LLC, and Cajyong, LLC’s Motion for Reconsideration of May 1, 2024 Order is DENIED.

CASE:                         Jose G. Sanchez v. Albertsons Companies Inc. et al.  

 

CASE NO.:                 23AHCV00226

 

 

MOTION TO DISMISS

 

MOVING PARTY:              Defendant Albertsons, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     None

 

SERVICE:                             Filed June 3, 2024

 

OPPOSITION:                      None as of July 2, 2024

 

REPLY:                                 Filed June 28, 2024 (reply and notice of non-opposition)

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

Defendant moves for an order dismissing the Complaint of Plaintiff Jose Sanchez for failure to comply with the Court Orders compelling verified answers to discovery. In the event that terminating sanctions are not awarded, Albertsons further requests that Plaintiff be ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $1,310.00 to Albertsons representing reasonable expenses including the attorney's fees incurred by the moving party in connection with this proceeding as well.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff alleges that on or about February 15, 2021, he was at the premises maintained by Albertsons when he sustained injuries. Plaintiff filed suit for personal injuries on February 1, 2023, and served same on Albertsons on June 13, 2023.

 

No response has been provided to any of the written discovery requests for which Plaintiff was ordered to provide verified responses, on or before May 20, 2024. Albertsons now brings this Motion for Dismissal (terminating sanctions) for Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the court’s Orders issued on April 29, 2024.

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court grants the unopposed motion to dismiss.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure §2023.010(g) defines misuse of the discovery process to include “disobeying a court order to provide discovery.”

 

Section 2023.030 continues: “[T]he court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after an opportunity for hearing, may impose the following sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process: . . . . (d) The court may impose a terminating sanction by of the following orders: (3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.”

 

DISCUSSION

 

On May 2, 2024, the Court ordered Plaintiff to provide verified responses to Albertsons’ First Set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories and Request for Production of Documents by May 20, 2024. (Galstyan Decl. ¶ 9.) On May 20, 2024, counsel for Albertsons’ called Plaintiff and left him a voicemail inquiring as to the status of the responses. (Galstyan Decl. ¶ 9.) To date, Albertsons has not received any responses to its’ First Set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories and Request for Production of Documents. (Galstyan Decl. ¶ 9.) Notably, Plaintiff failed to file an opposition. Therefore, there is a misuse of discovery and dismissal is appropriate.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Thus, the Court grants the unopposed motion to dismiss.

Case Number: 23AHCV00512    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: X

Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

 

 

HEARING DATE:     July 8, 2024                                         TRIAL DATE: No date set.

                                                          

CASE:                         Chris Arredondo v. John Doe et al  

 

CASE NO.:                 23AHCV00512

 

 

MOTION TO DISMISS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Philip Tse Chow

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      None

 

SERVICE:                              Filed June 4, 2024

 

OPPOSITION:                       None

 

REPLY:                                  None

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

Defendant, Philip Tse Chow, named as “DOE 1”, moves the Court for an order to dismiss Plaintiff’s complaint. This Motion is based on the following: It is abundantly and unequivocally clear that Plaintiff knew that Defendant PHILIP TSE CHOW as DOE 1 should have been named much earlier in this lawsuit as a Defendant – long before the expiration of the Statute of limitations.

 

BACKGROUND

 

 This matter arises from an automobile accident that occurred on March 14, 2021. Plaintiff Chris Arredondo alleges that he was injured in a collision caused by Defendant Philip Tse Chow. Plaintiff filed the Complaint on March 9, 2023, alleging motor vehicle negligence and general negligence.

 

On October 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment naming PHILIP TSE CHOW as “DOE 1” and it was served on October 27, 2023, by substitute service on “Anita Doe.”

 

 

On February 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel advised Defendant that he was withdrawing as counsel of record for Plaintiff and granted an open extension to respond to the complaint. On March 11, 2024, the Court granted the motion to relieved as counsel.

 

On June 4, 2024, Defendant Philip Tse Chow filed the instant motion.

 

As of July 2, 2024, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court GRANTS the Defendant Philip Tse Chow’s unopposed Motion to Dismiss because the Doe amendment is barred by the statute of limitations.

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The name of the motion to dismiss an action when a known defendant has been brought into the lawsuit as a “DOE” after the statute of limitations has been expired has interchangeably been used by the courts as a “motion to dismiss”, or “a motion to quash” or a “Barrows motion”. (A.N. v. County of Los Angeles (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1058; Barrows v. Am. Motors, Corp. (1983); C.C.P. 581(m).) It’s also been referred to as a “motion to strike”. (See: A.N. v. County of Los Angeles (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1064, [“The substance of the ‘motion to quash’ is what mattered, not its label”].)

 

In cases where persons that were known to a plaintiff at the time the complaint was filed have been allowed to be brought in by a DOE amendment after the statute of limitations had run, there was some showing that the plaintiff was ignorant of something having a bearing upon the new defendant’s liability. (Miller v. Thomas (1981) 121 Cal. App. 3d 440, 445, 175 Cal. Rptr. 327, 330.) The plaintiff “must be genuinely ignorant of the defendant’s identity or the facts rendering the defendant liable when the original complaint was filed.” (Taito v. Owens Coming (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 798, 802, 9 Cal. Rptr. 2d 687, 699.)

 

Moreover, “even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, unwarranted delay in presenting it may—of itself—be a valid reason for denial.” ’ ” (Huff v. Wilkins (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 732, 746, 41 Cal.Rptr.3d 754 (Huff ); Record v. Reason, supra, 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 486.) Thus, appellate courts are less likely to find an abuse of discretion where, for example, the proposed amendment is “ ‘offered after long unexplained delay ... or where there is a lack of diligence....’ ” (Hulsey v. Koehler (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 1150, 1159, Melican v. Regents of Univ. of California, 151 Cal. App. 4th 168, 175 (2007).

 

DISCUSSION

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1 states that the injured party or accident victim has 2 years, from the time of the incident, to file a personal injury lawsuit to seek compensation from the party at fault. The statute of limitations begins “from the date of the injury, or the date it was discovered, or the date on which it would have been discovered with reasonable efforts.”

 

Here, the accident occurred on March 14, 2021, and the complaint was filed on March 9, 2023 which was within the 2 year statute of limitations. However, on October 12, 2023 – more than 2 and ½ years after the accident -- Plaintiff filed an amendment naming PHILIP TSE CHOW as “DOE 1.” The Traffic Collision Report dated March 14, 2021, shows Driver 1 and the owner of the vehicle as Philip Tse Chow while Driver 2 and owner of the vehicle is Chris Arredondo. (Kang Decl. ¶ 6; Ex. C.)

 

The Court is granting the motion of Defendant Philip Tse Chow to dismiss Plaintiff's complaint in this action. The Court is so ruling because the evidence presented to the Court shows that the Plaintiff was aware of the existence of Philip Tse Chow during the filing of the complaint. Plaintiff's awareness of Philip Tse Chow’s involvement stems from the Traffic Collision Report dated March 14, 2021. Because of the Plaintiff's awareness of Philip Tse Chow, his Doe amendment to name Philip Tse Chow as a Defendant was improper under Code of Civil Procedure Section 474. Additionally, Plaintiff failed to file an opposition providing any explanation for the delay and or that he was “genuinely ignorant.” Since, the Doe amendment was improper, Plaintiff's claims against Philip Tse Chow do not relate back to Plaintiff's filing of the complaint and thus are barred under the applicable statute of limitations. Therefore, dismissal is warranted.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS the unopposed Motion to Dismiss.

CASE:                         5623 PECK ROAD, LLC vs CULTIVA GROUP, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23AHCV01071

 

MOTION FOR ORDER OF SALE AND ISSUANCE OF ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE WHY ORDER FOR SALE SHOULD NOT BE MADE

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Cultiva Group and Qirong Lin

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      Plaintiff 5623 PECK ROAD, LLC

 

SERVICE:                              Filed May 28, 2024

 

OPPOSITION:                       None

 

REPLY:                                  None

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

Plaintiff 5623 PECK ROAD, LLC, the judgment creditor, petitions the court pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §704.750, for an order permitting the sale of a residential premises located at 2449 E. Roughneck Place, Brea, CA 92821 (“the Property”).

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an unlawful detainer case filed on May 11, 2023, by Plaintiff 5623 PECK ROAD, LLC against Defendants Cultiva Group and Qirong Lin, claiming past-due rent totaling $137,435.90, attorney fees, forfeiture of the lease agreement, damages accruing daily since January 1, 2023, and additional expenses around $20,000. On October 16, 2023, Plaintiff obtained a Judgment-Unlawful Detainer for past-due rent in the amount of $137,435.90; Holdover damages for $177,933.24; Attorney fees in the amount of $2,467.41; and Costs of $1,127.19. A Credit of $216,176.78 was stated, and the Total Judgment was for $102,787.56.

 

On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion. As of July 2, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

The Court CONTINUES the motion to allow Plaintiff to file a Notice of Motion and properly notice Defendant. Plaintiff is ordered to file and serve a Notice of Motion within 10 days of this hearing.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 1005 requires written notice for  “any other proceeding under this code in which notice is required, and no other time or method is prescribed by law or by court or judge.” (Code of Civil Procedure § 1005.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes the instant motion is procedurally defective because Plaintiff failed to file a Notice of Motion. Here, Plaintiff only filed a proposed order and a memorandum of points and authorities. Therefore, the Court orders Plaintiff to file a Notice of Motion to properly notice Defendant.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Therefore, the Court CONTINUES the motion to allow Plaintiff to file a Notice of Motion. Plaintiff is ordered to file and serve a Notice of Motion within 10 days of this hearing.
