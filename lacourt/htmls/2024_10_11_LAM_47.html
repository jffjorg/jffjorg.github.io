DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV45351    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 11, 2024                   TRIAL DATE: November 12, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Barry Ben-Ron et al. v. Maria De Lourdes Ruiz.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV45351

           

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Barry Ben-Ron and Brian Sean Costello

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Maria De Lourdes Ruiz

 

CASE HISTORY:

  • 12/13/21:         Complaint filed.
  • 06/15/22:         Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a breach of contract case. Plaintiffs purchased a property from Defendant which Plaintiffs allege was subject to a PACE loan. Plaintiffs allege that Defendant agreed to pay off the loan through escrow, such that the property would be transferred to Plaintiffs clear of any lien and failed to do so, despite receiving money in an amount equal to the balance on the loan, from escrow.

 

            Plaintiffs move for summary adjudication of the issue of whether Defendant owed a duty to either Plaintiff to pay off a PACE lien through escrow for sale of the subject property to Plaintiffs, and for summary adjudication of Defendant’s First Affirmative Defense.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED in its entirety.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

            Plaintiffs move for summary adjudication of the issue of whether Defendant owed a duty to either Plaintiff to pay off a PACE lien through escrow for sale of the subject property to Plaintiffs, and for summary adjudication of Defendant’s First Affirmative Defense.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition by a plaintiff is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a defendant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

Analysis

 

            Plaintiffs request a determination that Defendant owed a duty to both Plaintiffs to pay off the PACE lien on the subject property through escrow for the sale of that property. Plaintiffs also request summary adjudication of Defendant’s First Affirmative Defense that the Seller Property Questionnaire contains the printed language “This disclosure is not intended to be part of the contract between Buyer and Seller.” As the issues of duty and the affirmative defense are intertwined, the Court will address them together.

 

            The undisputed facts of this case are that, in October 2020, Plaintiffs and Defendant executed a written contract for the purchase of real property located at 212 East 24th Street, Los Angeles, CA. (Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Fact & Defendant’s Separate Statement of Disputed Fact Nos. 1, 8.) The contract was prepared on a form entitled California Residential Purchase Agreement and Joint Escrow Instructions with a Seller Multiple Counter Offer. (Id.) The Agreement was signed on October 10, 2020 by Defendant, as Seller, and Plaintiff Ben-Ron as Buyer. (Id.) Plaintiff Ben-Ron assigned an interest in the agreement to Plaintiff Costello. (Id. No. 11.) Paragraph 13.B of the Agreement provides:

 

Title is taken in its present condition subject to all encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters, whether of record or not, as of the date of Acceptance except for (1) monetary liens of record (which Seller is obligated to pay off) unless Buyer is assuming those obligations or taking the Property subject to those obligations; and (ii) those matters which Seller has agreed to remove in writing.

 

(SSUMF No. 2, 9; SSDF No. 2, 9.) Defendant and Plaintiffs also signed a Seller Property Questionnaire indicating that the property was subject to a PACE lien. (SSUMF Nos. 3-4, 10, 12; SSDF Nos. 3-4, 10, 12.) Specifically, Paragraph 14.E of the Questionnaire asks if the seller is aware of “Any PACE lien (such as HERO or SCEIP) or other lien on your Property securing a loan to pay for an alteration, modification, replacement, improvement, remodel or material repair of the Property?” (SSUMF No. 4, 12; SSDF No. 4, 12.) The box labeled “Yes” next to Paragraph 14.E is checked, and in the “Explanation” section there is a handwritten statement “Has a Loan for improvements that will be paid through escrow.” (Id.) At the time of the sale, there was a recorded PACE lien on the subject property. (SUMF Nos. 5, 13, SSDF Nos. 5, 13.)

 

            Plaintiffs categorically state that they did not agree to assume the obligations on the PACE lien, nor did they agree to take the property subject to the PACE lien. (SSUMF Nos. 6, 13.) Further, Defendant admitted in her deposition that she understood that the bank mortgage and PACE lien were to be paid through escrow. (SSUMF Nos. 7, 14.) In opposition, Defendant argues that the PACE Lien is properly considered an “assessment” by the express terms of the Notice of Assessment. (SSDF Nos. 6, 14; see RJN Exh. E.) Defendant contends that under Paragraph 17 of the Agreement, certain listed items “shall be paid current and prorated between Buyer and Seller as of close of escrow,” including real property taxes and assessments. (SSDF Nos. 6, 14.) In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant ignores that paragraph 17 expressly states that applies “[u]nless otherwise agreed in writing. (See Plaintiffs’ Exh. C. ¶ 17.) Plaintiffs contend that the parties executed such an agreement in the form of the Seller Property Questionnaire via the handwritten explanation that the loan would be paid through escrow.

 

            The effect of the Seller Property Questionnaire is also the subject of the First Affirmative Defense, which contends that the Questionnaire is not a promise to pay off the PACE loan through escrow, as alleged in the Complaint, because the Questionnaire states in Paragraph 1 that “[t]his disclosure is not intended to be part of the contract between Buyer and Seller.” (See Answer to Complaint.) Plaintiffs argue that the handwritten explanation stating that the Pace Lien would be paid through escrow overrides the form language in Paragraph 1 under general rules of contract interpretation. (See Civ. Code § 1651 [handwritten contract language controls over printed; original language controls over form language].) In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs beg the question by assuming that the Questionnaire is a contract in the first place. According to Defendant, the Questionnaire, by its express language, is not a contract and therefore is not subject to contractual rules of interpretation. In reply, Plaintiffs dismiss Defendant’s argument as “sophistry” and stand on their position.

 

            As stated in the Court’s September 10 ruling, the threshold issue is whether the Agreement’s reference to “assessments” properly includes the PACE lien. This question is subject to “well-established” rules for contract interpretation in the context of a summary adjudication motion:

 

“The interpretation of a contract is a judicial function. [Citation.] In engaging in this function, the trial court ‘give[s] effect to the mutual intention of the parties as it existed’ at the time the contract was executed. [Citation.] Ordinarily, the objective intent of the contracting parties is a legal question determined solely by reference to the contract's terms. [Citation.]” (Wolf v. Walt Disney Pictures & Television (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1107, 1125-1126, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585 (Wolf).)

 

“The court generally may not consider extrinsic evidence of any prior agreement or contemporaneous oral agreement to vary or contradict the clear and unambiguous terms of a written, integrated contract. [Citations.] Extrinsic evidence is admissible, however, to interpret an agreement when a material term is ambiguous. [Citations.]” (Wolf, supra, 162 Cal.App.4th at p. 1126, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585; see also Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. G.W. Thomas Drayage & Rigging (1968) 69 Cal.2d 33, 39-40, 69 Cal.Rptr. 561, 442 P.2d 641 [if extrinsic evidence reveals that apparently clear language in the contract is, in fact, “susceptible to more than one reasonable interpretation,” then extrinsic evidence may be used to determine the contracting parties’ objective intent].)

 

“The interpretation of a contract involves ‘a two-step process: First the court provisionally receives (without actually admitting) all credible evidence concerning the parties’ intentions to determine “ambiguity,” i.e., whether the language is “reasonably susceptible” to the interpretation urged by a party.  If in light of the extrinsic evidence the court decides the language is “reasonably susceptible” to the interpretation urged, the extrinsic evidence is then admitted to aid in the second step – interpreting the contract. [Citation.]’ [Citation.]” (Wolf v. Superior Court (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1343, 1351, 8 Cal.Rptr.3d 649 (Wolf II); [citations].)

 

“When there is no material conflict in the extrinsic evidence, the trial court interprets the contract as a matter of law. [Citations.] This is true even when conflicting inferences may be drawn from the undisputed extrinsic evidence [citations] or that extrinsic evidence renders the contract terms susceptible to more than one reasonable interpretation. [Citations.] If, however, there is a conflict in the extrinsic evidence, the factual conflict is to be resolved by the jury. [Citations.]” (Wolf, supra, 162 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1126-1127, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585; see id. at p. 1134, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585 [“that extrinsic evidence may reveal an ambiguity subjecting a contract to more than one reasonable interpretation does not mean resolution of that ambiguity is necessarily a jury question. Absent a conflict in the evidence, the interpretation of the contract remains a matter of law”].)

 

(Brown v. Goldstein (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 418, 432-33.)

 

When a dispute arises over the meaning of contract language, the first question to be decided is whether the language is ‘reasonably susceptible’ to the interpretation urged by the party.  If it is not, the case is over.  If the court decides the language is reasonably susceptible to the interpretation urged, the court moves to the second question: what did the parties intend the language to mean? ... Thus, where contract language is clear and explicit and does not lead to absurd results, we ascertain intent from the written terms and go no further. If the contract is capable of more than one reasonable interpretation, it is ambiguous, and we apply the standard rules of interpretation in order to give effect to the mutual intention of the parties.  In sum, courts must give a reasonable and commonsense interpretation of a contract consistent with the parties' apparent intent. 

 

(Department of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Lawrence Livermore National Security, LLC (2015) 239 Cal. App. 4th 1060, 1066 [internal quotation marks, ellipses, and citations omitted].)

 

The Court ordered supplemental briefing from the parties as to the proper interpretation of Paragraphs 13 and 17 of the Agreement, with particular focus on the meaning of the terms “encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters,” “monetary liens of record,” and “real property taxes and assessments” and the application of those terms to the Notice of Assessment recorded against the Property. (September 10, 2024 Minute Order.) According to the parties’ Agreement, Plaintiffs were to take title subject to all “encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters,” except for “monetary liens of record,” which Defendant was obligated to pay absent a contrary agreement.  (Agreement, ¶ 13.)  The Agreement also provided, however, that “real property taxes and assessments” were to be paid current and allocated between the Plaintiff Buyers and Defendant Seller on a pro rata basis as of the Close of Escrow.  (Id., ¶ 17.)   The parties submitted supplemental papers in compliance with the briefing schedule set by that ruling.

 

            Plaintiffs, in their supplemental brief, argue that the language of the contract itself, standing independent of the sales documents and the PACE legislation, is ambiguous as to whether the PACE obligation is a “monetary lien of record,” as described in Paragraph 13, or a “real property tax” or “assessment” as described in Paragraph 17. As Plaintiffs state, the broad construction of Paragraph 13’s language dealing with “encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters” appears “all inclusive,’ covering any lien or assessment not otherwise excluded by the Agreement. (SSUMF No. 2, 9; SSDF No. 2, 9.) Thus, Plaintiff states, both “monetary liens of record” under Paragraph 13 and “real property taxes and assessments” under Paragraph 17 are exceptions or exclusions to the general rule of Paragraph 13. The Purchase Agreement defines neither “monetary liens of record” as used in Paragraph 13 nor “real property taxes and assessments” as used in Paragraph 17. (See Plaintiffs’ Exh. C.) Further, as Plaintiffs detail, there are legal bases for describing the PACE obligation as a lien of record, a loan, and an assessment.  (Plaintiffs’ Supp. Brief, pp. 2-4.) 

 

Defendant does not meaningfully address Plaintiff’s arguments. Instead, Defendant merely emphasizes that real property taxes and assessments are paid current under Paragraph 17 as an exception to Paragraph 13B and argues that the status of the PACE obligation as a monetary lien is not material. Defendant also argues the Purchase Agreement “is not ambiguous when read carefully.” The Court disagrees. The parties are expressly disputing the application of the Purchase Agreement to a financial obligation that is not mentioned in the Purchase Agreement based on contract terms which the Agreement employs without explicit definitions. The Court concludes the Purchase Agreement is plainly ambiguous as to how the PACE obligation should be treated.    

 

Defendant relies heavily on the language of the Notice of Assessment without arguing explicitly whether it views the Notice as part of the contract terms or as extrinsic evidence that may be used to resolve any ambiguity in the Purchase Agreement.  While it is the Court’s view that the Notice should not be considered part of the parties’ Purchase Agreement, this conclusion is immaterial because a review of its language does not resolve the Agreement’s ambiguity. Defendant remarks that the Notice of Assessment describes the PACE obligation as an assessment “dozens of times” but ignores that the Notice also states that, upon recording, the assessment “shall become a lien upon such real property.”  (Exhibit E, first page.) Of even greater significance, Exhibit D to the Notice of Assessment reinforces rather than dispels the uncertainty about how the PACE obligation is to be treated under the Purchase Agreement.  (Id., Exh. D, at pdf p. 93.)  Exhibit D discusses certain problems that may arise upon the transfer of the underlying property. Specifically, it reveals an August 2010 announcement by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac that they “will not purchase mortgage loans secured by properties with an outstanding PACE obligation.” Disclosures to the Property Owner about possible issues arising from the federal agencies’ positions include that “lenders . . . may conclude the PACE assessment should be paid off before a property transfers or is refinanced” or that “participating in a PACE program is a violation of typical mortgage terms prohibiting senior liens without lender consent.”  These situations, Exhibit D continues, may result in a buyer’s lender refusing “to finance the buyer’s first mortgage loan unless the assessment is paid off.”  The Court concludes from the warnings in Exhibit D that a transfer of property may be taken subject to the PACE obligation, or it may be paid off before the transfer to convey clean title to the buyer and give priority to the new mortgagor’s lien. Because it acknowledges either possibility, the Notice of Assessment taken as a whole offers little guidance for how the parties’ Purchase Agreement should be interpreted. 

   

Plaintiffs contend that the ambiguity is conclusively resolved by the Seller Property Questionnaire, which should be considered either part of the Purchase Agreement or parol evidence admissible to resolve the contract ambiguity.  (Exhibit C.)   Because the Questionnaire explicitly states it is “not intended to be part of the contract between Buyer and Seller,” and the Purchase Agreement requires a written document to modify its terms, the Court does not agree that the Questionnaire is either a separate contract or a modification of the Purchase Agreement. 

 

Nonetheless, it is critical parol evidence of the parties’ intentions in entering into the Agreement.  Pursuant to Paragraphs 10A(4) and 14A of the Purchase Agreement, Defendant was required to provide this Questionnaire within 7 days of execution of the Agreement.  (Exhibit C, ¶¶ 10A(4), 14A.)  In response, Plaintiffs had to approve the disclosures or request repairs or other action from Defendant.  (Id., ¶ 14B.)  Through the Seller Property Questionnaire, Defendant made a series of disclosures about “Title, Ownership Liens, and Legal Claims.”  One of the pre-printed questions on this standard form provided by the California Association of Realtors was Paragraph 14.E, which asked Defendant to state whether there was “Any PACE lien (such as HERO or SCEIP) or other lien on your Property securing a loan to pay for an alteration, modification, replacement, improvement, remodel or material repair of the Property?” (SSUMF No. 4, 12; SSDF No. 4, 12.) The box labeled “Yes” next to Paragraph 14.E is checked, and in the “Explanation” section there is a handwritten statement “Has a Loan for improvements that will be paid through escrow.” (Id.) In contrast, Paragraph 14.F inquired whether the seller was aware of “[t]he cost of any alteration, modification, replacement, improvement, remodel or material repair of the Property being paid by an assessment on the Property tax bill?” (See Id.; Plaintiffs’ Exh. D ¶ 14.F.) Unlike Paragraph 14.E, the box labeled “No” next to Paragraph 14.F is checked. (Id.) Based on these responses, Plaintiffs argue that, at the time of the execution of the Purchase Agreement, Defendant understood the PACE Assessment to be considered a “monetary lien” to be paid off in escrow and not an assessment on the property tax bill.  This argument is reinforced by Defendant’s own deposition testimony in which she states under oath that she understood at the time of the escrow that the PACE obligation was to be paid off in escrow out of the sales proceeds.  (Plaintiff’s Exh. G.)   

 

Defendant, in opposition, argues that the Notice of Assessment clearly indicates that it is an “assessment” to be collected in the same manner and at the same time as the general taxes on real property. (See Plaintiffs’ Exh. E §3(d).) As explained above, however, the Notice of Assessment interjects additional uncertainty into the contract interpretation task before the Court as its Exhibit D conveys the notion that the assessment may have to be paid off in connection with a property transfer and, thus, does not forestall the PACE obligation’s treatment either as an assessment under Paragraph 17 or a monetary lien under Paragraph 13B.  As Plaintiffs argue in reply, standing alone, the Notice of Assessment is not evidence of the intent of the parties in executing the Purchase Agreement. There is no evidence, for example, that Defendant examined, analyzed, and relied on the Notice, and given her assertion that she speaks no English and had to have her simple declaration translated for her, the idea that Defendant relied on this Notice is questionable.  Defendant’s contentions regarding the understanding of the escrow company are likewise irrelevant to the determination of what the parties understood the Purchase Agreement to mean with respect to the PACE obligation at the time the Agreement was executed.

 

In summary, Plaintiffs have provided evidence tending to demonstrate that Defendant, at the time of the Agreement, understood the PACE obligation to not be an “assessment” for the purpose of the Agreement but rather a “Loan for improvements that will be paid through escrow.”  Defendant bears the burden to show with evidence that there is a triable issue of fact as to the parties’ understanding of the Agreement when it was executed. While the parties dispute the legal significance of the Questionnaire, the record exposes no triable factual issues about its execution or meaning in the context of the sales transaction.  Evidence of Defendant’s subsequent understanding or confusion is not relevant, nor is evidence of the view of the escrow company. The Notice of Assessment itself is not evidence of the parties’ intention, as it does not speak to the subjective understanding of the parties. Defendant has therefore failed to carry her burden to demonstrate a triable issue of fact on this point. The Court therefore finds that the PACE obligation is subject to Paragraph 13B of the Purchase Agreement rather than an “assessment” within the meaning of Paragraph 17. Defendant therefore owed a duty to Plaintiffs to pay off the PACE obligation through escrow.

 

Further, the Court finds that the First Affirmative Defense is without merit because whether the Seller Property Questionnaire is a contract itself is immaterial; Defendant is required to pay off the PACE obligation under the Purchase Agreement.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED in its entirety.

·         06/26/24: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a Proposition 65 enforcement action. Plaintiff alleges that first-aid kits sold by Defendant contain Diisonyl Phthalate and that Defendant failed to disclose this information.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to form interrogatories and special interrogatories, and for sanctions.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Request for Sanctions is DENIED.

 

            Defendants are ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to form interrogatories and special interrogatories, and for sanctions. As the motions are subject to the same legal standard and concern substantially identical factual and legal issues, the Court addresses the motions jointly in the interest of brevity.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to an interrogatory when the court finds that: “(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete[;] (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate[; or] (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Timing:

 

            A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

            Plaintiff’s counsel propounded Requests for Admissions, Requests for Production, Form Interrogatories, and Special Interrogatories on Defendant on May 3, 2024. (Declaration of Daniel M. Greenbaum ISO Mot. ¶ 6.) Defendant served responses to the complete set of discovery on July 20, 2024 by mail and electronic service. (Id. ¶ 11; Exh. 5.) Accounting for a two-day extension for electronic service, these motions were due on September 5, 2024, the date these motions were filed and served. Plaintiffs’ Motions are timely.

 

Meet and Confer

 

A party making a motion to compel further responses must also include a declaration stating facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally the issues presented by the motion before filing the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300 (b)(1).)

Plaintiff’s counsel states his office sent Defendant’s counsel a meet-and-confer letter regarding the discovery responses on August 14, 2024. (Greenbaum Decl. ¶ 12, Exh. 6.) Plaintiff does not state whether Defendant responded to this letter, or whether there was any additional effort to communicate with Defendant’s counsel to informally resolve this dispute. A single letter is not sufficient to demonstrate a “reasonable and good faith attempt” at informal resolution. Plaintiff has not satisfied its statutory meet-and-confer obligations. Nevertheless, the Court will address the merits of the motions to expeditiously resolve this dispute.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of (1) the Articles of Organization for MONARCH ACTION, LLC filed February 22, 2022; (2) the September 12, 2023 Amendment to those Articles of Organization; (3) the October 12, 2023 Amendment to those Articles of Organization; and (4) a printout of a search report for “MONARCH ACTION” on the official website of the Los Angeles County Registrar-Recorder.

 

            Defendant’s Requests Nos. 1-3 are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(c) (official acts). Defendant’s Request No. 4 is DENIED as irrelevant to the Court’s ruling. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 (“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff propounded sets of Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories to Defendant on May 3, 2024. (Greenbaum Decl. Exh. 1.) In response, Defendant served boilerplate objections claiming that the discovery “was propounded by a non-entity in the State of California and non-party to this case and was therefore unlawfully propounded, void and without force or effect.” (Id. Exh. 5.)

 

            Defendant contends in its opposition to these motions that MONARCH LLC is a non-existent entity and not a party to this case. The Complaint in this action, filed March 22, 2024 originally named “MONARCH LLC” as the Plaintiff. The First Amended Complaint, filed June 26, 2024, clarifies that “MONARCH” is an abbreviation for “Mothers Oversight Network for Actionable Contaminant Harm.” (FAC ¶ 4.) Moreover, the Amendments of Articles of Organization filed with the Secretary of State, which Defendant purports to rely upon in opposition, plainly states that MONARCH is an abbreviation for the name of the entity as of September 2023. (See RJN Exhs. 2-3.) Defendant offers no authority in support of the conclusion that applying an abbreviation of an LLC’s legal name is improper, and Defendant’s bare assertion that an abbreviation is a “fictitious business name” subject to the requirements of Business & Professions Code section 17918 is not persuasive. Defendant’s objections and arguments are wholly specious and without merit. Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an order compelling further responses to both form and special interrogatories.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff also requests sanctions against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally, for Defendant’s invalid objections.

 

            Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subd. (a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

            Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.300(d).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Id.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff’s counsel claims that he incurred 2.5 hours on each motion at an hourly rate of $600, for a total of $1,500 on each motion. (Greenbaum Decl. ¶ 13.) Plaintiff’s counsel offers no evidence or testimony as to the reasonableness of those expenses by, for example, speaking to his legal training or years of experience. Plaintiff has therefore failed to demonstrate that the attorney’s fees sought are, in fact, reasonable. The Court thus declines to award sanctions.

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Request for Sanctions is DENIED.
