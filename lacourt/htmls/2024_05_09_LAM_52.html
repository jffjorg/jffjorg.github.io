DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 19STCV15834    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Richard Owens’s Motion for Reconsideration

Plaintiff Richard Owens moves for reconsideration of the court’s order granting defendant City of Los Angeles’s motion for nonsuit.

The court cannot consider a motion for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008.  “[A] motion for reconsideration may only be considered before final judgment is entered and while the case is still pending in the trial court.”  (Betz v. Pankow (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 931, 937.)  The court entered final judgment in this action on March 27, 2024.  It is too late for reconsideration. 

Assuming the court had the power to reconsider its order granting nonsuit, it would not do so.  A motion for reconsideration must be “based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law.”  (CCP § 1008(a).)  The moving party “shall state by affidavit… what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff does not show new or different facts, circumstances, or law that warrant reconsidering the court’s order granting nonsuit.

The motion is denied.   


Case Number: 23STCV22536    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Peter Alexan Enterprises Inc.’s Motions to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production and Form Interrogatories to Defendant Jara Blash

Plaintiff Peter Alexan Enterprises Inc., doing business as RA Consulting, moves to compel defendant/cross-complainant Jara Blash to further respond to requests for production and to form interrogatories.

Meet and Confer

Blash argues plaintiff did not meet and confer in good faith before filing these motions.  Before moving to compel further discovery responses, a party must make a “reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)  This rule aims “to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order.”  (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1293.)  “The level of effort at informal resolution which satisfies the ‘reasonable and good faith attempt’ standard depends upon the circumstances.”  (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.) 

Plaintiff made an adequate effort to informally resolve these discovery disputes.  Plaintiff’s counsel sent Blash’s counsel detailed meet and confer correspondence about Blash’s discovery responses.  (Kernan Decl., ¶¶ 4-7, Exs. C-F.)

Opposing Separate Statements

            Plaintiff argues Blash waived any opposition because she did not file a separate statement in support of either opposition.  California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a) provides, “Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request must be accompanied by a separate statement.”  Rule 3.1345(c) provides, “A separate statement is a separate document filed and served with the discovery motion... .”  This requirement does not apply when opposing discovery motions.

Requests for Production

Plaintiff moves to compel Blash to serve further responses to requests for production Nos. 1-3, 6-21, and 35-40.  A party demanding documents may move to compel further responses if “[a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete,” “[a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive,” or “[a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.”  (CCP § 2031.310(a).)

Blash made meritless objections in response to these requests.  Her response begins with eight “general objections.”  The responding party “shall respond separately to each item or category.”  (CCP § 2031.210(a).)  Such “general objections” are “too general.”  (CCP § 2031.310(a)(3).)  Blash also does not substantively justify these “general objections.”  (See Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255 [“if a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on responding party to justify any objection”].)

Blash also responded that she “objects to this request to the extent it seeks documents already in Plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, or equally available to Plaintiff.”  This objection does not apply to requests for production.  The objection only applies to interrogatories, and only when “the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully.”  (CCP § 2030.220(c).)  Responding parties must conduct “a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry … in an effort to comply.”  (CCP § 2031.230.)  Responding parties necessarily can determine which documents they possess.  If they possess responsive documents and have no valid basis for withholding them, they must produce the documents regardless of whether the requesting party also possesses them. 

Blash further objected that each request “is overbroad and burdensome.”  These requests are “reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (CCP § 2017.010.)  They are not overly broad.  They are sufficiently tailored to the claims in this action.  During the meet and confer process, plaintiff agreed to narrow the time period of documents requested to three years before plaintiff filed this action.  (Kernan Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. F.)  For her objection that the requests are “burdensome,” Blash does not present “ ‘evidence showing the quantum of work required.’ ” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549.) 

The court will not overrule objections based on attorney-client and work product privileges.  Courts may not “find a waiver of the attorney-client privilege and work product doctrine when the objecting party submits an inadequate privilege log that fails to provide sufficient information to evaluate the merits of the objections.”  (Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1120.)  Blash may assert these privileges but must serve a privilege log to “provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of” the objections.”  (CCP § 2031.240(c)(1).) 

Nos. 6, 15, and 40

            Blash made meritless objections but also gave valid statements of compliance in response to Nos. 6, 15, and 40.  After objecting, she responded, “Defendant will produce all documents responsive to this Request in her possession, custody, and control.”  When objecting, the response must “[i]dentify with particularity any document, tangible thing, land, or electronically stored information falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made.”  (CCP § 2031.240(b)(1).)  When the responding party agrees to produce all responsive documents, there is nothing withheld and no reason to object. 

            Plaintiff’s motion argues Blash failed to produce documents in response to Nos. 6, 15, and 40.  Blash later produced some documents.  Whether Blash produced all responsive documents in her possession is not properly before the court.  That is the subject of a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320(a) after a party “fails to permit the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling in accordance with that party’s statement of compliance.” 

            Except for objections based on attorney-client privilege and the work product doctrine, Jara Blash’s objections to the requests for production are overruled.

Interrogatories

Plaintiff moves to compel defendant Jara Blash to further respond to form interrogatory – general, No. 17.1.  A party propounding interrogatories may move to compel further responses when an answer “is evasive or incomplete,” “[a]n exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate,” or “[a]n objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”  (CCP § 2030.300(a).) 

Blash provided supplemental responses after plaintiff filed this motion.  (Ly Decl., ¶ 20, Ex. P.)  The supplemental responses include evasive answers to No. 17.1(d), which ask the responding party to “identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.” 

As to several requests for admission, Blash responded, “Plaintiff has possession, custody, and control of the documents that may support the response to this interrogatory.  Blash will be seeking those documents in discovery, which remains ongoing.”  (E.g., Nos. 2, 8, 11.)  That is not “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.”  (CCP § 2030.220(a).)  If Blash does not know of any such documents, she must state so instead of speculating that plaintiff has unspecified documents that “may support” her responses. 

Blash’s response also included improper general objections.  Her objections to form interrogatory – general, No. 17.1, are overruled.

Sanctions

In each motion, plaintiff moves for $4,560 in sanctions against Blash and her counsel.  A court must impose monetary sanctions against any party or attorney who unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or other circumstances would make sanctions unjust.  (CCP §§ 2030.300(d) [interrogatories]; 2031.310(h) [requests for production].)  Blash unsuccessfully opposed these motions and made meritless objections to discovery.  She did not act with substantial justification.  Sanctions are just under the circumstances.

Disposition

Plaintiff Peter Alexan Enterprises Inc.’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production is granted.  Defendant/cross-complainant Jara Blash is ordered to serve further verified responses to requests for production Nos. 1-3, 6-21, and 35-40 and a privilege log within 30 days.  Blash must make no objections except those based on attorney-client privilege or the work product doctrine.  Blash’s responses shall be limited to documents from September 18, 2020, to the present.    

Defendant shall produce all additional responsive documents concurrently with her written responses.  Defendant/cross-complainant Jara Blash and her counsel John K. Ly are ordered to pay plaintiff $4,560 in sanctions within 30 days.  Defendant and her counsel shall be jointly and severally liable for the sanctions.

Plaintiff Peter Alexan Enterprises Inc.’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories is granted.  Defendant/cross-complainant Jara Blash is ordered to serve further verified responses without objections to form interrogatory – general, No. 17.1(d) within 30 days.  Defendant/cross-complainant Jara Blash and her counsel John K. Ly are ordered to pay plaintiff $4,560 in sanctions within 30 days.  Defendant and her counsel shall be jointly and severally liable for the sanctions.

Defendant Jara Blash’s Motion for Protective Order

Defendant Jara Blash moves for a protective order to delay her deposition and to take the deposition either in Florida or by remote means.  Generally, “the burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought.”  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

Assuming the motion is not moot as to scheduling the deposition, Blash does not show good cause for an order delaying her deposition.  Not being available may be a reason to not attend as a practical matter, but it is not a valid objection to a notice of deposition.  (See CCP §§ 2025.210 & 2025.410.)  It may be a professional courtesy to mutually schedule a deposition, but the Civil Discovery Act does not require that.  “An oral deposition shall be scheduled for a date at least 10 days after service of the deposition notice.”  (CCP § 2025.270(a).)  “The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action… to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.”  (CCP § 2025.280(a).)

That Blash prefers to resolve disputes regarding the pleadings and other discovery issues does not constitute good cause to delay her deposition. 

Blash shows good cause to require her deposition take place either remotely or in Florida.  It is undisputed that she lives in Florida.  “Unless the court orders otherwise under Section 2025.260, the deposition of a natural person, whether or not a party to the action, shall be taken at a place that is, at the option of the party giving notice of the deposition, either within 75 miles of the deponent’s residence, or within the county where the action is pending and within 150 miles of the deponent’s residence.”  (CCP § 2025.250(a).)  Nowhere in California is within 150 miles of Blash’s residence.  

For depositions in another state, “[t]he deposition notice shall specify a place in the state, territory, or insular possession of the United States that is within 75 miles of the residence or a business office of a deponent.”  (CCP § 2026.010(b).)  Defendant cannot require Blash to attend a deposition more than 75 miles from her residence.

Disposition

Defendant Jara Blash’s motion for protective order is granted in part.  The court hereby orders that, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2026.010, Blash shall not be required to attend deposition more than 75 miles from her residence.

Defendant Jara Blash’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

Defendant Jara Blash demurs to the first and second causes of action alleged in the first amended complaint by plaintiff Peter Alexan Enterprises Inc. dba RA Consulting. 

1. Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action against Blash.  “Intentional interference with prospective economic advantage has five elements: (1) the existence, between the plaintiff and some third party, of an economic relationship that contains the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant's knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentionally wrongful acts designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm proximately caused by the defendant’s action.”  (Roy Allan Slurry Seal, Inc. v. American Asphalt South, Inc. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 505, 512.)

A. Blash as Plaintiff’s Agent or Representative

Blash argues she cannot be liable for any interference done while she worked for plaintiff.  “[T]he representative of a contracting party may not be held liable for the tort of interfering with its principal’s contract.”  (Mintz v. Blue Cross of California (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1594, 1607.)  This rule “ ‘derives from the principle that ordinarily corporate agents and employees acting for and on behalf of the corporation cannot be held liable for inducing a breach of the corporation's contract since being in a confidential relationship to the corporation their action in this respect is privileged.’ ”  (Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, 576.) 

None of the authority Blash relies on addresses when the principal sues its own agent for interfering with the principal’s business.  In these cases, a third party sues the agent for interfering with a contract between the third party and the principal.  The above rule originates from the law generally limiting an agent’s liability to third persons.  (Civ. Code, § 2343.)  In contrast, agents generally owe numerous duties to their own principals.  (See Rest.3d Agency, §§ 8.01-8.12.)  The rule that a party’s representative cannot be liable for interference with the principal’s contract or business relations does not apply to actions by the principal.

Moreover, even if this rule applies, it does not mean plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts for a cause of action arising from Blash’s conduct while she was plaintiff’s agent.  “Irrespective of the labels attached by the pleader to any alleged cause of action, we examine the factual allegations of the complaint, ‘to determine whether they state a cause of action on any available legal theory.’ ”  (Adelman v. Associated Intern. Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 352, 359.)  Agents owe numerous duties to principals.  The factual allegations constitute a breach of at least one of those duties, even if not precisely the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage.

B. Wrongful Conduct After Termination

Blash also argues she cannot be liable for any conduct after her termination because plaintiff does not allege anything independently wrongful.  “[A] plaintiff seeking to recover for alleged interference with prospective economic relations has the burden of pleading and proving that the defendant’s interference was wrongful ‘by some measure beyond the fact of the interference itself.’ ”  (Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 392–393.)  “The defendant’s conduct must ‘fall outside the boundaries of fair competition.’ ”  (Venhaus v. Shultz (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1079.) 

The first amended complaint alleges “Defendants … interfered with Plaintiff’s economic relationships with San Diego Comic Convention” and others by “spreading false statements about Plaintiff to Plaintiff’s vendors, contractors, and clients (including San Diego Comic Convention), refusing to return company files and property, egregious misconduct during the 2023 Comic-Con event in San Diego, improper private communications with Comic-Con representatives at Comic-Con after termination (Defendant Blash), various improper attempts to hire Plaintiff’s managers after Defendants’ termination, and multiple illegal attempts to access company accounts after termination.”  (¶ 21.)  When liberally construed, these allegations suffice to constitute independently wrongful conduct. 

Moreover, the court found Blash can be liable for interference carried out during her employment.  Even if she cannot be liable for conduct after her termination, that would not dispose of an entire cause of action as required to sustain a demurrer.  (Southern California Pizza Co., LLC v. Certain Underwriters at Lloyd’s, London etc. (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 140, 154.) 

2. Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action against Blash.  She repeats her argument that she cannot be liable for acts during her employment.  The court again rejects that argument.  Blash further argues plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to establish a duty of care as to acts after her termination.  Again, because she can be liable for negligent acts during her employment, this argument would not dispose of the entire cause of action.  The court therefore need not address the issue of duty after Blash’s termination.  It declines to do so.

Blash also argues the first amended complaint does not sufficiently identify the business relationships she allegedly interfered with.  Blash did not raise this argument until the reply.  Moreover, the first amended complaint adequately alleges at least one such relationship: “Plaintiff’s economic relationships with San Diego Comic Convention.”  (FAC, ¶ 42.) 

Disposition

Defendant Jara Blash’s demurrer to the first amended complaint is overruled.  Blash is ordered to answer within 20 days.
