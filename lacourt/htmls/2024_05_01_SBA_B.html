Case Number: 23TRCV00966    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

KEEP AMERICA SAFE AND BEAUTIFUL, INC.,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV00966

 

vs.

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

DELTA GALIL USA, INC.,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          May 1, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Galil USA, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Keep America Safe and Beautiful, Inc.

(1)   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One)

(2)   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One)

(3)   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents (Set One)

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motions are DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On June 28, 2023, plaintiff Keep America Safe and Beautiful, Inc. filed a complaint against Delta Galil USA, Inc. for civil penalties and injunctive relief for violation of Proposition 65.

On July 27, 2023, the court overruled defendant’s demurrer.

On February 5, 2024, defendant filed a substitution of attorney.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

45-Day Rule:  This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP §1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).

Meet-and-Confer Requirement:  The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).

Separate Statement:  Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3). 

Interrogatories

CCP §2030.300 states:  “(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:   (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. . . . (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.  (b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories. . . .”

CCP §2030.220 states:  “(a) Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.  (b) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.  (c) If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”

Request for Production of Documents

On receipt of a response to an inspection demand, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  CCP §2031.310(a).  A statement of compliance shall state that the production, inspection, and related activity demanded will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.  CCP §2031.220.

A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).  “To establish ‘good cause,’ the burden is on the moving party to show both:  [1] Relevance to the subject matter (e.g., how the information in the documents would tend to prove or disprove some issue in the case); and [2] Specific facts justifying discovery (e.g., why such information is necessary for trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial).  The fact that there is no alternative source for the information sought is an important factor in establishing ‘good cause’ for inspection.  But it is not essential in every case.”  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1495.6 (citations omitted).  “Declarations are generally used to show the requisite ‘good cause’ for an order to compel inspection.  The declarations must contain ‘specific facts’ rather than mere conclusions.”  Id. at 8:1495.7 (citation omitted).  “The declarations may be on information and belief, if necessary.  However, in such cases, the ‘specific facts’ supporting such information and belief (the sources of the information) must also be alleged.”  Id. at 8:1495.8 (citation omitted).  “Most declarations are made by the attorney for the moving party, who is usually more familiar with the relevancy and ‘specific facts’ constituting ‘good cause’ for inspection.”  Id. at 8:1495.9.

“If ‘good cause’ is shown by the moving party, the burden is then on the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure (the same as on motions to compel answers to interrogatories or deposition questions. . . ).”  Id. at 8:1496 (citation omitted). 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Delta Galil USA, Inc. requests that the Court compel plaintiff to provide further responses and to produce responsive documents to its initial form and special interrogatories and request for production of documents.

            The complaint alleges that it is brought by plaintiff in the public interest of the People of the State of California to enforce their right to be informed of the presence of chemicals listed by the State of California, pursuant to the Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986, codified at Health & Safety Code §25249.6, et al. (“Proposition 65”), including diisononyl phthalate (DINP).  Complaint, ¶1.  Plaintiff seeks to remedy defendant’s failure to warn citizens of California, in violation of Proposition 65 about the presence of DINP (“Listed Chemical”) in defendant’s Splendid x Rachel Miriam R&R Gift Set offered for sale throughout California (“Products”).  Id., ¶2.  Defendant’s Products contain the Listed Chemical and consumers of Products in California are exposed to the Listed Chemical through dermal exposure and ingestion of the Products.  Id., ¶3.  Plaintiff attaches a copy of the 60-Day Notice, dated January 4, 2023, which plaintiff alleges it sent to defendant, California’s Attorney General, every District Attorney in the state, and to the City Attorneys of every California city with a population greater than 775,000.  Plaintiff alleges that attached to the 60-Day Notices were Certificates of Merit attesting to the reasonable and meritorious basis for this action, Certificates of Service attesting to service of the letters on each entity, and a description of Proposition 65 prepared by the California Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment.  Further, factual information sufficient to establish the basis of the Certificates of Merit were enclosed with the 60-Day Notices sent to California’s Attorney General.  Id., ¶5.  After receiving the claims in the 60-Day Notice, the public enforcement agencies identified in paragraph 5 have failed to commence and diligently prosecute a cause of action against defendant under Proposition 65.  Id., ¶6. 

            Defendant explains that it served written discovery requests on plaintiff on October 6, 2023.  On November 8, 2023, plaintiff served responses, consisting mostly of objections.  On February 16, 2024, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter to plaintiff’s counsel as to the deficiencies, requesting a response by February 23, 2023.  On February 22, 2023, defense counsel responded that plaintiff intended to supplement its discovery responses by March 13, 2023.

            Defendant asserts that plaintiff failed to serve supplemental discovery responses by March 13, 2024 and defendant served its motions by March 25, 2024.

            In opposition, plaintiff contends that it has served supplemental discovery responses.  Plaintiff also asserts that defendant’s motions are premature because they were required to do an IDC.  Plaintiff further contends that it had agreed to extend defendant’s motion cutoff date to April 22, 2024, but yet defendant “rushed” to file the motions.

            In reply, defendant contends that it was not required to do an IDC before filing the motions and that plaintiff’s “supplemental” responses were the same as the original responses.

            The Court rules as follows:  The motions were untimely filed, and thus, the Court does not have jurisdiction to rule on them.  Plaintiff’s responses were served on November 8, 2023 by email and mail.  Thus, defendant was required to file and serve its motions within 45 days plus two court days (see CCP §1010.6(a)(3)(B) for electronic service), which was by December 27, 2023, or five court days, which was by January 2, 2024, considering the holidays.  Defendant has not shown any written agreement to an extension to file the motions.  It appears that the first correspondence to meet and confer was dated February 16, 2024, which was after the deadline to file the motions.

            The motions are thus DENIED.

 

ORDER

            The motions are DENIED.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion for Leave to Amend Pleading

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to file the (corrected) SAC within five days.

BACKGROUND

            On June 7, 2023, plaintiff Michael Ward filed a complaint against City of Torrance and Eino Jussila Pentti for motor vehicle negligence.  It is missing the cause of action attachment to the form complaint.

            On September 12, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC alleging an incident that occurred on June 9, 2022 at Torrance Blvd. and Madrid Ave.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are:  (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 486, 490.  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff requests leave to file a Second Amended Complaint.  

Plaintiff explains that after defendants were served, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter.  Plaintiff asserts that the “issues of concern” “were resolved except for one issue requiring an amendment of the pleading” and plaintiff filed a FAC.  Plaintiff contends that the FAC has a file stamp in the top right hand corner and thus, plaintiff asserts, defendant would not file a responsive pleading because of the file stamp.  Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to file another amended pleading but the clerk’s office rejected it.

Plaintiff’s counsel failed to file a declaration in accordance with CRC Rule 3.1324.  In any event, the court notes that the complaint was filed on June 7, 2023 but was missing the attachment for a cause of action for motor vehicle negligence.  Plaintiff then filed a FAC on September 12, 2023, but it contains the same file stamp dated June 7, 2023 (and along the side a file stamp for September 12).

The court thus finds that filing a SAC without the file stamp dated June 7, 2023 in the top right hand corner is warranted and that plaintiff has shown good cause based on the court’s review of the complaint and FAC.

“Leave to amend is in general required to be liberally granted.”  Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal. App. 4th 402, 412 (citation omitted).

The court notes though that the proposed SAC is defective.  See paras. 3.a. and 3.b. (City of Torrance is not a plaintiff) and MV-2.b. and e. (boxes are not checked).  Also, the “Attachment to” should state Second Amended (and not First Amended) and contain the correct date at bottom of page 3 of 3 of counsel’s signature.

The motion is thus GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to file a (corrected) SAC within five days.

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to file a (corrected) SAC within five days.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

Motion for Leave to File an Amendment to the Complaint

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART.  The motion is GRANTED as to filing an amended complaint as to the proposed second and third causes of action and DENIED as to adding the proposed fourth cause of action against Hyatt Corporation.  Plaintiff is ordered to file a First Amended Complaint within ten days.

BACKGROUND

            On August 16, 2023, plaintiff Gary R. Herron, II filed a complaint against City of Los Angeles and Hyatt Corporation, asserting a cause of action for premises liability against each defendants based on a trip and fall on a raised sidewalk on March 17, 2023.

            On October 4, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint designating Prism Hospitality, L.P. as Doe 1.

            On October 24, 2023, defendant City of Los Angeles filed a cross-complaint.

            On November 28, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment designating SWC CA LAX LLC as Doe 2.

            On February 1, 2024, Hyatt Corporation, SWC CA LAX, and Prism Hospitality, LP filed a cross-complaint against City of Los Angeles for indemnification, contribution, and declaratory relief.

            On March 12, 2023, the court sustained defendants Hyatt, SWC CA LAX, and Prism Hospitality’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action with thirty days leave to amend and deemed the motion to strike as moot.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Herron requests leave to file a First Amended Complaint alleging a second cause of action for negligence against SWC CA LAX LLC, a third cause of action for negligence against Prism Hospitality, L.P., and a fourth cause of action for negligence against Hyatt Corporation “for remuneration, licensed, and authorized SWC CA LAX LLC to use the name Hyatt Regency on the hotel.” 

Plaintiff asserts that while walking on a public sidewalk adjacent to the hotel named Hyatt Regency located at 6225 Century Boulevard, Los Angeles, he tripped and fell over a raised section of the sidewalk. 

            The court notes that it had sustained with thirty days leave to amend the cause of action for premises liability against moving defendants, finding that the allegations were insufficient.  Defendants Prism (Doe 1) and SWC (Doe 2) had been added after the complaint was filed and there were no allegations as to them.  The allegations were also insufficient as to Hyatt.  The first cause of action is as to the City of Los Angeles, which has answered.

            Plaintiff’s counsel states in his declaration that prior to filing the complaint, counsel did not know that Hyatt Corporation did not own the hotel named Hyatt Regency or the property on which it is located.  Counsel did not know that Prism Hospitality was the management company of the hotel and the property or that SWC CA LAX LLC was the owner of the hotel and the property.  Jack Kaufman decl.  Plaintiff contends that he could reasonably assume that Hyatt Corporation would exercise due care in managing and assuring the safety of the sidewalk adjacent to the hotel, citing to Evidence Code §623, which provides that whenever a party has, by its conduct led another to believe that a particular thing is true, it is not permitted to contradict it in litigation.  Plaintiff also asserts that he has “now discovered that the raised section of the sidewalk over which plaintiff tripped and fell was caused by the roots from bushes and trees adjacent to the sidewalk” and that the bushes and trees are “on the property belonging to the owner of the hotel.” 

            In opposition, defendants argue that plaintiff’s proposed second, third, and fourth causes of action amount to a “sham pleading” because plaintiff alleges a new theory of recovery based on negligence.  Defendants also contend that plaintiff “omits facts regarding Hyatt Franchising L.L.C.’s known relationship to the subject property and its actual name.”  Defendants further argue that plaintiff’s proposed amendments do not state a cause of action against Hyatt Franchising because plaintiff has no facts to suggest defendant created or had control over the nuisance.

            In reply, plaintiff asserts that it is seeking leave to amend via a motion in lieu of amending the complaint, and that he seeks to avoid further use of a judicial council form.  Plaintiff’s counsel states in his declaration that an attorney for the City of Los Angeles informed him that the raised section of the public sidewalk over which plaintiff tripped and fell was caused by the roots of trees and bushes on the property of the hotel adjacent to the sidewalk.  Counsel also states that he was not aware of the name Hyatt Franchising when the motion was filed, and that “[i]f the instant motion is granted, Hyatt will demurrer to the fourth cause of action, and the court will rule on this issue.”  Plaintiff also argues that it has not engaged in sham pleading; he has not disingenuously omitted facts or added inconsistent facts.

            The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that plaintiff complied with CRC Rule 3.1324.  Further, “[l]eave to amend is in general required to be liberally granted.”  Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal. App. 4th 402, 412 (citation omitted).  Plaintiff had previously been given leave to amend to allege facts against Prism and SWC CA LAX LLC as they had been identified after the complaint was filed.  As is procedurally proper, plaintiff sought leave to add causes of action based on negligence rather than amend only the second cause of action for premises liability.  Alleging a different theory under negligence is not a sham pleading.  Also, as to whether plaintiff provides facts to support the proposed causes of action, at the pleading stage plaintiff is only required to allege ultimate facts, not evidentiary facts. 

As to the proposed fourth cause of action, though, an amendment would be futile as there does not appear to be any basis for liability against Hyatt Corporation (or Hyatt Franchising), including a duty based on licensing and authorizing the owner to use the name Hyatt Regency.  Further, plaintiff acknowledges that he has not found specific authority in support of liability against Hyatt, and as argued by defendants, plaintiff’s justification citing to Evidence Code §623 is “confusing and obscure.”

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART.  The motion is GRANTED as to filing an amended complaint as to the proposed second and third causes of action and DENIED as to adding the proposed fourth cause of action against Hyatt Corporation.

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART.  The motion is GRANTED as to filing an amended complaint as to the proposed second and third causes of action and DENIED as to adding the proposed fourth cause of action against Hyatt Corporation.

Plaintiff is ordered to file a First Amended Complaint within ten days.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.
