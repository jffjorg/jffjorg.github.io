Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                    Wednesday, September 11, 2024

Department M                                                                                                                           Calendar No. 12

 

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

Saima Aziz v. Starbucks Corporation, et al.   

23TRCV02210

1.      Starbucks Corporation’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication   

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Starbucks Corporation’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is denied.  

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on July 10, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff slipped and fell on property owned or controlled by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges causes of action for General Negligence and Premises Liability.

 

Objections

 

Defendants’ Objections

 

Objections 1 to 11 are overruled. Defendant seeks to object to matters set forth in the separate statement of facts rather than actual evidence.

 

Objections 12 to 22 are moot. Since the burden did not shift to Plaintiff, the Court need not consider Plaintiff’s evidence submitted in opposition. However, the Court notes that as to the objection with respect to the timeliness of the filing and service of the exhibits, the Court would have considered a continuance had Defendant requested one. However, it appears that Defendant was able to file and serve a timely Reply addressing their substantive issues with the exhibits.

 

Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.

 

“A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(1).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Avivi, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467; CCP § 437c(c).)

 

“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” CCP § 437c(f)(1).

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment as to Plaintiff’s Complaint on the grounds that there is no triable issue of material fact. Defendant also purports to move for summary judgment, or, in the alternative summary adjudication “on the grounds that:”  

“Issue Number 1: Ms. Aziz’s causes of action for general negligence and premises liability fail, as a matter of law, because a dangerous condition did not exist in the parking lot and Starbucks did not owe Ms. Aziz a duty of care.

Issue Number 2: Ms. Aziz’s causes of action for general negligence and premises liability fail, as a matter of law, because the white pavement marker upon which Ms. Aziz struck her foot was open and obvious.

Issue Number 3: Ms. Aziz’s causes of action for general negligence and premises liability fail, as a matter of law, because the duty to reasonably inspect and maintain the premises in a reasonably safe condition is immaterial where no dangerous condition existed.” (Notice of Motion, page 2, lines 3-14.)

 

The Court notes that with the purported issues 1 to 3, rather than a procedurally proper mechanism to adjudicate “causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty,” the purported motion for summary adjudication provides three separate reasons why the two causes of action should be adjudicated. This is not a procedurally proper motion for summary adjudication. It is simply a motion to attempt to adjudicate the two causes of action based on these three separate reasons.

 

“Plaintiff's claims for premises liability and negligence rest on the same elements—namely, (1) a legal duty of care, (2) breach of that duty, and (3) proximate cause resulting in injury.” Martinez v. City of Beverly Hills (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 508, 517.

 

Defendant has failed to submit competent evidence to meet its “burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party [by showing] that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2). Therefore, the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to “show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). 

 

The only evidence submitted by Defendant was Defendant counsel’s declaration and exhibits attached. The exhibits included the Complaint, a transcript of Plaintiff’s deposition, photos produced by Plaintiff during her deposition, and Plaintiff’s responses to Special Interrogatories. None of the matters set forth in these exhibits provide competent evidence to show that no dangerous condition existed on the property or that the condition was open and obvious. Instead, the evidence submitted by Defendant simply provides a short cogent distillation of the facts of the incident as relayed by Plaintiff.

 

Apparently, Defendant believes that by simply relaying these facts and showing the photos taken by Plaintiff after the fall, the Court can or must discern, as a matter of law, on its own, that the condition was not dangerous or conversely open and obvious. However, Defendant provides no actual evidence to support this position. Defendant did not provide a declaration from an individual who has competence and knowledge to testify as to these matters who states that he or she has reviewed all the evidence and then provides a statement concluding that the condition was not dangerous or conversely open and obvious with the underlying factual reasoning for this conclusion. The Court notes that, in contrast, in fact, Plaintiff provided evidence to support her conclusion that the condition was dangerous in the form of the declarations of Mark J. Burns and Boyce Oandasan.

 

Apparently, Defendant believes that simply because Plaintiff had visited the store before, and that weather, wetness on the ground, or cleanliness may not have been a factor in the incident, it must mean that the condition was not dangerous.  In addition, Defendant apparently believes that the raised white pavement markers must be deemed by the Court, as a matter of law, as not dangerous. Further, Defendant apparently believes that simply by showing the Court the photographs, the Court must conclude that the condition was open and obvious. The Court cannot act as Defendant’s own expert and simply adopt Defendant’s counsel’s position. Defendant must present competent evidence that supports the positions and conclusions presented by Defendant. As mentioned above, in contrast to Defendant, Plaintiff did present evidence from an individual with competency to testify as to these matters stating that the pavement markers were dangerous based on the height, lack of contrast with the white line, and lack of warning. (Decl., Mark J. Burns).

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.


Case Number: 24TRCV01321    Hearing Date: September 11, 2024    Dept: M

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                      Wednesday, September 11, 2024

Department M                                                                                                                        Calendar No. 11

 

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

Dorothy E. Parrish, et al. v. Simla Villas, Inc., et al.

            23TRCV03611

1.      Simla Villas, Inc., et al.’s Petition to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Simla Villas, Inc., et al.’s Petition to Compel Arbitration and Stay Action is granted.

 

Background

           

            Plaintiffs filed the Complaint on October 30, 2023. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiffs’ decedent, Dorothy E. Parrish, suffered injuries and eventually died while in the care of Defendants.  Plaintiffs set forth the following causes of action: 1. Negligence/Willful Misconduct 2. Elder Abuse and Neglect; 3. Wrongful Death.   

 

            Objections

 

            Plaintiffs’ objections 1 to 4 are overruled.

 

Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            “California law reflects a strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a relatively quick and inexpensive method for resolving disputes.  [Citation.]  To further that policy, [Code of Civil Procedure] section 1281.2 requires a trial court to enforce a written arbitration agreement unless one of three limited exceptions applies.  [Citation.]  Those statutory exceptions arise where (1) a party waives the right to arbitration; (2) grounds exist for revoking the arbitration agreement; and (3) pending litigation with a third party creates the possibility of conflicting rulings on common factual or legal issues.  (§ 1281.2, subds. (a)–(c).)”  Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 959, 967.

 

“The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.”  Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.

 

“The party opposing arbitration has the burden of demonstrating that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.  Nonetheless, this policy does not override ordinary principles of contract interpretation.  [T]he contractual terms themselves must be carefully examined before the parties to the contract can be ordered to arbitration:  Although [t]he law favors contracts for arbitration of disputes between parties, there is no policy compelling persons to accept arbitration of controversies which they have not agreed to arbitrate.”  Rice v. Downs (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 1213, 1223 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

In Rowe v. Exline (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1286, the Court of Appeal found that “a nonsignatory sued as an agent of a signatory may enforce an arbitration agreement.”  Id. at 1286.  In addition, “a nonsignatory who is the agent of a signatory can even be compelled to arbitrate claims against his will.”  Id. at 1285, citing Harris v. Superior Court (1986) 188 Cal.App.3d 475, 477–78.  Further, “in many cases, nonparties to arbitration agreements are allowed to enforce those agreements where there is sufficient identity of parties.”  Valley Casework, Inc. v. Comfort Construction, Inc. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 1013, 1021.  This includes nonparties as agents of a party as well as “a third party beneficiary of an arbitration agreement.”  Ibid. 

 

            Defendants move to compel arbitration and to stay this action pursuant to the FAA and CCP 1281.4 on the grounds that Plaintiffs executed an arbitration agreement wherein Plaintiffs are bound to arbitrate the claims alleged in the Complaint.

 

Defendants attached as Exhibit 1 to the Declaration of Simla Mehta the purported arbitration agreement that was signed by an individual named Daniel Parrish on April 12, 2021. The arbitration provision states, in relevant part, as follows: “It is in the interest of both the facility and the resident that any and all disputes or claims against the facility or its employees be resolved fairly and quickly. Any controversy or claim or relating to this contract, or the breach thereof, shall be settled by arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association.” (Ex. 1, page 33.)

 

Defendants move to compel arbitration and to stay the civil action based on the Plaintiffs’ decedent’s purported son and attorney-in-fact, Daniel Parrish’s signature on the subject arbitration agreement. Defendant acknowledges that the Plaintiffs’ decedent did not sign the arbitration agreement.

 

The following portion of the analysis applies solely to the first and second causes of action and not the third cause of action for Wrongful Death which belongs solely to Daniel E. Parrish, in his individual capacity, and Sean Flavin, an individual. The analysis with respect to the third cause of action will be addressed near the end of this ruling.

 

A person is not the patient’s authorized agent simply by virtue of being the patient’s next of kin.  Pagarigan v. Libby Care Center, Inc. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 298, 302 (holding that the patient’s daughters had no authority to sign arbitration agreement even though they were next of kin where there was no evidence that she had signed a durable power of attorney despite the fact that she was comatose at admission).  Similarly, a patient who signs a power of attorney form after a family member already signed an arbitration agreement for her does not automatically ratify the earlier arbitration agreement.  Flores v. Evergreen At San Diego, LLC (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 581, 588 (holding that husband could not bind wife to the arbitration agreement because there was no evidence showing that the wife gave her express or implied consent to have husband act as her agent); (emphasis added).

 

Here, however, there is no dispute that a power of attorney was executed and notarized on June 5, 2015. (Plaintiffs’ Ex. B.)  Thus, unlike Flores, this document was executed nearly six years prior to the execution of the subject arbitration agreement. However, Plaintiffs contend that the durable power of attorney is ineffective because, on page 2, the line naming the actual agent was left blank. There is no dispute that the portion just above this section requiring Daniel E. Parrish to name himself and to sign was executed properly.

 

In Torres v. Torres (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 870, the respective parties contested the validity of a power of attorney. In that case, the grantor of the power of attorney merely placed an “X” on the line which conferred the powers of attorney. The Court of Appeal held that, while the form may not have substantially complied with the statutory form, the form did substantially comply with California Law, specifically Prob. Code § 4121. The Court stated that the form that was executed did substantially comply with the statute and there was no evidence of fraud, undue influence, or incapacity.

 

Prob. Code, § 4121 states:

“A power of attorney is legally sufficient if all of the following requirements are satisfied:

(a) The power of attorney contains the date of its execution.

(b) The power of attorney is signed either (1) by the principal or (2) in the principal's name by another adult in the principal's presence and at the principal's direction.

(c) The power of attorney is either (1) acknowledged before a notary public or (2) signed by at least two witnesses who satisfy the requirements of Section 4122.”

 

Here, the power of attorney complies with the all the sections of the statutory provision noted above. There is no evidence of fraud, undue influence, or incapacity (at the time of the making). In addition, the California Power of Attorney for Health Care, which was apparently executed on the exact same date, specifically names the same authorized agent and alternate agents.

 

Thus, the Court determines that the California General Durable Power of Attorney, attached as Exhibit B, to the declaration of Daniel E. Parrish sufficiently establishes his authority to sign the arbitration agreement on behalf of decedent as her agent at the time of signing the arbitration agreement.

 

Because the moving party met its initial burden to show the existence of a valid arbitration clause with respect to the claims of the Plaintiffs, the burden shifts to Plaintiffs to show that the arbitration clause should not be enforced as to these claims. Rice, supra, 247 Cal.App.4th at 1223.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the arbitration clause is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable.  A court can invalidate an arbitration agreement when it is unconscionable or against public policy.  See, Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 99.  Unconscionability contains two elements:  procedural unconscionability and substantive unconscionability. 

 

“[U]nconscionability has both a procedural and a substantive' element, the former focusing on ‘oppression' or 'surprise' due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on 'overly harsh' or 'one-sided' results.  The prevailing view is that [procedural and substantive unconscionability] must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability." But they need not be present in the same degree. Essentially a sliding scale is invoked which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves.  In other words, the more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.”  See Armendariz, 24 Cal.4th at 114.     

 

“The traditional standard of unconscionability . . . is that the inequality amounting to fraud must be so strong and manifest as to shock the conscience and confound the judgment of any man of common sense.   Subsequent decisions have defined an unconscionable contract in varying but similar terms, such as a contract that no man in his senses and not under delusion would make on the one hand, and as no honest and fair man would accept on the other.”  See California Grocers Assn. v. Bank of America (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 205, 214-15 (internal citations omitted).

 

Procedural unconscionability may be established by showing oppression and surprise.  Oppression occurs where the parties have unequal bargaining power, and the contract is not the result of meaningful negotiations.  Surprise recognizes the extent to which the agreed upon terms were hidden.

 

 “In many cases of adhesion contracts, the weaker party lacks not only the opportunity to bargain but also any realistic opportunity to look elsewhere for a more favorable contract; he must either adhere to the standardized agreement or forego the needed service.”  Madden v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (1976) 17 Cal.3d 699, 711. Simply because a contract may have elements of an adhesion contract does not render the agreement procedurally unconscionable.  “The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.”  Sanchez v. Valencia Holding (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 912. 

 

Plaintiffs fail to meet their burden to establish unconscionability. To support the procedural unconscionability argument, Plaintiffs states that the arbitration clause was not adequately explained, and that Plaintiffs were not given a choice to negotiate. Plaintiffs also contend that the AAA rules were referred to but not attached.  Defendants have no obligation to highlight, explain, or discuss the terms of the arbitration agreement as any such requirement would be contrary to the FAA. Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 914-15. As to the failure to attach AAA rules, the following passage in Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1472 is instructive: “Plaintiff does not argue that there are any other provisions in the Agreement that would support a finding of procedural unconscionability. Nor does she identify any feature of the AAA rules that prevent fair and full arbitration. Thus, we find the failure to attach the AAA rules, standing alone, is insufficient grounds to support a finding of procedural unconscionability.” Id.

“[C]ourts, including ours, have used various nonexclusive formulations to capture the notion that unconscionability requires a substantial degree of unfairness beyond a simple old-fashioned bad bargain. This latter qualification is important. Commerce depends on the enforceability, in most instances, of a duly executed written contract. A party cannot avoid a contractual obligation merely by complaining that the deal, in retrospect, was unfair or a bad bargain. Not all one-sided contract provisions are unconscionable; hence the various intensifiers in our formulations: ‘overly harsh,’ ‘unduly oppressive,’ ‘unreasonably favorable.’ The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.” Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1245 (internal citations and quotations omitted; emphasis in original).

 

The Court determines that Plaintiffs have failed to establish procedural unconscionability.

 

As noted above, both procedural and substantive unconscionability must exist for the Court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce the arbitration provision. As noted, Plaintiff submitted no competent evidence to support the procedural unconscionability element. Thus, the Court need not even address the substantive unconscionability arguments. However, Plaintiffs did not establish substantive unconscionability.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the agreement is one-sided and only applies to claims made by the Plaintiffs. However, the plain language belies this argument. The agreement specifically states: “Any controversy or claim or relating to this contract, or the breach thereof, shall be settled by arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association.” Certainly, claims made by and against the Plaintiffs would be governed by the agreement as those would be considered “any” controversy. Plaintiffs also argue that the agreement is substantively unconscionable because it requires each side to bear their own costs of arbitration. Plaintiffs rely upon Bickel v. Sunrise Assisted Living (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 1, 13. Here, however, the agreement specifically notes the holding of this case and provides language acknowledging the responsibility of the facility for potential attorneys’ fees: “Attorney’s fees would be void to the extent that a claim was brought on an elder abuse case. In the event of elder abuse cases, the facility is responsible to provide an award of reasonable attorney’s fees as those fees are not waivable. (See Bickel v. Sunrise Assisted Living (2012) 206 Cal.App.5th). (sic)” (Defendant’s Ex. 1, p. 34).

 

Thus, the arbitration agreement is not substantively unconscionable.

 

The following analysis applies solely to the Wrongful Death cause of action. Defendants acknowledge that this cause of action is not subject to the arbitration provision.

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2(c) provides an exception to arbitration when:

“A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. For purposes of this section, a pending court action or special proceeding includes an action or proceeding initiated by the party refusing to arbitrate after the petition to compel arbitration has been filed, but on or before the date of the hearing on the petition. This subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.”

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 further provides:

“If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c), the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement and may order intervention or joinder of all parties in a single action or special proceeding; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action or special proceeding pending the outcome of the arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.”

 

Thus, a court may refuse to enforce arbitration if it determines that a party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action with a third party arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. California Code of Civil Procedure §1281.2(c). Here, it is undisputed that non-parties to the arbitration agreement, Daniel E. Parrish, individually, and Sean Flavin, an individual, are parties to the instant action. However, Plaintiffs have not adequately identified that there is a significant potential of conflicting rulings on common issues of fact and law between the parties to the arbitration agreement and these third parties. Thus, the proper course herein would be to stay the pending court action and proceed to arbitration as to Defendants and Plaintiff Dorothy E. Parrish, Deceased, by and through her Successor-in-Interest, Daniel Parrish pursuant to CCP § 1281.2(c). 

 

Therefore, Defendants’ Petition to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action is granted, in part.

 

Arbitration is ordered between Plaintiff Dorothy E. Parrish, Deceased by and through her Successor-in-Interest, Daniel Parrish and Defendants Simla Villas, Inc. dba Simla Villas Redondo Beach and Simla Mehta.

 

The court action, including the third cause of action brought by Daniel E. Parrish, individually, and Sean Flavin, an individual, is hereby ordered stayed pending arbitration.
