DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24PSCV01041    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  PVOM LLC v. LTG South Hills LLC, et al. 

1 – Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint; and

2 – Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court OVERRULES Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. 

The Court DENIES Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang’s motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. 

Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang must file an answer to the First Amended Complaint within 10 days of the Court’s order. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a breach of contract action. On April 2, 2024, plaintiff PVOM LLC (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants LTG South Hills LLC (LTG), LT Management Group, Shao Xing Max Yang (Shao) (collectively, Defendants), and Does 1 through 50, alleging causes of action for breach of contract and specific performance. On July 31, 2024, following the Court’s sustaining of the Defendants’ respective demurrers and a motion to strike, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same Defendants and alleging the same causes of action. On August 27, 2024, LTG answered the FAC. 

On August 27, 2024, LT Management Group and Shao (collectively, Moving Parties) demurred to and moved to strike portions of the FAC. On October 3, 2024, Plaintiff opposed both motions. On October 9, 2024, Moving Parties replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Demurrer 

            A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (Code Civ. Proc., § 422.10; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 (Donabedian).) It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Ibid.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862, disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287 [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery. (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-972.) The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint, or in a superseded complaint in the same action. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”]) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.)  

DISCUSSION – Demurrer 

Meet and Confer 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), Moving Parties were required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference before bringing this demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The Court finds Moving Parties’ efforts to meet and confer satisfactory. (Huang Decl., ¶ 2.) 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract 

“A cause of action for breach of contract requires pleading of a contract, plaintiff's performance or excuse for failure to perform, defendant's breach and damage to plaintiff resulting therefrom. [Citation.] A written contract may be pleaded either by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. [Citation.]” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) 

Moving Parties demur to the First Cause of Action for breach of contract on the grounds that the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Moving Parties contend the FAC lacks specificity regarding Moving Parties’ alleged breaches and purported misrepresentations. Moving Parties contend the FAC fails to specify how the prorations were inaccurate, which records were not provided, and how the tenant files and plans were mishandled. Moving Parties also contend they were not signatories to the subject purchase agreement. 

Moving Parties then contend that the FAC lacks the requisite specificity regarding alleged misrepresentations, such as which specific representations were false, how they were false, or how the Plaintiff detrimentally relied on those misrepresentations. Moving Parties contend the FAC mischaracterizes the entity relationships by mischaracterizing Shao’s role, fails to recognize that LTG is a special purpose vehicle created to hold commercial property that was later dissolved, and inaccurately claiming Shao is involved in the lease of the Okawa Wagyu property. Moving Parties further contend the FAC fails to establish unity of interest, ownership, and commingling for purposes of alter ego liability, and does not sufficiently allege facts demonstrating commingling, disregard of corporate formalities, or that LTG and LT Management Group acted as one entity. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends the FAC properly pleads facts demonstrating alter ego liability between the Moving Parties, such as the terminated status of LTG and Defendants using the same address. Plaintiff contends a heightened pleading standard does not apply to an alter ego claim. Plaintiff contends Moving Parties do not need to have been signatories or have actually engaged in the substantive acts to be liable under the alter ego doctrine. Plaintiffs contends it does not need to plead specific factual details demonstrating commingling or violation of corporate formalities, and such information would be in the possession of the adverse party. Plaintiff contends it does not need to allege “unified operations” either, and that the FAC alleges facts of inequitable result that Plaintiff would suffer if it was not permitted to sue Moving Parties as alter ego defendants. 

Plaintiff further contends Moving Parties’ arguments regarding a heightened pleading standard for a breach of contract claims is erroneous and that the Court already rejected this argument previously. Plaintiff also contends Moving Parties attempt to improperly argue the case at the pleading stage. 

The Court finds the FAC alleges sufficient facts to state a cause of action for breach of contract against Moving Parties. The FAC alleges that Plaintiff and Defendants entered into a written agreement, and that Defendants were obligated to perform certain tasks thereunder, such as prorating expenses, providing original accounting records, and transferring tenant files and property plans. (FAC, ¶¶ 8-9.) The FAC further alleges Defendants failed to perform their respective obligations, and that Defendants’ representations were false, resulting in damages to Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶¶ 10-14.) The Court assumes these allegations to be true at the pleading stage. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) 

Moreover, as the Court previously stated, a breach of contract claim is not subject to a heightened pleading requirement. (Order re Tentative Ruling (7/11/24); Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10, subd. (a)(1) [complaint need only allege facts constituting the cause of action in ordinary and concise language]; compare Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 [heightened pleading requirements for fraud claims].) 

Furthermore, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that Moving Parties improperly attempt to argue the facts of the case and introduce facts outside the four corners of the FAC. (Demurrer, 5:9-14, 5:24-28, 6:1-24; Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson, supra, 110 Cal.App.3d at p. 881 [error to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer].)

The Court also finds the FAC alleges sufficient facts demonstrating alter ego liability against the Moving Parties for purposes of this demurrer. To state a claim for alter ego liability, the plaintiff need only allege sufficient facts showing a unity of interest and ownership, and an unjust result if only the entity is held liable. (Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.) Allegations demonstrating unity of interest and ownership include that Shao is the sole owner, manager, and member of LTG, that LTG’s sole asset is the commercial property at issue in this action, that Shao took the proceeds from LTG after it was dissolved on January 12, 2024, that LTG and LT Management Group have the same office as the property at issue, and that Shao is a principal and owner of LT Management Group. (FAC, ¶ 6; Zoran Corp. v. Chen (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 799, 811-812 (Zoran) [test for alter ego liability includes identical equitable ownership in two entities, same office address, sole ownership of all stock by one individual, and inadequate capitalization, among other things].) 

Allegations demonstrating an unjust result if only LTG is held liable include Defendants concealing from Plaintiff outstanding unpaid rents and common area maintenance expenses chargeable to the tenants of the subject property, and Moving Parties continuing to occupy the subject property as tenants with Shao owning one of the other tenants. (FAC, ¶ 12.) Coupled with the allegation that LTG has since been dissolved, the Court finds these allegations sufficiently demonstrate the wrongful conduct necessary to support alter ego liability. (FAC, ¶ 6, subd. (c); Code Civ. Proc., § 452; Mid-Century Ins. Co. v. Gardner (1982) 9 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1213, [alter ego liability protects against bad faith inequitable conduct, not just a creditor’s inability to recover].) Whether all of these allegations are sufficient to establish alter ego liability against Moving Parties is generally a question of fact not resolvable on a demurrer. (Zoran, supra, 185 Cal.App.4th at p. 811 [alter ego liability is generally a question of fact]; M.F. v. Pac. Pearl Hotel Mgmt. LLC (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 693, 703 [questions of fact generally not suitable for demurrer].) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the First Cause of Action. 

Second Cause of Action – Specific Performance 

A plaintiff seeking specific performance of a contract must demonstrate the inadequacy of the legal remedy, a contract that is both reasonable and supported by adequate consideration, the existence of mutual remedies, sufficiently definite contractual terms, and substantial similarity of the requested performance to that promised in the contract. (Tamarind Lithography Workshop, Inc. v. Sanders (1983) 143 Cal.App.3d 571, 575.) 

Moving Parties demur to the Second Cause of Action on the grounds that the FAC fails to plead facts sufficient to state a cause of action. Moving Parties contend this cause of action should be dismissed for failure to allege the inadequacy of monetary damages. Moving Parties contend the FAC does not explain why monetary damages would not adequately compensate the Plaintiff. Moving Parties also reiterate their contention that they are not signatories to the subject purchase agreement. Moving Parties further contend that this cause of action fails because the obligations Plaintiff seeks to enforce are not sufficiently clear or certain with respect to prorating expenses, providing original accounting records, and transferring tenant files and property plans. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends this cause of action is properly pled, as Plaintiff has demonstrated the inadequacy of a legal remedy due to the need for original accounting records, tenant files, and property plans for Plaintiff’s tax, accounting, and reconciliation purposes. Plaintiff reiterates the argument that the FAC alleges sufficient facts demonstrating alter ego liability. 

The Court finds the FAC alleges sufficient facts to state a claim for specific performance. As discussed above, Plaintiff adequately pled a cause of action for breach of contract. Plaintiff also adequately pled a claim for specific performance. (FAC, ¶¶ 7-17; see Collins v. Marvel Land Co. (1970) 13 Cal.App.3d 34, 40-41 [trial court erred in sustaining a demurrer to cause of action for specific performance].) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the Second Cause of Action. 

Fraud 

Moving Parties argue that the FAC fails to adequately plead a fraud claim, but the Court declines to consider this argument because the FAC does not allege a separate cause of action for fraud, but instead alleges only two causes of action for breach of contract and specific performance. To the extent there are any fraud allegations in the FAC, they are alleged in the context of Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim, not as a standalone cause of action. (FAC, ¶ 12.) Accordingly, the Court rejects Moving Parties’ arguments here.

 

LEGAL STANDARD – Motion to Strike 

            “Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(2).) “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id., § 436.) 

PRELIMINARY ISSUE – Motion to Strike 

            “A notice of motion to strike a portion of a pleading must quote in full the portions sought to be stricken except where the motion is to strike an entire paragraph, cause of action, count, or defense. Specifications in a notice must be numbered consecutively.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (a).) Plaintiff’s notice of motion does not identify the portions of the FAC that it seeks to strike. The Court will still consider the motion, but admonishes Plaintiff to comply with the requirements of the California Rules of Court going forward. 

            Plaintiff notes in the opposition that Moving Parties’ moving papers cite cases that do not exist. (Opp., p. 2, fn. 1.) The Court checked the purported citations of Bernard v. Mayer (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 593 and Kirkpatrick v. City of Los Angeles (1956) 144 Cal.App.2d 470, and confirmed that those citations are not correct and the case names do not exist; nor do the cases with those citations stand for the propositions for which they were cited, as they mention nothing about motions to strike. Moving Parties indicate in their reply that they were inadvertent misquotes, but this does not adequately explain the mistakes. (Reply, p. 2, fn. 1.) The above-cited cases simply do not exist and it is unclear to the Court how Moving Parties could cite non-existent cases. The Court will hear further from counsel for Moving Parties on this issue at the hearing. 

DISCUSSION – Motion to Strike 

Meet and Confer 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a), Moving Parties were required to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference before bringing this motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) The Court finds Moving Parties’ efforts to meet and confer satisfactory. (Huang Decl., ¶ 2.) 

Paragraph 6 of the FAC 

Moving Parties seek to strike paragraph 6 of the FAC in its entirety on the grounds that the FAC fails to provide concrete facts showing a unity of interest and ownership exists or that an injustice would result from treating the entities separately. Plaintiff’s opposition does not directly address this argument, but does generally contend that Moving Parties’ arguments here are similar to those made in their demurrer regarding lack of factual specificity, and incorporates by reference the arguments made in Plaintiff’s opposition to the demurrer. 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff that Moving Parties’ arguments here are the same as those made in their demurrer. Thus, for the same reasons set forth in the Court’s ruling on the demurrer, the Court finds the FAC alleges sufficient facts to state a claim for alter ego liability. (FAC, ¶¶ 6, 12; Zoran Corp. v. Chen (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 799, 811-812 (Zoran) [test for alter ego liability includes identical equitable ownership in two entities, same office address, sole ownership of all stock by one individual, and inadequate capitalization, among other things]; Id. at p. 811 [alter ego liability is generally a question of fact]; see M.F. v. Pac. Pearl Hotel Mgmt. LLC (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 693, 703 (M.F.) [questions of fact generally not suitable for demurrer].) Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as to paragraph 6 of the FAC. 

Paragraph 6a of the FAC 

Moving Parties seek to strike paragraph 6 of the FAC in its entirety on the grounds that the FAC erroneously claims Shao is the sole owner, manager, and member of LTG, and that LT Global Investment is the sole member of LTG. In opposition, Plaintiff contends the allegations in paragraph 6a regarding Shao’s interest in LTG are properly pled as they set for factors for invoking the alter ego doctrine, and that Plaintiff is entitled to discovery regarding the facts and evidence surrounding these allegations. 

The Court finds Moving Parties’ arguments here unavailing. On a motion to strike, the Court assumes the allegations to be true. (Clauson v. Superior Ct. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) The Court therefore assumes the allegations in paragraph 6a to be true for purposes of this motion to strike. Moreover, as noted above, Moving Parties are improperly attempting to introduce facts from outside the four corners of the FAC. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 (Ion Equip.) [error to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer].) The Court declines to consider the purported facts raised in Moving Parties’ motion to strike, and therefore DENIES the motion to strike as to paragraph 6a of the FAC. 

Paragraph 6d of the FAC 

Moving Parties seek to strike paragraph 6d of the FAC in its entirety on the grounds that it is conclusory and lacks the necessary factual specificity. Moving Parties contend the FAC does not detail how the alleged commingling occurred or how it led to a lack of separation between Shao and LTG. Moving Parties contend the allegations that the entities shared the same office address do not establish the level of control or financial intermingling necessary to prove an alter ego claim. In opposition, Plaintiff contends the allegations in paragraph 6d are properly pleaded as they set forth allegations regarding the factors for invoking the alter ego doctrine, and that Plaintiff is entitled to discovery regarding the facts and evidence surrounding these allegations. 

As noted above, the Court finds the FAC alleges sufficient facts to state a claim for alter ego liability. (FAC, ¶¶ 6, 12; see Zoran, supra, 185 Cal.App.4th at pp. 811-812; M.F., supra, 16 Cal.App.5th at p. 703.) Therefore, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as to paragraph 6d of the FAC. 

Paragraph 6e of the FAC 

Moving Parties seek to strike paragraph 6e of the FAC in its entirety on the grounds that it incorrectly alleges Shao signed the lease for Okawa Wagyu when LT Management Group signed it. Moving Parties also contend that the FAC inaccurately describes the management structure and relationship between LT Management and LTG, as LT Management was engaged under a management contract and trust account to manage LTG’s property, not as a member or owner. In opposition, Plaintiff contends there is no such allegation in paragraph 6e that Shao signed a lease for Okawa Wagyu. Plaintiff also contends this is a factual allegation assumed to be true on a motion to strike. 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff. Paragraph 6e does not allege Shao signed a lease for Okawa Wagyu. Also, the Court assumes the allegations about management structure to be true on a motion to strike. (Clauson v. Superior Ct., supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at p. 1255.) This again is an improper attempt to introduce facts from outside the four corners of the FAC. (See Ion Equip., supra, supra, 110 Cal.App.3d at p. 881.) The Court declines to consider the facts raised in Moving Parties’ motion to strike, and therefore DENIES the motion to strike as to paragraph 6e of the FAC. 

Paragraph 12 of the FAC 

            Moving Parties seek to strike paragraph 12 of the FAC in its entirety on the grounds that the FAC alleges fraudulent misrepresentations regarding financial records and expenses without specifying how these misrepresentations occurred or their impact on Plaintiff. Moving Parties also contend the fraud allegations are based on the same facts as the breach of contract claim and are thus barred by the economic loss rule, which precludes tort recovery for purely economic losses arising from a breach of contract. In opposition, Plaintiff contends there is no heightened pleading standard for a breach of contract claim, and that the argument regarding the economic loss rule is irrelevant and should be disregarded for the same reasons. 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff. Paragraph 12 is part of Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim, and therefore does not trigger the heightened pleading requirements of a fraud claim. (FAC, ¶ 12; see Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10, subd. (a)(1) [complaint need only allege facts constituting the cause of action in ordinary and concise language]; compare Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 [heightened pleading requirements for fraud claims].) Since the FAC is alleging a contract based claim and not a tort based claim, the economic loss rule does not apply here. (See Rattagan v. Uber Techs., Inc. (2024) 17 Cal.5th 1, 553 P.3d 1213, 1225.) Accordingly, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as to Paragraph 12 of the FAC. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court OVERRULES Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. 

The Court DENIES Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang’s motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. 

Defendants LT Management Group and Shao Xing Max Yang must file an answer to the First Amended Complaint within 10 days of the Court’s order. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.
