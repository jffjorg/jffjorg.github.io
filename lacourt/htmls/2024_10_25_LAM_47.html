DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22STCV18323    Hearing Date: October 25, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 25, 2024                   TRIAL DATE: June 3, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Jeff Shin v. James Lai, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV18323           

 

(1)   MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE

(2)   MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL FOR JEFF SHIN

(3)   MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL FOR JC BUSINESS CENTER, INC.

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1) Plaintiff Jeff Shin; (2)-(3) Attorney Brad H. Lee, counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Jeff Shin and Cross-Defendant JC Business Center, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1) Defendants James Lai, Kevin Lai, & Broadway Hill Investment Group, LLC; (2)-(3) No response on eCourt as of 10/23/24,

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         06/06/22: Complaint filed.

·         06/08/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         07/08/22: Cross-Complaint filed.

·         10/23/23: Second Amended Complaint filed.

·         09/11/24: Complaint filed in JC Business Center, Inc. v. Broadway Hill Investment Group, LLC, et al.; Case No. 24STCV23433.

·         09/17/24: Cases deemed related. This case (22STCV18323) designated as lead case.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for fraud and breach of contract arising out of a commercial lease dispute. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants breached the lease agreement by not allowing Plaintiff unfettered access to the rental property.

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Jeff Shin moves to consolidate this action with the related case JC Business Center, Inc. v. Broadway Hill Investment Group, et al. Case No. 24STCV23433. Attorney Brad H. Lee, counsel for Plaintiff Shin and Cross-Defendant JC Business Center Inc., also moves to be relieved as counsel.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Consolidate is GRANTED. The action JC Business Center, Inc. v. Broadway Hill Investment Group, et al. Case No. 24STCV23433 is consolidated with this case for all purposes. This case is designated as the lead case. Moving party to give notice.

 

            Attorney Brad H. Lee’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Jeff Shin is GRANTED. This ruling is STAYED until the Motion for Summary Judgment set for November 7, 2024 at 9:00 AM is resolved. This ruling is also conditioned on Moving Counsel giving notice to all parties and filing proof of service with the Court.

 

            Attorney Brad H. Lee’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for JC Business Center, Inc. is GRANTED. This ruling is STAYED until the Motion for Summary Judgment set for November 7, 2024 at 9:00 AM is resolved. This ruling is also conditioned on Moving Counsel giving notice to all parties and filing proof of service with the Court.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion to Consolidate

 

            Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Jeff Shin moves to consolidate this action with the related case JC Business Center, Inc. v. Broadway Hill Investment Group, et al. Case No. 24STCV23433.

 

Legal Standard for Consolidation

 

When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1048(a), bold emphasis added.) 

 

Procedural Requirements 

 

A motion to consolidate must satisfy the requirements of California Rules of Court Rule 3.350, which provides, in relevant part: 

 

(a) Requirements of motion 

 

(1) A notice of motion to consolidate must: 

 

(A) List all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record; 

 

(B) Contain the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest numbered case shown first; and 

 

(C) Be filed in each case sought to be consolidated. 

 

(2) The motion to consolidate: 

 

(A) Is deemed a single motion for the purpose of determining the appropriate filing fee, but memorandums, declarations, and other supporting papers must be filed only in the lowest numbered case; 

 

(B) Must be served on all attorneys of record and all nonrepresented parties in all of the cases sought to be consolidated; and 

 

(C) Must have a proof of service filed as part of the motion. 

 

(Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.350(a).) 

 

The moving party has not listed the parties who have appeared in each case in the notice of motion, as required by Rule 3.350(a)(1)(A). That said, Plaintiff sets forth each involved party in the body of the motion, though he does not state whether each party has appeared. Plaintiff merely recites the abbreviated case names with docket numbers for each of the cases at issue. (See Notice of Motion p.2.) The moving party also has not listed the names of the respective attorneys of record, as required by Rule 3.350(a)(1)(A).

 

The moving party has not included the captions of both cases, as required by Rule 3.350(a)(1)(B). Further, the notice of motion was not filed in the related action, as required by Rule 3.350(a)(1)(C).  

 

It appears that the moving party served the attorneys of record for each party, as required by Rule 3.350(a)(2)(B). As noted, however, the moving party did not comply with the other procedural requirements. Nevertheless, since the opposing parties did not raise objections to these procedural deficiencies and both actions concern the same parties, the Court will consider the motion on its merits.  

 

Consolidation of Actions for All Purposes

 

            Plaintiff seeks to consolidate the later-filed action JC Business Center, Inc. v. Broadway Hill Investment Group, et al. Case No. 24STCV23433 with this case for all purposes, on the basis that both cases concern the same dispute over the same commercial lease agreement for the same property. Comparison of the Second Amended Complaint in this action and the Complaint in the related action bear this out, as both pleadings allege the same substantive breach of contract—i.e., that Defendants failed to provide unfettered access to the rental property. (SAC ¶ 10-15; Complaint filed 09/11/24 ¶ 10-15.) The principal difference between the two pleadings is that this action is brought by Plaintiff Jeff Shin in his individual capacity, and the related action was brought by JC Business Center Inc., for whom Jeff Shin is alleged to be the principal. (Id.)

 

            Defendants argue that consolidation is improper because Plaintiff Shin lacks standing to bring his claims against Defendants because he is not the proper party to the lease agreement. This contention is entirely irrelevant. Whether Plaintiff’s claims in this action have merit do not bear on whether, as alleged, they arise out of the same transaction and involve common questions of law or fact. The pleadings, on their face, evidence that the two cases concern substantively identical disputes, and Defendants’ bare conclusions to the contrary are not persuasive. The Court therefore finds that there are common questions of law and fact to both actions and that they should be consolidated for all purposes.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Consolidate is GRANTED. The action JC Business Center, Inc. v. Broadway Hill Investment Group, et al. Case No. 24STCV23433 is consolidated with this case for all purposes. This case is designated as the lead case.

 

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel for Jeff Shin

 

            Attorney Brad H. Lee moves to be relieved as counsel for Jeff Shin, Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant in Case No. 22STCV18323.

 

Moving counsel filed all three required forms (MC-051, -052, and -053) and filed proofs of service with the Court. Moving counsel served the motion on Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Shin by mail at his last known address, and confirmed the address was current by conversation. (MC-052 ¶ 3(b).)

 

In general, an attorney may withdraw with or without cause so long as the withdrawal would not result in undue prejudice to the client’s interest – i.e., counsel cannot withdraw at a critical point in the litigation, because that would prejudice client, but can withdraw otherwise. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915.) The court has discretion to deny an attorney’s request to withdraw where the withdrawal would work an injustice or cause undue delay in the proceeding, but the court’s discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.)

Here, trial in this matter is set for June 3, 2025, and the next hearing on the matter is a Motion for Summary Judgment scheduled for November 7, 2024, at 9:00 AM. This ruling is STAYED until the Motion for Summary Judgment set for November 7, 2024, at 9:00 AM is resolved. Case Number: 23STCV30527    Hearing Date: October 25, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 25, 2024                               TRIAL DATE: July 22, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         John & Sheryl Associates d/b/a Discovery World Early Education Center v. CYP LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV30527           

 

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant CYP LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff John & Sheryl Associates d/b/a Discovery World Early Education Center.

 

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract that was filed on December 14, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant, as the landlord of property leased by Plaintiff, refused to approve Plaintiff’s request to install new playground equipment without adequate justification.

 

Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED with respect to the first cause of action without leave to amend and otherwise DENIED. 

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings.

 

Legal Standard

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings is the functional equivalent of a general demurrer.  (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198).  Like demurrers, motions for judgment on the pleadings challenge the legal sufficiency of the allegations, not their veracity.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994). Any defects must either appear on the face of the pleading, or else be taken by judicial notice.  (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-22).  The parties’ ability to prove their respective claims is of no concern.  (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 99.)  Though the Court must accept the allegations of the complaint and answer as true (Gerawan Farming, Inc. v. Lyons (2000) 24 Cal.4th 468, 515), it will not do so for “conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or [judicially noticed] facts…” (Stevenson Real Estate Servs., Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Servs., Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-20).

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to grant or deny a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of David Ching in support of the Motion states that the parties met and conferred electronically on April 8, 2024 and by video conferencing on April 10, 2024. (Declaration of David Ching ISO Mot. ¶ 2; Exh. 1.) The declaration does not state the outcome of those discussions, but the Court infers from Plaintiff’s full-throated opposition to the motion that these efforts were not successful. The Court therefore finds that Defendant has therefore satisfied its statutory meet-and-confer obligation.

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

            Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must plead the contract, the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, Defendant’s breach, and finally the resulting damage. (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458.) Further, the complaint must indicate whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(g).) General allegations stating that defendants violated a contract are insufficient, and plaintiffs must state facts showing a breach. (Levy v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1, 5-6.) For breach of a written contract, the essential terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written instrument must be attached and incorporated by reference. (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co., supra, 166 Cal.App.3d at 459.)

 

Defendant contends that the first cause of action is fatally deficient because the contractual provision on which it rests does not impose a duty on Defendant. As alleged in the Complaint, Plaintiff John & Sheryl Associates, doing business as Discovery World Early Education Center, has been a tenant at the subject property at 550 Deep Valley Drive, Rolling Hills Estates, CA 90274 since 2006. (Complaint ¶ 6.) Although ownership of the property has changed hands multiple times, the tenancy remains governed by the original commercial lease agreement dated May 24, 2006. (¶¶ 6, 10.) Defendant has been the current owner of the property since October 2022 and has assumed all rights and obligations as the Landlord under the 2006 Lease Agreement. (Complaint ¶¶ 6-7.) As relevant here, section 7.1 of the Lease Agreement states:

 

Tenant shall, at its sole cost and expense, install first class new trade fixtures and equipment required to operate its business. All trade fixtures, signs or other personal property installed in the Premises by Tenant shall remain its property and may be removed at any time, provided that Tenant is not in default and that the removal thereof does not cause, contribute to or result in Tenant's default hereunder. The term “trade fixtures” excludes carpeting, floor coverings, lighting fixtures, wall coverings or similar Tenant improvements, all of which shall become the property of Landlord upon surrender of the Premises. Tenant shall perform no structural work, no exterior work and no other work costing more than Two Thousand Five Hundred and No/l00ths Dollars ($2,500.00) in the aggregate per Year, without the prior written approval of Landlord. Any work permitted shall be at Tenant's sole cost and expense, shall be done in a good and workmanlike manner in compliance with all governmental requirements and the Handbook and shall be done without any liens attaching to the Premises or the Project. Upon the conclusion of any work by Tenant, whether or not Landlord's consent thereto is required, Tenant will promptly provide Landlord with a copy of a then current set of “as-built” plans with respect to the Premises.

 

(Complaint ¶ 10 [Emphasis added].) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant breached this term by denying Plaintiff an approval to install new outdoor playground equipment “[w]ithout any valid reason or business justification.” (Complaint ¶¶ 26-27.) Defendant argues that this contention is inadequately pled because section 7.1 does not impose a requirement for a “valid reason” or “business justification” to withhold approval.

 

“When a dispute arises over the meaning of contract language, the first question to be decided is whether the language is ‘reasonably susceptible’ to the interpretation urged by the party. If it is not, the case is over.” (S. Cal. Edison Co. v. Super. Ct., (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 839, 847-48.) “If the court decides the language is reasonably susceptible to the interpretation urged, the court moves to the second question: what did the parties intend the language to mean? ... Thus, where contract language is clear and explicit and does not lead to absurd results, we ascertain intent from the written terms and go no further. If the contract is capable of more than one reasonable interpretation, it is ambiguous.”  (Department of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Lawrence Livermore National Security, LLC (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1060, 1066 [internal quotation marks, ellipses, and citations omitted].) Interpretation of an ambiguous contract requires the consideration of extrinsic evidence and is therefore not well-suited to resolution via demurrer. (See Brown v. Goldstein (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 418, 432-33 [describing evidentiary considerations necessary for determination of the legal question of the meaning of a contract via motion for summary judgment].)

 

Defendant contends that the express language of section 7.1 supports only one reasonable interpretation: that Defendant has discretion whether to approve installation of any fixtures or improvements, and has no obligations imposed by that section which could be breached. As Defendant states, a claim for breach of contract is subject to demurrer when the sole reasonable interpretation of the contract tends to contradict the plaintiff’s claims. (See Baldwin v. AAA N. Cal., Nev. & Uta Ins. Exch. (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 545, 553 [finding on appeal from demurrer that an insurance contract was not ambiguous and its sole reasonable interpretation defeated plaintiff’s claim for breach of contract].)

 

Plaintiff, in opposition, contends that Baldwin is factually distinguishable from this case because the contractual term at issue specified alternative actions which the defendant insurance company could take. Plaintiff’s argument on this point is misplaced, as Defendant offers the citation to Baldwin for general rules of contract interpretation on demurrer, not for its factual application. Plaintiff also asserts that discovery is necessary to examine Defendant’s reasons for denial of approval—an assertion which is entirely unsupported by legal authority justifying the request. Moreover, Plaintiff fails to address the central question on this motion: whether the contract is subject to a reasonable interpretation which imposes an obligation on Defendant with which Defendant has not complied. On this central issue, Plaintiff offers no substantive argument beyond the extraordinary claim that the Court should permit extrinsic evidence to aid in the interpretation of an agreement even if the agreement does not appear ambiguous. Although Plaintiff cites Walter E. Heller Western Inc. v. Techrim Corp. (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 149 at page 158 in support of that contention, a cursory review of that opinion reveals that it states the exact opposite. This is unsurprising, as the standards for contract interpretation on dispositive motions are long-established by our Supreme Court. (See Walsh v. Walsh (1941) 18 Cal.2d 439, 443.) Plaintiff cannot escape its obligation to present an alternative reasonable interpretation of section 7.1 which supports its contentions by claiming that this section could be ambiguous. Rather, Plaintiff must demonstrate, through citation to some part of the contract, that section 7.1 is ambiguous. Plaintiff has not done so.

 

            The sole term of the Lease Agreement at issue on the first cause of action plainly states, in its entirety, that “Tenant shall perform no structural work, no exterior work and no other work costing more than Two Thousand Five Hundred and No/l00ths Dollars ($2,500.00) in the aggregate per Year, without the prior written approval of Landlord.” This language, on its face, imposes no express obligations on Defendant with respect to that approval. As Plaintiff has not demonstrated an alternative reasonable interpretation, the Court finds that the sole reasonable interpretation precludes a claim for breach of contract premised on section 7.1 of the Lease Agreement because this provision creates no obligations which could be breached by Defendant. Defendant is therefore entitled to judgment on the pleadings with respect to the first cause of action.

 

Second Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith & Fair Dealing

 

            Defendant also moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the second cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

“There is an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in every contract that neither party will do anything which will injure the right of the other to receive the benefits of the agreement.” (Comunale v. Traders & General Ins. Co. (1958) 50 Cal.2d 654, 658 [internal citations omitted].) “The covenant of good faith and fair dealing, implied by law in every contract, exists merely to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made. The covenant thus cannot ‘“‘be endowed with an existence independent of its contractual underpinnings.’”’ It cannot impose substantive duties or limits on the contracting parties beyond those incorporated in the specific terms of their agreement.” (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349-350 [internal citations omitted, emphasis in original].)

 

            The second cause of action is premised on the same facts as the first cause of action but alleges that Defendant’s refusal to approve the installation of outdoor playground equipment was done in bad faith as an attempt to gain improper leverage in a separate proceeding and force Plaintiff to vacate the property. (Complaint ¶ 32.) Defendant argues that this cause of action is deficient because Plaintiff is purportedly attempting to force Defendant to “choose one option over the other” (Motion p.5:14) because Defendant has discretion whether to approve the installation or not. In support of this position, Defendant relies on Bevis v. Terrace View Partners, LP, a 2019 Court of Appeal decision reviewing a judgment and fee award following a jury verdict on a contract dispute between residents of a mobile home park and the park’s owners. (Bevis v. Terrace View Partners, L.P. (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 230, 233.) With respect to a claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, the Court of Appeal concluded that the claim could not be premised on the owners offering buyers of mobile homes owned by the park to rent space in the park at lower rates, while requiring buyers of resident-owned homes to assume the seller’s existing lease which had higher rent, because both actions were permitted by express terms of the lease agreements. (Id. at 255-56.) The Court stated that “[t]he implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be read to require defendants to take a particular action that is discretionary under the contract when the contract also expressly grants them the discretion to take a different action.” (Id. at 256.) Defendant argues that, as in Bevis, Plaintiff is attempting to apply the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing to “obliterate a right expressly given under a written contract.” (Id. [internal citations omitted].)

 

            The Court disagrees with Defendant with respect to this claim. The Court of Appeal’s ruling in Bevis was premised, in relevant part, on the existence of a separate express term of the contract upon which the application of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing would have interfered. (Bevis, supra, 33 Cal.App.5th at 256.) Here, however, Defendant has not identified any similar term. Moreover, unlike the relevant provision in Bevis, section 7.1 does not clearly establish that Defendant has absolute discretion to withhold approval by stating, for example, that approval may be given or withheld “at [Defendant]’s sole option” or by otherwise reserving rights. (See Bevis, supra, 33 Cal.App.5th at 237 fn. 6.) The Court therefore does not conclude that Plaintiff’s claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is deficient in this respect. Defendant is therefore not entitled to judgment on the pleadings as to the second cause of action.

 

Leave to Amend

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate how they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Plaintiff has not demonstrated how the complaint might be amended to cure the deficiency with respect to the first cause of action. Moreover, as the Court has found that section 7.1 of the Lease Agreement unambiguously does not impose any express obligations on Defendant, the Court finds that it is not possible to amend the first cause of action to state a claim for breach of contract premised on that contractual provision. The Court therefore does not find that leave to amend is warranted in this instance with respect to a claim for breach of contract premised on section 7.1 of the Lease Agreement. This ruling is without prejudice to any other potential claims which Plaintiff’s might assert, including claims for breach of contract premised on any other relevant provision of the Lease Agreement.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED with respect to the first cause of action without leave to amend and otherwise DENIED.
