DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV00428    Hearing Date: June 10, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Maria Angela Rodriguez v. Mercado Latino, Inc., et al. 

Defendants Mercado Latino, Inc., Grosac, Inc., Richard Rodriguez, and Roberto Rodriguez’s Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint 

TENTATIVE RULING           

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint with 20 days’ leave to amend as to Richard, Roberto, Jorge, and Grosac. This is the last opportunity the Court will give Plaintiff to attempt to plead this cause of action. 

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint without leave to amend as to Mercado Latino.

Moving Parties are ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days. 

BACKGROUND 

This is an involuntary dissolution action. On May 21, 2021, Plaintiff Maria Angela Rodriguez (Plaintiff) filed this action against Defendants Mercado Latino, Inc. (Mercado Latino), Grosac, Inc. (Grosac), Richard Yarruths Rodriguez (Richard), Roberto Rodriguez (Roberto), Jorge Rodriguez (Jorge) (collectively, Defendants), and Does 1 through 50. On March 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (SAC), alleging causes of action for involuntary dissolution of Mercado Latino and Grosac, breach of fiduciary duty, financial elder abuse, and accounting. 

On April 24, 2024, Defendants Mercado Latino, Grosac, Richard, and Roberto (collectively, Moving Parties) demurred to the Fourth Cause of Action in the SAC. On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the demurrer. On May 31, 2024, Moving Parties replied. On the same date, Jorge filed a joinder to the demurrer. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (Code Civ. Proc., § 422.10; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Id.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862, disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287 [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery. (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-972.) The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint, or in a superseded complaint in the same action. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”]) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.)            

DISCUSSION           

            Meet and Confer 

            Per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), Moving Parties were required to meet and confer telephonically or in person before bringing this demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The Court finds Moving Parties’ meet-and-confer efforts to be insufficient, as the declaration of Michelle Leight fails to indicate whether the parties met and conferred telephonically or in person. (Leight Decl., ¶ 2, subds. (a), (b).) The declaration also is a Judicial Council form that requires the declarant to select either subdivision (a) or (b), not both as Moving Parties’ counsel did here. (Id.) The Court further notes that it admonished Ms. Leight regarding this very issue for Moving Parties’ prior demurrer. (Order re Tentative Ruling (2/27/24).) Nevertheless, the Court may not overrule a demurrer for failure to adequately meet and confer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).) The Court will still consider the demurrer, but admonishes Ms. Leight to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure going forward. 

Financial Elder Abuse - Grosac Stock Certificates

“Financial abuse” of an elder or dependent adult occurs when a person or entity does any of the following: 

(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. 

(2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. 

(3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70. 

(Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30, subd. (a).) 

Elder abuse claims are statutory causes of action and therefore must be pleaded with particularity. (See Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790, citing Lopez v. Southern Cal. Rapid Trans. Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795). 

Moving Parties demur to the Fourth Cause of Action in the FAC on the grounds that it fails to state a cause of action for financial elder abuse. More specifically, they contend the FAC fails to allege that Grosac stock certificates were ever issued. Moving Parties contend that Plaintiff’s allegations regarding Defendants’ alleged refusal to return Plaintiff’s physical Grosac stock certificates is conclusory and that the SAC does not allege Plaintiff suffered any harm as a result of the alleged misappropriation or retention of the Grosac stock certificates, such as dividends issued to Defendants but not to Plaintiff. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff disputes Moving Parties’ contentions regarding the Grosac stock certificates, contending that the SAC alleges Grosac was authorized to issue stock, that Plaintiff acquired Grosac stock by gift and by an investment of $50,000, that Plaintiff and Grosac agreed to Plaintiff acquiring a 25% ownership interest in Grosac, that Plaintiff did not receive any Grosac stock certificates, that Plaintiff asked Defendants for her Grosac stock certificates long ago, and that Defendants wrongfully withheld and retained Plaintiff’s stock certificates. Plaintiff contends the SAC need not expressly allege the existence of the stock certificates, but that their existence is implicit from the SAC’s allegations. Plaintiff cites to other allegations from the SAC to show it sufficiently alleged a claim for financial elder abuse. 

            Plaintiff further contends the SAC alleges facts demonstrating Plaintiff’s alleged harm with respect to the stock certificates, as they are legal proof of stock ownership, which Plaintiffs needs to do anything with her stock, and obtains no benefit without them. Plaintiff contends Defendants converted both the stock itself and the stock certificates. Plaintiff contends it is undisputed that Grosac stock has value and that the stock certificates therefore also have an associated value, so Plaintiff is being harmed by the value of those stock certificates. Plaintiff then contends the SAC alleges Plaintiff could have otherwise sold her Grosac stock. Plaintiff also contends the SAC alleges she was harmed in other ways, such as Defendants siphoning off to themselves and their family members the dividends, profits, and distributions of excessive compensation from Grosac and Mercado. 

The Court finds the SAC fails to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action for financial elder abuse. First, the SAC lacks the specificity required for a financial elder abuse claim. (See Covenant Care, Inc., supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 790.) The SAC does not allege enough facts regarding the stock certificates, such as when they were issued, their corresponding certificate numbers, and when or how Plaintiff made the demand for those stock certificates. (See SAC, ¶¶ 25, 59.) Additionally, Plaintiff’s arguments regarding Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153 (Tarmann) and Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150 (Daniels) are inapposite because those cases involved misrepresentation claims, not financial elder abuse claims. (See Tarmann, supra, 2 Cal.App.4th at p. 158; Daniels, supra, 246 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1166-1167.) 

Second, the SAC otherwise alleges in a conclusory fashion that Defendants wrongfully took Plaintiff’s stock. (See SAC, ¶¶ 25, 59; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591 [a demurrer admits the truth of properly pleaded factual allegations, not conclusions of law].) The SAC fails to allege any specific facts regarding when and how Plaintiff’s stock was taken and by whom. The SAC also fails to allege any specific facts regarding Richard, Roberto, or Jorge’s siphoning of dividends, profits, and distributions, such as the amounts at issue, when any dividends were declared or distributions were made, when they were siphoned off, or how they were siphoned off. (See SAC, ¶¶ 33, 45.) Further, “the loss of corporate profits, revenues, or assets as a result of negligent or intentional wrongdoing by a corporation's officers, directors, or majority shareholders results in corporate injury, for which only the corporation may sue to recover.” (Sole Energy Co. v. Petrominerals Corp. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 212, 230.) 

Third, the Court also finds that the SAC fails to allege facts demonstrating Plaintiff suffered harm due to the alleged withholding of Plaintiff’s stock certificates. Stock certificates are merely evidence of ownership, and the rights to the stock may exist without the certificates. (See Lowe v. Copeland (1932) 125 Cal.App. 315, 325 (Lowe) [“a certificate is only evidence of ownership, which may exist without it…”]) Even assuming for the sake of argument that Defendants did take Plaintiff’s stock certificates or otherwise fail to produce them after being asked for them, it is unclear how this constitutes harm for purposes of financial elder abuse since Plaintiff’s rights would otherwise persist even if the stock certificates were missing or destroyed. (See Id.; Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.30, subd. (b); but see Lowe, supra, 125 Cal.App. at p. 325 [“a demand is necessary to put a corporation in default for failure to deliver certificates of stock where the right thereto has not been denied [citations]…”]) It is equally unclear how these stock certificates or lack thereof would prevent Plaintiff from selling her shares in Grosac. (See Id.) 

Fourth, Plaintiff does not necessarily have a personal property right in the profits, dividends, or distributions of the corporate Defendants here. “Shareholders do not own and have no right to receive corporate profits, except in limited circumstances: ‘It is fundamental, of course, that the corporation has a personality distinct from that of its shareholders, and that the latter neither own the corporate property nor the corporate earnings.’ [Citation.] Shareholders own stock in the corporation, from which they derive income only upon liquidation of the corporation or the declaration of a dividend by the corporate directors. [Citation.]” (Sole Energy Co., supra, 128 Cal.App.4th at p. 229.) As noted above, the SAC does not allege any specific distributions or dividends having been made or declared, nor does it allege the corporate Defendants were sold. (See SAC, ¶¶ 33, 45.) Thus, even assuming these allegations to be true, they do not state a cause of action for financial elder abuse. 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action with leave to amend. 

            Financial Elder Abuse – Mercado Latino 

Moving Parties also demur to the Fourth Cause of Action in the SAC as to Mercado Latino on the grounds that it fails to state a cause of action for financial elder abuse. In particular, Moving Parties contend the SAC contains no allegations as to how Mercado Latino allegedly took any of Plaintiff’s Grosac stock certificates, and that the allegations against Mercado Latino are conclusory. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends the SAC alleges Defendants are controlling officers and directors of Mercado Latino and that they are the ones who wrongfully retained Plaintiff’s property on behalf of Mercado Latino. Plaintiff contends that the SAC needs only to allege that Mercado Latino assisted Defendants in their alleged wrongdoing. Plaintiff contends that exacting particularity of alleged facts is not required when the Defendants already have the relevant information since Plaintiff alleges she has not been involved with Mercado Latino since 2018. Plaintiff further disputes the legal authorities Moving Parties cited in their demurrer, contending that they do not rebut the rule regarding the relaxation of pleading requirements when the defendants already have the knowledge. 

The Court finds the SAC fails to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action against Mercado Latino for financial elder abuse. As noted above, the Court finds the SAC fails to allege facts with sufficient particularity to state a cause of action for financial elder abuse. (See Covenant Care, Inc., supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 790.) As also noted above, the Tarmann and Daniels cases apply to misrepresentation claims, not financial elder abuse claims, so the heightened pleading requirement applies here. (See Tarmann, supra, 2 Cal.App.4th at p. 158; Daniels, supra, 246 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1166-1167.) 

Moreover, it is not enough that the individual Defendants here may also be officers and directors of Mercado Latino. (See SAC, ¶ 30.) “[A] principal may be liable for the wrongful conduct of its agent, even if that conduct is criminal, in one of three ways: (1) if the principal directly authorizes ... [the tort or] crime to be committed, (2) if the agent commits the tort in the scope of his employment and in performing service on behalf of the principal, or (3) if the principal ratifies its agent's conduct after the fact by ... voluntar[ily] elect[ing] to adopt the [agent's] conduct ... as its own…” (Doe v. Roman Cath. Archbishop of Los Angeles (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 953, 969, internal citations and quotation marks omitted.) The SAC contains no factual allegations showing that the individual Defendants were directly authorized by Mercado Latino, that they were acting in the scope of their employment, or that Mercado Latino ratified their conduct in connection with Defendants allegedly taking Plaintiff’s stock, certificates, or the dividends. (See SAC, ¶¶ 56-67.) It also does not make sense how the individual Defendants allegedly siphoning off Mercado Latino’s funds in any way supports Plaintiff’s allegations that Mercado Latino participated in wrongfully taking Plaintiff’s Grosac stock and stock certificates, as Plaintiff contends. (See SAC, ¶¶ 33, 59; Opp., 8:8-21.) 

Furthermore, Plaintiff’s citation to Klein v. Oakland Raiders (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 67 is distinguishable because that case involved the opposite situation, i.e., imposing liability against the individual when the corporation was otherwise liable. (Id., at pp. 79-80.) Here, Plaintiff is attempting to hold Mercado Latino liable for the alleged actions of the individual Defendants in connection with the Grosac stock certificate issue, not hold Defendants liable for the alleged actions of Mercado Latino. (See SAC, ¶¶ 56-67.) 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action as to Mercado Latino without leave to amend. 

CONCLUSION           

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint with 20 days’ leave to amend as to Richard, Roberto, Jorge, and Grosac[1]. This is the last opportunity the Court will give Plaintiff to attempt to plead this cause of action. 

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint without leave to amend as to Mercado Latino. 

Moving Parties are ordered to give notice of this ruling and file proof of service of same within five calendar days.


[1] As the issue was not raised by either party, it is unclear to the Court whether Plaintiff alleges that Grosac is liable for financial elder abuse as the cause of action is named against all defendants but is based on the alleged taking of Plaintiff’s Grosac stock and/or stock certificates.
