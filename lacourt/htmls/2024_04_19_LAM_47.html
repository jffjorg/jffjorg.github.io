DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV18521    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     April 19, 2024                        TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Luicila Leon v. Concord Farms Inc. et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV18521           

 

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Concord Farms, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Lucila Leon

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         05/15/20: Complaint filed.

·         08/16/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a wrongful termination and employment discrimination action. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants refused to accommodate Plaintiff for a work-related injury and terminated her for pursuing accommodations.

 

Defendant Concord Farms, Inc. moves to set aside the default entered against it under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Concord Farms, Inc.’s Motion to Set Aside Default is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant Concord Farms, Inc. moves to set aside the default entered against it under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 provides that, “[w]hen service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default . . . has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default . . . and for leave to defend the action.” The notice must be served and filed “within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5(a).) Upon a finding by the Court that “the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default . . . on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5(c).) “Actual notice” under this statute means “genuine knowledge of the party litigant,” and not “constructive notice” or “imputed notice.” (Rosenthal v. Garner (1983), 142 Cal. App. 3d 891, 895.)

 

Notice of Motion

 

            Defendant served its motion via electronic service on March 26, 2024. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1005(b), notice of a motion must be given a minimum of sixteen court days before the date of hearing, plus two court days for electronic service. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1005(b); 1010.6.) Thus, for a hearing date of April 19, 2024, the deadline to serve and file this motion was March 25, 2024. As Plaintiff has not objected to the motion on this basis, however, the Court will consider Defendant’s arguments on their merits.

 

Timeliness

 

            Defendant was defaulted on September 25, 2023. (September 26, 2023 Minute Order.) As of this date, no default judgment has been entered. Thus, there is no dispute that this motion is timely.

 

Actual Notice

 

            Defendant requests that the Court set aside the default entered against it on September 25, 2023, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 and the Court’s inherent authority on the ground that it never received notice of this action.

 

            Defendant contends that it never received actual notice of the action because it was never served with the Summons and First Amended Complaint and had no notice of this action, according to Defendant’s agent for service of process, Grace Tung. (Declaration of Grace Tung ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-3.) The record tells a different story. Plaintiff filed her complaint on May 15, 2020. (See Complaint.) According to the Proof of Service filed on July 7, 2020, Plaintiff Served Ms. Tung on June 16, 2020, by substitute service at 4005 Whipple Road, Union City, CA, 94587 by leaving the papers in the presence of Abbey Chan and thereafter mailing the papers to the same address. (July 7, 2020 Proof of Service.) This service was apparently successful, as Defendant answered the Complaint on July 15, 2020. (See Answer.) On June 18, 2021, Anahita Kouroshnia—who brought this motion on Defendant’s behalf—successfully moved to be relieved as counsel for Defendant. (June 18, 2021 Minute Order.) Defendant failed to retain substitute counsel, and Defendant’s answer was stricken in August of 2021 for that failure. (August 10, 2021 Minute Order.) Plaintiff amended her Complaint on August 16, 2022. (First Amended Complaint.) The First Amended Complaint was served on Defendant in the same manner as the original on September 12, 2022, according to the Proof of Service filed on October 12, 2022, by serving the First Amended Complaint at the same address by leaving it with the same individual and mailing a copy to that address. (October 12, 2022 Proof of Service.)

 

            Defendant offers absolutely no explanation for why a mode of service that sufficed to allow Defendant to answer the original Complaint somehow failed to do the same for the First Amended Complaint. Ms. Tung’s conclusory statement that she was unaware of the action is not credible, especially since her service in 2020 resulted in Defendant’s appearance in this case soon there after. The Court is not persuaded that relief should be granted on this record.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant Concord Farms, Inc.’s Motion to Set Aside Default is DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

CASE:                         Employers Compensation Ins. Co. v BaronHR, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV11980           

 

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

MOVING PARTY:               Antonio Ramos, Counsel for Defendant BaronHR, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 4/16/24

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for equitable indemnity that was filed on May 26, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that that Defendant failed to maintain insurance for Workers’ Compensation, causing Plaintiff to bear the liability to an employee on a successful workers’ compensation claim.

 

Attorney Antonio Ramos moves to be relieved as counsel for Defendant BaronHR, LLC.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Attorney Antonio Ramos moves to be relieved as counsel for Defendant BaronHR, LLC.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1005(b), notice of a motion must be given at least 16 court days before the date the motion is to be heard. The time for notice is extended by two court days if notice is served electronically, and by five calendar days if served by mail. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1010.6; 1013.) This motion was served by mail and electronic service, and notice was therefore due by March 25, 2024, for electronic service and March 22, 2024 for mail service. Notice of this motion was given on March 27, 2024. This motion was not timely served.

 

Moreover, although Moving Counsel filed a notice of motion and motion on form MC-051 and a Declaration in Support of the Motion on form MC-052, Moving Counsel did not file a proposed order on form MC-053 as required by California Rules of Court rule 3.1362(d).

 

As the motion is procedurally defective in multiple respects, the Court cannot grant Moving Counsel the relief sought.

 

Accordingly, Attorney Ramos’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Defendant is DENIED. This ruling is without prejudice to a procedurally proper motion containing all required filings.

 

This ruling is conditioned on Moving Counsel giving notice to all parties of the Court’s ruling.

CASE:                         Jenna Lawrence v. Eureka Restaurant Group, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV20283           

 

MOTION TO SET ASIDE STIPULATION TO ARBITRATE

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Jenna Lawrence

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Eureka Restaurant Group, LLC

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a sexual harassment and employment discrimination action that was filed on August 23, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that she was sexually harassed by a colleague and terminated in retaliation for her complaints and for requiring accommodations for a disability.

 

Plaintiff moves to set aside a stipulation to arbitrate filed on December 12, 2023.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Stipulation to Arbitrate is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

            Plaintiff moves to set aside a stipulation to arbitrate filed on December 12, 2023. Plaintiff does not identify the authority for her motion in the Notice of Motion. The body of Plaintiff’s moving papers discuss the Court’s inherent authority to reconsider its own motions, but this power does not entitle a party to bring a motion for relief. (See Darling, Hall & Rae v. Kritt (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 1148, 1156.) However, under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b):

 

The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b).) There is an extensive body of caselaw broadly construing the term “proceeding” within the meaning of this statute to include “any step taken by a party in the progress of a civil action.” (Zellerino v. Brown (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1097, 1105 [quoting Stonesifer v. Kilburn (1892) 94 Cal. 33, 43]; see also Burns v. Superior Court (1903) 140 Cal. 1, 5-6; Gipe v. Superior Court (1981) 124 Cal.App.3d 617, 626; Marblehead L. Co. v. Superior Court (1923) 60 Cal.App. 644, 655-56.)

 

            Moreover, there is express authority standing for the position that the Court may set aside a stipulation to arbitrate signed by an attorney when the client did not consent to that stipulation. (Blanton v. Womancare, Inc. (1985) 38 Cal.3d 396, 407-408; Sanker v. Brown (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 1144, 1147.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff’s counsel states categorically that she signed the stipulation to arbitrate on December 6, 2023, without first obtaining the consent of her client. (Declaration of Ann Guleser ISO Mot. ¶ 3.) Attorney Guleser further states that Plaintiff did not consent to the stipulation and that Plaintiff’s consent was never even sought. (Id. ¶ 5.) Plaintiff thus argues that, under existing precedent, she should be relieved from the obligation to arbitrate her claims.

 

            Defendant, in opposition, argues that Plaintiff should be held to the stipulation because she should be accountable for the failure of counsel to obtain her consent to an agreement. Defendant cites absolutely no authority for this position, which is unsurprising since our Supreme Court categorically rejected that position in 1985. Further, although Defendant casts aspersions on the conduct of Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant offers no evidence demonstrating that Plaintiff ratified her counsel’s actions or otherwise consented to arbitration beyond what may be inferred solely from the acts of counsel.  

 

            In light of the express authority cited by Plaintiff and the express statements by Plaintiff’s counsel that she acted without authority from her client, the Court finds good cause to set aside its December 12, 2023, order and permit the case to go forward on the merits.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Stipulation to Arbitrate is GRANTED.
