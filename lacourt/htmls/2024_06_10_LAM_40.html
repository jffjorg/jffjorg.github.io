DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 22STCV07972    Hearing Date: June 10, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

MICHELLE LEONARD, an individual,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

BRIAN E. CLAYPOOL, an individual, THE CLAYPOOL LAW FIRM, and DOES 1 through 20 inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:                           22STCV07972

 Hearing Date:   6/10/24

 Trial Date:         8/6/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendants Brian E. Claypool and Claypool Law Firm’s Motion to Deem All Facts Specified in Defendants’ Request for Admissions, Set Three to Plaintiff Michelle Leonard Admitted, and Motion for Monetary Sanctions Against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s Counsel Kevin Gerry, Jointly and Severally, in the Amount of $1,915 Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2023.010, 2023.030, and 2023.280(c) [Res ID # 0341]; and

Defendants Brian E. Claypool and Claypool Law Firm’s Motion for Summary Judgment [Res ID # 3174].

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Michelle Leonard (Plaintiff Leonard) sues Defendants Brian E. Claypool (Brian Claypool), The Claypool Law Firm (Claypool Law), and Does 1 to 20 pursuant to a May 3, 2023, First Amended Complaint (FAC) alleging claims of (1) Legal Malpractice and (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty.

The claims arise from allegations that, during their legal representation of Michelle Leonard for injuries sustained during the Route 91 Harvest Festival concert shooting in Las Vegas, Nevada (the underlying action or the class action case), Brian Claypool and Claypool Law (the Claypool Defendants), as well as Does 1 to 20, engaged in conduct amounting to legal malpractice and breach of fiduciary duty. This conduct included: (1) the Claypool Defendants’ failure to submit key medical records for Michelle Leonard’s injuries in relation the Route 91 shooting and class action case in Nevada; (2) the Claypool Defendants’ failure to file documentation from the federal government regarding Michelle Leonard’s total disability as a result of her injuries in relation the Route 91 shooting and class action case in Nevada; (3) the Claypool Defendants providing erroneous tax information regarding Michelle Leonard’s personal injury claims; and (4) the Claypool Defendants’ omission of the aforementioned misconduct through failure and refusal to communicate with Michelle Leonard to keep her apprised of the significant developments in the class action case or respond to Michelle Leonard’s documents requests, including giving Michelle Leonard a copy of her retainer agreement upon execution.

B. Motion for Summary Judgment

On February 26, 2024, the Claypool Defendants filed a motion for summary judgment of the FAC’s two causes of action. The motion was set for hearing on May 9, 2024.

On March 12, 2024, the Court granted a separate motion for issue and evidence sanctions filed by the Claypool Defendants against Plaintiff Leonard.

On March 19, 2024, the Claypool Defendants filed an ex parte application to refile their motion for summary judgment, as updated to incorporate the Court’s March 12th order and its effects as part as the evidentiary bases for relief. The ex parte application also requested that the Court continue the trial date in this action—initially set for June 11, 2024, by the Court on May 7, 2023—by 90 days, with the continued date to be considered the new “initial” trial date in this action.

On March 21, 2024, the Court granted the ex parte application, continued the hearing on to-be-refiled motion for summary judgment to June 10, 2024, and continued trial to August 6, 2024.

II. Motion to Deem the Truth of Facts Specified in Requests for Admission Admitted and Motion for Monetary Sanctions

A. Motion to Deem the Truth of Facts Specified in Requests for Admission Admitted: GRANTED.

1. Legal Standard

The discovering party can make a motion to deem as admitted any unanswered requests for admission or any requests answered in a late or unverified response. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.240, subd. (a) [RFA responses must be signed by responding party under oath]; see Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636 (Appleton) [unsworn response to RFAs is treated like no response].) These requests are not automatically deemed admitted; the discovery party must make the motion. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)

To establish this ground, a movant must show:

(1) Proper service (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.070);

(2) Expiration of the deadline for the initial response 30 days after service or on date agreed to by parties (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subds. (a), (b)); and

(3) That (a) the responding party served no response (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b)), (b) the propounding party served a late response (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b)); or (3) the responding party served an unsworn response (see Appleton, supra, 206 Cal.App.3d at p. 636 [unsworn response to RFAs is treated like no response]).

A court must deny a motion to compel initial discovery where the discovery sought is outside the scope of discovery. (See CBS, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 19; see also Code. Civ. Proc., § 2017.010 [scope of discovery].) The scope of discovery is intended to be very broad. (See Emerson Elec. Co. v. Superior Court (19970 16 Cal.4th 1101, 1108; Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1249.) Because of this broad scope, discovery statues are interpreted liberally in favor of discovery. (See Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541; Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)

2. RFA’s at Issue

RFAs, Set Three, No. 31: Admit that YOU (“YOU” and “YOUR” refer to plaintiff MICHELLE LEONARD and/or her agents, attorneys, representatives, and any other individuals who have acted on her behalf) were only entitled to receive points under EF-7 in the class action settlement of Clark County District Court case no. A-18-7697520C.

RFAs, Set Three, No. 32: Admit that YOU were not entitled to receive points under EF-6 in the class action settlement of Clark County District Court case no. A-18-7697520C.

RFAs, Set Three, No. 33: Admit that the class action settlement protocol in Clark County District Court case no. A-18-17697520C did not allow YOU to receive points under both EF-6 and EF-7.

RFAs, Set Three, No. 34: Admit that YOU would not have received more than 19.33575 points under EF-6 even if YOU qualified for EF-6.

(RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., Ex. A, p. 2.)

3. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of the Claypool Defendants.

The Court initially notes that this motion is properly being heard no fewer than 15 days prior to the now-initial trial date of August 6, 2024, as ordered by the Court on March 21, 2024. Moreover, RFAs, Set Three, were served no fewer than 30 days prior to the now-initial trial date of August 6, 2024.

Moving to the merits, the Claypool Defendants have shown evidence that:

(1) The Claypool Defendants served RFAs, Set Three, on Plaintiff’s counsel on March 15, 2024 (RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., Ex. A);

(2) The deadline to respond has expired, i.e., 30 calendar days from the date of service, landing on Sunday, April 14, 2024, and thus pushed to Monday, April 15, 2024, plus two court days for service, pushing the final date for response to Wednesday, April 17, 2024 (RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., ¶ 7 [no responses as of May 3, 2024] & Proof of Service [service on March 15, 2024]); and

(3) A failure to respond by Plaintiff Leonard. (RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., ¶ 7 [no responses as of May 3, 2024].)

The Court also notes that the admission requests are directly relevant to this action insofar as the malpractice and breach of fiduciary duty claims are premised on the Claypool Defendants’ alleged failure to submit Plaintiff Leonard’s medical records and federal disability documentation in the One October protocol, allegedly resulting in a lower award in the underlying action. (Compare RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., Ex. A, p. 2, RFAs, Set Three, Nos. 31-34, with 5/3/23 FAC, ¶¶ 9-9.C.)

Based on these grounds, the Court GRANTS the Claypool Defendants’ motion.

B. Motion for Sanctions: GRANTED in part.

1. Legal Standard

The Court must award sanctions when a party’s response is untimely, and the discovering party makes a motion to deem the requests admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c); see Stover v. Bruntz (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 19, 31-32; see e.g., Appleton, supra, 206 Cal.App.3d at pp. 635-636 [sanctions are mandatory].)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)

2. Court’s Determination

The Court GRANTS sanctions, in part, in the amount of $1,120, as against Plaintiff Leonard and counsel Kevin Gerry, jointly and severally.

The failure to respond to admission requests is a ground for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ., §§ 2023.010, subd. (d), 2023.030, subd. (a), 2033.280, subd. (b).)

Here, despite valid service on counsel via email, Plaintiff Leonard has failed to respond to an authorized method of discovery in RFAs, Set Three. (RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., ¶ 7 [no responses as of May 3, 2024] & Proof of Service [service on March 15, 2024] & Service List [service on Plaintiff’s counsel].)

Sanctions are equally merited against Plaintiff and counsel for non-response based on service on counsel and non-response thereafter from Plaintiff Leonard.

Last, sanctions are appropriate in the amount of $1,120, not $1,915. The $1,915 request was based on two hours preparing this motion, five expected hours to review any opposition, draft a reply, and attend the hearing on the motion, and a $60 filing fee for the RFAs motion. (RFAs Mot., Yee Decl., ¶ 11.) However, there was no opposition and thus no review and no reply. Thus, the Court finds that the proper award should be comprised of a reasonable fee rate of $265 per hour (as requested), two hours reasonably spent on the RFAs motion (as requested), two hours to prepare and attend this hearing (i.e., four hours rather than the total of seven requested since there was no need to review an opposition or prepare a reply), and a $60 filing fee, totaling $1,120.

 

III. Motion for Summary Judgment: DENIED.

A. Request for Judicial Notice

Per the Claypool Defendants’ request, the Court takes judicial notice of the Nevada court order for the global settlement resulting from the Route 91 Harvest Festival concert shooting, and a copy of the One October Settlement Fund: Claims Protocol and Allocation Methodology (consistent with prior rulings, hereafter the One October Protocol) approved by the Nevada court to explain who was eligible for a share in the global settlement funds and the allocation methodology for any given claim. (Refiled MSJ, RJN, Exs. A-B; see Evid. Code, §§ 452, subds. (d), (h), 453, subds. (a)-(b).)

B. Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact for trial or that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make prima facie showing no triable material fact issues. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (Aguilar).) This burden on summary judgment or adjudication “is more properly one of persuasion rather than proof, since he must persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find, and not to prove any such fact to the satisfaction of the court itself as though it were sitting as the trier of fact.” (Id. at p. 850, fn. 11.)

If the moving party meets this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a rebuttal prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Id. at p. 849.)

“[I]n ruling on motions for summary judgment courts are to ‘“liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”’ [Citations].” (Cheal v. El Camino Hospital (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 736, 760.)

B. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Leonard.

A defendant moving for summary judgment has the burden “to identify and respond to all theories of liability reflected in the complaint, ‘even if not separately pleaded.’ [Citation.] … While a plaintiff … need sustain its burden of proof only on one of the theories in order to prevail at trial, a defendant who seeks a summary judgment must define all of the theories alleged in the complaint and challenge each factually.’” (Lopez v. Superior Court (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 705, 714 (Lopez); see Johnson v. Chui (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 775, 778 [“[A] defendant is entitled to summary judgment only when all theories of liability have been negated”]; Farley v. El Tejon Unified School Dist. (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 371, 379 [“[T]o prevail on its motion for summary judgment, [the defendant] had the burden of negating every alternative theory of liability presented by the pleadings”].)

Where a motion for summary judgment does “‘not negate theories of [the defendant’s] liability, the trial court should’” rule “‘[the defendant] failed to carry [its] initial burden and stop[] there.’” (Hedayati v. Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 833, 846; see Hawkins v. Wilton (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.)

The FAC presents what the Court has previously summarized as four factual paths to damages:

(1) The Claypool Defendants’ failure to submit key medical records for Michelle Leonard’s injuries in relation the Route 91 shooting and class action case in Nevada (FAC, ¶¶ 9, 9.A.);

(2) The Claypool Defendants’ failure to file documentation from the federal government regarding Michelle Leonard’s total disability as a result of her injuries in relation the Route 91 shooting and class action case in Nevada (FAC, ¶¶ 9, 9.B.);

(3) The Claypool Defendants providing erroneous tax information regarding Michelle Leonard’s personal injury claims (FAC, ¶¶ 9, 9.C.); and

(4) The Claypool Defendants’ omission of the aforementioned misconduct through failure and refusal to communicate with Michelle Leonard to keep her apprised of the significant developments in the class action case or respond to Michelle Leonard’s documents requests, including giving Michelle Leonard a copy of her retainer agreement upon execution (FAC, ¶ 10-10.C.).

The Claypool Defendants’ motion for summary judgment addresses paths (1) and (2) and path (4) insofar as it is derivative of paths (1) and (2). This is because the motion for summary judgment relies on arguments for the most part relying on whether Plaintiff Leonard could have recovered a greater award in the underlying action, necessarily relating to the alleged failure to submit documents in that action regarding Plaintiff Leonard’s disability. (See, e.g., Refiled MSJ, pp. 6-9 [focusing majority of the ‘arguments’ section of the points and authorities on arguments relating to recovery under the protocol applied in the underlying action; no mention of tax advice].)

However, path (3) for damages—erroneous tax advice—and path (4) as derivative of path (3) do not appear in the Claypool Defendants’ points and authorities and separate statement, which fail to mention “tax advice” altogether. (Cf. FAC, ¶ 9.C. [erroneous tax advice as basis for liability].) This omission cannot be overlooked.

As recently as April 15, 2024, in relation to a motion to reclassify, the Court ordered the parties to brief arguments for damages arising from erroneous tax advice, resulting in the Court’s May 1, 2024, ruling. (5/1/24 Ruling, pp. 5-6.) At that time, Plaintiff Leonard was not able to make an evidentiary showing of such damages in relation to the motion to reclassify. (Ibid.) However, here, the initial burden on summary judgment is on the moving party or parties, which are the Claypool Defendants. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 850.) That burden includes disproving “all theories of liability reflected in the complaint.” (Lopez, supra, 45 Cal.App.4th at p. 714.) If path (3) for damages is not addressed in the motion for summary judgment, then all theories of liability in the FAC have not been addressed, and the Court cannot grant summary judgment. Even if the Court has previously heard arguments in relation to other motions that may bear upon path (3) for damages, those arguments were not raised in the motion for summary judgment.

The sole panacea argument advanced by the Claypool Defendants—added at the end of the points and authorities in a cursory argument—is that a plaintiff cannot recover general damages in a legal malpractice action absent a loss of a liberty right for the client, citing Camenisch v. Superior Court (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1689, 1694 (Camenisch) in support. (Refiled MSJ, p. 9.) Otherwise stated, the Claypool Defendants argue that because there are no damages at issue here, both pleaded counts fail.

However, a review of the cited portion of Camenisch does not support this proposition. The Claypool Defendants, without much elaboration, argue that Camenisch’s holding involved general damages, whereas Camenisch at p. 1694, as cited, merely discussed cases that addressed whether emotional distress damages could be recovered in legal malpractice cases. Moreover, the only portion of Camenisch at p. 1694, as cited, that discusses a liberty interest is Camenisch’s reference to Holliday v. Jones (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 102 (Holliday), which, as discussed in Camenisch, addressed the propriety of emotional distress damages due to attorney negligence. However, Camenisch’s reference to Holliday did not quote any rule holding that a property interest must always be at issue in relation to legal malpractice damages. To the extent that the Claypool Defendants cite Camenisch for a different purpose, the Court invites further clarification at oral argument.

The Claypool Defendants’ motion is thus DENIED.

II. Motion to Quash Subpoena or for Protective Order: Motion to Quash is DENIED in part and GRANTED in part; Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.

A. Legal Standard

1. Quash Subpoena

A motion to quash a deposition subpoena or deposition notice is used to strike, modify, or impose conditions on a subpoena or notice that is procedurally or substantively defective. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1987.1, subd. (a), 2025.410, subd. (c); see, e.g., Catholic Mut. Relief Soc’y v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 358, 365 [motion based on ground that subpoenas sought information outside scope of discovery]; John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186 [motion based in part on ground that subpoenas violated right of privacy]; McClatchy Newspapers v. Superior Court (1945) 26 Cal.2d 386, 391 [motion based on ground that subpoenas were unreasonable and oppressive]; Far W. S&L Assn. v. McLaughlin (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 67, 71 [motion based on ground that subpoena was not properly served]; City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 883, 888 [procedural remedy for a defective subpoena is generally a motion to quash under § 1987.1].)

Such a motion may be made by:

(1) Any party to the action (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(1));

(2) The subpoenaed witness (e.g., a custodian of another person’s personal records) (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(2); Monarch Healthcare v. Superior Court (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1287-1288); or

(3) A person (party or nonparty) whose consumer, governmental, or employment information or records have been subpoenaed (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (g) [party consumer], 1985.4 [state or local-agency employee or any other natural person], 1985.6, subd. (f)(1) [employee], 1987.1, subd. (b)(3) [consumer], 1987.1, subd. (b)(4) [employee]; see also Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1985.3, subd. (g), ¶ 2, 1985.6, subd. (f)(2) [nonparty consumer can make written objections instead of motion to quash]).

A motion to quash may be based on various grounds, including that:

The subpoena contains error or irregularities in the deposition notice (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subds. (a), (c));

The subpoena seeks information that is not relevant to the issues in the case (see, e.g., Catholic Mut. Relief Soc’y v. Superior Court, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 365 [motion to quash deposition subpoena because documents were outside the scope of discovery]; cf. Cadiz Land Co. v. Rail Cycle, L.P. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 74, 122-123 [motion to quash deposition notice because deposition would not lead to admissible evidence]);

The disclosure of the records sought will violate the consumer’s constitutional right to privacy (see Cal. Const., art. I, § 1; see, e.g., Fett v. Medical Bd. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 211, 213 [petitions to quash to nonparties’ doctor because of privacy rights]; Manela v. Superior Court (2009) 117 Cal.App.4th 1139, 1150-1151 [motion to quash a subpoena to party’s doctor because of privacy rights]); and

The disclosure of records will violate a privilege of the consumer, the witness, or a person with whom the witness has a relationship that requires the witness to assert the privilege on the person’s behalf. (See, e.g., Monarch Healthcare v. Superior Court, supra, 78 Cal.App.4th at p. 1290 [objections to subpoena for business records based on trade-secret privilege]).

2. Protective Order

A motion for a protective order is used to ask the court to modify, prohibit, or limit discovery procedures to protect a party from an excessive or unnecessary burden, expense, or intrusion. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2017.020, subd. (a) [burden, expense, or intrusiveness of the discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence], 2019.030, subds. (a)(1)-(2), (b) [discovery obtainable from other source, cumulative or duplicative, or discovery method is unduly burdensome or expensive]; see also Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1987.1, subd. (a) [same or similar standards for subpoenas], 2025.420 [same or similar standards for depositions], 2025.460, subd. (a) [same or similar standards for depositions], 2025.470 [same or similar standards for depositions], 2030.090 [same or similar standards for interrogatories], 2031.060 [same or similar standards for demands to produce], 2032.510, subds. (d), (e) [same or similar standards for medical examinations], 2033.080 [same or similar standards for admission requests], 2034.250 [same or similar standards for expert-witness discovery], 2034.420 [same or similar standards for expert-witness discovery]; see, e.g., Liberty Mut. Ins. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1285 [defendant sought protective order to prohibit apex deposition on grounds of annoyance and embarrassment]; Stony Brook I Homeowners Ass’n v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 691, 693 [defendant made motion for protective order to prevent plaintiff’s inquiry into how much defense work defense’s expert had performed in personal-injury actions].)

Good cause must normally be shown to prevail on a motion for protective order. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.420, subd. (b) [oral deposition] 2028.070 [written deposition], 2030.290, subd. (b) [interrogatories], 2031.060, subd. (b) [demands to produce], 2033.080, subd. (b) [admission requests], 2034.250, subd. (b) [expert-witness discovery].)

4. Court’s Determination

a. Motion to Quash Subpoena at Attach. 3, Category Nos. 2-17, 30-33, 42-48

Pursuant to the receivership order and subject to the receivership modification proposed order pending before the Court, Invenz is currently empowered to obtain documents from Preferred Bank in relation to (1) Ridge Property LLC, (2) SJS Long Beach LLC, (3) 737 N La Brea LLC, (4) 3170 Cherry Avenue Property LLC, (5) Kip Prop LLC, (6) Redondo Prop LLC, and (7) Slauson Gas Station LLC.  (3/11/24 Signed Order; 5/31/24 Proposed Orders.)

In this sense, the purpose of 1351 Orizaba’s August 15, 2023, subpoena has been satisfied by the March 11, 2024, order. Invenz’s receivership over these LLCs permits the parties, including Plaintiff, to receive access to information on the financial condition of these LLCs and distributions from these LLCs to Hooman Nissani.

Hooman Nissani’s motion is thus determined to be DENIED as moot as to Categories 2-17, 30-33, and 42-48 listed in Attachment 3 of the subpoena at issue. (Mot., McCreary Decl., Ex. A, SUBP-010, Attach. 3.)

b. Motion to Quash Subpoena at Attach. 3, Category Nos. 26-29, 34-41

Relief is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART as to Category Nos. 26-29 and 34-41, with relief limited to modifications of the requests to limit their scope.

Category Nos. 26-29 and 34-41 involve LLCs that are part of the January 25, 2022, charging orders, but not part of the March 11, 2024, receivership order: (1) Culver City Prop, (2) Lennox Car Wash, and (3) Playa Vista. (Compare 1/25/22 Orders re: Culver City Prop, Lennox Car Wash, and Playa Vista, with 3/11/24 Signed Order.)

Judgment was first entered in this action on May 6, 2020, and was then amended on May 4, 2021, and again amended on January 2, 2024. Judgment has not been satisfied in any way, however. Discovery as to these three LLC’s and connections to Hooman Nissani is highly relevant to the post-judgment collection efforts raised by the subpoena at issue here.

To the extent that privacy and confidentiality issues are raised by Hooman Nissani, the Court notes that the risks of divulging third-party or non-relevant information in this case can be mitigated through specific orders. However, as to Hooman Nissani’s own privacy and his privacy in documents that may show his interest in Culver City Prop, Lennox Car Wash, and Playa Vista, that information is directly at issue in post-judgment collection efforts, overcoming his right to privacy where discovery is required for a fair resolution of the proceedings, including judgment and collection efforts.

The Court thus grants relief in part by modifying the requests to permit redactions of third-party identifying information, such as names, addresses (physical or electronic), titles, or other information from which the persons or entities can be reasonably identified. The Court makes this determination based on a balancing of the relevance and need for the discovery on one hand and Hooman Nissani’s, the LLCs’, and third parties’ privacy interests on the other. (See Ameri-Med Corp. v. Worker’s Comp. Appeals Bd. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1286-1287 (Ameri-Med) [corporation has no standing to assert a constitutional right to privacy but has a general right to privacy that depends on the circumstances, including the strength of the nexus between the artificial entity and human beings and the context in which the controversy arises]; Boler v. Superior Court (1987) 201 Cal.App.3d 467, 472, fn. 1 [party did not waive third parties’ sexual privacy rights by not timely objecting to discovery demand because third parties were not notified and given opportunity to object, i.e., cannot waive third parties’ privacy rights].)

c. Motion to Quash Subpoena at Attach. 3, Category Nos. 18-25

Relief is GRANTED as to Category Nos. 18-25.

The proposed order relating to the receiver completely suspends the receivership order over Willow and Cielo pending the August 23, 2024, hearing on a motion to vacate the charging orders. The reasoning two subsections ago is therefore not applicable here.

However, there are doubts as to the relationship between Willow and Hooman Nissani and between Cielo and Nissani. Those doubts are to be resolved at the August 23rd hearing, with an arrest of the status quo pursuant to a stipulation in the meantime. (See 5/31/24 Proposed Orders.) If the January 25, 2022, charging orders related to Willow and Cielo are not appropriate, then the discovery at issue is irrelevant.

With the parties proposing to arrest the status quo, the Court determines that the result that best balances the parties’ needs is to GRANT Hooman Nissani’s motion as to Category Nos. 18-25, without prejudice, with the August 23rd hearing set to resolve disputes as to Willow and Cielo’s connection to this case.

d. Motion to Quash Subpoena at Attach. 3, Category No. 1

The Court finds relief is DENIED as moot as to Category No. 1 insofar as it relates to (1) Ridge Property LLC, (2) SJS Long Beach LLC, (3) 737 N La Brea LLC, (4) 3170 Cherry Avenue Property LLC, (5) Kip Prop LLC, (6) Redondo Prop LLC, and (7) Slauson Gas Station LLC.

The Court GRANTS relief in part as to Category No. 1 insofar as it relates to (1) Culver City Prop, (2) Lennox Car Wash, and (3) Playa Vista, as modified above.

The Court GRANTS relief as to Category No. 1 insofar as it relates to Willow and Cielo.

The Court otherwise DENIES relief for Category No. 1 based on overbreadth and/or vagueness. Briefly, Category No. 1, insofar as it relates to companies other than the charging order LLCs, is vague and may include records for companies that conceivably have nothing to do with post-judgment collection efforts, e.g., documents from times preceding the controversies between the parties, unrelated to the charging order LLCs.

e. Alternative Motion for Protective Order

The Court DENIES the alternative request for a protective order as moot based on the Court’s rulings on the motion to quash, which include modifications responding to privacy and confidentiality concerns.

 

III. Motions to Be Relieved as Counsel: GRANTED.

A. Legal Standard

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel ([without client consent pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section] 284, subd. (2)) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’” (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)

For an attorney to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with the requirements of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subdivisions (a) and (c) to (e). These subdivisions require that an attorney file and show proof of service of (1) a notice and motion to be relived as counsel on Judicial Council form MC-051, (2) a declaration in support of the motion to be relived as counsel on Judicial Council form MC-052, and (3) a proposed order in support of the motion to be relieved as counsel on Judicial Council form MC-053.

The court should consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.” (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.) Moreover, the motion should also be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.)

B. Court’s Determination

First, on May 2, 2024, counsel complied with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362. Within 16 court days of hearing, plus additional days to respond based on manner of service, counsel for HTL and Hooman Nissani filed proofs of service reflecting service of all three applicable forms on the parties that have appeared in this case. And a review of the three Judicial Council forms for HTL and Hooman Nissani shows that the forms are complete and properly filled out. (See 5/2/24 Judicial Council forms MC-051, MC-052, MC-053 & Proof of Service.)

Second, there is a valid reason for withdrawal: “I [Christopher S. Reeder] am the managing partner of Reeder McCreary, LLP. I have had no involvement with this case. Late in the evening on March 5, 2024, I became aware that a conversation occurred on March 5, 2024[,] between Hooman Michael Nissani and Duncan J. McCreary of Reeder McCreary, LLP which resulted in a serious conflict of interest between Mr. Nissani and Mr. McCreary. On March 6, 2024, after consulting with our outside ethics counsel, it was concluded that the conflict is so serious that no additional legal services can ethically be performed by either Mr. McCreary or Reeder McCreary, LLP for Hooman Michael Nissani and any of his related entities. [¶] It is respectfully submitted that Mr. McCreary and Reeder McCreary, LLP must ethically cease all representation of Hooman Michael Nissani and any of his related entities immediately due to the serious nature of the conflict. [¶] If the court requires, Mr. McCreary can make an in-camera disclosure regarding the substance of the conversation. [¶] As such, it is respectfully requested that Reeder McCreary, LLP be relieved as counsel immediately” (5/2/24 MC-052 from C. Reeder at § 2 & Attach. 2.)

Third, there appears to be no undue prejudice or delay at work here. These are post-trial proceedings in which the prevailing party is seeking to collect on the awarded judgment, no trial prejudice can exist for HTL and Hooman Nissani in relieving their counsel of representation. Neither has Orizaba filed an opposition, which could have addressed questions such as self-representation by HTL. And there are no arguments before the Court relating to delay.

However, the Court will have to address the issue of who will represent HTL, because a corporation or LLC cannot represent itself.

Counsel’s motions are thus GRANTED.
