DEPARTMENT F43 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23CHCV02813    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 6-14-24

Case # 23CHCV02813, Elliott Wolfe vs. Judith Wolfe, et al.

Trial Date: N/A

 

DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Judith Wolfe

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Elliot Wolfe

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Demurrer to the Complaint

·         1st Cause of Action for Assault

·         2nd Cause of Action for Battery

·         3rd Cause of Action for Trespass to Chattels

·         4th Cause of Action for Conversion

·         5th Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

·         6th Cause of Action for Elder Abuse

 

RULING: Demurrer sustained with leave to amend.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On September 19, 2023, Plaintiff Elliott Wolfe (Plaintiff) filed a complaint with six causes of action for Assault, Battery, Trespass to Chattels, Conversion, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Elder Abuse.

 

Defendant Judith Wolfe (Defendant) previously filed a demurer on October 23, 2023. That demurrer was sustained with leave to amend on February 29, 2024. On March 5, 2024, Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint (FAC) was filed. Defendant filed her demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC on April 24, 2024. Plaintiff opposes Defendant’s demurrer.

 

Defendant Judith is listed as one of the defendants to the FAC. It is mentioned in the FAC that Judith was Plaintiff’s wife and that they have separated. (FAC, ¶ 11.) The FAC states that Judith assaulted Plaintiff on August 6, 2022. (FAC, ¶ 7.) Next, it states that Judith bit Plaintiff on that date. (FAC, ¶ 9.) The FAC also alleges that Judith causes Plaintiff emotional distress. (FAC, ¶ 15.)

 

ANALYSIS

Defendant brings a demurrer to the First through Sixth Causes of Action on the basis that they are deprived of subject matter jurisdiction and fail to state sufficient facts to state a cause of action.

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP § 430.30(a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (CCP § 452.) The court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law…” ’ ” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

Third, Fourth, and Sixth Causes of Action

Plaintiff’s Third, Fourth, and Sixth Causes of Action are devoid of any specific facts against Defendant Judith Wolfe. It is impossible to tell from Plaintiff’s complaint what Plaintiff is alleging happened. Rather than state facts, Plaintiff has made conclusory statements under these causes of action that items of his property were taken, but he does not state who took what or when it was taken. These conclusory statements seem designed to simply check the boxes for the requirements of the causes of action, and do not state any facts specific to Plaintiff’s case.

 

While Judith’s name is mentioned in the Third and Fourth Causes of Action, it is only in the context of her being Plaintiff’s then-wife. There are no allegations against her. Plaintiff has not pled sufficient facts to maintain these causes of action. Defendant’s demurrer to these causes of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

First Cause of Action

For the First Cause of Action for Assault, the essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed he was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff's harm. (CACI No. 1301.)

 

Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action only mentions that the date of the assault by Judith was August 6, 2022. (FAC, ¶ 7.) There are no facts concerning what the assault entailed or what the harmful or offensive contact was. Plaintiff has not pled fact sufficient to maintain a cause of action for assault against Defendant Judith. Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            Second Cause of Action

The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant's conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff's position would have been offended by the touching. (CACI No. 1300.)

 

Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action for battery only mentions that Judith bit Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 9.) There is no context given for this allegation, nor does the FAC address where, when, and how the biting took place. Plaintiff has not stated facts sufficient to maintain a cause of action for battery. Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            Fifth Cause of Action

A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress must allege that (1) the defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, severe emotional distress to the plaintiff; (2) the plaintiff actually suffered severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) the outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the emotional distress. (Cervantez v. J.C. Penney Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 579, 593.)

 

The FAC only states that Judith caused an incident of IIED, but the FAC contains no information about what the incident entailed or what the alleged extreme and outrageous conduct was. (FAC, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff has not stated facts sufficient to maintain this cause of action against Defendant. Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Fifth Cause of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            Pending Dissolution of Marriage Case

Defendant also argues in her demurrer that Plaintiff cannot maintain a civil action against her while their dissolution matter is pending. In Neal v. Superior Court (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 22, the Court of Appeal held that a demurrer to a complaint should be sustained on the basis that family law cases should not be allowed to spill over into civil court. (Id. at 25.) When the civil case is clearly an extension or rehash of a family law case, this amounts to a jurisdictional defect in the case. (See also Burkle v. Burkle (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 387; Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441.)

 

While Defendant represents that the divorce matter between Plaintiff and Defendant is still pending, it is impossible to tell from the FAC or from the arguments in Defendant’s demurrer if this case is an extension of their family law case. There are no specific facts to support the causes of action, so it is unknown if the causes of action are related to the divorce matter. While Plaintiff has alleged that his separate property was taken, Defendant has not made a sufficient showing concerning the nature of the family law proceeding and the status of the property division issues. In order for the demurrer to be sustained on this issue, Defendant would need to provide more information about the status of the family law case, what rulings have been made, and what issues remain for resolution concerning property disposition and issues related to the restraining order.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s First, Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Causes of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

Plaintiff is given thirty days to file an amended complaint.

Dept. F43

Case # 23CHCV02813, Elliott Wolfe vs. Judith Wolfe, et al.

DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT WITH MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Elliott Wolfe

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Demurrer to the Complaint

·         1st Cause of Action for Assault

·         2nd Cause of Action for Battery

·         5th Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

·         6th Cause of Action for Elder Abuse

Motion to Strike

·         Punitive Damages [Page 6, Paragraphs 18 and 19; For Assault: Prayer, Page 6, lines 25-26; For Battery: Prayer, Page 7, lines 6-7; For Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: Prayer, Page 8, lines 5-6; and For Elder Abuse: Page 8, lines 15-16.]

·         Attorney Fees [For Elder Abuse: Prayer, Page 8, line 9.]

 

RULING: Demurrer is sustained with leave to amend; motion to strike is granted

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On September 19, 2023, Plaintiff Elliott Wolfe (Plaintiff) filed a complaint with six causes of action for Assault, Battery, Trespass to Chattels, Conversion, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Elder Abuse. Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint (FAC), filed on March 5, 2024, contains allegations against Defendant Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital (Defendant) employees.

 

First, the FAC alleges that employees of Defendant approached Plaintiff in an aggressive and threatening manner on October 3, 2022. (FAC, ¶ 7.) Next, Plaintiff alleges that employees of Defendant grabbed Plaintiff, carried him outside of a medical facility, and dropped him onto the ground on October 3, 2022. (FAC, ¶ 9.) Finally, Plaintiff alleges that employees of Defendant intentionally caused him emotional distress on October 3. (FAC, ¶ 15.)

 

Defendant filed a demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC on May 1, 2024. Defendant filed a motion to strike Plaintiff’s requests for punitive damages and attorney fees on May 3, 2024. Plaintiff opposes Defendant’s demurrer and motion to strike.

 

ANALYSIS

Defendant brings a demurrer to Plaintiff’s First, Second, Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action on the basis that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP § 430.30(a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (CCP § 452.) The court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law…” ’ ” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

            Sixth Causes of Action for Elder Abuse

Plaintiff’s Sixth Cause of Action is devoid of any specific facts against Defendant. It is impossible to tell from Plaintiff’s complaint what Plaintiff is alleging happened. Rather than state facts, Plaintiff has made conclusory statements under this cause of action. These conclusory statements seem designed to simply check the boxes for the requirements of the causes of action, and do not state any facts specific to Plaintiff’s case. Plaintiff has not pled sufficient facts to maintain this cause of action. Defendant’s demurrer to this cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

First Cause of Action

For the First Cause of Action for Assault, the essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed he was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff's harm. (CACI No. 1301.)

 

Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action only mentions that the date of the assault was October 3, 2022, and that Defendant’s employees approached Plaintiff in an aggressive and threatening manner then committed the batteries described in the Second Cause of Action. (FAC, ¶ 7.) There are no facts concerning what the assault entailed or other context, such as the location or context. Plaintiff has not pled fact sufficient to maintain a cause of action for assault against Defendant. Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            Second Cause of Action

The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant's conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff's position would have been offended by the touching. (CACI No. 1300.)

 

Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action for battery only mentions that Defendant’s employees grabbed Plaintiff, carried him outside a medical facility, and dropped him onto the ground. (FAC, ¶ 9.) There is no context given for these allegations, such as the location of the medical facility or what the sequence of events was that led to the alleged battery. Plaintiff has not stated facts sufficient to maintain a cause of action for battery. Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

            Fifth Cause of Action

A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress must allege that (1) the defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, severe emotional distress to the plaintiff; (2) the plaintiff actually suffered severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) the outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the emotional distress. (Cervantez v. J.C. Penney Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 579, 593.)

 

The FAC only states that employees of Defendant caused incidents of IIED, but the FAC contains no information about what the incidents entailed or what the alleged extreme and outrageous conduct was. Plaintiff has not stated facts sufficient to maintain this cause of action against Defendant. Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Fifth Cause of Action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

This Court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter. Under CCP § 435, “[a]ny party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” Under CCP § 436(a), “[t]he court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper . . . [s]trike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.”

 

            Punitive Damages

Punitive damages are governed by Civ. Code § 3294: “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.” (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)

 

The “conclusory characterization of defendant’s conduct as intentional, willful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of oppression, fraud or malice…within the meaning of section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) 

 

“It is not sufficient to allege merely that defendant ‘acted with oppression, fraud or malice.’ Rather, plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that defendant’s conduct was oppressive, fraudulent or malicious (e.g., that defendant acted with the intent to inflict great bodily harm on plaintiff or to destroy plaintiff's property or reputation).” (Croskey, et al., Cal. Prac. Guide: Insurance Litigation Ch. 13-C (Thomson Reuters, 2016) ¶ 13:197.2; and see, Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. v. Snepp (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 598, 643 [allegations that defendant’s conduct was “intentional, and done willfully, maliciously, with ill will towards Plaintiffs, and with conscious disregard for Plaintiff's rights” did not satisfy specific pleading requirements].) 

 

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants acted with malice and oppression, but in doing so, he has only made conclusory statements about what Defendants did. (FAC, ¶ 19.) Plaintiff has not satisfied the pleading requirements for a request for punitive damages.

 

Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages and references to punitive damages from Plaintiff’s FAC is granted.

 

            Attorney Fees

Attorney fees are only recoverable when authorized by contract, statute or law. (CCP § 1021; Reid v. Valley Restaurants, Inc. (1957) 48 Cal.2d 606, 610.) In the absence of allegations demonstrating that plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney fees, a request for attorney fees is improper. (Wiley v. Rhodes (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 1470, 1474.)

 

Plaintiff has requested attorney fees through his elder abuse cause of action (Welfare & Institutions Code § 15657(a)), but the Court found that Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to maintain that cause of action. In the absence of allegations demonstrating Plaintiff’s entitlement to the fees, the request is improper.

 

Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for attorney fees is granted.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend for Plaintiff’s First, Second, Fifth, and Sixth Causes of Action.

 

Defendant’s motion to strike is granted.

 

Plaintiff has 30 days to file an Amended Complaint.

 

Moving party to give notice.
