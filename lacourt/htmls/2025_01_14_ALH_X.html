DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendants demur and move to strike the Complaint’s First Cause of Action for Elder Abuse and Neglect and the Fourth Cause of Action for Violation of the Patient’s Bill of Rights.  They also move to strike portions of the Complaint which reference the prayers for relief regarding the same causes of action.

Background

The lawsuit arises from Plaintiff Henglan Zhao’s development of bedsores at a skilled nursing facility operated by Defendant Royal Vista Care Center, LLC.  According to the facial allegations of the Complaint, one of these bedsores has since resolved, and the other was treated with surgery in April 2024, but was still present to some degree at the time the Complaint was filed on May 14, 2024.  The Complaint alleges that Royal Vista failed to timely change plaintiff’s diapers, to adequately assist her in repositioning, and to provide her adequate hydration, and that such failures amounted to “Elder Abuse and Neglect” under Welfare & Institutions Code §§ 15610, et seq., as well as a violation of the Patient’s Bill of Rights under Health & Safety Code § 1430.

Tentative Ruling

The Demurrer is overruled as to each cause of action.  The Motion to Strike is denied.  Defendants shall file an Answer to the Complaint within 20 days of this Order.

Legal Authority and Analysis

“The function of a demurrer is to test the sufficiency of the complaint as a matter of law.”  (Holiday Matinee, Inc. v. Rambus, Inc. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1413, 1420.)  A complaint “is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.”  (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550 (“Doe”)), but the plaintiff must set forth the essential facts of his or her case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint [the] defendant with the nature, source and extent” of the plaintiff’s claim.  (Doheny Park Terrace Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Truck Ins. Exchange (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1076, 1099.)

Legal conclusions are insufficient.  (Id., at pp. 1098–99.)  The Court “assume[s] the truth of the allegations in the complaint, but do[es] not assume the truth of contentions, deductions, or conclusions of law.”  (California Logistics, Inc. v. State of California (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 242, 247.)  A demurrer lies only for defects appearing on the face of the complaint or from matters of which the court must or may take judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40; see Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

Legal Authority and Analysis

The Demurrer to the First Cause of Action (Elder Abuse) is OVERRULED

Under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (EADACPA) (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15600, et seq.), heightened remedies are available in limited circumstances.  A plaintiff must prove, by clear and convincing evidence, that a defendant is liable for either physical abuse under section 15610.63 or neglect under section 15610.57, and that the defendant committed the abuse with “recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.”  (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15657.)  Section 15610.57, in turn, provides two definitions of neglect.  First, “[t]he negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder or a dependent adult to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.”  (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.57, subd. (a)(1).)  Second, “[t]he negligent failure of an elder or dependent adult to exercise that degree of self care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.”  (Id., subd. (a)(2).)

Complementing these two definitions is the statute’s explicitly non-exhaustive list of “neglect” examples.  These include failures “to assist in personal hygiene” or to provide “food, clothing, or shelter” (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.57, subd. (b)(1)); “to provide medical care for physical and mental health needs” (id., subd. (b)(2)); “to protect from health and safety hazards” (id., subd. (b)(3)); and “to prevent malnutrition or dehydration” (id., subd. (b)(4)).

Here, Plaintiff alleges neglect arising in the context of medical care and not self-care.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to provide adequate care, resulting in severe dehydration, pressure ulcers, and other injuries.  The Complaint details systemic understaffing, failure to implement care plans, and neglect of Plaintiff’s hygiene, hydration, and repositioning to prevent pressure wounds.  Plaintiff further alleges that Defendants were aware of Plaintiff’s high risk for dehydration and pressure ulcers but consciously disregarded these risks to maximize profits.

Defendants demur, arguing that the Complaint does not allege conduct rising to the level of recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice, as required to sustain a claim for enhanced remedies under section 15657.  Instead, Defendants argue, the allegations would qualify, at most, as medical negligence, because that “the statutory definition of ‘neglect’ speaks not of the undertaking of medical services, but of the failure to provide medical care.”  (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 783 [emphasis in original].)

The Court determines that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged facts to support a claim for elder abuse.  The Complaint includes specific allegations of reckless neglect, such as Defendants’ failure to monitor Plaintiff’s hydration.  Further, the Complaint supplied further allegations that may show recklessness.  The Complaint alleges Defendant had a systemic pattern of improperly understaffing to cut costs and increase profits.  According to the Complaint, Plaintiff’s injuries were a result of the understaffing (Compl., ¶ 55), and had Defendants Royal Vista, Arlante, and Dorschner the ability to provide the requisite care to Plaintiff, Plaintiff would not have suffered these injuries (Compl, ¶¶ 67-68).  The Court must accept these allegations as true and express no opinion on whether Plaintiff can ultimately prove them. 

Defendants’ reliance on cases such as Covenant Care and Worsham is unpersuasive at this stage. While those cases emphasize the distinction between professional negligence and elder abuse, Plaintiff’s allegations of systemic understaffing and intentional cost-cutting measures go beyond negligence and suggest a pattern of reckless conduct.

The Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action (Patient’s Bill of Rights) is OVERRULED

Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ conduct described above violated Plaintiff’s rights under the Patient’s Bill of Rights.  Plaintiffs cite multiple provisions of section 72527 of title 22 of the California Code of Regulations which Defendants violated based on that conduct.  (Compl., ¶ 101 citing 22 C.C.R. § 72527(a)(1), (10), (12), and (25).)

Title 22 California Code of Regulations section 72527(a) provides that “Patients have the rights enumerated in this section and the facility shall ensure that these rights are not violated.”  The rights include the patient’s right to: “be fully informed, as evidenced by the patient’s written acknowledgement prior to or at the time of admission and during stay, of these rights and of all rules and regulations governing patient conduct” (22 C.C.R. § 72527(a)(1)); “be free from mental and physical abuse” (22 C.C.R. § 72527(a)(10)); and “be treated with consideration, respect and full recognition of dignity and individuality, including privacy in treatment and in care of personal needs” (22 C.C.R. § 72527(a)(12)).  Lastly, patients shall have other rights as specified by Health and Safety Code, Section 1599.1.  (22 C.C.R. § 72527(a)(25).)

Defendants contend the Complaint merely recites regulatory language without identifying specific conduct or causal links to Plaintiff’s injuries.  Paragraph 98 of the Complaint incorporates into the Patient’s Bill of Rights cause of action all prior allegations of fact, such as the failure to provide adequate hygiene, hydration, and pressure ulcer prevention.  The cause of action further alleges Defendant Royal Vista failed to (1) prevent mental and physical abuse, (2) provide an adequate number of qualified personnel to carry out all the functions of the facility, and (3) failed to provide Plaintiff with adequate medical care.  (Compl., ¶ 103.)  As a proximate result of these failures, Plaintiff suffered her injuries—she was immobile yet left unattended lying in bed for prolonged periods resulting in her sores (Compl., ¶ 61, 63), and Defendants’ failure to provide basic needs left her to suffer from dehydration, diaper rashes, a UTI, and a pressure sore on her foot, which required further surgery in April 2024 (Compl., ¶¶ 23-24).

The Complaint sets forth facts sufficiently detailed and specific to support an inference that each of the statutory elements of liability is satisfied.  These allegations are sufficient to state a claim under the Patient’s Bill of Rights.

Defendants’ Motion to Strike is DENIED

The Court may, in its discretion and upon a motion to strike by defendant: (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading, or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 435-436.)  The matter must appear on the face of the complaint or be subject to judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

Defendants move to strike the prayers for relief under the first and fourth causes of action on similar grounds as Defendants’ arguments in the demurrer.  Given the above discussion regarding the demurrer, the motion to strike is DENIED.
