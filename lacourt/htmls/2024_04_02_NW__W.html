DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV01054    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: W

LAURA RUANO v. JULIE GOLDBERG, et al.

 

demurrer to the fifth amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        April 2, 2024                                      Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        21VECV01054

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Luis R. Ruano and Luis Roberto Ruano II

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Laura Ruano and Thomas Monteleone

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Goldberg Decl. ¶¶7-8.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case was originally an action by Plaintiff Laura Ruano (“Laura”) against Defendants Luis Roberto Ruano II ("Luis”) and Julie Goldberg ("Goldberg”).  On August 10, 2021, Laura filed her original complaint, alleging: (1) breach of fiduciary duties as to Goldberg; (2) intentional interference with marital relations as to both Defendants; and (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress (“IIED”) as to both Defendants. 

 

On April 19, 2022, Goldberg filed her cross-complaint against Laura, alleging the following causes of action against various cross-defendants: (1) assault; (2) battery; (3) trespass to chattels; (4) false light; (5) intentional interference with contractual relations; (6) IIED; (7) negligence; (8) negligent retention and supervision of employee; (9) defamation; (10) abuse of process; (11) professional negligence; and (12) negligent infliction of emotional distress. On August 2, 2022, the court granted Ms. Ruano’s Special Motion to Strike and struck Cross-Complainants’ claim for false light, intentional interference with contractual relations, IIED, defamation, abuse of process, and NIED.     

 

On March 5, 2022, Laura filed her first amended complaint (the “FAC”) against Goldberg and Luis, alleging: (1) ejectment; (2) conversion; (3) defamation per se; and (4) NIED.  On July 8, 2022, the Court struck Laura’s FAC without prejudice on the ground that Plaintiff’s counsel had failed to meet and confer with defense counsel prior to its filing. 

 

On August 27, 2022, Laura filed her second amended complaint (the “SAC”) against Goldberg and Luis, alleging: (1) breach of fiduciary duty; (2) IIED; (3) ejectment; (4) conversion; (5) defamation per se; and (6) NIED. On December 13, 2022, the Court struck the SAC on the ground that Plaintiff’s counsel had failed to meet and confer with defense counsel prior to its filing. 

 

On March 2, 2023, Laura filed her motion for leave to file a third amended complaint (the “TAC”).  On March 28, 2023, the court granted Laura’s motion. Laura filed her TAC on April 6, 2023 for ejectment, conversion, defamation per se, and negligent infliction of emotional distress. Defendants demurred to the TAC and on October 30, 2023, Laura filed a 4AC for ejectment, conversion, and defamation per se. On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed a 5AC asserting four additional causes of action for unfair business practices, elder abuse, and civil harassment.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the Fifth Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request this court take judicial notice of several court documents (RJN Exhs. A-Q.) The court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §452(d).)

 

Plaintiff also requests judicial notice of certain documents filed with the court (RJN Exhs. A-E.) The court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §452(d).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Luis Roberto Ruano II (“Robert”) and Luis R. Ruano (“Luis”) demur to the first cause of action for ejectment, second cause of action for conversion, third cause of action for defamation per se, fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, fifth cause of action for unfair business practices, sixth cause of action for elder abuse, and seventh cause of action for civil harassment.

 

Ejectment

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action for ejectment on the grounds Laura does not hold title to the subject property and there are no other facts sufficiently pleaded to disclose a right to possession of the subject property.

 

Plaintiffs note this cause of action has already been substantively ruled on. On September 28, 2023, the court found Plaintiff’s allegations against Robert of right to possession are good against demurrer. The court did overrule the demurrer as to Luis Roberto Ruano II and sustained without leave to amend the demurrer as to Luis R. Ruano. The 5AC has removed Luis from the cause of action.

 

Accordingly, the court overrules the demurrer to the first cause of action.

 

Conversion

 

Defendants demur to the second cause of action for conversion on the grounds Plaintiffs cannot maintain a cause of action for conversion against Robert, based on the rule of exclusive, concurrent jurisdiction as Laura’s conversion claim pertains to community property. Defendants further demur on the grounds Plaintiffs have failed to state with specificity which property was converted nor did she consent either expressly or impliedly for the community property to be in the home.

 

As noted by Plaintiff, these allegations have already been substantively ruled on. Accordingly, the court overrules the demurrer to the second cause of action.

 

Defamation

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for defamation on the grounds Laura’s cause of action fails to set forth a single place, statement, or person who heard the alleged defamatory statements that were allegedly made or took place.

Defendants previously demurred to the defamation cause of action for statute of limitations purposes. Now, Defendants contend Plaintiffs’ claim for defamation is void of critical facts such as to whom the alleged communications were made, how the alleged communications were made and more importantly when those communications were made. Plaintiffs only generally allege within “one year of the filing of the complaint” the alleged statements were made.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue Defendants, as the individuals who made the defamatory statements, have complete and superior knowledge of the relevant facts making less particularity needed under California’s liberal pleading rules. (Okun v. Superior Court (1981) 29 Cal.3d 442, 458; see Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245; Pointe San Diego Residential Cmty., L.P. v. Procopio, Cory, Hargreaves & Savitch, LLP (2011) 195 Cal. App. 4th 265, 276-78.)

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient for a demurrer. As noted by Plaintiffs, “slander can be charged by alleging the substance of the defamatory statement.” (Okun v. Superior Court (1981) 29 Cal.3d 442, 458.) Here, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the substance of the defamatory statement and when it occurred – within one year of filing the complaint, Defendants told people that Plaintiff was embezzling money from her place of employment and had an extramarital affair while married to Luis. (5AC ¶10.)

 

Accordingly, the court overrules the demurrer to the third cause of action.

 

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds Plaintiffs do not allege any special relationship between the parties.

 

The elements for a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) an actual and proximate causal link between the tortious conduct and the emotional distress.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.)   

 

Defendants argue the court previously sustained Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ negligent infliction of emotional distress claim but because Plaintiffs could not allege any special relationship, she has changed her claim to her previously abandoned intentional infliction of emotional distress. However, the IIED claim still fails because there are no allegations of a special relationship or Defendants engaging in outrageous conduct.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue no special relationship is required of IIED. The court agrees. “A requirement of a special relationship does not appear in the California Supreme Court's formulation of the elements of IIED.” (Crouch v. Trinity Christian Center of Santa Ana, Inc. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 995, 1009.) As such, no special relationship is required.

 

Plaintiffs further argue the complaint sufficiently alleges outrageous conduct. The complaint alleges “Defendants’ defamatory statements about an alleged extra-marital affair were made for the sole purpose of outcasting Plaintiff from society and intentionally causing her psychological injury. Similarly, Defendants’ statements regarding Plaintiff’s purported embezzlement from her employer were intentionally made with the sole purpose of depriving Plaintiff the ability to earn income and causing her to become destitute and without financial means to pay for food, clothes and other necessities of daily life.” (5AC ¶30.) Moreover, “Defendants have repeatedly harassed and threatened Monteleone in an attempt to extort him and Plaintiff into ‘walking away’ from the dissolution proceeding pending between Plaintiff and Luis Ruano. (5AC ¶31.)

 

The court finds these allegations are sufficient to support a claim for IIED for the purposes of a demurrer. “While the outrageousness of a defendant's conduct normally presents an issue of fact to be determined by the trier of fact [Citation], the court may determine in the first instance, whether the defendant's conduct may reasonably be regarded as so extreme and outrageous as to permit recovery. [Citation.]” (Trerice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883.) However, Plaintiff has alleged Defendants essentially spread rumors about her personal life including an affair and embezzlement from her place of employment. A jury may decide the alleged intentional act of spreading gossip or rumors is extreme and outrageous which would cause someone severe and emotional distress.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Unfair Business Practices

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for unfair business practices on the grounds this cause of action is predicated upon a completely made-up attorney-client relationship which this court and the Divorce Court has already found to have never existed.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendants mischaracterize the FAC’s allegations and improperly conflates two separate individuals – Robert and Julie Goldberg – to suggest this cause of action pertains to a purported attorney-client relationship. Plaintiff states the allegations are Robert’s conduct towards Laura in providing legal services without a license to practice law.

 

The court finds the current allegations sufficient to state a claim for unfair business practices against Robert. 

 

Elder Abuse

 

Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action for elder abuse on the grounds judicial notice of property record shows Laura and Thomas sold the property and the rule of exclusive, concurrent jurisdiction of community property applies.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue Defendants are attempting to intertwine family court matters with the civil torts in this matter to confuse the issues and distract the court. Plaintiffs contend whether Luis has a community property interest in the Odyssey Property or not does not mean it is impossible for Robert and Luis to engage in elder abuse against Monteleone with respect to it or that they have not already engaged in such misconduct, as well as other financial and emotional abuse to gain an edge in the highly contentious divorce proceeding between Laura and Luis.

 

The court agrees the allegations are sufficient. As also noted by Plaintiffs, the allegations are not limited to the Odyssey Property but also extends to harassment and threats that cause Monteleone extreme emotional pain and suffering.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Civil Harassment

 

Defendants demur to the seventh cause of action for civil harassment on the grounds this cause of action appears, to be nothing more than a repeat of the allegation (regarding the service of process on Laura) against the former Defendant, Ms. Julie Goldberg, of which this court already granted an anti-SLAPP under the litigation privilege.

 

“The elements of unlawful harassment, as defined by the language in section 527.6, are as follows: (1) ‘a knowing and willful course of conduct’ entailing a ‘pattern’ of ‘a series of acts over a period of time, however short, evidencing a continuity of purpose’; (2) ‘directed at a specific person’; (3) ‘which seriously alarms, annoys, or harasses the person’; (4) ‘which serves no legitimate purpose’; (5) which ‘would cause a reasonable person to suffer substantial emotional distress’ and ‘actually cause[s] substantial emotional distress to the plaintiff’; and (6) which is not a ‘[c]onstitutionally protected activity.’  (Schild v. Rubin (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 755, 762.) 

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. The complaint alleges Defendants have repeatedly harassed and threatened Plaintiffs in an attempt to extort them into Plaintiff “walking away” from the dissolution proceeding pending between Plaintiff and Luis Ruano as well as assaulting and battering Plaintiff, and sending thugs to Plaintiffs’ place of employment to loiter, disturb the peace, and engage in conduct which terrified Plaintiffs. This is sufficient to show a pattern of conduct/series of acts.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the seventh cause of action is OVERRULED.  30 days to answer.


Case Number: 23VECV01106    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: W

JOHN THOMAS SKIDMORE v. LUIS ANGEL CORTEZ, et al.

 

motion to compel discovery

 

Date of Hearing:        April 2, 2024                                      Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01106

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff John Thomas Skidmore

Responding Party:     Defendants Verizon Wireless Services, LLC and Luis Angel Cortez

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff John Thomas Skidmore alleges he was a customer of Verizon Wireless Services. Defendant Luis Angel Cortez, an employee of Verizon, allegedly offered to reduce Plaintiff’s monthly charges on five of his phone lines; however, Defendant Cortez made unauthorized changes to Plaintiff’s account with Verizon which caused him to be overcharged $15,436.30 by opening over 60 ‘ghost lines’ in Plaintiff’s name.  

 

On March 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants for breach of contract, money had and received, and fraud.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant's Luis Angel Cortez Further Responses to Requests for Production - Set One is DENIED.

II.                  Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Verizon Wireless Services LLC, Responses to Requests for Production - Set One is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT'S LUIS ANGEL CORTEZ, FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION - SET ONE

 

Plaintiff moves this court for an order compelling Defendant Luis Angel Cortez to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production, Set One. Plaintiff also requests an order requiring Defendant to pay monetary sanctions to reimburse Plaintiff for its reasonable attorney’s fees and costs incurred in bringing this motion.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1)

 

Plaintiff argues to date, the Defendant has failed to serve any documents with respect to his promise that “Defendant will produce responsive nonprivileged documents in his possession, custody and control.”

 

In opposition, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s characterization of Defendant’s response is inaccurate. Specifically, Defendant conditioned production of documents upon the entry of an appropriate protective order, and subject to objections. Defendant further argues Plaintiff also fails to comply with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requiring that a Separate Statement set forth the “full response” to each discovery demand. Because Plaintiff omitted the phrase “after entry of an appropriate protective order”, Defendant argues the motion should be denied.

 

The court agrees the Separate Statement is not the full response as Defendant states “after entry of an appropriate protective order, Defendant will produce responsive nonprivileged documents in his possession, custody and control.” The court orders the parties to meet and confer to enter a protective order agreement. Once executed, Defendant is to produce the documents as they are relevant. 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant's Luis Angel Cortez Further Responses to Requests for Production - Set One is DENIED.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests $940.00 in sanctions against Defendant. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied. The court finds substantial justification in opposing the motion.

 

II.                  MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S VERIZON WIRELESS SERVICES LLC, RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION - SET ONE

 

Plaintiff moves this court for an order compelling Defendant Verizon Wireless Services LLC to provide responses Plaintiff’s Request for Production – Set One and an order requiring Defendant to pay monetary sanctions.

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.)

 

According to Plaintiff, they served Plaintiff’s discovery demands on July 11, 2023. (Landsman Decl. ¶5.) Defendant responded; however, in essence, Defendant refused to respond and contended that the case would be ordered to arbitration. (Landsman Decl. ¶6.) When Defendant’s motion for arbitration was denied, Defendant did not meet and confer regarding their overdue discovery responses and instead, unilaterally asserted it would respond to Plaintiff’s discovery demand at their convenience. (Landsman Decl. ¶¶9, 10.) Defendant eventually served its responses December 11, 2023. (Landsman Decl. ¶11.) However, Defendant’s response included no documents. (Landsman Decl. ¶12.) Plaintiff reached out and Defendant informed Plaintiff “We will produce documents soon, along with Verizon's verification.” (Landsman Decl. ¶14.) No further responses have been given. (Landsman Decl. ¶16.)

 

First, it is true unverified discovery responses are tantamount to no response at all and are subject to a motion to compel responses (rather than a motion to compel further responses).  (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal. App. 3d 632, 635-36.) However, objections do not need to be verified.  (See Blue Ridge Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 339, 345; Food 4 Less Supermarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 651, 657.)  Regardless, Defendant has now provided verified answers. (Ekmekchyan Decl. ¶6, Exh. D.) As for the responses that asserted a need for a protective order prior to the production of certain documents, as noted above, the parties are to work together to enter a protective order.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Verizon Wireless Services LLC, Responses to Requests for Production - Set One is DENIED.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests $940.00 in sanctions against Defendant. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied. The court finds substantial justification in opposing the motion.
