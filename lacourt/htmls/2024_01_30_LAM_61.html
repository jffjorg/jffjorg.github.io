DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 18STCV05076    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendants Sina and  Azadeh Mardani’s Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication against the Complaint of Plaintiff County of Los Angeles is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff County of Los Angeles to provide notice.

 

I.                   OBJECTIONS

Plaintiff County of Los Angeles submits objections to the declarations of Azadeh Mardani, her counsel, and Kevin Cook. Objections No. 3 and 7 are SUSTAINED in part, specifically as to Azadeh’s testimony concerning Sina Mardani’s subjective reliance on any given fact, for which she lacks basis in personal knowledge. Objections No. 11–24 are SUSTAINED in part, specifically as to the truth of the matters asserted, not as to Azadeh’s lack of knowledge of them at the time of executing the settlement agreement. The remaining objections to Azadeh Mardani Declaration are OVERRULED. Plaintiff’s objections to the Cook declaration, based largely on relevance, are OVERRULED. The objections to the declaration of Sarvena Bahar are also OVERRULED.

 

II.                SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (a).) “[I]f all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law,” the moving party will be entitled to summary judgment.  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (f)(2).)

 

The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if he does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; accord Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  The plaintiff may not rely upon the mere allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  (Ibid.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Defendants Sina and Azadeh Mardani (Defendants) move for summary judgment against the claims made by Plaintiff County of Los Angeles (Plaintiff) in the consolidated Complaint filed on November 6, 2019, in LASC Case No. 19STCV35515. Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s claims arise from a settlement agreement executed in December 2013, nominally requiring them to install a backwater valve on their private sewer line. But they argue that this agreement is unenforceable, as the requirement to install a backwater valve is implicitly dependent upon said installation being within the $500 allowance included in the settlement agreement. (Motion at pp. 9–14.) Defendants also argue that Plaintiff cannot establish the requirements for specific performance, because the $500 allowance was not adequate consideration for the requirement of installation, and requiring Defendants to install the valve at present would cost them upwards of $50,000.00, rendering the consideration inadequate and the requirement unreasonable as applied to them. (Motion at pp. 14–17.) Defendants also argue that Plaintiff’s claims for breach of contract are barred by the four-year statute of limitations, since Defendants were obligated under the contract to install the valve by June 2014, and Plaintiff filed the claim in 2019. (Motion at pp. 17–18.) Defendants argue that the settlement agreement was void at the time of its formation, as it contravened existing law allowing public entities to be held strictly liable for sewage overflows. (Motion at pp. 18–19.) Defendants finally argue that the contract claims are barred by the doctrine of equitable estoppel and unclean hands. (Motion at pp. 19–20.)

 

The interpretation of a contract involves “a two-step process: ‘First the court provisionally receives (without actually admitting) all credible evidence concerning the parties' intentions to determine “ambiguity,” i.e., whether the language is “reasonably susceptible” to the interpretation urged by a party. If in light of the extrinsic evidence the court decides the language is “reasonably susceptible” to the interpretation urged, the extrinsic evidence is then admitted to aid in the second step—interpreting the contract. [Citation.]’ (Winet v. Price (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 1159, 1165, 6 Cal.Rptr.2d 554.) The trial court's determination of whether an ambiguity exists is a question of law, subject to independent review on appeal. (Ibid.) The trial court's resolution of an ambiguity is also a question of law if no parol evidence is admitted or if the parol evidence is not in conflict. However, where the parol evidence is in conflict, the trial court's resolution of that conflict is a question of fact and must be upheld if supported by substantial evidence. (Id. at p. 1166, 6 Cal.Rptr.2d 554.) Furthermore, ‘[w]hen two equally plausible interpretations of the language of a contract may be made ... parol evidence is admissible to aid in interpreting the agreement, thereby presenting a question of fact which precludes summary judgment if the evidence is contradictory.’

(Wolf v. Superior Court (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1343, 1351.)

 

Defendants’ arguments as to the interpretation of the contract are as follows. The settlement agreement’s requirement for installation of a backwater valve is included in paragraph 3.1, which states, “This Release is executed with the full understanding that Claimants agree to install a backwater valve on their private sewer lateral line as a corrective action measure.” (Complaint Exh. 1.) Defendants refer to a statement of loss, sent by the claims manager to Defendants, indicating that of the settlement’s $29,511.39 total payment, $28,861.39 was to pay vendors employed in rectifying the sewer overflow and directly compensating Plaintiff. (Motion Exh. 26.) The claims manager sent county counsel a letter prior to the parties’ execution of the agreement that the extra $500 was an “allowance toward a backwater valve.” (Motion Exh. 25.)

 

Defendants thus argue that the covenant to install a backwater valve is an “independent covenant,” incidental to the overall settlement agreement. (Motion at p. 11.) Defendants argue that their installation of the valve was conditioned on their doing so “in accordance with all applicable provisions of the Plumbing Code.” (Motion at p. 12; Complaint Exh. 1.) And their obligation was conditional upon their ability to do so within the limits of the $500 allowance. (Motion at p. 12.) Because the evidence shows that installing a Plumbing Code-compliant backwater valve would cost them in excess of $50,000 (Cook Decl. ¶ 13), they argue that they are not bound to install such an agreement. (Motion at pp. 12–13.)

 

This argument is unpersuasive. The settlement agreement does not specifically earmark $500 as consideration for the backwater valve. Rather, the agreement states that the whole of the $29,511.39 sum payable constitutes “valuable consideration” for Defendants’ “release and . . . discharge” of their claims. (Complaint Exh. 1.) The paragraph describing the backwater valve states that Defendants execute the release “with the full understanding that Claimants agree to install a backwater valve.” (Complaint Exh. 1.) The installation is therefore a part of Defendants’ overall agreement to dismiss their claims. And even if the $500 allowance were to be considered an “independent covenant” whose sole consideration was Defendants’ obligation to install a backwater valve (Colaco v. Cavotec SA (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 1172, 1183), Defendants received the $500. There is no indication in the contract’s text, the statement of loss, or surrounding communications that their obligation to do so was contingent on Defendants’ ability to accomplish installation for less than the $500 received.

Defendants argue that the settlement agreement does not represent a meeting of the minds, since the agreement does not specify a deadline to install a backwater valve, does not state what the Plumbing Code requires, and does not state how the $500 was calculated. This argument is also unpersuasive. “If no time is specified for the performance of an act required to be performed, a reasonable time is allowed.” (Civ. Code § 1657.)[1] Defendants present no authority for the proposition that the parties’ subjective ignorance as to the applicable provisions of the Plumbing Code or the manner in which the $500 was calculated are grounds for invalidating the agreement.

 

Defendants next argue that Plaintiff cannot prevail on its claim for specific performance — specifically requiring Defendants to install a backwater valve — because they did not receive adequate consideration for the requirement to install the backwater valve, because enforcing that requirement against them here is unfair and unreasonable, and because Defendant misrepresented their knowledge of the status of the sewer main line on their property before they signed the agreement. (Motion at pp. 14–17.)

 

“A claim for specific performance is an equitable one.” (Benach v. County of Los Angeles (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 836, 846.) “To obtain specific performance after a breach of contract, a plaintiff must generally show: “(1) the inadequacy of his legal remedy; (2) an underlying contract that is both reasonable and supported by adequate consideration; (3) the existence of a mutuality of remedies; (4) contractual terms which are sufficiently definite to enable the court to know what it is to enforce; and (5) a substantial similarity of the requested performance to that promised in the contract.” (Real Estate Analytics, LLC v. Vallas (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 463, 472.)

 

Civil Code § 3391 states that “[s]pecific performance cannot be enforced against a party to a contract in any of the following cases:”

 

1. If he has not received an adequate consideration for the contract;

 

2. If it is not, as to him, just and reasonable;

 

3. If his assent was obtained by the misrepresentation, concealment, circumvention, or unfair practices of any party to whom performance would become due under the contract, or by any promise of such party which has not been substantially fulfilled; or;

 

4. If his assent was given under the influence of mistake, misapprehension, or surprise, except that where the contract provides for compensation in case of mistake, a mistake within the scope of such provision may be compensated for, and the contract specifically enforced in other respects, if proper to be so enforced.

 

(Civ. Code § 3391.)

 

Defendants argue once more that the $500 allowance was not adequate consideration for the requirement to install the backwater valve, which they estimate will cost more that $50,000.00. (Motion at p. 14.) They argue that, for the same reasons, the contract is not fair and reasonably applied to them here. (Motion at p. 15.)

 

Defendants’ arguments concerning the adequacy of consideration and the reasonableness of the requirement to install a backwater valve do not require judgment as a matter of law. This is because Defendants’ arguments as to the reasonableness of the requirement address only the $500 allowance weighed against the cost of the valve’s installation, rather than the probable value of the valve’s installation. The estimated $55,000 cost would represent a pure loss to Defendants, assuming the truth of Defendants’ argument that a backwater valve would not have prevented any of the sewage overflows that thereafter occurred. (Separate Statement No. 72.) But Plaintiff disputes this contention, reasoning that a backwater valve would have prevented those parts of the sewage overflows that occurred on the interior of Defendants’ dwelling. (Plaintiff’s Responsive Separate Statement to Undisputed Material Facts (PUMF) No. 72–74.) The contract at issue expressly frames the installation of the backwater valve as a “corrective action measure.” (Complaint Exh. 1.) The adequacy of consideration and reasonableness of the requirement’s application is appropriately measured, not merely by the cost of its installation, but by the probable damage that any such installation would have prevented. As triable issues exist on that fact, these arguments do not support summary adjudication.

 

Moreover, triable issues remain as to whether the settlement was procured by concealment or misrepresentation. Defendants present evidence that after the June 2013 sewage overflow, County engineer Robert Swartz belatedly reviewed video of a televised inspection of the main line taken by contractors in 2010. PUMF No. 41.) The County did not conduct a new televisual inspection after the June 2013 overflow, as its policies require. (PUMF No. 34–35, 39.) Swartz’s review of the earlier video revealed a sag in the main sewer line filled to a large extent with water, which prevented further visual inquiry. (PUMF No. 41.) Swartz did not order a further inquiry, but instead confirmed the suspected cause relayed by the crew leader on the scene of the overflow, who informed Defendants that the cause of the overflow was blockage by tree roots. (PUMF No. 38, 48, Azadeh Decl. ¶ 3.) Although the crew leader who responded to the later 2018 overflow testified that a sag in the line increased the likelihood of overflow (PUMF No. 76), Swartz testified that sags are not necessarily structural defects, and that the line as reviewed was allowing wastewater to pass at appropriate velocities. (Motion Exh. 31 at pp. 159, 177–178.) Swartz testified at deposition, however, that the prior maintenance rating for the line should have been given a structural rating, warranting further evaluation. (Motion Exh. 31 at pp. 166–167.) Maintenance superintendent Paul Bradford testified that his search of maintenance history revealed no prior overflows on the property. (PUMF No. 4.)  Defendants contend that the records reveal three overflows on the property prior to 2008, but the parties dispute the interpretation of these records. (PUMF No. 5; Bahar Decl. 31–40, Exhs. 38, 39.) The corrective action that Plaintiff implemented after the 2013 overflow consisted of an expedited cleaning schedule for the line. (PUMF No. 9.) Based on the above, triable issues exist as to whether Plaintiff concealed any material information from Defendants, as Swartz disputes the materiality of the sag.

 

Defendants next argue that the statute of limitations bars Plaintiff’s claim, because more than four years elapsed between the payment of the settlement funds in 2014 and Plaintiff’s filing of the operative complaint in 2019. (Motion at pp. 17–18; Code Civ. Proc. § 337.) But “[t]he statute of limitations for a breach of contract claim begins to run at the time of breach (that is, when one party fails to perform as contractually required).” (Piedmont Capital Management, L.L.C. v. McElfish (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 961, 964.) Here, because the contract specified no deadline in which to install the backwater valve, the time to perform was any “reasonable time.” (Civil Coe § 1657.) Plaintiff does not allege to the contrary in the Complaint. (Complaint ¶ 24.) In the absence of facts showing what a reasonable time constituted, the limitations argument is unpersuasive.

Defendants’ argument as to the contract being void for containing unlawful provisions is also unpersuasive. Defendants rely on case authority holding for the proposition that public entities are strictly liable for the damages proximately caused by overflowing sewer mains, in effect at the time of the 2013 settlement. (Motion at p. 18, citing California State Automobile Assn. v. City of Palo Alto (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 474, 479 [41 Cal.Rptr.3d 503, 506] disapproved of by City of Oroville v. Superior Court (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1091.) But this authority did not hold for the proposition that public entities cannot settle inverse condemnation claims, or that plaintiffs bringing such claims cannot bind themselves by contract to mitigating measures.

Defendants’ final arguments as to equitable estoppel and unclean hands rely on the same facts as their argument addressed to misrepresentation, and warrant the same result. The elements of equitable estoppel are: “(1) The party to be estopped must know the facts; (2) he must intend that his conduct shall be acted upon, or must so act that the party asserting the estoppel had the right to believe that it was so intended; (3) the party asserting the estoppel must be ignorant of the true state of facts; and (4) he must rely upon the conduct to his injury.” (Migliore v. Mid-Century Ins. Co. (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 592, 606.) Although Defendants argue that the County should have disclosed the true cause of the sewer overflow and the “seriously compromised condition of the sewer line,” triable issues exist as to whether the sewer line was seriously compromised at that time, or that the County had knowledge of such defects. Triable issues thus also exist as to whether Plaintiff’s failure to disclose this information was inequitable or done in the absence of good faith, as would be necessary to invoke the doctrine of unclean hands. (Meridian Financial Services, Inc. v. Phan (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 657, 685.)

The motion is therefore DENIED.

Defendants’ assertion in the present motion and reply papers that Plaintiff is bound to a contractual theory requiring instantaneous installation of the backwater valve upon signing the settlement agreement is based on a strained reading of the Complaint (Reply at pp. 11–12.)

Plaintiff FML Management Corp.’s Motion for Attorney Fees After Trial is GRANTED in the amount of $2,000.00.

 

I.                   MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

“Except as attorney's fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties; but parties to actions or proceedings are entitled to their costs, as hereinafter provided.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1021.)

 

“In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).) “[T]he party prevailing on the contract shall be the party who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (b)(1).)

 

In determining the proper amount of fees to award, courts use the lodestar method.  The lodestar figure is calculated by multiplying the total number of reasonable hours expended by the reasonable hourly rate.  “Fundamental to its determination . . . [is] a careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney . . . in the presentation of the case.”  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48 (Serrano III).)  A reasonable hourly rate must reflect the skill and experience of the attorney.  (Id. at p. 49.)  “Prevailing parties are compensated for hours reasonably spent on fee-related issues.  A fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether.”  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635 (Serrano IV).)  The Court in Serrano IV also stated that fees associated with preparing the motion to recover attorneys’ fees are recoverable.  (See id. at p. 624.)

Plaintiff FML Management Corp. (Plaintiff) seeks $2,000.00 in attorney fees from Defendants Jose D. Chinchilla and Marta J. Villegas (Defendants), following trial and judgment in Plaintiff’s favor. Plaintiff relies on a provision in the lease requiring Defendants to pay any actual attorney fees incurred in any actions arising from a breach of the lease agreement. (Ehrich Decl.,Exh. 1, ¶ 18.) Plaintiff’s counsel charged a flat fee of $2,000.00 for prosecuting the present action. (Ehrich Decl. ¶ 4.) The actual compensable hours spent on this matter, though difficult to disentangle from time spent on other matters, would yield a figure greater than $2,000.00, according to Plaintiff’s counsel. (Ehrich Decl. ¶ 5; Feffer Decl. ¶¶ 7–12.)

Plaintiff has shown entitlement to fees under the lease agreement, and further that the $2,000.00 sought here was actually incurred and reasonable. No opposition to the motion has been filed.

The motion is therefore GRANTED.

I.                   MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

The court may impose terminating sanctions, include an order striking pleadings, and order dismissing an action, or an order rendering judgment by default against a party, for conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.) This conduct include “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.)

Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.  (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.)  Dismissal is a drastic measure, and terminating sanctions should only be ordered when there has been previous noncompliance with a rule or order and it appears a less severe sanction would not be effective.  (Link v. Cater (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1326.)  “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.”  (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.)

Plaintiff FML Management moves for terminating, issue, or evidentiary sanctions against Cross-Complainant Ninfa Cruz. Plaintiff argues that Defendant has failed to comply with this court’s order. (Ebel Decl. ¶¶ 3–4.) Plaintiff seeks an order of terminating sanctions, or alternatively an order preventing Defendant from presenting any facts, persons, or damages in relation to their claims. (Motion at p. 7.) Alternatively, Plaintiff seeks $1,460.00 in monetary sanctions, representing seven hours of attorney work at $200 per hour plus a $60 filing fee. (Ebel Decl. ¶ 5.)

Plaintiff has demonstrated that Defendant failed to comply with this court’s prior discovery order. Defendant has failed to file an opposition to this motion.

The motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED.

Case Number: 23STCP03573    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 61

Petitioner The Royalton Drive Trust’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is GRANTED.

 

Petitioner to provide notice.

 

I.       MOTION TO CONFIRM ARBITRATION AWARD

Petitioner The Royalton Drive Trust (Petitioner) seeks to confirm a $2,233,079.70 arbitration award entered against Respondent Kolin Klein Construction.

 

A.    CONTENTS OF THE PETITION

“Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award. The petition shall name as respondents all parties to the arbitration and may name as respondents any other persons bound by the arbitration award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.) A petition must include (a) the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement, (b) the names of the arbitrators, and (c) a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4.)

The petition here includes the arbitration agreement, the name of the arbitrator (Hon. Anita Rae Shapiro), and a copy of the award.

B.     Service

“No petition may be served and filed under this chapter until at least 10 days after service of the signed copy of the award upon the petitioner.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1288.4.) “A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4, subd. (a).) 

Subdivision (b) provides:

If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision:

(1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.

(2) Service outside this State shall be made by mailing the copy of the petition and notice and other papers by registered or certified mail. Personal service is the equivalent of such service by mail. Proof of service by mail shall be made by affidavit showing such mailing together with the return receipt of the United States Post Office bearing the signature of the person on whom service was made. Notwithstanding any other provision of this title, if service is made in the manner provided in this paragraph, the petition may not be heard until at least 30 days after the date of such service.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4, subd. (b).)

Here, Petitioner served the petition by personal service upon Respondent. No opposition to the petition has been filed.

The petition is therefore GRANTED.


Case Number: BC692058    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendant Permacity Construction Corp.’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED in the amount of $18,660.90., leaving a total costs award of $49,435.97.

 

I.                   MOTION TO TAX COSTS

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(a)(4).” (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1700, subd. (b)(1).)

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (b) [], guarantees prevailing parties in civil litigation awards of the costs expended in the litigation: ‘Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.’” (Williams v. Chino Valley Independent Fire Dist. (“Williams”) (2015) 61 Cal.4th 97, 100.).

 

“If the items on a verified cost bill appear proper charges, they are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred.” (Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Service, Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.) Although individual cost items are ordinarily challenged by a motion to tax costs, no cost-item is effectively put in issue by “mere statements” claiming them to be unreasonable. (Ibid.) However, where “it cannot be determined from the face of the cost bill whether the items are proper,” “the mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a ‘proper objection’ to an item.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131, 132.)

 

Prevailing Plaintiffs Daniel Sherlock and Jason Blaylock (Plaintiffs) have filed a memorandum of costs seeking $68,096.87 in costs. Defendant Permacity Construction Corp. (Defendant) has filed a motion to tax these costs, reasoning as follows. Eight other defendants were dismissed or settled out of this case prior to trial, and the costs sought must be apportioned such that Defendant bears only those reasonably necessary to prosecute the action against itself. (Motion at pp. 2–3.) Defendant identifies the following specific costs which should be taxed:

 

·         $456.66, representing seven-eighths of the initial filing fee for the complaint;

·         $254.40 for unrelated Doe amendments and ex parte applications;

·         $1,387.65 for motions to compel that were ultimately taken off-calendar;

·         $8,692.20 in deposition costs related to other defendants;

·         $3,775.78 in deposition costs for witnesses who never testified at trial;

·         $1,900.25 for service of process for witnesses who did not appear at trial;

·         $16,506.25 for court reporter fees the parties agreed to share, including $3,468.75 for reporter transcripts not ordered by the court, and $442 for a date on which no hearing was held;

·         $8,728.59 in other costs, including parking fees, mileage fees, courtcall costs, and mediation fees.

 

(Motion at pp. 4–7.)

 

Defendant is correct that the costs incurred in a multi-party action ought to be apportioned, so far as possible, between costs related to dismissed or settling defendants and those defendants against whom plaintiffs have ultimately prevailed. (Heppler v. J.M. Peters Co. (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1265, 1297.) Costs need not be apportioned between multiple parties, however, where the issues are “inextricably intertwined.” (Id. at p. 1297.) Here, the costs sought are sufficiently specific to allow Defendant to object to particular costs that were incurred in prosecuting claims for other defendants.  

Costs related solely to other defendants include $254.40 in filing fees for unrelated Doe amendments and ex parte applications.  Plaintiffs argue that $8,692.20 in deposition costs incurred for each defendant are recoverable against all of them, as each defendant tried to limit their scope of liability by apportioning fault to the other. (Opposition at pp. 6–7.) This argument is persuasive. Further, Defendant is not entitled to taxing of the costs associated with the filing of the complaint, since those same filing fees would have been incurred had Plaintiffs filed suit against Defendant alone.

Defendant is correct that Plaintiff is not entitled to court reporter fees, as the parties agreed to share these costs without express provision for subsequent reimbursement to the prevailing party. (Motion at pp. 5–6.) “[W]here, as in this case, the parties agree to share costs during litigation, the courts will enforce those agreements as written under the principles that when the language of a document is unambiguous, we are not free to restructure the agreement, and if the parties wanted to allow recovery of the apportioned fee by the prevailing party as an item of cost, they were free to spell this out in their agreement,” but such a provision will not be read into the agreement.” (Anthony v. Xiaobin Li (2020) 47 Cal.App.5th 816, 824, internal quotation marks and alterations omitted.) Here, the parties each “agree[d] to pay half of the court reporter costs,” without provision for subsequent recovery via costs memorandum. (Motion Exh. I.) These fees are properly taxed.

The $1,900.25 for service of process fees are also properly taxed, as they were incurred for subpoenas to witnesses who did not appear at trial. Plaintiff’s case for the inclusion of the costs is because Defendant objected to the admissibility of a report authored by the witnesses so subpoenaed. (Opposition at p. 8, Exh. Q.) This evidence suggests that Plaintiffs’ efforts to obtain the testimony of these witnesses was belated, and ultimately rendered unnecessary by the testimony of others at trial. (Opposition at p. 8.)

Plaintiffs’ remaining costs are properly preserved, however. The costs sought in relation to the motions to compel are proper, as the fees were taken off-calendar after Defendant provided tardy responses, after the motions were filed. Plaintiff’s argument concerning the $3,775.38 for deposition costs related to Defendant’s witnesses who never testified is also persuasive, as the costs were incurred after Defendant changed its person most knowledgeable, or else incurred in a deposition taken by Defendant. (Opposition at pp. 9–10.) Plaintiffs’ $8,728.59 in other costs was also reasonably incurred, and is supported by the declaration of counsel. (Bederman Decl. ¶¶ 24–25, Exh. R.)

The motion to tax costs is therefore GRANTED in the amount of $18,660.90, leaving a total costs award of $49,435.97.
