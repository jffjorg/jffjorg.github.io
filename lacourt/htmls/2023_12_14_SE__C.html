DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22NWCV00150    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: C

LORRAINE L. COTA v. MARCO ANTONIO MARTIN aka TONY MARTIN, ET AL.

CASE NO.:  22NWCV00150

HEARING:  12/14/23 @ 9:30 a.m.

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

     I.        Specially Appearing Defendant Excellence RE Real Estate. Inc.’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

 

    II.        Plaintiff Lorraine Cota’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

 

  III.        Plaintiff Lorraine Cota’s Application for Writ of Attachment is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice. 

 

 

Background

 

On March 3, 2022, Plaintiff Lorraine Cota filed a complaint against Defendants Marco Antonio Martin (“Martin”), 365 Kapital, LLC (“365”), My Rights Inc. (“MRI”), Rinaldo Rios (“Rios”), Excellence RE Real Estate. Inc. (“ERRE”), and Does 1-50, inclusive, alleging causes of action for 1) financial elder abuse, 2) breach of fiduciary duty, 3) actual fraud, 4) constructive fraud, 5) conversion, 6) recovery of payment from unlicensed contractor, and 7) money had and received.

 

Default was entered against all parties on April 15, 2022.

 

On September 20, 2023, Rios filed a Verified Answer to the Complaint.

 

On September 25, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to strike the verified answer on the grounds that it was filed after default had been properly entered against Rios.   

 

On October 3, 2023, the Court denied ERRE’s motion to set aside entry of default entered against ERRE on April 15, 2022.

 

On October 12, 2023, ERRE moved for reconsideration of the order. On November 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed an opposition to ERRE’s motion along with a request for judicial notice. To date, ERRE did not file a reply.

 

On November 27, 2023, the Court denied without prejudice Plaintiff’s ex parte application for writ of attachment. In the November 27, 2023 minute order, the Court ruled that the ex parte application be deemed the moving papers. To date, no opposition or reply papers have been filed.

 

On December 7, 2023, Rios filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion to strike Rios’s Verified Answer. On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

Analysis

 

1) Motion for Reconsideration

 

Specially appearing Defendant Excellence RE Real Estate. Inc. (“ERRE”) moves pursuant to CCP § 1008 for reconsideration.

 

ERRE requests the Court reconsider its October 3, 2023 order wherein the Court denied ERRE’s motion to set aside the April 15, 2022 entry of default. In its motion to set aside, ERRE argued that it was served by substituted service rather than personal service and therefore that default was prematurely entered before the time allowed for responding to the complaint expired. The Court disagreed, finding that Defendant Reynaldo Rios was a person authorized to accept service on behalf of ERRE, as Rios was the person in charge of ERRE’s Pico Rivera office. The Court found that “it is undisputed that Rios was employed by [ERRE], he was in charge of the Pico Rivera office, and he is a co-defendant in this lawsuit. From a practical standpoint, these factors make it “highly probable that [ERRE] would receive actual notice.” (10.03.23 Minute Order, p. 4.)

 

ERRE now contends that Rios was not at ERRE’s office on the day Plaintiff contends the summons and complaint were served on Rios. (Rios Decl., para. 4.)  ERRE attaches the Declaration of Christina Nunez, an Executive Assistant for ERRE, wherein Nunez states that she keeps a sign in sheet at the office that does not indicate that Rios was present on March 14, 2022 to accept service. (Nunez Decl., para. 3, Exh. 3.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that the motion must be denied because 1) a motion for reconsideration must be based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law and ERRE’s request is based on facts known at the time of the October 3, 2023 order, and 2) ERRE has not shown a right to relief from default.

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff. Though ERRE argues for the first time that Rios never received actual service, the facts underlying this contention are not new and have in fact existed dating back to before the hearing on ERRE’s motion to set aside. Thus, they are not sufficient to grant a motion for reconsideration.

 

To be entitled to reconsideration, the moving party must show that “(1) evidence of new or different facts exist, and (2) the party has a satisfactory explanation for failing to produce such evidence at an earlier time. [Citation.]” (Kalivas v. Barry Controls Corp. (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1152, 1160-61.) The burden under § 1008 “is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.”¿ (Ibid.; Even Zohar Construction & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 833 [finding that § 1008 imposes the special requirement of having to not only show new or different facts, circumstances, or law, but also to “show diligence with a satisfactory explanation for not presenting the new or different information earlier…”].)¿

 

Here, ERRE has not met the high burden of demonstrating it was diligent in searching for all supporting evidence available to it at the time of the set aside hearing. Accordingly, ERRE’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

 

2) Motion to Strike

 

Plaintiff Lorraine Cota moves pursuant to CCP §§ 435 and 436(b) to strike Defendant Reynaldo Rios’s Verified Answer to the Complaint as it was filed after default had been entered against Rios.

 

Rios, the broker for the sale of the property that is the subject of the Complaint, was served personally with a copy of the summons and complaint at Defendant ERRE’s office building on March 14, 2022. After Rios failed to respond, Plaintiff filed a request for entry of default on April 15, 2022. Judgment was entered that same date against all Defendants. Then, on August 7, 2023, Rios was served with a copy of an amended summons and complaint correcting the spelling of Rios’s first name, which was initially spelled “Rinaldo”. Subsequently, on September 20, 2023, Rios filed a Verified Answer to the Complaint.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to meet and confer with Rios prior to filing this motion to strike but Rios, who filed the Verified Answer in pro per, only responded that he would talk with his attorney about this motion. Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

CCP § 435 allows a party to file a motion to strike to a whole pleading or any part thereof. CCP § 436(b) allows a court to strike out all or any part of any pleading that is not drawn in conformity with the laws of the state. “The entry of a default terminates a defendant's rights to take any further affirmative steps in the¿litigation until either its default is set aside or a default judgment is entered.¿[Citations]¿‘A defendant against whom a default has been entered is out of court and is not entitled to take any further steps in the cause affecting plaintiff's right¿of action; he cannot thereafter, until such default is set aside in a proper proceeding, file pleadings or move for a new trial or demand notice of subsequent proceedings.’¿[Citation]”¿(Devlin v. Kearny Mesa AMC/Jeep/Renault, Inc.¿(1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 381, 385-386.)¿

 

Plaintiff contends that she is entitled to an order striking Rios’s Verified Answer because it was filed after a proper entry of default. In opposition, Rios contends for the first time that service of the Complaint was improper. Without considering the merits of this contention, the Court finds that Rios’s arguments are improperly placed in the opposition to Plaintiff’s motion to strike. Instead, if Rios intends to undo the entry of default, Rios must bring a separate motion to set aside the entry of default. Thus, Plaintiff’s motion to strike Rios’s Verified Answer is GRANTED.

 

3) Writ of Attachment

 

Plaintiff seeks to attach the following property to secure $918,542.55: 3811 Marber Avenue, Long Beach, CA 90808, Michelle Denise Toscano. The legal description of the property is Lot 16 of Tract No. l3001, in the City of Long Beach, County of Los Angeles, State of California, as per Map recorded in Book 248, Page 8 through 10 inclusive of Maps, in the office of the County Recorder of said County.

 

An application for writ of possession must be executed under oath and include: 

 

·        A “showing of the basis of the plaintiff’s claim and that the plaintiff is entitled to possession of the property claimed,” 

·        A copy of the written instrument if the basis of the claim is a written instrument, 

·        A “showing that the property is wrongfully detained by the defendant,” 

·        A showing of “the manner in which the defendant came into possession of the property,” 

·        A showing of “the reason for the detention,” 

·        A particular description of the property and its value, 

·        A statement of the location of the property, including a showing that there is probable cause to believe the property is located there if alleged to be within a private place, and 

·        A “statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine” or “seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff[.]” 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 512.010, subd. (b).) 

 

The court shall issue a writ of possession if, after considering pleadings and other papers in the record—in addition to other evidence that may be produced at the hearing—it finds the plaintiff has “established the probable validity of” its claim to possession of the property and satisfied the undertaking requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 515.010. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 512.050 & 512.060, subd. (a).) Code of Civil Procedure section 515.010 requires the plaintiff to file an undertaking of at least “twice the value of the defendant’s interest in the property” with the court before the court issues any writ of possession, unless the court finds the defendants have “no interest in the property.” (Id., § 515.010, subds. (a) & (b).) 

 

Here, Plaintiff demonstrates the basis of its claims and that he is entitled to possession of the property at issue. The property that is the subject of the writ of attachment is owned by Defendant Toscano. Toscano, who is believed to be Defendant Martin’s girlfriend, was a participant in the scheme to defraud Plaintiff of her finances, having been the recipient of funds Martin claimed were owed to him under the alleged contract for home improvement services. Martin directed payments for those services and there is evidence of a wire transfer of $65,000 to Toscano. Toscano shortly thereafter made a purchase of the Marber Property from Defendant 365, one of Martin’s business entities in which he has 100% ownership.

 

Contingent on Plaintiff filing an undertaking of twice Tuscano’s interest in the property, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s Writ of Attachment is GRANTED.

 

Conclusion

 

1) Defendant ERRE’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

2) Plaintiff Lorraine Cota’s motion to strike Defendant Rios’s Verified Answer is GRANTED.

3) Plaintiff Lorraine Cota’s application for writ of attachment is GRANTED.

Plaintiff Elizabeth A. Velsaco’s motion for summary adjudication:

This action arises from alleged legal malpractice. Judicial Notice

Legal Standard

 

“A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.”  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.”  (Ibid.) “[A] demurrer accepts as true all well pleaded facts and those facts of which the court can take judicial notice but not deductions, contentions, or conclusions of law or fact.” (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations plead to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) 

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245). 

 

 

The Orange County Action

The trial date in the action against America First Credit Union has been vacated and an OSC re: Dismissal in the Orange County action is scheduled for March 11, 2024 in the action against America First Credit Union. (Id., Exhibit B.) The Court ordered terms of settlement to be completed on or before January 9, 2024. (Id.)

 

Given that the action against America First Credit Union is still pending[1] and a dismissal has not been entered in such action, the Court finds that a continuance of the hearing on Moving Defendants’ demurrer is appropriate. As was stated in the Court’s August 24, 2023 Minute Order, part of Plaintiff’s claims in the instant action arise from Defendants’ alleged malpractice pertaining to Plaintiff’s action against America First Credit Union.

 

Therefore, the Court CONTINUES the hearing on Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the complaint to Thursday, January 11, 2024 at 9:30 AM in this department.

 


[1] The memorandum of points and authorities in support of the demurrer asserts that the “the Plaintiff’s malpractice action is not ripe as the underlying case OC Case is still pending.” (Demurrer at 5:6-8.) Case Number: 22STCV29276    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: C

KHACHIKYAN, et al. v. PLATINUM TRANSPORTION GROUP, INC., et al.

CASE NO.:  22STCV29276 (r/w 23NWCV03161 and 23NWCV03263)

HEARING:  12/14/23 @ 10:30 a.m.

 

#7

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

     I.        Defendants Platinum Transportation Group Inc. and Worldwide Transpiration Group, Inc.’s Motion to Consolidate is GRANTED.

 

    II.        Plaintiff-in-Intervention Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Company’s Motion to Sever is GRANTED

 

  III.        Plaintiff-in-Intervention Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Company’s Motion to Reclassify is DENIED. 

 

Defendants to give notice.

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs Ani Khachikyan, Elah Kaseblyan, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Ani Khachikyan; and Leah Kaseblyan, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Ani Khachikyan, (collectively, the Khachikyan Plaintiffs), filed this action against defendants Platinum Transportation Group, Inc. and Miguel Angel Pacheco for damages arising from a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs allege the accident was caused by the Defendants' employee, Miguel Angel Pacheco, while he was transporting Plaintiff and two of her minor daughters, along with two other mothers and their children, in Defendants' common carrier 2021 Mercedes Sprinter van. The complaint alleges claims for (1) motor vehicle negligence, (2) general negligence, and (3) negligent hiring/training/supervision/retention.

 

On April 5, 2023, the Khachikyan Plaintiffs filed an Amendment to Complaint adding Worldwide Transportation Group, Inc. as a named defendant.

 

On October 4, 2023, in a separate action (23NWCV03161), Plaintiff Vincent Sarni (Sarni) also filed a complaint against Defendants Platinum Transportation Group, Inc. and Miguel Angel Pacheco based on the same accident, alleging causes of action for (1) motor vehicle negligence, and (2) general negligence.

 

Similarly, on October 12, 2023, in a different case (23NWCV03263), Plaintiffs Karine Kirakanyan and Madelina Tatevosian filed a complaint against Defendants Platinum Transportation Group, Inc., Worldwide Transportation Group, Inc., and Miguel Angel Pacheco based upon the same accident, asserting claims for (1) motor vehicle negligence, and (2) general negligence.

 

On November 27, 2023, the Court found that cases 22STCV29276, 23NWCV03161, and 23NWCV03263 are related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). Case no. 22STCV29276 was designated the lead case.

 

Now, Defendants Platinum Transportation Group Inc. and Worldwide Transporation Group, Inc. (hereinafter, Moving Defendants”) move to consolidate these related cases. Plaintiff-in-Intervention Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Company move to sever and reclassify the claims by Minor Plaintiffs.  The Khachikyan Plaintiffs oppose each of these motions. 

 

 

I.             Motion to Consolidate Related Cases

 

Legal Standard

 

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1048, subd. (a).)  The purpose of consolidation is to enhance trial court efficiency by avoiding unnecessary duplication of evidence and the danger of inconsistent adjudications.  (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978-979.) 

 

A Notice of Motion to consolidate cases must (1) include a list of all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record; (2) include the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated; and (3) be filed in each case sought to be consolidated.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(1).)   

 

Procedural Compliance

 

As a preliminary matter, the instant motion is not procedurally proper pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.350 because the notice of motion was not filed in the related cases. However, this defect is minor and shall be excused because all parties have been served. (Motion, Proof of Service.) Therefore, the Court shall consider the instant motion on its merits.

 

Merits

 

The Moving Defendants move to consolidate case 22STCV29276 with related cases 23NWCV03161 and 23NWCV03263, arguing that all three actions involve common issues of law and fact in so far as each action arises out of the same multi-vehicle accident that occurred on May 7, 2022 and involves similar theories of liability. (Motion at pp. 6-7.) The Moving Defendants further assert that consolidation of these actions would promote judicial economy by avoiding the duplication of procedures and unnecessary costs and delays. (Id. at pg. 7.) Consequently, they reason that no party would be prejudiced if both actions were consolidated. (Ibid.)

 

In opposition, the Khachikyan Plaintiffs oppose the instant motion on the ground that consolidating the various cases would prejudice the minor plaintiff’s trial preference rights. (Opposition at pg. 4.)

 

In reply, the Moving Defendants contend that the issue regarding the minor plaintiff’s trial preference will be resolved by the Plaintiff-in-Intervention’s motion to sever the minor’s claims from the claims of the mother. (Reply at pg. 3.) Regardless, even if that motion is not granted, the Moving Defendants reason that consolidation would still be proper because the minor plaintiffs’ injuries are insignificant compared to their mother’s. (Id. at pp. 3-4.)

 

Under the circumstances, the Court is inclined to grant the motion to consolidate. It would be an inefficient use of judicial resources to have three separate trials proceeding at different times when they all involve common issues of law and fact. Notably, the Khachikyan Plaintiffs’ opposition does not contest this issue.

 

With regard to the argument raised in the opposition, the Court is cognizant of the Minor Plaintiff’s right to a trial preference pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 36(b). The Court, with Judge Audri Mori presiding, found that the Minor Plaintiffs have a substantial interest in this action as a whole “because they were involved in and to continue to suffer injuries from the subject accident.” (February 22, 2023 Minute Order at pg. 2.) Furthermore, the parties stipulated that this action is a Code of Civil Procedure § 36 priority case warranting trial preference. (See Stipulation and Order, filed on March 10, 2023.)  Nothing has occurred in this case that would disrupt this stipulated order. While the Khachikyan Plaintiffs are concerned that consolidating the three actions would infringe on the Minor Plaintiffs’ trial preference rights, this concern is mitigated by severing the Minor Plaintiffs’ claim from the consolidated action and allowing their claims to proceed sooner. As explained below, the Plaintiff-in-Intervention’s Motion to Sever has merit.  Because consolidation will promote judicial efficiency and minimize prejudice to the parties, consolidation is warranted.

 

Accordingly, because the Court intends to sever Minor Plaintiffs’ claims, the Motion for Consolidation is GRANTED.

 

 

II.            Motion to Sever Minor Plaintiffs’ Claims

 

Legal Standard

 

A court may order a separate trial of any cause of action or of any separate issue in furtherance of convenience, to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy.  (Code Civ. Proc., §¿1048, subd. (b).)  Whether there will be a severance and separate trials on issues in a single action is a matter within the discretion of the trial court. (Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 847, 911-912.) 

 

Merits

 

Philadelphia argues that the issues raised by Plaintiff Khachikyan are different from the issues raised by Minor Plaintiffs.  Philadelphia argues that the injuries sustained by Minor Plaintiffs appear minimal and, if severed, their matters can be transferred to a limited jurisdiction court.  In contrast, Plaintiff Khachikyan requests damages in the amount of $4.2 million and alleges substantial injuries including traumatic brain injury, miscarriage of a baby, visual deficits, and orthopedic injuries.  Plaintiff Khachikyan has visited over 11 doctors since the incident.  Philadelphia argues that severance will promote judicial economy because Minor Plaintiffs will receive an expedited trial untethered from the complexities of Plaintiff Khachikyan’s trial.  Severance will also avoid undue prejudice to Defendants and Philadelphia because they will have additional time to defend against Plaintiff Khachikyan’s extensive and significant allegations. 

 

Plaintiffs argue that the new February 23, 2024 trial date will allow all parties to complete discovery, including expert witness discovery, concerning Plaintiffs’ injuries.  Plaintiffs contend that a severance will not promote judicial economy because Plaintiff Khachikyan, as guardian ad litem, would have to testify as a percipient witness in both trials.   

 

In the Court’s view, however, promoting judicial economy and minimizing prejudice to the parties are best served by severing Minor Plaintiffs’ claims.  As discussed above, the Court is inclined to consolidate this lawsuit with two other related cases: Sarni v. Platinum Transportation Group, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23NWCV03161, and Sirakanyan v. Platinum Transportation Group, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23NWCV03263, filed on October 4, 2023 and October 12, 2023 respectively.  The court does not see how the consolidated cases can be tried by the February 23, 2024 trial date, and any attempt to do so will likely prove extremely costly to the parties.  Severing the Minor Plaintiffs’ claims from the consolidated action will allow the former to proceed to trial sooner. 

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Sever is GRANTED.

 

 

III.          Motion to Reclassify Minor Plaintiffs’ Claims

 

A motion to reclassify an unlimited civil case as a limited civil case may be granted only if the court determines that plaintiff’s claim necessarily involves less than $25,000.00; i.e., that a greater recovery “could not be obtained” or is “virtually unobtainable.” (Walker v. Superior Court (1991) 53 Cal.3d 257.) 

 

Philadelphia argues the minors’ collective claims should be reclassified as a limited civil case because their medical treatment consisted of one phone call between their mother and their pediatrician, the minor children do not claim lost earnings, and the minor children do not claim future care.  Philadelphia asserts that the special damages for both Minor Plaintiffs total at most about $500.00. 

In Opposition, Plaintiffs submit reports from Neuropsychologist Claude Ruffalo who concludes that both Minor Plaintiffs experienced concussive head injuries with residual posttraumatic stress disorder symptoms. (Burunsuzyan Decl., Exs. 1 and 2.)  Based upon this record, the court cannot determine that Minor Plaintiffs will necessarily recover less than $25.000.00.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Reclassify is DENIED.

No Oppositions filed as of December 13, 2023.

 

This action arises from a motor vehicle accident which occurred on February 3, 2021. On February 3, 2023, Plaintiff Randa Issa (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Tyrone Montero (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10, alleging causes a single cause of action for Negligence.

 

On August 14, 2023, Defendant filed and served the following three unopposed discovery motions: (1) a motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, and for sanctions against Plaintiff and/or his/her attorney of record in the amount of $461.65 (the “Form Interrogatories Motion”); (2) a motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, and for sanctions against Plaintiff and/or his/her attorney of record in the amount of $461.65 (the “Special Interrogatories Motion”); and (3) a motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and for sanctions against Plaintiff and/or his/her attorney of record in the amount of $461.65 (the “Request for Production Motion”) (collectively, the “Motions”).

 

The failure to file an opposition creates an inference that the motion is meritorious. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) Plaintiff has failed to file an opposition to the Motions. Any opposition to the Motions was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

The Court will address the Motions in this one ruling.

 

 

Legal Standard

 

“Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).)  If the party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, “[t]he party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) “The party propounding interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) 

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by inspecting, copying, testing, or sampling documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically stored information in the possession, custody, or control of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010, subd. (a).) Where a party fails to timely respond to demand for inspection, copying, testing or sampling “[t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b) provides that “[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”

 

Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(h) states that a misuse of the discovery process includes “[m]aking or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery.” A court has discretion to “impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct” under Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

 

THE FORM INTERROGATORIES MOTION

 

Defendant served Plaintiff with Form Interrogatories, Set One, on May 1, 2023. (Fakih Decl., ¶ 3 and Exhibit A.) As of the date of the Form Interrogatories Motion, neither responses nor a request for an extension have been received. (Id., ¶ 5.) Defendant is seeking sanctions in the amount of $461.65 as to the Form Interrogatories Motion, at the hourly rate of $200 per hour which represents: 2 hours preparing the motion for a total of $400.00 and a filing fee of $61.65. (Id., ¶ 7.)

 

Based on the declaration of counsel, Plaintiff has failed to provide discovery responses. The Court therefore finds it appropriate to compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Set One of Defendant’s Form Interrogatories and the Court GRANTS the Form Interrogatories Motion. Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, complete, and code-compliant responses, without objections, to such discovery within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

As to monetary sanctions, the Court exercises its discretion and GRANTS IN PART Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel of record, Lawrence Hoodack, Esq. of the Law Offices of Lawrence Hoodack, in the reasonable amount of $261.65. Such amount represents 1 hour of work on such motion plus the $61.65 filing fee. The Court reduces the hours given the simplicity of the motion, the fact that all the Motions are substantially similar, and the fact that the Motions are unopposed. Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, Lawrence Hoodack, Esq. of the Law Offices of Lawrence Hoodack are ordered to pay monetary sanctions to Defendant in the total amount of $261.65, jointly and severally, within 30 days of notice of this order as to the Form Interrogatories Motion.

CONCLUSION

 

Given that the Motions are all unopposed there is an inference that they are meritorious under Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410. The Court GRANTS the Motions.

 

The Court GRANTS the Form Interrogatories Motion. Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, complete, and code-compliant responses, without objections, to Set One of Defendant’s Form Interrogatories within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

The Court GRANTS the Special Interrogatories Motion. Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, complete, and code-compliant responses, without objections, to Set One of Defendant’s Special Interrogatories within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

The Court GRANTS the Request for Production Motion. Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, complete, and code-compliant responses, without objections, to Set One of Defendant’s Request for Production of Documents within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

As to monetary sanctions, the Court GRANTS IN PART Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions and ORDERS Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel of record, Lawrence Hoodack, Esq. of the Law Offices of Lawrence Hoodack to pay monetary sanctions to Defendant in the total amount of $584.95, jointly and severally, within 30 days of notice of this order. This amount represents total monetary sanctions as to the Form Interrogatories Motion, Special Interrogatories Motion, and Request for Production Motion.
