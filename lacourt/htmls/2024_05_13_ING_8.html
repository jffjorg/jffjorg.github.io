Case Number: 23TRCV00381    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 May 13, 2024¿¿ 

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                  23TRCV00381 (related to case no. 23IWUD02179)

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Juanita Canche Pech v. Lucila Parra; et al.

¿¿ ¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Lucila Parra

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Anthony Trujillo

¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        Civil – Not Set;

Unlawful Detainer - May 13, 2024

 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

                                               

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) GRANTED, but Trujillo is granted immediate leave to amend and the proposed Amended UD Complaint attached to the Opposition will be deemed filed and served forthwith.  Trujillo is ordered to file the amended UD complaint forthwith as a stand-alone pleading.  The Court deems Defendant’s Answer to the original UD Complaint to stand in response to the amended UD Complaint

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿¿ 

 On February 10, 2023, Plaintiff, Juanita Canche Pech (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, Lucila Parra, Antonio Trujillo, and DOES 1 through 20. On June 20, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Quiet Title; (2) Financial Elder Abuse; (3) Fraud; (4) Violation of Penal Code § 496; and (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (6) Breach of Contract. This Court SUSTAINED the demurrers from Antonio Trujillo and Lucila Parra’s demurrers to the SAC.

 

Also, the FAC is associated with the consolidated unlawful detainer case, Case No. 23IWUD02179, filed on September 27, 2023.

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant, Lucila Parra files a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to the unlawful detainer complaint on the grounds that Parra asserts Trujillo’s 60-day notice is defective as a matter of law and that the 60-day notice is at odds with the allegations of the FAC.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On April 9, 2024, Parra filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings. On April 30, 2024, Trujillo filed an opposition brief. On May 6, 2024, Parra filed a reply brief.

 

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

With Defendant/Cross-Complainant, Lucila Parra’s moving papers, she filed a Request for Judicial Notice requesting this Court to take judicial notice of the following document:

 

1.      Trujillo’s Unlawful Detainer complaint herein (initially case number 23IWUD02179 prior to its consolidation) on September 27, 2023

 

This Court GRANTS this request and takes judicial notice of the above document.

 

Defendant, Antonio Trujillo, with his opposition brief, filed a Request for Judicial Notice, requesting this Court take judicial notice of the following documents:

 

1.      Exhibit 1: Complaint for Unlawful Detainer filed December 13, 2022;

2.      Exhibit 2: Request for Dismissal in Unlawful Detainer case;

3.      Exhibit 3: Plaintiff’s Verified Complaint filed February 10, 2023 in the civil case;

4.      Exhibit 4: Verified Cross-Complaint by Lucila Parra filed March 15, 2024 in the civil case.

 

This Court GRANTS this request and takes judicial notice of the above.

 

III. ANALYSIS  

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

The standard for ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings is essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer, that is, under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-322, citing Schabarum v. California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216.)  Matters which are subject to mandatory judicial notice may be treated as part of the complaint and may be considered without notice to the parties. Matters which are subject to permissive judicial notice must be specified in the notice of motion, the supporting points and authorities, or as the court otherwise permits. (Id.)  The motion may not be supported by extrinsic evidence. (Barker v. Hull (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 221, 236.)  

 

When the moving party is a defendant, he must demonstrate either of the following exist: 

 

(i)                 The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint. 

(ii)              The complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(a)(B)(i)-(ii).)  

 

Additionally, a motion for judgment on the pleadings must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to meet and confer in person or by telephone, at least five days before the date a motion for judgment on the pleadings is filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439.)

 

B.     Discussion  

 

Parra argues that Trujillo’s 60-day notice is defective. Parra further contends that Trujillo’s UD Complaint, which attaches said notice, admits that the 2019 Tenant Protection Act (“TPA”) applies. As such, Parra argues that this means the tenancy may not be terminated “without just cause, which must be started in the written notice…” (Civ. Code, § 1946.2(a).) However, Parra argues that the notice is missing both the statement of the “at fault just cause” or the existence of “no-fault just cause.” (Civ. Code, § 1946.2(b)(1), (2).) As such, Parra contends that because the notice defect cannot be cured mid-lawsuit, Parra requests this Court grant the motion and dismiss the unlawful detainer aspect of the action.

 

In opposition, Trujillo argues that this motion is brought on the grounds that his Unlawful Detainer Complaint has mistakenly checked boxes in paragraphs 7b and 8b(1) on the form Complaint and that no “just cause” is stated for termination of the tenancy in the attached 60-day notice. However, Trujillo concedes and admits this was an error on part of Plaintiff’s counsel and can be corrected by amendment or at time of trial to conform to proof.  Trujillo further argues that the defect is in the UD Complaint, not as defendant’s MJOP argues a defect in the 60-day notice.  According to Plaintiff, the 60-Day notice correctly states the premises are exempt from AB 1482 and that, therefore, no relocation assistance need be offered.

 

On reply, Parra argues in response to Trujillo’s untimeliness argument raised in opposition to the ex parte application that the motion is brought in a timely fashion – stating that this motion could be made at any time, even in the middle of trial. (citing Weil & Brown, Cal. Civ. Proc. Before Trial (2022) ¶ 7:285 (unlike a statutory MJOP, a “nonstatutory motion could be made at any time, even during trial, since the grounds for general demurrer are never waived [internal citation].) The Court does not believe the motion could be made at any time. 

 

Section 438, subsection (e) clearly states: “No motion may be made pursuant to this section if a pretrial conference order has been entered pursuant to section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits.” (Code Civ. Proc., §438(e).) Trial in this case was originally set for on December 4, 2023, continued to March 13, 2024, continued again to April 10, 2024, and now is set for May 13, 2024 (the same day Parra reserved for this motion.) Thus, pursuant to the Code, and disregarding Parra’s analysis (which relies on outdated precedent prior to the enactment of section 438), this Court finds that this motion is untimely. Even if the motion were timely, the Court finds that the motion should be granted on the merits but with immediate leave to amend.  An inconsistency between the Notice to Quit and the Complaint renders the pleading uncertain and ambiguous, but the Court can and hereby does grant leave to amend the UD Complaint. 

 

The reply brief also argues that, assuming the Court were to grant the MJOP but give leave to amend, Defendant argues that this Court should not grant leave to amend. The Court disagrees.  The equities and the state policy of deciding cases on their merits favor the Plaintiff here and favor leave to amend an admitted mistake by counsel.  Parra has filed this motion seven months after the Complaint was filed, and almost six months after she filed her Answer. She also scheduled this untimely motion on the same date set for trial in this case.  Attached to Trujillo’s opposition is an amended pleading which would sufficiently amend the pleading. The Court does not find that Parra would be prejudiced if this were amended, specifically because this is not a failure to pay rent case, but one where Trujillo has attempted to end tenancy by providing a sixty-day notice half a year ago.  The parties will be entitled to present evidence at trial bearing on whether the 60-day notice was or was not defective as a matter of disputed fact.  But the Court cannot determine that the notice was defective as a mater of law merely because it is inconsistent with the allegations of the admittedly mistaken box-checking of the form Complaint. 

 

 

III. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Parra’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED, but Trujillo is granted immediate leave to amend, and the proposed Amended UD Complaint attached to the Opposition will be deemed filed and served forthwith.  Trujillo is ordered to file the amended UD complaint as a stand-alone pleading immediately for ease of tracking in the digital court files, i.e., not as an attachment to the Opposition but as its own stand-alone pleading.  The Court will also deem Defendant’s Answer to the original Complaint to stand as the response to the amended UD Complaint so that the trial need not be delayed any longer. 

 

Trujillo is ordered to give notice.¿¿¿¿

I.                    Background  

 

On December 12, 2023, Plaintiff, Blue Nile Properties, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, Ronnie Cann Duah and all other Adult Occupants in Possession, and DOES 1 through 10. The Complaint is based on an alleged unlawful detainer.

 

On March 13, 2024, Plaintiff’s attorney, John B. Barriage (“Barriage”) filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff, Blue Nile Properties, LLC, citing irretrievable communication breakdown between attorney and client.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion  

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 284 states that “the attorney in an action…may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination, as follows: (1) upon the consent of both client and attorney…; (2) upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 284; CRC 3.1362.)  The withdrawal request may be denied if it would cause an injustice or undue delay in proceeding; but the court's discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably.  (See Mandell v. Superior (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4; Lempert¿v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) 

 

In making a motion to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with procedures set forth in Cal. Rules of Court 3.1362.  The motion must be made using mandatory forms: Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel directed to the client – Civil (MC-051); Declaration “stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship” reasons the motion was brought (MC-052); and a Proposed Order (MC-053).  (Ibid.)  The forms must be filed and served on all parties who have appeared in the case.  (Ibid.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel, Barriage, moves the Court to relieve him as attorney of record for Plaintiff. Barriage properly filed a Notice of Motion, Motion to be Relieved as Counsel, Declaration, and Proposed Order in accordance with Cal. Rules of Court 3.1362. On March 13, 2024, all forms for the pending motion were served on Plaintiff by mail. Barriage further notes that he has confirmed the client’s address is current within the past thirty (30) days by the company’s current address on March 13, 2024 determined by conformation with California Secretary of State website. Further, on March 13, 2024, proof of service for said documents were filed with the Court.

 

In the declaration Barriage notes that this motion is made on the grounds that he and his client have an irretrievable break down of the attorney-client relationship.

 

Since Plaintiff’s attorney has complied with all procedural requirements in filing a motion to be relieved as counsel and because the withdrawal would not cause an injustice or undue delay in proceedings, the Court finds that withdrawal of John B. Barriage as attorney of record for Plaintiff, can be accomplished without undue prejudice to its interests.

 

III.              Conclusion & Order 

 

For the foregoing reasons, John B. Barriage’s, Motion to Be Relieved As Counsel is GRANTED and the Order will be signed at the hearing. “After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).) The Order on this Motion will not be effective “until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on Plaintiff and Defendant has been filed with the court.” (Id.) 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
