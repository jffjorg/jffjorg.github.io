Case Number: 23AVCV01017    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Vertis Bayne, a minor by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Trevale Tucker (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on or about May 12, 2023, he was on the basketball court at Palmdale High School, located at 2137 East Avenue R, Palmdale, California 93550 (the “Premises”), during school hours when a staff member of Defendant Antelope Valley Union High School District (“Defendant”) ran over Plaintiffwith a golf cart.

 

On September 08, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging one cause of action for Negligence.

 

On October 30, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

 

On July 03, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held.

 

On August 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed three motions: (1) Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One; (2) Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One; and (3) Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), Set One.

 

On August 28, 2024, Defendant filed its Opposition.

 

On September 03, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Replies.

 

-----

 

       I.            Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Further Responses to Requests for Production (“RFPs”) – “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿For discovery purposes, information is regarded as relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof.” (City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 272, 288.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.310(a).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿2031.310(b)(1).) In¿Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court¿(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there¿is¿“a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v.¿Sup. Ct¿(2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ.¿Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party’s subjective intent to create burden, or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to actually respond. (See¿West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County¿(1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face, no such evidence is necessary. (See¿Obregon v. Superior Court¿(1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Trial courts are vested with “wide discretion” to allow or prohibit discovery. (Williams v. Superior Court¿(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 540.)¿ 

 

----- 

 

Standard for Compelling Further Interrogatories –Under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, a court may order a party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the court finds that any of the following apply:¿¿(1) an answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete; (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate; and (3) an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(a).)¿Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).)¿¿”Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220(a).) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible and, if the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party. (Id. at (b)-(c).) “If only a part of an interrogatory is objectionable, the remainder of the interrogatory shall be answered. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc § 2030.240(a).) If an objection is made to an interrogatory or to a part of an interrogatory, the specific ground for the objection shall be set forth clearly in the response. If an objection is based on a claim of privilege, the particular privilege invoked shall be clearly stated. If an objection is based on a claim that the information sought is protected work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010), that claim shall be expressly asserted. (Id. at (b).) 

 

----- 

 

Meet and Confer Requirement – Before filing a motion to compel further, the moving party is required to meet and confer with the party to engage in a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b), 2016.040.)  A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing such an attempt occurred. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)   

 

All declarations filed with the corresponding motions by Plaintiff’s counsel, Hazel S. Chang (“Chang”), discuss meet and confer attempts and the IDC held by this Court. The Court’s Minute Order for the IDC shows that no agreement was reached. (See 07/03/2024 Minute Order.)  

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – As an initial matter, the Court notes that Defendant’s Oppositions do not include a separate statement. As such, Defendant’s Oppositions are procedurally defective. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345.) However, the Court has reviewed the Oppositions which are concise and cite to Cal. Educ. Code.[1]  As such, the Court considers the Oppositions.

 

The following Form Interrogatories are at issue:

 

1.      Form Interrogatory 12.1 which asks for witness information;

2.      Form Interrogatory 12.2 which asks for information regarding individuals who were interviewed;

3.      Form Interrogatory 12.3 which asks for information regarding individuals who made written or recorded statements;

4.      Form Interrogatory 12.4 which asks for information regarding individuals who know of any photographs, films, or video tapes depicting any place, object, or individual concerning the incident; and

5.      Form Interrogatory 12.6 which asks for information regarding individuals who made a report for the incident.

 

Regarding Form Interrogatory 12.1, Plaintiff argues that Coito v. Superior Court (2012) 54 Cal.4th 480 (“Coito”) overrules the holding of Nacht & Lewis Architects, Inc. v. Superior Court (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 214, cited by Defendant in its Response, and that the identities of any witnesses, including the names of students involved and those involved in the investigation are necessary.

 

Regarding Form Interrogatory 12.2, Plaintiff argues reiterates the Coito argument; argues that he is seeking foundational facts, including the identities of any witnesses; and presents that if any reports or statements are being withheld under the basis of privilege, a privilege log should be provided.

 

The argument for Form Interrogatory 12.2 is used for the remainder of Form Interrogatories at issue.

 

Defendant argues that it cannot release information concerning student witnesses under Cal. Educ. Code § 49073 et seq. Defendant highlights that Cal. Educ. Code § 49076(a) requires written parental consent and Cal. Educ. Code § 49073.5(b) expresses that school districts need to minimize the release of pupil telephone numbers in the absence of express parental consent. Defendant also argues that there is a video of the incident;  that the video cannot be produced because it depicts minor students; and if the video is required to be produced that there is no need for the names of the minor students who were nearby the incident.

 

In Reply, Plaintiff argues that the Curt can order such information disclosed as such information is integral to the case as these students are witnesses and may provide testimony, that there is very little burden or expense on Defendant when compared to the need to disclose, and that there is no less intrusive manner to obtain this information.

 

Defendant’s Opposition conveniently omits Cal. Educ. Code § 49077 which reads:

 

(a) Information concerning a pupil shall be furnished in compliance with a court order or a lawfully issued subpoena. The school district shall make a reasonable effort to notify the pupil’s parent or legal guardian and the pupil in advance of compliance with a lawfully issued subpoena and, in the case of compliance with a court order, if lawfully possible within the requirements of the order.

(b) Once a court order or lawfully issued subpoena is issued to obtain a pupil’s contact information, the school district shall make a reasonable effort to enter into an agreement with the entity that obtained the court order or subpoena requiring that the pupil contact information be maintained in a confidential manner.

(c) Notwithstanding the content or existence of any agreement with a school district, a party that obtains pupil contact information pursuant to this section shall not use or disseminate that information for any purpose except as authorized by the court order or subpoena.

 

A school district such as Defendant is prohibited from disclosing pupil “directory information" to private entities (with some exceptions that are not relevant here.) (Cal. Educ. Code § 49073.) Directory information includes a pupil's name, address, and telephone number. (Cal. Educ. Code § 49061(c).) Despite the prohibition on disclosure of pupil contact information, Defendant must disclose the information when ordered by the court. (Cal. Educ. Code § 49077.) The statute requires a school district to make a reasonable effort to enter into an agreement with the party who obtained the court order to maintain the pupil contact information in a confidential manner. (Cal. Educ. Code §49077(b).) Even without such an agreement, the party obtaining the contact information “shall not use or disseminate that information for any purpose except as authorized by the court order or subpoena.” (Educ. Code §49077(c).) The court does make such an order here because the identity of witnesses is specifically discoverable under the Discovery Act. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

 

Plaintiff is not seeking “pupil records” (see Cal. Educ. Code § 49076), but rather the identities of students who witnessed the underlying incident to this action and their relevant contact information.

 

When Defendant is ordered to disclose pupil information, it must “make a reasonable effort to notify the parent or legal guardian and the pupil in advance of compliance with...a court order.” (Cal. Educ. Code § 49077.)

 

As these codes are the basis for Defendant’s Opposition to all relevant discovery motions, the Court’s analysis applies to the Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and the Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to the RFPs, Set One.

 

As to the Special Interrogatories, they are relatively similar to the Form Interrogatories and seek witness information, bet they school employees or students. Defendant’s argument that it provided documents also concedes that such documents have been redacted by Defendant to what Defendant believes is proper. As to school employees, it appears that Defendant believes that Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1985.6 applies and no notice was given to said employees. The Court inquires into this matter as Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1985.6 is not addressed in Plaintiff’s Separate Statement. As to Special Interrogatory No. 20 [“Identify all school employees who were on duty at the time of the INCIDENT and provide their job titles and responsibilities”], the Court limits this to the school in question, Palmdale High School.

 

As to RFPs, it appears that the Court’s analysis of the relevant Cal. Educ. Code sections addresses the issue.

 

As to all discovery responses, there are appear to be boiler plate objections regarding English words such as “incident” (see Special Interrogatory No. 1, see also Form Interrogatory 12.1, see RFP No. 4), “report” (see RFP No. 2), and the like. For all common English words, the plain definition is used. Such objections are overruled.

 

For objections in which privilege has been asserted, (1) a privilege log is not codified for interrogatories (see Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.240), and (2) a privilege log must be provided for RFPs (see Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240).

 

Accordingly, the Court rules as follows:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

All orders are subject to limitations discussed, ante.

 

The Court notes that the motions do not request sanctions and the Court believes that Defendant’s actions have justification as it appears that the goal was to protect the privacy of minor students.

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

The responses are subject to limitations discussed within the Court’s Statement of Decision.

Case Number: 24AVUD01056    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a forcible detainer action. Plaintiff Cla-Mar, L.P., a Limited Partnership, dba Grecian Isle (“Plaintiff”) presents that it entered into a lease agreement on or about February 01, 2011 with park-approved tenant Kenneth Thompson (“Thompson”) and said lease agreement included Defendant Wayne E. Ybarra (“Defendant”) as guest without rights of tenancy for 4444 East Avenue R, Space 9, Palmdale, CA 95552 (the “Premises”). Plaintiff further presents that on or about May 2024, Defendant informed it of Thompson’s death in December 2023; Plaintiff obtained Thompson’s death certificate on May 23 2024 which showed Thompson died on or about July 07, 2023; Defendant’s right to occupy the premises under Cal. Civ. Code § 798.34 terminated upon Thompson’s death; after becoming aware of Thompson’s death, Plaintiff did not accept money order payments as Defendant did not apply for nor obtained approval for tenancy; and Defendant remains in possession of and occupies the Premises without Plaintiff’s approval or consent.

 

On August 01, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Complaint for Forcible Detainer.

 

On August 20, 2024, Defendant filed this Demurrer.

 

On August 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed (1) its Opposition; and (2) an Ex Parte Application requesting an order shortening time to hear Defendant’s Demurer.

 

On August 27, 2024, Defendant filed a Notice of Non-Stipulation stating that he would not stipulate to a commissioner hearing this case and the case was reassigned from Commissioner Lim to Judge Morgan in Department A14 at Michael Antonovich Antelope Valley Courthouse.

 

After the reassignment, a hearing was held on the Ex Parte Application in which the Ex Parte Application was denied; however, the Court advanced the hearing at the request of the moving party and the September 18, 2024 hearing date was advanced and continued to September 10, 2024.

 

On September 03, 2024, Defendant filed his Reply.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a).)¿A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Ibid.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified (Dec. 23, 2003).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

-----¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Meet and Confer Requirement – Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

There is no declaration filed with the Demurrer. The demurrer is procedurally defective. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a).) However, as “[a] determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer,” the Court addresses the Demurrer on its merits. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a)(4).)

Discussion

 

Application – Defendant presents that Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1160(a) provides that forcible detainer is brought by a person who: (1) by force, or by menaces and threats of violence, unlawfully holds and keeps the possession of any real property, whether the same was acquired peaceably or otherwise; or (2) who, in the night-time, or during the absence of the occupant of any lands, unlawfully enters upon real property, and who, after demand made for the surrender thereof, for the period of five days, refuses to surrender the same to such former occupant. Defendant argues that Plaintiff has not alleged facts showing that Defendant engaged in any form of violence to satisfy Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1160(a)(1), and that Plaintiff has not alleged facts showing that it was an occupant or party in possession of the property under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1160(a)(2).

 

Defendant’s arguments do not address the California Mobilehome Residency Laws that are alleged in the Complaint to apply to this action.

 

Cal. Civ. Code § 798.75 provides:

 

(a) An escrow, sale, or transfer agreement involving a mobilehome located in a park at the time of the sale, where the mobilehome is to remain in the park, shall contain a copy of either a fully executed rental agreement or a statement signed by the park's management and the prospective homeowner that the parties have agreed to the terms and conditions of a rental agreement.

 

(b) In the event the purchaser fails to execute the rental agreement, the purchaser shall not have any rights of tenancy.

 

(c) In the event that an occupant of a mobilehome has no rights of tenancy and is not otherwise entitled to occupy the mobilehome pursuant to this chapter, the occupant is considered an unlawful occupant if, after a demand is made for the surrender of the mobilehome park site, for a period of five days, the occupant refuses to surrender the site to the mobilehome park management. In the event the unlawful occupant fails to comply with the demand, the unlawful occupant shall be subject to the proceedings set forth in Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 1159) of Title 3 of Part 3 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

 

(d) The occupant of the mobilehome shall not be considered an unlawful occupant and shall not be subject to the provisions of subdivision (c) if all of the following conditions are present:

 

(1) The occupant is the registered owner of the mobilehome.

 

(2) The management has determined that the occupant has the financial ability to pay the rent and charges of the park; will comply with the rules and regulations of the park, based on the occupant's prior tenancies; and will comply with this article.

 

(3) The management failed or refused to offer the occupant a rental agreement.

 

(Emphasis added.)

 

Here, Defendant is an occupant on the lease. (See Complaint, Exh. 1.) Because of this, it is alleged that Defendant had no right of tenancy. (See id. at ¶ 6.) There is no evidence that Plaintiff himself ever requested a rental application. Rather, it is alleged and Defendant does not provide argument to contradict that he did not inquire about applying for a tenancy, submit an application for tenancy, or execute a rental agreement and, because of that, Plaintiff has had no opportunity to determine the financial ability of Defendant to pay the rent and other charges of the mobilehome park or even offer a rental agreement to Defendant; and Defendant did not acquire or purchase the mobilehome as a registered owner. (See id. at ¶¶ 9-11, Exh. 3.) Exhibit 3 to the Complaint evidences this allegation.

 

The plain language of Cal. Civ. Code § 798.75 incorporates Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1160 into the Mobilehome Residency Laws, providing a basis for Plaintiff to bring an action of forcible detainer against an “occupant of a mobilehome has no rights of tenancy and is not otherwise entitled to occupy the mobilehome.” (See Cal. Civ. Code § 798.75(c).) Plaintiff is a mobilehome park management company (see Exh. 1) and Defendant is an “occupant of a mobilehome has no rights of tenancy and is not otherwise entitled to occupy the mobilehome” and considered an unlawful occupant as Plaintiff provided the demand specified in Cal. Civ. Code § 798.75(c) on May 03, 2024 (see Exh. 4) and Defendant remains in possession and occupancy of the Premises, exceeding the statutory five-day period.

 

Thus, Plaintiff there is no defect on the face of the Complaint as Plaintiff has met the elements of forcible detainer as a mobilehome park management company.

 

Plaintiff’s Opposition emphasizes Defendant’s lack of discussion of the Mobilehome Residency Laws, arguing that it is detrimental to the Demurrer. This is in line with the Court’s analysis, ante, and the Court’s opinion remains unchanged.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is OVERRULED.

Proc. § 1160(a), and argues that Plaintiff’s representation is inconsistent with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 798.60 and 798.75, the Complaint, a treatise, and the summons.

 

The Court believes its analysis of Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1160 with Cal. Civ. Code § 798.75 addresses the issues relevant to this Demurrer.

 

The Court’s opinion remains unchanged.

Defendant Wayne E. Ybarra’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.
