DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV03495    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO QUASH 

Hearing Date: 12/4/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV03495 JOSIAH ORELLANA, et al. vs OVIG VARTANIAN, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff Vanessa Smith

Responding Party: Defendants Ovig Vartanian and Ruzanna Tagvoryan

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: GRANTED IN PART

 

Background

This matter arises from a series of multiple motor vehicle collisions involving eight vehicles, which occurred on the I-5 Freeway Northbound in Los Angeles, California, on January 30, 2020. Plaintiff and Defendants represent three of the vehicles involved. On October 9, 2024, Defendants Ovig Vartanian and Ruzanna Tagvoryan issued subpoenas to five of Plaintiff Vanessa Smith’s prior employers, requesting the production of the following:

“Plaintiff’s complete personnel file, including but not limited to employment applications; performance evaluations and employee reviews; insurance records; workers’ compensation or disability records, including documentation related to employer notifications, benefit reviews, indemnity and medical benefits, releases to return to work, doctors’ reports, and medical bills; medical records; accident or incident reports, including witness statements; records related to employee benefits; interoffice correspondence or notes; absenteeism records and timecards; and Plaintiff’s entire payroll file, including earning ledgers, W-2 forms, 1099 forms, time, and absenteeism records.”

The language of each subpoena is identical, with the scope of records limited to the periods during which Plaintiff was employed by each respective employer.

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ subpoenas to Plaintiff’s prior employers are improper for several reasons. First, because Plaintiff is not alleging a loss of earnings claim, the requested documents are irrelevant to the issues in this case. Second, the subpoenas are overly broad, seeking a wide array of sensitive documents, including medical records, payroll information, and personnel files, much of which is unrelated to the claims in this lawsuit. Third, the subpoenas implicate Plaintiff’s financial privacy rights, as the requested wage and payroll information is not narrowly tailored and unrelated to Plaintiff's traumatic brain injury (TBI) claim. Fourth, the subpoenas improperly request medical records and bills from Plaintiff’s employers, who are not the custodians of such records.

Legal Standard 

CCP section 1987.1 states that “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1(a).)  “In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”  (Id.)  Subdivision (b) provides that a party may make a motion pursuant to subdivision (a).  (Id., § 1987.1(b).) 

        As a general rule, all unprivileged information that is relevant to the subject matter of the action is discoverable if it would itself be admissible evidence at trial or if it appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010; Schnabel v. Superior Court. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 711.) When the information sought to be discovered impacts a person’s constitutional right to privacy, limited protections come into play for that person. (Shaffer v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 993, 999.) The protections cover both a person’s personal and financial matters. (Id.) The court must balance competing rights — the right of a litigant to discover relevant facts and the right of an individual to maintain reasonable privacy — in determining whether the information is discoverable. (Id.) 

        For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.) 

Discussion

Although Plaintiff is not seeking lost wages, the Court agrees with Defendants that the subpoenas seek relevant information directly tied to Plaintiff Vanessa Smith’s claim of a traumatic brain injury (TBI) allegedly sustained in the January 2020 motor vehicle collision.

Plaintiff testified that her TBI caused memory loss, vision problems, and severe migraines, which impacted her ability to work, including requiring accommodations such as wearing sunglasses at her desk and experiencing difficulties retaining information and completing tasks. Records such as timecards, attendance logs, performance evaluations, and accommodations provided by employers are relevant for evaluating the impact and extent of Plaintiff’s TBI. Specifically, these records will show when Plaintiff returned to work, the number of hours worked, any changes in performance, and whether she requested or received accommodations for her injuries. Additionally, evidence of promotions, terminations, or other employment milestones can shed light on Plaintiff’s functional capacity before and after the collision. This information is relevant or reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence, enabling Defendants to assess the causation, extent, and implications of Plaintiff’s claimed injuries. This is particularly important given that Plaintiff’s TBI diagnosis occurred four years after the accident, leaving a significant gap in documentation that the subpoenaed records may help address.

Having said that, the Court finds that the subpoenas are overbroad because they seek entire personnel files, including irrelevant and sensitive information unrelated to Plaintiff Vanessa Smith’s traumatic brain injury claim. Specifically, the subpoenas encompass documents such as financial records (e.g., payroll files with wage amounts), general inter-office correspondence, and medical records unrelated to the TBI or alleged injuries from the accident. Requests for comprehensive payroll files, including wage amounts, raise financial privacy concerns especially considering that Plaintiff is not making a loss of earnings claim.

To address these issues and balance Defendants’ right to discovery with Plaintiff’s privacy rights, the Court will limit the subpoenas as follows:

1.  Timecards, Attendance Records, and Scheduling Logs: The subpoenas are limited to records showing days and hours worked, time off, and breaks in employment. These are directly relevant to assessing the impact of Plaintiff’s TBI on her work performance while avoiding disclosure of sensitive wage information.

2.  Performance Reviews and Accommodations: The subpoenas re limited to Plaintiff’s performance evaluations, disciplinary actions, or accommodations requested and provided. These records are critical to evaluating whether Plaintiff’s alleged TBI affected her ability to work and whether accommodations were required and granted.

3.  Medical Records: The subpoenas are limited to records related to workplace accommodations, job modifications, or adjustments provided for medical reasons explicitly tied to Plaintiff’s TBI or alleged injuries. However, the Court finds that Defendants’ request for medical records or bills from Plaintiff’s employers is improper because employers are not the appropriate custodians of such records and are not necessarily skilled in determining medical record distinctions.

4.  Requests for payroll records must be limited to timekeeping data, including hours worked, days present, and any breaks in employment. Wage amounts are to be redacted. Timekeeping records are directly relevant to evaluating whether Plaintiff’s TBI impacted her attendance and productivity but disclosure of wage amounts, earnings, or other financial details raises privacy concerns and might not be relevant because Plaintiff is not asserting a loss of earnings or earning capacity claim. The Court finds that Defendants’ interest in understanding Plaintiff’s work patterns and attendance can be satisfied without requiring disclosure of sensitive financial information.

5.  Correspondence: The subpoenas are limited to correspondence related directly to Plaintiff’s work performance, accommodations for medical conditions, or any workplace adjustments explicitly tied to the symptoms Plaintiff attributes to her TBI, such as memory issues, vision problems, or sensitivity to light. The subpoena should not include broad requests for inter-office communications unrelated to Plaintiff’s TBI. For example, emails or memos regarding non-personnel matters, such as general operations, administrative policies, or unrelated HR matters, must not be included.

6.  Protective Order: Access to the subpoenaed records shall be limited to the parties, counsel and experts directly involved in evaluating Plaintiff’s TBI claim.

Sanctions are denied because, although Defendants’ subpoenas are overbroad, their arguments regarding the relevance of Plaintiff’s employment records to her traumatic brain injury (TBI) claim have merit. Defendants made good faith efforts to resolve the dispute by offering protective measures, including redacting wage information and proposing an informal discovery conference (IDC). While the scope of their requests may require refinement, it does not reflect bad faith or an unjustified violation of discovery obligations that would justify the imposition of sanctions.

The present motions concern Plaintiff Todd Walter Moore’s responses to Defendant Veda Thomas’s Form Interrogatories, Set One; Special Interrogatories, Set One; Requests for Admission, Set One; and Requests for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

On August 9, 2024, Defendant served the discovery requests at issue. Defendant granted Plaintiff an extension to serve responses by September 24, 2024, with no further extensions to be granted. Plaintiff made several representations that the responses were forthcoming; however, as of October 30, 2024, when the motions were filed, no responses had been provided. Defendant now moves the court to compel initial responses.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, asserting that the discovery responses have since been served. Defendant, in its reply, acknowledged receipt of the responses. As a result, the present motions to compel are moot, leaving only the issue of sanctions for the court’s consideration.

 

On the issue of sanctions, Defendant argues that although the discovery responses were served on the same day the motions were filed, they were served only after Defendant incurred attorney fees in preparing and filing the motions.

 

Defendant granted Plaintiff multiple extensions, and as part of the last of those, Plaintiff agreed to serve the discovery responses by October 11, 2024. However, no responses were provided by that deadline. On October 22, 2024, Plaintiff requested an additional extension, which Defendant reasonably denied. On October 30, 2024, at approximately 12:01 p.m., Defendant served the motions to compel on Plaintiff. Later that same day, at about 4:58 p.m., Plaintiff served the overdue discovery responses.

 

Given that Defendant had already provided several extensions, Plaintiff failed to meet the October 11 deadline, and the responses were served only after the motions were filed, the Court finds that sanctions are warranted under these circumstances. Defendant seeks sanctions in the amount of $5,840 per motion. Considering the straightforward nature of the issues, the Court reduces the sanctions to a total of $1,000 for all four motions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel jointly and severally, payable to Defendant within 20 days of today.
