[Tentative] Order RE:

 

defendants’ motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Defendants Persian-American Adult Day Health Care Center, Inc., d/b/a Forever Young Adult Day Health Care Center, Vardkes Ken Esrailian, and Abdul Toukhi

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:     Plaintiffs Kazem Sanikhatam, by and through his successor-in-interest Mitra Azizi, and Mitra Azizi  

Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS 

The court rules on plaintiffs Kazem Sanikhatam, by and through his successor-in-interest Mitra Azizi, and Mitra Azizi’s evidentiary objections, filed on April 11, 2024, as follows:

The court sustains Objections Nos. 2-6.

The court overrules Objection No. 1.[1]

The court does not rule on defendants Persian-American Adult Day Health Care Center, Inc., d/b/a Forever Young Adult Day Health Care Center, Vardkes Ken Esrailian, and Abdul Toukhi’s evidentiary objections, filed on April 16, 2024, because the objections are directed to evidence that is not material to the court’s disposition of this motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (q).)

LEGAL STANDARD

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant or cross-defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “Once the defendant or cross-defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)  “When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Id. at p. 467; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

DISCUSSION

Defendants Persian-American Adult Day Health Care Center, Inc., d/b/a Forever Young Adult Day Health Care Center (“Forever Young”), Vardkes Ken Esrailian, and Abdul Toukhi (“Toukhi”) (collectively, “Defendants”) filed the pending motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication against plaintiffs Kazem Sanikhatam, by and through his successor-in-interest Mitra Azizi (“Sanikhatam”), and Mitra Azizi (“Azizi”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) on February 8, 2024, moving the court for an order granting summary judgment in their favor and against Plaintiffs on the First Amended Complaint.

1.     Procedural Issues

As a threshold matter, the court notes that Plaintiffs have argued, in their opposition, that Defendants’ notice of motion for summary adjudication is defective.  The court agrees.

“If summary adjudication is sought, whether separately or as an alternative to the motion for summary judgment, the specific cause of action, affirmative defense, claims for damages, or issues of duty must be stated specifically in the notice of motion and be repeated, verbatim, in the separate statement of undisputed material facts.”  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1350, subd. (b).)

Here, Defendants’ notice of motion states only that they move the court for an order granting, “alternatively, . . . Summary Adjudication on the grounds that there are no triable issues of material fact on any of [Plaintiffs’] . . . claims and/or causes of action against Defendants, thus entitling the latter to summary judgment and/or summary adjudication.”  (Notice of Mot., p. 2:3-11.)  Defendants’ notice of motion does not state the specific causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty on which Defendants seek summary adjudication, and their separate statement of material facts does not repeat, verbatim, that information as required.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1350; Notice of Mot., p. 2:3-11; Def. Separate Statement.) 

Moreover, the court also notes that Defendants’ separate statement does not “separately identify” (1) each cause of action, affirmative defense, claim for damage, or issue of duty that is the subject of their motion, and (2) each supporting material fact claimed to be without dispute with respect to the cause of action, affirmative defense, claim for damages, or issue of duty that is the subject of the motion.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1350, subd. (d).)  Instead, Defendants’ separate statement sets forth 13 material facts without identifying the specific causes of action that are the subject of this motion and which material facts support their motion as to each cause of action.  (Def. Separate Statement, p. 1:22 [titling chart to be “Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts”] [internal emphasis omitted].)

Thus, the court finds that, because Defendants have not complied with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1350, their notice of motion for summary adjudication is defective. 

The court therefore evaluates whether Defendants have met their burden to show that they are entitled to summary judgment on Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint. 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

The court finds that Defendants have not met their burden to show that the first cause of action has no merit and therefore have not shown that they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law on Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

Plaintiffs have alleged their first cause of action for elder abuse / neglect against all Defendants under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15600 et seq.) (the “Elder Abuse Act”).

“[N]eglect as a form of elder abuse under the Elder Abuse Act refers ‘to the failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.’  [Citation.]”  (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 404.)  An Elder Abuse Act plaintiff must show “that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care [citations]; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs [citations]; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult’s basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness) [citations].  The plaintiff must also allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) that the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering.  [Citations.] “  (Id. at pp. 406-407 [internal citations omitted].)

The court notes that it is unclear which element(s) of the first cause of action Defendants contend cannot be established by Plaintiffs, but it appears that Defendants have argued that Plaintiffs cannot establish (1) that Defendants denied or withheld goods or services to Sanikhatam that were necessary to meet his basic needs, and (2) causation.  (Mot., pp. 9:12-14 [summarily stating that Defendants have met their burden to show facts “to negate all essential elements of each” cause of action] [emphasis added], 10:13-19 [quoting law setting forth the elements of (1) denial or withholding of goods or services necessary to meet the elder’s basic needs and (2) causation]; Carter, supra, 198 CalAp.4th at pp. 406-407.) 

The court finds that Defendants have not met their burden of showing that the first cause of action for elder abuse / neglect has no merit because Defendants have not shown that the elements of (1) Defendants’ withholding or denial of good or services to Sanikhatam, or (2) causation cannot be established.

First, the court finds that Defendants have not shown that Plaintiffs’ discovery responses prove that Plaintiffs are unable to establish the elements of their first cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act.

Defendants have submitted evidence of Azizi’s response to defendant Forever Young’s Form Interrogatories, number 17.1, contending that the statements made therein “are enough to prove [that Plaintiffs] cannot meet their burden to defeat summary judgment.”  (Mot., p. 11:19-20.)  The court disagrees.  In response to interrogatory number 17.1, Azizi set forth various facts in support of Plaintiffs’ claims, including, inter alia, that (1) Sanikhatam was an elder under the Elder Abuse Act, (2) Defendants were to provide to him care or services, (3) Defendants owed Sanikhatam a duty of care, (4) on January 16, 2020, Sanikhatam used the transportation service provided by defendant Forever Young, (5) on that date, the driver of the van, defendant Toukhi, did not assist Sanikhatam as he boarded the van, (6) Sanikhatam suffered a fall when he attempted to board the van, and (7) Toukhi called Forever Young to report the incident and was directed by the facility not to call 911 or transport Sanikhatam to the hospital for treatment in order to cover up the incident.  (Mitrovich Decl., Ex. C, pp. 36:21-39:12, 44:2-46:12, 51:3-53:15, 58:7-60:18.)  

While the court agrees that “[a] plaintiff’s ‘factually devoid’ discovery responses may be used to shift the burden of production onto the plaintiff[,]” the burden is shifted only “when the ‘logical inference’ is that the plaintiff possesses no facts to support his or her claims.”  (Bayramoglu v. Nationstar Mortgage LLC (2020) 51 Cal.App.5th 726, 733.)  Azizi’s discovery responses set forth various facts on which Plaintiffs base their causes of action and do not support the “‘logical inference’” that Plaintiffs possess “no facts to support” their claims.  (Ibid.)  Thus, the court finds that Defendants have not met their burden to show that Plaintiffs cannot establish the elements of denial or withholding of necessary goods to Sanikhatam or causation based on plaintiff Azizi’s discovery responses.

Second, the court finds that defendant Toukhi’s deposition testimony is insufficient to show that Plaintiffs cannot establish that Defendants (1) withheld or denied to Sanikhatam goods or services necessary to meet his basic needs or (2) caused Sanikhatam’s injuries.  

Defendants have submitted the deposition testimony of defendant Toukhi, in which he testified that, on an unidentified date, Sanikhatam instructed Toukhi “to hold his walker steady from the front, and [Toukhi] did[,]” and as Sanikhatam was getting onto an elevator, Toukhi was holding onto his walker.  (Mitrovich Decl., Ex. D, Toukhi Dep., p. 42:7-23.)  Defendants have submitted this evidence to argue that “there was no elder abuse or neglect that occurred” on the date of the subject incident.  (Mot., p. 12:27-28.)  However, the court finds that this evidence is insufficient to show that Plaintiffs cannot establish the challenged elements because (1) the deposition transcript submitted by Defendants omits pertinent information regarding the conduct described, including whether Toukhi testified that he was holding onto Sanikhatam’s walker on the date of the incident that is the subject of this action or on another date, and (2) this testimony does not address each legal theory supporting the first cause of action, including Plaintiffs’ claims that (i) Defendants knew that Toukhi was not trained to assist Sanikhatam with ambulating to and from the transport car (FAC ¶¶ 29, 109), and (ii) Defendants are guilty of neglect because they did not transfer Sanikhatam to an acute care facility in an attempt to cover up the incident[2] (FAC ¶¶ 34, 107).   (Mitrovich Decl., Ex. D, Toukhi Dep., p. 42:24 [asking Toukhi for confirmation that “This was on January 16th?”], 42:25 [partial response from Toukhi stating [“These things they happened what, like, four”].)

Third, the court finds that the deposition testimony of Eleanor Garcia (“Garcia”) is insufficient to show that Plaintiffs cannot establish the elements of (1) the denial or withholding of goods to Sanikhatam necessary to meet his basic needs or (2) causation.

Defendants contend that “further evidence that [they] did not abuse or neglect [Sanikhatam] can be found in the testimony of nurse [Garcia],” a nurse on duty when Sanikhatam arrived at Forever Young.  (Mot., p. 13:22-26.)  The court disagrees.  Garcia testified only that, on an unidentified date, Sanikhatam thanked an individual named Malak for helping him.  (Mitrovich Decl., Ex. E, Garcia Dep., p. 60:16-18.)  This testimony does not establish that (1) Defendants did not withhold or deny services to Sanikhatam that were necessary to meet his basic needs, or (2) Defendants’ conduct did not cause Sanikhatam to suffer physical harm, pain, or mental suffering.  (Carter, supra, 198 Cal.App.4t at pp. 406-407.)

Fourth, the court finds that Defendants have not submitted admissible evidence from an expert establishing that they did not (1) neglect Sanikhatam, or (2) cause Sanikhatam’s injuries.

The court has sustained Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections to Sanikhatam’s medical records because they were not properly authenticated.  Thus, Dr. Rosen’s expert medical opinions regarding the existence of neglect, the cause of Sanikhatam’s death, and the proper standard of care have “no evidentiary value[,]” and the court has sustained Plaintiffs’ objections to those conclusions.  (Garibay v. Hemmat (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 735, 743.) 

The court therefore finds that Defendants have not met their burden to submit admissible, competent evidence showing that they did not (1) withhold or deny goods or services necessary to meet Sanikhatam’s basic needs, or (2) cause Sanikhatam’s injuries.

Thus, for the reasons set forth above, the court finds that Defendants have not met their burden to show that Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for elder abuse / neglect has no merit.  Because Defendants have not shown that the first cause of action has no merit, the court finds that Defendants have not met their burden to “show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that [Defendants] [are] entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The court therefore denies Defendants’ motion for summary judgment.

Finally, the court notes that, as discussed, the court has found that Defendants’ request for summary adjudication was improper and defective.  However, even if Defendants had properly noticed their request for summary adjudication, the court would find that they did not meet their burden to show that Plaintiffs’ second, third, and fourth causes of action have no merit. 

As to the second and third causes of action for negligence and negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, respectively, Defendants have argued that Plaintiffs cannot establish the element of causation.  (Mot., pp. 15:21-24, 15:26-16:2; Peredia v. HR Mobile Services (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687 [elements of negligence].)  Defendants have relied solely on the declaration of Dr. Rosen in support of this assertion.  However, the court has found that Dr. Rosen’s conclusions have “no evidentiary value” and therefore are insufficient to show that Plaintiffs cannot establish the element of causation.  (Garibay, supra, 161 Cal.App.4th at p. 743.) 

Similarly, as to the fourth cause of action for wrongful death, Defendants contend that Plaintiffs cannot establish the element of causation, relying on Sanikhatam’s death certificate and his medical records.  (Mot., pp. 16:11-13, 16:26-17:11.)  However, the court (1) finds that the cause of death listed on the death certificate does not establish that Defendants did not contribute to Sanikhatam’s injuries and subsequent death and therefore does not show that Plaintiffs cannot establish the element of causation, and (2) has sustained Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections to the medical records, such that they cannot support Defendants’ assertion that they did not cause Sanikhatam’s death.  (Mitrovich Decl., Ex. A; Musgrove v. Silver (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 694, 705 [elements of cause of action for wrongful death].)

ORDER

The court denies defendants Persian-American Adult Day Health Care Center, Inc., d/b/a Forever Young Adult Day Health Care Center, Vardkes Ken Esrailian, and Abdul Toukhi’s motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication.

The court orders plaintiffs Kazem Sanikhatam, by and through his successor-in-interest Mitra Azizi, and Mitra Azizi to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion for Attorney’s Fees

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendants Teresa Gorman Martinez and Ruben Martinez, trustees of the GM Defined Benefit Pension Plan (“Defendants”) move the court for an order awarding them attorney’s fees, to be paid by plaintiffs Shai Harris, as successor trustee of the H. McClenon Trust dated December 12, 2019 and as trustee of the ADH Harris Trust dated January 14, 2020 (“Plaintiffs”), in the total amount of $12,061.

 “In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.”  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).)  “California courts liberally construe the term ‘on a contract’ in Civil Code section 1717.  [Citations.]  ‘ “As long as the action ‘involve[s] a contract, it is “ ‘on [the] contract’ ” within the meaning of section 1717.’ ” ’”  (Yoon v. Cam IX Trust (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 388, 393 [internal citations omitted].)

First, the court finds that this action is “on a contract” within the meaning of Civil Code section 1717.  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a); Yoon, supra, 60 Cal.App.5th at p. 393.)  Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint (1) asserted one cause of action for the administering of an unconscionable contract or clause, (2) alleged that Defendants administered an unconscionable contract of adhesion, and (3) requested that the court not enforce the contract.  (FAC pp. 1:21-25, 3:8-14.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiffs’ action “involve[s] a contract” and therefore is an action on a contract.  (Ibid.; Yoon, supra, 60 Cal.App.5th at p. 393 [internal quotations omitted].)

Second, the court finds that the contract that is the subject of this action includes an attorney’s fees provision.  Specifically, the “Promissory Note Secured by Deed of Trust” dated August 25, 2021 (1) states that Plaintiffs, as borrowers, agreed to pay to “EXHIBIT D, the Beneficiary,” the amount of $738,000; (2) includes as an attachment “Exhibit D,” which named Defendants under the title “Lender Vesting;” and (3) includes as an exhibit the “Deed of Trust [¶] and Assignment of Rents,” which includes a provision stating that (i) “If . . . (2) there is a legal proceeding that might significantly affect Lender’s interest in the Property and/or rights under this Security Instrument[,]” then the lender (i.e., Defendants) may appear in court and “pay[] reasonable attorneys’ fees to protect its interest in the Property and/or rights under this Security Instrument,” and (ii) any amounts disbursed by the lender under that section “shall become additional debt of the Borrower secured by this Security Instrument.”  (Goldstein Decl., Ex. A, p. 1; Goldstein Decl., Ex. D to Ex. A; Ex. A, Deed of Trust, § 8.) 

The court finds that this language “provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce [the promissory note], shall be awarded . . . to one of the parties” (i.e., Defendants), and therefore is an attorney’s fees provision within the meaning of section 1717.[1]  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).) 

Third, the court finds that Defendants are the prevailing parties on the contract because the court sustained their demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint without leave to amend on August 10, 2023, and thereafter entered judgment of dismissal in favor of Defendants and against Plaintiffs on October 30, 2023.

Fourth, the court finds that Defendants have established a lodestar amount of $9,367 for reasonable attorney’s fees.

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted).)¿ “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)¿ 

Defendants have submitted the declaration of Jason Goldstein, who attests to the qualifications, skill, and experience of the attorneys that worked on this action.  (Goldstein Decl., ¶¶ 18-20.)  The court finds that the hourly rates requested are reasonable.  (Goldstein Decl., ¶ 14 [stating (1) Goldstein’s hourly charge to be $515, (2) Tran’s hourly billing rate to be $495, and (3) Neumann’s hourly billing rate to be $375].)  The court also finds that the 22.20 hours expended by Defendants’ attorneys in litigating this action, as verified by counsel, are reasonable.  (Goldstein Decl., ¶ 15; Goldstein Decl., Ex. C.)

Thus, the court finds that Defendants have established a lodestar amount of $9,367.  The court further grants Defendants’ request for an award of attorney’s fees incurred in preparing the pending motion for attorney’s fees.  The court finds that $1,996.50 ((0.6 hours x Goldstein’s $515 hourly rate) + (4.5 hours x Neumann’s $375 hourly rate)) is reasonable considering that Plaintiffs did not file an opposition and Defendants therefore did not prepare and file a reply.  (Goldstein Decl., ¶¶ 24-25.)

The court therefore grants Defendants’ motion and orders that Defendants shall recover a total amount of $11,363.50 in attorney’s fees from Plaintiffs.

ORDER

            The court grants defendants Teresa Gorman Martinez and Ruben Martinez, trustees of the GM Defined Benefit Pension Plan’s motion for attorney’s fees.

            The court orders that defendants Teresa Gorman Martinez and Ruben Martinez, trustees of the GM Defined Benefit Pension Plan, shall recover a total of $11,363.50 in attorney’s fees from plaintiffs Shai Harris, as successor trustee of the H. McClenon Trust dated December 12, 2019 and as trustee of the ADH Harris Trust dated January 14, 2020, pursuant to Civil Code section 1717.

            The court orders defendants Teresa Gorman Martinez and Ruben Martinez, trustees of the GM Defined Benefit Pension Plan to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
