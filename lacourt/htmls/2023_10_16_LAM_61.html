DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV11815    Hearing Date: October 16, 2023    Dept: 61

Defendants Danna Munch, Trilar Management Group, and Arbor Ridge Venture, LLC’s  Motion for Attorney Fees and Costs are GRANTED in the amount of $417,500.00 in fees and $6,385.00 in costs.

 

Defendants to provide notice.

 

I.                   MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

“Except as attorney's fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties; but parties to actions or proceedings are entitled to their costs, as hereinafter provided.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1021.)

 

“In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).) “[T]he party prevailing on the contract shall be the party who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (b)(1).)

 

Defendants Danna Munch, Trilar Management Group, and Arbor Ridge Venture, LLC (Defendants) seek $428,260.00 in attorney fees and $6,385.00 in costs. (Motion at p. 2.) The motion is based on the lease agreement executed by Plaintiff Stephanie Greene (Plaintiff) on August 9, 2018. (Motion Exh. A.) The agreement provides for attorney fees to be awarded to the prevailing party “[i]n any legal action brought . . . to enforce this Agreement or relating to the Residence.” (Ibid.)

 

The fee lodestar is supported by the declaration of Harry Safarian, who provides the qualifications of the ten attorneys, one paralegal, and one law clerk who performed work on this action. (Safarian Decl. ¶ 5.) Together these identified individuals performed 910.6 hours of work at rates ranging from $75 to $850 per hour. (Safarian Decl. ¶ 7.) Most of the work (572.40 hours) was performed by attorney Kitty Xie at an hourly rate of $350, and the total average rate is $465.31 per hour. (Safarian Decl. ¶ 7.)

 

Plaintiff in opposition contends that she is financially unable to pay any fee award, as she receives $873 per month from social security, and is without other income or property. (Opposition at pp. 1–2.)  However, financial hardship is not a permissible consideration when evaluating a request for contractual attorney fees. “While we recognize that an award of contractual attorney fees may be subject to equitable considerations, we nonetheless conclude a losing party's financial condition should not be considered in setting the amount of such an award.” (Walker v. Ticor Title Co. of California. (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 363, 373.)

The motion is for the most part well-supported. However, certain fees identified in the hourly logs presented with the motion are unreasonable. There is one 1.2 hour ($1,020.00) charge for preparing a summary judgment motion which was never filed, entered on June 12, 2023. (Safarian Decl. Exh. B at p. 7.) The charges also include $6,135.00 for 16.1 hours of work preparing and defending motions to compel which were taken off calendar, as well as $3,605.00 for 10.3 hours of work bringing a motion to compel a second IME, which was also taken off calendar. The requested lodestar is therefore reduced by $10,760.00.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED in the amount of $417,500.00 in fees and $6,385.00 in costs.

 


Case Number: 21STCV20963    Hearing Date: October 16, 2023    Dept: 61

Plaintiff George Castro’s Motions to Compel Further Responses to Form and Special Interrogatories from Defendant County of Los Angeles are GRANTED as to Form Interrogatory No. 209.2. Defendant shall produce information concerning cases initiated in or after 2018, filed by current or former employees of the Probation Department, asserting claims for FEHA disability discrimination, failure to accommodate, or failure to engage in the interactive process. The motions are otherwise DENIED. No sanctions are awarded.

 

I.       MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.010(a).) If a propounding party is not satisfied with the response served by a responding party, the former may move the court to compel further interrogatory responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)  The propounding party must demonstrate that the responses were incomplete, inadequate or evasive, or that the responding party asserted objections that are either without merit or too general.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(a)(1)–(3); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 403.)

Plaintiff George Castro (Plaintiff) moves to compel further responses from Defendant County of Los Angeles (Defendant) to Form Interrogatories No. 209.2 and 217.1 and Special Interrogatories No. 39, 40, 43, 66, 67, and 68. 

Form Interrogatory No. 209.2 seeks information concerning litigation filed by employees of Defendant’s Probation Department against it within the last ten years, including case numbers and contact information for litigants and attorneys. Defendant served an initial response with only objections, including undue burden, and in a supplemental response reiterated the undue burden objection as to Plaintiff’s proposed narrowing of the scope of the interrogatory to only five years and for “similar” actions. (Separate Statement at pp. 2–3.) Form Interrogatory No. 217.1 sought facts and evidence justifying Defendant’s denials of requests for admission, for which Plaintiff contends that responses as to Requests for Admission No. 2, 6, 8–10, 15, 16, 18–20, 24–26, and 34 are inadequate. (Separate Statement at pp. 16–20.)

For the special interrogatories at issue, Defendant initially responded by reference to Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.030’s limitation to 35 special interrogatories. (Separate Statement at pp. 2–14.) Defendant provided supplemental responses, however, to which Plaintiff takes issue. These interrogatories sought information concerning Defendant’s reasons for failing to provide Plaintiff with a chair, for failing to return him to a previous assignment, and sought information concerning who took over that assignment and who supervised Plaintiff in the position.

Plaintiff has failed to meet and confer concerning the special interrogatories. The original responses, which consisted of objections based on Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.030, were served on July 5, 2022, and supplemental responses were served on October 7, 2022. (Xie Decl. ¶¶ 6–9.) After much meeting and conferring on these responses, including an IDC, the supplemental responses at issue were served on July 10, 2023. (Xie Decl. ¶ 14, Exh. 7.) Despite the fact that this supplement constituted the first provision of substantive responses without reliance on Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.030, Plaintiff’s counsel states, “I determined that Plaintiff has exhausted the meet and confer process at this time.” (Xie Decl. ¶ 15.) There has thus been no conference between the parties on the merits of these responses or the objections asserted therein. Plaintiff in reply contends that Defendant refused an extension of time in which to file the present motion, but presents no evidence or information concerning this request or any other meet-and-confer efforts that preceded this motion. (Reply at p. 1.) The motion is therefore DENIED as to the special interrogatories.

The same objection pertains to Form Interrogatory No. 217.1. Plaintiff takes no issue with the supplemental responses to this interrogatory, served on July 7, 2023, which concerned RFAs No. 36–41. (Xie Decl. Exh. 7.) But neither did Plaintiff take any issue with Defendant’s responses as they concerned RFAs No. 2, 6, 8–10, 15, 16, 18–20, 24–26, and 34, as is demonstrated by the absence of this interrogatory from the parties’ IDC materials. (See 1/10/2023 and 1/11/2023 IDC Separate Statements.) The motion is therefore DENIED as to Form Interrogatory No. 217.1.

This objection does not apply, however, to Form Interrogatory No. 209.2. Defendant raised its undue burden objection in its initial responses, and only repeated the objection in supplemental responses, despite Plaintiff’s offers to narrow the scope of the interrogatory from the preceding ten years of litigation to five, and to limit the scope of cases to those similar to the FEHA disability claims now remaining in this case. The parties have conferred on the character of this request and Defendant’s objection.

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.020, subd. (a).) “[T]he party opposing discovery has an obligation to supply the basis for this determination,” and to provide evidence “showing the quantum of work required.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549.) Defendant here presents the declaration of Chereise Martin, administrative service manager for the Los Angeles County Probation Department and assigned as manager of the Office of Litigation Management. (Martin Decl. ¶ 2.) Martin states that the department does not have a system or tool that allows the search of lawsuit information sought by Plaintiff’s interrogatory, and that it would have to perform a manual review of approximate 500 complaints (estimating about 100 complaints filed against the probation department per year) to determine if responsive information existed. (Martin Decl. ¶¶ 4–5.) At an estimated 10 minutes per case review, it would take over 80 hours to provide the responsive information that Plaintiff seeks. (Martin Decl. ¶ 5.)

Defendant’s showing is unpersuasive. First, the information sought by Plaintiff is relevant to the subject matter of this litigation. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) Evidence of the experiences of other employees may be relevant to show discriminatory intent or policy. (See Pantoja v. Anton (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 87, 115.) Martin’s estimate of 10 minutes per complaint to be reviewed is excessive. While it may take 10 minutes to process the requested information for a complaint that is responsive to the interrogatory, it ought not to take near so much time to determine that a complaint is not responsive. A determination of “similarity” need not involve in-depth review of the underlying facts of a complaint, but may be determined, as Defendant acknowledges in its interrogatory response, by seeing whether a complaint is one for disability discrimination, failure to accommodate, or failure to engage in the interactive process under FEHA. (Separate Statement at pp. 2–3.) Such a review is more appropriately conducted by Defendant’s Office of Litigation Management by reference to the cases it has on file, than by plaintiffs, who have access only to court records.

The motion is therefore GRANTED as to Form Interrogatory No. 209.2. Defendant shall produce information concerning cases initiated in or after 2018, filed by current or former employees of the Probation Department, asserting claims for FEHA disability discrimination, failure to accommodate, or failure to engage in the interactive process. The motions are otherwise DENIED.

II.    SANCTIONS

Statute provides that the court shall impose sanctions upon a party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, or requests for admission, absent substantial justification otherwise. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (d); 2031.310, subd. (h); 2033.290, subd. (d).)

 

Plaintiff seeks $1,321.60 sanctions for the special interrogatories motion and $1,115.1 for the form interrogatories motion, representing a total of 5.9 hours at $413 per hour. (Xie Decl. ¶ 2.) No sanctions are awarded.
