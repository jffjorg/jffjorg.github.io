DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 22STCV07972    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

 

County of Los Angeles

 

Department 40

 

MICHELLE LEONARD,

Plaintiff,

v.

BRIAN E. CLAYPOOL; THE CLAYPOOL LAW FIRM; and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive,

Defendants.

 Case No.: 22STCV07972

 Hearing Date: 12/7/23

 Trial Date: 6/11/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant Brian E. Claypool and Claypool Law Firm’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Michelle Leonard’s to Serve Further Verified Responses and to Produce all Responsive Documents for Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Motion for Monetary Sanctions.

 

On January 19, 2023, Defendants Brian E. Claypool and Claypool Law Firm (“Defendants”) served Plaintiff Michelle Leonard (“Plaintiff”) with their Request for Production of Documents, Set One (“RPDs”) via electronic mail. On March 20, 2023, Plaintiff served her responses and only produced limited documents. In Plaintiff’s responses, Plaintiff attested under penalty of perjury that she would produce all documents in her possession, custody, or control in response to request numbers 15, 16, and 29.

 

Defendants now bring this motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents, set one, which Plaintiff opposed on November 22, 2023 and to which Defendants’ replied on November 30 2023.

 

After review, the Court GRANTS the Motion because Plaintiff’s statement of compliance with the demand to produce is incomplete.

 

Background Allegations

Plaintiff Michelle Leonard (“Plaintiff”) sues Defendants Brian E. Claypool, Claypool Law Firm (“Defendants”), and DOES 1 through 20 pursuant to a March 4, 2022 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Professional Negligence; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (3) Breach of Contract.

 

            On April 28, 2023, Defendants’ counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter, which detailed why Plaintiff’s responses and document production were insufficient as to request numbers 15, 16, and 29 of Defendants’ Request for Production of Documents, Set One (“RPDs”).

 

            On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendants’ counsel a letter guaranteeing that Plaintiff would produce all documents in response to request number 15, 16, and 29, and extend any applicable Motion to Compel deadline until 45 days after the documents were produced. On the same day, Defendants’ counsel responded with an email confirming the extension and seeking clarification as to when Plaintiff would produce her additional documents.

           

On May 26, 2023; June 16, 2023; and July 7, 2023, Defendants’ counsel sent two more emails and another letter requesting the further responses or additional documents.

 

            On July 13, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendants’ counsel an email with a Dropbox link purportedly containing Plaintiff’s additional document production. On the dame day, Defendants’ counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel an email requesting access to the Dropbox because provided link did not work.

 

            On July 21, 2023, Defendants’ counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel another email stating he was not able to access the Dropbox link and requesting access to it. On July 28, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel produced certain additional documents, which Defendants’ counsel found to still be insufficient. On the same day, Defendants’ counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that if Plaintiff did not produce all of her responsive documents by August 4, 2023, he would filed this instant motion. As of the filing of the motion, Defendants have not received Plaintiff’s further verified responses of additional document production pursuant to Defendants’ RPDs.

Motion to Compel Further Production

 

Legal Standard

A motion to compel a further response is used when a party gives unsatisfactory answers or makes untenable objections to interrogatories, demands to produce, or requests for admission. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) To request further production, a movant must establish: (1) good cause for the production (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1); Sinaiko, supra, at p. 403); and (2) that a further response is needed because (a) the responding party’s statement of compliance with the demand to produce is incomplete Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(1)), (b) the responding party’s representation that it is unable to comply is inadequate, complete, or evasive (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(2)), (c) the responding party’s objection in the response is without merit or is too general (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(3); Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1127), or (d) if the responding party objected to the production of ESI on the ground that it is not reasonably accessible the movant can show that the (i) ESI is reasonably accessible or (ii) there is good cause for production of the ESI regardless of its accessibility (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (e)).

 

Order Compelling Further Responses, Production: GRANTED

Here, Defendants argue that despite agreeing to produce additional documents in response to Defendants’ RPDs Set One, Nos. 15, 16, and 29, Plaintiff has in bad faith failed to do so. (Mot., 7:15-19; Yee Decl., ¶¶ 6, 9, 19, Exhs. B, D.) Defendants’ original request for this production was not met with any objections. Plaintiff simply states that all responsive documents in her possession, custody, or control would be produced. However, Defendants are vague in the motion, declaration of counsel, exhibit to the declaration, and the separate statement as to precisely what the alleged inadequacy was.

Plaintiff’s opposition asserts that the responsive documents are in the exclusive possession of the requesting party, where she has been asking for her legal file and an accounting of funds from her former attorney Brian Claypool for over a year. Without such documents, Plaintiff contends that she has produced all documents in her possession in response to RPDs Nos. 15 and 16 [which request all documents that evidence payments that she received in the underlying action, or for the underlying incident]. (Opp at p. 4.) As to RPD Set One, No. 29, which request documents that evidence Plaintiff’s income for the 5 years preceding the incident, Plaintiff argues that the documents requested are not relevant to the material issues in this instant case, since she is not seeking any loss of income or loss of earning capacity. (Opp. at pp. 4-5.)

In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s opposition falsely asserts that the documents requested in Nos. 15 and 16 are in their exclusive possession and control. In fact, Defendants’ assert that within Exhibit A of Plaintiff’s opposition is a letter dated April 5, 2023 from Defendants’ counsel to Plaintiff’s counsel, where Defendants’ counsel specifically stated that Plaintiff files were already mailed to her via two boxes and that she confirmed receipt of those boxes. As for RPD, Set One, No. 29, Defendants argue that a responding party cannot later assert additional objections that were not previously asserted without a court order granting relief from waiver. Furthermore, Defendants’ contend that RPD, Set One, No. 29 has nothing at all to do with whether Plaintiff is claiming lost income or loss of earning capacity damages in this present lawsuit. (Reply, pp. 3-4.) Defendants are silent, however, as to the relevance of the loss of earning to this case, leaving the Court to speculate based on the nature of this case.

The Court finds that whether Plaintiff has, or has not, received any documents from Defendants is irrelevant to this motion to compel; her obligation is to produce all responsive documents that are in her possession, custody or control. To the extent that Plaintiff has not done so, she is ordered to provide all such documents responsive to Nos. 15 and 16 or a verified response stating that she has no responsive documents. Should she choose the latter approach, such a response may preclude her from producing any documents that would have been responsive at trial.

As to RPD No. 29, to the extent that Plaintiff contends that she did not receive full and appropriate compensation from the underlying action, and to the extent that economic loss was a part of the damages that she sought in the underlying action, then such documents are relevant and discoverable. Although she may not be seeking economic damages in the instant case, if she sought them in the underlying case, and is seeking a finding that her payout in that case was insufficient, they should be produced.

Thus the Court finds tentatively that Defendants are entitled to further, and objection-free, verified responses and production to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15, 16, and 29, for which reason this motion is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions: GRANTED

Except in certain circumstances involving electronic stored information, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

The Court finds that sanctions are merited here. Plaintiff did not timely produce the additional documents in response to Defendants’ RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15, 16, and 29, as initially indicated. Plaintiff has also opposed a meritorious motion. Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ meet and confer efforts did not include one single telephone call. However, Defendants sent multiple meet and confer emails and letters to address the deficient responses. Moreover, the meet and confer here was adequate given that Plaintiff opposed the motion on the merits and did not provide the responses after the motion was filed.

The Court GRANTS sanctions, in the reduced amount of $2,180.00, comprised of a fee rate of $265 per hour, 3.0 hours for preparing this motion, 3.0 hours reasonably expended reviewing and replying to the opposition, 2.0 hours preparing and appearing at this hearing, and $60 for filing this motion. (Compare Yee Decl., ¶¶ 23-26.) The Court notes that while counsel could have done more to explain the years of experience and general grounds supporting counsel’s fee rate, it is a low enough rate in the Los Angeles market as not to require additional support.

Conclusion

Defendant Brian E. Claypool and Claypool Law Firm’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Michelle Leonard’s to Serve Further Verified Responses and to Produce all Responsive Documents for Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 15, 16, and 29 is GRANTED.

Plaintiff Michelle Leonard is ORDERED to provide production to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 15, 16, and  29 within 10 days of this ruling.

Defendant Brian E. Claypool and Claypool Law Firm’s corresponding Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED.

Plaintiff Michelle Leonard, Century Law Group and the Law Offices of Kevin Gerry, jointly and severally, is ORDERED to pay sanctions in the amount of $2,180.00 to Defendants’ attorneys within 30 days of this ruling.

Background Allegations

Pleadings

            Plaintiff Karen Flores (Plaintiff) sues Defendants Everbrands, Inc., a California Corporation (Everbrands CA), Everbrands, Inc., a Delaware Corporation (Everbrands DE) (collectively, Everbrands, Inc.), Michael Florman (Florman), Yon Lai (Lai), and Does 1-25 pursuant to a January 18, 2023 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Failure to Pay a Minimum Wage, (2) Failure to Pay Overtime, (3) Failure to Provide rest Breaks, (4) Failure to Provide Meal Breaks, (5) Inaccurate Wage Statements, (6) Waiting Wage Penalties (Cal. Labor Code §§ 201-203), (7) Unfair Competition and Unlawful Business Practices – (Cal. Bus. Code § 17200), and (8) Failure to Provide Records.

            The claims arise from allegations that when Plaintiff was employed by Defendants, she was paid less than the applicable minimum wage, was not compensated for overtime, and was not compensated for and/or provided meal and rest breaks.

            Motion Before the Court

            On July 24, 2023, Plaintiff served discovery on Defendant Lai. Discovery was served on Allyson K. Thompson, Esq., and Laura P. Birnbaum, Esq., Plaintiff’s counsel. On September 11, 2023, substantive responses were received by Plaintiff from Defense counsel, however the responses were not verified.

            Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter on September 19, 2023, but Defendant did not respond. Plaintiff then filed this motion before the Court, the Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories on October 2, 2023. An opposition was filed by Defendant on November 22, 2023. An untimely one-page reply brief was filed by Plaintiff on December 4, 2023.

 

Discussion

Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Interrogatories -  

            If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subds. (b), (c).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (CCP § 2030.290, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

            If a motion to compel responses to interrogatories is filed, the Court may impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c).)

 

Analysis –

            Plaintiff filed this Motion for the stated purpose of achieving two goals: (1) to obtain verified responses and (2) to issue sanctions in the amount of $3,620.51 against Defendant Lai and his attorney of record. As the first goal has been achieved, the request for an order compelling verified responses is moot.

            For this Motion Plaintiff puts forth the Declaration of Arash Shirdel, Esq. (“Shirdel Dec.”) which states that on July 24, 2023, Plaintiff propounded discovery on Defendant Lai. (Shirdel Dec., ¶ 2.) After an extension was granted responses were served, but without verification. (Shirdel Dec., ¶ 4.) “Unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.” (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.) However, Plaintiff received verified responses on November 13, 2023, albeit two months after the agreed upon deadline of September 11, 2023, and well after this Motion was filed on October 2. (Declaration of Allyson K. Thompson, Exh. H.)

Plaintiff has not provided authority to the effect that, where substantive responses to written discovery are made with verification to follow, a failure to verify within a certain period of time renders the initial unverified responses a nullity such that subsequent verification cannot remedy the lack of verification. The proper vehicle to challenge the sufficiency of the now-verified September 11, 2023 responses is to file a motion to compel further responses to the form interrogatories.

            Thus, the Court finds the motion moot.

Sanctions –

Although the motion is moot at this point, the issue of sanctions still remains. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348 subd. (a).)

Plaintiff Flores brought this motion to obtain verified responses to her discovery request. The verifications provided on November 13, 2023 brought the responses up to Code. Plaintiff thus has the sought after verified interrogatory responses and has not provided authority to the effect that, where substantive responses to written discovery are made with verification to follow, a failure to verify within a certain period of time renders the initial unverified responses a nullity such that subsequent verification cannot remedy the lack of verification. The proper vehicle to challenge the sufficiency of the now-verified responses, is to file a motion to compel further responses. Therefore, the Court declines to impose sanctions at this time.

While the Court recognizes the Plaintiff’s frustration at receiving the verification late, the Court finds that the imposition of sanctions in this case would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. section 2030.300 subd. (d).) Defendant argues that sanctions should be denied because of an inadequate service and meet and confer, claiming the motion was sent only by email without Plaintiff confirming the correct email address as required by Code Civ. Proc. section 1010.6. They further point out that multiple mistakes were made in the proofs of service, including the usage of a name that does not belong in this case. Further, Defendants argue that the Motion should be denied because Plaintiff did not engage in a good faith meet and confer when they only sent over one letter during a time when Defendants’ counsel was heavily involved in preparing a motion for summary judgment in a different case. (Declaration of Allyson Thompson, ¶ 5.)

The Court accepts Defendant’s representations that the failure to provide the verifications was inadvertent, and finds that the verification was thereafter provided. Awarding sanctions in every case where a motion to compel is filed before the responses are provided paints with too broad a brush. When ruling on requests for sanctions in discovery, the Court is called upon to distinguish between cases in which a party is either stonewalling and failing to adhere to its duty to provide discovery, or taking needlessly antagonistic or unreasonable positions in discovery on the one hand, and inadvertence and mistakes on the other. Rare is the attorney who has never made a mistake during litigation; not all such mistakes should result in sanctions. On the other hand, where one party is taking unreasonable positions and failing to cooperate, sanctions are an important tool in gaining cooperation or creating consequences.

Moreover, the amount of sanctions requested in this motion is excessive, where Plaintiff has filed at least four motions on the subject of the failure to provide verifications which are identical but for the precise item of written discovery at issue.

Therefore, the Court finds that the imposition of sanctions in this matter would be unjust.

The Court again encourages the parties to use the Court’s Informal Discovery Conference program which in this case, could have saved Plaintiff from having to file the instant motion to compel and at least three others previously heard.         

Conclusion -

            Accordingly, the Motion is DENIED as MOOT and the request for sanctions is DENIED.
