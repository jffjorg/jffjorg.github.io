DEPARTMENT 74 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 



 




Case Number: 20STCV09612    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 74

Glenn Feldman, et al. v. Fraser Ross, et al.

Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a contractual dispute.

            On March 9, 2020, Plaintiff Glenn Feldman (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Kitross Apparel Los Angeles, LLC and Fraser Ross (Defendants).

            In the complaint, Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, constructive trust, accounting, and fraudulent conversion.

            On August 23, 2023, Defendants served Plaintiff with a notice of deposition. The deposition was set for September 14, 2023.

            On September 7, 2023, Plaintiff served objections to the notice.

            The deposition did not go forward.

            On October 20, 2023, Defendants filed this motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition and request for sanctions.

LEGAL STANDARD

            “The service of a deposition notice . . . is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

DISCUSSION

            Defendants seek to compel Plaintiff’s deposition and production of documents responsive to Request for Production Nos. 1-89. In opposition, Plaintiff first argues the court should deny this motion because Plaintiff raised a valid and timely objection to the notice of deposition.[1] That is, the notice did not state the address where the deposition would take place. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.220, subd. (a)(1), 2025.250, subd. (a).) However, any party may take a deposition by telephone, videoconference, or other remote electronic means. (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1010, subd. (a).) Because the notice indicated that the deposition would be held via video conference, Defendants did not need to provide an address. (Najemy Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. A.) Plaintiff’s objection is unavailing as a result.

            In addition, Plaintiff objects that Defendants failed to sufficiently meet and confer. Defendants have provided evidence to the contrary, though. (Najemy Decl., ¶¶ 4, 5; Ex. C; Ex. D.) Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ meet and confer efforts were not in good faith because Defendants did not appear at their own depositions and rejected Plaintiff’s offers to set firm dates for all party depositions. Such conduct does not excuse Plaintiff from appearing for his noticed deposition, though.

            Last, Plaintiff notes that the parties have agreed to conduct Plaintiff’s deposition on June 5, 2024. (Opposition, p. 2; Reply, p. 3.) But the requests for production remains. Plaintiff’s initial boilerplate objections to the requests are improper. (Korea Data Systems v. Superior Court (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1513, 1516.) Moreover, Plaintiff does not expand on his objections in his opposition or a separate statement. Plaintiff also objects that he has already produced documents responsive to nearly every request. But Plaintiff does not specify which requests he has satisfied.

            Given Plaintiff’s failure to justify his objections to the requests for production of documents, the court finds that sanctions are warranted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g).) Defendants’ counsel’s hourly rate is reasonable, but the court finds the hours for which he seeks compensation excessive. (Najemy Decl., ¶ 12.) Accordingly, the court will reduce the award to    $4,260 ($350 x (4 hours (motion) + 3 hours (separate statement) + 3 hours (review of opposition and reply) + 2 hours (preparation for hearing and hearing)) + $60 filing fee).

CONCLUSION

            The court orders Plaintiff to appear by video conference for his deposition on the agreed date of June 5, 2024. The court grants Defendants’ motion to compel Plaintiff’s production of documents responsive to Request Nos. 1-89. The court grants Defendants’ request for sanctions. Plaintiff shall pay Defendants’ counsel $4,260 in attorney fees and costs within 30 days of this order.

            Defendants shall give notice.

Case Number: 22STCV17079    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 74

Almost Nothing, Inc. v. Omid Reyhanian, et al.

Defendants’ Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a dispute over a lease agreement.

            On May 24, 2022, Plaintiff Almost Nothing, Inc. (Plaintiff) filed a complaint.

            On November 2, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint.

            On March 22, 2023, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (SAC) against Defendants Omid Reyhanian, Peyman Balakhane, and Pejamn Balakhaneh (Defendants), in addition to Pacific Eurotex Corp. (Co-Lessor).

            The SAC alleges the following causes of action: breach of lease, breach of joint venture agreement, breach of warranty of good faith and fair dealing, breach of fiduciary duty, conspiracy to defraud, negligence, conversion, rescission of contract, declaratory relief, and violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200.

            On November 21, 2023, Defendants filed this demurrer.

DISCUSSION 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Lease

            The elements for this cause of action are as follows: (1) a contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for non-performance, (3) defendant’s breach, (4) damages. (Acoustics, Inc. v. Trepte Constr. Co. (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 887, 913.)

            Defendants contend the court should sustain their demurrer for this cause of action because Plaintiff has not alleged that Defendants breached the lease. Rather, Defendants have paid their rent in full. But the parties’ course-of-conduct can aid in contractual interpretation, so long as the conduct does not contradict unambiguous terms. (Wind v. Dancer Production Group v. Walt Disney Pictures (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 56, 72.) For years, Defendants allegedly paid the rent directly to Plaintiff, who then divided the proceeds between Plaintiff and Co-Lessor. (SAC, ¶¶ 10, 11.) Defendants acquiesced to this arrangement. (SAC, ¶ 10.) However, Defendants allegedly breached the lease by paying the rent to Co-Lessor instead of Plaintiff. (SAC, ¶ 13.) Moreover, the alleged course of conduct does not contradict the express terms of the lease: Defendant acknowledges the lease is silent about the delivery of rent. (Demurrer, p. 9.)

            Thus, the court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action.

Third Cause of Action – Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

            The covenant of good faith and fair dealing is implied by law in every contract. (Durrell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1369.) In support of their demurrer, Defendants argue they have paid the rent in full and have not breached an express term of the lease.

            However, breach of a specific provision of the contract is not necessary for a claim for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. (Carma Developers (Cal.), Inc. v. Marathon Development California, Inc. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 342, 373.) The covenant serves to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct that frustrates the other party’s rights to the benefits of the contract. (Thrifty Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230, 1244.)

            Here, Plaintiff is a co-lessor under the lease. (SAC, ¶ 8.) As such, Plaintiff is entitled to rent payments. (SAC, ¶ 8; Ex. 1, ¶ 4.) But Defendants allegedly paid the rent to Co-Lessor in violation of the parties’ course of conduct. (SAC, ¶ 13.) As a result, Plaintiff has not received all rent owed under the lease. (SAC, ¶ 15.) These allegations are sufficient.

            Thus, the court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action.

Fifth Cause of Action – Conspiracy to Defraud

            The elements are as follows: “the formation and operation of the conspiracy, (2) wrongful conduct in furtherance of the conspiracy, and (3) damages. (Kidron v. Movie Acquisition Corp. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1571, 1581.) Moreover, this cause of action requires the commission of an underlying tort by a coconspirator. (Kenne v. Stennis (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 953, 968-969.) Defendants argue in part that Plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to show that they or Co-Lessor committed the underlying tort of fraud. In its opposition, Plaintiff does not sufficiently articulate how Defendants or Co-Lessor committed fraud.

            Thus, the court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to this cause of action with leave to amend.

Sixth Cause of Action – Negligence

            Defendants contend that this cause of action fails because a party ordinarily cannot recover in tort for a breach of contract. (Stop Loss Ins. Brokers, Inc. v. Brown & Toland Medical Group (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1041.)

            In opposition, Plaintiff notes a tortious breach of contract may be found when (1) the breach is accompanied by a traditional common law tort, (2) the means used to breach the contract are tortious, or (3) one party intentionally breaches the contract intending or knowing that such a breach will cause severe harm in the form of mental anguish, personal hardship, or substantial consequential damages. (Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 553-554.) However, Plaintiff does not articulate how Defendants’ alleged breach was tortious under these criteria. Nor does Plaintiff direct the court to supporting allegations.

            The court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action with leave to amend.

Seventh Cause of Action – Conversion

            The applicable elements are as follows: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property, (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights, and (3) damages. (Los Angeles Federal Credit Union v. Madatyan (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1383, 1387.) Here, the property at issue is rent money Plaintiff has not received. (SAC, ¶ 67.) The conversion claim fails because it misapplies the concept. Knowing how much money one is owed – even if to the very penny – does not make that debt “identifiable” personal property subject to conversion because money is fungible. A debt can be paid, among other ways, by cash, check, money order, or even an online banking app, all of which belie a debt being identifiable personal property. Defendants do not owe Plaintiff “identifiable” personal property; Defendants instead owes Plaintiff a debt, which is intangible property to which conversion does not apply. The court therefore sustains Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action with leave to amend.

Eighth Cause of Action – Rescission

            As Defendants note, rescission is not a cause of action. (Nakash v. Superior Court (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 59, 70.) Thus, the court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to this cause of action without leave to amend.

Ninth Cause of Action – Declaratory Relief

            In an action for declaratory relief, the complaint is sufficient if it sets forth facts showing the existence of an actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the parties and requests that the rights and duties be adjudged. (Childhelp, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 224, 235.) Defendants argue this cause of action fails because it adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact, theory, or recovery. The court disagrees. Moreover, Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts: the parties dispute their rights and duties under the lease. (SAC, ¶ 77.) Plaintiff has also requested that the court determine those rights and duties. (SAC, ¶ 77.)

            Thus, the court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action.

Tenth Cause of Action – Unfair Business Practices

            Business and Professions Code section 17200 defines unfair competition as any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practice. To determine whether a business practice is unfair, courts must examine its impact on the alleged victim, balanced against reasons, justifications, and motives of the alleged wrongdoer. (Motors, Inc. v. Times Mirror Co. (1980) 102 Cal.App.3d 735, 740.) Defendants argue Plaintiff has not alleged that they engaged in any unfair business practices. But Plaintiff alleges Defendants are joint venturers in a clothing business. (SAC, ¶ 82.) Defendants entered a commercial lease with Plaintiff and Co-Lessor. (SAC, ¶ 8.) Moreover, Defendants withheld rent from Plaintiff, who was entitled to the payment. (SAC, ¶¶ 13-15, 87.) The utility of Defendants’ conduct is a factual inquiry better suited for a motion for summary judgment. (Motors, Inc. v. Times Mirror Co., supra, 102 Cal.App.3d at p. 740.)

            Defendant also argues that Plaintiff fails to state a claim for recoverable damages. But the unfair competition law permits a party to initiate an action for restitutionary or injunctive relief. (Wilner v. Sunset Life Ins. Co. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 952, 964.) Here, Plaintiff seeks an injunction that would prohibit Defendants from withholding rent payments due to Plaintiff. (SAC, ¶ 87.) Additionally, Plaintiff seeks restitution from Defendants. (SAC, ¶ 89.)

            Thus, the court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action.

CONCLUSION 

The court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the eighth cause of action without leave to amend. The court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action with leave to amend. The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the first, third, ninth, and tenth causes of action.

Defendants shall give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV21745    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 74

Setareh Nelson v. Rombro & Manley, LLP, et al.

Defendants’ Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings

 

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from an attorney-client dispute.

            On September 11, 2023, Plaintiff Setareh Nelson (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendants Rombro & Manley, LLP and S. Roger Rombro (Defendants). The complaint alleges professional negligence – legal malpractice.

            On April 4, 2024, Defendants filed this motion to compel arbitration and stay the proceedings.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants seek to compel arbitration and stay proceedings. To that end, Defendants attach the parties’ attorney-client agreement, which provides for arbitration of all disputes arising from the agreement, including claims of malpractice. (Rombro Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. A.) The court grants Defendants’ motion because Plaintiff signed the agreement and alleges legal malpractice in the complaint. Moreover, Plaintiff has not filed a timely opposition.

CONCLUSION

The court grants Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration and stay the proceedings.

Defendants shall give notice.
