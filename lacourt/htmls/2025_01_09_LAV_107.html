DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03440    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Hasmik Sukiasyan,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

BMW of North America, LLC. 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  23VECV03440                     107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:  Grant in the reduced amount of $26,742.05.

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff Hasmik Sukiasyan (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants BMW of North America, LLC, Mini at Universal City, (“Defendants”) and Does 1 through 50, alleging causes of action for: (1) violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of express warranty; (2) violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of implied warranty; (3) violation of the Song-Beverly Act section 1793.2, subdivision (b); and (4) violation of the Song-Beverly Act section 1796.5.

 

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of settlement.

 

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for attorneys’ fees (the “Motion”). On December 18, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition. On December 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

II.             DEFENDANTS’ OBJECTIONS

 

Defendants raise seven objections to Michael Saeedian’s declaration.

 

This court sustains Objection Nos. 1 through 6 because Saeedian does not appear to have personal knowledge of these matters. (See Evid. Code §¿702.)

 

This court overrules Objection No. 7.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD 

The settlement agreement provided that Plaintiff could recover “‘attorney’s fees and costs reasonably incurred by Plaintiff” by way of a stipulation agreed upon between the parties, or, if the parties cannot agree on an amount, by way of a noticed ‘motion to the Court.’” (Saeedian Declaration ¶ 21.)

 

Further, California Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) states:

 

If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.

 

“The plain wording of the statute requires the trial court to base the fee award upon actual time expended on the case, as long as such fees are reasonably incurred – both from the standpoint of time spent and the amount charged.” (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 817) The “prevailing party has the burden of showing that the fees incurred were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, and were reasonable in amount.” (Id. at 817-818.) “The amount of an attorney fee to be awarded is a matter within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Id. at 822.)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff seeks to recover $30,194.80 in attorneys’ fees and costs. (Motion p. 2.) This includes $28,784.50 for 72.2 hours of billed attorneys’ fees and costs totaling $1,410.30. (Motion p. 2; Saeedian Declaration Ex. 1 pdf p. 18.)

 

  1. Service

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff did not serve Defendants with the Motion at Defendants’ counsel’s correct service address. (Opposition p. 6.) Defendants filed an ex parte application to continue the hearing, but subsequently withdrew the application. Regardless, this court continued the hearing from January 2, 2025, to January 9, 2025. (01/02/25 Minute Order.)

 

  1. Hourly Rate

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rates are excessive and “should be reduced to what is consistent with the ‘2023 Real Rate Report: The Industry’s Leading Analysis of Law Firm Rates, Trends, and Practices.’” (Opposition p. 13.)

 

In the reply, Plaintiff argues “Defendant has intentionally searched for the lowest rates it can, does not state those are for Los Angeles, does not state the rates represent contingency work, and does not explain how those rates reflect the skill level of any attorney in any possible way.” (Reply p. 9.)

 

“In determining hourly rates, the court must look to the ‘prevailing market rates in the relevant community. [Citation] The rates of comparable attorneys in the forum district are usually used. [Citation] In making its calculation, the court should also consider the experience, skill, and reputation of the attorney requesting fees. [Citation] The court may rely on its own knowledge and familiarity with the legal market in setting a reasonable hourly rate. [Citation] ‘Affidavits of the plaintiffs’ attorney and other attorneys regarding prevailing fees in the community, and rate determinations in other cases, particularly those setting a rate for the plaintiffs’ attorney, are satisfactory evidence of the prevailing market rate.’” (Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 1009.)

 

Plaintiff submits a declaration by Plaintiff’s counsel, Michael Saeedian. Saeedian discusses the background and experience of the attorneys, law clerk, and paralegal who billed on this case. (Saeedian Declaration ¶¶ 3-6.) Counsel Michael Saaedian billed at $695.00 per hour, managing attorney Christopher Urner billed at $525.00 per hour, attorney Jorge Acosta billed at $350.00 per hour, and law clerk Evelyn Ghazarian billed at $250.00 per hour. (Saeedian Declaration ¶ 12.) Saeedian states that “[t]he rates charged by my office in this case are below the contingency rates reflected in the Laffey Matrix which is a summary of rates charged by contingency attorneys.” (Saeedian Declaration ¶ 13.)

 

This court finds that the hourly rates billed by Plaintiff’s counsel are reasonable. (See Goglin v. BMW of North America, LLC (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 462, 464, 473-474 [concluding a trial court did not abuse its discretion in finding a $575 hourly rate was appropriate in a Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act action.])¿ 

 

  1. Hours

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff “seeks reimbursement for tasks that were never completed, for example, Plaintiff’s counsel claims that on May 15, 2024, he drafted and served a meet and confer letter regarding dealership discovery responses, which never occurred.” (Opposition p. 5, bolding and underlining in original.)

 

In response to Defendants’ argument that Plaintiff seeks fees for “legal tasks not performed”, Plaintiff states “[t]he letter certainly exists, was drafted and was served on Ms. Silver directly” and submits the letter as an exhibit. (Reply p. 5.) Based on the exhibit, this court is satisfied Plaintiff drafted and sent the meet and confer letter. (See Reply Ex. A.)

 

Defendants also argue that “Plaintiff’s counsel knew that Defendants were interested in settling this matter within two weeks of being served with the Complaint, yet repeatedly elected to ignore defense counsel’s emails and propound unnecessary discovery instead.” (Opposition p. 8.) Defendants’ counsel submits a declaration stating that, two weeks after Defendants were served with the complaint, Defendants’ counsel requested a call with Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss “’coming to an early resolution’ in the case[.]” (Silver Declaration ¶ 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond but “instead chos[e] to proceed with litigation by serving needless discovery on Defendant BMW NA[.]” (Silver Declaration ¶ 3.) Defendants’ counsel stated they reached out to Plaintiff’s counsel about settlement on November 21, 2023, December 12, 2023, and January 3, 2023, but “Plaintiff ignored defense counsel’s attempts to resolve the matter yet again and instead chose to proceed with serving depositions notices on February 21, 2024, discovery requests to the dealership defendant, and further meet and confer letters regarding Defendant’s discovery responses.” (Silver Declaration ¶¶ 4-7.) On March 20, 2024, Defendants’ counsel attempted to speak with Plaintiff’s counsel again about settlement but was unable to reach Plaintiff’s counsel. (Silver Declaration ¶ 8.) Defendants’ counsel’s declaration states:

 

It is undisputed that Defendant took no depositions, conducted no vehicle inspection, and then settled the matter easily without any kind of fight or friction . . .  Plaintiff’s counsel solely served one set of boilerplate discovery requests (Form interrogatories, special interrogatories, requests for admission, and requests for production) only to Defendant BMW NA, as used in most cases with defense counsel’s law firm. Plaintiff then served a second set of only Special interrogatories and requests for production only to BMW NA. No depositions were taken, no vehicle inspections were conducted, and very little discussions took place given that Plaintiff and his counsel chose to sit on their hands throughout the entire year and ignore defense counsel’s emails . . . Other than appearing for a case management conference and serving boilerplate discovery requests and motions, this matter was never actively litigated[.]”

 

(Silver Declaration ¶¶ 12-13.)

 

Finally, Defendants’ counsel states that: “The subject vehicle was not eligible for a buyback under Song Beverly at the time the lawsuit was filed. It was not until the final repair visit one year ago, that the subject vehicle became eligible for a repurchase. Shortly after Defendants became aware of their duty to buyback the subject vehicle, they communicated the offer.” (Silver Declaration ¶ 14.)

 

In the reply, Plaintiff argues this court cannot reduce or eliminate the attorneys’ fees award solely because Defendants wanted to informally discuss settling the case. (Reply pp. 3-4.) Plaintiff states that Defendants’ April 30, 2024, offer was for the statutory repurchase price plus $7,500 in fees and costs. (Reply p. 2.)  Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration also states that the “firm did not have any direct authority to accept or deny a settlement offer” and “[i]t is very rare that a client will try to give us specific ‘blanket’ authority to accept or reject any offer of settlement and because this can easily result in a misunderstanding, we do not accept such authority. Each offer of settlement is handled with limited authority as to that time period only and for a specific amount of money.” (Saeedian Declaration ¶ 3.)

 

Plaintiff cites Reck v. FCA US LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 682, 687, which states “it is an error of law for the trial court to categorically deny or reduce an attorney fee award on the basis of a plaintiff's failure to settle when the ultimate recovery exceeds the Code of Civil Procedure section 998 settlement offer.” (Reply p. 3.) Plaintiff argues “this rule applies with even more force in this case where BMW NA LLC did not make any Section 998 offer and in fact, admits that it only wanted to ‘discuss settlement for months.’” (Reply p. 3.)  Plaintiff also cites Greene v. Dillingham Construction, N.A., Inc. (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 418, 425-426, where the Court of Appeal stated, “to reduce [a party’s] request for fees simply because he rejected an informal settlement offer would be inconsistent with the incentives provided in the [California Fair Employment and Housing Act] to prosecute discrimination claims.” (Reply p. 4.)

 

Based on the authority cited above, this court cannot categorically reduce or deny the attorneys’ fees award solely because Plaintiff rejected an informal settlement offer.

 

Defendants also argue Plaintiff billed attorneys for clerical and/or administrative tasks. (Opposition p. 9.) And Defendants argue that “the billable hours sought in Plaintiff’s Fee Motion are clearly unreasonable and manifestly inflated” or “should have been handled by a paralegal or legal support staff member.” (Opposition p. 11.) The billed amounts here include a series of small entries that, when considered together, are excessive. (Saeedian Declaration Ex. 1.) Plaintiff’s counsel billed 0.1 hours to “Review driver's license from Client”, 0.2 hours for “Receipt and review of Conformed Summons, Complaint, Civil Case Cover Sheet and additional forms and compile documents for Service of Process”, 0.2 hours to “Review Notice of CMC from Court; calculate CMC deadlines and update file re same.”, 0.2 hours to “Review email from Client re service records and vehicle videos”, 0.2 hours to “Analyze additional service records from Client; draft notes and include to file”, 0.2 hours to “Access court docket re case status, download ROA and update file re same”, 0.2 hours to “Receipt of POS of Summons for BMW NA from Process Server”, 0.2 hours for “Receipt of POS of Summons for Mini at Universal City from Process Server”, “0.1 hours for “Receipt and review Notice of Case Reassignment from Court”, and 0.2 hours to “Review email from Client re vehicle registration and service record.” (Saeedian Declaration Ex. 1.) This court finds that these tasks were excessively billed and reduces the total billed amount to 0.9 hours.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel also billed 0.4 hours to “Analyze vehicle repair history report and update file”, 2.2 hours to “Draft Repair Chronology”, 0.7 hours for “Communication with Client re: service history and revise repair chronology re same”, 0.2 hours to “Revise repair chronology”, and 0.4 hours to “Revise Repair Chronology.” (Saeedian Declaration Ex. 1.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s firm purportedly specializes in lemon law actions, and it is excessive for a firm specializing in lemon law cases to spend 3.9 hours drafting and revising the repair chronology for this case. (See Saeedian Declaration ¶ 2.) Thus, this court reduces the billed amount to 1 hour.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel also billed, or anticipates billing, 10.2 hours related to this Motion. This court finds that billing 10.2 hours for this Motion is excessive and reduces the billed amount to 5.1 hours.

 

  1. Pre-Litigation Fees

 

Defendants argue “Plaintiff is not entitled to fees incurred prior to the commencement of an action.” (Opposition p. 12.) Defendants cite Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d), which states that buyers can recover attorneys’ fees “in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” Thus, section 1794, subdivision (d) explicitly states a buyer may recover attorneys’ fees incurred “in connection with the commencement . . . of such action.” (Emphasis added.) Defendants cite no other authority for the proposition that the attorneys’ fee award here cannot include fees incurred prior to drafting and filing the complaint. (See Opposition p. 12.) Accordingly, this court will not reduce the fee award because some of the fees were incurred before Plaintiff filed the complaint.

I.                MOTION TO DEEM RFAS ADMITTED

 

a.     Meet and Confer

 

There is no requirement that parties meet and confer before bringing a deemed admitted motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280. (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 777.)

 

Because Plaintiff states no response was provided to the instant discovery request, there is no requirement for the parties to have met and conferred.¿(Motion to Deem RFAS Admitted p. 3.)

 

b.     Legal Standard

 

When a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the requests, unless the court grants them relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) “ has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2033.210, 2033.220, and 2033.230”; and (2) the “failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a)(1)-(2).)¿  

 

The court shall grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)¿

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)¿ 

 

c.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff served its RFAs to Defendant on August 7, 2024, by mail and email. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4; Ex. 1.) Responses to RFAs are due within thirty days after service, unless the court has shortened or extended the time to respond. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).) When discovery is served by mail to an address within California, the time to respond is extended by five calendar days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013(a).) Accordingly, Defendant Saro Dereghishian’s responses were due by September 11, 2024. Defendant Saro Dereghishian submitted no verified responses by that date. (Javheri Declaration ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant submits a declaration stating Plaintiff served the RFAs to Defendant on August 7, 2024. (Dereghishian Declaration ¶ 2.) And “On September 7, 2024, Defendants were granted an extension to provide responses no later than September 16, 2024.” (Dereghishian Declaration ¶ 3.) Defendant states Defendant served Plaintiff with responses to the RFAs on September 16, 2024. (Dereghishian Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

In response, Plaintiff’s counsel submits a supplemental declaration stating that, on September 13, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel told Defendant’s “paralegal/mediator” that “there is no ‘extension’ to respond and explaining that Defendants have waived any objections and must respond with actual responses (no objections) by September 16, 2024 so Plaintiff is not forced to file a Motion To Compel and Request for Sanctions, including Terminating Sanctions[.]” (Javaheri Supplemental Declaration ¶ 5, bolding in original.) These emails show that on September 13, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel told Defendant “Please be advised that if Defendants do not provide actual responses by Monday September 16, 2024, Plaintiff will be forced to make a Motion To Compel and Request for Sanctions, including Terminating Sanctions.” Defendant responded by emailing: “thank you for the extension and thank you for your cooperation throughout this matter and noted that the responses are due on or before September 16, 2024.” Plaintiff then responded: “Just to be clear, there is no "extension" to respond. Defendants have waived any objections and must respond with actual responses (no objections) by September 16, 2024 so Plaintiff is not forced to file a Motion To Compel and Request for Sanctions, including Terminating Sanctions.” All of these communications are dated September 13, 2024.

 

Although the initial September 13 email is ambiguous re: whether Plaintiff was agreeing to an extension, the subsequent email clarifies that Plaintiff was not agreeing to an extension - but merely stating Plaintiff would file the motions if Defendant did not provide responses without objections.

Plaintiff submits the email correspondence as an exhibit. (Javaheri Supplemental Declaration Ex. 1.) On September 19, 2024, after Plaintiff filed the motion to deem RFAs admitted, Plaintiff received Defendant’s “objection-only ‘responses,’ with no admissions or denials.” (Javaheri Supplemental Declaration ¶ 6.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.260, subdivision (a) provides that “(a) The party requesting admissions and the responding party may agree to extend the time for service of a response to a set of admission requests, or to particular requests in a set, to a date beyond that provided in Section 2033.250.” Subdivision (b) provides that “This agreement may be informal, but it shall be confirmed in a writing that specifies the extended date for service of a response.” Although Defendant submits a declaration stating Defendant received an extension to timely file responses to the RFAs, Defendant submits no writing showing the parties agreed to an extension. In contrast, Plaintiff submits an email showing Plaintiff did not agree to an extension. (Javaheri Supplemental Declaration Ex. 1.)

Defendant also states Defendant timely served Plaintiff with the responses on September 16, 2024. However, Defendant has not submitted Defendant’s responses to the RFAs to this court, so this court cannot determine whether Defendant’s responses were in substantial compliance with section 2033.220. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming its RFAs, Set One, admitted against Defendant.

 

d.     Sanctions

 

Plaintiff seeks $2,608.91 in monetary sanctions against Defendant. (Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted p. 7.) This includes $68.91 in filing fees, four hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing the motion, and one hour for appearing at the hearing. (Javheri Declaration ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant did not timely respond to Plaintiff’s RFAs, Set One. Accordingly, sanctions are mandatory. However, this court finds that four hours in attorneys’ fees for preparing the motion is excessive, given the motion’s simplicity.

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is granted in the reduced amount of $1,268.51, reflecting $68.51 in filing fees, two hours for preparing the motion, and one hour for appearing at the time, at $400.00 per hour.

 

II.             MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

a.     Meet and Confer and Timeliness

 

Plaintiff submits a separate statement stating they are seeking to compel Defendant’s “further responses/amended responses” but given Defendant did not timely serve Plaintiff with responses, this court finds that this should be a motion to compel, not a motion to compel further.

Within the separate statement, Plaintiff correctly points out that Defendant waived objections by not timely serving Plaintiff with responses. (See e.g., Separate Statement p. 2.) Regardless, the motion was timely.

 

“Unlike a¿motion¿to¿compel¿further¿responses, a¿motion¿to¿compel¿responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet¿and¿confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel submits a declaration stating they sent Defendant a meet and confer letter requesting supplemental responses, but Defendant failed to serve any supplemental responses. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 5.) Further, even if the motion to compel responses to Requests for Production was subject to the forty-five day time limit, Plaintiff filed the motion within forty-five days of receiving Defendant’s responses. (See Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.)

¿¿ 

b.     Legal Standard

 

“I]f a party to whom¿a¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]…[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the¿demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.300.) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that . . . (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)¿¿ 

 

“[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(c).¿ 

 

c.      Discussion

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with Plaintiff’s Request for Production. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 2; Ex. 1 pdf p. 22.) On September 7, 2024, Defendant’s “paralegal/mediator” requested an extension to respond. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3.) On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel responded: ““[t]o date, Plaintiff has not received responses to the discovery requests sent to Defendant on August 7, 2024. Please note that Defendants have waived any objections and must respond. Please be advised that Defendants do not provide actual responses by Monday September 16, 2024, Plaintiff will be forced to make a Motion To Compel and Request for Sanctions, including Terminating Sanctions” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3, bolding in original.) On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff “received mostly objection-only responses[.]” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.) The proof of service accompanying the objections stated the responses were served on September 16, 2024. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.) But the envelope was postmarked September 19, 2024. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s “paralegal/mediator” stating that “[t]o avoid Plaintiff having to file a motion to compel on Thursday October 31, 2024, serve Supplemental Actual Responses, without Objections, to Defendant Saro Dereghishian’s “Responses” to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production (inducting actual production of documents), Requests for Form Interrogatories and Requests for Special Interrogatories specified above, on or before the close of business on Tuesday October 29, 2024.” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 5, bolding and underlining in original.)

 

Plaintiff served Defendant with Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories by mail and email on August 7, 2024. Responses are due “[w]ithin 30 days after service of a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260(a).) “In case of service by mail . . . [s]ervice is complete at the time of the deposit, but any period of notice and any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended five calendar days . . . if the place of address and place of mailing is within the State of California[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013(a).)  Thirty days after August 7, 2024, plus five calendar days for service by mail, was September 11, 2024. Plaintiff’s counsel told Defendant “there is no ‘extension’ to respond.” (Javaheri Declaration Ex. 2 p. 1.) Thus, even if Defendant served Plaintiff with the responses on September 16, 2024, and not when the envelope was postmarked on September 19, 2024, Defendant’s responses were untimely.

 

The court may relieve a party from its waiver if: “(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)¿¿¿

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.240, subdivisions (b) and (c)(1) states that:

 

(b) If the responding party objects to the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of an item or category of item, the response shall do both of the following:

(1) Identify with particularity any document, tangible thing, land, or electronically stored information falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made.

(2) Set forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground for, the objection. If an objection is based on a claim of privilege, the particular privilege invoked shall be stated. If an objection is based on a claim that the information sought is protected work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010), that claim shall be expressly asserted.

(c)(1) If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.

Defendant’s untimely responses did not identify the document, tangible thing, land, or electronically stored information falling within the category or set forth the specific ground for the objections. (Javaheri Declaration Ex. 3 pdf pp. 31-53.)  Defendant also raised the attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine, but Defendant did not provide any factual information such that Plaintiff could evaluate the merits of those objections. (See Javaheri Declaration Ex. 3 pdf pp. 31-53.) Further, as to almost all the responses that Defendant provided responses to without objections, Defendant simply repeated: “Plaintiff, has been paid in full and acknowledged payment in full.” (Javaheri Declaration Ex. 2 pdf pp. 48-50.) That response was non-responsive to the applicable Requests for Production. (Ex. 2 pdf pp.48-50.) Accordingly, this court finds that Defendant’s untimely responses were not in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)

 

Defendant has also not argued to this court that Defendant’s untimely responses were due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

 

Thus, Defendant waived any objections to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production.

 

d.     Sanctions

 

Plaintiff seeks $1,460.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 7.) This includes the $60.00 motion filing fee and four hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing the meet and confer letter and motion and attending the hearing. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

Defendant did not timely respond to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production. This court finds Defendant did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances makes awarding sanctions unjust. This court also finds that three hours in attorneys’ fees for preparing the meet and confer letter and motion are excessive, given the motion’s simplicity.

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is granted in the reduced amount of $1,110.00, reflecting the $60.00 motion filing fee, two hours for preparing the motion and supporting documents, and one hour for appearing at the hearing, at $350.00 per hour.

c.      Discussion

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories by mail and email. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 2; Ex. 1 pdf p. 30.) On September 7, 2024, Defendant’s “paralegal/mediator” requested an extension to respond. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3.) Please note that Defendants have waived any objections and must respond. Please be advised that Defendants do not provide actual responses by Monday September 16, 2024, Plaintiff will be forced to make a Motion To Compel and Request for Sanctions, including Terminating Sanctions.” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3, bolding in original.) That same day, Plaintiff’s counsel sent another email “clarifying that ‘there is no “extension” to respond’” and explaining Defendants waived any objections. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3.) On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff “received mostly objection-only responses[.]” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.) The proof of service stated the responses were sent on September 16, 2024, but the envelope was postmarked September 19, 2024. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

Plaintiff served Defendant with Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories by mail and email on August 7, 2024.  Responses are due “[w]ithin 30 days after service of interrogatories[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260.) “In case of service by mail . . . [s]ervice is complete at the time of the deposit, but any period of notice and any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended five calendar days . . . if the place of address and place of mailing is within the State of California[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013(a).)  Thirty days after August 7, 2024, plus five calendar days for service by mail, was September 11, 2024. Plaintiff’s counsel told Defendant “there is no ‘extension’ to respond.” (Javaheri Declaration Ex. 2 p. 1.) Thus, even if Defendant served Plaintiff with the responses on September 16, 2024, and not when the envelope was postmarked on September 19, 2024, Defendant’s responses were untimely.

 

There are a total of 131 Special Interrogatories.

Defendant raised some valid objections. Some of these Special Interrogatories do not appear to be relevant. For example, Special Interrogatory No. 26 asks: “State whether cheating people out of money is normal practice in YOUR family.” (Ex. 3 pdf p. 49.) And Special Interrogatory No. 109 states: “Describe what YOU were wearing at the New Years Eve Event at the VENUE, including the designer name of all articles of clothing.” (Ex. 3 pdf p. 93.) Special Interrogatory No. 110 states: “Describe ANY and All jewelry YOU were wearing at the NEW YEARS EVE EVENT at the VENUE, including the designer name of all Jewelry.” (Ex. 3 pdf p. 94.) . Special Interrogatory No. 111 states: “Describe the watch YOU were wearing at the NEW 1 YEARS EVE EVENT at the VENUE, including the designer name and year purchased.” (Ex. 3 pdf p. 94.)

This case is about Defendant’s giving Plaintiff bad checks. (Complaint p. 4.) These are just a few examples of special interrogatories that this court cannot see how they are relevant to this action.

However, Defendant did not argue Defendant’s failure to serve a timely response was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. And section 2030.290, subdivision (a) states that “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. (2) The party's failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”

The court may relieve a party from its waiver if: “(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)¿ 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.210 states:

 

(a)   The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following:

 

(1)   An answer containing the information sought to be discovered.

 

(2)   An exercise of the party's option to produce writings.

 

(3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.240 states:

 

(a) If only a part of an interrogatory is objectionable, the remainder of the interrogatory shall be answered.

(b) If an objection is made to an interrogatory or to a part of an interrogatory, the specific ground for the objection shall be set forth clearly in the response. If an objection is based on a claim of privilege, the particular privilege invoked shall be clearly stated. If an objection is based on a claim that the information sought is protected work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010), that claim shall be expressly asserted.

Defendant responded to some of the Special Interrogatories with various boilerplate objections and/or by stating “this information is also available to Plaintiff and Plaintiffs counsel who is the wife of Plaintiff.” (Ex. 3 pdf p. 40.) Defendant provided responsive answers to some of the Special Interrogatories. (Ex. 3 pdf p. 42.) Defendant also raised the attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine, but Defendant did not provide any factual information such that Plaintiff could evaluate the merits of those objections. (See Ex. 3 pdf p. 43.) As to other Special Interrogatories, Defendant responded: “Plaintiff, has been paid in full.” (Ex 3. Pdf pp. 95-97.)

 

Even if this court found that Defendant’s answers were in substantial compliance with sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240, Defendant has not argued Defendant’s failure to serve a timely response was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

 

Thus, Defendant waived the objections to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories.

 

d.     Sanctions

 

Plaintiff seeks $1,460.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 7.) This includes the $60.00 motion filing fee and four hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing the meet and confer letter and motion and attending the hearing. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

Defendant did not timely respond to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories. This court finds Defendant did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances makes awarding sanctions unjust. This court also finds that four hours in attorneys’ fees for preparing the meet and confer letter and motion and attending the hearing are excessive, given the motion’s simplicity.

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is granted in the reduced amount of $1,110.00, reflecting the $60.00 motion filing fee, two hours for preparing the motion and supporting documents, and one hour for appearing at the hearing, at $350.00 per hour.

 

IV.           MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES

 

a.     Meet and Confer and Timeliness

 

“Unlike a¿motion¿to¿compel¿further¿responses, a¿motion¿to¿compel¿responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet¿and¿confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel submits a declaration stating they sent Defendant a meet and confer letter requesting supplemental responses, but Defendant failed to serve any supplemental responses. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 5.) Further, even if the motion to compel responses to Special Interrogatories was subject to the forty-five day time limit, Plaintiff filed the motion within forty-five days of receiving Defendant’s responses. (See Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

b.     Legal Standard

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [that party] waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290.) “The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)¿ 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).)¿ 

 

c.      Discussion

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories by mail and email. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 2; Ex. 1 pdf p. 19.) On September 7, 2024, Defendant’s “paralegal/mediator” requested an extension to respond. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel responded: “[t]o date, Plaintiff has not received responses to the discovery requests sent to Defendant on August 7, 2024. Please note that Defendants have waived any objections and must respond. Please be advised that Defendants do not provide actual responses by Monday September 16, 2024, Plaintiff will be forced to make a Motion To Compel and Request for Sanctions, including Terminating Sanctions.” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3, bolding in original.) That same day, Plaintiff’s counsel sent another email “clarifying ‘there is no “extension” to respond[.]” (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 3.) On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff received “mostly objection-only responses” from Defendant. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.) The proof of service stated the responses were sent on September 16, 2024, but the envelope was postmarked September 19, 2024. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

Responses are due “[w]ithin 30 days after service of interrogatories[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260.) “In case of service by mail . . . [s]ervice is complete at the time of the deposit, but any period of notice and any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended five calendar days . . . if the place of address and place of mailing is within the State of California[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013(a).)  Thirty days after August 7, 2024, plus five calendar days for service by mail, was September 11, 2024. Plaintiff’s counsel told Defendant “there is no ‘extension’ to respond.” (Javaheri Declaration Ex. 2 p. 1.) Thus, even if Defendant served Plaintiff with the responses on September 16, 2024, and not when the envelope was postmarked on September 19, 2024, Defendant’s responses were untimely.

 

The court may relieve a party from its waiver if: “(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)¿ 

 

Even if this court found that Defendant’s untimely responses were in substantial compliance with the discovery statutes, section 2030.290 requires both a substantially compliant response and that the failure to serve the timely response was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. Defendant has not argued that Defendant’s failure to serve timely responses was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

 

Thus, Defendant waived the objections to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories.

 

d.     Sanctions

 

Plaintiff seeks $1,460.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 7.) This includes the $60.00 motion filing fee and four hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing the meet and confer letter and motion and attending the hearing. (Javaheri Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

Defendant did not timely respond to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories. This court finds Defendant did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances makes awarding sanctions unjust. This court also finds that four hours in attorneys’ fees for preparing the meet and confer letter and motion and attending the hearing are excessive, given the motion’s simplicity.

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is granted in the reduced amount of $1,110.00, reflecting the $60.00 motion filing fee, two hours for preparing the motion and supporting documents, and one hour for appearing at the hearing, at $350.00 per hour.

 

V.              CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants Plaintiff’s motions to deem RFAs admitted, compel responses to Requests for Production, compel responses to Special Interrogatories, and compel responses to Form Interrogatories.

 

This court also grants Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions against Defendant Saro Dereghishian in the reduced amount of $4,598.51, to be paid within thirty (30) days’ notice of this court’s order.

Tag Holdings Corporation,

David Benzaken, et al.,

 

 Case No.:  24VECV04570

 

 Motion to Quash Service of Summons

 

 

 

Tentative Ruling Deny

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff Tag Holdings Corporation (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants David Benzaken, Basad Corporation, Sapir Razla, Raz Razla, and Does 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract; and (2) indemnification.

 

Plaintiff filed two proofs of service showing Defendants Raz Razla and Sapir Razla (“Defendants”) were served by substituted service on October 23, 2024. (Proofs of Service filed 11/04/24.)

 

On December 6, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to quash service of summons (the ‘Motion”). On December 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On January 2, 2025, Defendants filed a reply.

 

II.             DEFENDANTS’ REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request this court take judicial notice of the proofs of summons. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

The court need not take judicial notice of a documents that are part of the case file. Thus, Defendants’ requests for judicial notice are denied.

 

III.            PLAINTIFF’S REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of Defendant Raz Razla’s California Department of Real Estate License Information. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c) permits the court to take judicial notice of “[o]fficial acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.” This includes taking judicial notice of a license issued by a state agency. (See Arroyo v. Plosay (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 279, 296 [stating a “Hospital’s license issued by the State Department of Public Health was properly subject to judicial notice under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c)[.]”].)  

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is granted.

 

IV.           DEFENDANTS’ OBJECTIONS

 

Defendants raise five objections to evidence submitted with Plaintiff’s opposition:

 

  1. Objection No. 1. Cosgrove Declaration, Paragraph 1: page 1, lines 26-27, stating: “A true and correct copy of the Notice of Acknowledgement and Receipt that was mailed to Raz Razla on September 25, 2024 is attached as Exhibit 1.”
  2. Objection No. 2: Cosgrove Declaration, Paragraph 4: page 2, lines 7-9, stating: “A true and correct copy of the website showing Raz Razla was listed as an employee at HML Investments as of December 10, 2024, and its address is 15391 Chatsworth Street, Granada Hills, California, is attached as Exhibit 4.”
  3. Objection No. 3: Cosgrove Declaration, Paragraph 5: page 2, lines 10-11, stating: “A true and correct copy of the Notice of Acknowledgement and Receipt that was mailed to Sapir Razla on September 25, 2024 is attached as Exhibit 5.”
  4. Objection No. 4: Cosgrove Declaration, Paragraph 6: page 2, lines 12-14, stating: “A true and correct copy of the results of a skip trace for Sapir Razla showing her address to be 14151 Chandler Boulevard, Sherman Oaks, California 91401, is attached as Exhibit 6.”
  5. Objection No. 5: Cosgrove Declaration, Paragraph 7: page 2, lines 15-16, stating: “A true and correct copy of a proof of service in LASC Case No. 24CHLC05154, that showed Sapir Razla was served at 14151 Chandler Boulevard, Sherman Oaks, California 91401 on March 22, 2024, is attached as Exhibit 7.”

 

This court overrules Defendants’ objections.

 

V.              TIMELINESS

 

“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion. . . [t]o quash service of summons[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10(a)(1).) Generally, a responsive pleading is due within thirty days after service of the summons. (Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).) When a party is served by substitute service, “[s]ervice . . . is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20(a).)

 

Plaintiff filed proofs of service showing Defendants were served by substituted service on October 23, 2024. (Proofs of Service filed 11/24/24.) The documents were mailed to Defendants that same day. (Proofs of Service filed 11/24/24.)  Accordingly, service was deemed complete on November 2, 2024, and a responsive pleading was due by December 2, 2024.

 

However, Defendants submit an email from Plaintiff’s counsel stating that “Requests for entry of default will be filed if answers are not filed by December 6, 2024.” (Ex. 1 p. 1.)

 

Accordingly, this court finds the Motion was timely filed.

 

VI.           LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A defendant, . . . may serve and file a notice of motion…to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿418.10(a)(1).) 

 

When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) The plaintiff must establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence. (Aquila, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 568.)

 

“The filing of a proof of service declaration ordinarily creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper, but only if the service declaration ‘complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.’” (Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1164.) That presumption may be rebutted by contrary evidence submitted by a defendant. (Fernandes v. Singh (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 932, 941, fn. 6.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

If service of summons is not properly and validly effectuated by one or more of the approved methods, California courts lack personal jurisdiction over the defendants, and any judicial actions taken without such service violates principles of fundamental due process and is therefore void. (County of San Diego v. Gorham (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1226-1227.)¿

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 415.20, subdivision (a), states:

 

In lieu of personal delivery of a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served as specified in Section 416.10, 416.20, 416.30, 416.40, or 416.50, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in his or her office or, if no physical address is known, at his or her usual mailing address, other than a United States Postal Service post office box, with the person who is apparently in charge thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left. When service is effected by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at a mailing address, it shall be left with a person at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.

 

“[A]n individual may be served by substitute service only after a good faith effort at personal service has first been made: the burden is on the plaintiff to show that the summons and complaint ‘cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered’ to the individual defendant. [Citations] Two or three attempts to personally serve a defendant at a proper place ordinarily qualifies as ‘reasonable diligence.’” (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 389.)

 

VII.         DISCUSSION

 

Defendants argue this court has no personal jurisdiction over Defendants because Plaintiff’s proofs of service “indicate that Plaintiff only attempted personal service one time before leaving the documents with Jane ‘Lynette’ Doe[.]” (Motion p. 2, original underlining, bolding, and italics.)

 

On November 4, 2024, Plaintiff submitted proofs of service showing Defendants were served by substituted service on October 23, 2024. (Proofs of Service filed 11/04/24.) The attached declarations of diligence indicate Plaintiff only attempted personal service once before serving Defendants via substituted service. (Proofs of Service filed 11/04/24 pdf p. 3.) Accordingly, the proofs of service do not show that the summons and complaint could not “with reasonable diligence be personally delivered” to Defendants.   (See American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara, supra, 199 Cal.App.4th at p. 389.) Thus, the proofs of service filed on November 4, 2024, do not create a rebuttable presumption that service was proper.

 

Plaintiff seeks to cure this defect by submitting with its opposition two declarations of diligence showing Plaintiff’s process server made four unsuccessful attempts to serve Defendants at their residence before serving Defendants by substitute service at their business address. (Cosgrove Declaration ¶¶ 2-3, 8; Ex. 2; Ex 8.)

 

In the reply, Defendants argue the declarations of diligence Plaintiff submits with the opposition are insufficient because “not one of the documents attached to the December 26, 2024, Declaration of Phillip R. Cosgrove . . . bears a stamp indicating that Plaintiff submitted the document for filing to the Court” and “Court rules require that a proof of service, in proper form, attesting to proper service be filed with the court.” (Reply p. 2, original underlining, bolding, and italics.))

 

Defendants cite Code of Civil Procedure section 417.30, which states: “After a summons has been served on a person, proof of service of the summons as provided in Section 417.10 or 417.20 shall be filed, unless the defendant has previously made a general appearance.” Defendants also cite Code of Civil Procedure section 417.10, that discusses the manner in which a proof of summons can be served on a person within California.

 

The statutes cited above do not state that a proof of service must be filed with the court as a separate document. Defendants cite no other authority for the proposition a proof of service must be filed with the court as a separate document. Accordingly, this court is not persuaded by Defendants’ argument that Plaintiff’s declarations of diligence are invalid.

 

VIII.       CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies Defendants’ Motion.
