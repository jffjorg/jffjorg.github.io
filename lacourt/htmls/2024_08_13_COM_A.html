DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

Case Number: 21STCV45537    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: A

21STCV45537 Grace Rodriguez, et al. v. Paramount Convalescent Group, Inc., dba Paramount Convalescent Hospital

Tuesday, August 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR A DETERMINATION OF COSTS AND ATTORNEY’S FEES INCURRED IN CONNECTION WITH PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The first amended complaint filed January 8, 2024, alleges that Plaintiff, Grace Rodriguez, is a sister and heir of Evangeline Rodriguez (“Decedent”), who was a resident of Defendant’s skilled nursing facility. Plaintiffs allege that Defendant failed to establish mandated safety protocols due to the COVID-19 pandemic. As a result, Decedent died on December 17, 2020, after contracting the virus. Plaintiffs alleged claims for elder abuse, violation of residents’ rights, negligence, and wrongful death.

       A jury trial commenced on March 13, 2024. The Court declared a mistrial on March 18, 2024, at Plaintiffs’ oral request. On May 3, 2024, the court determined that Plaintiffs were entitled to an award of attorney’s fees and costs pursuant Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5  as a result of defense counsel’s frivolous and bad faith conduct but deferred determination of the amount of fees and costs to be awarded to Plaintiffs until the conclusion of the matter. (M.O. 5/3/24.)

       On June 10, 2024, the court entered judgment on a special verdict in favor of Defendant. (Jgmt. 6/10/24.)

II.      ARGUMENTS

       Plaintiffs request an award of fees totaling $68,450 and costs of $34,857.81, incurred in connection with their motion for sanctions, which the court granted. Plaintiffs contend that the request is warranted given the highly specialized nature of the claims for elder abuse. Plaintiffs request fees for 63.5 hours of work expended as a result of defense counsel’s conduct.

       Defendant argues that Plaintiffs are not entitled to relief because the jury decided in Defendant’s favor. Plaintiffs incurred 13.5 hours attributable to the mistrial. All costs and fees are not recoverable pursuant to the sanctions motion. The court has yet to rule on the amount of sanctions. Plaintiffs seek additional costs and fees which should be denied because Plaintiffs’ motion for sanctions sought $81,536 in fees and costs. Plaintiffs’ request should be reduced to between $10,125 and 14,850 and costs reduced to $8,564.26.

       In reply, Plaintiffs contend that that they seek a determination of fees and costs incurred as a result of the mistrial since the court already determined that Plaintiffs were entitled to an award of sanctions.  

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

       To determine whether fees are reasonable, the court begins with the lodestar, which is the number of hours reasonably spent multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) The court considers a number of factors including "the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.’” (PLCM Group at 1096.) The lodestar figure can be adjusted based on the factors specific to the case. (Id.)

       To determine a reasonable market rate, "the courts will look to equally difficult or complex types of litigation.” (Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 700.) The “market rate” is generally based on the rates prevalent in the community where the court is located. (Id.) The trial court is in the best position to value the services rendered by the attorneys in his or her courtroom for the type of litigation at issue.

       Plaintiffs are entitled to “’compensation for all the hours reasonably spent‘ in litigating the action to a successful conclusion. (Ibid., italics in original.) ‘Reasonably spent’ means that time spent ‘in the form of inefficient or duplicative efforts is not subject to compensation.’”(Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 394.)

 

IV.    DISCUSSION

       The court previously determined that Plaintiffs met their burden of showing that Defendant engaged in “frivolous actions or tactics” entitling Plaintiffs to fees and costs incurred as a result of such conduct. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5 subd. (a); (M.O. 5/3/24.) Whether Defendant is entitled to fees and costs as the prevailing party after trial is a separate matter to be decided under Code Civ. Proc., § 1032 and 1033.5.

       The court grants Defendant’s request for judicial notice of Plaintiffs’ motion for sanctions filed on April 3, 2024, Defendant’s opposition thereto, and the court’s order granting Plaintiffs’ motion for sanctions. (Defendant’s RJN, Exhibits A-C; Evid. Code, § 452(d).)

       While the Court has discretion to award fees based on declarations of counsel describing the work they have and done and the court’s own review of the number of hours reasonably spent, this court cannot determine whether costs and fees were reasonably incurred based on the conclusory declarations of counsel, which do not describe the work done that was required to be duplicated as a result of Defendant’s conduct. (Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 698.)

       Neither Plaintiffs’ motion for sanctions nor the present motion includes billing records. Plaintiffs provided a declaration by Dawn Smith with the motion for sanctions and belatedly provided what appears to be the same declaration in their reply, however, both declarations are unsupported by billing records. Plaintiffs provided a table of costs purportedly incurred in preparation for the first trial, although it is not clear that Plaintiffs were required to duplicate any of those costs at the time of re-trial and is, therefore, attributable to defense counsel’s conduct. (Reply, Ex. ii.) The Smith declaration alone does not provide sufficient facts for the court to exercise its discretion in determining whether reasonable fees and costs can be attributed to Defendant’s bad faith actions or tactics.  Plaintiffs’ counsel did not provide any support, other than their own conclusions, that $750 and $350 per hour are prevailing and reasonable market rates in the community for similar actions.

 

V.      CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion is continued to October 3, 2024,  at 9:15 a.m. in Department A of the Compton Courthouse. Plaintiffs’ counsel is ordered to provide additional evidence, including billing records, preferably in spreadsheet format, and any other evidence to demonstrate that costs and fees incurred prior to the mistrial can be attributed to defense counsel’s conduct sanctionable under Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5. A supplemental declaration with supporting evidence shall be filed 10 court days prior to the hearing.

22CMCV00598 Lourdes Avalos Paz, et al. v. Kia America, Inc.

Tuesday, August 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR AN ORDER AND ENTRY OF JUDGMENT ENFORCING THE SETTLEMENT AGREEMENT

 

I.       BACKGROUND

      The complaint alleges that Defendant issued a written warranty to Plaintiffs in connection with Plaintiffs’ lease of a 2022 Kia Nero-EV. The vehicle developed electrical and other defects. Defendant allegedly failed to fulfill its statutory duties under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“SBA”).

      Plaintiffs accepted Defendant’s statutory offer of compromise to repurchase Plaintiffs’ vehicle for $18,537.60. Plaintiffs dismissed the action on June 25, 2024, with the court retaining jurisdiction pursuant to Civil Procedure Section 664.6.

II.     ARGUMENTS

      Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s deadline to tender the settlement check was June 17, 2024. Plaintiffs have inquired with defense counsel on numerous occasions, but Defendant has not complied with the settlement agreement. There is no issue with surrendering the vehicle since it was repossessed before the case settled. Plaintiffs are entitled to attorney’s fees pursuant to the terms of settlement.

      Defendant argues that it has encountered unavoidable delays due to the late receipt of necessary documents from Plaintiff. On May 3, 2024, Defendant requested that Plaintiffs send a W-9 and pay-off quote for the vehicle. Defendant was unable to open the documents and did receive Plaintiffs’ W-9  until June 17, 2024, when Plaintiffs resent the documents. Defendant requires four to six weeks to process the settlement check. Plaintiffs’ former counsel has a lien on the judgment which must be resolved before the issuance of settlement funds. Plaintiffs are not entitled to post-judgment interest because a judgment has not been issued.

      In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant waited 45 days to request the W-9 form, and there was no issue pertaining to its readability. For the first time, Defendant conditions payment upon resolution of prior counsel’s lien but cites no authority requiring that it be resolved prior to issuing payment.

 

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

      A party can move for entry of judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6.) The court’s power is limited to determining the existence of the agreement and to enforce its settlement. (Corkland v. Boscoe (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 989, 994.) The court may receive oral testimony or may determine the motion upon declarations alone. (Id.)

      The court applies general contract principles when interpreting a settlement agreement.
(Leeman v. Adams Extract & Spice, LLC (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1374.)The mutual intent of the parties and interpretation of the contract are based on the language of the agreement alone. (Id.) In construing a contract, "it is not a court's prerogative to alter it, to rewrite its clear terms, or to make a new contract for the parties."(Moss Dev. Co. v. Geary (1974) 41 Cal.App.3d 1, 9.)

IV.   DISCUSSION

      Plaintiffs signed an offer of compromise made by Defendant on May 2, 2024. (Plaintiff’s Ex. A.) Defendant agreed to pay $18,537.60 within 45 days of acceptance, buy out the lease, and pay any other residual amount to take title to the vehicle from the lienholder. (Id. ¶ 2.) Defendant also agreed to pay Plaintiffs’ costs, expenses, and attorney’s fees as provided by statute. (Id. ¶ 3.) The parties agreed that the court would retain jurisdiction to enforce settlement. (Id. ¶ 4.)

      Defendant failed to comply with its agreement to tender settlement funds within 45 days of acceptance. The terms of settlement do not require Plaintiffs to resolve its contractual lien with prior counsel before Defendant issues the check, nor has Defendant cited any authority that precludes its tender of settlement upon resolution of Plaintiffs’ lien with prior counsel.

      Plaintiffs’ prior counsel filed its Notice of Lien which arose pursuant to its fee agreement with Plaintiffs. (Defendant’s Ex. A, pp. 9.) As such, it is a matter of contract between attorney and client and "must be resolved as between those contracting parties.” (Aresh v. Marin-Morales (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 296, 304; Mojtahedi v. Vargas (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 974, 977–978 ["It is well established that ‘[a]fter the client obtains a judgment, the attorney must bring a separate, independent action against the client to establish the existence of the lien, to determine the amount of the lien, and to enforce it."].) Accordingly, Plaintiffs are entitled to a judgment pursuant to the settlement agreement.

      Plaintiffs have not established why the judgment should include 10 percent interest or how the amount should be calculated. The settlement agreement does not provide for recovery of interest in the event of Defendant’s delay in payment. Accordingly, the court strikes the proposed order requesting interest (Prop. Ord. 2:1-2.)

      The parties’ settlement agreement does not provide for recovery of fees incurred to enforce the settlement. However, as prevailing parties, Plaintiffs are entitled to statutory attorney’s fees that were reasonably incurred “in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794 subd.(d).) Accordingly, the court will consider fees and costs incurred here as part of Plaintiffs’ fee motion.

 

V.     CONCLUSION

      Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion is GRANTED. The court enters judgment pursuant to the parties’ settlement agreement.

     

     


Case Number: 22STCV29728    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: A

22STCV29728 Johnna Mike-Price, et al. v. Toshiba Lifestyle Products and Services Corporation, et al.

Tuesday, August 13,  2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF HOME DEPOT’S PERSON MOST QUALIFIED AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS WITH RESPECT TO INSURANCE COVERAGE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL THE PRODUCTION OF CONSUMER PRODUCT SAFETY COMMISSION REPORT (MIDEA 434-438) WITHELD ON THE BASIS OF PRIVELEGE

 

 

I.        BACKGROUND      

       The complaint alleges that Plaintiffs sustained injury when the minor Plaintiff, Commander Mike-Price, who was then six years old, ingested a button battery from the remote for an air conditioning unit made and sold by Defendants. Plaintiffs allege claims for negligence and strict products liability.

 

II.      ARGUMENTS RELATING TO THE MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

       Plaintiffs argue that they served a Notice of Deposition of Home Depot’s Person Most Qualified (“PMQ”) with production of documents on May 29, 2024. Home Depot objected to the notice on June 6, 2024, stating it would not produce a witness on June 13, 2024, but agreed to identify the scope of permissible inquiry and provide a mutually convenient date for the deposition. Despite Plaintiffs’ effort to meet and confer, Home Depot did not provide alternative dates. On July 1, 2024, Home Depot indicated it would not provide a PMQ on insurance coverage contrary to its earlier responses. Plaintiffs contend that Home Depot’s insurance coverage is relevant and discoverable. Plaintiffs request imposition of monetary sanctions.

       In opposition, Home Depot argues that the purpose of the PMQ deposition is to harass, bully, and intimidate Home Depot. Defendant asserts it served responses to Plaintiffs’ form interrogatory 4.1 identifying the layers of insurance available up to $50 million and various other layers of insurance up to $75 million. Plaintiffs did not request production of any insurance policy or documents. Instead, Plaintiffs stated their belief that Defendant’s declaration does not provide enough information to make a settlement demand. Home Depot’s insurance coverage cannot help Plaintiffs evaluate a settlement demand. Plaintiffs do not require additional information.

       In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Home Depot does not oppose the discoverability of insurance information. Plaintiffs requested a PMQ deposition because Home Depot’s heavily redacted disclosures of insurance are not fully understood by Plaintiffs’ counsel. Plaintiffs require information on how the complex “layers of insurance coverage” work and their effect on indemnity agreements with manufacturers. This is discovery, not harassment.  Insurance information is discoverable to assist in evaluating a case for trial or facilitating settlement.

 

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

       Service of a deposition notice is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action “or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying." (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280 subd.(b).) The attendance and testimony of any other deponent, including for production of documents requires service of a deposition subpoena on the witness. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280 subd. (c).)

       If a party fails to appear for a deposition after service of a deposition notice, without having served a valid objection, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document or tangible thing described in the notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450.) The moving party must show good cause for the deposition and attempt to meet and confer. (Id.)

 

IV.    DISCUSSION

       The scope of discovery is liberally construed in favor of disclosure “as a matter of right unless statutory or public policy considerations clearly prohibit it.” (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court of Merced County (1961) 56 Cal. 2d 355, 377-378.) The broad scope of permissible discovery includes “any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence”. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

       Defendant’s insurance policy information falls within the broad scope of discovery. (Irvington-Moore, Inc. v. Superior Court (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 733, 739. Moreover, "It follows that the selection of the method of discovery to be utilized is to be made by the party seeking discovery. It cannot be dictated by the opposing party." (Id.) Thus, it is not for Home Depot to unilaterally determine that Plaintiffs are not entitled to the deposition to explain the application of Home Depot’s insurance coverage.

       Home Depot has not offered any argument to support redacting insurance information, nor has it moved for a protective order. The discovery of insurance coverage information is relevant to Plaintiffs’ preparation for settlement. (Pettie v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1960) 178 Cal.App.2d 680, 690 ["as far as the investigation and conduct of the defense is concerned, it would seem to be relevant, if not indispensable, that plaintiff's attorney have knowledge of the existence of insurance in order to prepare for the case he has to meet and be apprised of his real adversary. Such knowledge, furthermore, would also lead to more purposeful discussions of settlement, and thereby effectuate the dispatch of court business. This aspect is most significant in terms of effective judicial administration in coping with today's congested dockets which are largely attributable to the increasing volume of personal injury litigation.”].)

       Plaintiffs are entitled to an award of monetary sanctions against Home Depot. While Plaintiffs’ counsel requests $700 per hour to prepare this discovery motion, the court finds that $450 per hour is reasonable under the circumstances. The court finds that four hours incurred to prepare the motion, reply, and to appear at the hearing is reasonable.

 

V.      CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel the deposition of Home Depot’s PMQ is GRANTED. Home Depot is ordered to designate and produce a person qualified to testify to the topics as set forth in Plaintiffs’ Notice of Deposition and produce unredacted documents responsive to Plaintiffs’ request. The court imposes sanctions of $1,800 against Defendant, Home Depot, payable to Plaintiffs’ counsel within 10 days.

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL THE PRODUCTION OF CONSUMER PRODUCT SAFETY COMMISSION REPORT (MIDEA 434-438) WITHELD ON THE BASIS OF PRIVELEGE

 

I.        ARGUMENTS RELATING TO THE MOTION TO COMPEL PRODUCTION OF CPSC REPORT

       Plaintiffs argue that the court ordered Defendants, Wuhu Maty, and GD Midea, (“Defendants”) to produce jurisdictional discovery without objection. (Creed Decl, ¶ 9, Ex. 1.) Defendants’  partial production included a privilege log identifying in part a document marked Midea 434-438 consisting of a report authored by Jonathan Judge at ArentFox Schiff, LLP, counsel for Midea America, regarding the remote for the air-conditioning unit at issue, and sent to the Consumer Production Safety Commission (“CPSC Report.”) Defendants assert the report is not discoverable on grounds of attorney-client and work product privileges. (Creed Decl., Ex. 3.) Plaintiffs assert that the cited privileges do not apply because the report was transmitted to a government agency, not to a client. By doing so, Defendants waived the privileges. The CPSC Report is required by federal law with the expectation that it would be disclosed to a government agency, and therefore, its preparation for and transmission to federal authorities constitute a waiver of such privileges. Defendants have not produced any confidentiality agreement with the CPSC to protect against disclosure of the document.

       All Defendants oppose the motion on grounds that the CPSC Report is confidential as provided by statute governing an attorney’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, legal research or theories. The work product privilege is held by the attorney and not the client. The CPSC Report is clearly marked as confidential and is derived from attorney-client communication and falls within the definition of work product. It also includes Midea’s or others’ trade secrets. It is protected from disclosure as it is “self-critical analysis.” The CPSC cannot publicly disclose reportable information under circumstances defined by statute, and none of the statutory criteria apply. The case on which Plaintiffs rely (McKesson) is distinguishable. The product of Defendants’ internal investigation is protected by the attorney work product privilege.

       In reply, Plaintiffs assert that Defendants concede that the CPSC Report is discoverable and is not attorney work product because it was prepared with the expectation that the report would be revealed, at least to federal authorities. Defendants improperly rely on Regents v. Superior Court, which involved documents that were not created with the expectation of public disclosure to federal authorities and were not required by law. The CPSC’s interest in requiring the report at issue to protect the public from unreasonable risks of injury associated with consumer products are aligned with Plaintiffs’ identical interests. Defendants improperly rely on an amicus brief which is irrelevant.  

II.      DISCUSSION

       The attorney-client privilege does not apply to the CPSC Report at issue as it does not involve a communication between attorney and client. A “confidential communication” for purposes of the privilege is “information transmitted between a client and his or her lawyer in the course of that relationship and in confidence by a means which, so far as the client is aware, discloses the information to no third persons other than those who are present to further the interest of the client in the consultation or those to whom disclosure is reasonably necessary for the transmission of the information or the accomplishment of the purpose for which the lawyer is consulted, and includes a legal opinion formed and the advice given by the lawyer in the course of that relationship." (Evid. Code, § 952.) Defendants concede that the report was provided to CPSC by Midea America “in good faith consistent with the self-reporting requirements of CPSA section 15b, 15 U.S.C.2064(b) - - which require companies to report potentially defective or unsafe products - - and the information disclosure procedures in section 6(b) (15 U.S.C. 2055(b)), which work together to strike a balance between important, competing interests.” (Opp. 16:16-20.)

       A writing that reflects an attorney’s impression, conclusions, legal research, or theories are not discoverable  "under any circumstances." (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030 (a).) If the writing does not reflect impressions and conclusions under subpart (a) the privilege is qualified; it is not discoverable unless the court determines that denial of discovery will "unfairly prejudice the party seeking discovery in preparing that party's claim or defense or will result in an injustice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030 (b).) The only exception to the bar against discovery is "under the waiver doctrine, which has been held applicable to the work product rule as well as the attorney-client privilege.” (McKesson HBOC, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1229, 1239.) Whether A waiver of the privilege occurs "by an attorney's ‘voluntary disclosure or consent to disclosure of the writing to a person other than the client who has no interest in maintaining the confidentiality of the contents of the writing.’” (Mckesson at 1239.)

       Defendants do not dispute that the CPSC Report at issue was required by federal statute. (Opp. 16:16-17; 15 U.S.C.A. § 2064 subpart (b) [A manufacturer of a consumer product who obtains information to support a conclusion that a product fails to comply with a consumer product safety rule or contains a defect which could create a substantial product hazard or creates an unreasonable risk of serious injury or death "shall immediately inform the Commission" of such defect or risk.].) Plaintiffs’ reliance on Cloud v. Superior Court (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1552 has merit.

       The “self critical analysis privilege” has not been recognized by the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals. (T.W.A.R., Inc. v. Pacific Bell (N.D. Cal. 1992) 145 F.R.D. 105, 108.) Under state law, no person (including a businesses) “has a privilege to refuse to disclose any matter or to refuse to produce any writing, object, or other thing” except as otherwise provided by statute. (Cloud v. Superior Court (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1552, 1558.) The California Evidence Code exclusively provides for available privileges "and the courts are not free to create new privileges as a matter of judicial policy.” (Cloud at 1558–1559.)

       Cloud involved a federal statute requiring contractors to discuss hiring, firing, and promotion practices. (Cloud at 1556.) Cloud instructs that even if an attorney prepared the self-critical analysis, "the analyses are conducted and written with the expectation that they will be revealed, at least to the federal authorities. As with the lawyer-client claim, no basis has been presented to justify withholding the analyses on the basis of attorney's work product." (Cloud at 1560.) Defendants’ did not address Cloud and its implications in its opposition.

       Defendants contend that the CPSC Report is confidential and not subject to disclosure under federal law where the information is marked as a confidential trade secret. (15 U.S.C.A. § 2055 subpart (a)(2).) Defendants have not provided evidence to support that any information contained in the report is a trade secret.

       Defendants argue that the CPSC report cannot be disclosed by the Commission, if it is confidential trade secret information, the company enters into a voluntary recall, the Commission has begun an administrative proceeding for mandatory recall, the Commission finds that public health and safety requires disclosure, or the Commission believes that the product is in violation of the CPSA. (15 U.S.C.A. § 2055 subpart (b)(5).) That section governs the Commission’s disclosure of information; it does not govern the discoverability under California law of a document that Defendants were required to disclose.

       Defendants argue that Southern California Edison Company v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 573 holds that the mandatory report is not subject to disclosure. That case concerned discovery of internal investigations conduct by SCE in order to comply with state law. (Id. at 578.) SCE was required by law to a report accidents occurring on its property that directly or indirectly arose from its operation that resulted in loss of life or injury. (Id.)

       The case is distinguishable because the requesting party did not seek the report of accidents required to be submitted, but rather emails initiated by in-house counsel that were part of an attorney-led investigation. There, the court concluded that the dominant purpose of the documents requested was to facilitate the provision of legal advice to the client. (Southern California Edison at 589.) The court applied the work product protection as it advanced an important public policy to “allow attorneys the freedom ‘to investigate not only the favorable but the unfavorable aspects’ of a client's situation, and in turn will promote more accurate and effective investigations." (Southern California Edison at 590.) Here, Plaintiffs do not seek production of investigative efforts including attorney emails that may have facilitated the mandatory CPSC Report submitted to the Commission. Plaintiffs do not ask for preliminary, investigative documents preceding the final report submitted to the CPSC. Southern California Edison does not apply.

       Defendants also cite  Regents of University of California v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 672 which is equally distinguishable as it determined that the opposing party did not waive the attorney work product privilege by providing results of its internal investigation conducted by counsel to federal agencies. (Regents at 676.) In Regents, one of the defendants obtained an agreement from the government under which the government agreed that disclosure was not a waiver of the attorney-client and work product privilege. (Regents at 676–677.) Additionally, the court held that the failure to cooperate with a government’s request for documents pursuant to a government investigation for fraud would subject the defendants to severe regulatory or criminal consequence if they were labeled as “uncooperative.” (Regents 678 and 684 ["because of the dramatic impact that failure to cooperate might have on the corporations and the absence of any cost-free redress, the trial court here correctly found that the defendants' cooperation with the government did not waive the attorney-client and attorney work product privileges. Under the circumstances existing at the time the cooperation took place, it would not have been reasonable for the defendants to resist or otherwise challenge the government's requests."].)  The factual circumstances at issue in Regents are not present in this matter.

VI.    CONCLUSION

       Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion is GRANTED. Defendants are ordered to produce to Plaintiffs, without objection, the CPSC Report at issue within 10 days. As Plaintiffs did not request imposition of sanctions, sanctions are not awarded.

Case Number: 23CMCV01157    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: A

23CMCV01157 Goldorazena, et al v. Felipe Gonzalez, Jr. Mark Hickman

 

 Tuesday, August 13, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING THE HEARING ON PLAINTIFFS’ REQUEST FOR COURT JUDGMENT AGAINST DEFENDANT, MARK HICKMAN

 

 

       The first amended complaint alleges that Plaintiffs suffered injury arising from a car accident allegedly caused by Defendants that resulted in the wrongful death of their decedent, Megan Goldorazena. Defendant, Felipe Gonzalez, Jr. answered the complaint on June 7, 2024. The clerk entered default against Defendant, Mark Hickman, on June 17, 2024.

       Plaintiffs allege that both Defendants were jointly liable for Plaintiffs’ injuries. Plaintiffs must submit a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment. (Cal Rules of Court 3.1800 (7).) Plaintiffs have not explained why a separate judgment is appropriate in this case.

       Plaintiffs state that they were not able to personally serve Hickman with the amended Statement of Damages. Plaintiffs served the Statement of Damages by mail. (Plaintiffs’ Ex. D, E.) Where a party has not appeared in an action, the Statement of Damages must be served in the same manner as a summons before default may be taken. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11 subpart (c), (d).) Therefore, the court cannot issue a judgment of default against Defendant Hickman for improper service of the Statement of Damages.

       Assuming Plaintiffs can properly serve Defendant Hickman with the Statement of Damages,  Defendant is entitled to the same period of time within which to respond to the Statement of Damages as would have been allowed to respond to an amended complaint. (Plotitsa v. Superior Court (1983) 140 Cal.App.3d 755, 761.) Section 425.11 “was designed to give defendants ‘one last clear chance’ to respond to allegations of complaints by providing them with ‘actual’ notice of their exact potential liability." (Connelly v. Castillo (1987) 190 Cal.App.3d 1583, 1588. [Declined to follow Plotitsa’s 30-day rule but required that defendant be given “reasonable” time to respond to the Statement of Damages before default may be taken.].)

       While a defaulting defendant confesses the material allegations of the complaint, the court is required to hear the evidence supporting the amount of damage sought. (Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357.) Plaintiffs submit the declaration of their Guardian ad Litem, Decedent’s fiancé, stating conclusory allegations of damages. (Decl of Larry Lucas.) Plaintiffs in a wrongful death action are entitled to recover damages for their own “pecuniary loss, which may include (1) the loss of the decedent's financial support, services, training and advice, and (2) the pecuniary value of the decedent's society and companionship—but he may not recover for such things as the grief or sorrow attendant upon the death of a loved one, or for his sad emotions, or for the sentimental value of the loss. " (Nelson v. County of Los Angeles (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 783, 793.) Accordingly, “ ‘the court or jury must compute damages, if any, by considering the pecuniary damage suffered by all the heirs and return a verdict for one sum.’" (King v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 440, 450.) Plaintiffs have not provided any evidence relevant to a determination of the pecuniary value of loss sustained. Plaintiffs do not explain how they arrived at a principal damage of $710,000. Nor is there evidence of funeral expenses incurred. (Lucas Decl., ¶ 8.)

       Plaintiffs request for attorney’s fees of $12,465.25 can only be awarded if permitted by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5 (a) (10.) Plaintiffs have not identified a basis for an attorney’s fees award.

       Plaintiffs’ request for “other” costs of $1,337.50 is not substantiated. Permissible costs as a prevailing party must be reasonably incurred, reasonably necessary, and reasonable in amount. Plaintiffs have not demonstrated that costs identified as “other” are permitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5 subd. (c).)

       Plaintiffs allege that Defendant Hickman was the owner of the vehicle operated by Defendant Gonzalez. Plaintiffs have not explained why the statutory limits of owner liability do not apply. (Veh. Code, § 17151 ["($15,000) for the death of or injury to one person in any one accident and, subject to the limit as to one person, is limited to the amount of thirty thousand dollars ($30,000) for the death of or injury to more than one person in any one accident and is limited to the amount of five thousand dollars ($5,000) for damage to property of others in any one accident.”].)

       Based on the foregoing, the court continues the hearing to October 8, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. in Department A of the Compton Courthouse. Plaintiffs are ordered to submit an amended request for court judgment 10 court days before the hearing supported by evidence that cures the foregoing defects.

 


Case Number: 23CMCV01435    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: A

23CMCV01435 Susan Boyd v. City of Compton, et al.

Tuesday, August 13, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEMURRER BY CROSS-DEFENDANT, CITY OF COMPTON, TO THE CROSS-COMPLAINT FILED BY GUANDA, LLC

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The complaint alleges that Plaintiff slipped and fell on an uneven sidewalk. Plaintiff alleges claims for negligence and premises liability. On January 9, 2024, Defendant, Guanda, LLC (“Guanda”), filed a cross-complaint for indemnity, declaratory relief, contribution, and apportionment against the City of Compton (“Cross-Defendant” or “City”). On May 1, 2024, the City of Compton filed a cross-complaint for indemnity and contribution against Cross-Defendant Guanda, LLC.

       Cross-Defendant City demurs to all claims alleged in the Guanda cross-complaint as they are barred by Guanda’s failure to submit a timely claim in compliance with the Government Claims Act (“GCA.”) The claims are now time barred. Guanda did not allege a basis for a duty allegedly owed by City to indemnify Guanda for injuries sustained by Plaintiff, or a basis for contribution, apportionment, or declaratory relief in the event of a judgment against Guanda.

       Cross-Complainant Guanda did not file an opposition by Wednesday, July 31, 2024 (nine court days of the hearing.) Code Civ. Proc., § 1005 (b).

II.      LEGAL STANDARDS

       A demurrer tests the sufficiency of the pleading as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.) In testing its sufficiency, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The Court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638.) The Cross-Complainant must show that the pleading alleges facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where the pleading fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1126.)

       Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case stated with reasonable precision to acquaint the Cross-Defendant with the nature, source, and extent of the alleged cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.) A demurrer may be sustained based on uncertainty, where the factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise an opposing party of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

       A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.)

 

III.    DISCUSSION

A.      The City has not established that Guanda failed to timely present a claim to the City under the GCA.

       A party cannot commence an action for money or damages against a public entity unless it first presents a timely claim to the public entity. (Gov. Code, § 945.4.) A “public entity” “includes the state, the Regents of the University of California, the Trustees of the California State University and the California State University, a county, city, district, public authority, public agency, and any other political subdivision or public corporation in the State. (Gov. Code, § 811.2.)

       The City argues incorrectly that presentation of a claim is “a condition precedent to the maintenance of any cause of action against a public entity.” (Dem. 10:24-5.) A claim for declaratory relief does not require compliance with the GCA. “[O]nly claimants seeking money or damages, as distinguished from other types of judicial relief, are required to conform to the claims procedure.”  (Minsky v. City of Los Angeles (1974) 11 Cal.3d 113, 121, quoting Van Alstyne, California Governmental Tort Liability (1964), §§ 8.8-8.9, p 363–365.) The claims statutes do not “impose any . . . requirements for non-pecuniary actions, such as those seeking injunctive,12 specific, or declaratory13 relief.” (Id.)  Thus, the claim for declaratory relief is exempt from the GCA. 

       However, where the cross claims for equitable indemnity, contribution, and apportionment are based on facts beyond those alleged in the City’s answer, compliance may be necessary as a new investigation of the facts is required. (Southern California Edison Co. v. City of Victorville (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 218, 239.) The cross-complaint alleges that Guanda gave timely written notice of its claim to the City on January 8, 2024 although the City contends it was due on September 14, 2023, one year after the date Plaintiff allegedly suffered injury. (Cross-complaint, ¶ 7; Dem. 11:5-9.) 

       The deadline for presenting a claim for a cause of action other than death or injury to a person is no later than one year after the accrual of the cause of action. (Gov. Code, § 911.2.) "[F]or statute of limitations purposes, the defendant's indemnity action does not accrue until he has suffered actual loss through payment." (Valley Circle Estates v. VTN Consolidated, Inc. (1983) 33 Cal.3d 604, 612.) Accordingly, Defendant has not established that Guanda’s cross-complaint is time barred.

 

B.      The claim for equitable indemnity claim is adequately alleged.

       The principle behind equitable indemnity is to allocate loss among multiple tortfeasors on a comparative fault basis. (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc. v. Forcum/Mackey Construction, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852 ["The doctrine applies only among defendants who are jointly and severally liable to the plaintiff."].) There must be some basis for tort liability against the proposed indemnitor (ReCon). (Id.)

       The City’s duty to indemnify Guanda may arise “when in equity and good conscience” the burden of judgment in Plaintiff’s favor should be lifted from Guanda and imposed on the City, the Cross-Defendant, alleged in the complaint to be a joint tortfeasor.  (Fireman’s Fund Ins Co. v. Haslam (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1347, 1353-1354.) The right depends on the principle that everyone is responsible for the consequences of his or her own wrong. (Id.).

       To state an indemnity claim, the Cross-complaint must allege “(1) a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor [City] and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee [Guanda] for which the indemnitor is ... equitably responsible." (Bailey v. Safeway, Inc. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 206, 217.) Guanda alleges that if Plaintiff proves damages, they were caused by the City’s primary and active negligence and not by any active negligence on Guanda’s part. (Cross-complaint, ¶ 9.) Guanda adequately alleged a basis for the City’s duty to indemnify arising from the City’s acts in causing Plaintiff’s injuries. The City’s “actual fault” is alleged and is adequate under

Bailey. (Id. at 217.) Moreover, to the extent the City alleges that Guanda failed to allege facts to support the City’s fault, Guanda incorporated the facts alleged in the complaint and attached the complaint to the cross-complaint. (Cross-complaint, ¶ 6, Ex. A.)

 

C.      Demurrer to the implied indemnity claim lacks merit.

       Generally, the duty to indemnify may be expressly provided by contract, implied from a contract not specifically mentioning indemnity, or it may arise from the equities of a particular situation. (Bear Creek Planning Com. v. Title Ins. & Trust Co. (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 1227, 1237.) Guanda did not allege a basis for implying a duty to indemnify other than that the  Plaintiff’s injuries were caused solely by the City. (Cross-complaint, ¶ 12.) Thus, this claim is based on the same facts to support equitable indemnity and is duplicative as it asserts no other basis from which the duty can be implied.  The City, however, did not demur on this ground, and has not shown how that duplication causes prejudice. (Dale v. City of Mountain View (1976) 55 Cal.App.3d 101, 105 ["The court must in every stage of an action disregard any defect in the pleadings that does not affect the substantial rights of the parties."].)

 

D.     The claim for declaratory is adequately stated.

       The claim for declaratory relief seeks a judicial determination of the Cross-Defendant’s duties and obligations to defend and indemnify Guanda for the alleged misconduct. A claim for declaratory relief is sufficient “if it sets forth facts showing the existence of an actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties.” (Condor Ins. Co. v. Williamsburg Nat. Ins. Co. (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 554, 565.) As this claim is “wholly derivative”  of the first cause of action for equitable indemnity, the declaratory relief claim adequately states a basis for a declaration of rights. (Ball v. FleetBoston Financial Corp. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 794, 800.)

 

E.      Contribution is adequately stated.

       In contrast to indemnity claims, a claim for contribution “distributes the loss equally among all tortfeasors, each bearing his pro rata share. (E. W. Bliss Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 210 Cal.App.3d 1254, 1261.) The City argues Guanda did not allege facts regarding the City’s obligation to reimburse Guanda if found liable for Plaintiff’s injuries. (Dem. 10:7-9.)  The City reads this portion in isolation. The Cross-Complainant should be read as a whole and is sufficient if it apprises the City of the basis for the claims against it. (Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 684, 690.) The cross-complaint, which incorporates facts alleged in the complaint, is adequately stated under this standard.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendant’s Demurrer to the Cross-Complainant is OVERRULED. Cross-Defendant is ordered to file and serve its answer forthwith.

24CMCV00181 Mortgage Assets Management, LLC v. People’s Federal Savings and Loan Association

Tuesday, August 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SUBSTITUTE PARTY PLAINTIFF

 

     

      The complaint alleges that Plaintiff holds a 2006 deed of trust encumbering real property to secure a reverse mortgage obtained by the homeowner. Through a series of assignments, Plaintiff is the current beneficiary of that trust deed. Plaintiff determined through a title report that Defendant holds a deed of trust that is superior to Plaintiff’s that should have been released after multiple refinances, but was never reconveyed. Plaintiff alleges claims for declaratory relief and cancellation of instrument. The clerk entered default against Defendant on July 30, 2024.

            Plaintiff seeks an order to substitute PHH Mortgage Corporation (“PHH”) in place of Plaintiff because the beneficial interest in the 2006 deed of trust at issue has been assigned to PHH. (Code Civ. Proc., § 368.5 ["An action or proceeding does not abate by the transfer of an interest in the action or proceeding or by any other transfer of an interest. The action or proceeding may be continued in the name of the original party, or the court may allow the person to whom the transfer is made to be substituted in the action or proceeding."].)

      Plaintiff submits an assignment of deed of trust recorded on April 28, 2024, establishing that Plaintiff assigned the deed of trust at issue to PHH. (Charles Correia Decl., Ex. A.)

      Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED. This action shall be continued in the name of PHH Mortgage Corporation as Plaintiff.
