DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV18236    Hearing Date: January 11, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 January 11, 2024¿ 

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                  21STCV18236

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Homa Deris v. Byron Kim, DDS, et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Defendant, Kevin Choi, DDS and Bardi-Nugent Dental Corporation dba Rootvision Endo

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff, Homa Deris

¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        March 22, 2024

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Summary Judgment and in the alternative, Summary Adjudication

                                               

¿ Tentative Rulings:                 (1) Denied as to the malpractice cause of action but MSA granted as to the concealment, fraud, and Elder Abuse causes of action

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On May 14, 2021, Plaintiff, Homa Deris (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, Byron Kim, DDS, Rojin Erfan, DDS, Walter Jefferson, DDS, Walter Jefferson Dental Corporation dba PCH Smiles Dentistry and Orthodontics, Kevin Choi, DDS, Bardi-Nugent Dental Corporation dba Rootvision Endo, and DOES 1 through 10. On October 26, 2021, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Dental Malpractice; (2) Fraudulent Concealment; (3) Intentional Misrepresentation; (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (5) Elder Abuse. 

 

Defendants, Kevin Choi, D.D.S. (“Choi”) and Bardi-Nugent Dental Corporation dba Rootvision Endo (“Rootvision”) now file a Motion for Summary Judgment or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On October 23, 2023, Defendant, Choi and Rootvision filed a Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication. On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On January 5, 2023, Choi and Rootvision filed a reply brief.  The Court previously ruled on a dispositive motion brought by co-Defendant Dr. Erfan in October of 2023.

 

 

 

II. ANALYSIS¿ 

 

            Here, Choi and Rootvision argue that there is no triable issue of fact for Plaintiff to establish the elements necessary for her Dental Malpractice, Fraudulent Concealment, Intentional Misrepresentation, and Elder Abuse/Neglect causes of action against Choi and Rootvision.

 

A. Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿ 

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520. ) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿ (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ 

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.¿¿¿ 

To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) 

 

B. Discussion 

 

Here, Choi and Rootvision assert that their motion is based on five interrelated grounds: (1) compliance with the applicable standard of care for Dental Malpractice; (2) Lack of causation of Plaintiff’s claimed injuries, (3) disclosure of all treatment options by Dr. Choi so there could be no fraudulent concealment; (4) lack of any untrue statement or representation of fact, particularly as to the success or lack of success of the root canal procedure, and (5) lack of any substantial caretaking or custodial relationship as required for a health care provider to be subject to the enhanced remedies of the Elder Abuse Act, per Winn v..Pioneer Medical Group.  The Court will discuss each ground in turn below. 

 

Dental Malpractice

 

Here, Defendants Choi and Rootvision argue that Plaintiff’s cause of action for Dental Malpractice fails because Plaintiff cannot establish the requisite elements of Breach and Causation. In a medical malpractice action, a plaintiff must establish the following elements: “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.  [citations.]”  (Galvez v. Frields (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 1410, 1420.)

 

Standard of Care

 

Defendants Choi and Rootvision provide the declaration of John Buoncristiani, DDS as evidence that moving defendants did not fall below the standard of care. Defendants Choi and Rootvision note that Dr. Buoncristiani’s declaration establishes that to a reasonable degree of dental and medical probability, all of the dental and endodontic care and treatment provided by Dr. Choi to the Plaintiff complied at all times with the applicable standard of care for Endodontists, practicing in the Southern California community then and now. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶¶ 88-89.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that triable issues of material fact exist and first argues that Defendant Choi and Rootvision’s expert witness provided ultimate facts without reasoned explanation and does not satisfy the standard for summary judgment motions. Specifically, Plaintiff contends that Dr. Buoncristiani’s declaration is conclusory and unsupported by evidence. For example, Plaintiff contends that when providing a narrative of the record that Dr. Buoncristiani reviewed, he states in paragraph 7(f) that on February 4, 2020, Dr. Choi’s “goal was to remove all the existing materials inside tooth #9, disinfect the tooth, and place medication inside the tooth.” However, Plaintiff asserts that Dr. Choi did not write any notes as to what his goal was on this date, and thus, such a statement is Dr. Buoncristiani’s assumption, and speculation as to what Dr. Choi’s goal might have been on this specific date.

 

Plaintiff also submitted a declaration from expert witness Stephen Howard, DDS, MSCD, FASDC, FIAO to support her contention that Defendants Choi and Rootvision breached the standard of care in numerous ways. First, Dr. Howard contends that both Dr. Choi as well as Dr. Buoncristiani are Endodontists, and as such, Dr. Howard believes that they should be expected to provide endodontic diagnosis and evaluation to a probable degree greater than what was done by Dr. Choi. (Declaration of Stephen Howard, DDS, MSCD, FASDC, FIAO (“Howard Decl”), ¶ 19.) However, Dr. Howard contends that as endodontists, Dr. Choi approached Plaintiff’s pain issues strictly from the perspective of root damage and treatment within tooth #9. (Howard Decl., ¶ 22.) Ultimately, Dr. Howard asserts that while the initial diagnosis and treatment of tooth #9 on February 5, 2019 was correct, the diagnosis and treatment subsequent to that time by Dr. Erfan, and then by Dr. Choi was below the standard of care. (Howard Decl., ¶ 31.)

 

In their reply brief, Choi and Rootvision argue that Plaintiff’s expert witness is not qualified to rebut Defendants’ expert because Dr. Howard is not an endodontist and is not qualified to opine on the standard of care for endodontics. Defendants Choi and Rootvision argue it was Plaintiff’s responsibility to submit a competent expert testimony that Dr. Choi breached the standard of care for endodontics to survive summary judgment. Moving Defendants also argue that Dr. Howard does not opine that Dr. Choi’s actions caused or worsened Plaintiff’s alleged injury.

 

A similar argument was made in the Motion for Summary Judgment by Dr. Erfan. Similar to the Court’s decision in that motion, the Court disagrees as to Defendants’ narrow view of what the standard of care implicated here is, and the Court disagrees that on a Section 437c motion the Court can weigh the qualifications of competing expert witnesses. Even though it appears that Defendants’ expert appears to have more relevant qualifications and expertise, Plaintiff’s expert meets the minimum level of qualifications for an expert witness. It will be for the jury to decide as to what weight they will give to the competing experts’ qualifications and those of the moving defendants, and whose opinion the jury determines to be the more persuasive. Plaintiff’s FAC alleges that Dr. Choi negligently performed a second root canal although he was fully aware that the first root canal was not done properly, that the tooth was not viable enough for a second root canal, and that this second root canal would not undo the damage already done with the first root canal and would not resolve Plaintiff’s constant pain and potentially make the tooth’s condition worse. (FAC, ¶ 60.)   The Court’s view is that Dr. Howard is not required in an MSJ motion to provide testimony as to whether the root canal itself was done properly, or whether the circumstances surrounding the performance of the root canal fell below the standard of care.

 

            The Court finds that triable issues of fact have been raised by the Declaration of Dr. Howard as to the standard of care.

 

Causation

 

            Defendants Choi and Rootvision also argue that there are no triable issues of fact that they caused Plaintiff’s injuries. As to this issue, moving Defendants rely on Dr. Buoncristiani’s declaration in which he opined that, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, that no action or omission on the part of Dr. Choi or Rootvision proximately caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s claimed injury. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 215.) Instead, Dr. Buoncristiani contends that due to Plaintiff’s unchanging pain following an extraction, it is highly probable that Plaintiff’s pain was neurogenic and that tooth #9 was not the source of Plaintiff’s pain. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 240.)

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff notes that Dr. Howard opined that Defendants, including Dr. Choi at Rootvision caused Plaintiff’s complaints and injury, including the trigeminal neuralgia that she suffers from today. (Howard Decl., ¶ 29.)

 

            Based on the differing expert opinions, the Court finds that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the causation element of Dental Malpractice.

 

Fraudulent Concealment

 

Next, Defendants Choi and Rootvision argue that Plaintiff’s claim for fraudulent concealment does not have triable issues of material fact. “The elements of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment are: (1) concealment of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose; (3) the defendant intended to defraud by failing to disclose; (4) plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as it did had it known the fact; and (5) damages.” (Butler America, LLC v. Aviation Assurance Company, LLC (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 136, 144.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

In Plaintiff’s FAC, she alleges that Dr. Choi was aware that the root canal done by Dr. Erfan had failed but failed to disclose this fact to Plaintiff in an attempt to protect PCH Smiles. (FAC, ¶ 77.) Moreover, Plaintiff contends that Dr. Choi was aware that tooth #9 was not viable enough for a second root canal and that the second root canal would not undo the damage already done to the tooth, but he nevertheless concealed this fact from Plaintiff and instead advised her that he could perform a second root canal, thus advising her that a second root canal was a viable option that could save her tooth. Plaintiff notes this was not true. (FAC, ¶ 77.) Dr. Choi argues that this is incorrect, noting that Dr. Choi testified that he never became aware that Plaintiff had a root canal done on tooth #9 by Dr. Erfran at PCH Smiles. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 244.) Moving Defendants also argue that there is no evidence that Dr. Choi failed to disclose any fact to Plaintiff to protect PCH Smiles. Dr. Choi presents his testimony that he has never had any communication with Dr. Erfran and has only spoken to Dr. Kim once on the phone. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 246.) Moving Defendants also argue that Plaintiff cannot establish he concealed any facts during retreatment or other treatment actions. Moving Defendants assert that on multiple occasions, the evidence indicates Dr. Choi informed that Plaintiff’s prognosis of her tooth #9 was guarded, meaning the treatment was questionable. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 249, Choi Deposition, p. 38.) Dr. Choi’s testimony from his deposition also indicates that he discussed the treatment options, noting re-treatment or apical surgery or extraction/implant were discussed. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 250.) Moving Defendants emphasize that Plaintiff herself even testified that Dr. Choi stated: “if [the second root canal] doesn’t work, you have to extract the tooth,” confirming that the potential for failure was communicated to her. (SSUMF ¶ 251.) Lastly, moving Defendants argue Plaintiff’s allegation that she was given no other option except retreatment is incorrect based on Dr. Choi’s testimony that he gave Plaintiff two treatment options (retreatment or possible apicoectomy or extraction and implant) (Defendants’ SSUMF, ¶ 252), and Plaintiff’s admission Dr. Choi gave her a second option other than retreatment (extraction) (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 252.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that moving defendants have not met their burden of proof because: (1) Rootvision initially “concealed” 26 pages of records from Plaintiff; (2) none of Dr. Choi’s alleged handwritten notes were given to Plaintiff prior to the action being filed, but were given on January 13, 2023; (3) the records provided prior to her filing of the suit contained notes stating she had endodontic treatment, and notes for the second root canal; (4) that Choi had talked to Dr. Kim, was aware of root canal done by PCH Smiles; (5) that records showing Choi did not conceal information from Plaintiff were not provided to Plaintiff prior to her filing the action, but only during discovery, raising a triable issue of material fact as to whether the records were intentionally concealed from Plaintiff or fabricated by Defendants after Plaintiff’s lawsuit; (6) that Plaintiff testified Choi did not tell her the cause of the pain in tooth #9; and (7) That in the settlement agreement raised by defendants in their motion, Plaintiff testified that the agreement concerned the treatment rendered by Choi and Rootvision and that she was told that she was not going to receive further treatment if she did not sign it, thus, forcing her to sign it – and that there is triable issue of fact as to why she was forced to sign the agreement by Dr. Kim.

 

Here, the Court is confused as to Plaintiff’s opposition points. First, the discussions of the alleged concealment of documents not provided prior to the lawsuit being filed, but which were provided after discovery had been conducted. The Court is unsure how this raises a triable issue of fact as to the fraudulent concealment claim. These factors are not present in the required elements of a claim for fraudulent concealment, nor touch on what Plaintiff is claiming was concealed from her: i.e., that the first root canal failed, that her tooth was not viable enough for a second root canal, and that the second root canal would not undo the damage already done to the tooth. In disputing Defendants’ SSUMF, Plaintiff contends that these facts are disputed on the grounds that: (1) The records provided show that Dr. Choi notated in his records that Plaintiff was referred for a second opinion as to an RCT done with Dr. Kim and that Choi testified he spoke on the telephone with her; (2) that it is disputed whether Choi fell below the standard of care; (3) that Plaintiff is questioning whether the records relied on were generated subsequent to Plaintiff filing her lawsuit.

 

The Court does not believe that any of these arguments dispute the facts provided by moving Defendants. The fact that Choi spoke with Dr. Kim after a referral does not evidence the claimed proposition that he knew the first root canal had failed. Further, the disputed fact that Choi fell below the standard of care does not provide evidence that his genuine belief was that the prior root canal was done properly, or that his own treatment may fail, that the treatment was guarded, or that the treatment may not be successful. Besides the expert testimony being argued as to this issue, and the Plaintiff’s own testimony as to what WAS disclosed to her, the only piece of evidence this Court has is Dr. Choi’s declaration.

 

On a Motion for Summary Adjudication, the Court is required to consider inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence presented by the declarations for and against the motion, and to deny the motion unless the only reasonable inferences to be drawn in favor of the moving party are uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence. Here, there is no reasonable inference that could be drawn of intentional or deliberate concealment of a risk of bodily harm or recuperative problems after Dr. Choi’s procedures had been completed. There is no chart entry and no radiograph and no other evidence from which a reasonable jury could infer that Dr. Choi fraudulently concealed anything. Accordingly, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication as to the concealment cause of action.

 

Intentional Misrepresentation

 

Defendants Choi and Rootvision also argue that there are no triable issues of material fact as to the cause of action for intentional misrepresentation. “The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

Moving Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s cause of action for intentional misrepresentation is based upon the allegation that Dr. Choi represented to Plaintiff that he was able to perform a second root canal on tooth #9 and thus, also represented to her that this tooth was viable enough for a second root canal. (FAC, ¶ 88.) However, Plaintiff asserts in her FAC that this was false because the tooth was not viable enough for a second root canal and the only possible option was to extract it. (FAC, ¶ 89.) Defendants rely on the deposition of Dr. Choi, noting that he informed Plaintiff of the retreatment’s potential for failure and presented Plaintiff with alternate treatment options. (Defendants’ SSUMF ¶ 257.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that although Dr. Choi stated this in his deposition, Dr. Choi also admitted in his deposition that he had no notes as to whether he explained to Plaintiff what retreatment of tooth #9 meant, no notes as to whether the patient agreed to retreatment of tooth #9, and testified he had no recollection of his treatment of this patient, and could only testify from his notes, and records of Rootvision. While this is true, the deposition of Plaintiff does indicate: “According to the chart, [Dr. Choi] discussed the treatment options to the patient that re-treatment or apical surgery or extraction/implant was discussed with the patient and also prognosis was discussed. And the prognosis is guarded, which is questionable, due to possible fracture of the tooth. So we instructed the patient to discuss with – the findings or the options treatment options with the general dentist.” (Deposition of Dr. Choi, p. 37:9-37:16.) Plaintiff again argues that Dr. Howard’s declaration notes that Dr. Choi’s treatment fell below the standard of care, but as this Court has previously noted, simply because an individual fell below the standard of care does not mean they intentionally did so or knowingly did so – as this cause of action seems to allege. Plaintiff provides no evidence that Dr. Choi knew the second root canal would not work despite telling her it might not and if so she would lose the tooth.  

 

Instead of providing any evidence at all under this argument heading besides her evidence of Dr. Choi potentially treating below the standard of care per Dr. Howard’s declaration, in opposition, Plaintiff merely argues that fraud is a question of fact to be decided by a jury, and thus this motion should be denied. Although this is true, Plaintiff’s lack of any evidence, besides her expert opinion as to the alleged malpractice shows that Plaintiff has no evidence of Dr. Choi’s scienter/knowledge that a second root canal would fail and that extraction was the only option. Even if Plaintiff were to show that Defendant fell below the standard of care, this would not be sufficient to show that Defendant Choi knew the root canal would certainly fail. It is not fraudulent to tell a patient that a procedure may or may not be successful if it later turns out the procedure did not succeed.  In this case, the Court finds that Defendants Choi and Rootvision have carried their initial burden, but that Plaintiff was unable to carry the shifted burden. As such, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication as to this issue.

 

Elder Abuse

 

Finally, Defendants Choi and Rootvision argue that there are no triable issues of material fact as to Plaintiff’s cause of action for Elder Abuse. To state a cause of action for elder abuse and neglect, Plaintiff must allege “[p]hysical abuse, neglect, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering.” (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.07, subd. (a)(1).) Acts which are prohibited under the Elder Abuse Act provisions of the Welfare and Institute Code “do not include acts of simple professional negligence, but refer to forms of abuse or neglect performed with some state of culpability greater than mere negligence.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 781.) To constitute abuse and neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act, thereby triggering the enhanced remedies available under the Act, Plaintiff “must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of something more than negligence; he or she must show reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct,” with recklessness referring “to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a “deliberate disregard” of the “high degree of probability” that an injury will occur,” and “[o]ppression, fraud and malice [involving] intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 88-89, quotation marks omitted.)

 

Plaintiff’s FAC alleges that Dr. Kim forced her under duress and/or undue influence to sign a settlement agreement to release Dr. Choi of any liability for the second root canal because she was made to believe she was not going to receive dental treatment if she did not sign the agreement (FAC, ¶ 117.) The FAC also alleges that she was financially abused because Dr. Kim, instead of covering Dr. Choi’s fee of $1,200, since Plaintiff had already paid the fee for the first unsuccessful root canal. (FAC, ¶ 119.) As to Dr. Choi, Plaintiff contends that the unnecessary root canal done by him as well as his concealment of the true condition of her tooth, placed her at risk of pain, infection, loss of teeth and greater bodily injury. (FAC, ¶ 120.)

 

As noted above, a claim for elder abuse need show more than mere professional negligence. Instead, clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of something more than negligence; he or she must show reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct,” with recklessness referring “to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a “deliberate disregard” of the “high degree of probability” that an injury will occur,” and “[o]ppression, fraud and malice [involving] intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature.” (Sababin, supra, 144 Cal.App.4th at 88-89.)

 

Moving Defendants argue that the Elder Abuse Act “does not apply unless the defendant health care provider had a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship, involving ongoing responsibility for one or more basic needs, with the elder patient.” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 152.) Defendants argue that here, that there is no “substantial caretaking or custodial relationship” between Plaintiff and Dr. Choi as she only had seven (7) visits with him, three of which were consultations. Winn explained at p. 156 the scope of the Act as follows:

 

“The Elder Abuse Act's heightened remedies are available only in limited circumstances. A plaintiff must prove, by clear and convincing evidence, that a defendant is liable for either physical abuse under section 15610.63 or neglect under section 15610.57, and that the defendant committed the abuse with “recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.” (§ 15657.) Section 15610.57, in turn, provides two definitions of neglect.  First, “[t]he negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder or a dependent adult to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.” (§ 15610.57, subd. (a)(1).) Second, “[t]he negligent failure of an elder or dependent adult to exercise that degree of self care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.” (Id., subd. (a)(2).) Because plaintiffs allege neglect arising in the context of medical care and not self-care, we deal only with section 15610.57's first definition of neglect.” 

 

Here, the Court finds, as did the California Supreme Court in Winn, that there is no proof by the Plaintiff that the allegedly neglectful health care provider defendant had the requisite care or custodial relationship with the plaintiff in order to be subject to the Act’s enhanced remedies beyond those in a garden-variety malpractice case.  The sort of conduct the Legislature sought to address from individuals “having the care or custody” of an elder seems to contemplate is “the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship — that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder's basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.”  (Winn, supra, 63 Cal.4th at p. 158.)  Dentists, endodontists, and periodontists are not providing for an elder patient’s “basic needs” that the patient would “ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance;” rather, Dr. Kim and Dr. Choi provided highly specialized and expert treatment (competently or not) that an able-bodied patient could not provide for herself.  The Winn Court explained that “beyond doubt” the Legislature was “distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together -- claims of medical malpractice [from claims of] neglect.”  (Id. at p. 159.) 

 

Several cases decided by Courts of Appeal after Winn confirm this Court’s analysis.  (See Kruthanooch v. Glendale Adventist Medical Center (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1128 (Second District affirmed the granting of JNOV in defendant MRI technologist’s favor where no evidence was presented at trial of the “robust caretaking or custodial relationship” required by the Act); Oroville Hospital v. Superior Court (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 382, 389 (based on the absence of a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship by the defendant in-home nurses who provided wound care but were not Decedent’s caretaker, plaintiffs failed to raise a triable issue of material fact so the Third District granted a writ directing the trial court to grant the defense MSA.)  These cases are much more akin to the episodic role played by the moving defendants than the more robust caretaking role undertaken by the acute care facility defendants in Stewart v. Superior Court (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 87, 102 where summary adjudication of the Elder Abuse cause of action was denied.

 

As such, this Court determines that Defendants Choi and Rootvision have carried their initial burden of showing this Court that they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law as Plaintiff’s Elder Abuse cause of action.  Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of fact that the required custodial relationship or basic needs care exists here as to the moving defendants’ involvement with Plaintiff.  As such, the Motion for Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Fourth Cause of Action for Elder Abuse is GRANTED.

I. BACKGROUND 


A. Factual 


On December 30, 2022, Plaintiffs Susan Udewitz and David Udewitz (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, South Coast Botanic Garden Foundation, Inc, County of Los Angeles, and DOES 1 through 25. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Negligence; and (2) Loss of Consortium. 

 

On April 13, 2023, Plaintiffs assert that Plaintiff, Susan Udewitz served her first sets of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents, and Requests for Admission on Defendant, South Coast Botanic Garden Foundation, Inc. (“SCBGF”). Per the motions, defendants’ responses were originally due on May 15, 2023, but that numerous extensions were granted to Defendant SCBGF through August 26, 2023.  As of that date, Plaintiffs claim that objections were waived by Defendant SCBGF due to the expiration of the last extension to “respond” even though subsequent extensions were granted to “answer” without objections through November 16, 2023. However, to date, Plaintiffs assert that SCBGF had not provided any responses to the written discovery as of the date these motions were filed.  

 

On April 13, 2023, Plaintiffs also served Susan Udewitz’s first set of discovery on Defendant, County of Los Angeles (“County”). Plaintiffs note that discovery responses were due on May 15, 2023. Similarly, Plaintiffs note that after numerous extensions were granted, on August 26, 2023, objections were waived by Defendant County. Plaintiff continued to provide extensions for answers only – no objections – to and including November 16, 2023. However, Plaintiff notes that Defendant County still had not answered the written discovery as of the filing of these motions.  

 

Defendants essentially fall on the sword of their current counsel Jaion Chung, who admits to carelessness in the handling of the discovery requests and responses thereto resulting from a series of mis-handling errors both by prior counsel and by current counsel who had associated in on July 7, 2023 and substituted in as of August 25 for the SBBG and as of September 8, 2023 for the County.  Essentially, Ms. Chung asserts that prior counsel did nothing on the discovery requests, that she was unaware of the discovery requests until a September 28, 2023 meet and confer letter from Plaintiffs’ counsel, and that she mishandled the retrieval of the file from former counsel and dropped the ball in early November for several weeks after the last of plaintiffs’ extensions expired.   

 

B. Procedural


On November 21, 2023, Plaintiffs filed these motions for orders compelling. On December 28, 2023, both defendants filed oppositions. On January 3, 2024, Plaintiffs filed reply briefs to both oppositions.  


II. ANALYSIS


Motion to Compel Defendant South Coast Botanical Garden Foundation’s Responses 

 

Plaintiffs request this Court order Defendant SCBGF to answer Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, without objections, and deem the truth of the matters specified in Plaintiff’s Request for Admission (Set One) admitted. Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions as discussed below.  

 

In opposition, Defendant SCBGF argues that this motion is now moot as it has served verified responses on November 29, 2023. Plaintiffs’ Reply admits that counsel received the belated responses but that the responses are inadequate in several respects, the responses contain preliminary sets of objections as well as individual objections to certain of the requests, and other deficiencies.  Defendants fail to attach their responses to the discovery for the Court to determine whether their responses are in substantial compliance; only an email referencing pdf digital files was attached to the Chung declaration. (Plaintiffs’ reply papers included the November 29, 2023 objection-filled responses, so the Court was able to determine the extent of the responses’ compliance.)  The Chung declaration fails to explain the week and a half delay after counsel finally received the entire file where SCBGF could have but failed to provide timely responses to discovery before counsel went out of town on a pre-planned vacation. Further, even though not granted, defense counsel’s request to extend the response time for two more weeks on November 2, 2023, would have had their responses to Plaintiffs by November 16, 2023, but instead, they were not provided nearly two weeks later, and after the motions were filed. While Plaintiffs’ reply papers argue that Ms. Chung’s excuses do not rise to the level required by Section 473(b), the Court disagrees.  The Court finds defense counsel to be remorseful, she admitted a series of mistakes, and implicitly accepts responsibility for the consequences despite the embarrassment of having to do so.   

 

Based on this, the Court determines that the motions to compel initial discovery responses have been mooted by the service and admitted receipt of objection-filled responses long after objection had been deemed to be waived.  However, the belated service of the responses does not moot the requests for monetary sanctions, which will be discussed below.   

 

The parties’ briefing suggests that Plaintiffs next would file motions to compel further (i.e., not initial) responses, for an order overruling all objections, and for additional monetary sanctions.  To guide the parties and potentially avoid another set of additional motions, the Court will issue an advisory ruling to perhaps limit the need for much of that anticipated further activity.   If the parties have or can stipulate to extend the 45-day period to move to compel the November 29, 2023 discovery responses, the Court’s advisory ruling is that, based on the record currently before the Court, the Court would be inclined to require Defendant SCBGF to provide verified further or supplemental responses, without objection, to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One by January 31, 2024. Moreover, this Court would be inclined to require SCBGF to provide further or supplemental verified responses to the requests for admission with no objections, also by January 31, 2024.  These advisory rulings are without prejudice to a future motion to compel further responses to the subject discovery requests. 

 

Sanctions 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Defendant SCBGF in the amount of $2,475. Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel.¿¿(CCP. §§ 2030.290(c),¿2030.300(d),¿2031.300(c),¿and 2031.310(h).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”¿¿(Id.)¿ The Court does not find substantial justification but does find that the belated responses only came as a result of the motions being filed, which justifies mandatory monetary sanctions. 

Plaintiffs’ sanction amount is based on Plaintiffs’ counsel spending 3.5 hours preparing this motion and engaging in meet and confers, an anticipated 2 hours traveling and attending the hearing, and an hourly billing rate of $450 an hour. In opposition, Defendant SCBGF argues that sanctions should be denied due to the handling attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, and excusable neglect, but the Court disagrees with defense counsel. Even without receiving the final extension requested, Defendant SCBGF still failed to provide the answers within the two weeks which it claimed it would need to complete discovery, still failed to provide objection-free responses, and essentially required these motions to be filed in order to facilitate service and receipt of the responses.  The objection-filled discovery responses were not provided until six business days after the filing of these motions. The claim that the sanctions request was mooted is unfounded.  

The Court GRANTS the monetary discovery sanctions as to SCBGF and its counsel in the lowered amount of $1,750, to be paid to Plaintiffs’ counsel by January 31, 2024. 

Motion to Compel Defendant County of Los Angeles’ Response 

 

 

In addition to their motions against SCBGF, Plaintiffs also request this Court to order Defendant County to answer Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Production of Documents without objections. Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions as discussed below.  

The Court’s analysis and rulings as to the County are the same as to the SCBGF.  The motions to compel initial responses are mooted but the monetary sanctions are not.  The COuort’s advisory opinion is the same as tot eh County for the same reasons as outlined for the SCBGF.   The Court GRANTS the motion for sanctions against Defendant County and its counsel of record in the lowered amount of $1,750. This amount is to be paid to Plaintiffs’ counsel by January 31, 2024.
