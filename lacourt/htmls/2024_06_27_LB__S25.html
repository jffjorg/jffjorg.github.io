DEPARTMENT S25 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23LBCV00315    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S25

Factual Background

On February 21, 2023, Plaintiff Victor M. Morales (“Plaintiff”) filed this lemon law action against Defendant Ford Motor Co. (“Defendant”) for violations of the Song Beverly Act. Plaintiff alleges that he purchased a 2020 Ford F-150 (“Subject Vehicle”) that contained and developed defects such as engine, electrical, emission, and transmission system defects. (Compl., ¶¶ 8-10, 18; Altman Decl., ¶¶ 6-9.) Plaintiff alleges that presenting the Subject Vehicle to Defendant’s authorized repair facility on numerous instances to repair the defects, but none of the defects were permanently repaired. (Compl., 18, 22-24, 37; Altman Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.)

Procedural History

On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff served a notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMK and request for production of documents for the deposition to take place on September 7, 2023. (Altman Decl., ¶ 15, Exh. 2; Lee Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) On September 1, 2023, Defendant served objections to Plaintiff’s Deposition notice and indicated that witnesses would be produced as to some but not all of Plaintiff’s “Matter of Examination.” (Altman Decl., ¶ 16, Exh. 3; Lee Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. C.) On September 28, 2023, Defendant offered its witness availability for December 7, 2023, and requested Plaintiff served an amended notice, but received no response. (Lee Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. E.) Subsequently, on October 24, 2023, Defendant contacted Plaintiff again regarding its witness’ availability on December 7, 2023, but received no response. (Lee Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. F.) On November 30, 2023, Defendant notified plaintiff that is witness was no longer available on December 7, 2023, but would be available on January 31, 2024. No response was received from Plaintiff. (Lee Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. H.) On December 21, 2023, Defendant notified Plaintiff if no amended notice for the deposition was received by January 2, 2024, Defendant would release their witness’ availability. No response was received to this request. (Lee Decl., ¶ 10, Exh. I.) On April 15, 2024, Plaintiff sent an amended notice for Defendant’s PMK for a deposition on April 29, 2024. (Altman Decl., ¶ 17, Exh. 4; Lee Decl., ¶ 14, Exh. K.) On April 24, 2024,

Defendant responded to the amended notice with objections and an indicated no produce a deponent. (Altman Decl., ¶ 18, Exh. 5; Lee Decl., ¶ 15, Exh. L.)

Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of the following exhibits:

· Exhibit 1: The Los Angeles Superior Court’s Order on Ford’s Ex Parte Application to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition, dated February 19, 2024, in Victor Morales v. Ford Motor Company, Case No. 23GDCV00315

· Exhibit 2: The Los Angeles Superior Court’s Order on Ford’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition, dated May 9, 2024, in Victor Morales v. Ford Motor Company, Case No. 23GDCV00315.

Defendant’s request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code, § 452, subds. (d) & (h).

Meet and Confer

Plaintiff sent a letter to Defendant on April 15, 2024, regarding Defendant’s objections to the notice of deposition and Defendant’s opposition to producing its PMK. (Altman Decl., ¶ 21, Exh. 7.) In response, Defendant stated discovery was closed. (Ibid.) On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff requested Defendant produce its PMK for deposition; Defendant again indicated that the discovery window was closed, thereby, requiring Plaintiff to file the instant motion. (Altman Decl., ¶ 22, Exh. 8.) Plaintiff believes Defendant has not participated in meet and confer attempts in regard to the PMK deposition and its request for production of documents. (Altman Decl., ¶ 24.) Defendant contests Plaintiff’s declaration, asserting that Defendant reached out to Plaintiff at least four times on September 28, 2023, October 24, 2023, November 30, 2023 and December 21, 2023 to schedule the PMK deposition, but received no response from Plaintiff each time. (Lee Decl., ¶¶ 6-10, Exhs. E, F, H, I.) The Court finds both sides have fulfilled the meet and confer requirements.

Ruling

As set forth in the February 16, 2024 Minute Order, all discovery cut off dates remained based on the March 4 2024 trial date. (February 16, 2024 Minute Order; Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”), Exh. 1.) Accordingly, fact discovery window closed on February 5, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).) Plaintiff served an amended notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMK on April 15, 2024 and filed the instant motion on May 16, 2024. The Court finds Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel as untimely as there was no extension of any discovery deadlines. While the Court has discretion to grant leave to reopen discovery under Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, the Court notes that Plaintiff has not submitted a response to Defendant’s Opposition as to why Plaintiff failed to respond to Defendant’s multiple attempts, over several months, to comply with the deposition request. Accordingly, the motion is DENIED

Case Number: 23LBCV01324    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

On July 14, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants Berro Property Management, Jonathan Hove and Does 1 to 25. In the complaint, Plaintiffs allege five causes of action: (1) Negligence; (2) Constructive Eviction; (3) Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability; (4) Trespass; and (5) Negligent Hiring and Supervision. Plaintiffs allege that on November 25, 2022 a fire occurred at Plaintiff’s residence, a complex on 403 W. 4th Street, Long Beach, CA 90802 (“property”) and that the property was owned by Defendant Jonathan Hove and managed by Defendant Berro Property Management (“Defendant Berro”). (Compl., ¶¶ 1-3, 8, 11.) Plaintiffs allege that the day before, on November 24, 2022, Defendant Berro had hired Doe 1 to make repairs in plumbing in one of the lower unit bathrooms and that an investigation by the Long Beach Fire Department determined the fire was caused by an open flame from a propane torch Doe 1 was using to perform plumbing repairs. (Compl., ¶¶ 10, 11.) Plaintiffs also assert that the property lacked working smoke detectors and they were displaced from their home due to the fire. (Compl., ¶¶ 12, 14.)

On August 22, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Doe amendment to the complaint, amending Defendant Jonathan Hove to Defendant Halj Apartments, LLC. (“Defendant Halj”). On October 24, 2023, Defendant Berro filed an answer. On January 12, 2024, Defendant Halj filed an answer. On March 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Doe amendment to the complaint, amending Doe 1 to CCL Handyman Corporation.

On May 24, 2024, Defendant Berro filed the instant motion for leave to file a cross-complaint. As of June 21, 2024, no opposition has been filed. No reply has been filed either.

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 428.10, subdivision (b), states that a party may file a cross-complaint setting forth “[a]ny cause of action he [or she] has against a person alleged to be liable thereon, whether or not such a person is already a party to the action, if the cause of action in his [or her] cross-complaint, arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause brought against him [or her].” “Cross-complaints for comparative equitable indemnity would appear virtually always transactionally related to the main action.” (Time for Living, Inc. v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All American Development Co. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 30, 38.) If the proposed cross-complaint is permissive, leave of court may be granted “in the interest of justice” at any time during the course of the action. (Code Civ. Proc. § 428.50, subd. (c).)

Ruling

Defendant Berro seeks indemnification and declaratory relief claims in the proposed cross-complaint against Defendant Halj based upon (1) a 2016 management contract between Defendant Halj and Defendant Berro; (2) Mr. Hove’s testimony on April 15, 2024; and (3) Defendant Halj’s insurance company’s letter confirming Defendant Berro as an additional insured. (Holloway Decl. ¶¶ 3-5, Exh. 1-3.). As these causes of action arise out of the same occurrence as the underlying action, the Court grants leave to file the cross-complaint within 10 days of this order.

Case Number: 23LBCV02441    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S25

Background

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint for contract damages and enforcement of security interest against Defendants IK Holdings, Inc., Instakush Holdings, LLC., Case Farms, LLC., Waleed Mona and Does 1 to 25. On January 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) against the same Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that it entered into a note purchase agreement with Defendant IK Holdings, Inc. (“Defendant IK”). (Compl., ¶ 10; FAC ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges Defendant Waleed Mona (“Defendant Mona”) personally guaranteed repayment of a $1,000,000 promissory note issued by a company that Defendant Mona solely owns and operates. (Compl., ¶¶ 11, 12; FAC ¶¶ 14-16.) On February 28, 2024, Defendant Waleed Mona (“Defendant Mona”) filed an answer.

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed for entry of default judgment as to Defendant IK. On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed for entry of default judgment separately as to each of Defendants IK, Instakush Holdings, LLC. and Case Farms, LLC. Following the default prove-up hearing, this Court entered judgment on against Defendants IK, Instakush Holdings, LLC., Case Farms, LLC. (April 23, 2024 Minute Order; May 13, 2024 Judgment.)

On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel Defendant Mona to appear and testify at deposition and for sanctions. On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of Defendant’s non-opposition. No reply has been filed.

Legal Standard

“The service of a deposition notice…is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action…to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).)

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for

inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).) “The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)

Monetary sanctions are mandatory against an unsuccessful moving or opposing party unless they acted with substantial justification or imposition of sanctions would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) The court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust." (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).)

Meet and Confer

On April 22, 2024, Plaintiff served a deposition notice on Defendant Mona by way of his then-counsel of record, Nicholas Kritikos. (Fauver Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 1.) After meeting and conferring with Defendant Mona’s counsel as to the deposition’s time and location, an amended deposition notice was served, calling Defendant Mona to appear remotely and testify at 10:00 am on May 15, 2024. (Fauver Decl. ¶ 5, Exhs. 1-2.) Defendant Mona did not appear for the May 15, 2024 deposition and a record of non-appearance was taken. (Fauver Decl., ¶ 5, Exhs. 1-2.) A second amended notice was served by Plaintiff on May 15, 2024 calling for a remote deposition of Defendant Mona at May 21, 2024 and Defendant Mona’s counsel confirmed in writing availability of both counsel and client; however, Defendant Mona appeared late on May 21, 2024 and excused himself with a “work emergency” and did not reappear. (Fauver Decl., ¶ 6-8.) Defendant Mona’s then-counsel of record, Mr. Kritikos, filed a substitution of attorney on May 24, 2024 and served Plaintiff’s counsel via email on May 28, 2024. (Fauver Decl., ¶ 10.) Plaintiff’s counsel asserts despite multiple voicemails and emails to Defendant Mona requesting to reschedule the deposition, Defendant Mona has failed to respond, necessitating Plaintiff’s counsel filing the instant motion. (Fauver Decl., ¶¶ 11-12.) Plaintiff has fulfilled the meet and confer requirements.

Ruling

The Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated that Defendant Mona has refused to comply with the deposition notice and that Plaintiff has made good faith attempts to secure Defendant Mona’s compliance. Specifically, Defendant Mona failed to appear on May 15, 2024 despite earlier agreement by all parties. (Fauver Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. 2.) When the deposition was rescheduled for May 21, 2024, Defendant Mona arrived late to the deposition and left only 90 minutes into the proceeding despite Defendant Mona’s counsel’s written confirmation of Defendant Mona’s attendance. (Fauver Dec., ¶¶ 6-8.) On May 21, May 22, May 24, May 28, and May 30, Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to secure a date and time to resume Defendant Mona’s deposition but was unable to do so. (Fauver Decl., ¶¶ 9-11, Exhs. 4-5.) Despite Plaintiff’s counsel attempts to secure a date and time to resume Defendant Mona’s deposition, it appears Defendant Mona refuses to cooperate. (Fauver Decl., ¶¶ 9-11, Exhs. 4-5.) Accordingly, Defendant Mona is ordered to appear for deposition on July 9, 2024 as requested by Plaintiff.

Monetary sanctions are mandatory against an unsuccessful moving or opposing party unless they acted with substantial justification or imposition of sanctions would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) Here, Defendant Mona provides no justification for his actions as he has even failed to provide an opposition to this motion. Thus, the Court finds the imposition of monetary sanctions justified. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).) The Court imposes $5,000 in sanctions (for attorney’s fees and court reporter costs incurred as a result of Defendant’s failure to attend and to remain for deposition) against Defendant Mona in favor of New Alpha Limited to be paid within 45 days of this order.
