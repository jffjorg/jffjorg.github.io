DEPARTMENT SEC LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT SE-C LAW & MOTION PROCEDURES ARE AS FOLLOWS: APPEARANCES: The Court will hear oral arguments on all matters at the scheduled time of hearing. If all counsel intend to submit on the Tentative Order and do not want oral argument, please advise the clerk, in Department “C”, by calling (562-345-3702). If all sides submit on the Tentative Order and the clerk is so advised, the Tentative Order will become the final order of the court and the prevailing party shall give written Notice of Ruling per CRC 3.1312. If the Moving and Responding parties do not agree to submit on the Tentative Order, the motion will be called as calendared for hearing. There is no need to contact Department “C”, as the matter will remain on calendar for hearing. If the Moving party does not call Department “C” to submit on the Tentative Order and there is no appearance by any party, then the motion(s), at the Court’s discretion, may be taken off calendar without ruling on the motion(s). ORDERS: The minute order reflecting the Court’s Order will constitute the final Order. No additional orders should be submitted to the Court for signature unless required by law or by the Court. Prevailing party shall give written Notice of Ruling per CRC 3.1312. Minute orders, which constitute the final Order of the Court, will only be sent to the parties via U.S. mail  for the following: OSC re: sanctions, OSC re: contempt or matters taken under submission after oral arguments or briefing. Counsel or parties may request copies of all other minute orders/final orders either at the clerk’s office or in writing. If a request is in writing, a self-addressed stamped envelope and the appropriate fee for copies shall be submitted.

AGUILA v. SHABANI, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 21NWCV00144

HEARING: 12/18/2024

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

I.             Defendants’ motion for attorney’s fees is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $36,612.

 

II.            The Clerk of this Court will release $36,612 in attorney’s fees and $3,805 in costs to Defendants from the proceeds of the July 27, 2023, bond.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Joseph Shabani, Kamyar D. Shabani, Mercury Bowl, LLC, Green Rivera LLC, Optimus Properties, LLC, and Pico Rivera Holdings LVT, LLC (collectively, Defendants) move the Court for an order determining Defendants to be the prevailing parties in this action and for an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $37,212.00.

 

Background

 

On March 10, 2021, Plaintiff Henry Aguila (Plaintiff) filed this lawsuit against Defendants. The second amended complaint alleged: (1) interference with prospective advantage, (2) negligence, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (4) breach of contract.

 

On March 1, 2023, the Court granted Defendants’ motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant. The Court ordered Plaintiff to furnish a security in the sum of $50,000.

 

On April 2, 2024, after granting summary judgment in favor of Defendants, the Court entered judgment for Defendants.

 

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of appeal. On August 9, 2024, a notice of default was filed placing the appeal in default for failure to timely pay deposit for a Reporter’s Transcript.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1717 subd. (a) provides, “[i]n any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.”

 

In determining what fees are reasonable, California courts apply the “lodestar” approach. (See, e.g., Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.) This inquiry “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) From there, the “[t]he lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Ibid.) Relevant factors include: “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

 

The trial court has broad authority to determine the amount of a reasonable fee. (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) The party bears the burden of proof as to “reasonableness” of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5).) The party seeking fees has the burden of documenting the appropriate hours expended and hourly rates. (City of Colton v. Singletary (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 751, 784.) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.)

 

An attorney's testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records or billing statements, and there is no requirement that such records or statements be offered in evidence. (Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, 293; see also Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)

 

If the items appear to be proper charges, a verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the prevailing party, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that the items are unreasonable. (Decoto Sch. Dist. of Alameda County v. M & S Tile Co. (1964) 225 Cal. App. 2d 310, 316-17.)

 

Discussion

 

Defendants move the Court, pursuant to Civil Code section 1717, Code of Civil Procedure section 1032 subd. (a)(4)(b), Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 subd. (a)(10), and California Rules of Court rule 3.1702 for an order determining Defendants to be the prevailing parties in this action and for an award of attorney fees in the amount of $37,212.00 and costs in the amount of $3,805. Defendants request the Court release this amount from the $50,000 cash bond posted by Plaintiff. As of this date, this motion is unopposed.

 

          Prevailing Party

 

Defendants seek attorney’s fees pursuant to an attorney’s fee provision in the reciprocal easement agreement (REA). In relevant part, the REA states: “should any of the provisions hereof be violation, it will be difficult or impossible to determine the amount of damages resulting therefrom….Any costs and expenses of any such proceeding, including reasonable attorneys’ fees, shall be paid by the losing Owner to the prevailing Owner upon demand therefor.” (RJN, Exh. A., REA, ¶ 9.1.)

 

Pursuant to the REA and judgment in favor of Defendants, Defendants are deemed the prevailing party.

 

          Reasonableness of Attorney’s Fees

 

Defendants request $37,212 in attorney’s fees. Defendants concurrently submit the declaration of counsel for Defendants, Barak Isaacs (Isaacs), and a memorandum of costs.

 

Isaacs has been a practicing attorney for nearly twenty-five years and has a normal billing rate of $400 per hour. (Isaacs Decl., ¶ 3.) In this action, Isaacs reduced his billing rate to $300 per hour. (Isaacs Decl., ¶ 3.) Isaacs spent 114.04 hours of work on this case, including an additional seven hours preparing this motion. (Isaacs Decl., ¶ 5.) Isaacs includes billing statements reflecting the hours of work expended on this case. (Isaacs Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A.) Isaacs anticipates two hours of work for replying to an opposition and one hour for a hearing. (Isaacs Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

Here, Defendants have provided attorney testimony as well as detailed billing statements. The Court finds that Isaac’s reduced billing rate of $300 and 114.04 hours of work are reasonable. This action was filed on March 10, 2021, and there have been multiple complex motions filed including demurrers and a motion for summary judgment. However, because no opposition was filed, the Court deducts Isaac’s anticipated two hours of work on a reply.

 

Additionally, Defendants include a memorandum of costs. Defendants seek a total of $3,805 in costs. Here, the items requested in the memorandum of costs appear to be proper charges and, therefore, the memorandum of costs is prima facia evidence of the costs incurred by Defendants. (See Decoto Sch. Dist. of Alameda County v. M & S Tile Co. (1964) 225 Cal. App. 2d 310, 316-17.)

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for attorney’s fees in the reduced amount of $36,612 reflecting 114.04 hours of work in this action, and 8 hours of work related to bringing this motion. Additionally, the Court awards $3,805 in costs.

 

          Vexatious Litigant

 

Defendants contend a separate basis for attorney’s fees exists in the vexatious litigant statute and request the Court release the attorney’s fees and costs from Plaintiff’s bond.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 391 defines a “security” as “an undertaking to assure payment, to the party for whose benefit the undertaking is required to be furnished, of the party’s reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and not limited to taxable costs, incurred in or in connection with a litigation instituted, caused to be instituted, or maintained or caused to be maintained by a vexatious litigant.”

 

On July 27, 2023, Plaintiff posted bond in the amount of $50,000 with the Clerk of this Court pursuant to the Court’s Order on July 25, 2023. This bond assured payment of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs incurred in this action.

 

Accordingly, the Clerk of this Court will release $36,612 in attorney’s fees and $3,805 in costs to Defendants from the proceeds of the July 27, 2023, bond.

TENTATIVE ORDER

I. Defendant Doe 1’s demurrer to the first and second, and third causes of action is OVERRULED.

IV. Defendant’s motion to strike portions of the second amended complaint seeking punitive damages is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

Doe 1 (Defendant) demurs to the first through third causes of action in Peter Gustavo Pacheco Cateriano’s (Plaintiff) second amended complaint (SAC). Defendant additionally seeks to strike portions of the SAC for irrelevant and improper allegations.

Background

Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant on December 27, 2022. Plaintiff’s SAC alleges three causes of action: (1) sexual battery, (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (3) negligent infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiff, a self-represented litigant, alleges that in 1988, when Plaintiff was 8 years old, his half-brother, Defendant, “dropped my pants and throw me to the floor and try to penetrate me.” (SAC, ¶ 3.) It happened many more times for approximately 5 years, and later in life when Plaintiff stayed at Defendant’s place in the City of Downey. (SAC., ¶¶ 4-5.)

On January 16, 2024, this Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint with leave to amend on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to submit a certificate of merit and certificate of corroborative fact. On February 5, 2024, Plaintiff submit a certificate of corroborative facts and certificate of merit from a licensed mental health practitioner. On February 6, 2024, Plaintiff submit a declaration and certificate of merit by his counsel.

Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41 subd. (a).)

Here, Defendant filed a declaration of demurring party regarding meet and confer in which he states that Plaintiff did not respond to meet and confer requests.

Legal Standard

Demurrer Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, at p. 747.) A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

“Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)

Motion to Strike Standard

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer (i.e., words, phrases, prayer for damages, etc.).

(Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435-437.) A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleadings or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.)

Discussion

Defendant demurs to all three causes of action in Plaintiff’s SAC on the grounds that the claims are time-barred and that Plaintiff fails to plead facts sufficient to support the causes of action.

First Cause of Action – Sexual Battery

Statute of Limitations

“In an action for recovery of damages suffered as a result of childhood sexual assault, the time for commencement of the action shall be within 22 years of the date the plaintiff attains the age of majority or within five years of the date the plaintiff discovers or reasonably should have discovered that psychological injury or illness occurring after the age of majority was caused by the sexual assault, whichever period expires later, for…[a]n action against any person for committing an act of childhood sexual assault.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1 subd. (a).) Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 subd. (a) contains revival provisions. “[A] claim for damages described in paragraphs (1) through (3), inclusive, of subdivision (a) that has not been litigated to finality and that would otherwise be barred as of January 1, 2020, because the applicable statute of limitations, claim presentation deadline, or any other time limit had expired, is revived, and these claims may be commenced within three years of January 1, 2020.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1 subd. (q); see also Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1 subd. (r) “[t]he changes made to the time period under subdivision (a) as amended by the act that amended this subdivision in 2019 apply… to any action filed before the date of enactment, and still pending on that date, including any action or causes of action that would have been barred by the laws in effect before the date of enactment.”)

Here, Plaintiff alleges he was first sexually battered when he was eight years old in approximately 1988. (SAC, ¶ 3.) This occurred on multiple occasions for approximately five years, until Defendant reached legal age. (SAC, ¶ 4.) This occurred again in Los Angeles County in 1995 once Plaintiff was fifteen years old. (SAC, ¶ 5.) Plaintiff would have been forty years old in 2020. Therefore, having brought the claim in 2022, the claim is time-barred unless he filed the claim within five years of the date of discovery or it is otherwise revived.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the delayed discovery doctrine applies because he was unaware of the connection between his psychological injuries and the abuse until recent events of psychological trauma with Defendant. Specifically, Plaintiff argues that he discovered the psychological injuries by Defendant on February 3, 2024. This is the date Plaintiff executed a certificate of merit with licensed mental health practitioner, Dr. Algaed Mayorga. February 3, 2024, is also after Plaintiff filed the original complaint. The Court is not convinced that Plaintiff first discovered psychological injuries resulting from these allegations after filing the complaint.

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s claim may be revived under Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 subd. (q) because Plaintiff filed the complaint on December 27, 2022, which is within the three-year statutory revival deadline. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1 subd. (q).) While Defendant argues that K.M. v. Grossmont Union High School Dist. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 717 states that Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 revival provisions do not apply retroactively, Defendant misstates the law. The court in K.M. v. Grossmont Union High School Dist., specifically states that Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1. subd. (b) regarding treble damages does not apply retroactively, but Code of Civil Procedure sections 340.1. (q) and (r) apply retroactively. (K.M. v. Grossmont Union High School Dist., supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at 730, 743.)

Sexual Battery

Defendant next argues that Plaintiff fails to plead facts sufficient to support its first cause of action.

Civil Code section 1708.5, subd. (a), states that “[a] person commits a sexual battery who does any of the following: [¶] (1) Acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with an intimate part of another, and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. [¶] (2) Acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with another by use of his or her intimate part, and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. [¶] (3) Acts to cause an imminent apprehension of the conduct described in paragraph (1) or (2), and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results.” Subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 1708.5 makes “[a] person who commits a sexual battery upon another [] liable to that person for damages, including, but not limited to, general damages, special damages, and punitive damages.”

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Defendant’s demurrer and Plaintiff’s opposition both refer to a sexual assault cause of action. The Court will proceed to analyze this cause of action as sexual battery, as alleged in the SAC.

The Court finds the SAC contains sufficient facts to sustain a cause of action for sexual battery. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant “dropped my pants and throw me to the floor and try to penetrate me and not let me cry.” (SAC, ¶ 3.) This demonstrates acts with the intent to cause a harmful contact with the intimate part of another. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant took Plaintiff’s hand in his hand and performed a sexual act on himself without Plaintiff’s consent. (SAC, ¶ 5.1.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendant forced Plaintiff into oral sex without consent. (SAC, ¶ 5.2.) These facts demonstrate resulting sexually offensive contact. Therefore, the SAC pleads facts sufficient to sustain a cause of action for sexual battery. Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first cause of action.

Second Cause of Action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Statute of Limitations

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1, the statute of limitations to bring a personal injury cause of action is two years. However, in an action alleging childhood sexual statute, the statute of limitations in Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 subd. (a) applies to “[a]n action for damages suffered as a result of childhood sexual assault. (See also Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1 subd. (a)(3), subdivision (a) applies in “[a]n action for liability against any person or entity if an intentional act by that person or entity was a legal cause of the childhood sexual assault that resulted in the injury to the plaintiff.”)

Plaintiff’s SAC argues that the acts of sexually molesting Plaintiff supports a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress. Therefore, because Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress claim falls under the scope of Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 subd. (a), it is revived under section 340.1 subd. (q) revival provisions.

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.) “There can be no recovery for mere profanity, obscenity, or abuse, without circumstances of aggravation, or for insults, indignities or threats which are considered to amount to nothing more than mere annoyances.”

(Yurick v. Superior Court (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 1116, 1129.)

Here, Plaintiff meets the first element by alleging Defendant sexually molested Plaintiff as a minor. (SAC, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges he suffered severe emotional distress including psychological injuries and illnesses which he identified were caused by the childhood sexual abuse later in life. (SAC, ¶ 8.) Plaintiff additionally alleges Defendant abused a relationship of trust and committed the acts knowing Plaintiff was susceptible to injuries because of his age. (SAC, ¶ 14.)

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer as to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

Statute of Limitations

Plaintiff’s third cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress is also revived. Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 subd. (a)(2) applies in “[a]n action for liability against any person or entity who owed a duty of care to the plaintiff, if a wrongful or negligent act by that person or entity was a legal cause of the childhood sexual assault that resulted in the injury to the plaintiff.”

Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

“The law of negligent infliction of emotional distress in California is typically analyzed by reference to two theories of recovery: the ‘bystander’ theory and the ‘direct victim’ theory. The negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence. The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply. Whether a defendant owes a duty of care is a question of law.” (Spates v. Dameron Hosp. Ass’n (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 213, ellipses, quotation marks, brackets, and paragraph breaks omitted.) “Direct victim theory involves a duty owed directly to the plaintiff ‘that is assumed by the defendant or imposed on the defendant as a matter of law, or that arises out of a relationship between the two.’” (Ibid.)

Here, Plaintiff alleges Defendant had a duty of care to not cause sexual, emotional, or psychological harm because Plaintiff was a minor. (SAC, ¶ 17.)

However, this allegation does not state how Defendant owed a duty directly to Plaintiff and is not sufficient to constitute a “duty of care” in negligence.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

Motion to Strike

Defendant seeks to strike portions of the SAC on the grounds they are irrelevant and improper or improperly seek punitive damages.

Given the Court’s ruling on the demurrer, Defendant’s motion to strike paragraphs 3, 4, 5.1-5.3 is DENIED.

Given the Court’s ruling on the demurrer, and because Civil Code section 1708.5, subd. (b) supports punitive damages, Defendant’s motion to strike paragraph 11 and page 8 lines 26-27 is DENIED.

Given the Court’s ruling on the second cause of action in the demurrer, Defendant’s motion to strike paragraph 15 and page 9 lines 5 through 6 is DENIED. Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts to support a finding of malice in its allegations that Defendant made fun of past sexual abuse.

GIRON v. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV00237

HEARING: 12/18/24

#14

Plaintiffs Melissa Giron and Rudy Giron’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED.

Moving Party to give notice.

This is a continuation. On December 6, 2024, the Court granted a continuance to give the Court time to read and consider an untimely opposition, filed on December 5, 2024.

This “lemon law” action was filed by Plaintiffs Melissa Giron and Rudy Giron (“Plaintiffs”) on January 24, 2023. On April 7, 2023, the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) was filed. Plaintiffs allege that American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant” or “Honda”) did not disclose and actively concealed defects involving Honda’s Transmission Defect affecting Plaintiffs’ 2017 Honda Pilot vehicle. (See e.g., FAC ¶¶20, 43.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendant Honda manufactured the Subject Vehicle. (FAC ¶13.)

Plaintiff’s FAC asserts the following causes of action: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act (Breach of Express Warranty); and (2) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment.

On October 31, 2023, Defendant Honda filed its Answer to Plaintiffs’ FAC, Defendant’s Answer asserts a general denial and 28 affirmative defenses to the FAC. Honda alleges “that it possesses a qualified third-party dispute resolution process, thereby barring Plaintiffs from any civil penalty in this lawsuit.” (Answer to FAC ¶8.)

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c subd. (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿ (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿ “Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿

Evidentiary Objections

Defendant objects to evidence in Plaintiffs’ separate statement of undisputed facts.

Under California Rules of Court, rule 1.1354(b), “[a]ll written objections to evidence must be served and filed separately from the other papers in support of or in opposition to the motion.” Objections may be referenced in the separate statement but must not be restated or reargued in the separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 1.1354(b); see also Hodjat v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (2012) 211 Cal.4th 1, 8-9, a court did not abuse its discretion in refusing to consider objections not filed separately.)

Accordingly, the Court declines to rule on Defendant’s evidentiary objections.

Discussion

Plaintiff now moves for summary adjudication of the following issues:

(1) Plaintiffs are entitled to summary adjudication as to Defendant’s Fifth Affirmative Defense (Third Party Resolution Process), because Defendant does not have a qualified third-party dispute resolution process;

(2) Plaintiffs are entitled to summary adjudication as to Defendant’s Eighth Affirmative Defense (No Civil Penalty), because Defendant does not have a qualified third-party resolution process; and

(3) Plaintiffs are entitled to summary adjudication as to Defendant’s Eighth Affirmative Defense because willful and civil penalties are not barred by the existence of a qualified third-party dispute resolution process. Plaintiff argues that summary adjudication of these issues is proper because “[t]here is no evidence that Honda maintains a qualified third-party dispute resolution process[.]” (Motion 2:24.)

“If the buyer establishes that the failure to comply was willful, the judgment may include, in addition to the amounts recovered under subdivision (a), a civil penalty which shall not exceed two times the amount of actual damages.” (Cal. Civ. Code §1794(c).) Under Cal. Civ. Code §1794(e), “if the buyer establishes a violation of paragraph (2) or subdivision (d) of Section 1793.2, the buyer shall recover damages and reasonable attorney’s fees and costs, and may recover a civil penalty of up to two times the amount of damages. [¶] If the manufacturer maintains a qualified third-party dispute resolution process which substantially complies with Section 1793.22, the manufacturer shall not be liable for any civil penalty pursuant to this subdivision.” (Cal. Civ. Code §1794(e)(2).)

In support of their Motion, Plaintiffs proffer the following evidence:

(1) Honda conceded in discovery that it does not have a certified third-party dispute resolution process. (UMF No. 3, Swanson Decl., ¶¶6-7.);

(2) The Department of Consumer Affairs (“DCA”) maintains a publicly available list of automakers for which it has certified their “qualified third-party dispute resolution process,” and Honda is not among them. (UMF No. 4.)

(3) The Program Chief for the DCA Arbitration Certification Program has attested in a signed affidavit that Honda has not had a certified dispute resolution program since 2013, that Honda’s National Center for Dispute Settlement Program is not certified by the DCA, that a qualified program must be certified by the DCA, and that Honda’s non-certified program has not been subject to any regulatory review or oversight by the DCA. (UMF Nos. 5-10.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that there is no existence of disputed issues of material fact with regard to Plaintiffs’ ability to obtain civil penalties because Defendant fails to establish that it maintains a qualified third-party resolution process. Moreover, as argued in the Moving Papers, even if Defendant did have a third-party dispute resolution process, that would not be a shield to civil penalties under Cal. Civ. Code §1794(c) (which depends on a finding of willfulness). The burden now shifts to Defendant.

Defendant argues there are issues of material fact regarding its substantial compliance, good faith efforts to provide a third-party dispute resolution process, and whether its conduct meets the statutory standard for “willfulness.”

The Court notes that Defendant has not proffered any evidence in opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication.

Instead, Defendant relies on case law explaining the legislative intent behind “substantial compliance” under Cal. Civ. Code §1794(e). One such case is Suman v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1309, 1318 which notes the Act is intended to protect consumers and deter deliberate violations. At no point does the court in Suman suggest that a lack of certification constitutes “substantial compliance” within the meaning of Cal. Civ. Code §1794(e). Defendant contends that Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1104 stands for the proposition that a manufacturer’s efforts to offer consumer remedies, even if not certified, constitutes substantial compliance and good faith under the Act. (Opposition 5:16.) The court in Oregel states “a violation is not willful if the defendant's failure to replace or refund was the result of a good faith and reasonable belief that the facts imposing the statutory obligation were not present.” (Ibid.) At no point does the Court in Oregel discuss lack of certification. Additionally, to the extent Defendant argues its NCDS program demonstrates its “good faith” and “substantial compliance,” Defendant does not offer any evidence in support of its burden of production.

Accordingly, Plaintiffs Melissa Giron and Rudy Giron’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to Honda’s Fifth and Eighth Affirmative Defenses is GRANTED.

GATEWOOD, ET AL. V. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV00716

HEARING: 12/18/2024

#11

TENTATIVE ORDER

I. Plaintiffs Sheena Gatewood and Isaac Luke’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s PMQ is GRANTED.

II. Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

Sheena Gatewood and Isaac Luke (Plaintiffs) move the Court for an order compelling American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (Defendant) to produce a person most qualified and custodian of records (PMQ).

Background

This lemon law action was filed on March 9, 2023, and involves a new 2022 Honda Accord (Subject Vehicle). Plaintiff alleges two causes of action against Defendant: (1) violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of express warranty and (2) fraudulent inducement – concealment. Plaintiffs alleges Defendant did not disclose and actively concealed a defect in the Subject Vehicle known as the “Honda Sensing Defect.” (Complaint, ¶¶ 11, 25.)

Legal Standard

Under Code of Civil Procedure section¿2025.450 subd. (a), a party may move to compel a deponent’s attendance and production for inspection of documents if after serving the deposition notice the responding party fails to serve valid objections and fails to appear for the deposition or produce any document described in the deposition notice. A motion under Code of Civil Procedure section¿2025.450 subd. (a) must (1) provide specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document and (2) be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450 subd. (b).)

“If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450 subd. (g)(1).)

Meet and Confer

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040, “[a] meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” “[W]hen the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd. (b)(2).)

Here, the parties have not adequately met and conferred. Counsel for Plaintiffs sent one meet and confer letter addressing deficiencies in Defendant’s objections. (Tran Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. D.) Plaintiffs have not stated whether there was any response or subsequent meet and confer efforts. Further, Plaintiffs took a nonappearance on the June 18, 2024, deposition, after Defendant stated it will not provide a witness to appear. (Tran Decl., ¶ 10.) The parties’ emails scheduling a deposition date or sending a meet and confer letter do not constitute sufficient meet and confer efforts.

Discussion

Plaintiffs move the Court for an order compelling Defendant to produce a person most qualified and custodian of records (PMQ).

On April 30, 2024, Plaintiffs unilaterally noticed the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ for June 18, 2024. (Tran Decl., ¶ 4; Exh. A.) On June 10, 2024, Defendant served a response with objections to the notice of deposition and stated it will not produce a witness for the June 18, 2024, deposition date. (Tran Decl., ¶ 7; Exh. C.) Counsel for Plaintiffs, Thach Tran (Tran), states Defendant did not propose alternative dates. (Tran Decl., ¶ 7.) However, counsel for Defendant, Jeffrey M. Tsair (Tsair), includes an exhibit offering February 5, 2025, as an alternative date for the deposition. (Tsair Decl., ¶ 10. Exh. A.) On June 18, 2024, Tran appeared for the deposition through a video link and took a nonappearance. (Tran Decl., ¶ 10.) To date, no deposition of Defendant’s PMQ has taken place. Defendant has not provided alternative deposition dates nor responded to requests for alternative deposition dates for a deposition. (Tran Decl., ¶ 11.)

Upon review of Defendant’s Opposition, the Court notes that Defendant does not oppose producing its PMQ. Rather, Defendant argues that the failure to timely respond to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect; and that Defendant’s objections are necessary.

Objections to Matters for Examination

Plaintiffs’ separate statement identifies issues for matter for examination nos. 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 12, 19, and 22.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.230 provides, in relevant part, that “[i]f the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

Defendant objects to these requests on the basis they are vague, ambiguous, broad, unduly burdensome, harassing, argumentative, seek information protected by attorney-client privilege, and seek confidential information.

For example, Plaintiffs put forth the following matters for examination:

MATTER NO. 1:

Design and manufacturing processes for the HONDA “SENSING” SYSTEM.

MATTER NO. 4:

All pre-release and post-release testing of the HONDA “SENSING” SYSTEM.

MATTER NO. 5:

All reports, surveys, and summaries, which identify safety concerns with the HONDA “SENSING” SYSTEM.

MATTER NO. 22:

The rate and frequency of warranty repairs to correct symptoms of the HONDA “SENSING” SYSTEM, including summaries, reports, and the repairs per thousand, for all of YOUR HONDA “SENSING” SYSTEM -equipped vehicles

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.230, Plaintiffs’ matters for examination are intended to guide Defendant in selecting a PMQ for deposition who is knowledgeable on the matters. The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ matters for examination are reasonably particularized. Defendant does not provide objections as to any error or irregularity in the deposition notice.

Requests for Production

Defendant objects to these requests for production on the basis they are vague, ambiguous, broad, unduly burdensome, harassing, argumentative, seek information protected by attorney-client privilege, and seek confidential information.

A request for documents in conjunction with a deposition notice is not the equivalent of a request for production of documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010 et seq. A request for production of documents in a deposition notice is intended to specify with reasonable particularity the materials or category of materials to be produced by the Defendant. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.230 subd. (a)(4).) “Objections to the competency of the deponent, or to the relevancy, materiality, or admissibility at trial of the testimony or of the materials produced are unnecessary and are not waived by failure to make them before or during the deposition.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.460 subd. (c).)

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel Defendant’s PMQ is GRANTED. Defendant’s PMQ is ORDERED to appear for a deposition and produce the documents contained within the notice of deposition, without objection. Defendant has waived its objections to the document requests because it failed to object to them pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.410. The parties shall meet and confer to determine the date, time, and location of the depositions, which shall take place no later than 10 days from the date of the Court’s issuance of this Order. The date may be extended by agreement of the parties.

Sanctions

The Court declines to award sanctions. Here, while Defendant’s PMQ’s failed to appear for its duly noticed deposition, Plaintiff brought this motion and did not reply to Defendant’s proposed alternative date. Additionally, the parties failed to adequately meet and confer. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $2,468 is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

Hector Mora (Plaintiff) moves pursuant to the mandatory relief provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subd. (b) to vacate dismissal and place this matter back on the calendar.

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a request to dismiss the complaint. On February 12, 2024, the Court dismissed the matter in its entirety.

Background

This personal injury action was filed on August 21, 2023. Plaintiff alleges a cause of action for premises liability against Los Angeles Unified School District, Los Angeles County Office of Education, County of Los Angeles, and City of Maywood (Defendants). Specifically, Plaintiff alleges Defendants failed to properly maintain a field on their premises. (Complaint, Attachment.) As a result of the failure to maintain the field or properly barricade it, Plaintiff fell and sustained injuries. (Complaint, Attachment).

Legal Standard

Discretionary and mandatory relief is available to parties or legal representatives from “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473 subd. (b).) “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any…resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”

“The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney's affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473 subd. (b).)

Discussion

Plaintiff now brings this motion to vacate dismissal and place the matter back on the calendar due to Plaintiff’s counsel’s inadvertence and mistake. As of this date, this motion is unopposed.

Here, counsel for Plaintiff, Igor Fradkin (Counsel), filed the instant motion on March 11, 2024, which is within six months from February 2, 2024, the date the Court dismissed this action. “‘[W]hen relief under section 473¿is¿available, there is a strong public policy in favor of granting relief and allowing the requesting party his or her day in court.” (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 981-82, citations omitted.) Thus, there is strong public policy in favor of granting Plaintiff’s motion to set aside dismissal.

The Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated excusable mistake and inadvertence. Counsel states that on February 2, 2024, he unintentionally requested to dismiss the case. (Fradkin Decl., ¶ 4.) The request for dismissal was drafted by Defendant County of Los Angeles. (Fradkin Decl., ¶ 5.) Counsel unintentionally did not see that the request for dismissal sought to dismiss the complaint, rather than Defendant County of Los Angeles. (Fradkin Decl., ¶ 5.) Counsel made this mistake after a similar request for dismissal was filed dismissing only Defendant Los Angeles County Office of Education. (Fradkin Decl., ¶ 5.) Counsel learned about the error upon receiving the Court’s February 12, 2024, minute order. (Fradkin Decl., ¶ 6.) Therefore, Counsel’s mistake and inadvertence was excusable within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subd. (b). Accordingly, based on the excusable mistake and public policy in favor of granting relief, Plaintiff’s motion to set aside dismissal is GRANTED.

SOLIS V. DAVIS, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00580

HEARING: 12/18/2024

#6

TENTATIVE ORDER

Pursuant to Roldan v. Callahan & Blaine (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 87, Defendant can elect to pay Plaintiff’s share of the arbitration fees or waive the right to arbitrate Plaintiff’s claim.

Moving party to give notice.

Vincent W. Davis (Defendant) moves the Court for an order compelling Sara Solis (Plaintiff) to arbitrate all claims and stay this action pending arbitration.

Background

This is a legal malpractice action. On February 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Vincent W. Davis and Joyce Hope Vega alleging four causes of action: (1) professional negligence, (2) breach of contract, (3) breach of fiduciary duty, (4) conversion. Defendants substituted in as Plaintiff’s counsel in a separate proceeding in 2019. (Complaint, ¶ 25.) Plaintiff alleges that during that proceeding, Defendants sought multiple continuances of trial extending the five-year statute deadline and failed to make timely filings ultimately leading to the Court dismissing Plaintiff’s complaint with prejudice. (Complaint, ¶¶ 38-55.)

Legal Standard

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) This is usually done by presenting a copy of the signed, written agreement to the court. “A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330.) The moving party must also establish the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)

Under the FAA, general contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability, may provide grounds for invalidating an arbitration agreement if they are enforced evenhandedly and do not interfere with fundamental attributes of arbitration. (9 U.S.C.A. § 1 et seq.) To invalidate an arbitration provision, a plaintiff must show that it is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable. (Marin Storage & Trucking, Inc. v. Benco Contracting & Eng'g, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal. App. 4th 1042, 1053; Chemours Co. v. DowDuPont Inc. (2020) No. CV 2019-0351- SG, 2020 WL 1527783, at *12.) “A court should consider substantive unconscionability only after procedural unconscionability has been established.” (Ramirez v. Charter Communications, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 478, 494.) Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form. (Crippen v. Central Valley RV Outlet. Inc. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1159, 1280-81.)

Discussion

Defendant moves to enforce the arbitration provision in the attorney client fee agreement (Agreement) entered into by the parties on September 30, 2019. Defendant provides a copy of the Agreement. (Davis Decl., Exh. A.) The arbitration provision states:

11. ARBITRATION OF FEE OR OTHER DISPUTES:

11.1 Any dispute between Client and Attorney as to the reasonable value of services under this Agreement shall be arbitrated under the provisions of the State Bar Fee Arbitration Program by the Los Angeles County Fee Arbitration Program.

11.2 Any dispute (other than one concerning legal fees only), including, but not limited set forth in section 6200-6206 of the California Business and Professions Code, disputes as to the quality of Attorneys’ services, breach of fiduciary duties, fraud or any other aspect of the attorney-client relationship, will be determined by submission to binding arbitration as provided by California law, and not by a lawsuit or resort to court process, except as California law provides for judicial review or arbitration proceedings. Client may also have the right, under California law to mandatory nonbinding arbitration before a binding arbitration. If Client is entitled to and asks for, nonbinding arbitration, and Client and/or Attorney is/are unhappy with the result, it will be followed by binding arbitration. All parties to this Agreement, by entering into it, are giving up their rights to have any dispute decided in a court of law before a judge or jury and instead, are accepting the use of arbitration

11.3 By signing this Agreement, Client is consenting to binding arbitration of any and all disputes

The Court finds Defendant has proven the existence of a valid, signed arbitration agreement with Plaintiff. In opposition, Plaintiff does not dispute the existence of an arbitration agreement. Rather, Plaintiff argues that due to her limited financial means, this agreement is unconscionable and unenforceable unless Defendants pay all arbitration fees.

Unconscionability

Plaintiff argues that the Agreement is unconscionable because it denies her of a forum to litigate her claims. In support of this argument, Plaintiff relies on Roldan v. Callahan & Blaine (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 87 (Roldan) and Aronow v. Superior Court (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 865 (Aronow). In Roldan, the California Court of Appeals reviewed a trial court decision to deny a motion seeking an order to compel a defendant to advance the upfront cost of an arbitration forum. (Roldan, supra, 219 Cal.App.4th at 89.) The Roldan court declined to reach the issue of whether arbitration agreements are unenforceable based on a plaintiff’s ability to pay but concluded that plaintiffs who were granted permission to proceed in forma pauperis in trial court can be excluded from the obligation to pay arbitration fees. (Id. at 95.) Therefore, the court gave the defendant a choice: “if the trial court determines that any of these plaintiffs is unable to share in the cost of the arbitration, [defendant] can elect to either pay that plaintiff's share of the arbitration cost and remain in arbitration or waive its right to arbitrate that plaintiff's claim.” (Id. at 96.) In reaching this conclusion, the court reasoned “to rule otherwise might effectively deprive [plaintiffs] of access to any forum for resolution of their claims against [defendant].” (Ibid.)

The Court of Appeals in Aronow affirmed Roldan in deciding that a trial court which grants a defendant’s petition to compel arbitration has jurisdiction to lift a stay when a plaintiff demonstrates a financial inability to pay arbitration costs. (Aronow, supra, 76 Cal.App.5th at 870.) The court in Aronow concluded:

In response to a petition to compel arbitration, a litigant who claims financial inability to pay the arbitrator's fee could submit the Judicial Council application form and the financial statement prescribed by Government Code section 68633, subdivisions (c)(1) and (c)(2). “The court may delegate to the clerk the authority to grant applications for an initial fee waiver that meet the standards of eligibility and application requirements set forth in Sections 68632 and 68633.” (Gov. Code, § 68634, subd. (d).) Where a party seeking relief from paying the arbitrator's fee is one of the persons enumerated in Government Code section

68632’s list of persons “eligible to proceed without paying court fees and costs,” the decision would be ministerial.

(Id. at 884.)

Here, Plaintiff is not opposed to arbitration and only takes issue with the associated fees. Plaintiff has already submitted the Judicial Council application, and this Court granted a fee waiver based on Plaintiff’s economic status. (Request to Waive Court Fees, 02/23/2024.) Prior to filing this case, Plaintiff’s counsel requested Defendants pay the arbitration fees, but did not receive a response. (Moore Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. 1.) Defendant did not raise this issue in his moving papers nor file a reply.

Given Plaintiff’s financial status and that she would be deprived of a forum in which to litigate her claims, the Court follows the direction of the Roldan court and provides a choice to Defendant: Defendant can elect to pay Plaintiff’s share of the arbitration fees or waive the right to arbitrate Plaintiff’s claim.

This lemon law action was filed on April 18, 2024, involving Plaintiff’s new 2022 BMW 430i (Subject Vehicle). Plaintiff alleges six causes of action: (1) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability, (2) failure to promptly repurchase or replace – Civil Code §§ 1793.2 subd. (d); (3) failure to commence repairs within a reasonable time and to complete them within 30 days – Civil Code § 1794, (4) violation of Civil Code § 1793.2 subd. (a)(3), (5) breach of express warranty, and (6) negligent repair. Plaintiff alleges the Subject Vehicle contains electrical and software defects, defects activating warning lights, braking defects, and parking sensor defects.

Legal Standard

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300 subd. (a), “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7…against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (d).)

Meet and Confer

The parties have complied with the meet and confer requirement. On July 24, 2024, the parties’ counsels discussed via phone call about the case and Defendant’s responses and objections. (Pardo Decl., ¶ 16.)

Discussion

Plaintiff seeks further responses to Plaintiff’s SROGS, set one, nos. 1-16.

Plaintiff served Defendant with SROGs, set one, on May 13, 2024, and granted an extension for responses by July 15, 2024. (Pardo Decl., ¶¶ 4, 7.) On July 15, 2024, Defendant served unverified responses. (Pardo Decl., ¶ 10.)

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s July 15, 2024, responses were nonresponsive, evasive, incomplete, and contained general and boilerplate objections. In opposition, Defendant contends it supplemented its document production and SROGs, set one, responses code-compliant on August 7, 2024. (Silver Decl., ¶ 6.) In reply, Plaintiff argues that the SROG responses have not been supplemented or amended.

Requests Nos. 1 through 6

Defendant argues its supplemental responses to SROGS set one, nos. 1-6 are code-compliant because it exercised the option to produce documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230

“If the answer to an interrogatory would necessitate the preparation or the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of or from the documents of the party to whom the interrogatory is directed, and if the burden or expense of preparing or making it would be substantially the same for the party propounding the interrogatory as for the responding party, it is a sufficient answer to that interrogatory to refer to this section and to specify the writings from which the answer may be derived or ascertained. This specification shall be in sufficient detail to permit the propounding party to locate and to identify, as readily as the responding party can, the documents from which the answer may be ascertained.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.230.)

For example, Defendant’s supplemental response to SROGs, set one, no. 6 is as follows:

INTERROGATORY NO. 6:

Please IDENTIFY all DOCUMENTS concerning, reflecting, or memorializing any of YOUR internal communications concerning the SUBJECT VEHICLE

DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSE:

BMW NA’s responses to the Interrogatories are in full compliance because it exercised its option to produce writings in response. The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered; (2) An exercise of the party’s option to produce writings; (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory. require the responding party to make reference to a pleading or document, the pleading or document should be identified and summarized so the answer is fully responsive to the question.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 784.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant properly referred Plaintiff to responsive documents. INTERROGATORY NO. 11:

Please IDENTIFY each of YOUR agents or employees involved with implementing YOUR rules, policies, and procedures for complying with the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act as of the year 2021.

Here, Plaintiff seeks information regarding Defendant’s Song-Beverly Act policies because it is relevant to Plaintiff’s request for civil penalties. SROGs, set one, nos. 12 through 16 are as follows:

Please IDENTIFY each of YOUR authorized repair facilities, agents, or employees who participated in, was involved in, or was present for, any inspection, service, diagnosis, analysis, evaluation, or repair of the SUBJECT VEHICLE

INTERROGATORY NO. 13:

For each authorized repair facility, agent, or employee identified in response to Special Interrogatory No. 13, supra, please describe how the PERSON identified participated in, was involved in, or was present for, any inspection, service, diagnosis, analysis, evaluation, or repair of the SUBJECT VEHICLE.

RAMOS V. UNION PACIFIC RAILROAD, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV01381

TENTATIVE ORDER

Counsel Raymond Perez’s motion to be relieved as counsel is DENIED without prejudice.

Moving party to give notice.

Background

Francisco Ramos (Plaintiff) filed this premises liability action on May 2, 2024. Counsel for Plaintiff, Raymond Perez (Counsel), moves to be relieved as counsel. As of this date, this motion is unopposed.

Legal Standard

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant Code of Civil

Procedure section 284, subd. (2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (Form MC-051); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subd. (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subd. (1) (Form MC-052); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (Form MC-053). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (a), (c), (d), (e).)

Good cause exists to grant the motion based on any of the grounds under Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3-700(C).

Rule 3-700(c) provides that an attorney may withdraw based on any of the following: (1) The client (a) insists upon presenting a claim or defense that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law, or (b) seeks to pursue an illegal course of conduct, or (c) insists that the member pursue a course of conduct that is illegal or that is prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (d) by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively, or (e) insists, in a matter not pending before a tribunal, that the member engage

in conduct that is contrary to the judgment and advice of the member but not prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (f) breaches an agreement or obligation to the member as to expenses or fees. (2) The continued employment is likely to result in a violation of these rules or of the State Bar Act; or (3) the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal; or (4) The member's mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively; or (5) The client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the employment; or (6) The member believes in good faith, in a proceeding pending before a tribunal, that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal.

Discussion

Here, Counsel has filed a notice of motion and motion on Form MC-051, a declaration on Form MC-052, and a proposed order on Form MC-053. Counsel has also supplied proof of service. The Court notes that the “Case Name” on Form MC-053 does not depict the name of the instant case.

Further, the Court cannot determine whether good cause exists because Counsel has not provided any reasoning or justification. Counsel states that the reason he brings this motion is because Plaintiff has not responded to Counsel’s request to find new counsel. This is not enough information to support a finding of good cause.

Accordingly, Counsel Raymond Perez’s motion to be relieved as counsel is DENIED without prejudice.
