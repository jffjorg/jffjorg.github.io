DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV02075    Hearing Date: November 22, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S STARBUCKS MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION PLAINTIFF CHARLENE PARKER

I.         INTRODUCTION

            Plaintiff Charlene Parker (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendants Starbucks Corporation, a Washington Corporation (“Starbucks”); DWHJR Holdings LLC, a California Limited Liability Company; and Does 1 to 50 (collectively, “Defendants”), asserting claims for (1) General Negligence, (2) Products Liability, and (3) Premises Liability. Plaintiff alleges that on September 12, 2021, while attempting to sit on a bench on Starbucks’ premises, the bench collapsed without warning, causing her to fall and sustain severe injuries.

On October 24, 2024, Starbucks filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. The motion is unopposed.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

            Code Civ. Proc. §2025.450 provides relief where a party fails to appear for deposition without having served a valid objection. It provides as follows:

(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) [omitted]

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

. . .

(g)(1) If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(2) On motion of any other party who, in person or by attorney, attended at the time and place specified in the deposition notice in the expectation that the deponent's testimony would be taken, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of that party and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust..

III.      ANALYSIS

            Defendant Starbucks moves to compel Plaintiff Charlene Parker’s deposition, asserting that Plaintiff has failed to appear for deposition despite being properly served with three deposition notices. Starbucks contends that these nonappearances have hindered the discovery process and delayed its ability to prepare for trial.

Starbucks served its first Notice of Deposition and Request for Production of Documents on April 4, 2024, scheduling the deposition for April 30, 2024. On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel informed Defendant’s counsel that Plaintiff would not attend due to a medical condition. (Melo Decl., ¶ 3a; Exh. C.)

Starbucks served a second deposition notice on May 16, 2024, setting the deposition for July 2, 2024. On June 26, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel again notified Starbucks’ counsel that Plaintiff could not attend, citing her ongoing medical condition. (Melo Decl., ¶ 3b; Exh. E.)

A third deposition notice was served on August 12, 2024, scheduling the deposition for September 25, 2024. The day before the deposition, Plaintiff’s counsel informed Starbucks that Plaintiff would not appear. Starbucks subsequently obtained a Certificate of Non-Appearance from the court reporter. (Melo Decl., ¶¶ 3c-d; Exhs. F, G.)

Despite these three attempts, Plaintiff has failed to appear or provide an alternative date for deposition. Starbucks’s counsel inquired on October 21, 2024, regarding Plaintiff’s availability but received no response. (Melo Decl., ¶ 3e; Exh. H.)

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a), a party may move to compel a deposition if the deponent fails to appear after being properly noticed. The moving party must demonstrate good cause for the deposition and submit a declaration detailing attempts to meet and confer. Here, Starbucks has satisfied these requirements by filing this motion, outlining the necessity of Plaintiff’s deposition to evaluate her claims and damages, and demonstrating repeated efforts to secure her attendance.

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s failure to provide alternative dates, lack of communication regarding the September 25, 2024, notice, and the absence of opposition to this motion lack substantial justification. Defendant has made repeated good faith attempts to depose Plaintiff, who has yet to comply with her discovery obligations.

Accordingly, Starbucks motion to compel deposition of Plaintiff is GRANTED

Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a), authorizes the Court to impose a monetary sanction for misuse of the discovery process, requiring the offending party, or their attorney, to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by the opposing party, including attorney’s fees. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the Court must impose that sanction unless it finds that the offending party acted with substantial justification or that the imposition of the sanction would be unjust.

When a motion to compel under Section 2025.450(a) is granted, the Court is required to impose monetary sanctions in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the Court finds that the subject of the sanction acted with substantial justification or that the circumstances render the imposition of the sanction unjust. (C.C.P. § 2025.450(c)(1)). The purpose of discovery sanctions is to prevent abuse of the discovery process and to correct the problem presented. (Do v. Superior Court (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 1210, 1213.)

Defendant Starbucks Corporation seeks $1,060.00 in monetary sanctions, which includes 2.5 hours for preparing the motion, 1.0 hour for preparing a reply to any opposition, 0.5 hour for appearing remotely for the hearing, and $60 for the filing fee. Defendant’s counsel charges $250 per hour, a rate the Court finds reasonable. (Melo Decl., ¶ 4.).However, given the lack of complexity of the motion and the absence of any opposition, the Court finds that some of the requested time is excessive. The Court awards sanctions for 1.5 hours spent preparing the motion, 0.5 hour for attending the hearing, and the $60 filing fee, for a total of $560.00.

    The Court imposes monetary sanctions in the amount of $560.00 on Plaintiff.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Starbucks' motion to compel Plaintiff's deposition is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear for deposition within 30 days of this order. Failure to abide by this order may result in Plaintiff being precluded from testifying at trial. 


Starbucks’ request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $560.00 as to Plaintiff.


Starbucks is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24NNCV00582    Hearing Date: November 22, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT AUGUSTO COYNE’S (COEN-BROWN)  DEMURRER TO THE COMPLAINT WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

    I.         INTRODUCTION

            Plaintiff Yadira Alejandra Torres (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action on March 8, 2024, against Mofongo’s Inc, (“Mofongos”) Augusto Coen-Brown erroneously sued as Augusto Coyne Marcos Lopez (“Augusto”), Alexis Fuentes and Does 1 through 100. Plaintiff asserts eleven causes of action: (1) Failure to Provide Compliant Meal Periods, (2) Failure to Provide Compliant Rest Periods, (3) Failure to Pay for All Hours Worked, (4) Failure to Pay All Overtime Owed, (5) Wage Statement Penalties, (6) Waiting Time Penalties, (7) Violation of Unfair Competition Law, (8) Sexual Harassment in Violation of the FEHA, (9) Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy, (10) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (11) Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA).

On September 18, 2024, Defendant Augusto filed a general demurrer to the entire Complaint, arguing that Plaintiff failed to allege sufficient facts to state any cause of action against him. On November 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition, conceding the insufficiency of the allegations but requesting leave to amend the Complaint to address the deficiencies. On November 13, 2024, Defendant filed a reply, asserting that Plaintiff failed to demonstrate how the Complaint could be amended to cure its defects and arguing that leave to amend should be denied.

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, on September 10, 2024, Augusto’s counsel, Richard A. Rogers, telephoned Plaintiff’s counsel, Dan Ginzburg, to meet and confer before filing the demurrer. (Rodgers Decl., ¶ 6) The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”¿ (C.C.P. §473(a)(1).)¿ 

“Trial courts are vested with the discretion to allow amendments to pleadings ‘in furtherance of justice.’ That trial courts are to liberally permit such amendments, at any stage of the proceeding, has been established policy in this state since 1901.”¿ (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489.)¿ ¿

      III.      ANALYSIS

Defendant Augusto demurs to the entire Complaint, alleging that all eleven causes of action are directed at Mofongo’s Inc., not him. He further argues that Plaintiff fails to allege any specific facts connecting him to the claims or establishing personal liability. Augusto emphasizes that the Complaint lacks allegations identifying him as a party to the employment relationship or as a wrongdoer in his personal capacity.

In opposition Plaintiff acknowledges the Complaint’s deficiencies as to Augusto but requests leave to amend, asserting the ability to allege facts and claims to hold him liable. Plaintiff concedes Augusto was not named in the list of parties or explicitly tied to the asserted causes of action but maintains that factual allegations in the Complaint would support viable claims upon amendment.

In reply, Augusto argues Plaintiff fails to meet the burden of showing how the Complaint can be amended to cure the defects. Augusto argues that Plaintiff’s Opposition does not include a proposed amended pleading or articulate new allegations to support the claims.

The Court agrees that the Complaint does not allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action against Augusto. However, the Court must evaluate whether Plaintiff has demonstrated a reasonable possibility of curing the defects through amendment.

California courts are required to exercise their discretion liberally in permitting amendments to pleadings in furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(a)(1); Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488) Granting leave to amend is particularly appropriate where there is no showing of prejudice to the opposing party and the plaintiff asserts an ability to correct the defects.

Here, Plaintiff’s Opposition asserts an intent to amend and acknowledges that the Complaint fails to explicitly name Augusto as a party to the action under the section designating the names of the parties and their county of residence. (Opposition, p. 2.) Plaintiff further contends that, although the Complaint currently lacks sufficient allegations against Augusto, the existing factual allegations could support viable claims if properly amended. Plaintiff requests leave to amend, stating that specific facts and causes of action will be included in an amended complaint to address the deficiencies.

Based on the foregoing and policy favoring liberal amendment, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer with leave to amend.

IV.       ORDER

            The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to the Complaint with 20 days leave to amend.
