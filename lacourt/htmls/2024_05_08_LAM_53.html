Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 19STCV42155    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

isabel castellanos , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

devore scott trust , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

19STCV42155

 

 

Hearing Date:

May 8, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

(1)   defendant and cross-complainant’s motion permitting cross-complaint to be filed nunc pro tunc as of may 3, 2022

(2)   defendant and cross-complainant’s motion for leave to re-file his summons and cross-complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant and cross-complainant Karlen Galstyan  

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    (1) Defendant Canon Business Properties, Inc., and (2) Scott R. Devore, Trustee of the Scott R. Devore Revocable Living Trust dated February 13, 2016, erroneously sued and served as co-trustee of the Don and Bonita Devore Family Trust dated May 28, 2003, Bonita Devore, erroneously sued and served as co-trustee of the Don and Bonita Devore Family Trust dated May 28, 2003, Paul Devore, and Ed Devore

(1)   Motion Permitting Cross-Complaint to be Filed Nunc Pro Tunc as of May 3, 2022

(2)   Motion for Leave to Re-File Summons and Cross-Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with each motion.

 

DISCUSSION

The following two motions filed by defendant and cross-complainant Karlen Galstyan (“Galstyan”) are now pending before the court: (1) motion for order permitting his Cross-Complaint to be filed nunc pro tunc as of May 3, 2022, and (2) motion for leave to re-file his summons and cross-complaint.

As a threshold matter, the court notes that, on April 8, 2024, Galstyan filed an ex parte application seeking an order permitting his Cross-Complaint to be filed nunc pro tunc as of May 3, 2022.  On April 9, 2024, the court issued an order (1) denying Galstyan’s ex parte application, but (2) exercising “its discretion to deem [Galstyan’s] ex parte application to be a motion seeking the same relief” and setting the hearing on that motion for May 8, 2024.  (April 9, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)  Thus, the motion for order permitting Galstyan’s Cross-Complaint to be filed nunc pro tunc as of May 3, 2022 is properly before the court.

The court acknowledges, as defendants Canon Business Properties Inc. (“Canon”) and Scott R. Devore, Trustee of the Scott R. Devore Revocable Living Trust dated February 13, 2016, erroneously sued and served as co-trustee of the Don and Bonita Devore Family Trust dated May 28, 2003, Bonita Devore, erroneously sued and served as co-trustee of the Don and Bonita Devore Family Trust dated May 28, 2003, Paul Devore, and Ed Devore (“Devore Defendants”) (collectively, “Defendants”) have pointed out in their opposition papers, that Galstyan’s second motion for leave to re-file his summons and complaint appears to seek relief in addition to that which is requested in the first motion.  However, Defendants have not cited any authority establishing that Galstyan was required to obtain leave of court to file such a motion. 

Thus, the court finds that Galstyan properly filed and served his second motion to re-file the summons and cross-complaint 16 court days before the May 8, 2024 hearing date and therefore rules on the merits of the motion.  (Mot. for Leave, pp. 10-11 [proof of service of personal and electronic service of motion on April 16, 2024]; Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

1.     Motion for Order Permitting Galstyan’s Cross-Complaint to be Filed Nunc Pro Tunc as of May 3, 2022

Galstyan moves the court for an order permitting his Cross-Complaint against Devore Defendants, Canon, and the City of Los Angeles to be filed in the action filed by plaintiffs Isabel Castellanos, Lilian Ortega, Antonio Ortega, and Valeria Ortega (Case No. 19STCV42155) (the “Castellanos Action”) nunc pro tunc as of May 3, 2022, pursuant to (1) California Rules of Court, rule 8.77, and (2) Code of Civil Procedure section 473.  (Karapetian Decl., Ex. 3 [Rejected “Cross-Complaint of Karlen Galstyan to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint for Damages”].)

Although not raised by the parties, the court notes that Galstyan already filed a Cross-Complaint against Canon, Devore Defendants, City of Los Angeles, and plaintiffs Isabel Castellanos, Lilian Ortega, Antonio Ortega, and Valeria Ortega on June 5, 2020 in the Castellanos Action (Case No. 19STCV42155) before the court issued its order consolidating cases numbered 19STCV42155, 20STCV34002, 20STCV42358, and 20STCV34214.  (Galstyan June 5, 2020 Cross-Compl., p. 2:10-15; July 29, 2021 Minute Order, p. 1.)  Thus, the June 5, 2020 Cross-Complaint is Galstyan’s operative Cross-Complaint in the Castellanos Action.[1]  However, Galstyan did not advise the court of that Cross-Complaint in his motion.

The court finds that Galstyan’s motion is, in effect, requesting that the court allow him to file an amended Cross-Complaint in the Castellanos Action nunc pro tunc as of May 3, 2022.  However, a motion to amend a pleading must (1) set forth the information specified by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, including the identification of the allegations in the pleading that are proposed to be deleted or added, and (2) be accompanied by a supporting declaration that specifies the information set forth by that rule, including the effect of the amendment, why amendment is necessary, and when the facts giving rise to the amendment were discovered.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).)  Galstyan did not, as set forth above, advise the court and the parties that he has already filed a Cross-Complaint in the Castellanos Action, and has not explained the effect of the requested amendment as required.

Thus, the court (1) finds that Galstyan did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, and therefore (2) denies Galstyan’s motion.

2.     Motion for Leave to Re-File Galstyan’s Summons and Cross-Complaint

Galstyan moves the court for an order granting him leave to re-file his Cross-Complaint in the Castellanos Action pursuant to (1) California Rules of Court, rule 8.77, (2) Code of Civil Procedure sections 426.40, 428.50, and 473, and (3) the court’s inherent powers.  (Karapetian Decl., Ex. E [Rejected “Cross-Complaint of Karlen Galstyan to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint for Damages”].)

As set forth above, Galstyan already filed a Cross-Complaint in the Castellanos Action on June 5, 2020.  Thus, Galstyan’s request that the court grant him leave to file a Cross-Complaint is, in effect, a request that the court permit him leave to amend his Cross-Complaint.  A motion to amend a pleading must comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324.

For the same reasons as set forth above, the court (1) finds that Galstyan did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, and therefore (2) denies Galstyan’s motion.

ORDER

            The court denies defendant and cross-complainant Karlen Galstyan’s (1) motion for order permitting his Cross-Complaint to be filed nunc pro tunc as of May 3, 2022, and (2) motion for leave to re-file his summons and cross-complaint.

            The court orders defendant Canon Business Properties, Inc. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment, if Entered

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with this motion.  No reply papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Cristina Amado Loomis (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order setting aside her default, entered by the clerk on February 16, 2023, on the First Amended Complaint filed by plaintiff Chris Loomis (“Plaintiff”), pursuant to (1) Code of Civil Procedure section 473, (2) Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5, and (3) Civil Code section 1788.61.

Under section 473, subdivision (b), “[t]he court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)  The “six-month time limitation is jurisdictional; the court has no power to grant relief under section 473 once the time has lapsed.”  (Austin v. Los Angeles Unified School District (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 918, 928.) 

Similarly, “[w]hen service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action.  The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).) 

First, the court finds that Defendant’s motion made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is untimely because it was filed on March 20, 2024, i.e., more than six months after the clerk entered Defendant’s default on February 16, 2023.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Austin, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 928.)

Second, the court finds that Defendant’s motion made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 is untimely because (1) Plaintiff has submitted evidence establishing that, on February 24, 2023, he served Defendant with the completed “Request for Entry of Default” form (on which the default of Defendant was entered by the clerk on February 16, 2023), (2) Defendant filed this motion on March 20, 2024, and (3) Defendant therefore did not file and serve this motion no later than 180 days after the February 24, 2023 service of written notice that her default had been entered.  (Seañez Decl., ¶ 8 [“The entered default was served by mail”]; Seañez Decl., Ex. F, p. 1 [February 24, 2023 proof of service of document entitled “Request for Entry of Default”]; Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).)

Third, the court denies Defendant’s motion made pursuant to Civil Code section 1788.61 because (1) that statute applies to service of a summons that has not resulted in actual notice to a person in time to defend “an action brought by a debt buyer[,]” and (2) Defendant has not shown that this action filed by Plaintiff is “an action brought by a debt buyer[.]”  (Civ. Code, § 1788.61, subd. (a)(1).)

The court therefore denies Defendant’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 473, subd. (b), 473.5; Civ. Code, § 1788.61, subd. (a)(1).)

ORDER

            The court denies defendant Cristina Amado Loomis’s motion to set aside default and default judgment, if entered.  

            The court orders plaintiff Chris Loomis to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
