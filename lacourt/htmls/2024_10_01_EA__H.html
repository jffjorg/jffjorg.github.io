DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV02576    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: H

Nash, et al. v. Lafferty Equipment Manufacturing LLC, Case No. 23PSCV02576

ORDER ON MOTION TO INTERVENE

Proposed Plaintiff-in-Intervention’s Pacific Compensation Insurance Company’s Motion for Leave to Intervene as Party Plaintiff Pursuant to Labor Code § 3853 and CCP § 376 is GRANTED.

 

Background 

In this lawsuit involving asserting causes of action of (1) strict products liability, (2) negligent products liability, and (3) loss of consortium, Plaintiffs Joshua Michael Nash (“Nash”) and Angela Maire Tyson (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

Lafferty Equipment Manufacturing, LLC (“Lafferty”) manufactured, designed, and sold the Sanitize/Rinse/LCSS foam hose drop station, model 1035SS, part number 915125SS (the “sanitation station”). On December 13, 2022, while Nash was working at Pocino Food Company in La Puente, California, the sanitation system exploded immediately after Nash connected to it, causing Nash to sustain injuries to his eyes and face. 

Pending before the Court is a motion to intervene as party plaintiff filed by Pacific Compensation Insurance Company. 
  

Legal Standard

“A nonparty shall petition the court for leave to intervene by noticed motion or ex parte application. The petition shall include a copy of the proposed complaint in intervention or answer in intervention and set forth the grounds upon which intervention rests.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (c).)

“The court shall, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if either of the following conditions is satisfied:

(A) A provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene.

(B) The person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person’s ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(1).) 

“The court may, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(2).) 

“Any employer who pays, or becomes obligated to pay compensation. . . may likewise make a claim or bring an action against the third person. In the latter event the employer may recover in the same suit, in addition to the total amount of compensation, damages for which he or she was liable including all salary, wage, pension, or other emolument paid to the employee or to his or her dependents. . .” (Lab. Code, § 3852.) 

“If the action is brought by either the employer or employee, the other may, at any time before trial on the facts, join as party plaintiff or shall consolidate his action, if brought independently.” (Lab. Code, § 3853.) 

“If the employer is insured against workmen’s compensation liability, the insurer has the same right of action as the employer, or is subrogated to the employer’s right. In enforcing its cause of action the insurer may . . . join as party plaintiff in the suit previously filed by the employee . . .” (Burum v. State Compensation Ins. Fund (1947) 30 Cal.2d 575, 580-581 [citations omitted].)

Discussion

Proposed Intervenor Pacific Compensation Insurance Company (“Proposed Intervenor”) moves the Court for an order permitting its intervention as a party plaintiff.

This case involves a personal injury accident by Plaintiff who, at the time of the accident, was in the course and scope of his employment with Pocino. (Johnsen Decl., ¶ 2.) Proposed Intervenor, under provisions of the Workers’ Compensation Insurance and Safety Act of California, has become obligated to pay, and has paid, to Plaintiff certain monies for indemnity and medical expenses. (Id., ¶3.) Proposed Intervenor seeks to intervene in the instant case by filing the subject Complaint-in-Intervention for recovery of workers’ compensation benefits. (Id., ¶4, Exh. A.)

The Court grants the unopposed motion and permits Pacific Compensation Insurance Company to intervene as a party plaintiff.

Butler v. Gonzalez, Case No. 24PSCV00129

ORDER ON MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

Uriel Gdolian and Oakwood Legal Group’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED, effective upon the filing of a proof of service showing service of the final order upon Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last known address, return receipt requested.

 

Background

Plaintiff Sue Ann Butler (“Plaintiff”), who is represented by counsel (Uriel Gdolian of Oakwood Legal Group), commenced this lawsuit after allegedly sustaining injuries and damages in a January 20, 2022 motor vehicle accident. 

Pending before the Court is Uriel Gdolian and Oakwood Legal Group’s motion to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff.

Discussion 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398.) 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure § 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)). The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

Attorney Uriel Gdolian (“Gdolian”) attests that counsel is unable to reach Plaintiff via telephone and text messaging. Plaintiff did not provide counsel with an email address. On January 17, 2023, counsel hired a private investigator. The private investigator ran multiple searches and attempted to locate Plaintiff through various outlets such as contacting Plaintiff’s place of employment, contacting the postal office for any forwarding addresses, and trying to locate relatives of Plaintiff. On May 8, 2024, the private investigator informed counsel that they are still unable to locate Plaintiff and have been advised that Plaintiff is no longer using any post office box facilities. The private investigator is still trying to reach Plaintiff. 

Gdolian states that he has served Plaintiff by mail at Plaintiff’s last known address with copies of the motion papers served with his declaration but that he has been unable to confirm that the address is current or to locate a more current address for Plaintiff after mailing the motion papers to Plaintiff’s last known address, return receipt requested, calling Plaintiff’s last telephone number, conducting a background check with the address matching what Plaintiff provided upon intake and checking for additional addresses, and hiring a private investigator.

The motion is not opposed. The Court determines that the requirements of Rules of Court Rule 3.1362 have been met.

Accordingly, the motion is granted, effective upon the filing of a proof of service showing service of the Court’s order upon Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last known address, return receipt requested.

The Court sets an Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to Prosecute for January 6, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. in Department H of Pomona Courthouse South, 400 Civic Center Plaza, Pomona, CA 91766.

Case Number: 24PSCV00375    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: H

Xu, et al. v. Huang, Case No. 24PSCV00375

Defendant Judy May Huang, Trustee of the Huang Family Trust’s Motion to Set Aside Default and File Answer and Cross-Complaint is GRANTED. The Court is inclined to reduce the amount of fees and costs requested by Plaintiffs to $300.00, payable by Liddle & Liddle to Plaintiffs within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling. The Court will hear further from counsel in this regard at the time of the hearing.


Background

Plaintiffs Wei Xu aka George Xu (“G. Xu”) and Mandy Sun (“together, Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

On or about March 12, 2023, Plaintiffs, as tenants, and Defendant Judy May Huang, Trustee of the Huang Family Trust (“Huang”), as landlord, entered into a written lease agreement for one of the houses on the premises located at 11549 Embree Drive in El Monte (the “subject property”). G. Xu and Huang also agreed that G. Xu would make repairs to the subject property and to the other house on the premises in exchange for payment. Huang failed to pay G. Xu for the repairs and to reimburse him for materials expenses. Huang also failed to correct numerous habitability issues with the subject property. The city inspector came to the subject property on and after May or June 2023 and issued numerous violations.

Huang evicted Plaintiffs as a result of G. Xu speaking with the city inspector.

On February 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Huang and Does 1-10 for:

1.       Breach of Lease;

2.       Breach of Warranty of Habitability;

3.       Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment;

4.       Negligence and Negligence Per Se;

5.       Unjust Enrichment;

6.       Nuisance California Civil Code 3479; and

7.       Wrongful Eviction.

On July 8, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Huang.

A Case Management Conference is set for October 1, 2024. Pending before the Court is Huang’s motion to set aside default and file answer and cross-complaint.

 

Legal Standard

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

“Whenever the court grants relief from a default, default judgment, or dismissal based on any of the provisions of this section, the court may do any of the following:

(A) Impose a penalty of no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) upon an offending attorney or party.

(B) Direct that an offending attorney pay an amount no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) to the State Bar Client Security Fund.

(C) Grant other relief as is appropriate.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c)(1).)

“However, where the court grants relief from a default or default judgment pursuant to this section based upon the affidavit of the defaulting party’s attorney attesting to the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, the relief shall not be made conditional upon the attorney’s payment of compensatory legal fees or monetary penalties imposed by the court or upon compliance with other sanctions ordered by the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c)(2).)

 

Discussion

Huang moves the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b), for an order setting aside the July 8, 2024 entry of default and permitting her to file her proposed answer and cross-complaint.

Huang seeks mandatory relief pursuant to an attorney affidavit of fault. Huang’s counsel Raymond Zakari (“Zakari”) represents that his firm was hired to represent Huang to serve a 30-day notice to quit on Plaintiffs (Zakari Decl., ¶ 3); that Plaintiffs vacated the premises and subsequently filed their complaint on February 5, 2024 (Id.); that Huang ultimately retained his firm to represent her interests on or about June 6, 2024 (Id.); that his firm handles a couple hundred new case files each month, most of which are plaintiff cases for either unlawful detainer or breach of contract (Id., ¶ 4); that due to a staff error, Huang’s file was designated as a plaintiff breach of contract file, such that no responsive pleading deadline was calendared (Id.); that since no response date was calendared no timely response was filed and he unintentionally failed to reach out to Plaintiffs’ counsel for an extension of time to respond or to file any responsive pleading (Id.); and that Huang contacted his firm after receiving the request for entry of default, thus notifying them that they did not file a responsive pleading (Id., ¶ 5.)

Plaintiffs, in opposition, make much of the fact that Huang’s counsel never contacted their counsel to seek a stipulation to set aside the default. This is not required, however. Plaintiffs also assert that Huang has not set forth a sufficient showing of attorney mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. The Court disagrees, based on the above information provided by Zakari.

It does not matter that Plaintiffs’ counsel contacted Huang’s counsel’s firm on March 27, 2024 regarding accepting service of the summons and complaint on behalf of Huang but never received any response. Zakari previously explained that, while his firm was initially hired to represent Huang to serve a 30-day notice to quit on Plaintiffs, his firm was not hired as counsel in this instant lawsuit until on or about June 6, 2024. The motion is granted.

The Court recognizes that section 473, subdivision (b) provides that it shall, whenever relief is granted pursuant to an attorney affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay “reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs” to opposing counsel or Plaintiffs. The Court must award some fees and costs pursuant to this language.

Plaintiffs seek $2,000.00 in fees and costs [calculated as follows: 2 hours reviewing motion and preparing opposition plus 2 hours preparing for and attending hearing at $500.00/hour]. It is unclear to the Court why Plaintiffs felt the need to oppose the motion under the circumstances. Plaintiffs do not appear to have needed to incur any costs and fees other than those associated with the preparation of the request for entry of default. The Court is inclined to reduce the amount of fees and costs requested by Plaintiffs to $300.00, payable by Liddle & Liddle to Plaintiffs within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling. The Court will hear further from counsel in this regard at the time of the hearing. 

Case Number: 24PSCV00619    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: H

Jayewardene v. Mercury Insurance Company LLC, et al., Case No. 24PSCV00619

ORDER ON DEMURRERS AND MOTIONS TO STRIKE 

1.       Defendant Maral Chorbajian’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED. 

2.       Defendants Abraham Chorbajian, Eli Chorbajian, and Maral Chorbajian’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is GRANTED. 

3.       Defendants Mercury Insurance Company LLC and Mercury Insurance Services’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED. The Court will hear from counsel for Plaintiff as to whether leave to amend is requested and if so, will require an offer of proof. 

4.       Defendants Mercury Insurance Company LLC and Mercury Insurance Services’ Motion to Strike Punitive Damages from Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is DENIED as MOOT. 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Sean Jayewardene (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he sustained injuries and damages in a June 12, 2023 motor vehicle accident.

On April 22, 2-24, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC” or the “Operative Complaint”), asserting a motor vehicle cause of action against Mercury Insurance Company and Mercury Insurance Services (sued as “Mercury Insurance”) (together, “Mercury”), Abraham Chorbajian (“A. Chorbajian”), Eli Chorbajian (“E. Chorbajian”), and Maral Chorbajian (“M. Chorbajian”) (together, the “Chorbajians”). 

Pending before the Court are: (1) the Chorbajians’ demurrer and motion to strike and (2) Mercury’s demurrer and motion to strike. A Case Management Conference is set for October 1, 2024.

II.      Legal Standards

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) 

 “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.) 

III.     Discussion

A.       The Chorbajians’ Demurrer

The Chorbajians demur, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Mercury or M. Chorbajian.

At the outset, the Chorbajians may not demur on behalf of Mercury, nor may E. Chorbajian and A. Chorbajian demur on behalf of M. Chorbajian. The Court’s analysis is limited to M. Chorbajian only as the moving party.

M. Chorbajian argues that she is an improper party because she “is neither the driver nor the owner of the defendant’s vehicle.” (Motion, 2:13-15.) However, this contention is extrinsic to the face of the Operative Complaint. Plaintiff has identified M. Chorbajian as a “defendant[] who operated a motor vehicle.” (FAC, ¶ MV-2.) Accordingly, the Court overrules M. Chorbajian’s demurrer. 

          B.       The Chorbajian’s Motion to Strike

The Chorbajians move the Court for an order striking out Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages (i.e., FAC, ¶ 14(a)(2).).

Punitive damages may be awarded in an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract upon clear and convincing evidence that a defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)

“Malice” is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

A “conclusory characterization of defendant’s conduct as intentional, willful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of ‘oppression, fraud, or malice. . .’” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.)

The Operative Complaint is devoid of any factual allegations showing that the Chorbajians’ conduct was malicious, oppressive and/or fraudulent to support a claim for punitive damages. The motion is granted.

C.       Mercury’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike

Mercury demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against it.

Mercury’s demurrer is sustained. The Operative Complaint is devoid of any factual allegations as against Mercury.

In light of the ruling on the demurrer, the Court denies as moot Mercury’s motion to strike punitive damages from the Operative Complaint. 

Case Number: BC697068    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: H

Olsen v. Valmont Industries, Inc., et al., Case No. BC697068  

The hearing on Plaintiff Kyle Olsen’s Motion for Entry of Judgment Pursuant to CCP § 664.6 is CONTINUED to November 15, 2024, at 9:00 a.m.

Background

In this negligence and strict liability action, Plaintiff Kyle Olsen (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he sustained injuries after a 2017 accident where his vehicle collided with an X-Lite guardrail end terminal that bordered the I-10 freeway. In 2018, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Valmont Industries, Inc. (“VII”), Valmont Highway, Armorflex International Limited (“Armorflex”), Lindsay Corporation (“Lindsay Corp.”), Lindsay Transportation Solution Sales & Service LLC (“Lindsay TSSS”), Ferreira Construction Co., Inc. (“Ferreira”), State of California (“CalTrans”), and Does 1-50. 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a conditional “Notice of Settlement of Entire Case.” 

On July 2, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the case against VII, Valmont Highway, and Armorflex, without prejudice. 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion for an order enforcing the settlement agreement.

Legal Standard

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).)

Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order enforcing the settlement agreement between Plaintiff, CalTrans, Powell, and Ferreira.

On or about March 26, 2024, Plaintiff, CalTrans, Powell and Ferreira entered into a written settlement agreement and release (“Agreement”), wherein CalTrans agreed to pay Plaintiff $2,000,000.00. (Itkin Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.) The settlement was conditioned upon the Court’s finding and determination that the settlement was in good faith under Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6, which determination was made on May 30, 2024. (Id., ¶¶ 3 and 4, Exhs. 2 and 3). Plaintiff complains that CalTrans has not made payment and that CalTrans’ counsel has communicated that it could take 16 additional weeks for the settlement to fund. (Id., ¶¶ 8 and 9.)

The Court notes that the Agreement is silent on a time for payment. “[T]he law implies that the contract shall be performed within a reasonable time. Where a contract does not fix a time for payment, reasonable diligence and good faith must be required in such instances and it is the duty of the court to hear evidence and therefrom fix a time which would be fair.” (Estate of Jones (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 948, 954 [quotations and citations omitted].)

Counsel for CalTrans Benson Chan (“Chan”) and Trang Le (“Le”), in turn, represent that, once the Court determined that the settlement was made in good faith, they immediately engaged in the process of having the settlement payment issued. (Chan Decl., ¶ 5; Le Decl., ¶ 4.) This process involves the submission of a detailed settlement memorandum and check request package to CalTrans’ headquarters office in Sacramento and requires multiple levels or review and approval. (Le Decl., ¶ 4.) There have been several delays in processing the payment, including the settlement package being deemed incomplete due to the need for a signed Payee Data Record from Plaintiff instead of a W-9. (Id., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff provided a signed Payee Data Record on or about July 9, 2024, which was then submitted to Sacramento; however, Le was unable to respond to certain inquiries and pending issues regarding the settlement package until after July 26, 2024, because he was out of the office on a personal family emergency. (Id., ¶¶ 5 and 6.)

Chan points out that, unlike the settlement funds to be paid on behalf of Powell and Ferreira by insurance carriers, the monies due from CalTrans are to be paid through State funds, which require the following of specific procedures. (Chan Decl., ¶ 6.) Further, Le notes that the submission of the settlement check request package coincided with the end of the State’s fiscal year (June 30, 2024) and the beginning of the new fiscal year, which causes delays in processing of payments statewide. (Le Decl., ¶ 7.) Le has reached out to several departments, informed CalTrans’ Deputy Chief Counsel and Assistant Chief Counsel, and tasked his assistant to follow up weekly on the status, and is informed and believes that it will take approximately 4 to 6 weeks for the settlement check to be issued. (Id., ¶ 8.)

The Court is inclined, based upon the above information, to continue the hearing for approximately 45 days. It appears to the Court that CalTrans is making every effort to perform the Agreement within a “reasonable time,” despite being hampered by procedural constraints involved with the disbursement of State funds.

The Court requests that counsel for CalTrans file a status report regarding the settlement payment no later than five court days prior to the continued hearing date.

The hearing is continued to November 15, 2024, at 9:00 a.m. The Court also continues the Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal (Settlement) (currently set for November 8) to November 15, 2024, at 9:00 a.m.
