[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S RESPONSES AND PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS TO FIRST SET OF FORM INTERROGATORIES

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

September 18, 2024

 

Defendant Dilys Jones, trustee of the Louis Petrie Separate Property Trust, moves for an order compelling Plaintiff Café on Lemon (“Plaintiff”) to serve responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, which were propounded on March 12, 2024. Defendant also seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,995 against Plaintiff.

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290.) A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff did not oppose the Motion and it is undisputed responses were not served. Defendant’s Motion is therefore GRANTED and Plaintiff is ordered to serve verified responses without objections to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set One) within 20 days of the date of this Order.

The Code of Civil Procedure provides that the court shall impose a monetary sanction against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).) The Court finds evidence of neither.

Accordingly, Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED and imposed against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $285 for 1 hour at defense counsel’s hourly rate of $285, to be paid within 20 days of the date of this Order.

Moving party to give notice.

Dated this 18th day of September 2024

 

 

 

 

William A. Crowfoot

Judge of the Superior Court

The motion was originally scheduled to be heard on July 25, 2024, and prior to this hearing, the Court issued a tentative ruling denying the motion and the request for sanctions on the grounds that the proof of service for the Form Interrogatories was incomplete because it did not identify the date the Form Interrogatories were served, despite the proof of service being executed on December 5, 2023. The Court then continued the hearing to allow Plaintiff to file a response to the tentative ruling.

On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a response as well as a declaration from Louis Francis explaining that a copy of the motion was served by mail on April 5, 2024.

The Court’s previous tentative ruling denied Plaintiff’s motion because there was no evidence when the form interrogatories were served. Item 3.c. in the proof of service signed by Louis Francis on December 5, 2023, is blank. The affidavit signed by Plaintiff on April 5, 2024, states that “[o]n date I served my Special/Form Interrogatories.”

The Court still has no evidence of when the form interrogatories were actually served on Defendant. There was never an issue with the service of the motion. Therefore, the recent filings from Plaintiff and Mr. Francis attesting that the motion to compel was served on April 5, 2024, and attaching the proof of service for the motion to compel, are unavailing.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion and request for sanctions are DENIED without prejudice. If Plaintiff can show that the form interrogatories were served on Defendant and that Defendant failed to respond within the timeframe to do so, Plaintiff may bring a renewed motion to compel a response.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On September 14, 2023, plaintiff Jose Rodriguez filed this action against defendants Ford Motor Company (“Defendant”) and WC Performance Ford dba Performance Ford. On June 27, 2024, Defendant filed this motion requesting the Court enter a protective order governing the production of confidential materials during discovery. The protective order is largely based on the Los Angeles Superior Court’s Model Order (“LASC Model Order”) with several revisions to Paragraphs 7, 8, and 21 to further restrict the individuals who have access to any confidential information, impose further obligations on those who have access to those confidential materials, and require the return of all confidential documents at the conclusion of the case.

On July 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief.

On August 1, 2024, Defendant filed a reply brief.

On August 7, 2024, the Court heard oral argument and continued the hearing to September 18, 2024.

Defendant filed a supplemental brief on August 14, 2024, and Plaintiff filed a supplemental brief on August 21, 2024.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

“[T]he burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought. [Citation.]” (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) “The state has two substantial interests in regulating pretrial discovery. The first is to facilitate the search for truth and promote justice. The second is to protect the legitimate privacy interests of the litigants and third parties. The interest in truth and justice is promoted by allowing liberal discovery of information in the possession of the opposing party. The interest in privacy is promoted by restricting the procurement or dissemination of information from the opposing party upon a showing of ‘good cause.’ The trial court is in the best position to weigh fairly the competing needs and interests of parties affected by discovery.” (Stadish v. Superior Court (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 1130, 1145 [internal citations omitted].) A trial court must balance the various interests in deciding “whether dissemination of the documents should be restricted.” (Id. at p. 1146, 84 Cal.Rptr.2d 350.) Even where a motion for a protective order is denied in whole or in part, the trial court may still impose “terms and conditions that are just.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.420, subd. (g), 2031.060, subd. (g).)

III.        DISCUSSION

A.   Timing

As an initial matter, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s motion is untimely because the confidential documents were sought in February 2024 and Defendant did not file this motion until June 27, 2024. The Court disagrees. In a letter dated May 15, 2024, Plaintiff requested Defendant send a proposed protective order. The parties did not agree on the proposed revisions, thus necessitating the filing of this motion in June.  

B.   Material Subject to the Protective Order

Defendant specifically identifies three categories of documents which it seeks to protect through this proposed protective order: (1) Warranty Policy & Procedure Manuals (“Warranty Manuals”), (2) Customer Relationship Center (“CRC”) Policies and Procedures, and (3) Reacquired Vehicle (“RAV”) Policy and Procedures Manuals. (Declaration of Jacob Doss, ¶ 6.) Defendant submits the declaration of its employee, Jacob Doss, who explains that these documents are proprietary because they reveal Defendant’s policies and practices which are developed over time, including “processes and methodologies to identify, document, assess, and resolve customer or product concerns.” (See, e.g., Doss Decl., ¶ 9.) Doss states that disclosure of these documents would benefit Defendant’s competitors who are training their own employees and/or developing their processes for warranty claims, customer service, and repurchasing vehicles, without the need to invest the same time and resources expended by Defendant. (Doss Decl., ¶¶ 10-12.) Defendants have therefore demonstrated “good cause” for a protective order.

C.   Terms of the Protective Order

The parties do not dispute the propriety of a secrecy agreement but cannot agree upon its parameters. Therefore, the motion requests the Court enter a protective order based on the LASC Model Order with modifications made to three separate paragraphs. The Court addresses these proposed modifications in turn.

First, Defendant argues that Paragraph 7 of the Model Order should be modified so that any confidential information produced may be accessed by attorneys who are “[a]ttorneys of record in the Proceedings and their affiliated attorneys in the same firm.” Defendant also demands that all employees and non-lawyers within the firm sign a certification before accessing any confidential information acknowledging the terms and strictions of the protective order. The Model Order only requires the certification be signed by officers, directors, partners, members, employees and agents of the party receiving the documents, as well as outside experts and consultants. In addition, Defendant wishes to: (1) preclude mock jurors from accessing the confidential documents, (2) include videographers, litigation support companies, and non-attorney consultants as categories of individuals who are explicitly subject to the protective order, and (3) confirm that no confidential materials will be shown to experts or consultants who are currently employed by an automobile manufacturer competitor of Ford.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that these proposed modifications are unwarranted and burdensome. First, Plaintiff claims that requiring attorneys be part of the same firm before being provided access to confidential materials “could conceivably prevent Plaintiffs’ counsel from disclosing the documents to any other attorneys that serve as Plaintiffs’ co-counsel” or trial counsel if they are from a different firm. (Opp., pp. 4-5.) The Court does not see such an obstacle as the protective order also allows any attorney of record to the proceeding to access the confidential information. Therefore, if Plaintiff is indeed associating co-counsel or trial counsel, they would be included in that category of persons with access. Accordingly, this proposed modification is reasonable.

Plaintiff also argues that requiring all non-lawyer employees to separately execute a certification before being “given access” to those confidential documents would be unduly burdensome because there are over 100 employees within the firm who have access to case files. Plaintiff argues that this would be an onerous undertaking for every single litigation brought against Defendant. This argument lacks merit because the protective order at issue governs this particular action. Also, it is possible for Plaintiff’s attorneys to limit an individual employee’s access to a case file if that individual has not executed a certification and Plaintiff submits no evidence that implementing such restrictions would be unduly burdensome. Therefore, Defendant’s proposed modification which requires non-lawyers to sign a certification is reasonable.

As for Defendant’s proposed prohibition on the dissemination of confidential material to mock jury participants, the Court finds that adopting this proposal would cause undue burden on Plaintiff’s ability to prepare for trial. The Model Order allows disclosure upon securing the mock jury participants’ signature on the certification. Although Defendant argues that it has “no ability to confirm the identity of such persons or to track or trace their access if [Defendant]’s documents were to become public,” the identities of those individuals can be recorded on the certification. (Motion, p. 9.) Therefore, Defendant’s modification prohibiting disclosure of confidential information to mock jury participants is rejected.

Second, Defendant wishes to modify Paragraph 8 of the Model Order to explicitly prohibit posting the confidential information to any website accessible to anyone other than the persons listed in the protective order and or selling, offering to sell, advertising, or publicizing the confidential information or the fact that the person has obtained the confidential information.

Plaintiff argues that this modification is unnecessary because Paragraph 8 of the Model Order already requires that “Confidential Materials shall be used by the persons receiving them only for the purposes of preparing for, conducting, participating in the conduct of, and/or prosecuting and/or defending the Proceeding, and not for any business or other purpose whatsoever. The Court does not see how Defendant’s proposed modification would cause any undue burden to Plaintiff especially when, as Plaintiff points out, the effect is the same. Therefore, the proposed modification to paragraph 8 of the Model Order is reasonable.

Third, Defendant seeks to modify Paragraph 21 of the Model Order to require Plaintiff’s counsel to return or destroy all confidential documents at the conclusion of the case. Notably, Defendant removes language from the Model Order allowing counsel for each party to maintain all work product, one copy of each pleading filed with the Court, and one copy of each deposition together with the exhibits marked at the deposition. Defendant argues that it would not be able to track the location and use of confidential information over time if Plaintiff’s counsel were allowed to keep a copy of documents “in perpetuity.” (Reply, p. 5.) However, Defendant’s proposed requirement would prevent Plaintiff’s counsel from keeping a complete copy of its files which, under the Model Order, would still be kept “in continuing compliance” with the terms of the protective order. Additionally, pursuant to Defendant’s modifications to Paragraph 7, the only attorneys who may access the information are those affiliated in the same firm as the attorneys of record, and they are bound not to use the confidential information for any other purpose other than this action.

D.  The Parties’ Supplemental Briefs

In its supplemental brief filed on August 14, 2024, Defendant repeats its argument that paragraphs 7(d) and 7(g) should be modified. Defendant’s proposed modification to paragraph 7(d) requires videographers, litigation support consultants, and vendors retained by the parties to provide litigation support services to be subject to the terms of the protective order. The proposed modifications to paragraph 7(g)1 would subject non-attorney consultants to the terms of the protective order and preclude disclosure of confidential information to any expert or consultant who is currently employed by an automobile manufacturer competitor of Ford.

Plaintiff states in his supplemental brief that he does not oppose these specific changes. Accordingly, the Court grants this unopposed portion of Defendant’s motion which seeks to modify paragraphs 7(d) and 7(g) of the Model Protective Order.

IV.        CONCLUSION

In light of the foregoing, the Court grants Defendant’s motion in part. Defendant is to submit a proposed order incorporating the Court’s ruling as expressed above.

Plaintiff Jose Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”)
moves for an order compelling defendant Ford Motor Company (“Defendant”) to
serve further responses to Plaintiff’s First Set of Requests for Production
Nos. 16 to 24. Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of
$2,280 against Defendant and its attorneys of record. Plaintiff generally complains
that Defendant’s responses are insufficient because they: (1) unilaterally
condition production of responsive documents on a protective order, (2) fail to
provide a complete statement of compliance, and (3) include unwarranted
objections. Because the Court separately grants Defendant’s request for a
protective order, Plaintiff’s complaint that the responses inappropriately
condition production on a protective order is moot. The Court thus proceeds to
analyze the rest of Plaintiff’s motion to determine whether Defendant’s
responses are Code-compliant.



RFP No. 16: Plaintiff demands all of
Defendant’s warranty claims policy and procedure manual(s) from 2022 to the
present. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s warranty claims policy and manuals
in effect from 2022 to the present are relevant to determining whether
Defendant complied with its obligations under the Song-Beverly Act.



In response, Defendant agreed to
produce only its warranty policy and procedure manual applicable in 2023,
subject to the protective order. However, Plaintiff purchased his vehicle in
2022, therefore Defendant’s policies and manuals in effect from 2022 to the
present (as well as Defendant’s compliance with those policies and manuals or
lack thereof) are relevant to determining whether Defendant is subject to a
willfulness civil penalty. Therefore, the motion is GRANTED with respect to RFP
No. 16.



RFP No. 17 demands all document “which
evidence, describe, refer, or relate to [Defendant’s] rules, policies, or
procedures since 2022 concerning the issuance of repurchases or replacement
vehicles to buyers in the State of California under the Song-Beverly Consumer
Warranty Act.”



This request is overbroad in demanding
all documents which “evidence, describe, refer, or relate to” Defendant’s
policies for repurchasing or replacing vehicles under the SBA. However, a
further response is required because Defendant must produce its policies and
manuals applicable from 2022 to the present. Also, although Defendant “refers”
Plaintiff to various guides available on websites as responsive, Defendant must
also include a statement of compliance which agrees to produce those documents.
Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED IN PART with respect to RFP No. 17.



RFP 18 seeks documents “which evidence,
describe, refer, or relate to [Defendant’s] procedures for the handling of
complaints by consumers regarding [Defendant’s] vehicles.” The request is
overbroad insofar as it seeks all documents “which evidence, describe, refer,
or relate” to Defendant’s procedures. Nevertheless, the motion to compel a
further response is GRANTED N PART because Defendant’s statement of compliance
is too narrow. Defendant only agreed to produce its Warranty Policy and
Procedure Manual containing “its policies and procedures for Ford's Customer
Relationship Center ("CRC"), which handles communications from
customers to Ford regarding vehicle concerns, applicable to 2023.” As stated
above, the policies and procedures to which Plaintiff are entitled are limited
to those applicable in 2022 to the present.  



RFP Nos. 19 and 20 demand all training
materials for handing consumer requests for a vehicle repurchase in California
since 2022, and all training materials for employees and agents tasked with
determining whether a vehicle is eligible for a repurchase since 2022.



Defendant only agreed to produce its
Warranty Policy and Procedure Manual applicable to 2023, which is an
inappropriate limitation since Plaintiff purchased his vehicle in 2022. Also,
the “reference” to documents available on the Internet is not a Code-compliant
agreement to produce the responsive documents. There are also no valid
objections justifying the withholding of training materials (other than the
objection based on privilege, which requires the preparation of a privilege log
if any documents are actually withheld on that basis). Therefore, the motion is
GRANTED with respect to RFP Nos. 19 and 20.



RFP No. 21 demands all scripts and flow
charts utilized since 2022 in handling California consumers’ requests for a
vehicle repurchase or replacement since 2022. Defendant states that it “did not
locate scripts responsive” to this RFP, but this response is not compliant with
CCP 2031.230 because it does not state whether responsive scripts never existed
or were lost, misplaced, stolen, destroyed, nor does it set forth the name and
address of the person or organization known or believed to have possession, custody,
or control of those scripts. Defendant also agrees to produce policies and
procedures applicable to 2023, which is an inappropriate limitation because
Plaintiff purchased his vehicle in 2022. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED
with respect to RFP No. 21.



RFP Nos. 22 through 24 ask for all documents
describing Defendant’s policies, procedures, and parameters for defining what
constitutes: (1) a repair presentation, (2) a “substantial impairment” of a
vehicle’s use, value, or safety, and (3) a “reasonable number of repair
attempts.”



These requests are overbroad insofar as
they demand all documents “describing” Defendant’s policies, procedures, and
parameters. Yet, Defendant’s responses to RFP Nos. 22 through 24 are
insufficient because Defendant only agrees to produce its Warranty Policy and
Procedure Manual and its RAV Policy & Procedure Manual applicable to 2023,
when the relevant timeframe for responsive documents is from 2022 to the
present. Also, Defendant’s “reference” to documents available on the Internet
is not Code-compliant. Therefore, the motion is GRANTED IN PART as to RFP Nos.
22 and 24.



          In sum,
Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED and Defendant is ordered to serve further
responses to RFP Nos. 16 through 24 within 20 days of this Order in a manner
consistent with this order. The Court declines to impose sanctions on Defendant
because some of Plaintiff’s RFPs were overbroad and because Defendant’s
privilege objections were timely asserted. The Court reminds Defendant that if
it is withholding any documents on the basis of privilege, then Defendant must
provide a privilege log.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On December 22, 2023, plaintiff Yihsuan Wu (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Zhuming Zhang (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer on May 13, 2024, and subsequently filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings on August 7, 2024.

Plaintiff attempted to file an amended complaint on August 15, 2024, which was rejected because she did not obtain leave of court. Plaintiff did not oppose Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings on the grounds that the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii).) The grounds for motion provided for in this section shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (d).)

III.        DISCUSSION

Form complaints must contain material facts essential to state a cause of action and include the mandatory attachments. (See People ex rel. Dept. of Transp. v. Superior Court (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1480, 1484.)

Plaintiff’s form complaint fails to state a cause of action because the necessary attachment for the purported cause of action, intentional tort, is not included and Plaintiff otherwise pleads no facts in support of her complaint. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED with 30 days leave to amend.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED with 30 days leave to amend.
