DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 22STCV01510    Hearing Date: April 26, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Robert La Masney’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production

            Plaintiff Robert La Masney moves to compel defendant Christian Schrader to provide further responses to requests for production Nos. 47 and 48.  A requesting party may move to compel further responses to requests for production if “[a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete,” “[a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive,” or “[a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.”  (CCP § 2031.310(a).)

            Schrader made meritless objections to requests for production Nos. 47 and 48.  No. 47 demands, “Any and all Supplemental Audio LLC calendars from January 1, 2015 through December 31, 2017 in WEEKLY FORMAT.”  Schrader made numerous objections.  Overall, the objections rely on the premise that plaintiff already requested these documents and Schrader already produced them.  Plaintiff’s request No. 44 demanded, “Any and all Supplemental Audio LLC calendars from January 1, 2015 through December 31, 2017.”  In response, Schrader produced calendars in monthly format.  (Schulman Decl., Ex. B.) 

Plaintiff shows good cause to demand the calendars in weekly format.  The monthly calendars do not show the duration of each event.  The monthly calendars state, for example, on January 7, 2015, “10:00 AM People’s Choice Awards.”  (Schulman Decl., Ex. B, p. CCG006689.)  After plaintiff filed this motion, Schrader produced some calendars in weekly format.  The weekly calendar entry for the same event states, “People’s Choice Awards 10:00 – 20:00.”  (Schulman Reply Decl., Ex. A, p. SSG-001601.)  This request elicited additional evidence that may be admissible.

No. 48 demands, “Any and all Supplemental Audio LLC calendars from January 1, 2015 through December 31, 2017 in NATIVE FORMAT.”  Schrader made numerous objections that again rely primarily on the premise that he already produced the responsive documents and the request was cumulative and duplicative. 

Plaintiff shows good cause for this demand.  Code of Civil Procedure section provides, “A party demanding inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of electronically stored information may specify the form or forms in which each type of electronically stored information is to be produced.”  (CCP § 2031.030(a)(2).)  Plaintiff defined “NATIVE FORMAT” to “mean the default format of a file, access to which is typically provided through the software program on which it was created, with all metadata intact.”  Documents in native format often contain metadata (for example, information about how the files were created), which may be admissible.  (See Ellis v. Toshiba America Information Systems, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 853, 859, fns. 4 & 5 (Ellis).)  The request was reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and was not cumulative or duplicative. 

Schrader contends no native format exists.  He states, “I used Google calendar, which to my understanding does not have ‘Native Format’ on my computer.”  (Schrader Decl., ¶ 7.)  The record shows he emailed some of the calendars to plaintiff in pdf files.  (Supp. Schulman Decl., Ex. A, p. 1; Ex. B, p. 1.)  Plaintiff presents evidence there is a way to download native versions of the calendar files.  Plaintiff’s counsel states, “On April 10, 2024, I conducted a google search for how to export files from a Google Calendar” and found a webpage explaining how to do that.  (Supp. Schulman Decl., ¶ 7.)    

            Schrader’s objections to requests for production Nos. 47 and 48 are overruled.

Schrader must respond to request Nos. 47 and 48 with a statement of compliance asserting that “all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party … will be included in the production.”  (CCP § 2031.220.)  

Sanctions

Plaintiff moves for monetary sanctions against Schrader.  “[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes” a motion to compel further responses to requests for production “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2031.310(h).) 

Schrader acted with substantial justification.  Though his objections were ultimately meritless, it was reasonable to object that demanding calendars in monthly, weekly, and native format was cumulative or duplicative.

Plaintiff’s reply argues defendant later produced calendars that “appear to be created from a different program than calendars previously produced in this matter.”  (Supp. Schulman Decl., ¶ 4.)  He does not present sufficient evidence of that.  Practice guides recommend first learning how the opposing party maintains or creates electronically stored information before demanding the information itself.  (Thomas, Electronic Evid. in Cal. (2023-2024 ed.) § 3:1.)  Plaintiff does not present such evidence.  That Schrader produced calendars that look different (Supp. Schulman Decl., Exs. A-D) does not necessarily mean Schrader used different software to create or maintain them.  The only evidence in the record of how Schrader maintained this electronically stored information is his declaration.  He states, “When doing work for clients of Crowd Control Group, LLC, I tracked my time on Google calendar.”  (Schrader Decl., ¶ 4.)  He further states, “I do not have any other calendars for that period of time for the work that I did for Crowd Control Group, LLC.”  (Id., ¶ 6.) 

Schrader also acted with substantial justification in opposing the request for documents in native format for another reason.  Plaintiff used a common and generally applicable definition of “native format”: the way files are “typically provided through the software program.”  (See Ellis, supra, 218 Cal.App.4th at p. 859, fn. 4.)  That definition     does not necessarily fit files stored on the internet that the user accesses through a web browser or other application.  Plaintiff’s counsel shows there is a way “to export files from a Google Calendar.”  (Supp. Schulman Decl., ¶ 7.)  Exporting the raw or native format files is not how the files are “typically provided through the software program.”  The exported files are not how users like Schrader view or edit their calendars.  They are an alternative format or the format by which Google maintains or hosts the files.

Disposition

Plaintiff Robert La Masney’s motion to compel defendant Christian Schrader to provide further responses to requests for production is granted.  Defendant Christian Schrader is ordered to provide further responses without objections and with verified statements of compliance under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220 to requests for production, set three, Nos. 47 and 48 within 20 days.  Defendant Christian Schrader shall produce all responsive documents concurrently with his verified written responses.

 

 


Case Number: 23STCV01875    Hearing Date: April 26, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendants Armando Berriz and Berriz Law Group, PC’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

Defendants Armando Berriz and Berriz Law Group, PC (collectively, Berriz), move for judgment on the pleadings on plaintiff The Law Offices of Jacob Emrani, APC’s first amended complaint.

Plaintiff’s claims against Berriz arise from a dispute between plaintiff as the discharged attorney and Berriz as the subsequent attorney in a personal injury action on behalf of former client and current defendant Karen Edmonson.  Defendants rely on the rule that “ ‘[a]fter the client obtains a judgment, the attorney must bring a separate, independent action against the client to establish the existence of the lien, to determine the amount of the lien, and to enforce it.’ ”  (Mojtahedi v. Vargas (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 974, 977–978 (Mojtahedi).)  There, the Court of Appeal held a demurrer was properly sustained without leave to amend because the complaint “failed to allege facts establishing that he adjudicated the existence, value, or enforceability of” the initial attorney’s “lien.”  (Id. at p. 978.)  The court noted the initial attorney “admits that he never brought a separate action against his clients to litigate those issues.”  (Ibid.)

Mojtahedi does not apply here because plaintiff has sued the client.  The “independent action” rule refers to an action independent of the underlying action representing the client, i.e., Edmonson’s personal injury case.  This is such an independent action.  Berriz provides no authority requiring the former attorney to bring two independent actions: one against the client, then a second against the subsequent attorney.  The Court of Appeal criticized that premise in Aresh v. Marin-Morales (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 296.  In a footnote, the court stated, “We believe Mojtahedi is distinguishable from this case.  In Mojtahedi, the initial attorney attempted to enforce his fee lien against the subsequent attorney without involving the clients.  …  In contrast to Mojtahedi, Aresh did include his former clients in his lawsuit, thus giving them the opportunity to be heard that was improperly denied to the clients in that case.  The issue of simultaneous litigation was never raised in Mojtahedi.”  (Id. at p. 302, fn. 3.) 

The court finds this reasoning persuasive.  Plaintiff simultaneously brings claims against former client Edmonson and subsequent attorney Berriz.  Mojtahedi therefore does not mandate dismissing plaintiff’s claims against Berriz.  Requiring plaintiff to litigate these claims in two actions would not serve the interests of justice.

Defendants Armando Berriz and Berriz Law Group, PC’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is denied.
