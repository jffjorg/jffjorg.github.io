DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00438    Hearing Date: November 9, 2023    Dept: W

ESPOSITO V. YOUSEF, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR AN AWARD OF ATTORNEY FEES TO THE PREVAILING PARTY UPON CONTRACT AND STATUTE

 

Date of Hearing:        November 9, 2023                            Trial Date:       None set. 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00438

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Samir Yousif and Yousif Family Trust

Responding Party:     None

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of a landlord-tenant dispute concerning a Porsche restoration business, ran by Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges that she and Defendants Samir Yousef (“Yousef”) and Yousef Family Trust (“Trust”) (collectively, “Defendants”) entered into a written lease permitting Plaintiff to rent the property located at 14520 Delano Street, Van Nuys, California in order to conduct her business operations. It is further alleged that, due to Covid-19, Plaintiff fell behind on her monthly rent payments. As a result, Defendants pursued an unlawful detainer action against her and achieved a default judgment against Plaintiff, despite her willingness to start resume rental payments and to cover past dues. Plaintiff was locked out from the subject property on February 15, 2022 and was improperly prevented from recovering her personal property in a reasonable manner. Consequently, Plaintiff claims that she is incurring lost business because she is unable to access her tools and equipment.

 

Plaintiff filed a Complaint on March 25, 2022 alleging: (1) Conversion; (2) Trespass to Chattels; (3) Interference with Contract; (4) Interference with Perspective Economic Advantage; (5) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; and (6) Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

On June 27, 2023, the Court issued a tentative Statement of Decision, entering judgment for Defendants against Plaintiffs. Defendants now move for attorney fees.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion for an Award of Attorney Fees to the Prevailing Party upon Contract and Statute’s is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Samir Yousif and Yousif Family Trust, move the court, for a Court Order awarding reasonable attorney’s fees in the sum of $53,079.15, against Plaintiffs Laurina Esposito and Gustavo Flores, jointly and severally. Defendants make the motion on the grounds that Defendants are entitled to recover their attorney’s fees which they have incurred as the prevailing party in this action, based on an attorney’s fees provision in the written agreement between the parties.  

 

As a general rule, each party to litigation must bear its own attorney fees, unless otherwise provided by statute or contract. (CCP §1021.) However, Civil Code section 1717, subdivision (a) provides in pertinent part: “In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” Contractual attorney's fees must ordinarily be sought by noticed motion, unless the parties stipulate otherwise. (See CCP § 1033.5(c)(5); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702(a).) A motion is necessary to determine both entitlement to, and the amount of, any fees. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702(a) [motion procedure applies when court determines entitlement to fees, amount of fees, or both]; P R Burke Corp. v Victor Valley Wastewater Reclamation Auth. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1052.)

 

Defendants seek $53,079.15 in attorney fees. First, the court notes Defendants are entitled to attorney fees as the prevailing party. On June 27, 2023, the court issued a tentative Statement of Decision. (Kim Decl. ¶4, Exh. 2.) On July 18, 2023, the court issued an order adopting the proposed Statement of Decision with no objections made by the parties and on August 1, 2023, a judgment for Defendants was entered against Plaintiffs. (Kim Decl., ¶¶5-6, Exhs. 3-4.) The court issued Notice of Entry of Judgment on August 7, 2023. (Kim Decl. ¶5, Exh. 5.) Plaintiffs’ claims arose out of a commercial lease agreement between the parties. Paragraph 31 of the agreement provided a broad fees provision for “full[] reimbursement” of actual reasonable attorney’s fees incurred without limitation by any court fee schedule, for any actions, founded in tort, contract or equity. (Kim Decl. ¶3, Exh. 1.)

 

Next, the court finds the fees reasonable and proper excluding the $6,000.00 in fees with predecessor counsel. Defendants contend defense counsel incurred 137.9 hours successfully defending the entirety of Plaintiffs’ complaints that only came to a resolution after a trial, despite Defendants’ pretrial attempts to settle the matter without a trial. Defendants provide itemized invoices, breaking down the hours as follows: 61.4 hours for Mr. Obagi, 31 hours for Mr. Kim, and 45.5 hours for administrative staff, prior to the instant motion. (Kim Decl. ¶12.) Defendants also request 6 hours, for a total of $2,975, as reasonable fees associated with this motion. (Kim Decl. ¶¶ 14-17.) However, upon review of the invoices and case at hand, Defendants have not substantiated the $6,000 in fees incurred by Defendants’ former counsel, Nader. (Obagi Decl. ¶19.)

 

Accordingly, the court grants Defendants’ request for attorneys’ fees in the total, reduced amount of $ 47,079.15

Case Number: 22VECV01165    Hearing Date: November 9, 2023    Dept: W

ROBIN LA DUE AND SABINE LA DUE. v. SHAHE BOYADJIAN

 

plaintiffs’ motion for trial preference and preliminary injunction

 

Date of Hearing:        November 9, 2023                             Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        22VECV01165

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Robin La Due and Sabine La Due

Responding Party:     Defendant Shahe Boyadjian

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of a landlord-tenant dispute as between Plaintiffs/Tenants Robin La Due and Sabine La Due (“Plaintiffs” or “La Dues”) and Defendant/Landlord Shahe Boyadjian (“Defendant”). Sabine La Due is Robin La Due’s elderly mother. Plaintiffs have sued Boyadjian for alleged issues that arose out of their tenancy.

 

The parties have a history of litigation between them that began prior to Plaintiffs filing the instant action. In January 2022, Boyadjian filed a small claims action against Robin La Due to recover unpaid rent. Judgment was ultimately entered in favor of Boyadjian, against La Due. (Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibit “A”.) On June 21, 2022, Boyadjian filed an unlawful detainer action against Robin La Due stemming from his longstanding unjustified failure to pay rent at the subject property, in direct violation of the lease agreement. That matter was also fully litigated and resulted in another judgment in favor of Boyadjian, against La Due. (Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibit “B”.) Following those two actions by Boyadjian, the La Dues subsequently brought the instant civil action on August 15, 2022

 

On November 16, 2022, the Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint. On January 13, 2023, they filed a second amended complaint. A third amended complaint was filed August 17, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Plaintiff’s Motion for Trial Preference is Denied unless Plaintiff can Provide Additional Evidence

II.                  Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preliminary Injunction is Denied without Prejudice

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.                     PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR TRIAL PREFERENCE

 

Plaintiffs move this court for an order granting a trial preference pursuant to California Rules of Court 3.1335 and Code of Civil Procedure section 36(a). Plaintiff moved ex parte and the court denied the motion. (See October 2, 2023 Minute Order.)

 

Pursuant¿to Code of Civil Procedure section 36(a), a party to a civil action who is over the age of 70 must be given preference if the party has a substantial interest in the¿action¿as a whole, and¿the health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.¿ To make the findings required by Section 36(a), evidence must be provided with the motion for preference establishing Plaintiff’s age and the relevant conditions of her health warranting a preference.¿ Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure¿section 36.5, an attorney affidavit¿offered in support of a motion for preference may be based on¿“information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of a party.”¿¿¿ 

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36(e), in its discretion, the court may grant a motion for preference that is supported by a showing that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting preference.¿¿¿If a motion for preference based on a party’s age is granted, the matter must be set for trial not more than 120 days from the date the motion is granted.¿ (CCP §36(f).)¿¿ 

 

“The trial court has no power to balance the differing interests of opposing litigants in applying¿[CCP¿§ 36]. The express legislative mandate for trial preference is a substantive public policy concern which supersedes such considerations.”¿ (Swaithes¿v. Superior Court¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1082, 1085-86.¿¿See¿also¿Vinokur¿v. Superior Court¿(1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 500, 503 (“[T]here can be no inroads into the mandate of section 36.”)¿¿¿ 

 

The ex parte motion is not supported by any evidence of Plaintiff Sabine’s age or an affidavit regarding Plaintiff Sabine’s dementia diagnosis. The provisions of Section 36 are mandatory. The motion will be granted once Plaintiff Sabine submits evidence of her age and an affidavit regarding Plaintiff Sabine’s medical diagnosis. The court will require the additional evidence at the hearing to support Plaintiff’s request.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

Plaintiff applies for a preliminary injunction to enjoin Defendants from selling or giving way property which belongs to the Plaintiff.[1]

 

The purpose of a preliminary injunction is to preserve the status quo pending a decision on the merits.  (Major v. Miraverde Homeowners Ass’n. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 618, 623.)  In deciding whether or not to grant a preliminary injunction, the court looks to two factors, including “(1) the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits, and (2) the relative balance of harms that is likely to result from the granting or denial of interim injunctive relief.”  (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 553-54.) The factors are interrelated, with a greater showing on one permitting a lesser showing on the other.  (Dodge, Warren & Peters Ins. Services, Inc. v. Riley (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1414, 1420.)  However, the party seeking an injunction must demonstrate at least a reasonable probability of success on the merits.  (IT Corp. v. County of Imperial (1983) 35 Cal.3d 63, 73-74.)  The party seeking the injunction bears the burden of demonstrating both a likelihood of success on the merits and the occurrence of irreparable harm.  (Savage v. Trammell Crow Co. (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 1562, 1571.) Irreparable harm may exist if the plaintiff can show an inadequate remedy at law.  (CCP § 526(a).)   

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have not set forth reasonable probability that they will prevail on the merits as Plaintiffs fail to present evidence that their belongings are still actually there. As noted above, a preliminary injunction may not issue without some showing of potential entitlement to relief. (Doe v. Wilson (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 296, 304.) Moreover, Defendants present declarations, under oath, that they lawfully removed and disposed of all abandoned property after giving Plaintiffs proper notice. (Shahe Boyadjian Decl. ¶¶6-11; Maral Boyadjian Decl. ¶¶6-11.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the court denies Plaintiffs’ motion for preliminary injunction.

 

 

 

 


 


Case Number: 23VECV00091    Hearing Date: November 9, 2023    Dept: W

ROBIN LA DUE vs SHAHE BOYADIJAN, et al.

 

defendants’ demurrer to the amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        November 9, 2023                            Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV00091

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Shahe Boyadjian and Maral Boyadjian  

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 9, 2023, Plaintiff Robin La Due filed a complaint against Defendants Shahe Boyadjian and Maral Boyadjian. Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint on August 24, 2023 for Breach of Contract, Stolen Property, and Malicious Sabotage.

 

The parties have a history of litigation between them that began prior to Plaintiffs filing the instant action. In January 2022, Boyadjian filed a small claims action against Robin La Due to recover unpaid rent. Judgment was ultimately entered in favor of Boyadjian, against La Due. On June 21, 2022, Boyadjian filed an unlawful detainer action against Robin La Due stemming from his longstanding unjustified failure to pay rent at the subject property, in direct violation of the lease agreement. That matter was also fully litigated and resulted in another judgment in favor of Boyadjian, against La Due. Following those two actions by Boyadjian, Plaintiff subsequently brought two civil actions against the Boyadjians. In this action, Plaintiff alleges Defendants did not properly give Plaintiff enough time to vacate the premises upon the Sheriffs arrival and left their personal property at the unit, which Defendants have refused to return. Plaintiff also alleges Defendant withheld Plaintiff’s security deposit.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Shahe Boyadjian and Maral Boyadjian’s Demurrer to the Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request this court take judicial notice of Judgment in Case Number 22VEUD00858 (Exh. A) and Return on Attachment/Execution in Case Number 22VEUD00858 (Exh. B). The court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants demur to the amended complaint on the grounds the claims are uncertain and fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action against Defendants.

 

First Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action on the grounds it cannot be ascertained what are the material terms of the "rental agreement” and no such “rental agreement” is attached to the complaint.

 

The court agrees. To recover damages for a breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for non-performance; (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) The pleadings must specify that the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct. (CCP § 430.10(g).) Further, “[a] written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. [Citation.] In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms. This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’  [Citation.]” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489 [citing 4 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (4th ed. 1997) Pleading, §§ 479-480, pp. 572-73].)

 

Here, the written agreement is not attached to the complaint nor are the terms alleged. Moreover, as noted by Defendants, it is unclear from the allegations how Defendants breached the security deposit agreement when it withheld the money.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Second Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the second cause of action on the grounds the property is not identified and moreover, after the lockout, unless and until Plaintiff paid storage costs, Plaintiff had no right to possession.

 

To plead a cause of action for conversion, one must allege (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)

 

The court finds Plaintiffs fail to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for ‘stolen property’. The complaint alleges Defendants caused damages to Plaintiffs in the amount of $252,726 or alternatively, Plaintiff will take the personal property back instead of the money. It is unclear whether this is the security deposit amount. Additionally, Civil Code section 1950.5 applies to security deposits for residential property used as a tenant’s dwelling.  (Civ. Code § 1950.5(a).)  “The elements of an action for wrongful retention of a security deposit under Civil Code §¿1950.5 are: (1) the plaintiff paid a security deposit; (2) the security deposit was for a residential property; (3) the plaintiff used the property as a dwelling; and (4) the amounts deducted by the defendant were not reasonably necessary. If the plaintiff is seeking punitive damages, then it must also be shown that the defendant made the deductions in bad faith.”  (Peviani v. Arbors at California Oaks Property Owner, LLC (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 874, 899.)  The bad faith retention of a security deposit may subject the landlord to statutory damages of up to twice the amount of the security deposit in addition to actual damages.  (Civ. Code, § 1950.5(l).) The complaint fails to allege any facts supporting a claim under Civil Code section 1950.5.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Third Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for ‘malicious sabotage’ on the grounds it is unclear what the cause of action is actually alleged against Defendants and moreover, Defendants committed no act which was wrongful independent of the interference in that Defendants was lawfully permitted to store Plaintiffs personal property once Plaintiff was locked out.

 

The court agrees. There is no cause of action for ‘malicious sabotage’. Looking at the complaint, it is uncertain whether the cause of action is for interference with economic advantage. If Plaintiff is claiming Defendants knowingly destroyed Plaintiff’s business, Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to show an (1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and a third party which contained a reasonably probable future economic benefit or advantage to plaintiff; (2) defendant knew of the existence of the relationship and was aware or should have been aware that if it did not act with due care its actions would interfere with this relationship and cause plaintiff to lose in whole or in part the probable future economic benefit or advantage of the relationship; (3) defendant was negligent; and (4) such negligence caused damage to plaintiff in that the relationship was actually interfered with or disrupted and plaintiff lost in whole or in part the economic benefits or advantage reasonably expected from the relationship. (North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 786.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.
