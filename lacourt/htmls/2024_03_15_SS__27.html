Case Number: 19STCV03217    Hearing Date: March 15, 2024    Dept: 27

Complaint Filed:      10/2/22¿ 

¿

Department 27¿ 

Tentative Ruling¿ 

 

Hearing Date:           3/15/2024 at 1:30 p.m.¿ 

Case No./Name:       19STCV03217 MADISON CASTELLANOS, A MINOR vs LOS ANGELES COMMUNITY COLLEGE

Motion:                    MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Moving Party:           Defendant LOS ANGELES COMMUNITY COLLEGE

Responding Party:    Plaintiff

Notice:                     Sufficient¿ 

 

Ruling:                     SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS DENIED.

 

SUMMARY ADJUDICATION TO ISSUES 1,2,3 AND 6 IS DENIED.

 

SUMMARY ADJUDICATION TO ISSUES 4 AND 5 IS GRANTED.

 

 

¿ 

BACKGROUND¿ 

 

On January 30, 2019, Plaintiff filed the present case, alleging injury from an incident where her school teacher, Yenifer Galicia (“Galicia”), closed a door on her finger, resulting in the severance of parts of her pinky. Plaintiff asserts two causes of action: (1) Negligence and Dangerous Condition of Public Property and (2) Premises Liability. The allegations focus on the school district's negligence due to its teacher's failure to ensure no children were near the door when closing it, and the absence of protective measures or layers around the door's edges to prevent accidents. Plaintiff further alleges that the door, having sharp edges, constituted a dangerous condition, especially around very young children. Defendant now moves the court for summary judgment, arguing that the discovery responses provided by Plaintiff were factually devoid and introduced evidence that its employees did not act negligently. Alternatively, Defendant seeks summary adjudication on the following six issues: 1) No breach of duty for Plaintiff's negligence claim; 2) No proximate cause for the negligence claim; 3) Defendant's property was free from any dangerous conditions; 4) Defendant did not create the alleged dangerous condition; 5) Plaintiff cannot demonstrate that Defendant was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property; 6) Defendant's actions did not substantially cause Plaintiff's injury.

Although Plaintiff brings forth two causes of action for 1) negligence and dangerous condition of public property, and 2) premises liability, it is well-established law that a public entity, such as Los Angeles Community College District, cannot be liable under general tort principles in the absence of a specific statute that declares the entity may be liable. (Gov. Code, § 815(a); Trinkle v. Cal. State Lottery (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 1198, 1202). The applicable statutes here are Government Code Section 835, for dangerous condition of public property and Government Code Section 815.2(a), which states, "A public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee." Plaintiff thus has two potentially viable causes of action: 1) a claim under Section 815.2, related to the teacher's negligence, and 2) a dangerous condition of public property under Section 835.

Legal Standard¿ 

¿ 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite its allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve its dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843). “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119).¿¿ 

¿ 

A “defendant moving for summary judgment must show the plaintiff’s causes of action have¿no merit.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849, emphasis added). In other words, summary judgment is not to be granted simply because a case is “weak” or because a “weak” showing was made in opposition. (Hagen v. Hickenbottom¿(1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 168, 187-188, superseded by statute on another point as recognized in¿Rice v. Clark¿(2002) 28 Cal.4th 89, 96;¿Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc.¿(2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 589, 722. [“to avoid summary judgment a showing need not be strong; it need only be sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact.”]).¿ 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519). A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). The elements of negligence are (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318). 

 

Defendant has two methods to shift the burden of proof. In addition to the standard method of presenting evidence that negates an essential element, the defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. (Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 590).¿“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).¿ 

 

 

GOVERNMENT CODE SECTION 835 
 
The sole statutory basis for imposing premise liability on public entities as property owners is Government Code section 835. (See Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1131- 32.) To establish a dangerous condition of public property under Government Code section 835, Plaintiff must prove (1) the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of injury; (2) the injury that occurred was proximately caused by the dangerous condition; (3) the kind of injury that occurred was reasonably foreseeable as a consequence of the dangerous condition; and (4) either:

 

(a) the dangerous condition was created by a public employee’s negligent or wrongful act or omission within the scope of his or her employment, or

 

(b) the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition with sufficient time before the injury occurred to have taken reasonable measures to protect against the injury. (Gov. Code, § 835.) 

 

Analysis

 

1.  Dangerous Condition (Issue 3)

 

Defendant requests summary adjudication on the issue that Defendant's property did not contain any dangerous conditions. In meeting its initial burden, Defendant refers to Plaintiff's discovery responses to Defendant's Special Interrogatory No. 40, which requested Plaintiff to state all facts in support of her contention that there are dangerous conditions at the care center. (Exhibit I.) Plaintiff objected to this request on the grounds that it was vague, overly broad, and unduly burdensome and that it invaded the attorney-client privilege and work product doctine. (Exhibit J-1.) Defendant did not pursue this matter further, and thus Plaintiff did not provide additional responses to interrogatory No. 40 in her further responses to Defendant’s special interrogatories (Exhibit J2.)

Defendant cannot meet its burden by relying on Plaintiff’s objections. Objections are not treated the same as factually devoid interrogatories responses (Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 892-93). When the plaintiffs in Union Bank v. Superior Court (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 378 stated “mere beliefs” in response to a question requesting all facts supporting an element of their claims, the logical inference was that after discovery they possessed no facts to support that element. (Id.) However, no such logical inference of an absence of facts can be drawn from an objection even though it was not a proper objection. (Id.)  For summary judgment purposes, an objection is not the equivalent of a factually devoid response, and therefore cannot be used to shift the burden. (Id.) Thus, Defendant has failed to meet its burden and Summary Adjudication to Issue 3 is DENIED.

2.  Dangerous Condition Created by Defendant’s Employee (Issue 4 and 5)


Defendant seeks summary adjudication on issues 4 and 5 that Defendant did not create the dangerous condition and was not negligent in the use or maintenance of the property. These two issues relate to element 4(a) of Gov. Code, § 835, requiring Plaintiff to prove that (a) the dangerous condition was created by a public employee’s negligent or wrongful act or omission within the scope of his or her employment.

 

It should be noted that granting summary adjudication does not dispose of Plaintiff's dangerous condition of public property cause of action as long as Plaintiff can prove element 4(b) that the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition with sufficient time before the injury occurred to have taken measures to protect against the injury. In addition to the first three elements under section 835, Plaintiff can satisfy its burden of proof by establishing either element 4(a) or 4(b).

 

A.  Defendant’s Initial Burden

 

Defendant points to Plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to Defendant’s Special Interrogatories 44 to meet its initial burden. Interrogatory 44 requested Plaintiff to state all facts in support of her contention that Defendant was responsible for the "planning, design, supervision, control, construction, installation, servicing, management, inspection, operation, signing, maintenance, and repair" of the door involved in the INCIDENT at the CHILD CENTER, as alleged in the Complaint. (Exhibit I.) Initially Plaintiff objected to this special interrogatory, but provided further responses stating that defendants were responsible for insuring that the property was safe, they were aware that children put their hands in area that maybe dangerous, and defendants should have taken safety measures knowing that children were on premise to prevent children from harm. (Exhibit J2.) The responses did not contain any specific facts, but rather only legal conclusions and mere beliefs. A logical inference can be made that after discovery plaintiff possessed no facts to support this particular issue. Thus, Defendant has met its initial burden.

 

B.  Plaintiff’s Burden

 

A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture but must instead produce admissible evidence that raises a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524). "If Plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted." (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467).

Public property is deemed to be in a dangerous condition within the meaning of section 835 if it "is physically damaged, deteriorated, or defective in such a way as to foreseeably endanger those using the property itself." (Cordova v. City of Los Angeles (2015) 190 Cal.Rptr.3d 850, 853-54.) Furthermore, public property has been considered to be in a dangerous condition if its design, location, or a physical feature while in use may create a danger. (Bonanno v. Central Contra Costa Transit Authority (2003) 30 Cal.4th 139, 149.) The Court has examined Plaintiff's evidence and concludes that Plaintiff failed to introduce any evidence demonstrating that Defendant or its employees either created or negligently maintained the property that resulted in a physical feature of the property, like the door, to create a risk of harm. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of fact. Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to Issues 4 and 5 is GRANTED.

 

3.  Breach and Causation for the Negligence claim

 

Defendant seeks summary adjudication on issues 1, 2, and 6, contending that Defendant did not breach its duty and did not cause Plaintiff's injury. It is undisputed that Defendant's employee, Galicia, closed the door on Plaintiff's finger, severing pieces of her pinky. (UMF 10-11). The remaining question is whether Defendant breached her duty of care and whether Plaintiff's injury is a foreseeable result of that breach.

"Although a school district is not an insurer of its pupils' safety [citation], our cases have established that a school district bears a legal duty to exercise reasonable care in supervising students in its charge and may be held liable for injuries proximately caused by the failure to exercise such care. [Citations.] We reaffirmed this rule in Dailey v. Los Angeles Unified Sch. Dist. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 741, 747, declaring that 'California law has long imposed on school authorities a duty to supervise at all times the conduct of the children on school grounds and to enforce those rules and regulations necessary to their protection.' [Citations.] The standard of care imposed upon school personnel in carrying out this duty to supervise is identical to that required in the performance of their other duties. This uniform standard to which they are held is that degree of care 'which a person of ordinary prudence, charged with [comparable] duties, would exercise under the same circumstances.' [Citations.]" (Hoyem v. Manhattan Beach City School Dist. (1978) 22 Cal.3d 508, 513). The degree of adequate supervision, such as its comprehensiveness and intrusiveness, should align with the students' maturity level, ensuring it is suited to their capacity for discretion, judgment, and self-control appropriate to their age. (Dailey v. Los Angeles Unified Sch. Dist. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 741, 748).

 

A.  Parties’ Respective Burdens

 

Defendant introduced several pieces of evidence to meet its initial burden. First, Galicia was reported to continuously warn, "Be careful with your fingers, be careful, walking feet," as she was closing the door (Yasamin Zarabi Depo. 44:11-17 Exhibit C). The Unusual Incident Report stated that Galicia was carefully closing the door and telling the children to watch their fingers. Galicia continued to tell children to watch their fingers as the door was almost closed. Plaintiff then stuck part of her left hand on the left/other side of the door and her pinky was pinched by the door. (Unusual Incident Report, Exhibit F), Galicia stated in the Campus Incident Report that as she was closing the door from the inside of classroom CDC 122, she was making sure the toddlers did not have their fingers near the door jam. When she closed the door from the inside, that is when Plaintiff put her left pinky finger on the other side of the door jam. (Campus Incident Report Exhibit G.)

However, the evidence does not address whether the teachers were overseeing all of the children, including Plaintiff, near the door. Several critical questions remain unanswered, including: Was Galicia distracted by two other children near her, causing her not to notice Plaintiff on the other side of the door? How quickly did Plaintiff place her hand in the door crack? Did Galicia ensure the area near the door was clear before closing it? Did Galicia ensure that she had a full view of the door and the children near the door when she closed it? The evidence presented in the moving papers appears to reveal that Galicia was watching over the two children next to her and cautioning them to careful as she closed the door. While this level of caution might be deemed sufficient for adults or adolescents, the present case involves very young children under the age of two. The degree of adequate supervision, such as its comprehensiveness and intrusiveness, should align with the students' maturity level, ensuring it is suited to their capacity for discretion, judgment, and self-control appropriate to their age. (Dailey v. Los Angeles Unified Sch. Dist. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 741, 748.) Considering their very limited awareness of danger, self-control, and discretion, the Court concludes that reasonable minds can differ on whether Galicia, merely by issuing a verbal warning to be careful, fulfilled her duty of care, or whether the harm was a foreseeable consequence of Galicia's failure to meet her duty of care.

Moreover, even if Defendant can meet its initial burden, Plaintiff produced a declaration stating that “on the date of the incident, I never heard anyone tell me to be careful, or to stay away from the door, or that the door is closing, before one of the teachers closed the door on my finger." (Exhibit 1 Castellanos Decl. ¶ 6.)[1] Given Defendant's heavy reliance on Galicia issuing warnings to the children before she closed the door, Plaintiff's declaration raises a triable issue of fact. Summary Adjudication as to Issues 1, 2 and 6 is DENIED.

Background   

 

On October 10, 2019, Plaintiff filed the present assault and battery lawsuit. Defendant now moves the court to compel Plaintiff to provide discovery responses to the discovery request at issue.

 

Legal Standard 

 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.¿ 

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)¿¿¿¿¿

Analysis

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

 

Background

 

On November 16, Defense counsel, Michael Ryan, issued a notice of unavailability from December 12 through December 19, 2023, and from December 21, 2023, to January 1, 2024, due to arbitration commitments and international travel. On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff served notices for the depositions of 35 Persons Most Knowledgeable (PMQ), scheduled for January 3, 2024. On December 19, during one of the few days that defense counsel was available in December, he contacted Michael Kim, Plaintiff's attorney, to meet and confer and narrow the scope for both depositions and written discovery via a stipulation and clearer definition of PMQ categories. As part of their meet and confer, counsel agreed that Plaintiff would provide photographs of individuals, after which the defense would identify those individuals  and clarify their scope of knowledge.

 

Given this ongoing discussion and defense counsel’s unavailability, Defendant did not object to the January 3 depositions. On January 2, 2024, having received no objections, Kim emailed Ryan to confirm the next day's deposition. Ryan replied that the depositions would not proceed due to ongoing discussions to narrow the list of individuals to be deposed. Later that day, Defendant sent a draft stipulation to Plaintiff. On January 3, 2024, the deposition was nevertheless held, but Defendant did not appear. Plaintiff is now seeking a court order to compel the PMQ depositions, while defense counsel contends that Plaintiff knew he was away for most of December and when he was available the parties were still engaged in narrowing the scope, issues, and identifying individuals for deposition through meet and confer efforts.

 

Legal Standard

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral  deposition of any person, including any party to the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party or party-affiliated deponent to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).)   

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . or employee of a party . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  

 

“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)   


Analysis

 

Based on the sequence of events, defense counsel Michael Ryan was unavailable for a significant portion of December, limiting opportunities to meet and confer with the Plaintiff. When available, he engaged in discussions regarding the scope of depositions and the identification of necessary individuals to be deposed. The Court reviewed the email exchanges and noted that the parties exchanged photographs of individuals for deposition purposes and potentially narrowed down the list of individuals. Ryan also sent a draft stipulation on January 2, 2024. Considering the parties' prior meet and confer efforts, the court ORDERS the parties to further meet and confer and to schedule a PMQ deposition within 30 days from today and meet and confer regarding requested documents.

 

SANCTIONS

 

Defendant is entitled to sanctions in relation to the Supplemental Discovery to which Plaintiff responded late, which necessitated the filing of a motion.  The Court awards reasonable sanctions to Defendant against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $500 to be within 30 days.

 

With regard to sanction for the deposition and documents related thereto, the Court continues that hearing to May 1, 2024, at 1:30  p.m.

Case No./Name:       22STCV29743 MARIAN THOMPSON vs SHAWN O'NEIL KINSEY, et al.

Motion:                    Motion for Relief for Waiver of Jury Trial

Moving Party:           Plaintiff

Responding Party:    Unopposed

Notice:                     Sufficient

 

Ruling:                     MOTION FOR RELIEF FOR WAIVER OF JURY TRIAL IS GRANTED

 

 

Background

 

On September 12, 2022, Plaintiff filed the auto accident case. Plaintiff filed a notice of posting jury fees on January 4, 2023, but jury fees weren’t posted. On January 24, 2024, Plaintiff's counsel, Timothy D. McGonigle, first became aware that the jury fees had inadvertently not been posted. Plaintiff now moves the court to request relief from waiver of jury trial.

 

Legal Standard

 

“The right to a trial by jury as declared by Section 16 of Article I of the California Constitution shall be preserved to the parties inviolate.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 631(a).)  “In civil cases, a jury may only be waived pursuant to subdivision (f).”  (Id.)  CCP section 631(f) provides that a party waives trial by jury by (1) failing to appear at the trial; (2) written consent filed with the clerk or judge; (3) oral consent, in open court, entered in the minutes; (4) failing to announce that a jury is required, at the time the cause is first set for trial, if it is set upon notice of stipulation, or within five days after notice of setting if it is set without notice or stipulation; (5) failing to timely pay the fee described in subdivision (b), unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee; or (6) failing to deposit with the clerk or judge, at the beginning of the second and each succeeding day’s session, the sum provided in subdivision (e).  (Id., § 631(f).)   

 

“The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.”  (Id., § 631(g).)  “The trial court should grant a motion for relief of a jury waiver ‘unless, and except, where granting such a motion would work serious hardship to the objecting party.’”  (Mackovska v. Viewcrest Road Properties LLC (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1, 10 (quoting Boal v. Price Waterhouse & Co. (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 806, 809).)  “When there is doubt about whether to grant relief from a jury trial waiver, the court must resolve that doubt in favor of the party seeking a jury trial.”  (Id.) 

 

“In a motion for relief from waiver of a jury trial, the crucial question is whether the party opposing relief will suffer any prejudice if the court grants relief.”  (Mackovska, supra, 40 Cal.App.5th at 10.)  “‘‘The prejudice which must be shown from granting relief from the waiver is prejudice from the granting of relief and not prejudice from the jury trial.’’”  (Id. (quoting Massie v. AAR Western Skyways, Inc. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 405, 411).)  “‘The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.’”  (Id. (quoting Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 808, 811).)  “Denying relief where the party opposing the motion for relief has not shown prejudice is an abuse of discretion.”  (Id.) 

 

Analysis and Conclusion

 

The complaint was filed on September 12, 2022. Jury fees must be paid no later than 365 calendar days after the filing of the complaint, meaning that the jury fee must be paid by September 12, 2023. (Code of Civil Procedure, section 631(c)(2).) Plaintiff believed they had paid the jury fee in January 2023, but later discovered the fees were not paid, thus waiving their right to a jury trial under CCP section 631(f)(5). No opposition was filed and other parties do not contend that they would be prejudiced by the election of a jury trial. Further, in the Court’s view, imposing a trial waiver on the facts here is too punitive.  Therefore, the motion is GRANTED.

Motion:                    MOTION TO SUBSTITUTE SUCCESSOR IN INTEREST 

Moving Party:           Plaintiff

Responding Party:    Unopposed

Notice:                     Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:                     MOTION TO SUBSTITUTE SUCCESSOR IN INTEREST IS DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE

 

 

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the present slip and fall case on 6/30/23. Plaintiff died on July 7, 2023, but plaintiff’s claims survive his death. DOMITILA MARTINEZ, as decedent’s personal representative and executor of decedent’s estate, now moves the Court to be substitute as successor in interest in the present case.

 

Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 377.31 provides that the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, the decedent’s successor in interest may continue a decedent’s pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.30; see Adams v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 71, 78-79.) A successor in interest is the beneficiary of the decedent’s estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of the cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.11.) Section 377.33 provides that the court in which an action is continued may make any order concerning parties that is appropriate to ensure proper administration of justice, including the appointment of the decedent’s successor in interest as a special administrator or guardian ad litem.  

 

Under Section 377.32(a), the person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent's successor in interest under this article, shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating all of the following:

 

(1) The decedent's name.
(2) The date and place of the decedent's death.
(3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent's estate.”
(4) If the decedent's estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent's cause of action to the successor in interest.
(5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof:
     (A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”
     (B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”
(6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.”
(7) “The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.”

(b) Where more than one person executes the affidavit or declaration under this section, the statements required by subdivision (a) shall be modified as appropriate to reflect that fact.

(c) A certified copy of the decedent's death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Id., § 377.32(c).)¿ 

Analysis

Plaintiff did not comply with CCP § 377.32, which requires a declaration providing specific information for the continuation of an action after a decedent's death. The required details that are missing in Plaintiff’s affidavit include CCP § 377.32 (a)(2) the place of death, (3) a declaration confirming that “No proceeding is now pending in California for the administration of the decedent's estate,” and (4) if the decedent's estate was administered, a copy of the final order that demonstrates the distribution of the decedent's cause of action to the successor in interest. Additionally, it must be stated that (6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.” Although the decedent’s death certificate was provided, it is illegible. Plaintiff needs to provide a legible copy of the defendant’s death certificate.

Thus, Plaintiff’s present motion is DENIED without prejudice.
