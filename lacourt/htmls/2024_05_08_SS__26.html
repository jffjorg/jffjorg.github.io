Case Number: 19STLC09306    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 26

 

ACIC v. Ramirez, et al.

MOTION FOR ASSIGNMENT ORDER

(CCP § 708.510)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Judgment Creditor American Contractors Indemnity Company’s Motion for Order of Assignment of Payments, for Order Restraining Judgment Debtor from Assigning or Otherwise Disposing of the Right to Payment, and for Order Compelling Accounting is GRANTED WITH RESPECT TO PAYMENTS MADE TO JUDGMENT DEBTOR BY MGM DRYWALL, INC.

 

JUDGMENT DEBTOR IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED ORDER IN ACCORDANCE WITH THIS RULING WITHIN 20 DAYS.

 

 

 

ANALYSIS:  

 

On October 8, 2019, Plaintiff American Contractors Indemnity Company (“Judgment Creditor”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Jose Luis Guillen Ramirez aka Jose Luis Guillen (“Judgment Debtor) for breach of contract. On May 6, 2020, this Court entered default judgment against Judgment Debtor for the total amount of $15,728.96.

 

On February 27, 2024, Judgment Creditor filed the instant Motion for Order of Assignment of Payments, for Order Restraining Judgment Debtor from Assigning or Otherwise Disposing of the Right to Payment, and for Order Compelling Accounting. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.510, subdivision (a), the moving statute, the Court may order the judgment debtor to assign to the judgment creditor or to an appointed receiver all or part of a right to payment due or to become due. The types of payments that can be assigned include wages due from the federal government if not subject to a withholding order, rents, commissions, royalties, patent or copyright payments, and insurance policy loan value. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (a).)

 

Relevant factors the Court may take into consideration when making an assignment order include the judgment debtor’s reasonable requirements if they are a natural person, payments the judgment debtor is required to make to satisfy other judgments and wage assignments, the amount remaining due on the judgment, and the amount to be received in satisfaction of the right to payment that may be assigned.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (c).) Construing all the applicable statutes together, the “assignment order” contemplated by Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.510, et seq. must include a court order that assigns a right to payment outright (not simply an order directing the judgment debtor to do so). 

 

Here, Judgment Creditor seeks an order (1) directing payments made by MGM Drywall, Inc. or out of MGM Drywall, Inc.’s account(s) to Judgment Debtor be assigned to Judgment Creditor; (2) directing Judgment Debtor to turn over to Judgment Creditor any payments made by MGM Drywall, Inc. or out MGM Drywall, Inc.’s account(s); (3) restraining Judgment Debtor or any servant, agent, employee, attorney, or anyone else acting on his behalf or jointly with him from assigning or otherwise disposing of the right to payment; and (4) compelling Judgment Debtor to provide, file, and serve an accounting every other month as a means of monitoring Judgment Debtor’s activity and to ensure compliance with the Court’s orders. (Motion, pp. 1:24-2:15.)

 

On May 6, 2020, judgment was entered in Judgment Creditor’s favor and against Judgment Debtor in the amount of $15,728.96. (Motion, Murray Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.) Furthermore, Judgment Creditor has attempted to enforce its judgment but has only obtained $500.65 through a bank levy on JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (Id. at ¶ 3, Exh. 2.) Judgment Creditor also avers that it subpoenaed Judgment Debtor’s bank records from JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. and discovered he still works as an unlicensed contractor for certain customers, including MGM Drywall, Inc. (Id. at ¶¶4-5, Exhs. 3-4.) Judgment Debtor endorses checks from MGM Drywall, Inc. and deposits them. (Id. at ¶7 and Exh. 4.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Judgment Creditor’s motion for an order of assignment of payments, restraining Judgment Debtor from assigning or otherwise disposing of the right to payment, and compelling accounting is warranted under the law. Judgment Creditor has provided sufficient evidence to support that Judgment Debtor consistently receives payments from MGM Drwyall, Inc.

 

Conclusion

 

Therefore, Judgment Creditor American Contractors Indemnity Company’s Motion for Order of Assignment of Payments, for Order Restraining Judgment Debtor from Assigning or Otherwise Disposing of the Right to Payment, and for Order Compelling Accounting is GRANTED WITH RESPECT TO PAYMENTS MADE TO JUDGMENT DEBTOR BY MGM DRYWALL, INC.

 

JUDGMENT DEBTOR IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED ORDER IN ACCORDANCE WITH THIS RULING WITHIN 20 DAYS.

Case Number: 22STLC00251    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 26

 


 

Zazueta v. Reihaneh, et al.

MOTION TO RECOVER NON-ATTORNEY FEES


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Richard R. Zazueta’s Amended Motion to Recover Non-Attorney Fees is DENIED.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Richard Zazueta (“Plaintiff”), in propria persona, filed the instant action for motor vehicle negligence against Defendant Sarraf Shirazi Reihaneh (“Defendant”) on January 13, 2021. Defendant answered the Complaint on March 1, 2022. On June 9, 2022, the Court granted Defendant’s Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production, and Requests for Sanctions against Plaintiff. (Minute Order, 06/09/22.) On November 1, 2022, the Court denied Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions. (Minute Order, 11/01/22.)

 

The action came for trial on September 14, 2023, at which time the parties stipulated that Defendant is liable for damages. (Minute Order, 09/14/23.) Judgment was entered in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $3,792.41. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff filed the instant Amended Motion for Non-Attorney Fees on March 15, 2024. An additional memorandum was filed by Plaintiff on April 29, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

Discussion

 

The Motion appears to seek recovery of costs Plaintiff incurred prosecuting this action.

 

First, the Motion is not accompanied by a proper proof of service; the proofs of service are not attested to by the person who served the papers. (Motion, filed 03/15/24, p. 5; Memo, filed 04/29/24, p. 15.) Failure to give notice of a motion is not only a violation of the statutory requirements but of the due process owed to Defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005; Jones v. Otero (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 757.)

 

Second, the Motion cite to “Rule #1717.” (Motion, filed 03/15/24, pp. 1-2.) apparently meaning California Civil Code Section 1717, but this does not authorize the specified costs. The second memorandum cites dozens of statutes but does not explain how any of them provides for an award of non-attorney fees to Plaintiff in this action. (Memo, filed 04/29/24, pp. 1-2.) This fails to comply with the requirements for a memorandum of points and authorities under the California Rules of Court. “The memorandum must contain a statement of facts, a concise statement of the law, evidence and arguments relied on, and a discussion of the statutes, cases, and textbooks cited in support of the position advanced.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1113(b).)

 

Therefore, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a basis for an award of non-attorney fees.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Richard R. Zazueta’s Amended Motion to Recover Non-Attorney Fees is DENIED.

Garcia v. Prater, et al.

MOTION TO VACATE DISMISSAL

(CCP § 473(b))


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Isidro Humberto Herrera Garcia’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal is GRANTED. THE DISMISSAL ENTERED ON JANUARY 16, 2024 IS HEREBY VACATED.

 

ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE FAILURE TO FILE PROOF OF SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND COMPLAINT IS SET FOR JUNE 5, 2024 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On July 19, 2022, Plaintiff Isidro Humberto Herrera Garcia filed the instant action for motor vehicle negligence against Defendant Robert D. Prater (“Defendant”). The action came for trial on January 16, 2024, at which time Plaintiff failed to appear and the Court dismissed the action without prejudice. (Minute Order, 01/16/24.) Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal on March 26, 2024. No opposition to the Motion has been filed to date.

Nor has proof of service of the Summons and Complaint been filed.  

 

Discussion

 

The motion is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 473, subdivision (b). Under this statute, an application for relief must be made no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the moving party’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) When based on attorney fault with respect to entry of default, default judgment, or involuntary dismissal, a timely request for relief must be granted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) When brought pursuant to the provision for discretionary relief based on party fault, the request must have been filed within a reasonable amount of time.

 

The motion was timely filed less than two months after dismissal of the action and is supported by an attorney affidavit of fault. Plaintiff’s counsel declares that they believed they would receive a notice of the trial. (Motion, Paley Decl., ¶¶2-3.) Therefore, the case must be reinstated under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b).

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Isidro Humberto Herrera Garcia’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal is GRANTED. THE DISMISSAL ENTERED ON JANUARY 16, 2024 IS HEREBY VACATED.

 

ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE FAILURE TO FILE PROOF OF SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND COMPLAINT IS SET FOR JUNE 5, 2024 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

Case Number: 22STLC05572    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 26

  

Pafundi Law Firm v. Vera Roca, LLC, et al.

SUMMARY JUDGMENT

(CCP § 437c)

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Cross-Defendant Pafundi Law Firm’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Pafundi Law Firm (“Cross-Defendant”) filed the instant action for breach of contract, fraud, promissory estoppel, and conversion against Defendants Vera Roca, LLC (“Cross-Complainant Vera Roca”) and Pablo Vera (“Cross-Complainant Vera”) (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”) on August 23, 2022. Cross-Complainants filed a Cross-Complaint for breach of contract and fraud against Cross-Defendant on January 20, 2023. On February 3, 2023, Cross-Complainants filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint.

 

On July 13, 2023, the Court sustained Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the fraud cause of action in the First Amended Cross-Complaint with leave to amend. (Minute Order, 07/13/23.) Cross-Complainants filed the Corrected Second Amended Cross-Complaint on August 22, 2023. On October 19, 2023, the Court sustained Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the fraud cause of action in the Second Amended Cross-Complaint without leave to amend, leaving only the cause of action for breach of contract. (Minute Order, 10/19/23.) Cross-Defendant filed an answer to the Corrected Second Amended Cross-Complaint on November 17, 2023.

 

Cross-Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment on February 2, 2024. Cross-Complainants filed an opposition on April 4, 2024 and Cross-Defendant replied on April 12, 2024.

 

Discussion

 

Allegations in the Complaint and Corrected Second Amended Cross-Complaint

 

Cross-Defendant alleges this action arises out of an oral agreement between the parties for Cross-Complainants to completely rebuild Cross-Defendant’s website and launch the rebuilt website after Cross-Defendant’s approval, in exchange for $5,000.00, reached on March 2, 2022. (Compl., ¶5.) On March 23, 2022 and March 29, 2022, the parties reached a further agreement that Cross-Complainants would create a landing page website while the full website was being rebuilt, in exchange for $1,250.00. (Id. at ¶6.)

 

Cross-Complainants allege that on or about July 5, 2022, the website was about 85 percent completed but Cross-Defendant materially breached the agreement by refusing to provide the promised content necessary to complete and launch the website. (SACC, ¶9.) Cross-Defendant also allegedly failed to pay the $1,250.00 owed for completing the landing page website. (Ibid.) Cross-Defendant is allegedly estopped from claiming Cross-Complainants’ failure to complete the website as a defense. (Ibid.)

 

On or about March 29, 2022, Cross-Complainant Vera and Robert Pafundi met in person and Robert Pafundi promised to pay $1,250.00 for the completed landing page, which he had approved by text message earlier that day. (Id. at ¶10.) Robert Pafundi’s representations on March 29, 2022 were intentional and false and he had no intention of paying for the landing page. (Id. at ¶¶12-13.) In reliance on the representations, Cross-Complainants entered into the agreement and expended time, resources, and funding to complete their obligations. (Id. at ¶14.) Robert Pafundi also knowingly and intentionally interfered with Cross-Complainants’ ability to complete its obligations under the agreement. (Id. at ¶15.) As a result of the misrepresentations, Cross-Complainants suffered damages. (Id. at ¶16.) 

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Cross-Defendant requests the Court take judicial notice of (1) Pafundi Law Firm, A Professional Corporation, Articles of Incorporation, filed on January 19, 2012; (2) Pafundi Law Firm, A Professional Corporation, Statement of Information, filed on January 25, 2024; (3) Vera Roca, LLC, Articles of Organization for California Limited Liability Company, filed on March 16, 2022; and (4) Screenshot of the Official Records of the California Secretary of State which

indicates that the Statement of Information for Vera Roca, LLC, is delinquent as of June 14, 2022. The request is granted pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d).

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Cross-Complainants’ evidentiary objections to paragraphs 4-12 of the declaration of Dan Goldwasser are sustained.

 

Cross-Complainants’ evidentiary objections to the Pafundi declaration are ruled on as follows:

Nos. 1, 3-8, 15, 18-19, 21, 35 overruled; Nos. 2, 9-14, 16-17, 20, 22, 33, 34, 36-40 sustained.

 

Cross-Complainants’ evidentiary objections to the Wong declaration are ruled on as follows: Nos. 1, 2, 4, sustained; No. 3 overruled.

 

Cross-Complainants’ evidentiary objections to the Simmons declaration are ruled on as follows: No. 1 sustained; No. 2 overruled.

 

Cross-Complainants’ evidentiary objections to the Gomez declaration are ruled on as follows: No. 2 sustained; No. 1 overruled.

 

Cross-Defendant’s evidentiary objections to the Vera declaration are ruled on as follows: Nos. 1-8 overruled.

 

Cross-Defendant’s Initial Burden of Proof

 

As an initial matter, the Court points out that the Motion is one for summary judgment, although entitled “Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication.” This is demonstrated by the notice of Motion itself, which moves “this Court for an order granting summary judgment in their favor and against Cross-Complainants . . . .” (Notice, pp. 1:28-2:2.)

 

An action for breach of contract must demonstrate the following elements: (1) the existence of the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff. (D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.)

 

Cross-Defendant’s Motion is brought based on three issues: (1) the breach of contract cause of action fails because Cross-Defendant never entered into a second contract for the creation of a landing page; (2) the breach of contract cause of action fails because Cross-Complainants did not perform their contractual obligations; and (3) the breach of contract cause of action fails because Cross-Defendant fulfilled all or substantially all its contractual obligations. (Notice of Motion, p. 1:6-15.) Despite dividing the Motion into these three issues, the moving separate statement does not correspondingly indicate which facts pertain to which issues. The Court, therefore, must make its own assessment of what facts are relevant to what issues.

 

            Issues 1 and 2: the existence of a second agreement and Cross-Complainants’ performance

 

In late February and early March 2022, the parties entered an enforceable contractual agreement whereby Cross-Complainants agreed to perform professional services related to designing, completely rebuilding, and, after approval thereof, launching an entirely new website for Cross-Defendant (“the Website”). (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 5; Pafundi Decl., ¶4 and Exh. 10.) Under the agreement, Cross-Complainants “agreed to design, rebuild, launch, and do all things necessary and related to the [Website], in exchange for a total payment of $5,000.00. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 6; Pafundi Decl., ¶5 and Exhs. 5, 12.) Cross-Complainants were to design and create the Website within 30 days, or before the end of March 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 10; Pafundi Decl., ¶7.)

 

Invoice # INV-836 states it covers 100% of all services to be performed for Cross-Defendant and was the only invoice ever submitted to Cross-Defendant. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 24-25; Pafundi Decl., ¶13 and Exh. 5.) In mid-March 2022 Cross-Defendant became concerned that the old website had been pulled down without a “coming soon” page up, which Cross-Complainant Vera stated was in the works on March 8, 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 41-42; Pafundi Decl., ¶27 and Exhs. 15-16.) During a March 17, 2022 meeting between Pafundi and Cross-Complainant Vera, the latter again stated it was no problem to get the “coming soon” page done. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 43; Pafundi Decl., ¶28 and Exh. 9.) Cross-Complainant Vera never mentioned an additional charge for the “coming soon” language on the already existing landing page and the “coming soon” page was posted for the public on March 22, 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 44-45; Pafundi Decl., ¶¶28-29 and Exh. 9.)

 

In late March 2022, Pafundi became increasingly concerned that Cross-Complainants would not fulfill their obligation to complete the website within 30 days. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 49; Pafundi Decl., ¶32.) On April 13, 2022, Grace Wong informed Pafundi that Cross-Complainant Vera informed her that he would have a draft ready for review the week of April 18, 2022 and the Website done by the end of April 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 51; Wong Decl., ¶8.) On March 29, 2022, Pafundi delivered another check for $1,250.00 to Cross-Complainants under Invoice # INV-836 despite the lack of any tangible evidence that the design or creation of the website, or any part thereof, was truly underway or being accomplished. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 55, 50; Pafundi Decl., ¶¶38, 40 and Exh. 9.) At no time did Cross-Complainants suggest a second contract was formed to post a simple landing page, i.e., “coming soon” page. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 58; Pafundi Decl., ¶40.)

 

The parties arranged an in-person meeting to review all aspects of what was represented as the “virtually completed” new website for May 21, 2022 for which Cross-Complainants did not appear. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 60-61; Pafundi Decl., ¶¶46-47 and Exhs. 9, 37.) Cross-Complainants then did not communicate for several weeks. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 62; Pafundi Decl., ¶48 and Exh. X.) On July 5, 2022, Cross-Defendant sent Cross-Complainants a demand letter with an opportunity to cure the breach of contract before a lawsuit would be filed. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 63-67; Pafundi Decl., Exhs. 24 and 27.) In response, Cross-Complainants accepted the first option to cure the breach by completing the website to Pafundi’s satisfaction by close of business on July 22, 2022. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 69-70; Pafundi Decl., Exhs. 24 and 27.) Cross-Complainants did not perform by July 22, 2022, nor did they perform by the later dates promised by Cross-Complainant Vera. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 71-78; Pafundi Decl., ¶¶56-60 and Exh. 24.) On August 3, 2022, Pafundi sent Cross-Complainants a Notice of Termination of Contract. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 79; Exh. 25.) Cross-Defendant filed this action two-and-a-half weeks later. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 82 and Exh. 3.)

 

This evidence carries Cross-Defendant’s initial burden of proof as to Issues 1 and 2. Specifically, it shows that the parties only negotiated one contract with respect to the Website and there was no separate agreement regarding the “coming soon” or landing page website for which an additional charge was agreed. It also shows that Cross-Complainants breached the agreement by failing to rebuild and redesign the Website as agreed.

 

            Issue 3: Cross-Defendant’s performance

 

The parties’ agreement created rights of the Cross-Defendant and obligations, duties, and responsibilities by and of Cross-Complainants. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 9; Pafundi Decl., ¶6.) Cross-Defendant provided Cross-Complainants with all the information they requested so the Website could be designed, developed, and launched within 30 days. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 29; Pafundi Decl., ¶16 and Exh. 8-20.) This included Pafundi’s GoDaddy Admin Details and credentials, verbal answers to Cross-Complainant Vera’s questions, outlining what the Website needed to contain, the physical content requested to build the Website, the Google Drive folder labeled “Pafundi Law Website 2022,” and a Dropbox folder where Cross-Defendant stored images. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 30-32; Pafundi Decl., ¶¶17-18 and Exhs. 6, 12-14 and 19.) This information and content was (1) Cross-Defendant’s professional logos; (2) recently acquired professional photographs of Robert Pafundi; (3) photographs taken over time while on TV and film sets or at entertainment industry events with clients; and (4) old website content which included copy for all of the key subjects and topics that would be needed on the new website. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 33; Pafundi Decl., ¶19.) Pafundi also connected Cross-Complainant Vera with Laura Anglada Gómez (Marketing/Logo Designer), Grace Wong (Internet Marketer/Video Production), and Mary Simmons (Social Media Manager) for the specific purpose of coordinating the website design and buildout effort. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 34; Pafundi Decl., 20.)

 

This evidence carries Cross-Defendant’s initial burden of proof with respect to Issue 3 by demonstrating that it provided Cross-Complainants with the promised content.

 

Cross-Complainants’ Burden to Demonstrate the Existence of a Triable Issue of Material Fact

 

The burden now shifts to Cross-Complainants to demonstrate the existence of triable issues of material fact regarding Issues 1, 2, and 3.

 

            Issue 1: the existence of a second agreement

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants dispute that the parties’ agreement required them to “do all things necessary and related to the [Website].” (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact No. 6; Vera Decl., ¶¶2-3.) They contend that the parties separately agreed Cross-Complainants would rebuild and redesign the Website in exchange for $5,000.00 and would build a coming soon/landing page for $1,250.00. (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact Nos. 23-24, 45; Vera Decl., ¶¶2, 9.) This is also demonstrated by the invoice: INV-836 was issued in the amount of $5,000.00 solely with respect to the Website with no mention of the coming soon/landing page. (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact No. 24; Vera Decl., ¶2.) This demonstrates the existence of a triable issue of material fact regarding a separate agreement for the coming soon/landing page.

 

            Issues 2 and 3: Cross-Complainants’ performance or excuse for non-performance; Cross-Defendant’s performance

 

Cross-Complainants argue that triable issues of material fact exist regarding its non-performance under the agreements, which is excused by Cross-Defendant’s failure to perform its own obligations. (Opp., pp. 6:26-7:1.) Cross-Complainants dispute the Motion’s contention that the agreement only created obligations for Cross-Complainants and rights for Cross-Defendant by arguing that Cross-Defendant also had obligations to provide them with the necessary content and to review the content. (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact No. 9; Vera Decl., ¶4 and Pafundi Decl., Exh. 16.) The opposition evidence, however, does not demonstrate that Cross-Defendant had any obligations under the terms of the agreement. Vera’s declaration states that the first step in drafting the content for the site was for Pafundi to review a Google doc and make edits but does not state that this was discussed and agreed upon by the parties. (Opp., Vera Decl., ¶4.) Exhibit 16 also does not demonstrate to what the parties agreed. It only shows Pafundi and Cross-Complainant discussing what content would be included. (Motion, Pafundi Decl., Exh. 16 at Bates Nos. PLAINTIFF00110 and PLAINTIFF00111.)

 

Cross-Complainants also dispute promising to design and create the Website within 30 days; instead, they argue that Cross-Complainant Vera informed Pafundi that the average time to build a website is 30-45 days, depending on how quickly content for the site could be provided. (Opp., Separate Statement, Fact No. 10; Vera Decl., ¶¶3-5.) Again, Cross-Complainant’s evidence does not support this purported fact because it does not demonstrate that this information regarding the deadline to produce the Website was ever communicated to Cross-Defendant. Instead, Vera declares “if final draft content, including text, photographs, and/or videos, is provided to me without delay, then my estimated turnaround time to build a website is 30-40 days.” (Opp., Vera Decl., ¶5.) Therefore, Cross-Complainants have not demonstrated a dispute regarding the 30-day deadline to complete the Website under the agreement. Accordingly, the Court finds that no triable issues of material fact have been shown with respect to Issues 2 and 3.

 

However, the dispute regarding the existence of a separate agreement for payment for the coming soon/landing page precludes a grant of summary judgment.

 

Conclusion

 

Cross-Defendant Pafundi Law Firm’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.
