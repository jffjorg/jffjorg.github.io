DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV07201    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

July 8, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV07201

MOTION

Demurrers to Third Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendants TGB Promotions, Inc. and Tom Brown

OPPOSING PARTIES

Plaintiff Mario Cazares

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Mario Cazares (“Plaintiff”) alleges he, a professional boxer, retained Defendants TGB Promotions, Inc (“Defendant”) and Tom Brown (“Brown”).  Plaintiff contends Defendant violated conflict of interest laws.  Defendant demurs to the second, fourth, and sixth causes of action in Plaintiff’s third amended complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the demurrer.  Brown also demurred to Plaintiff’s third amended complaint, but after Brown filed the demurrer, Plaintiff dismissed his claims against Brown, and the dismissal was entered without prejudice on May 14, 2024.  As such, Brown’s demurrer is moot. 

ANALYSIS

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[] [the allegations [in the complaint], with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”   (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Plaintiff’s second cause of action is for violation of the Professional Boxing Safety Act “the Act”).  Under the Act, a boxing promoter cannot receive compensation in connection with a boxing match until the promoter discloses to the boxer the amounts of any compensation the promoter will receive, any fees, charges, and expenses that the promoter will assess on the boxer in connection with the event, and any reduction in the boxer’s purse.  (15 U.S.C. § 6307e, subd. (b).)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant “failed to disclose any of the information required under” the Act (Third Amended Complaint, ¶ 84), and “Plaintiff has sustained monetary damages in an amount of $5,000,000 . . . ,” which Plaintiff estimates based on viewership of the match, the sponsors of the match, and the attendance at the match (Id., ¶ 86.)  These allegations suffice for pleading purposes to state a claim against Defendant.  Defendant may obtain more information in discovery.

Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is under Business and Professions Code section 17200 Plaintiff must allege a claim under section 17200 “with reasonable particularity . . . .”  (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)  Plaintiff’s allegations are essentially that Defendant induced Plaintiff to contract with Defendant on unfavorable terms and without the intention to perform.  (See Third Amended Complaint, ¶ 105.)  These allegations suffice to state a claim under section 17200.  (See Wilner v. Sunset Life Ins. Co. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 952, 965.)

Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action is for negligence.  Plaintiff’s allegations are simply that Defendant breached the agreement between Defendant and Plaintiff.  (See Third Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 123-126.)  These allegations do not support a cause of action for negligence.  (Stop Loss Ins. Brokers, Inc. v. Brown & Toland Medical Group (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1041.)  The Court has already provided Plaintiff an opportunity to state a cause of action for negligence, and Plaintiff has failed to do so.  As such, the Court considers leave to amend futile and, therefore, unwarranted.  (See Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 168.) 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action for negligence in Plaintiff’s third amended complaint is sustained without leave to amend.  The demurrer is otherwise overruled.  Defendant is to answer the third amended complaint within 10 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service concerning it.

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

July 8, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV11070

MOTIONS

Demurrer to Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Casa Lu-An Motel and Vanmalibhai Patel

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Victoria Manriquez and Marissa Galaviz

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Victoria Manriquez and Marissa Galaviz (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Casa Lu-An Motel and Vanmalibhai Patel (“Defendants”) because Plaintiffs contend a motel room Plaintiffs rented from Defendants had a bedbug infestation.  Defendants demur to the first cause of action for battery, the third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, and the seventh cause of action for fraudulent concealment in Plaintiffs’ complaint.  Defendants also move to strike the punitive damages allegations from the complaint.  Plaintiffs oppose the motions. 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is for battery.  To state a claim for battery, Plaintiffs must allege Defendants intentionally acted in a way that resulted in harmful or offensive contact with Plaintiffs’ bodies; Plaintiffs did not consent to the contact; and the contact harmed Plaintiffs.  (Brown v. Ransweiler (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 516, 526.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants’ intentional acts include: “(1) deliberate actions in failing to properly and thoroughly inspect and clean the room assigned to Plaintiffs, and to ensure the room was free from infestations of bedbugs, and the like, prior to renting and assigning the room to Plaintiffs; (2) intentional and deliberate failures to treat and eliminate infestation of bedbugs, and the like, occurring in the rooms of the Subject Hotel; (3) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice not to inspect for bedbugs and the spread of bedbug infestations at the Subject Hotel; (4) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice in requiring Plaintiffs to be assigned and isolated in the room of the Subject Hotel despite having prior knowledge of the bedbug infestation from the Plaintiffs; (5) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice not to properly train housekeeping staff to thoroughly clean and maintain the rooms in a sanitary and habitable condition; (6) willful disregard of knowledge of Cimex lectularius (bedbugs) infestation and other uninhabitable conditions in Plaintiffs’ room; and  (7) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice in failing to give notice to Plaintiffs of the presence of the said uninhabitable conditions occurring in Plaintiffs’ room at the Subject Hotel.”  (Complaint, ¶ 53.)  These allegations are not of intentional actions Defendants took that injured Plaintiffs.  Instead, these allegations are that Defendants failed to maintain their hotel in an appropriate condition.  The Court sustains the demurrer to the first cause of action with leave to amend.

Defendants demur to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, or IIED.  “The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.  Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.  The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.”  (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants knew the hotel room Plaintiffs rented from Defendants had a bedbug infestation, but failed to remedy the infestation before Plaintiffs occupied the room.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 83, 85.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice.  Plaintiffs allege Defendants acted with reckless disregard for the likelihood of causing Plaintiffs severe emotional distress due to the bedbug infestation.  The court overrules the demurrer to the third cause of action.

Plaintiffs’ seventh cause of action is for fraud.  Plaintiff must allege fraud with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.)  To state a case of action for fraudulent concealment, Plaintiffs must allege Defendants concealed or suppressed a material fact that Defendants were under a duty to disclose to Plaintiffs with the intent to defraud Plaintiffs; Plaintiffs were unaware of the fact and would not have acted in the same way if Plaintiffs knew of the concealed fact; and the concealment caused damages to Plaintiffs.  (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 868.)  

Plaintiffs allege, “As evidenced by prior similar incidents and reviews posted on various online platforms by prior guests and invitees, Defendants . . . were aware of the presence of the infestation of bedbugs, and the like, to be present in the rooms at their Subject Hotel.”  (Complaint, ¶ 143.)  Plaintiffs further allege, “Defendants’ . . . failures to implement policies and procedures, training, and proper cleaning processes of Plaintiffs’ room—to prevent an incident such as this and the spread of insects, bed bugs, and the like—amounts to suppression and concealment of existing unsanitary conditions in the rooms at the hotel.”  (Id., ¶ 144.)  However, Plaintiff does not allege specific facts to support these conclusory allegations.  Plaintiffs must amend.

To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (Coll. Hosp.).)  In that vein, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (Coll. Hosp., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p.725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants acted with “willful and conscious disregard of Plaintiffs’ right to be free from such tortious behavior, such as to constitute oppression, fraud or malice pursuant to California Civil Code Section 3294 . . . .”  (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶  79.)  However, Plaintiffs do not allege specific facts to support these conclusory allegations.  Accordingly, Plaintiffs have not alleged facts to support the claim for punitive damages.

Defendants also move to strike the prayer for attorney fees.  Plaintiffs do not allege a basis for an award of attorney fees, nor do Plaintiffs oppose the motion to strike the prayer for attorney fees.  Accordingly, the Court grants the motion to strike the prayer for attorney fees without leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ demurrer to the first and seventh causes of action in Plaintiffs’ complaint is sustained with leave to amend.  Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations is granted with leave to amend.  Defendants’ motion to strike the prayer for attorneys’ fees is granted without leave to amend.

Plaintiffs are to file an amended complaint within 10 days of notice of this order. 

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV19761    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 39

DEPT:

HEARING DATE:

July 8, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV19761

MOTION: 

Motion for Protective Order

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Ariel Alfaro

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendant Man Staffing LLC

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Ariel Alfaro (“Plaintiff”) moves for a protective order regarding Special Interrogatories, set one (“SROG”), which Defendant Man Staffing LLC (“Defendant”) propounded on Plaintiff.  Defendant opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Plaintiff fails to specify the nature of the protective order she seeks in her notice of motion.  Instead, Plaintiff simply states she seeks a protective order “concerning” the SROG.  (See Notice of Motion for Protective Order.)  The moving party must state in the first paragraph of the notice of motion exactly what relief the party seeks, and on what grounds.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110, subd. (a).)  As authority for the motion, Plaintiff cites Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020, which permits the Court to limit discovery on a protective order if the Court determines that “the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).)   Plaintiff also cites Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.090, which sets forth various types of protective orders a responding party may obtain.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.090, subd. (a).)  These citations do not provide sufficient notice of the specific protective order Plaintiff seeks.  As such, the motion is denied.

Further, to the extent the court construes Plaintiff’s motion to request the Court strike Defendant’s SROG in its entirety, the motion is without merit.  The court cannot strike an entire set of interrogatories if any question in the set is proper.  (West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.)  The SROG are not all improper, and they are tailored to Plaintiff’s allegations.  (Declaration of Pouya B. Chami, Exhibit B.) “[A] defendant in California courts may be required through discovery to disclose not only the evidentiary facts underlying his affirmative defenses and denials but also whether or not he makes a particular contention, either as to the facts or as to the possible issues in the case.  A plaintiff, of course, is subject to analogous requirements.”  (Burke v. Superior Court of Sacramento County (1969) 71 Cal.2d 276, 281-282, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

To the extent Plaintiff challenges the necessity of Defendant’s SROG, the court finds the  SROG are justified.  To propound more than 35 special interrogatories, a party must attach a declaration from the party’s counsel to explain why more interrogatories are necessary.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.040, subd. (a), 2030.050.)  Defendant did that in this instance.  A party may use a motion for protective order to challenge another party’s assertion more than 35 interrogatories are warranted in the action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.090, subd. (b)(2).)  On such a motion, the burden is on the propounding party to prove the number of questions is justified.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.040. subd. (b).)

Plaintiff asserts ten causes of action against Defendant in a 15-page complaint.  Each SROG Defendant propounded on Plaintiff asks Plaintiff to state all facts in support of an allegation in Plaintiff’s complaint, to identify all witnesses in support of an allegation in Plaintiff’s complaint, or to identify all documents in support of an allegation in Plaintiff’s complaint.  Thus, these narrowly-tailored requests seek the evidence supporting Plaintiff’s complaint and are not excessive.

In reply, Plaintiff cites this court’s order in an unrelated matter: Caputo v. Costco Wholesale Corporation (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2019, No. 19STCV24527) (Caputo).  Such an order is irrelevant with respect to this case.  In any event, the court notes the motion for protective order in Caputo was procedurally proper, and it concerned a second set of 196 special interrogatories, which the propounding party served after having served an initial set of 177 special interrogatories, to which the responding party had already responded.  The motion for protective order in Caputo was sought, therefore, under vastly different circumstances in that Plaintiff in this action apparently seeks to avoid responding to any SROG from Defendant.

The parties’ individual requests for sanctions against each other are denied.

Defendant is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION: 

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel

MOVING PARTY:

Attorney Michael R. Parker of M.R. Law, PC

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

MOTION

            Attorney Michael R. Parker of M.R. Law, PC (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff Alireza Kehanpour (“Plaintiff”).

 ANALYSIS

Counsel has filed forms MC-051 and MC-052 and has lodged with the Court a copy of the proposed order on form MC-053 as required.  (Cal Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.)  The basis for the motion is a breakdown in the relationship between Plaintiff and Counsel.  This is a valid reason for withdrawal.  Accordingly, the motion is granted.  Counsel should note the order will become effective upon the filing of proof of service on Plaintiff of a signed copy of the order.  Counsel will remain the attorney of record until Counsel files with the Court proof of service of the signed order.  Counsel is to serve a copy of the signed order (form MC-053) on Plaintiff within 10 days and file proof of service thereof.

Counsel is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
