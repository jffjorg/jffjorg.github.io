DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV11207    Hearing Date: November 19, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Adam Dolewski filed this action against Defendants, Dollar Tree Stores, Inc., Berkshire Bakersfield, LLC, and Brentwood Group, LLC for premises liability and negligence arising out of an incident that occurred at a Dollar Tree location in Long Beach.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ negligence caused him to be confined in a freezer for a substantial period of time, which caused him injuries and damages. 

 

2.     Motion to Bifurcate

a.     Parties’ Positions

Dollar Tree moves to bifurcate the issue of liability from the issue of damages at trial.  It contends the liability phase of the trial will take only days, while the damages phase, if necessary, will take weeks.  It contends the damages phase will cause it prejudice if heard concurrently with the liability phase.  Additionally, it contends settlement may be possible at the conclusion of the liability phase if the jury finds liability on the part of Defendant. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  He argues the motion is a restatement of a prior motion that was denied and there is nothing new or different in the motion.  He argues there is no reasonable probability that Defendant will achieve a full defense verdict on the liability phase of the trial.  He contends duplication of efforts will be necessary if the case is bifurcated, as numerous witnesses will need to be called during both phases of the trial.  He contends exceptional circumstances do not exist that would justify bifurcation.  He also notes that bifurcation orders are inherently unfair to the plaintiff, as the jury knows it will be dismissed and go home after the first phase of the trial if there is a defense verdict, but it will have to stay for an extended period of time in the event it finds liability. 

 

b.     Law Governing Bifurcation

Bifurcation is the procedure whereby the court may order separate trials of issues of parties joined in a single action.  The objective of bifurcation is to avoid wasting time and money on the trial of damages issues if the liability issue is resolved against the plaintiff.  Also, the procedure is not limited to separate trials of liability and damages; nor is it limited to dividing a case into only two parts.  A party seeking bifurcation should request such relief as soon as the need becomes apparent.  Delay may be a factor affecting the court’s exercise of discretion.  Generally, the court has the inherent power to regulate the order of trial and, therefore, can entertain a motion to bifurcate at any time, even during the trial.  However, where bifurcation is sought pursuant to CCP §598 (e.g., to obtain bifurcation of liability issue), the order must be made no later than 30 days before trial.  (I.e., the motion must be heard and order made more than 30 days before trial.)

 

Pursuant to CCP §1048(b), “The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of the state or of the United States.”

 

c.     Analysis

The Court, on 2/14/23, denied a similar motion to bifurcate without prejudice.  The Court did so largely because the decision as to whether or not to bifurcate was premature, and the Court wished to rule on the motion closer to trial. 

 

As the Court noted in its prior order, motions to bifurcate always involve a difficult balancing between prejudice to the defendant if the trial is not bifurcated and the plaintiff’s injuries are very severe, prejudice to the plaintiff if the trial is bifurcated and the jury knows it can go home in the event of a defense liability verdict, and overall convenience to witnesses and the court. 

 

In this case, after hearing Defendant’s summary judgment motion and learning all the details of the case, the Court finds the potential prejudice to Defendant if the damages are introduced during the liability phase outweighs the potential prejudice to Plaintiff if the jury knows it can go home by rendering a defense verdict.  Additionally, the Court finds that, while some witnesses may need to be called twice, the liability phase of the trial will be very short and the damages phase will be very long; there will be no need to conduct the damages phase of the trial if Defendant achieves a defense verdict after the liability phase. 

 

On balance, the motion is granted.  Counsel should be prepared to proceed immediately to the damages phase of the trial if Plaintiff is successful in connection with the liability phase of the trial.

Case Number: 22LBCV00884    Hearing Date: November 19, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Debra Thompson Crowe filed this action against Defendants, Walmart, Inc., Wal-Mart Associates, Inc., Sam’s West, Inc., and Maria Varela for age discrimination, disability discrimination, failure to engage in the interactive process, failure to provide reasonable accommodation, harassment, retaliation, failure to prevent harassment/discrimination/retaliation, and wrongful termination.  Plaintiff’s original complaint, filed on 11/30/22, is her operative complaint. 

 

2.     Motion to Augment Expert Designation

  1. Procedural History

Defendants served a demand for exchange of expert designations on 6/03/24, setting the exchange date for 6/24/24.  Plaintiff served a demand for exchange of expert designations on 6/04/24, also setting the exchange date for 6/24/24.  Defendants served their designation on 6/24/24; Plaintiff served her designation on 6/26/24. 

 

On 8/23/24, Dr. Hunter Vincent issued a QME Report regarding Plaintiff.  The report was issued in connection with Plaintiff’s workers’ compensation claim.  Plaintiff, after reviewing the report, determined several doctors mentioned in the report are both percipient witnesses and also potential expert witnesses.  Plaintiff therefore amended her expert designation to list Drs. Culver, Chuang, Nick, Vincent, and Woods.  The parties met and conferred, and Defendants did not agree to the amended designation.   

 

  1. Law Governing Amendment of Expert Designation

Motions to augment or amend a party’s expert designation are governed by CCP §2034.620, which provides:

The court shall grant leave to augment or amend an expert witness list or declaration only if all of the following conditions are satisfied:

(a) The court has taken into account the extent to which the opposing party has relied on the list of expert witnesses.

(b) The court has determined that any party opposing the motion will not be prejudiced in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.

(c) The court has determined either of the following:

(1) The moving party would not in the exercise of reasonable diligence have determined to call that expert witness or have decided to offer the different or additional testimony of that expert witness.

(2) The moving party failed to determine to call that expert witness, or to offer the different or additional testimony of that expert witness as a result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, and the moving party has done both of the following:

(A) Sought leave to augment or amend promptly after deciding to call the expert witness or to offer the different or additional testimony.

(B) Promptly thereafter served a copy of the proposed expert witness information concerning the expert or the testimony described in Section 2034.260 on all other parties who have appeared in the action.

(d) Leave to augment or amend is conditioned on the moving party making the expert available immediately for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410), and on any other terms as may be just, including, but not limited to, leave to any party opposing the motion to designate additional expert witnesses or to elicit additional opinions from those previously designated, a continuance of the trial for a reasonable period of time, and the awarding of costs and litigation expenses to any party opposing the motion.

 

  1. Parties’ Arguments

Plaintiff contends she did not realize she needed to include the proposed experts until after she received the subject report, which is a sufficient showing of reasonable diligence and/or inadvertence.  She contends Defendants will not be prejudiced by adding the new experts, and she is not obligated to make them available for deposition because they are percipient witnesses. 

 

Defendants, in opposition to the motion, argue Plaintiff cannot amend her designation because she did not timely submit her designation in the first place.  They contend Plaintiff failed to make a timely motion before the discovery cut-off.  They contend Plaintiff cannot transform fact witnesses into expert witnesses without properly designating them.  They contend they will be prejudiced because they do not have time to explore the expert opinions of these witnesses prior to the trial date.

 

Plaintiff, in reply, argues she has standing to bring the motion because her original expert designation was timely.  She contends any argument re: the discovery cut-off is moot because the Court granted additional time to depose experts.  She contends there will be no prejudice because the parties have until 11/19/24 to conduct expert depositions.  She contends Defendants concede the subject witnesses are percipient witnesses, and the motion is only brought to ensure there is no problem in the event they introduce expert testimony. 

 

  1. Standing

A threshold issue is whether Plaintiff has standing to bring this motion.  Defendants contend she does not because she did not timely designate experts.  CCP §2034.610 provides, in pertinent part:

(a) On motion of any party who has engaged in a timely exchange of expert witness information, the court may grant leave to do either or both of the following:

(1) Augment that party's expert witness list and declaration by adding the name and address of any expert witness whom that party has subsequently retained.

 

As noted above, both parties served expert designation demands, and both parties scheduled the exchange for 6/24/24.  Defendants served their designation on 6/24/24, but Plaintiff served hers on 6/26/24.  Plaintiff argues, in her reply papers, that her designation was timely because Defendants served their demand by email, which adds two days to the time in which she was required to respond. 

 

Plaintiff would be correct if only Defendants had served a demand.  CCP §2034.230(b) makes clear that the time to respond to the demand is extended by two days if the demand is served by email, Plaintiff ignores the fact that she also served a demand, and she also scheduled the exchange for 6/24/24.  Notably, §2034.210 makes clear that, to prevent either party from taking advantage, the exchange must be “simultaneous.” 

Plaintiff failed to comply with her own exchange deadline, and therefore she is barred from seeking relief. 

 

  1. Additional Analysis

The Court also notes that Plaintiff failed to show she would not have reasonably listed these proposed experts in her initial designation, or that any failure to do so was the result of excusable neglect.  These proposed experts are her own treating physicians.  Plaintiff does not explain exactly what was in the subject report that she did not anticipate or that caused her, for the first time, to realize these percipient witnesses may also need to offer expert testimony. 

 

Notably, Plaintiff argues, in her moving papers, that she is not obligated to comply with CCP §2034.620(d), which requires her to make the experts immediately available for deposition, because the experts are percipient witnesses.  Plaintiff’s argument is confusing.  The point of this motion is to designate the percipient witnesses as expert witnesses, and if Plaintiff wishes to designate them as expert witnesses, then she must comply with the statutory requirements for doing so.

 

Finally, the Court finds Defendants will be prejudiced if Plaintiff is permitted to transform five of her percipient witnesses into expert witnesses.  The Court recently continued the trial date, but the continuance order required the parties to conduct all expert witness depositions by 11/19/24, the same date on which this motion is scheduled to be heard.  Trial is scheduled for 12/02/24, and Defendants cannot reasonably be expected to add five additional expert witness depositions to their schedule with sufficient time to prepare for trial. 

 

  1. Conclusion

Plaintiff failed to timely serve her initial designation and therefore lacks standing to bring this motion.  Additionally, she failed to meet the statutory requirements for the relief she seeks.  The motion to amend/augment is denied.

Plaintiff also complains of Defendant “switching attorneys.”  The City Attorney’s office has numerous attorneys in its litigation unit, and it is common for more than one attorney to work on a case.  There is nothing improper about having multiple attorneys assigned to the case.   

 

  1. Analysis

Defendant adequately shows, in the moving papers, that each of its grounds for demurrer is well-taken.  Defendant correctly notes that Plaintiff has not alleged he suffered damages as a result of any of Defendant’s actions, as required by Limon v. Circle K Stores, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 696-697.  Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, contends he will amend his complaint to allege a personal injury cause of action.  Plaintiff fails, however, to show how Defendant can be liable for his heart attack and/or strokes, which are the injuries mentioned in the complaint. 

 

Defendant also adequately shows that Plaintiff failed to plead exhaustion of administrative remedies and compliance with the Government Tort Claims Act.  Plaintiff concedes this failure in opposition to the demurrer, and indicates he can amend his complaint to allege exhaustion of administrative remedies and compliance with the Act. 

 

Defendant establishes various immunities apply and bar it and its employees from being subject to suit.  Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, indicates he will drop any claim against the employee defendants.  Plaintiff fails, however, to address the immunities that inure to the benefit of the City itself.

 

Finally, Defendant establishes no claim for selective enforcement can be made absent a showing that the plaintiff is a member of a protected class.  See Wayte v. United States (1985) 470 U.S. 598, 608.  Plaintiff does not address this argument in opposition to the demurrer. 

 

  1. Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained on all stated grounds.  Because this is the first pleading challenge, and because leave to amend is liberally granted, the Court grants leave to amend with respect to all issues except those expressly conceded in opposition to the demurrer.  If Plaintiff amends his complaint, and if there is a further pleading challenge, Plaintiff must meaningfully articulate a legal argument in opposition to each and every stated ground for demurrer in his opposition to the demurrer. 

 

4.     Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike the co-plaintiff Trust’s complaint because a trust cannot be represented by a non-attorney.  Plaintiff concedes the issue in opposition to the motion.  When Plaintiff amends his complaint, he must not include the Trust as a co-plaintiff unless he retains an attorney. 

 

5.     Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained without leave to amend as to the individual defendants, because Plaintiff concedes they have a complete defense to the claims pled against them.  The City’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff must amend within twenty days.  Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.  The Court strongly encourages Plaintiff to retain an attorney if he wishes to maintain his claims successfully. 

 

Defendant’s motion to strike all claims pled by the Trust is granted without leave to amend, as the issue is conceded by Plaintiff in opposition to the motion. 

 

6.     Order to Show Cause

There is an OSC on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the demurrer and motion to strike today.  The Court asks the parties to make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on the demurrer, motion to strike, and OSC.
