DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20GDCV00304    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT WITHOUT LEACVE TO AMEND AND DENYING MOTION TO STRIKE AS MOOT

I.         INTRODUCTION

This action arises from Plaintiff Anahid Cholakian ("Plaintiff") sustaining water damage to her home located at 250 West Fairview Avenue, Apartment 302, Glendale, California 91202. On March 6, 2020, Plaintiff filed her initial complaint, alleging negligence and related causes of action against several parties. On March 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint ("FAC"), adding defendants.

On August 2, 2024, the Court held a hearing on Defendant 360 Remodeling, Inc. and Defendant Patrick Havani's (collectively “Defendants”) demurrer to the FAC, which was sustained with leave to amend. Following the Court’s order, Plaintiff filed her Second Amended Complaint ("SAC") on August 22, 2024, stating claims of negligence, negligent hiring, and negligent entrustment against Defendants.

On September 9, 2024, Defendants filed a Demurrer with a Motion to Strike directed at the SAC, which is now before the Court¿. 

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, Defense counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter via email on August 30, 2024. On September 6, 2024, at 11:00 AM, the parties met and conferred in good faith. Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet-and-confer requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41.

MOVING PARTY’S ARGUMENT

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s entire SAC. They argue that all claims against them in the SAC are time-barred under Code of Civil Procedure section 338(b), as more than three years have passed since the alleged water damage occurred on June 21, 2019.They assert that Plaintiff failed to exercise reasonable diligence in discovering their involvement and cannot rely on the delayed discovery rule. Additionally, Defendants argue that the SAC does not sufficiently plead facts showing the time and manner of discovery or Plaintiff’s inability to discover their identities earlier.

OPPOSITION

            No Opposition has been filed as of 10/11/24.

Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendants request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.)

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

An action for injury to real property is subject to a three-year statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 338(b). In tort cases, the statute of limitations generally begins to run upon the occurrence of the last essential event, even if the plaintiff is unaware of the cause of action. (Wilshire Westwood Associates v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 732, 739). In the case of injury to property, the injury is considered to be to the property itself, not the property owner (Ibid.)

The discovery rule postpones the accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 807). Under the rule, a plaintiff has reason to discover a cause of action when they have reason to suspect a factual basis for its elements. Suspicion of wrongdoing, coupled with knowledge of any remaining elements, triggers the statute of limitations period (Ibid.). The discovery rule only delays accrual until the plaintiff has, or should have, inquiry notice of the cause of action (Ibid.).

However, Plaintiff must specifically plead facts showing (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence. The burden is on the plaintiff to show diligence, and conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc., supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 808).

 

III.      ANALYSIS

In the SAC, Plaintiff alleges that the delay in naming Defendants was due to several factors, including being unaware of 360 Remodeling, Inc.'s involvement until June 6, 2023, when Fairview Gardens Community Association named Defendant 360 as a cross-defendant (SAC ¶ 51). Plaintiff further claims that the defaults entered against Legend and Fairview halted the need for further discovery, as Plaintiff was pursuing default judgments against those parties (SAC ¶ 52). Upon learning about Defendant 360’s involvement, Plaintiff asserts that they acted diligently by amending the complaint on September 7, 2023, to include Defendant 360 as a defendant. Plaintiff cites these factors—default judgments, the lack of discovery during the interim period, and their late discovery of 360 Remodeling’s role—as reasons for not knowing about Defendants' potential liability sooner.

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s claims are time barred under Code of Civil Procedure section 338(b), which imposes a three-year statute of limitations for property damage claims. Defendants assert that Plaintiff failed to exercise reasonable diligence in discovering their involvement within the statutory period, pointing to significant gaps in Plaintiff's litigation activity. Specifically, Defendants highlight the period after Plaintiff settled with Farmers Insurance in September 2021, during which Plaintiff did not serve any discovery requests until September 21, 2023, despite the availability of discovery tools to inquire about other their identities as potential Defendants. (Demurrer p. 3; SAC ¶¶ 22-26) Defendants argue this delay does not meet the standard of reasonable diligence required. 

Defendants also point out that Plaintiff’s SAC states that she initially sued the HOA, Legend Construction and Farmers Insurance in March 2020 and settled with Farmers Insurance in September 2021. However, the SAC does not contain any allegations regarding Plaintiff’s diligence during the 18-month period following the settlement with Farmers Insurance. During this period, Defendants argue Plaintiff took no steps to discover potentially liable parties.  Defendants contend that this significant delay cannot reasonably be considered diligent under the law. (Gholian Decl. ¶ 2; Exh. A; Demurrer p. 3). Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s claims are barred, as Plaintiff has failed to meet the burden required to apply the delayed discovery rule.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not met the burden of pleading the delayed discovery rule with sufficient particularity. To rely on the delayed discovery rule, Plaintiff must specifically allege (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence. The SAC lacks detailed allegations regarding Plaintiff’s efforts to discover 360 Remodeling’s involvement prior to June 2023. The prolonged inactivity in pursuing discovery demonstrates a failure to act diligently. For instance, the SAC does not contain any allegations regarding Plaintiff’s diligence during the 18-month period following the settlement with Farmers Insurance in September 2021. Additionally, Plaintiff has not demonstrated or sufficiently pleaded reasonable diligence in the gap between the HOA’s answer in December 2022 and the filing of the cross-complaint naming 360 Remodeling in June 2023. Given these deficiencies, the Court concludes that the statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 338(b) bars Plaintiff's claims. 

On August 2, 2024, this Court sustained Defendant 360’s previous demurrer with the following language:

 

“Given that Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts in the FAC to invoke the delayed discovery rule, the Court finds that the FAC is barred on its face as to Defendant 360.

Due to the Court’s analysis as to the statute of limitations issue above, such analysis is dispositive of the demurrer before the                 Court. The Court does not find that there is no reasonable possibility of successful amendment. The Court therefore SUSTAINS               the demurrer of Defendant 360 to the first, second, and third causes of action in the FAC with leave to amend.” 

              In light of the foregoing, Defendants’ current demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is sustained WITHOUT leave to amend. 

Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.) 

Defendants moves to strike the allegations of the SAC on the grounds that the allegations asserted against it are barred by the statute of limitations. Given that the Court has sustained the demurrer to the SAC without leave to amend, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as MOOT.

 

IV.       ORDER

            The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the SAC without leave to amend.

            Defendants motion to strike is denied as moot.

            Defendants are to give notice.

Case Number: 22AHCV00268    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING CROSS-DEFENDANT CITIBANK’S MOTION FOR STAY

I.        INTRODUCTION

           Cross-Defendant Citibank, N.A. ("Citibank") moves for an order staying the proceedings on Cross-Complainant Edith Enterprise, LTD’s ("Edith") claims alleged in the Third Amended Cross-Complaint (“TACC”). In the TACC, Edith brings claims against Citibank for alleged wrongful conduct, asserting causes of action for: (1) breach of contract, (2) conversion, (3) negligence, (4) fraudulent transfer, (5) violations of California’s Unfair Practices Act, (6) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (7) financial elder abuse, (8) violations of the Electronic Fund Transfers Act and (9) Common Count.  Based on the Court’s ruling on September 18, 2024, only the 1st, 6th and 9th causes of action remain. 

Citibank seeks to stay the proceedings on the grounds that Edith, a Nevada limited liability company, transacted intrastate business in California without obtaining the required certificate of registration from the California Secretary of State, as mandated by Corporations Code section 17708.07(a). Citibank argues that under California law, Edith cannot maintain its cross-claims until it complies with the registration requirements. Accordingly, Citibank requests that the Court stay the proceedings until Edith obtains the required certificate and set a date by which the stay may be lifted, or alternatively, dismiss the TACC if Edith fails to comply.

 

Opposition

Edith filed its opposition on September 9, 2024. 

         

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of¿ justice and to promote judicial efficiency.” Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo(1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489. Trial judges have inherent powers to manage and fashion procedures to control litigation to insure the orderly administration of justice. Cottle v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1376-79; see also CCP §§ 128(a)(3) and (a)(5).

Corporations Code Section 17708.07 subdivision (a)-(b) provides: 

(a) A foreign limited liability company transacting intrastate business in this state shall not maintain an action or proceeding in this state unless it has a certificate of registration to transact intrastate business in this state. 

(b) The failure of a foreign limited liability company to have a certificate of registration to transact intrastate business in this state does not prevent the foreign limited liability company from defending an action or proceeding in this state. 

 

III.     ANALYSIS

Citibank argues that this matter should be stayed because Edith is a Nevada limited liability company (“LLC”) that transacted intrastate business in California without obtaining the required certificate of registration from the California Secretary of State, as mandated by Corporations Code sections 17708.02 and 17708.07(a). Under Corporations Code section 17708.07(a), a foreign LLC that transacts intrastate business in California may "file" but not "maintain" an action in the state until it obtains a certificate of registration. (Motion p. 3.) Citibank argues that because Edith has not obtained this certificate, it should not be permitted to pursue its cross-claims in the TACC. 

Citibank argues that it was unaware of the extent of Edith’s business dealings in California until the deposition of Edith’s Person Most Knowledgeable, Helen Young, on August 13, 2024, which revealed the scope of Edith’s intrastate business operations. (Motion p. 7.) Citibank alleges that Edith’s claims arise directly from its intrastate business activities, specifically an alleged $31,396 debit from Edith’s Citibank account and the closure of that account, which was tied to Edith’s sales of auto brake discs in California. Edith maintained a Citibank business account ending in 8207, into which payments for its California sales were deposited, and used this account to repay a business line of credit. Edith operated out of a home in Monterey Park, California, where it imported and sold auto brake discs to California wholesalers. (Motion p. 6.)

Citibank also contends it was unaware of Edith’s significant business dealings in California until the deposition of Edith’s Person Most Knowledgeable on August 13, 2024, which revealed the extent of Edith’s intrastate business operations. (Motion p. 7) Citibank also alleges that Edith’s claims arise directly from its intrastate business activities in California. Specifically, Citibank points to the alleged $31,396 debit from Edith's Citibank account and the closure of that account, which was linked to Edith’s sales of auto brake discs in California. Edith maintained a Citibank business account ending in 8207, into which payments for its California sales were deposited, and used this account to repay a business line of credit, further evidencing its intrastate activities and Edith operated out of a home in Monterey Park, California, where it imported and sold auto brake discs to California wholesalers (Motion p. 6).

Corporations Code section 17708.03(b) provides an exception to the registration requirement for foreign LLCs if they are involved in maintaining or defending an action, securing, or collecting debts or transacting business in interstate commerce. However, this carve out applies in cases where the foreign LLC’s purpose is collecting debt or enforcing judgment. Edith’s actions do not fall within this narrow exception because Edith is not merely seeking debt collection, rather her claims are broader and include breach of contract and negligence. These activities go beyond debt collection and constitute intrastate business, triggering the requirement of registration under §17708.07(a).

In opposition, Edith argues that it has not entered into repeated and successive business transactions in the state of California since it closed its business in November 2020.  In support, Edith submits the declaration of Helen Young, who avers that “Edith was effectively forced to close its business in November 2020 because Citibank emptied Edith’s checking account, which Edith was required to maintain as its primary business account and to pay its loan with Citibank. Because Edith is out of business, Edith did not enter into repeated and successive transactions of business in California since 2020.”  Nothing in the declaration of Mr. Kenney, or the attached transcript of Young’s deposition testimony, rebuts this.  Given these facts, and notwithstanding Corporations Code sections 17708.02 and 17708.07(a), Edith may proceed on its cross-complaint, which was filed in 2023. Therefore, Citibank’s Motion for a Stay of Proceedings is denied.

          IV.     CONCLUSION

The Court DENIES Citibank’s Motion for a Stay of Proceedings.

As prevailing party, Plaintiff is to give notice.

Case Number: 24AHCV00094    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT CITY OF LOS ANGELES’S MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS


I.         INTRODUCTION

           

This action arises from an alleged personal injury sustained by Plaintiff Ashleigh Robinson on June 15, 2023. Plaintiff claims that she was injured when the lid of a trash bin, operated by Deros Salza, an employee of Defendant City of Los Angeles (“City”), struck her while she was near her home. Plaintiff alleges that the incident occurred due to the negligent operation of the trash bin by the City’s employee.

 

Plaintiff submitted a Claim for Damages to the City on January 2, 2024. On January 23, 2024, the City denied Plaintiff’s claim for two reasons: (1) the claim was filed beyond the six-month claims presentation period required under Government Code section 911.2(a), and (2) the claim alleged that Plaintiff was harmed in the City of Pasadena, not Los Angeles.

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant action. The City filed its answer on March 26, 2024. Subsequently, on September 9, 2024, the City filed the present motion for judgment on the pleadings. On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed her opposition to the City’s motion.

Meet and Confer

Before filing a judgment on the pleadings pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings...” (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a).) The moving party shall file and serve with the motion for judgment on the pleadings a declaration concerning the parties meet and confer efforts.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a)(3).)¿¿ 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel, Caspar Jivalagian, states that no meaningful effort was made by the City to meet and confer regarding the issues presented in this motion. According to Mr. Jivalagian, he attempted to initiate the meet-and-confer process on October 2, 2024, but Deputy City Attorney Olivia Bona refused to engage in a discussion on the merits of the motion. Instead, Ms. Bona insisted that Plaintiff voluntarily dismiss the complaint without addressing the legal arguments Plaintiff intended to raise in opposition to the motion.

Code of Civil Procedure section 439(a)(4) provides that "a determination by the court that the meet-and-confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to grant or deny the motion for judgment on the pleadings." Therefore, even though the meet-and-confer process may have been insufficient, this does not provide a sufficient basis for granting or denying the motion for judgment on the pleadings. 

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

"A motion for judgment on the pleadings is analogous to a general demurrer but is made after the time to file a demurrer has expired." (International Assn. of Firefighters, Local 230 v. City of San Jose (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1179, 1196.) If the party moving for judgment on the pleadings is a defendant, there are only two permissible grounds for bringing the motion: the court lacks subject matter jurisdiction or the complaint "does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant." (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(b)(ii).) 

            “Because the motion is, in effect, a general demurrer, the same rules apply.” (Sofias v. Bank of America¿(1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586.) Therefore, when determining whether a complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the court must treat the complaint as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Schonfeldt v. State of California (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1465.) 

            If the court grants the motion, it may permit the opposing party to amend the pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (h)(1).) If granting leave to amend, the court must allow the party 30 days to file the amended pleading. (Id., § 438, subd. (h)(2).) But “[i]f there is no liability as a matter of law, leave to amend should not be granted.” (Schonfeldt v. State of California, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at 1465.) 

 

III.      ANALYSIS

            The City moves for judgment on the pleadings as to Plaintiff’s Complaint. Specifically, the City requests judgment on the pleadings on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to comply with the claims presentation requirement, the Court lacks jurisdiction over the Complaint, and the Complaint fails to state a cause of action with sufficient facts.

Claims presentation and Jurisdiction.

Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to comply with the six-month claim presentation requirement under Government Code section 911.2(a), which mandates that a claim for injury to person or property must be presented to a public entity within six months of the accrual of the cause of action. Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s cause of action accrued on June 15, 2023, the date she was allegedly injured by a trash bin lid operated by City of Los Angeles employee Deros Salza. However, Plaintiff did not file her claim with the City until January 2, 2024, which Defendant contends exceeds the statutory six-month deadline.

Defendant refers to Exhibit B, which shows that Plaintiff was informed by the City that her claim was untimely because it was filed after the six-month deadline. The City also advised Plaintiff that her only recourse was to “apply for leave to present a late claim under Government Code sections 911.4 and 912.2” (Bona Decl., Exh. B).  In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the City’s investigation and subsequent denial of her claim waived its ability to assert the timeliness defense. Plaintiff refers to Exhibit F, a letter from the City which states that the denial was based on the merits of the claim rather than its timeliness (Jivalagian Decl., Ex. F)¿. Plaintiff contends that the City's failure to mention the timeliness issue in its denial letter should preclude Defendant from now relying on that defense.

However, Government Code section 911.2(a) imposes a strict statutory deadline, and compliance with this deadline is a jurisdictional prerequisite to maintaining a lawsuit against a public entity. The fact that the City investigated the claim does not excuse Plaintiff’s failure to file within the required six months, nor does it waive the need for Plaintiff to seek leave to file a late claim under Government Code section 911.4. On January 23, 2024, the City properly notified Plaintiff of the untimeliness of her claim and provided clear instructions on how to potentially cure the issue by applying for leave to file a late claim. Despite this notice, Plaintiff failed to take the necessary steps to remedy her late filing.

Although Plaintiff argues that the City waived its ability to challenge the timeliness of her claim due to its investigation and formal denial, this does not overcome the jurisdictional bar created by her untimely submission. The City’s denial of the claim was based on both the jurisdictional issue and the untimeliness of the claim, as reflected in the record (Exh. F and Exh. B). Plaintiff cannot disregard the notice of untimeliness and fail to take corrective action. Her failure to seek relief under Government Code section 946.6 further bars her cause of action. As such, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the time requirements for presenting her claim under Government Code section 911.2(a) bars her cause of action.

Government Code section 911.4 requires that any application to present a late claim be filed within a reasonable time, but no later than one year after the accrual of the cause of action. The application must state the reason for the delay and include the proposed claim. Since the June 15, 2024 deadline for filing an application to present a late claim has passed, the Court cannot grant Plaintiff leave to amend the complaint to seek such relief. The statutory period has lapsed, and therefore, Plaintiff cannot cure the defect in her claim.

Accordingly, Defendant is entitled to judgment on the pleadings on this issue.

IV.       ORDER

            Accordingly, The City’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED without leave to amend. 

    Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24NNCV01022    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SEVER

I.        INTRODUCTION

          On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff SRS Distribution, Inc. dba JB Wholesale Roofing and Building Supply ("Plaintiff") initiated this action against Defendants Clean Initiative, LLC ("Clean Initiative") and Juan Pablo Quintero (“Quintero”) alleging Breach of Contract – Promissory Note and Common Counts – Account Stated. The action arises from a promissory note executed on February 9, 2024, with a principal amount of $166,816.82. Defendants failed to make the required payments under the terms of the note.

Clean Initiative was served with the summons and complaint on May 17, 2024, but failed to respond, resulting in an entry of default on July 11, 2024. Quintero filed for Chapter 13 bankruptcy protection on June 7, 2024, triggering an automatic stay of proceedings against him under federal bankruptcy law. Plaintiff now seeks to sever Clean Initiative from the action to pursue a default judgment while the case against Quintero remains stayed pending the resolution of his bankruptcy proceedings.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1048, subdivision (b): “The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States.”   

          “Whether there shall be a severance and separate trials on issues in a single action is a matter within the discretion of the trial court, whose ruling will not be disturbed on appeal absent a manifest abuse of discretion.” (Downey Savings & Loan Assn. v. Ohio Casualty Ins. Co. (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1072, 1086.) 

          III.     ANALYSIS

          Plaintiff moves for severance on the grounds that Clean Initiative is in default, and severing the claims against it would enable Plaintiff to pursue a default judgment without being prejudiced by the automatic bankruptcy stay affecting Quintero. Plaintiff contends that if the claims are not severed, it would suffer undue prejudice in one of two ways: first, Plaintiff would face unnecessary delay, as it would be forced to wait until Quintero's bankruptcy proceedings are resolved before it can enforce its rights against Clean Initiative. Second, if judgment is entered against Clean Initiative now without an order severing the claims, Plaintiff may be precluded from prosecuting its claims against Quintero due to the "one final judgment rule." (La Mendola Decl., ¶ 6.)

Plaintiff argues that severing the claims will allow it to proceed with a default judgment against Clean Initiative while avoiding any potential complications arising from Quintero's bankruptcy stay.

The Court agrees with Plaintiff and finds that severance is appropriate under these circumstances. Clean Initiative has defaulted and Plaintiff is entitled to pursue a separate judgment against this Defendant. The automatic stay as to Quintero should not delay Plaintiff’s ability to obtain relief from Clean Initiative. Severance will allow Plaintiff to obtain a several judgments against Clean Initiative while maintaining the stay as to Quintero.

Furthermore, the Court finds that severance will not prejudice any of the parties and will promote judicial economy by allowing Plaintiff to proceed against Clean Initiative without waiting for the resolution of Quintero's bankruptcy proceeding.

 

IV.     ORDER

          Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to sever is GRANTED.

 Plaintiff is to give notice.
