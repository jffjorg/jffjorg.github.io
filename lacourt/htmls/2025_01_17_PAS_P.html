DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01404    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER TO THE FIRST CAUSE OF ACTION OF THETHIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is a real property boundary dispute in which Plaintiffs Ralston C. Riggs and Tina M. Riggs (“Plaintiffs”) allege a disagreement regarding the boundaries of their property located at 1190 Morada Place, Altadena, California. The dispute arose following a survey conducted by Defendant Petra Wennberg (“Defendant”) in 2019, which was not disclosed to Plaintiffs until February 2022.

On June 26, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint (FAC). On October 10, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC with leave to amend. Plaintiffs subsequently filed their Second Amended Complaint (SAC) on April 5, 2024, and on August 16, 2024, the Court again sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the SAC with leave to amend. Plaintiffs filed their Third Amended Complaint (TAC) on September 19, 2024. Defendant filed the instant demurrer on October 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition on January 6, 2025, and Defendant filed a reply on January 10, 2025.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

In this case, Defendant’s counsel declares that, on October 11, 2024, they emailed Plaintiffs’ counsel a meet-and-confer correspondence regarding the purported deficiencies in the TAC. Plaintiffs’ counsel responded on October 20, 2024. (Rocha Decl. ¶¶ 5-6.) The Court finds that the meet-and-confer requirements have not been fully satisfied, as the statute mandates that the meet and confer occur in person, via telephone, or through video conference. Nevertheless, the Court notes that failure to meet and confer is not, by itself, grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

A pleader may not attempt to breathe life into a complaint by omitting relevant facts which made his previous complaint defective. See Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 946. Moreover, any inconsistencies with prior pleadings must be explained; if the pleader fails to do so, the court may disregard the inconsistent allegations. Id.; See also Vallejo Development Co. v. Beck Development Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 929, 946 (A court is not bound to accept as true allegations contrary to factual allegations in former pleading in the same case.); See further Deveny v. Entropin, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 408, 425.

The court also has discretion to deny “sham” amendments, i.e., those that omit or contradict harmful facts alleged in the original pleading, unless sufficient excuse exists. See Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945 – 946 (Generally, after an amended pleading has been filed, the court will disregard the original pleading. However, where the amended complaint attempts to avoid defects set forth in a prior complaint by ignoring them, the court may examine the prior complaint to ascertain whether the amended complaint is merely a sham. This rule is based on the rationale that a pleader may not attempt to breathe life into a complaint by omitting relevant facts that made the previous complaint defective.).

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs' first cause of action in the TAC for Agreed-Boundary Doctrine fails because it contradicts prior verified pleadings and does not state sufficient facts. Defendant asserts that while the TAC alleges that there was uncertainty regarding the boundary line when the fences and driveway were constructed in the 1960s (TAC ¶ 26(a)), prior verified pleadings alleged that the boundary was known but intentionally adjusted to make the Plaintiffs’ property more marketable (FAC ¶ 8; SAC ¶ 40).

Defendant contends that these conflicting allegations render the TAC a “sham” amendment intended to avoid defects in the prior pleadings. Defendant cites Zakk v. Diesel (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 431, emphasizing that when no explanation is offered for an inconsistency, the trial court is entitled to conclude the pleading is a sham and sustain a demurrer without leave to amend. Defendant further argues that Plaintiffs' TAC may not include contradictory or antagonistic facts, citing Faulkner v. California Toll Bridge Authority (1953) 40 Cal.2d 317, which held that a party cannot plead facts that contradict prior verified allegations. Defendant concludes that these inconsistencies constitute judicial admissions and bar Plaintiffs from pursuing the Agreed-Boundary Doctrine claim.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that the prior verified allegations were made “on information and belief” as permitted under CCP § 446(a), rather than on personal knowledge, and therefore do not constitute binding judicial admissions. (Opposition, pp. 1-2) Plaintiffs assert that their reliance on circumstantial evidence is due to the unavailability of original parties to the alleged agreement, and they argue that any inconsistencies are a result of this limited information. (Opposition, p.3) Plaintiffs further contend that pleadings should be construed liberally to achieve substantial justice (CCP § 452), and that the TAC adequately pleads the elements of the Agreed-Boundary Doctrine.

In reply, Defendant asserts that Plaintiffs’ reliance on the claim that prior allegations were made “on information and belief” lacks any supporting authority and fails to absolve Plaintiffs of their obligation to provide consistent pleadings. (Reply, pp.2-3) Defendant argues that this rationale cannot shield Plaintiffs from the legal consequences of their verified allegations, as the doctrine of judicial admissions applies regardless of whether the allegations were made on information and belief. (Id.) The Court agrees with Defendant’s position, finding Plaintiffs’ explanation inadequate.

The Court finds that where no explanation for an inconsistency between original and amended pleadings is offered, the trial court is entitled to conclude that the pleading party's cause of action is a sham and sustain a demurrer without leave to amend (Zakk v. Diesel (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 431, 245 Cal.Rptr.3d 215). In this instance, Plaintiffs in the TAC failed to provide any explanation for the inconsistencies between the FAC, SAC and the TAC. The Court further agrees that, absent any explanation for the discrepancies, the TAC cannot cure the defects present in the earlier pleadings.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Defendant's demurrer as to the first cause of action without leave to amend. 

IV.       ORDER

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the first cause of action of the Third Amended Complaint without leave to amend.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S ANTI-SLAPP MOTION and AWARDING MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.         INTRODUCTION

On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff Mary Rizzo (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendant Stacey Coombes (“Defendant”), asserting seven causes of action: Defamation, False Light, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Harassment, Invasion of Privacy, Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Malicious Prosecution. The parties are former neighbors.

On September 25, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Anti-SLAPP motion. Plaintiff filed an opposition on January 3, 2025, and Defendant filed a reply on January 9, 2025. The Court also notes that Plaintiff filed a request for leave to file a sur-reply on January 10, 2025, and Defendant filed a response on January 14, 2025.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

CCP section 425.16 permits the court to strike causes of action arising from an act in furtherance of the defendant’s right of free speech or petition unless the plaintiff establishes that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.

In assessing a defendant’s CCP section 425.16 special motion to strike, the court must engage in a two-step process. (Shekhter v. Financial Indem. Co. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.) First, the court must decide whether the defendant has met the threshold burden of showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the defendant’s constitutional rights of free speech or petition for redress of grievances. (Id.) This burden may be met by showing the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff’s cause of action was an act that falls within one of the four categories of conduct set forth in section 425.16(e).

Once a defendant has met its initial burden and established that the anti-SLAPP statute applies, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate a “probability” of success on the merits. (Code Civ. Proc., §425.16(b); Equilon Enters. LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.) “[T]he plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.” (Matson v. Dvorak (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 539, 548 (internal quotations omitted).) At this “second stage of an anti-SLAPP hearing, the court may consider affidavits, declarations, and their equivalents if it is reasonably possible the proffered evidence set out in those statements will be admissible at trial. Conversely, if the evidence relied upon cannot be admitted at trial, because it is categorically barred or undisputed factual circumstances show inadmissibility, the court may not consider it in the face of an objection. If an evidentiary objection is made, the plaintiff may attempt to cure the asserted defect or demonstrate the defect is curable.” (Sweetwater Union High School Dist. v. Gilbane Building Co. (2019) 6 Cal.5th 931, 949.)

The trial court properly considers the evidentiary submissions of both the plaintiff and the defendant, but it may not weigh the credibility or comparative strength of the evidence and must instead simply determine whether the plaintiff's evidence would, if believed by the trier of fact, be sufficient to result in a judgment for plaintiff. (McGarry v. Univ. of San Diego (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 97, 108-09.) The court “accepts as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff and evaluates the defendant’s evidence only to determine if it has defeated that submitted by the plaintiff as a matter of law.” (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 326.) Further, whether or not the evidence is in conflict in the context of a motion to strike under the anti-SLAPP statute, if the plaintiff has presented a sufficient pleading and has presented evidence showing that a prima facie case will be established at trial, the plaintiff is entitled to proceed. (Moore v. Shaw (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 182, 193.) Only a minimal showing of merit is required. (Robinzine v. Vicory (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1416, 1421.)

III.      ANALYSIS

First prong: protected activity

“To prevail on an anti-SLAPP motion, the movant must first make ‘a threshold showing the challenged cause of action’ arises from an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue.” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 192.) “A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim.” (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1062.) “‘[T] defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.’” (Id. at 1063 (quoting City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 78) (emphasis in original).) “‘[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’” (Id. (quoting Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89) (alteration in original).) “Instead, the focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’” (Id. (quoting Navellier, supra, 29 Cal.4th at 92) (alteration in original).) “‘The only means specified in section 425.16 by which a moving defendant can satisfy that [‘arising from’] requirement is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct by which plaintiff claims to have been injured falls within one of the four categories described in subdivision (e) . . ..’” (Id. (quoting Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 66) (emphasis in original).) “In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.” (Id.)

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s complaint arises entirely from protected activity under CCP section 425.16(e). Specifically, Defendant contends that the actions alleged in the complaint—contacting law enforcement, Child Protective Services (CPS), and the building management; filing for a restraining order; and hiring bodyguards—constitute protected speech and petitioning activity. Defendant asserts these actions fall squarely under CCP section 425.16(e)(3) and (e)(4), which protect statements made in connection with an issue of public interest and conduct furthering the exercise of free speech in connection with a public issue. (Motion, p. 14)

Defendant highlights that all alleged actions were undertaken in connection with matters of public concern, including domestic violence, child welfare, and public safety. Defendant asserts that communications with law enforcement and CPS about potential child endangerment and reports of domestic disturbances are inherently matters of public interest. Defendant argues that courts have consistently held that issues involving child abuse or safety fall within the scope of protected activity under CCP section 425.16(e), citing Terry v. Davis Community Church (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 1534 and Siam v. Kizilbash (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1563. (Motion, p.15; Reply p.7)

Next, Defendant contends that the statements to building management regarding tenant disputes and safety concerns, as well as the filing of a restraining order, were part of a legitimate effort to address these public concerns through judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings. Defendant relies on Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82 and Briggs v. Eden Council for Hope & Opportunity (1999) 19 Cal.4th 1106, which held that communications made in connection with judicial or official proceedings are protected under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Motion, p. 14-15)

Defendant further asserts that Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege specific facts linking the purported causes of action to unprotected activity. Instead, Defendant characterizes the complaint as reading more like a novel than a legal pleading, charged with emotional and speculative assertions. Nevertheless, Defendant contends the conclusory allegations in the complaint fail to undermine the fact that all of Defendant’s conduct alleged in the complaint constitutes protected activity under CCP section 425.16(e). (Motion, p. 16)

The Court finds that Defendant has met their burden under the first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis. Defendant has demonstrated that the alleged actions form the basis of Plaintiff’s claims and constitute protected activity under CCP section 425.16(e). The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to establish a probability of prevailing on the merits.

Second prong: probability of prevailing

As Defendant has met their burden of demonstrating that Plaintiff’s claims arise from protected activity, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish a probability of succeeding on the merits. (Kyle v. Carmon (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 901, 907.) To meet this burden, Plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by Plaintiff is credited. (Matson v. Dvorak (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 539, 548.) This evidentiary showing must consist of competent and admissible evidence. (Morrow v. Los Angeles Unified Sch. Dist. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1444.)

In this case, Plaintiff’s opposition fails to include any evidence demonstrating a probability of succeeding on the merits for any of the causes of action alleged in the complaint, including defamation, false light, intentional infliction of emotional distress, harassment, invasion of privacy, negligent infliction of emotional distress, or malicious prosecution. Plaintiff provides no declarations, documents, or other admissible evidence to support their claims or show that the complaint has legal or factual merit.

Additionally, the Court acknowledges Plaintiff’s sur-reply request but finds that Plaintiff can address any arguments raised in the reply during oral arguments. Plaintiff has not established a prima facie case or met their burden of showing that they are likely to prevail on the merits.

Given the complete absence of admissible evidence or substantive arguments addressing the legal and factual elements of the claims, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not met their burden of showing a probability of prevailing. 

            Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants motion.

Sanctions

Any defendant who prevails on a special motion to strike is entitled to mandatory, reasonable attorney fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c); Ketchum v. Moses¿(2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1141–1142.) The attorney fees and costs recoverable are only those incurred as to the special motion to strike, and not as to the entire litigation. (Lafayette Morehouse, Inc. v. Chronicle Pub. Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1383.) 

Defendant seeks an award of $8,885.00 in attorney fees and costs incurred in connection with the special motion to strike. Defendant submits the declaration of Kenneth L. Kazan, Defendant’s attorney of record, in support of this request. Mr. Kazan declares that his hourly billing rate is $595.00, which he asserts is reasonable based on his 43 years of legal experience and the nature of this matter. He further states that he spent 10 hours reviewing Plaintiff’s complaint, conducting legal research, and preparing the special motion to strike. (Kazan Decl., ¶¶ 3-7) Mr. Kazan also anticipates spending an additional 4 hours preparing for and personally attending the hearing. The total requested attorney fees amount to $8,330.00 for 14 hours of work. Additionally, Defendant requests $555.00 in costs, consisting of a $435.00 first appearance fee and a $60.00 motion filing fee. (Kazan Decl., ¶¶ 3-8)

The Court finds Mr. Kazan’s hourly rate reasonable given his extensive experience and the straightforward nature of the motion. However, the Court reduces the total time spent on the motion, including research, review, and preparation of the motion and reply, by 5 hours. This reduction reflects the Court’s assessment of the reasonable time required for a motion of this type by an attorney of Mr. Kazan’s experience. Additionally, the Court grants 1 hour for attending the hearing. In total, the Court awards 6 hours at $595.00 per hour, amounting to $3,570.00 in attorney fees, and $60.00 for the motion filing fee.

The Court awards Defendant a total of $3, 630.00 in attorney fees and costs.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Defendant’s anti-SLAPP motion and awards Defendant $3,630.00 in monetary sanctions to be paid within 45 days of this order.
