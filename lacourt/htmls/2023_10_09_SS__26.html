The e-mail address is SSCdept26@lacourt.org

The heading on your e-mail should contain the case name, number, hearing date, and that you submit. The message should indicate your name, contact information, and the party you represent. Please note, the above e-mail address is to inform the court of your submission on the tentative ruling. All other inquiries will not receive a response.

If there are no appearances by either side and no submission on the Court's tentative ruling, the matter will be placed OFF CALENDAR.

Case Number: 21STLC07521    Hearing Date: October 9, 2023    Dept: 26

  

Lazo v. QX Logistix, LLC, et al.

MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED;

REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(CCP § 2033.280)

TENTATIVE RULING:  

           

Defendant QX Logistix, LLC’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF MAHO LAZO IS ORDERED TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $535.00 TO DEFENSE COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On July 11, 2023, Defendant QX Logistix, LLC (“Defendant”) served Requests for Admissions on Plaintiff Maho Lazo (“Plaintiff”). (Motion, Wiseman Decl., Exh. 1) To date, Plaintiff has not served responses to the Requests for Admission. (Id. at ¶4.) Defendant filed the instant Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted on August 18, 2023. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

There is no requirement for a prior meet and confer effort before a motion to deem requests for admission can be filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.) Further, the motion can be brought any time after the responding party fails to provide the responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.) Due to Plaintiff’s failure to timely serve responses and resultant waiver of objections, Defendant is entitled to an order deeming the Requests for Admission admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.)

 

The Court is required to award sanctions under the moving statute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) The sanctions are properly noticed, however, the amount sought is excessive. Under a lodestar calculation, sanctions are granted against Plaintiff in the amount of $525.00, based on one hour of attorney time billed at $465.00 per hour, plus the $60.00 filing fee. (Motion, Wiseman Decl., ¶5.)

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant QX Logistix, LLC’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF MAHO LAZO IS ORDERED TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $525.00 TO DEFENSE COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER.

Steigleder v. Badalyans, et al.

DEMURRER; MOTION TO STRIKE

(CCP §§ 430.31, et seq., 435, et seq.)


TENTATIVE RULING:

Cross-Defendant Peter Steigleder’s Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Cross-Complaint is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff Peter Steigleder (“Cross-Defendant”) filed the instant action for breach of contract and common counts against Defendant Garnik Badalyans (“Cross-Complainant”) on December 6, 2022. Cross-Complainant filed a Cross-Complaint for breach of contract, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and fraud against Cross-Defendant, on January 20, 2023.

 

On June 29, 2023, the Court sustained Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint with leave to amend. (Minute Order, 06/29/23.) Cross-Complainant filed the First Amended Cross-Complaint on July 19, 2023.

 

Cross-Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to, and Motion to Strike Portions of, the First Amended Cross-Complaint on August 22, 2023. Cross-Complainant filed and served oppositions on September 12, 2023 and Cross-Defendant replied on October 2, 2023.

 

Discussion

 

Allegations in the Complaint and First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

The Complaint alleges that around May 22, 2021, the parties entered into an oral agreement (the “Agreement”) for the sole benefit of Cross-Complainant under which Cross-Defendant agreed to advance certain funds for the cost of an environmental site assessment for the real property at 1767 Standard Avenue, Glendale, California (“the Property”). (Compl., ¶6.) In exchange, Cross-Complainant agreed to timely reimburse Cross-Defendant upon demand for the funds advanced. (Id. at ¶7.) Cross-Defendant made three payments totaling $4,425.32 to Fulcrum Resources, Inc. for work done at the Property. (Id. at ¶9.) On July 2, 2021, AdvancedGeo, Inc. proposed an additional environmental site assessment at the Property and on July 8, 2021, Cross-Defendant entered into an agreement with AdvancedGeo, Inc. to pay for its work. (Id. at ¶¶10-11.) On July 24, 2021, Cross-Defendant paid AdvancedGeo $3,105.00 for the work. (Id. at ¶12.) Despite numerous demands, Cross-Complainant has not reimbursed Cross-Defendant for the payments. (Id. at ¶13-16.)

 

The First Amended Cross-Complaint alleges that Cross-Defendant entered into a written agreement to represent Cross-Complainant as a real estate agent. (FACC, ¶3.) Cross-Defendant was aware Cross-Complainant was trying to refinance the Property, and if the refinancing did not succeed, would sell the Property. (Id. at ¶4.) Cross-Defendant promised to inform each potential buyer that Cross-Complainant was trying to refinance so Cross-Complainant could pull out of a pending sale with no obligations to the potential buyer. (Id. at ¶5.) A potential buyer was identified but Cross-Complainant was able to refinance the Property during the pendency of the sales transaction. (Id. at ¶¶6-7.) Cross-Complainant informed Cross-Defendant that the pending sale should be canceled but learned that Cross-Defendant had not informed the potential buyer of the refinancing. (Id. at ¶¶7-8.) The potential buyer was unwilling to cancel the purchase of the Property without compensation for attorney’s fees and other losses. (Id. at ¶8.) Cross-Defendant falsely represented that he would make potential buyers aware of the refinancing to induce Cross-Complainant into signing the real estate representation agreement. (Id. at ¶10.) Cross-Complainant reasonably relied on Cross-Defendant’s representations to enter into the representation agreement and was substantially damaged as a result. (Id. at ¶11.) Upon learning of the false representation, Cross-Complainant elected to void the representation agreement pursuant to Civil Code section 1689, subdivision (b)(1). (Id. at ¶9.)

 

The parties also entered into an oral contract that each would pay for half of some expenses claimed by Cross-Defendant with a potential sale of the property (Id. at ¶13.) Cross-Complainant paid for half of the expenditures as agreed. (Id. at ¶¶14-15.) By filing the Complaint in this action, Cross-Defendant breached the oral agreement and caused Cross-Complainant damages. (Id. at ¶16.) Cross-Defendant’s false representation that he would share some expenses breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. (Id. at p. 5, ¶14.)

 

Demurrer to First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

The First Amended Cross-Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) fraudulent misrepresentation; (2) breach of contract; and (3) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Cross-Defendant demurs on grounds of failure to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action, the sham pleading doctrine, and uncertainty. (Citing Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) The Demurrer is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration that substantially complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. (Demurrer, Thompson-Mann Decl., Exh. 2.) The Court takes note of Cross-Cromplainant’s objection that the meet and confer was not in-person or over the phone. However, Cross-Complainant does not indicate any prejudice from the written meet and confer letter or indicate that the meet and confer effort would have been successful otherwise. Nor is non-compliance with the meet and confer statute grounds to overrule the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)

 

1.      First Cause of Action for Fraud

 

Cross-Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation on the grounds that it fails to allege sufficient facts under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e). The elements of fraud are (1) misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity (or "scienter"); (3) intent to defraud (induce reliance); (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. (Conroy v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2009) 45 Cal. 4th 1244, 1255.) It is also black letter law that fraud must be alleged with specificity, meaning that the pleadings must allege facts as to “‘how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal. App. 3d 59, 73.) The First Amended Cross-Complaint does not allege any of these details about Cross-Defendant’s alleged misrepresentation regarding informing potential buyers of Cross-Complainant’s attempt to refinance the property. (See FACC, ¶5.) It simply alleges that the promise to do so was made, but the manner in which the promise was made, the date, location, and other relevant details are not alleged. (See FACC, ¶5.) In opposition, Cross-Complainant does not address where these specific facts are alleged in the First Amended Cross-Complaint. (Opp., pp. 3:13-4:13.) The first cause of action, therefore, is not sufficiently alleged as a fraud cause of action.

 

To the extent Cross-Defendant also argues that Cross-Complainant is improperly attempting to allege a breach of contract claim as a tort, the Court declines to make such a finding. Cross-Defendant previously alleged the same facts and sought to frame them as a breach of oral contract cause of action. (See Cross-Compl., ¶¶1-11.) However, the alleged conduct must amount to a breach of contract a party can be barred from alleging it as a tort. (See Freeman & Mills, Inc. v. Belcher Oil Co. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 85, 95 [“[c]onduct amounting to a breach of contract becomes tortious only when it also violates an independent duty” [emphasis added].) According to Cross-Defendant, Cross-Complainant did not sufficiently allege that this conduct amounted to a breach of contract. Therefore, Cross-Complainant is not barred from seeking to frame it as a fraud cause of action, assuming the fraud cause of action is sufficiently alleged.

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained.

 

2.      2nd Cause of Action for Breach of Oral Contract

 

Cross-Defendant demurs to the second cause of action on the grounds that it confusingly alleges two different oral contracts, making it unclear which contract is at issue. The first oral contract concerns the parties’ agreement to split expenses Cross-Defendant claimed with respect to the potential sale of the Property. (FACC, ¶¶13-17.) The second contract is based on the earlier allegations that Cross-Defendant misrepresented that he would inform potential purchasers of the Property about the refinancing. (Id. at ¶¶19-22.) In addition to alleging damages from these breaches, Plaintiff alleges rescission of one or both contracts. (Id. at ¶18.) These remedies, however, cannot be sought together; one must be alleged as an alternative to the other. (Alder v. Drudis (1947) 30 Cal.2d 372, 381-382.)

 

Again, the opposition does not speak to why these two oral contracts are confusingly alleged together or how the Court can discern which remedy is sought. (Opp., p. 4:5-16.) In light of the allegations that improperly combine the breach of two separate agreements into a single cause of action and seek remedies that cannot be sought together, the demurrer to the second cause of action is also sustained.

 

3.       Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is based on the second cause of action failing to state a cause of action. “[T]he duty of good faith and fair dealing derives from and exists solely because of the contractual relationship between the parties.” (Austero v. National Cas. Co. (1976) 62 Cal.App.3d 511, 515.) Cross-Defendant cannot be liable for breach of the implied covenant if there are insufficient allegations of an existing contract. (Ibid.)

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the third cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is also sustained.

 

Motion to Strike Portions of Cross-Complaint

 

Cross-Defendant moves to strike the allegations in support of, and the request for, punitive damages. The allegations in relation to the request for punitive damages are only available with respect to the cause of action for fraud. (See Civ. Code, § 3294.) The Court having sustained Cross-Defendant’s Demurrer to the fraud cause of action, the Motion to Strike is moot.

 

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment, however, the burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Cross-Complainant was previously given a chance to amend and it remains that the causes of action are insufficiently pled. However, Cross-Complainant requests another opportunity to amend and the Court finds it possible for the fraud cause of action to be alleged with the requisite specificity and for the breach of contract cause of action to clarify the contract at issue and remedy sought. Therefore, leave to amend is granted.

 

Conclusion

 

Cross-Defendant Peter Steigleder’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED             WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Cross-Defendant Peter Steigleder’s Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Cross-Complaint is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.

 

 

Moving party to give notice.


Case Number: 23STLC01548    Hearing Date: October 9, 2023    Dept: 26

 

Orozco, et al. v. Ocampo, et al.

MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO INTERROGATORIES AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS 

(CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2023.030)


TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Manuel Ocampo’s (1) Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions; and (2) Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions are GRANTED. PLAINTIFFS YOHANSON WALKIR CASTELLANOS OROZCO AND ANA LUCIA ELIZABETH LANTAN GARCIA ARE TO SERVE VERIFIED RESPONSES TO THE DISCOVERY REQUESTS WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER. PLAINTIFFS ARE FURTHER ORDERED TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $520.00 TO DEFENSE COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Defendant Manuel Ocampo (“Defendant”) propounded Form Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, on Plaintiffs Yohanson Walkir Castellanos Orozco and Ana Lucia Elizabeth Lantan Garcia (“Plaintiffs”) on April 28, 2023. (Motions, McEwan Decl., Exh. A.) Following Plaintiffs’ failure to respond, Defendant sought to meet and confer. (Id. ¶¶4-5 and  Exh. B.) When there was no response from Plaintiffs after the extended deadline, Defendant filed the instant (1) Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions; and (2) Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions on August 16, 2023. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

Discussion

 

There is no requirement for a prior meet and confer effort before a motion to compel initial responses can be filed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.) Further, the motion can be brought any time after the responding party fails to provide the responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.) Based on Plaintiffs’ failure to respond to the propounded discovery, Defendant is entitled to an order compelling service of verified responses to the discovery requests without objections.

 

Plaintiffs’ failure to timely respond constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).) Sanctions are appropriate under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010 and 2023.030 and have been properly noticed. However, the amount sought is excessive. Pursuant to a lodestar calculation, sanctions are awarded in the amount of $520.00 based on two hours of attorney time billed at $200.00 per hour, and $60.00 in costs. (Motions, McEwan Decl., ¶7.) 

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Manuel Ocampo’s (1) Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions; and (2) Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions are GRANTED. PLAINTIFFS YOHANSON WALKIR CASTELLANOS OROZCO AND ANA LUCIA ELIZABETH LANTAN GARCIA ARE TO SERVE VERIFIED RESPONSES TO THE DISCOVERY REQUESTS WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER. PLAINTIFFS ARE FURTHER ORDERED TO PAY SANCTIONS OF $520.00 TO DEFENSE COUNSEL WITHIN 20 DAYS’ SERVICE OF THIS ORDER.

 

 

Moving party to give notice.
