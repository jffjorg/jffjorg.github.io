DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 21STCV23920    Hearing Date: December 5, 2023    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Mikel Rastegar’s Motion for Summary Judgment and/or Summary Adjudication

Plaintiff Mikel Rastegar moves for summary judgment on his first amended complaint against defendants Mark Blankenship and Jennifer Harper. 

Plaintiff did not file adequate proof of service of his moving papers.  Self-represented parties are not required to accept electronic service “unless they affirmatively consent to electronic service.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.251(c)(3)(B); accord CCP, § 1010.6(c).)  The proof of service submitted with the moving papers states the papers were served only by email.  Defendants Blankenship and Harper represent themselves in this action.  The record includes no document showing either defendant affirmatively consented to electronic service.  The service was therefore invalid and did not give proper notice to defendants.

The court notes that in their motion to quash service of the first amended complaint, defendants advised the court that they did not agree to accept service by email.  (See minute order dated 6/30/23.)  Plaintiff has no provided no evidence to the contrary.

The court further notes that the address given by defendants to the court—6250 Hollywood Boulevard, Suite 5F, Los Angeles, California—appears to no longer be the defendants’ address.  On September 7, 2023, the court received a return of correspondence to that address from the U.S. Postal Service.  Defendants have not updated their current address as required by the California Rules of Court.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.200.)

Plaintiff and the clerk of the court are ordered to serve future documents by (1) mailing the documents to the defendants’ last known address of record in the court’s file and (2) emailing the documents to defendants’ last known email address.

The motion is denied.

Case Number: 22STCV15743    Hearing Date: December 5, 2023    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Yolanda Perez’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Pursuant to CCP § 664.6 and for Attorney’s Fees and Costs

Plaintiff Yolanda Perez moves to enforce the parties’ settlement agreement and to enter judgment against defendant Color Story Textile, Inc.  “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court... for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (CCP § 664.6(a).) 

Plaintiff has fulfilled the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.  The parties stipulated in writing to settle this action.  They signed a settlement agreement in March 2023.  (Villarreal Decl., ¶¶ 2-3, Ex. A.)  They further signed an addendum to the agreement in June 2023.  (Id., ¶¶ 12-13, Ex. E; ¶¶ 24-25, Ex. J.)  Defendant agreed to pay plaintiff $60,000 in three installments.  (Id., Ex. E.)  Defendant further agreed that if it did not timely pay, plaintiff may submit a stipulation for entry of judgment and proposed judgment.  (Id., Ex. J.) 

Defendant did not make any payments required under the parties’ agreements.  (Villarreal Decl., ¶ 27.)  Plaintiff has moved to enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the parties’ agreement.  Plaintiff served this motion on defendant, who did not oppose it.  The court will therefore enter judgment. 

Plaintiff also seeks $3,212.10 in attorney fees and costs incurred in bringing this motion.  Plaintiff may recover reasonable attorney fees and costs pursuant to the parties’ settlement agreement.  The agreement provides, “If any action at law or in equity, including an action for declaratory relief, is brought to enforce or interpret the provisions of this Agreement, the prevailing party shall be entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs for material breaches of this Agreement.”  (Villarreal Decl., Ex. A, § 4.i.)  Plaintiff prevailed in this proceeding to enforce the settlement agreement. 

Plaintiff did not, however, reasonably incur $3,212.10 in expenses.  Plaintiff claims attorney fees for 5.48 hours drafting the moving papers and 2.5 hours anticipated to review the opposition and prepare a reply and to prepare for and attend the hearing.  (Villarreal Decl., ¶ 28.)  Plaintiff claims an hourly rate of $395.  (Id., ¶ 30.)  Drafting this simple motion did not reasonably require 5.48 hours of work.  Furthermore, defendant did not oppose the motion.  Plaintiff filed only a notice of non-opposition, not a reply brief.  The court therefore finds plaintiff reasonably incurred 5.0 hours of attorney fees at $395 hourly, plus the $60 filing fee (id., ¶ 31), for a total of $2,035 in expenses.

Plaintiff Yolanda Perez’s motion to enforce settlement is granted.  The court will enter judgment for plaintiff Yolanda Perez against defendant Color Story Textile, Inc. in the amount of $62,035.


Case Number: 23STCV03596    Hearing Date: December 5, 2023    Dept: 52

Plaintiff German Palominos Cervantes’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production

            Plaintiff German Palominos Cervantes moves to compel further responses to requests for production Nos. 13-16, 34, and 35.

            Plaintiff did not adequately meet and confer before filing this motion.  A motion to compel further responses to requests for production “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (CCP § 2031.310(b)(2).)  “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (CCP § 2016.040.)  This requirement aims “to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order.”  (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1293.)      

The declaration in support of this motion shows plaintiff sent a single meet and confer letter to defendant General Motors LLC on August 17, 2023.  (Yashar Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 3.)  The letter addresses the requests for production in general but only specifically addresses Nos. 2, 3, 8, and 13-16.  (Id., pp. 11-15.)  Plaintiff failed to give adequate notice that he sought further responses to Nos. 34 and 35 and failed to make a sufficient attempt to informally resolve any dispute over them.  Omitting No. 34 was particularly improper because defendant agreed to comply in part and produce some documents.  (Id., Ex. 2, p. 26.)    

For request Nos. 13-16, defendant replied to plaintiff’s letter on August 30 and offered to supplement its production.  (Yashar Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. 4, p. 7.)  Defendant asked plaintiff to agree to a stipulated protective order and to “provide … a proposed list of the specific issues/repairs comprising Plaintiff’s complaint(s) so that [defendant] can determine agreeable search parameters and search terms.”  (Ibid.)  Defendant tried to informally resolve the dispute over Nos. 13-16.  Nothing in the record shows plaintiff responded to defendant’s letter.  Plaintiff therefore filed this motion without first engaging with defendant in a reasonable and good faith effort at resolving the dispute as required.

Plaintiff German Palominos Cervantes’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production is denied.

Case Number: 23STCV11360    Hearing Date: December 5, 2023    Dept: 52

Defendant Long Beach Unified School District’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint

Demurrer

Defendant Long Beach Unified School District demurs to all six causes of action alleged by plaintiff Brenda Estrada.

Plaintiff’s complaint is both uncertain and fails to allege sufficient facts for any of her causes of action.  “ ‘[T]o state a cause of action against a public entity, every fact material to the existence of its statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity.’ ”  (Lopez v. Southern Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795.) 

Plaintiff’s complaint relies almost entirely on conclusory allegations.  She alleges, “Commencing in or about May, 2021, Plaintiff began experiencing serious, severe and pervasive harassment and discrimination based on her sex, gender and age.”  (¶ 22.)  Those are legal conclusions.  She alleges no facts stating what she experienced.  Plaintiff does not allege what happened such that “the social environment of the workplace [became] intolerable” (Roby v. McKesson Corp. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686, 706) and does not allege any facts constituting “intimidation, ridicule, and insult.”  (Rehmani v. Superior Court (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 945, 951.) 

Plaintiff also never alleges with particularity any adverse employment action against her.  The complaint alleges defendant took “adverse employment actions against Plaintiff” (¶ 26) and that plaintiff “suffered repeated and significant adverse actions at the workplace perpetrated by Defendants, including but not limited to demotion, refusal to promote and reduction in hours and wage compensation” (¶ 27).  Alleging adverse employment actions “including but not limited to” these three examples obfuscates the basis for any liability. 

For her second cause of action for retaliation, plaintiff alleges she made “repeated requests that [defendant] cease and desist in their unlawful misconduct” (¶ 23) and “refused to assist defendants in their unlawful misconduct” (¶ 25) without alleging any facts identifying what “unlawful misconduct” she opposed.

When read as a whole, the complaint never alleges facts adequate to apprise defendant of the basis for its liability.  Rather than alleging the facts with particularity, the complaint merely identifies the nature of plaintiff’s causes of action and asserts legal conclusions restating their elements.

Motion to Strike

            Defendant moves to strike three portions of plaintiff’s complaint.  The motion to strike is moot because the court will sustain defendant’s demurrer to the entire complaint.

Disposition

Defendant Long Beach Unified School District’s demurrer to plaintiff Brenda Estrada’s entire complaint is sustained with 20 days’ leave to amend.
