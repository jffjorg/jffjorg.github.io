DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 21STCV18717    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

JORGE HERNANDEZ CAMACHO and SARA MARTINEZ,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

TOYOTA MOTOR SALES U.S.A., INC., a California Corporation, and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive.

 Case No.:                           21STCV18717

 Hearing Date:   5/13/24

 Trial Date:         10/15/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses from Defendant, and Request for Sanctions – First Set of Request for Production of Documents.

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiffs Jorge Hernandez Camacho and Sara Martinez sue Defendants Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. (Toyota USA) and Does 1 through 10 pursuant to a May 18, 2023, Complaint alleging claims of (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty, (2) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty, and (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2.

The claims arise from the following allegations. On February 16, 2019, Plaintiffs purchased a 2019 Toyota Corolla (the Vehicle), pursuant to which Toyota USA and Plaintiffs entered express and implied warranties. According to those warranties, Toyota USA would conform any defects that arose in the Vehicle within the warranty period within a reasonable number of attempts or instead repurchase or replace the Vehicle. However, the Vehicle later manifested defects and nonconformities within the applicable warranty period, which Toyota USA was unable to repair or conform to warranty after a reasonable number of attempts, with Toyota USA also refusing to repurchase or replace the Vehicle.

B. Motion Before the Court

On February 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a motion to compel further responses to certain requests in Plaintiffs’ first set of production discovery served on Toyota USA (RPDs, Set One). Plaintiffs’ motion attaches a separate statement.

On May 6, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a notice of non-opposition that noticed Toyota USA’s failure to file an opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion.

On May 7, 2024, Toyota USA filed an ex parte application for an order allowing Toyota USA to file an opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion.

That same day, Plaintiffs filed an opposition to Toyota USA’s ex parte application.

On May 8, 2024, Toyota USA filed a reply to Plaintiffs’ opposition.

That same day, the Court granted Toyota USA’s ex parte application, which allowed Toyota USA to file an opposition by May 8, 2024, at midnight, and Plaintiffs to file a reply by May 10, 2024, at noon.

On May 9, 2024—one court day late—Toyota USA filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion. The opposition fails to attach a separate statement.

On May 10, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply to Toyota USA’s opposition.

Plaintiffs’ motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Compel Further Production and Request for Sanctions

A. Admonishment

The Court notes that Toyota USA and counsel repeatedly citing unpublished, uncitable California opinions in its opposition papers, contrary to the Rules of Court. (Opp’n, p. 12; see Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(a), (b).) Such authority will not be considered.

B. Motion to Compel Further Production: GRANTED, in part.

1. Legal Standard

A motion to compel a further response is used when a party gives unsatisfactory answers or makes untenable objections to interrogatories, demands to produce, or requests for admission. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)

To request further production, a movant must establish: (1) good cause for the production (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1); Sinaiko, supra, at p. 403); and (2) that a further response is needed because (a) the responding party’s statement of compliance with the demand to produce is incomplete Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(1)), (b) the responding party’s representation that it is unable to comply is inadequate, complete, or evasive (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(2)), (c) the responding party’s objection in the response is without merit or is too general (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(3); Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1127), or (d) if the responding party objected to the production of ESI on the ground that it is not reasonably accessible the movant can show that the (i) ESI is reasonably accessible or (ii) there is good cause for production of the ESI regardless of its accessibility (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (e)).

2. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiffs, in part.

The Court notes that it considers the opposition though it was filed late because the reply fails to address any prejudice arising from the late service of the opposition. (Arambula v. Union Carbide Corp. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 333, 343; see Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697; Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1288.) However, the Court strongly encourages counsel for the defendants to be more careful in calendaring, given that this Opposition was filed after the due date was already long past and should have been prepared and ready to go in the event the Court granted the ex parte to file a late Opposition. The Court will not condone repeated violations of the rules.

a. RPDs, Set One, Nos. 18, 20, 21, 24-27, 29-32, 37-41, and 45-46

Critical to the Court’s ruling is an understanding of the evidence presented by Toyota USA in connection with its opposition.

Toyota USA attaches excerpts of Plaintiff Jorge Hernandez Camacho’s deposition transcript. The transcript reflects that Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho has testified to the following effect:

(1) Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho apparently only presented the subject vehicle to an authorized dealership for repair a single time (see Mot., Ex. D, 142:8-10, 145:8-11);

(2) The visit to the authorized dealership for repair was spurred by Toyota USA issuing an interim recall notice, which Plaintiffs received, and which prompted Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho to present the vehicle for repair (Mot, Ex. C, 86:22-25);

(3) Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho had no complaints or issues with the subject vehicle prior to presenting the vehicle to an authorized dealership in response to the interim recall notice (Mot., Ex. C, 85:23-86:1 & Ex. D, 142:8-11);

(4) Prior to and after the visit to the authorized dealership for repair, Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho did not experience any issues with the vehicle’s airbags, seal belt pretensioners, stalling, or low pressure—i.e., the issues raised in the interim recall notice (Mot., Ex. D, 142:12-143:21); and

(5) Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho had no complaints or issues with the subject vehicle after presenting the vehicle to an authorized dealership in response to the interim recall notice (Mot., Ex. D, 145:8-11).

The Song-Beverly Act “give[s] recourse to the buyer of a new automobile that suffers from the same defect repeatedly, or is out of service for cumulative repairs for an extended period.” (Krotin v. Porsche Cars North America, Inc. (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 294, 301 (Krotin).)

Here, the evidence presented by Toyota USA and cited above shows that Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho only presented the Vehicle for a single repair visit to a Toyota and that Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho did not have any complaints regarding the vehicle’s condition before or after presenting the vehicle for repair in response to the interim call notice. If this is correct, then no relief  appears to be available under the SBA based on “the buyer of a new automobile … suffer[ing] from the same defect repeatedly.” (Ibid.)

As for whether the vehicle was “out of service for cumulative repairs for an extended period of time” (ibid.), the Court notes that the majority of the discovery at issue here does not appear geared toward this question. (Mot., Separate Statement, RPDs, Set One, Nos. 18, 20, 21, 24-27, 29-32, 37-41, and 45-46.) Instead, those production requests are geared toward discovering information pertinent to overall defects in Toyota Corollas of the same year. Moreover, the opposition papers argue that the four-month repair visit in this case—exceeding the 30-day limit (Civ. Code, § 1793.2, subd. (b)—was a result of parts unavailability. (Mot., p. 5, citing Mot., Ex. D, 146:11-147:2.) These circumstances connote a “[d]elay caused by conditions beyond the control of the manufacturer or its representatives,” seemingly amounting to circumstances that may “extend th[e] 30-day requirement” for reasonable repairs. (Civ. Code, § 1793.2, subd. (b).) Last, a single visit to a repair facility in response to a recall notice does not appear to support an SBA claim here. (See, Gutierrez v. Carmax Auto Superstores (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 1234, 1248 [In allegedly-defective-lamp case not arising from the SBA, “the existence of a safety recall does not adequately allege a substantial safety hazard existed”].)

Neither does the reply rebut the opposition’s characterization of Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho’s deposition testimony, which is fatal for the discovery here. This is because information is relevant only if it might reasonably help a party evaluate a case, prepare for trial, or facilitate a settlement. (See TBG Ins. Servs. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) It is not clear here how responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 18, 20, 21, 24-27, 29-32, 37-41, and 45-46 will assist Plaintiffs in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a settlement. This is because a threshold determination for discovery in SBA actions is whether more than one repair occurred (here, only one occurred) and whether the vehicle was out of service for an unreasonable amount of time (seemingly not the case here based on the need for extra time to receive the pars to be installed in the vehicle in response to the recall).

It is worth noting that the opposition papers characterize the subject vehicle as being solely used by Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho. (Mot., pp. 1, 3.) However, no direct evidence attached to the opposition papers supports that argument. On the other hand, Plaintiffs’ reply does not refute the opposition’s framing of Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho as the sole driver of the vehicle, which would make him the sole person who would be aware of problems arising from the operation of the vehicle.

Separately, even if Sara Martinez also drove the subject vehicle, Plaintiffs’ reply does not dispute the deposition testimony presented by Toyota USA, as discussed above. Indeed, Plaintiffs merely argue that “whether … there is any ‘basis for Plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly Claims’ is not what is before the Court in this Motion” and that “Defendant can make these arguments in a motion for summary judgement should it go to the trouble to file one,” for which reasons “the Court should not consider this argument as it has no applicability to the instant Motion.” (Reply, p. 2.) This argument fails because the intent of Toyota USA’s opposition is not to summarily dispose of this action in a discovery motion, but rather, to highlight that the information requested by Plaintiffs is irrelevant where Plaintiff Hernandez Camacho has given damaging testimony that appears to take this action outside of the SBA’s ambit.

Neither does the reply attach evidence showing that the vehicle was presented for repair to authorized Toyota USA dealerships more than once, which the Court could have considered for the first time on reply for good cause, i.e., the need to rebut the opposition’s deposition evidence. (Nordstrom Com. Cases (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 576, 583 [courts generally need not accept arguments raised for the first time on reply without good cause explanation as to why points were not raised earlier].)

Last, the Court notes that RPDs, Set One, Nos. 39-41 are overbroad. Requests No. 39 and 41 are not confined to repair codes germane to the issues for which the vehicle was presented for repair (the recalls). Request No. 40 requests all customer complaints from 2019 to the present, which is discovery not limited to vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the subject vehicle or to vehicles with similar components even if not the same year, make, and model.

For these reasons—lack of relevance, and in parts, overbreadth—Plaintiffs’ motion is DENIED as to compelling further responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 18, 20, 21, 24-27, 29-32, 37-41, and 45-46.

b. RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15, 17

However, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion as to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15 and 17.

These production requests are relevant to showing the applicable warranties on the Vehicle and the repair manual for the subject vehicle. Such information is eminently relevant where the pleadings allege breach of various warranties for lack of proper repair and where the four-month repair may still support an SBA claim.

Toyota USA’s objections to these production requests are unavailing. As for equal availability (RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15, 17), the Court rejects this objection. The Code does not provide a wholesale exception for “equal availability.” Instead, the proper test is found in Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.030, which permits limitations to discovery where the “discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2019.030, subd. (a)(1).) Here, Toyota made an insufficient showing that these documents can be obtained from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive. This is because even though Toyota USA refers Plaintiff to its website in response to both these production requests, such a referral is not any more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive where Toyota USA’s responses merely refer Plaintiffs to “Toyota.com” or to the public domain in search for this information.

For these reasons, Plaintiffs’ motion is GRANTED as to compelling further responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15 and 17.

C. Request for Sanctions: DENIED.

The Court DENIES sanctions for two reasons. First, Plaintiff’s motion was only granted in small part, specifically as to two production requests alone. Second, given that Toyota USA was for the most part justified in opposing these requests, the imposition of sanctions would not be commensurate to the results of this motion. 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses from Defendant, and Request for Sanctions – First Set of Request for Production of Documents is:

(1) GRANTED as to compelling further responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 18, 20, 21, 24-27, 29-32, 37-41, and 45-46 from Defendant Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc.;

(1) DENIED as to compelling further responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15 and 17 from Defendant Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc.; and

(3) DENIED as to sanctions.

Defendant Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. is ORDERED to provide further responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 15 and 17 within fourteen days of this order.

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Stephen Newland sues Defendants Tyler Gary Gallagher, Leah Ann Donoso, Christian Howard (collectively, Individual Defendants), Regal Assets, LLC (Regal Assets; with Individual Defendants, Regal Assets Defendants), Doe 1 Brink’s Incorporated (Brink’s) and Does 2-10 pursuant to a January 8, 2024 Second Amended Complaint (SAC) alleging claims of: (1) Fraud (False Promise), (2) Fraud (Intentional Misrepresentation), (3) Fraud (Concealment), and (6) Breach of Contract against Defendants Regal Assets, Gallagher, Donoso, Howard, and Does 2-10; and (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (5) Conversion, and (7) Breach of Contract (Third-Party Beneficiary) against all Defendants.

The claims arise from allegations that between August 24 and 25, 2018, Plaintiff Newland was enticed to and did purchase silver and gold coins from Regal Assets—a suspended California LLC that operated as the alter ego of Defendants Gallagher, Donoso, and Howard, alleged as co-conspirators, as well as agents of Regal Assets. Through Defendant Donoso, Regal Assets represented that it would secure Newland’s coins with Brink’s pursuant to an agreement through which Brink’s would store precious metals purchased from Regal Assets (the Precious Metal Storage Agreement or PMSA). Plaintiff Newland alleges having purchased the coins based on Howard’s representations as to the coins’ characteristics and based on the alleged agreement with Brink’s. Plaintiff Newland paid $15,007.67 for the coins, which were allegedly stored with Brink’s, but for which Regal Assets persistently failed to turn over account verification reports, which Plaintiff Newland requested on various occasions. Later, Plaintiff Newland requested that the coins be transferred from the possession of Brink’s to Newland, making his requests directly to Brink’s, and to Regal Assets’ agents Donoso and Howard. In October 2022, Brink’s finally responded to Plaintiff Newland, asserting that it had transferred the silver and gold coins to “Defendants ‘several months ago.’” The SAC implies but does not appear to directly state that Plaintiff Newland has not received his silver and gold coins from the Regal Assets Defendants.

The SAC attached copies of (1) an email from Brink’s showing it had possession of Newland’s coins on October 22, 2018, (2) an October 22, 2018 report by Brink’s, (3) an October 26, 2018 email from Defendant Donoso, (4) a copy of the PMSA, and (5) a written statement from Brink’s regarding Regal Assets’ relationship with Brink’s, the secure vault services provided by Brink’s, and the benefits of Regal Assets’ relationship with Brink’s.

B. Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Production

On January 31, 2024, Brink’s filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion.

II. Motion to Compel Further Production: GRANTED.

A. Request for Judicial Notice

Per Plaintiff’s request, the Court takes judicial notice of the existence of a federal civil action, No. 2:23-cv-8078, filed against various of the Regal Assets Defendants in this action, though the Court does not take judicial notice of the truth of the matters asserted in the federal complaint. (Mot., RJN, Ex. A; Evid. Code, §§ 452, subd. (d), 453, subds. (a)-(b); see Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 885 [documents are only judicially noticeable to show their existence and what orders were made such that the truth of the facts and findings within the documents are not judicially noticeable].)

B. Legal Standard, Further Production

A motion to compel a further response is used when a party gives unsatisfactory answers or makes untenable objections to interrogatories, demands to produce, or requests for admission. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)

To request further production, a movant must establish: (1) good cause for the production (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1); Sinaiko, supra, at p. 403); and (2) that a further response is needed because (a) the responding party’s statement of compliance with the demand to produce is incomplete Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(1)), (b) the responding party’s representation that it is unable to comply is inadequate, complete, or evasive (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(2)), (c) the responding party’s objection in the response is without merit or is too general (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(3); Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1127), or (d) if the responding party objected to the production of ESI on the ground that it is not reasonably accessible the movant can show that the (i) ESI is reasonably accessible or (ii) there is good cause for production of the ESI regardless of its accessibility (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (e)).

C. Court’s Determination

Around August 24, 2023, Plaintiff Newland served RPDs, Set One, on Defendant Brink’s. RPDs, Set One, contain thirteen requests for production. RPDs, Set One, Nos. 9-10 request production of all insurance policies with Regal as named or an additional insured entity, while RPDs, Set One, Nos. 11-12 request production of all insurance policies with Brink’s as a named or additional insured relative to the allegations in the First Amended Complaint. (Mot., Exhibits, Ex. A.)

On September 26, 2023, Brink’s served objections and verified responses to RPDs, Set One. The responses to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 9-12 contain long objections followed by a substantive response indicating that “[a]fter making a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry, [Brink’s] is not in possession, custody, or control of non-privileged responsive documents.” This substantive response is followed by a statement of compliance on all four responses. (Mot., Exhibits, Ex. B.)

Plaintiff argues that these responses are not proper because the discovery here is relevant and the responses provided by Brink’s are defective, e.g., “Brink’s is refusing to state whether it does or does not have any of these policies.”

The Court agrees with Plaintiff Newland.

First, the Court determines that the requests are relevant because they may lead to admissible evidence regarding whether Plaintiff Newland is a contemplated third-party beneficiary of the PMSA.

Second, the Court determines that to the extent that privileged information or trade secret information is contained in the insurance policies at issue, that information may be produced pursuant to a protective order between the parties, where such an order exists in this action. The same analysis applies to the objections based on privacy and confidentiality rights.

Third, the Court determines that the remaining objections to these requests are unavailing. The requests do not call for speculation or require an expert opinion on, for example, what agreements may be affected by the allegations in this action. And the objections based on inaccessibility and corresponding burden, equal availability, calls for legal conclusion, and violation of the Insurance Code are not sufficiently elaborated for this Court to rule in Brink’s favor.

Fourth, the Court determines that the substantive responses offered by Brink’s are defective for the same reasons. The statement that “[a]fter making a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry, [Brink’s] is not in possession, custody, or control of non-privileged responsive documents” is not sufficient in relation to RPDs, Set One, Nos. 9-12. (Mot., Exhibits, Ex. B, pp. 14-15, 16, 18, 19-20 [substantive responses at issue].) “[1] A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. [2] This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. [3] The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230, brackets added for emphasis.) Here, the above quoted response by Brink’s satisfies bracket [1], but it does not satisfy brackets [2] and [3]. Accordingly, the substantive responses offered by Brink’s are defective.

Based on the above, Plaintiff Newland’s motion is GRANTED.

 

III. Demurrer

A. Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212, superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) “[E]ach evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) Courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

A demurrer may only be used to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447 (Holland), superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

B. Analysis

1. Demurrer, SAC, Seventh Cause of Action, Breach of Contract (Third Party Beneficiary): SUSTAINED.

a. Relevant Law

“A contract is a voluntary and lawful agreement, by competent parties, for a good consideration, to do or not to do a specified thing.” (Robinson v. Magee (1858) 9 Cal. 81, 83.) “To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) Implicit in the element of damage is that the defendant’s breach caused the plaintiff’s damage.” (Troyk v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1352, italics omitted.)

b. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of Brink’s.

In its demurrer, Brink’s argues that this claim fails because the SAC does not sufficiently allege third-party beneficiary status or a breach of contract.

The opposition’s points and authorities focus on third-party beneficiary status without explicitly arguing which of the PMSA’s provisions were breached and how the facts alleged in the SAC, juxtaposed against the SAC’s exhibits (including the PMSA), show a breach of contract.

The only breach of contract allegation specifically alleged in the seventh cause of action is breach of the PMSA by removing or permitting the removal of Plaintiff Newland’s coins from Brink’s secure vault facility without authorization from Plaintiff Newland. (SAC, ¶ 94; cf. SAC, ¶¶ 86-93, 95-96 [remainder of cause of action not explicitly stating grounds for breach].)

As argued by Brink’s, this allegation is belied by the terms of the PMSA, which is attached to the SAC. (SAC, Ex. D, Preamble [naming Regal Assets as “Customer”] & § 3 [setting out the process by which the Customer may withdraw metals from the account with Brink’s] & § 4 [establishing that the Customer (Regal Assets) retained property of the stored metals such that the metals would be segregated from metals belonging to others, with Regal Assets accepting the PMSA’s terms as to itself and persons who have an interest in the stored metals].) Nowhere in these contractual terms does the PMSA impose an obligation on Brink’s to consult with third parties who have an interest in the stored metals prior to allowing Regal Assets to withdraw those metals pursuant to section 3 of the PMSA. The PMSA only provides that the metals would be released to the Customer—Regal Assets—upon following the proper procedure, and here, the SAC alleges that Brink’s released the coins to Regal Assets and no one else. (SAC, ¶ 34.) Thus, this factual ground for the breach of contract liability—releasing Newland’s coins without Newland’s authorization—is belied by the attachments to the SAC, with the exhibits superseding the allegations in the body of the pleadings. (Holland, supra, 86 Cal.App.4th at p. 1447.)

To the extent that the seventh cause of action relies on incorporated allegations for support (SAC, ¶ 86), those bases for breach of contract are not clear to the Court based on the incorporation alone, making the seventh cause of action uncertain insofar as it is supported by incorporated allegations rather than explicit allegations as with paragraph 94 of the SAC. While it is common practice to incorporate by reference allegations from causes of action or counts previously pleaded (Kajima Eng’g & Constr., Inc. v. City of L.A. (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 921, 931-932), incorporated facts should be germane to the alleged count to avoid problems such as ambiguity and redundancy. (See Kelly v. General Tel. Co. (1982) 136 Cal.App.3d 278, 285 [“chain letter” pleading, or wholesale incorporation of allegations by reference, should be avoided]; see, e.g., Philipson & Simon v. Gulsvig (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 347, 362 [for each count, P incorporated by reference all preceding allegations, which required court to sort through complaint to find allegations supporting each count].)

Brink’s demurrer is thus SUSTAINED.

The Court invites Plaintiff’s counsel at oral argument to point the Court to any other specific allegations of breach of contract in the SAC or, if there are none, to proffer specific facts that could be newly alleged to sufficiently state this claim in an amended pleading. If there are no such facts identified, the Court is inclined to sustain without leave to amend.

 

2. Demurrer, SAC, Eighth Cause of Action, Breach of Bailment: SUSTAINED.

a. Relevant Law

A bailment is called a deposit within the Civil Code. It is defined as “[a] voluntary deposit […] made by one giving to another, with his consent, the possession of personal property to keep for the benefit of the former, or of a third party.” (Civ. Code, § 1814.) Under California law, “a bailment for the benefit of both parties … is a bailment for hire, and imposes on the bailee the duty to use ordinary care with respect to the bailed property.” (Gerbert v. Yank (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 544, 551.) “A bailment ‘is made by one giving to another, with his consent, the possession of personal property to keep for the benefit of the former, or of a third party.’” (McKell v. Washington (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1491, citing Civ. Code, § 1814; see Gebert v. Yank, supra, at p. 551; see, e.g., McKell v. Washington, supra, at p. 1491 [“Plaintiffs allege[d] … [a] breach of bailment cause of action [when they alleged] that they ‘delivered to Washington Mutual, and Washington Mutual agreed to hold for the benefit of plaintiffs, money to pay the cost of underwriting, tax services and wire transfer services,” which Washington Mutual breached when it “paid only a portion of the money to the third party vendors and kept a portion for itself”].)

b. Court’s Determination

The eighth cause of action for breach of bailment relies on the same factual ground for liability as the seventh cause of action: harm to Plaintiff as a result of Brink’s permitting Newland’s coins to be removed from Brink’s secure vault facility. (Compare SAC, ¶ 100, with SAC, ¶ 94.)

The Court thus adopts its discussion as to the seventh cause of action to determine that because the allegations in the SAC and the PMSA attached to the SAC show that any release of the coins to Defendants was authorized by the PMSA, the Complaint fails to sufficiently allege Brink’s failure to act within the appropriate standard of care for a bailee.

Brink’s demurrer is thus SUSTAINED.

As above, the Court invites counsel to identify further allegations that have already been made, or could be made, before reaching a final decision on whether to sustain without leave to amend. 

IV. Conclusion

A. Compel Further Production

Plaintiff Stephen Newland’s Motion to Compel Defendant Brink’s Incorporated to Serve Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production (Set No. 1) Without Boilerplate Objections, to Serve a Privilege Log, and to Produce All Responsive Writings and ESI is GRANTED.

Plaintiff shall file a proposed order.

B. Demurrer

Defendant Brinks Incorporated’s Demurrer to Plaintiff, Stephen Newland’s Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED.

 Hearing Date:   5/13/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant Union Mutualista De San Jose’s Demurrer to Complaint

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Pursuant to a December 6, 2023 Complaint, Plaintiff Rafael Davila Madrigal, aka Rafael Davila, individually and doing business as 4 Wheel Auto Dismantler, and dba 4 Wheel Truck & Van Dismantlers (Davila Madrigal), and Alicia Brisila Davila (Brisila Davila)—both in pro per (collectively, the Davila Parties)—sue Defendants Union Mutualista De San Jose (Union Mutualista), Paulino Lopez (Lopez) as President of Union Mutualista, Rafael Barraza as President of Union Mutualista (Barraza), and Does 1 through 20.

(The Court refers to Plaintiffs Davila Madrigal and Brisila Davila as the “Davila Parties” for ease of reference where the parties stand both as plaintiffs and defendants, and where the Plaintiffs in the lead action share this surname.)

The Complaint alleges claims of (1) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED) and (2) Breach of Unfair Business Practices Act, § 17200.

The claims arise from allegations that the Davila Parties leased commercial premises from Defendants for the purpose of operating a car dismantling business and that Defendants engaged in conduct amounting to IIED and unfair business practices by: (1) increasing the Davila Parties’ monthly rent from $2,000 to $2,500 and then from $2,500 to $5,000 per month, with the dates of those increases not specifically alleged, for the purpose of vacating the property quickly for sale; and (2) demanding that the Davila Parties vacate the premises with only a month’s notice despite being on notice that the Davila Parties needed more time to remove heavy equipment from the premises, including the relocating of the Davila Parties’ car parts, heavy machinery, containers, recycling equipment and machinery, multi-dismantling machinery and equipment, vehicles, trucks, and car rim crushers, etc.

B. Related Case

On December 28, 2023, the Davila Parties—in pro per—filed a notice of related case identifying 23LBUD02282, Union Mutualista De San Jose v. Davila Madrigal, et al. (the Unlawful Detainer or UD Action), and 23STCV29782, Davila Madrigal, et al. v. Union Mutualista De San Jose, et al. (this, the Civil Action) as related cases.

On January 4, 2024, Department S13 of the South District, Deukmejian Court, granted a motion for summary adjudication filed by Union Mutualista in relation to the second cause of action in the UD Action.

On January 12, 2024, the Court related these actions, made the Civil Action the lead case, and assigned the two actions to this Court for all purposes.

On February 15, 2024, Union Mutualista filed an ex parte application seeking entry of judgment in the UD Action following its successful motion for summary adjudication.

On February 20, 2024, the Court entered the proposed judgment in the UD Action.

C. Demurrer Before the Court

On January 30, 2024, after this Court related the UD Action and the Civil Action but before Union Mutualista filed its ex parte application—Union Mutualista filed a demurrer in the Civil Action as against Plaintiffs’ Complaint. The demurrer principally argues that the Complaint’s claims in the Civil Action arise from conduct protected by the litigation privilege—i.e., filing the UD Action and raising the rent on the commercial property once the lease had terminated—which is not actionable conduct.

The demurrer attaches proof of service showing service of the demurrer on Rafael Davila Madrigal and Alicia Brisila Davila by regular mail.

No opposition appears in the record.

Union Mutualista’s demurrer is now before the Court.

 

II. Requests for Judicial Notice

Per Union Mutualista’s request, the Court takes judicial notice of the January 4, 2024, summary adjudication minutes and the January 8, 2024, proposed order relating to the January 4th order. (Demurrer, RJN, p. 2, Exs. 1-2.)

 

III. Demurrer

A. Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212, superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) “[E]ach evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) Courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

B. Court’s Determination

1. Demurrer, Complaint, First Cause of Action, Emotional Distress: SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

The Court finds in favor of Union Mutualista.

To determine the nature of a cause of action, California courts look at the facts alleged, not its label. (See, e.g., Saunders v. Cariss (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 905, 908.) Here, the first cause of action alleged in Plaintiffs’ pro per Complaint is “emotional distress,” which appears to connote an intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED) claim. (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶ 27 [“… extreme and outrageous …”].)

The Complaint’s IIED claim is premised on two grounds. The first is that, in order to sell the property that Plaintiffs are leasing from Union Mutualista, Union Mutualista raised Plaintiffs’ rent on the commercial property after Plaintiff’s lease ended (holdover tenants paying rent from month to month), with rent raised from $2,000 to $2,500 and then from $2,500 to $5,000. The second is that Union Mutualista filed the UD action for the same reason, i.e., to force a quick vacatur of the property for sale purposes. (Complaint, ¶¶ 25-26.)

Union Mutualista argues that the filing of the UD Action is protected by the litigation privilege, and the Court agrees.

The litigation privilege applies to IIED claims. (Action Apartment Assn., Inc. v. City of Santa Monica (2007) 41 Cal.4th 1232, 1242.)

The litigation privilege also applies to the filing of an unlawful detainer action. (Birkner v. Lam (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 275, 281.)

Thus, to the extent that the IIED claim relies on the filing of the UD Action as actionable conduct, such a claim is deficient as a matter of law, particularly where the UD Action was resolved in Union Mutualista’s favor. (Complaint, ¶ 25.)

Union Mutualista also argues that the raised rent allegations do not support an IIED claim where it is not clear from the face of the Complaint why raising the rent was a prohibited action in relation to Plaintiffs’ commercial lease. The Court agrees.

The Complaint fails to allege, for example, whether rent increases were prohibited by the parties’ agreements or local ordinance. Indeed, the Complaint instead alleges that when Union Mutualista increased rent on the property, Plaintiffs’ lease agreement had expired, i.e., they were holdover tenants. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) Moreover, the Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs accepted the rent increase and did not dispute it. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Thus, no basis is alleged for why the rent increases were prohibited and such contention is merely a legal conclusion.

The Complaint does allege that the purpose of raising rent and filing the UD Action was to pressure Plaintiffs to vacate the property so that Union Mutualista could sell it quickly. (Complaint, ¶¶ 13-20.) However, given that the Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs were holdover tenants, it is not clear from the face of the Complaint whether Union Mutualista’s desire to quickly sell the property amounts to actionable conduct given that Plaintiffs appear to have only been month-to-month tenants at the commercial property.

Union Mutualista’s demurrer is thus SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

2. Demurrer, Complaint, Second Cause of Action, Unfair Business Practices: SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

The second cause of action for unfair business practices is based on the same facts as the first cause of action for IIED. (Complaint, ¶ 31.) To state a claim under the Business and Professions Code section 17200, a party must identify an unfair business practice. And “[a]lthough the unfair competition law's scope is sweeping, it is not unlimited.” (Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 182].) Nor may a party avoid the bar of the litigation privilege by alleging an action under section 17200 where the conduct is the same. (Ibid.)

 As explained above, no specific explanation why the conduct of defendants was wrongful has been identified. For this reason, the Court adopts its IIED discussion to SUSTAIN Union Mutualista’s demurrer to the derivative claim of unfair business practices.

IV. Conclusion

Defendant Union Mutualista De San Jose’s Demurrer to Complaint is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend within 20 days of this Order. Failure to timely do so may result in dismissal of the action.
