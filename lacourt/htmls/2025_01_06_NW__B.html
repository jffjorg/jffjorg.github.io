DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV30622    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: B

 

Hearing:              Friday, January 6, 2025

Notice:             OK

Re:                         DASHIELL HAMMETT vs. SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA ALCOHOL & DRUG PROGRAM (19STCV30622) 

 

 

Petitioner Alejandra Tischhauser’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Alejandra Tischhauser’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a personal injury action in which Defendants Southern California Alcohol and Drug Program, Inc. (“SCADP”) allegedly owned, maintained, entrusted, and operated a daycare program without properly trained attendants. (See Complaint.) As a result, minor Claimant Dashiell Hammett (“Claimant”) suffered a skull fracture and brain bleed. (Ibid. at ¶ 70.)

 

On August 29, 2019, Claimant, through his guardian ad litem Sahar Malek, filed an action against SCADP for negligence and vicarious liability.

 

On May 3, 2021, Sahar Malek (“Malek”) filed an application and order for appointment of guardian ad litem for Claimant, which was granted.

 

On November 22, 2022, Petitioner Alejandra Tischhauser (“Petitioner”) filed an application to be substituted as Claimant’s guardian ad litem, which was granted.

 

On May 9, 2024, Claimant filed a notice of settlement.

 

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On October 4, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant petition for approval of claim or action or disposition of proceeds of judgment for minor or person with a disability.

 

Legal Standard

 

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.) 

 

Prob. Code § 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to § 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows: 

 

The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code § 3601(a).) 

 

In cases pursuant to Prob. Code § 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955(a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (CRC Rule 7.955(a)(2).)

 

Discussion

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $1,000,000.00. $2,625.76 will be paid to reimburse Medi-Cal. (MC-350 Item 12b(4)(a)-(c); Attachment 12b(4)(c).) Attorney fees are to be deducted in the amount of $250,000. (MC-350 Item 13a; Attachment 17a.) Additional costs are to be deducted in the amount of $26,385.20. (MC-350 Item 13b; Attachment 13b.) In total, Claimant will receive $720,989.04 out of the proposed $1,000,000.00 settlement amount.

 

Having reviewed the petition, the Court finds that the settlement is fair considering the risk of going to trial and the alleged injuries sustained by Claimant. Petitioner has satisfied the requirements under CRC 7.950 by disclosing information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise. (Mungcal Decl. Exh. A, Attachments 7 and 8c [medical records and invoices].) Counsel provides their retainer agreement which shows Petitioner agreed to a 25% contingency fee. (Mungcal Decl. Exh. A Attachment 17a.)

 

The remaining balance of $720,989.04 is to be transferred to a special needs trust (“SNT”) pursuant to Prob. Code § 3604. Petitioner requests an order for payment of funds to a special needs trust. (MC-350 Item 19; Attachment 19.)

 

Considering the request for a special needs trust, the Petition is denied for the following relevant deficiencies:

 

1.      Most notably, the proposed order cannot be approved because of the improper nature of the current petition which does not seek approval of the SNT instrument, appointment of trustee, and other issues. Los Angeles Superior Court Rule 4.115 requires that the court which has the underlying civil action review and approve the relevant SNT. Here, the SNT was reviewed and approved by a San Diego County superior court.

2.      Petitioner failed to provide notice of hearing and service of the Petition upon the Department of Mental Health, Department of Developmental Services, and Department of Health Care Services. (Prob. Code § 3611(c).)

3.       Petitioner failed to provide briefing regarding trust issues. Pursuant to Prob. Code § 3605(b), there are insufficient factual allegations in the Petition to approve the compromise and its attachments to support the requisite findings:

a.       The SNT beneficiary has a disability which substantially impairs the individual’s ability to provide for her own care or custody and constitutes a substantial handicap;

b.       The SNT beneficiary is likely to have special needs that will not be met without the trust; and

c.        The money to be paid to the trust does not exceed the amount that appears reasonably necessary to meet the SNT beneficiary’s special needs.

 

Therefore, the instant petition is denied without prejudice to allow for a new amended petition that seeks creation and funding of the proposed SNT by this civil department as the court that has the underlying civil action. The new amended petition should be filed after Petitioner files proof of an order vacating the San Diego County court’s approval of the SNT.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES without prejudice the petition to confirm minor’s compromise.

 

The Court further sets an OSC re Special Needs Trust and Minor’s Compromise for February 20, 2025 at 8:30am in Department B. 

 

Petitioner to give notice.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On September 27, 2021, Plaintiffs Adina Avila (“Avila”), Aaron Guerra (“Guerra”), and Margarita Chavez (“Chavez”)(collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Daniel Chan (“Chan”), Chanchenda Hou (“Chanchenda”), Chanvachana Hou (“Chanvachana”), Doe Attacker, and Does 1 to 20 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging six causes of action: (1) general negligence; (2) assault; (3) battery; (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (5) negligent contact; and (6) premises liability. Plaintiffs allege that, on or about October 29, 2019, Defendants negligently allowed Doe Attacker on the premises where Plaintiffs were invitees. Plaintiffs allege that Doe Attacker shot Plaintiffs several times without justification or good cause.

On December 8, 2023, Plaintiffs identified Carolyn Thao Nguyen as Trustee of the Nguyen Huynh Carolyn Trust dated October 11, 2018 (“Trust”), and Carolyn Nguyen (“Nguyen”)(collectively “Movants”) as Does 1 and 2.

On February 20, 2024, Movants filed an initial demurrer with motion to strike. On April 19, 2024, the demurrer was sustained in part as to Plaintiffs’ second, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action without leave to amend. The demurrer was also sustained as to the first and sixth cause of action with leave to amend.

On May 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint (“FAC”).

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On June 20, 2024, Movants and Defendant Chan filed another demurrer to Plaintiffs’ FAC. On August 1, 2024, the demurrer was sustained in its entirety.

On August 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their second amended complaint (“SAC”).

On September 23, 2024, Movants filed the instant demurrer to Plaintiff’s SAC.

Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer is a pleading that may be used to test the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10.) There are two types of demurrers: general demurrers and special demurrers. (See McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 77.)

General demurrers can be used to attack pleadings for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. (CCP § 430.10(e); McKenney, 167 Cal.App.4th at 77.) Such demurrers can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable; evidence or extrinsic matters are not considered. (CCP §§ 430.30 and 430.70; Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) To test the sufficiency of the cause of action, the Court admits “all material facts properly pleaded” and “matters which may be judicially noticed,” but does not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].” (Blank, 39 Cal.3d at 318.) It gives these facts “a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Ibid.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) "If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence." (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.) Special demurrers can be used to attack the pleadings on grounds that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible, or in a contract case, for failure to allege whether a contract is oral or written. (CCP § 430.10(f).)

CCP § 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due.  (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue. (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227 [“Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment. (Citation.) Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given."].)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer Requirement

On September 11, 2024, defense counsel met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel via telephone regarding the instant demurrer. (Reagan Decl. ¶ 3.) It was agreed that defense counsel would file the instant demurrer. (Ibid.) Therefore, Movants satisfy the meet and confer requirement pursuant to CCP § 430.41.

 

First Cause of Action

Movants demur to Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for negligence, arguing that Plaintiffs fail to state sufficient facts to support a showing of negligence. (Motion p. 3:6-7.)

To support a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must plead facts showing that a defendant (1) owed a legal duty to plaintiff to use due care; (2) defendant breached that duty; (3) this breach actually or proximately caused plaintiff harm; and (4) plaintiff suffered damages as a result. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.) “Ordinarily, negligence may be pleaded in general terms and the plaintiff need not specify the precise act or omission alleged to constitute the breach of duty.” (Lopez v. S. Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795.) “In order to state a cause of action for negligence, the complaint must allege facts sufficient to show a legal duty on the part of the defendant to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Bellah v. Greenson (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 614, 619.)

            Movants argue that Plaintiffs fail to allege sufficient facts showing that Doe Attacker’s violence was foreseeable. (Motion p. 6:15-26.) “[A] person ordinarily is obligated to exercise due care in his or her own actions so as not to create an unreasonable risk of injury to others. This legal duty generally is owed to the class of persons who it is reasonably foreseeable may be injured as the result of the actor’s conduct.” (McGarry v. Sax (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 873, 994.) Here, the underlying incident occurred when tenants at 2711 East 7th St., Long Beach, California 90804 held a birthday party for a coworker and several gang members arrived, opening fire, killing some individuals and wounding Plaintiffs. (Motion p. 4:3-9.) The Trust owns the property at 2711 East 7th St. (Ibid. at 13-15.) Nguyen is the Trust’s trustee. (Ibid.) In Plaintiffs’ SAC, Plaintiffs allege that Movants “knew of local gang activity in the surrounding area, based upon knowledge of prior criminal incidents at or near the subject premises including, but not limited to, gang-related assaults, batteries, and shootings, and failed to properly warn against or in any way attempt to prevent such activity from affecting reasonably foreseeable invitees of the premises, such as Plaintiffs . . .” (SAC p. 5 at ¶ 4.)

“[I]n the case of criminal conduct by a third party, an extraordinarily high degree of foreseeability is required to impose a duty on the landowner, in part because ‘it is difficult if not impossible in today’s society to predict when a criminal might strike.’ [Citation.]” (Melton v. Boustred (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 521, 532.) “In determining whether this heightened standard of foreseeability has been established, the defendant’s knowledge is critical.” (Ibid. at 536.) Therefore, to support a claim of negligence against a landowner whose property suffered from third-party criminal conduct, the plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the landowner had knowledge of prior criminal conduct on the property. (Ibid.) Here, Plaintiffs’ SAC fails to allege specific incidents of criminal conduct on Movants’ property that would make the underlying incident highly foreseeable. Therefore, Plaintiffs have failed to plead sufficient facts to support a cause of action for negligence against Movants. Movants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is sustained.

 

Sixth Cause of Action

Movants also demur to Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action for premises liability, arguing that Plaintiffs fail to state sufficient facts to support this claim. (Motion p. 3:8-9.)

To support a cause of action for premises liability, a plaintiff must plead facts showing that a defendant (1) was the owner, occupant, or lessor of the subject premises at the time of the underlying incident; (2) was negligent in the use, maintenance, or management of the premises; and (3) such negligence was the cause of injury, damage, loss, or harm to plaintiff. (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619.) Factors for determining whether a landowner owed plaintiff a duty of care includes the foreseeability of third-party conduct on the premises. (Garcia v. Paramount Citrus Ass’n, Inc. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1448, 1454.) Like negligence, a landowner’s duty related to third-party criminal conduct on their premises requires a heightened foreseeability based on prior criminal conduct. (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 245.) “Foreseeability must be measured by what the defendant actually knew.” (Margaret W. v. Kelley R. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 141, 156.)

Once again, Plaintiffs fail to plead specific facts showing that Movants knew of prior criminal conduct on the subject premises. Instead, Plaintiffs claim that Movants knew or should have known about “prior criminal activity at or near the premises.” (SAC p. 14 at ¶ 2.) Therefore, Plaintiffs have failed to plead sufficient facts to support a cause of action for premises liability against Movants. Movants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action is sustained.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

Plaintiffs have repeatedly failed to plead facts supporting causes of action for negligence and premises liability against Movants. Therefore, Movants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
