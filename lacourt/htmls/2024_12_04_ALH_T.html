DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV37991    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Strike

Case Name: Zenya Dennis v. Adam and Lilith, Business Entity Unknown
Moving Party: Defendant Omead Lavassani
Responding Party: Unopposed.
Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

This is a premises liability dispute in which Plaintiff allegedly went into a store intending to purchase an item and was stabbed by Defendant’s employee.  (Complaint, p. 4.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues the Court should strike certain paragraphs from Plaintiff’s complaint, including the following:

1. The words “punitive damages” at p.3, paragraph 14.a.(2). 
2. The words, “Willful Failure to Warn” at p.5, paragraph Prem.L-3. 
3. The words “willfully or maliciously” at p.5, paragraph Prem.L-3. 
4. The entirety of p.6, titled, “Exemplary Damages Attachment.” 
5. Alternatively, to striking the entirety of page 6, titled, “Exemplary Damages Attachment” the motion seeks to strike: 
a. The words “Exemplary Damages” at page 6 in the attachment title; 
b. The phrase, “Plaintiff alleges defendant was guilty of malice as defined in Civil Code section 3294, and plaintiff should recover, in addition to actual damages, damages to make an example of and to punish defendant,” at page 6, paragraph EX-1; and
c. The phrase, “The amount of exemplary damages sought is not shown, pursuant to Civil Code section 425.10.” at page 6, paragraph EX-3. a. 
6. Any and all other referenced to punitive damages and/or exemplary damages in any causes of action to which GREEN COSMOS INC. is a party.  (Motion, p. 2.)

Defendant argues this motion is made on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to properly allege recovery of punitive damages against Defendant. (Id.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition to this motion, which does not request leave to amend. (See, Plaintiff’s Notice of Non-Opposition.)

ANALYSIS

A court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter. Under CCP § 435, “[a]ny party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” “[P]unitive damages can only be awarded “where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the Defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.” Civil Code §3294(a). Civil Code §3294 defines malice, oppression and fraud as follows: (1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights. (3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material complaint known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Noonan v. Rousselot (1966) 239 Cal.App.2d 447, 452.)

It is appropriate here to grant Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages on the basis that Plaintiff has failed to allege specific facts of malice, oppression, or fraud. Conclusory allegations of fact are not sufficient to demonstrate conduct as oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious. (See Today's IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, 83 Cal.App.5th 1137 (2022) (granting a motion to strike punitive damages claims based on the plaintiffs' failure to allege specific facts demonstrating that the defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious, and instead relying on conclusory allegations.) Here, Plaintiff alleges facts that, in this context, are identical to conclusory facts, and are insufficiently specific to support his prayer for relief. (Complaint, p. 6.) Because punitive damages in this action are unsupported, Plaintiff’s request must be stricken.  Plaintiff’s notice of non-opposition to Defendant’s motion to strike does not request leave to amend.  (See, Plaintiff’s Notice of Non-Opposition.)

RULING

Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.

Motion to Compel Compliance

Cavanaugh Thompson v. City of Pasadena

Moving Party: Defendant City of Pasadena
Responding Party: Plaintiff Cavanaugh Thompson
Tentative Ruling: Continued

BACKGROUND

On November 17, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with Request for Production, Set One. (Declaration of Lucero Cordova Arellanes ("Arellanes Decl.") ¶ 2, Exhibit ("Ex.") A.) On December 18, 2023, Plaintiff served two unverified responses and objections to Defendant’s discovery. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. B.) On May 7, 2024, after multiple unsuccessful attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff regarding the perceived deficiencies in his responses, Defendant moved to compel Plaintiff's responses to the Defendant’s Request for Production, Set One. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 4.) Defendant’s motion was heard on May 21, 2024, and was granted as to Request for Production, Set One, Nos. 1, 2, 12–19, 23–26, and 30–31. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. C.) However, Plaintiff’s objections were sustained to the extent Defendant’s requests seek information contained within Plaintiff’s personnel records as defined in the Penal Code; Plaintiff was thus ordered to produce documents responsive to these requests, provide verifications, and "be prepared, should he stand by any Pitchess objection [to the remaining requests], to explain how any document or information withheld actually falls within [Pitchess protection]." (May 21, 2024 Minute Order, p. 4–5.) 

On July 11, 2024, Plaintiff served further unverified supplemental responses to the Request for Production, Set One, Nos. 1–3, 12–26, and 30–31, in which Plaintiff stated he was attaching documents responsive to Request Nos. 1, 2, 13–17, 19, 24–26, 30, and 31. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 6, Ex. D.) However, Plaintiff did not produce any documents. (Id.) For the remaining requests, Nos. 4–11 and 27–29, Plaintiff renewed his Pitchess objections, stating he had no further obligation to respond. (Id.) On August 29, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff an email requesting the missing documents, verifications, and a privilege log explaining his Pitchess objections. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 7, Ex. E.) Defendant received no response, and again emailed Plaintiff on September 13, 2024 to attempt to resolve the issues informally. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. F.) Without acknowledging Defendant’s assertion that no documents were produced, Plaintiff responded on September 14, 2024, that he would not be providing a privilege log. (Arellanes Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. G.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues Plaintiff's responses fail to comply with the Civil Discovery Act, and that Plaintiff failed to provide responsive documents as indicated in his responses. (Motion, p. 5.) Defendant also argues Plaintiff must explain how Pitchess applies if he continues to withhold responsive documents on Pitchess grounds. (Motion, p. 6.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s motion is untimely (Opposition, p. 4) and that Defense counsel failed to meet and confer in good faith prior to bringing the instant motion to compel further responses. (Id.) Plaintiff additionally argues a Pitchess motion and protective order is required. (Id. at 5.) Accordingly, Plaintiff maintains that he has complied with Defendant’s discovery, and that Defendant is not entitled to sanctions. (Id. at 8–9.)

REPLY

Defendant argues its motion to compel compliance with Judge Lofton's order is timely. (Reply, p. 4.) Defendant additionally asserts it met and conferred in good faith prior to filing this motion. (Id. at 5.) Defendant argues that a Pitchess motion is not required because the documents sought do not fall under the categories of documents enumerated in the Pitchess statutes, and that Plaintiff failed to comply with the Court's order to explain how any withheld document or information falls under Pitchess. (Id. at 5, 8.) Defendant accordingly maintains that Plaintiff's counsel should still be sanctioned for his failure to comply with Judge Lofton's order. (Id. at 9.)

ANALYSIS

The Court finds that the parties have failed to make good faith efforts to resolve this dispute.  Plaintiff claims that documents responsive to Request Nos. 1, 2, 13–17, 19, 24–26, 30, and 31 were served with the supplemental responses.  Plaintiff also claims that verifications were provided.  Defendant suggests that there are irregularities with the verification.  

As to Plaintiff's Pitchess objections, Plaintiff’s responses fail to state whether he has documents in his possession that were responsive and not subject to Pitchess protections that it appears Judge Lofton found to be applicable.  As such, regarding Request Nos. 4–11 and 27–29, the Court cannot determine whether Plaintiff: (1) has no responsive documents in his possession; (2) only has responsive documents that are protected by Pitchess; (3) has documents, some which are protected by Pitchess and some that are not; or (4) produced documents responsive to Request Nos. 4–11 and 27–29 that were not protected by Pitchess.

The hearing on this motion is continued to December 20, 2024 at 10:00 a.m.  Counsel for both parties are ordered to attend the hearing in person.

The parties are to provide a joint status report no later than December 16, 2024 addressing:

1. Whether Plaintiff produced supplemental responsive documents and a verification.
2. Whether Plaintiff: (1) has no responsive documents in his possession; (2) only has responsive documents that are protected by Pitchess; (3) has documents, some which are protected by Pitchess and some that are not; or (4) produced documents responsive to Request Nos. 4–11 and 27–29 that were not protected by Pitchess.

All counsel are reminded that the discovery statutes require serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution. Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1281.  All counsel are further reminded that the California State Bar advises that dignity, decorum and courtesy that have traditionally characterized the courts and legal profession of civilized nations are not empty formalities. They are essential to an atmosphere that promotes justice and to an attorney’s responsibility for the fair and impartial administration of justice.

Plaintiff’s counsel is reminded that pre-trial discovery is intended to allow a party to investigate the opposing party’s claims and to obtain the evidence supporting those claims.  Plaintiff’s counsel is also reminded that trial courts routinely exclude evidence that was requested but not produced during the discovery process.

To the extent that both parties agree that Plaintiff’s personnel file needs to be produced, the Court further suggests that the parties enter into an appropriate stipulated protective order to effectuate that goal without the need for unnecessary motion practice.


Case Number: 24NNCV01858    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Deposition

Oscar Herrera Perez, et al. v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc., A California Corporation

Moving Party: Plaintiffs Oscar Herrera Perez; Anabel Herrera Becerra
Responding Party: Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.
Tentative Ruling: GRANT, in part

BACKGROUND

This is a lemon law case regarding Plaintiffs’ purchase of a 2022 Honda Odyssey. Plaintiffs allege that shortly after buying the car, it began to experience serious defects and nonconformities with its electrical system. (Motion, p. 7.)  Plaintiffs allege they brought the car to an authorized repair facility several times, which failed to bring the car to conformity with its applicable warranties, and that Defendant refused to offer restitution or replacement of Plaintiffs’ car. Plaintiffs filed suit alleging violations of the Song-Beverly Act. (Declaration of Harita Nandivada, “Nandivada Decl.,” ¶ 2.) 

On August 29, 2024, Plaintiffs served Defendant with a Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian(s) of Record, which identified with twenty-six (26) matters for examination, including matters related to the purchase of the car, repairs to the car, warranty claims regarding the car, Defendant’s decision not to issue a buy back, and Defendant’s policies and procedures for responding to consumer complaints. (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.) The deposition was noticed for October 7, 2024, and requested Defendant’s counsel to provide alternative dates by September 16, 2024 if the October date did not work. (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. B.) 

On September 17, 2024, Plaintiffs followed up with Defendant regarding the requested deposition dates. (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. B.) That day, Defendant served its objections to Plaintiffs’ Notice of Deposition, informing Plaintiffs it would not produce a witness for the October 7, 2024 deposition. (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. C.) On September 27, 2024, Plaintiffs sent a written letter regarding Defendant’s objections, (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. D), and reached out on several dates via e-mail requesting alternative dates if Defendant or its witness was unavailable. (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. B.) 

On October 7, 2024, Plaintiffs sent Defendant a video link for Defendant’s witness to participate in the deposition. (Nandivada Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. B.) After Defendant did not appear, Plaintiffs followed up via email with Defendant for alternative dates for the deposition. (Nandivada Dec., ¶ 10, Exh. B.)

On October 9, 2024, Defendant offered three dates in May of 2025, which Plaintiffs rejected due to being too long after the originally noticed deposition date. After further efforts to find a mutually agreeable date for the deposition, the parties did not ultimately reach a mutually agreeable date to schedule a deposition. (Nandivada Dec., ¶ 11, Exh. B.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Plaintiffs argue they are entitled to an order compelling Defendant to produce a person most qualified for deposition because Plaintiffs satisfied the statutory requirement for bringing this motion. (Motion, p. 7.) Plaintiffs moreover argue the Court should award sanctions against Defendant for its willful abuse of the discovery process, as Defendant did not act with substantial justification. (Id. at 13.)

OPPOSITION

Defendant argues Plaintiffs’ motion should be denied as moot because Defendant has offered to produce a witness and documents. (Opposition, p. 4–5.) Defendant also asserts Plaintiffs’ meet and confer efforts were not made in good faith. (Id. at 6.) Defendant further argues its objections to Plaintiffs’ notice of deposition should be sustained because the notice seeks testimony that is overly broad, irrelevant, and unduly burdensome. (Id. at 7.) Lastly, Defendant asserts sanctions are not warranted against it, and should instead be applied to Plaintiffs for bringing this motion. (Id. at 8.)

REPLY

Plaintiffs assert they met applicable statutory and procedural requirements in pursuing a deposition, and argue that the topics of inquiry and requests for production of documents directly relate to the elements of Plaintiffs’ causes of action. (Reply, p. 3.)

ANALYSIS

I. Motion to Compel Deposition 

The Discovery Act allows “[a]ny party [to] obtain discovery . . . by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) Service of a deposition notice makes attendance by a party deponent and production of requested document mandatory. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) This notice must be served at least 10 days prior to the date scheduled for the deposition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.270, subd. (a).) If the deponent is not a natural person, the notice must “describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.230, 2020.310, italics added.) The deponent is then required to “designate and produce at the deposition those of their officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on their behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.230, italics added.) 

Where a would-be deponent fails to appear, proceed with, or produce documents for deposition in a duly noticed deposition notice, the noticing party may move for an order to compel said deponent’s attendance, testimony, and/or production for inspection of any document described in the deposition notice.¿ Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.230, 2025.450 (a). A motion to compel deposition attendance and production of documents must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the relief requested. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (b).) Good cause is established by a showing that the request is made in good faith and the documents sought are relevant to the subject matter of the action and material to the issues in the litigation. (Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 588.) 

If the court grants a motion to compel deposition and produce documents, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) 
In this case, Plaintiffs timely served Defendant with a Notice of Deposition which identified reasonably particularized matters for examination. To establish a prima facie case under the Song-Beverly Act, Plaintiffs must demonstrate that they bought a car from Defendant,  that Defendant provided a written warranty, that the car had a defect covered by the warranty, that an authorized repair facility failed to restore the vehicle to conformity after a reasonable number of opportunities to do so, and that Defendant did not promptly replace or buy back the car. (CACI No. 3201.) Plaintiffs also seek civil penalties against Defendant for its willful violation of the Song-Beverly Act. (Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (c).) 

Plaintiffs’ categories for examination are accordingly tailored to these issues; Plaintiffs’ listed categories of examination contain matters related to the request for repurchase of the car, repairs performed to it, warranty claims and coverage regarding the car, Defendant’s decision not to issue a buy back, and Defendant’s policies and procedures related to handling consumer complaints. (Nandivada Decl., Exh. A.) 

In opposition, Defendant contends that demanding a witness to testify on alleged failures, warranty repairs, and complaints related to other vehicles, e-mails and ESI, and TSBs are “not relevant to the claims or defenses in this case” and therefore that the instant motion should be denied. (Opposition, p. 8.) The Court agrees in part. The information sought regarding other vehicles may be relevant to the extent that the same or substantially similar parts exist in other vehicles.  Accordingly, the Court finds that the parties should further meet and confer in order to reach an agreement on the permissible scope of discovery on these issues.  

Despite granting Plaintiffs’ motion, the Court finds that sanctions are unwarranted in this case. Ordinarily, the Court imposes monetary sanctions in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the party the deponent is affiliated with; however, the Court finds “other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust” in this case. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) While the record reflects meet and confer efforts took place, these efforts do not justify sanctions. (Nandivada Decl., Exh. B.) Defendant here did not refuse to produce a person most qualified, but actively engaged in meet and confer efforts to schedule a date for deposition — offering to provide alternative dates in light of the limited availability of its person most qualified. (Nandivada Decl., Exh. B.) In stark contrast, Plaintiffs portray Defendant as a bad-faith actor who “has effectively stonewalled Plaintiffs from conducting crucial discovery without any substantial justification or even a showing in good faith.” (Motion, p. 10.) The Court fundamentally disagrees with Plaintiffs’ uncharitable characterization of Defendant’s conduct. The records reflects efforts by both parties to schedule a deposition which evidently reached an impasse — justifying the Court’s intervention. However, the record also reflects that the circumstances justifying the Court’s intervention, specifically regarding the nature of the parties’ meet and confer efforts, also justify the Court’s conclusion that imposing sanctions on either party in this case is not appropriate.

To the extent that this motion arises from the parties’ inability to arrive at an agreeable date, the Court orders that deposition of Defendant’s person most qualified be completed by the end of March 2025.

RULING

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel deposition is GRANTED. Deposition is to be completed by March 31, 2025.  Plaintiffs’ motion for sanctions is DENIED.
