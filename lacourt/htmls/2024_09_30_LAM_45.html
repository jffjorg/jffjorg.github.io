                              Defendants.

Motion to Strike Answer and First Amended Cross-Complaint of Defendant Autocom, Inc.            

The court considered the moving papers and opposition.

            The court CONTINUES the hearing for Plaintiff’s and Co-defendants’ motion to strike to November 12, 2024, at 8:30 a.m., in Department 45 to provide Plaintiff and Co-defendants an opportunity to comply with the meet-and-confer requirements of section 435.5 and Plaintiff the opportunity to remedy its suspension status with the Secretary of State. Background

This is a breach of lease action. On November 24, 2020, Plaintiff Rexford Industrial filed this action against Autocom, Inc. (Cross-Plaintiff), E-Freight Logistics, LLC (Defendant), Ehsaan Mesghali (Defendant), Ali Manale (Defendant), and Does 1 through 10. Plaintiff alleges causes of action for: (1) Breach of Lease Agreement; (2) Breach of Sublease Agreement; and (3) Breach of Guaranty.

The complaint alleges the following: Plaintiff owns real property at 16830 Avalon Blvd., Carson, located in the County of Los Angeles, State of California, 90746 (the “Property”). (Compl., ¶ 9.) Before March 5, 2020, Kingsview Industrial Center (KIC) owned this property. (Id. at ¶ 10.) On February 5, 2010, KIC entered into a lease agreement with Cross-Plaintiff leasing the Property for five years and two months beginning on March 1, 2010. (Id. at ¶ 11.) Cross-Plaintiff agreed to pay rent, various utilities, and taxes on the Property. (Ibid.)

On January 14, 2015, KIC and Defendant agreed to extend the Master Lease term “for an additional sixty one month [sic] period, commencing on May 1, 2015 and terminating on May 31, 2020” and incorporating all terms of the Master Lease. (Id. at ¶ 14.) On or about July 31, 2018, KIC, as master lessor, Cross-Plaintiff as sublessor, Defendant E-Freight Logistics, LLC, as sublessee, and Defendants Ehsaan Mesghali and Ali Manale, as guarantors, entered into a Standard Sublease Multi-Tenant agreement pursuant under which Cross-Plaintiff agreed to sublease a portion of the property to Defendant E-Freight Logistics, LLC for a term of one (1) year and nine (9) months, commencing on September 1, 2018 and ending May 31, 2020. (Id. at ¶ 16.) Under this agreement, Defendant E-Freight Logistics, LLC agreed to pay to Cross-Plaintiff a base rent of $7,556.50 for the first 12 months, then $7,783.20 thereafter until the end of the Sublease Agreement term, and pay 53.2 percent of the utilities owed on the Property. (Id. at ¶¶ 19-21.) On or about March 5, 2020, KIC executed a grant deed whereby granting Plaintiff the Property “subject to all other covenants, conditions, restrictions, reservations, rights, rights-of-way, easements, dedications, offers of dedications and other matters of record or that would be disclosed by an accurate . . . survey of the Property.” (Id. at ¶ 22.) Shortly afterward, Cross-Plaintiff defaulted under the Master Lease by failing to pay as agreed. (Id. at ¶ 23.)

On February 12, 2021, Cross-Plaintiff filed an answer denying 42 of Plaintiff’s factual allegations and admitting 31 of Plaintiff’s factual allegations.

On May 3, 2021, Cross-Plaintiff filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff and Co-Defendants alleging four causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Guarantee; (3) Conversion; and (4) Negligence.

On May 19, 2021, Plaintiff filed a demurrer in response to the Cross-Complaint seeking to dismiss Cross-Plaintiff’s cause of action for Negligence.

On February 18, 2022, Cross-Plaintiff filed an Amended Cross-Complaint.

On March 8, 2022, Plaintiff filed a demurrer and motion to strike Cross-Complaint’s cause of action for Negligence. The court overruled this demurrer on December 9, 2022.

On August 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to strike Cross-Plaintiff ’s Answer and First Amended Complaint. On September 5, 2024, Co-defendants filed a motion to join Plaintiff’s motion to strike.

 

 

Legal Standard

“The court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike “all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437, subd. (a).) Where the motion to strike is based on a matter of which the court may take judicial notice pursuant to Evidence Code sections 452 or 453, such matter must be specified in the notice of motion, or in the supporting points and authorities, except as the court my otherwise permit. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437, subd. (b).)

           

Judicial Notice

Courts may take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonable indisputable accuracy.” (Cal. Evid. Code, § 452(h).) Courts may take judicial notice of “acts and records of the Secretary of State[,] and it is not necessary to submit proof that a document has been filed with him to establish such fact.” (Gigax v. Ralston Purina Co. (1982) 136 Cal.App.3d 591, 601, fn. 6.; Cal. Evid. Code § 452(d)(2).) Courts may also take judicial notice of records and “acts” of administrative agencies. (Scott v. JP Morgan Chase Bank (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 752-753.)

Here, Plaintiff and Co-defendants request the court take notice of two exhibits as grounds for this motion to strike: (1) Secretary of State Certificate of Status – 05/01/2024; and (2) Franchise Tax Board Entity Status Letter – 08/28/2024. (Pl.’s Request for Judicial Notice – Exhibit “1” Secretary of State Certificate of Status (05/01/2024); Request for Judicial Notice – Exhibit “2” Franchise Tax Board Entity Status Letter (08/28/2024).) Exhibit “1” is a certificate of status from the California Secretary of State which bears the Secretary of State’s Seal and signature. (Exhibit “1”.) The certificate was generated on August 28, 2024. (Id.) Exhibit “2” is an entity status letter from the California Franchise Tax Board which bears the board’s logo. (Exhibit “2”.) Plaintiff and Co-Defendants present these exhibits to prove Cross-Plaintiff is not in good standing with the Franchise Tax Board and thus cannot properly defend itself in the current matter. (Pl.’s Mtn. to Strike, pgs. 4:7-27, 5:15-23.) Cross-Plaintiff does not oppose Plaintiff’s and Co-defendants’ request.

The court takes judicial notice of both the Certificate of Status and the Entity Letter status because both are records of state administrative agencies, found on the agency websites, and are not easily disputed. (Cal. Evid. Code § 452(d)(2); Code Civ. Proc. § 437, subd. (b).) Accordingly, the court admits both exhibits.

 

Evidentiary Objections (if applicable)

None.

 

 

Discussion

The Motion to Strike is Procedurally Deficient because Parties failed to meet and confer or attempt to meet and confer before filing the Motion to Strike.

            All motions to strike require parties to “meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference” before filing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) “If an amended pleading is filed, the responding party shall meet and confer again with the party who filed the amended pleading before filing a motion to strike the amended pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) The motion to strike must be accompanied by a declaration stating either that: (1) the moving party met-and-conferred with party that filed the subject pleading, and an agreement was not reached resolving all objections; or (2) the other party failed to respond to the meet-and-confer request, or otherwise did not meet-and-confer in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(3).) Although the Court will not deny a motion to strike based on noncompliance, the court may continue the hearing on the motion to strike until satisfactory meet-and-confer efforts have been made. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5 subd. (a)(4), (c).)

            The court finds Plaintiff and Co-Defendants fail to establish compliance with the meet-and-confer requirements under section 435.5. Plaintiff and Co-Defendants provide no proof that they attempted to meet and confer with Cross-Plaintiff before filing their motions to strike on August 28, 2024 and September 5, 2024 respectively. Plaintiff’s counsel’s declarations do not establish that the parties met and conferred in person or by telephone as required under section 435.5. Further, while Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration with Plaintiff’s motion to strike, the declaration does not state the parties were able to reach an agreement resolving the issues raised in the motion to strike. Instead, the declaration submitted with Plaintiff’s motion to strike solely specifies the steps taken to prove Defendant’s suspended status. (Uss Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.) Accordingly, the court does not find Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration establishes that the parties met and conferred in good faith in accordance with section 435.5.

Cross-Plaintiff’s Answer and First Amended Cross-Complaint are invalid because Cross-Plaintiff’s right to sue is currently suspended.

            Revenue and Taxation Code section 23301 “suspends” the “powers, rights and privileges” of corporate entities that do not pay “taxes, penalties or interest within a specified time.” (Cal. Rev. & Tax Code, § 7056; Grell v. Laci Le Beau Corp. (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1300, 1306.) Section 23301’s purpose is to “put pressure on the delinquent corporation to pay its taxes, and that purpose is satisfied by a rule which views a corporation’s tax delinquencies, after correction, as mere irregularities.” (Boyle v. Lakeview Creamery Co. (1937) 9 Cal.2d 16, 19.) Suspended corporations lack the “legal capacity to prosecute or defend civil action[s]” during the suspension and cannot defend themselves. (Grell, supra 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 1306 ; Casiopea Bovet, LLC v. Chiang (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 656, 652 (Citation omitted in original.).) However, a suspended entity may be sued. (Grell, supra, 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 1306.) A suspended entity may revive its right to defend itself “by filing all required tax returns, paying the necessary taxes, penalties or fees due, and applying to the Franchise Tax Board for a certificate of revivor.” (Corp. Code, § 2205, subd. (d); Benton v. County of Napa (1991) 226 Cal.App.3d 1485, 1490.)

However, “[a] plea of lack of capacity of a corporation to maintain an action by reason of a suspension of corporate powers for nonpayment of its taxes is a plea in abatement, which is disfavored by the law.” (Benton, supra, 226 Cal.App. 3d at p. 1491.) Pleas in abatement are generally disfavored because they “do not challenge the justness or merits of a plaintiff’s claim, but rather object to the place, mode, or time of asserting a claim.” (Center for Self-Improvement & Community Development v. Lennar Corp. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1543, 1552; Nevills v. Shortridge (1905) 146 Cal. 277, 278, 79 P. 972.) Thus, when a corporate litigant’s “suspended status” becomes an issue, trial courts usually “permit a short continuance to enable the suspended corporation” to remedy its suspended status. (Timberline, Inc. v. Jaisinghani (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 1361, 1366.)

            Here, Plaintiff and Co-defendants argue the court should strike Cross-Plaintiff’s Answer and First Amended Cross-Complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff has no right to defend itself due to a suspended status. Cross-Plaintiff filed its Answer on May 4, 2021 and a First Amended Cross-Complaint on February 18, 2022. Per the documents judicially noticed above, Plaintiff ’s legal status in California was suspended on May 1, 2024 for lack of good standing with the Franchise Tax Board. (Pl.’s Request for Judicial Notice – Exhibit “1” Secretary of State Certificate of Status (05/01/2024); Request for Judicial Notice – Exhibit “2” Franchise Tax Board Entity Status Letter (08/28/2024).) Plaintiff and Co-defendants argue Cross-Plaintiff cannot currently defend itself in motions or proceedings because it does not have the right to do so.

Cross-Plaintiff opposes the motion to strike, requesting a “reasonable opportunity to cure [its] suspended status.” (Plaintiff ’s Opp. to Mtn. to Strike, pgs. 2:24-28, 3:8-27.) Cross-Plaintiff argues the that the “normal practice” for cases like the instant action where a suspended status “comes to light during litigation” is to “permit a short continuance to enable the suspended corporation” to cure the defect such as paying back taxes and fees. (Timberline, Inc. v. Jaisginhani, (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 1361, 1366.) This would allow the Cross-Plaintiff to revive its corporate powers and validate its prior filings. Cross-Plaintiff presents a declaration from its CEO, Sang W. Kim, stating Cross-Plaintiff is already working with the Franchise Tax Board to restore its status. (Boyle, supra, 9 Cal.2d at p. 19; Declaration of Sang W. Kim [Kim Decl.], ¶¶ 1-3.) Cross-Plaintiff also estimates it will take two months to restore its status. (Kim Decl. at ¶ 4.)

The court finds that Plaintiff is not in good standing with the Franchise Tax Board under section 23301 and lacks the legal capacity to defend itself. Even though Cross-Plaintiff filed the motions while still in good standing, the timing of those filings does not entitle Cross-Plaintiff to the right to defend itself while suspended. Because the hearing on the motion to strike is September 30, 2024, and Cross-Plaintiff still has a suspended status, Cross-Plaintiff cannot avail itself of the rights and privileges of defending itself at the hearing.

However, considering Plaintiff and Co-defendants did not meet and confer with Cross-Plaintiff before filing this motion, Cross-Plaintiff has already begun the process of ending its suspension which will take about two months, and the motion does not address the merits of the case, the court will continue the hearing on the motion to strike for two months during which Plaintiff and Co-defendants must satisfy the meet-and-confer requirement and Cross-Plaintiff must remedy their suspension status.

Conclusion

Accordingly, the court CONTINUES the hearing for Plaintiff’s and Co-defendants’ motion to strike to November 12, 2024, at 8:30 a.m., in Department 45 to provide Plaintiff and Co-defendants an opportunity to comply with the meet-and-confer requirements of section 435.5 and Cross-Plaintiff the opportunity to remedy its suspension status with the Secretary of State. The court orders Plaintiff and Co-Defendants to file meet-and-confer declarations that comply with the requirements of section 435.5 no later than seven court days prior to the continued hearing date. The court orders Cross-Plaintiff to file declarations and evidence showing it is in good standing with the Franchise Tax Board and the Secretary of State no later than seven court days prior to the continued hearing date.

 

 

            It is so ordered.

Background

Plaintiffs James H. Tuggle, individually, and James H. Tuggle, as Trustee of the James Tuggle Family Revocable Living Trust, filed this action against Defendant Reshma Kamath on December 12, 2022. Plaintiffs allege three causes of action: (1) Malpractice; (2) Breach of Contract; and (3) Financial Elder Abuse. Plaintiffs engaged Defendant for legal services in April 2021 and allege malpractice based on the following factual allegations. (Compl., ¶ 4(a).)

Defendant represented Plaintiffs in a proceeding to stop foreclosure on the Olympic Hotel, a property owned by the James Tuggle Family Revocable Living Trust (Tuggle Trust). (Ibid.) Plaintiffs allege that instead of recommending or taking appropriate legal action, the Defendant filed a federal action personally naming James H. Tuggle, individually, as plaintiff and the Olympic Hotel and others as defendants, rather than the Trust itself. (Ibid.) This resulted in the defendants incurring legal fees and costs the trustee (here James H. Tuggle) paid. (Ibid.) In February 2022, Plaintiffs informed Defendant the foreclosure was now moot and requested Defendant dismiss the federal action. (Ibid.) Defendant countered that because Plaintiffs had not paid her attorney’s fees, she would not file a notice of dismissal. (Ibid.)

In June 2021 in a small claims case, Plaintiffs sought Defendant’s legal services for an appeal. (Id. at  ¶ 4(c).) Plaintiffs claim because Defendant never informed him when the appeal hearing was scheduled, the judgment was finalized against him costing him $7,791.70.  On December 20, 2021, Plaintiffs hired Defendant for a separate case to get a default judgment. (Id. at ¶ 4(b).) Defendant filed the motion for entry of default but failed to comply with Civil Procedure sections 473 and 473.5. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs allege that even though Defendant was informed of her deficiencies so that she could remedy them before the final hearing, she did not correct them. (Ibid.) The motion was denied, and Plaintiffs spent an additional $4,000.00 to substitute a different attorney. (Ibid.).

On February 21, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer and a Cross-Complaint. (Def.’s Cross-Complaint; Def.’s Answer.) Defendant’s Cross-Complaint alleges eleven causes of action against Plaintiff’s including Defamation, Malicious Prosecution, Breach of Contract, and Abuse of Process. (Def.’s Cross-Complaint.) The court struck all causes of action except the breach of contract claim.

Between March 2023 and July 2023, Plaintiffs propounded upon Defendant motions to compel various discovery responses. (Pl.’s Mtn. to Compel Responses.) The court denied these motions. On May 8, 2024, Plaintiffs propounded on Defendant Requests for Production. (Pl.’s Mtn. to Compel – Exh. “A” – First Set of Demands for Production of Documents.) On April 9, 2024 and July 1, 2024, Plaintiffs emailed Defendant requesting compliance with their discovery requests. (Pl.’s Mtn. to Compel – Exh. “B” – Emails from Plaintiffs’ Counsel to Defendant.) Defendant did not respond. On August 20, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this motion.

 

Legal Standard

If a party to whom requests for production of documents were directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300, subd. (b)-(c).) Section 2031.300 contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. The propounding party need not demonstrate good cause or satisfy a meet-and-confer requirement – all that needs to be shown in the moving papers is that a set of requests for production of documents was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403-04.)

The party who fails to timely respond to the discovery request waives “‘any objection’ to the request” including those based on “privilege” or “attorney work product.” (Ibid. (quoting Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, subd. (a); 2031.300, subd. (a)).) The trial court may relieve waiver if, the party demonstrates that “it subsequently served a response to the demand”; its response “‘is in substantial compliance’ with the statutory provisions governing the form and content of the response”; and “‘[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.’” (Id. at p. 404 (quoting Civ. Code Proc. §§ 2030.290, subd. (a)(1)-(2); 2031.300, subd. (a)(1)-(2)).)

 

Discussion

Requests for Production (Set One)

Plaintiffs move to compel responses from Defendant to Requests for Production of Documents (Set One).

Plaintiffs establish the following: On May 8, 2024, Plaintiffs served Defendant with Requests for Production of Documents (Set One). (Decl. of Robert N. Conrad, ¶ 3, Exh. “A”.) Plaintiffs allege they propounded by mail because on May 1, 2024, Defendant “demanded that all discovery be propounded to her only by mail.” (Ibid.) On July 1, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel emailed Defendant to follow up on the requests, which were due on June 12, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 4-5, Exh. “B”.) As of the date of this motion, Defendant has not served responses. (Id. at ¶ 5.)

The court finds these facts are sufficient to establish that Defendant failed to timely serve responses to Plaintiffs’ requested discovery. Plaintiffs establish that they properly served the requested discovery on May 8, 2024. Defendant had 30 days, plus five additional days because of service by mail, to serve responses on Plaintiffs by June 12, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.260, subd. (b), 1013, subd. (a).) Plaintiffs also establish that Defendant failed to respond by the June 12, 2024 deadline. Defendant does not challenge this motion, and thus waive any objections to the Plaintiffs’ requests.

The court therefore GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to compel responses from Defendant to Requests for Production of Documents (Set One). The court orders Defendant serve responses, without objections, to these discovery requests within 30 days of the date of this ruling.

Sanctions

Plaintiffs request $3,916.00 in attorney’s fees and costs for monetary sanctions against Defendant for this motion. (Conrad Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiffs’ counsel’s hourly rate is $595.00 per hour and Plaintiffs seek to recover 4.8 hours for this motion and $60.00 in costs. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs also seek $1,000.00 in sanctions under section 2023.050. (Ibid.)

Plaintiffs’ counsel’s hourly rate is reasonable, but the number of hours Plaintiffs seek to recover is unreasonable. This is a basic motion, and the issues raised in the motion, along with the absence of an opposition brief do not justify the amount Plaintiffs seek. Plaintiffs seek 2.3 hours for preparing this motion, 1.5 hours for preparing the reply, and 1 hour for preparing for and attending the hearing. (Ibid.) The court finds that reducing the total hours for replying to an opposition to zero hours is reasonable. The number of hours Plaintiffs seek for attending hearing is reasonable. As to costs, Plaintiffs seek $60 for filing fees. (Ibid.) The court finds this cost is reasonable. Thus, Plaintiffs’ total reduced recoverable amount in attorney’s fees and costs is $2,023.50 ([$595.00/hour x 3.3 hours preparing motion and attending hearing] + $60 filing fee).

The court must also grant Plaintiffs $1,000.00 in mandatory monetary sanctions. Defendant has not responded to Plaintiffs’ discovery demand “in good faith” and has provided no “substantial justification” for not responding. (Civ. Code Proc. § 2023.050, subd. (a)(1), (c).) Thus, Plaintiffs monetary sanctions include $1,000.00.

The court therefore GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions against Defendant in the reduced amount of $3,023.50 in attorney’s fees and costs. The court orders Defendant to pay $3,023.50 to Plaintiffs, within 20 days of the date of this hearing.
