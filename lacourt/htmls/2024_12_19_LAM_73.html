DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV34429    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: 73

12/19/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

ANIMAL PROTECTION AND RESCUE LEAGUE, INC., et al. v. GINOR, et al. (20STCV34229)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Bryan Pease (Pease Law, APC); David Tenenbaum (Law Offices of G. David Tenenbaum); John Maher (Law Offices of John Maher)

 

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Michael Tenenbaum (The Office of Michael Tenenbaum); Thomas Rickeman (Brown Neri Smith & Khan LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’s Motion FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed 11/21/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 4, 2020, Plaintiffs Animal Protection and Rescue League, Inc. (“APRL”), and Dina Kourda (“Kourda”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint.  The operative pleading is the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) which alleges causes of action for (1) Consumer Legal Remedies Act, (2) unfair business practices, and (3) declaratory relief against Defendants Michael Ginor, Izzy Yanay, HVFG LLC, D’Artagnan, Inc., D’Artagnan, LLC, Ariane Daguin, Andy Wertheim, Bella Bella Gourmet Foods LLC, La Belle Farm, Inc., Herman Lee, French Laundry Partners, L.P., Thomas Keller, Michael’s Gourmet Pantry, Inc.,  MGP Specialty Foods, J. Michael Stamm, and Cathy Stamm, Tyler Charman, Brian Redzikowski, Matthew Spencer, Kettner House LP, SDCM Inc., and Petrossian, Inc. 

 

Plaintiffs filed the instant lawsuit to enjoin the sale of foie gras, i.e., fattened duck liver, which is obtained from forced fed duck and geese, and which violates Health & Safety Code section 25982.  Plaintiffs also seek to enjoin false and misleading statements made by Defendants regarding the foie gras.  

 

On September 6, 2024, Defendants D’Artagnan, Inc., D’Artagnan, LLC, Ariane Daguin and Andy Wertheim (“D’Artagnan Defendants”) were dismissed without prejudice.

 

On November 21, 2024, Plaintiff APRL filed a Motion for Attorneys’ Fees, arguing:

·       The false advertising claims challenged in the complaint have been removed from D’Artagnan’s website, and D’Artagnan has stopped selling and shipping foie gras from force fed birds to California customers, including stating on their website that this is due to the sale ban. (Declaration of Attorney Bryan Pease (“Pease Decl.”), Ex. 10.)

·       Accordingly, injunctive relief is moot and Plaintiff dismissed D’Artagnan Defendants from the case. Plaintiff now seeks to recover reasonable attorneys’ fees incurred in obtaining this result, pursuant to CCP §1021.5.

·       Plaintiff asserts Defendants were violating the Force Fed Birds Ban. Plaintiff properly sought injunctive relief under the Unfair Competition Law (UCL). Plaintiff has standing under the UCL since “injury in fact” is demonstrated by showing that a defendant’s illegal activity has thwarted the organization’s core mission, or has caused it to expend resources it otherwise would not have expended in order to combat the activity.

·       Plaintiff obtained the relief it was seeking through Defendants complying with the law voluntarily as a result of the lawsuit.

·       Under CCP §1021.5, a plaintiff is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees if four criteria are met: (1) it is the “successful party”; (2) its lawsuit has enforced an important right affecting the public interest; (3) a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and (4) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make an award of fees appropriate. (CCP § 1021.5.)

·       Plaintiff asserts it has met these criteria and the fees sought are reasonable. Plaintiff seeks fees totaling $276,565.00.

 

In opposition, the D’Artagnan Defendants argue that:

·       APRL voluntarily dismissed the D’Artagnan Defendants from this action. (See Req. for Dismissal filed 9/5/2024.) As a result, the D’Artagnan Defendants are prevailing parties in this case.

·       APRL’s lawsuit was not a catalyst for D’Artagnan’s decision to stop shipping foie gras products to California and to revise its website.

·       Instead, discontinuing its remote sales of foie gras products to California and removing various language from its website were basic business decisions based on: (1) D’Artagnan’s physical entry into the California market with an in-state salesperson and logistics system for orders from California customers (see attached Wagner Declaration), i.e., which led it to avoid any potential risk of prosecution under California’s law banning the sale of certain foie gras products within the state; and (2) D’Artagnan’s performance of the terms of a settlement agreement it entered into with a different plaintiff in 2022 to resolve a separate lawsuit in the District of Columbia that was filed even before APRL commenced this one (see Ex. A to Daguin Declaration).

·       The D’Artagnan Defendants are Prevailing Parties under Section 1032.

o   Section 1032 of the Code of Civil Procedure provides that the D’Artagnan Defendants are the prevailing parties for purposes of costs, which includes attorney’s fees when provided for by statute. Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 1033.5(a)(10)(B). Specifically, section 1032(a)(4) provides that “a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered,” as well as a defendant “where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief,” is the “prevailing party.” Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 1032(a)(4).

·       APRL is Not Entitled to Fees or Costs Under Section 1021.5.

o   Here, APRL was not a successful party; it did not enforce any important right affecting the public interest; no benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and there was no need for private enforcement because the D’Artagnan Defendants’ alleged business practices have already been found to be lawful by the U.S. District Court and the Ninth Circuit in a case thoroughly litigated (and lost) by the California Attorney General.

In reply, Plaintiff argues that:

·       Defendants argue a federal declaratory judgment in another case provides that a sale does not occur in California in violation of Health & Safety Code §25982, “if conducted remotely from outside the state (e.g., online or by telephone).” (Oppo. at 4:7-8.) However, the federal case actually gives a lengthy list of criteria that must be met and does not give carte blanche to sell “online or by telephone.” The Declaration of Jason Wagner does not specify that each of these criteria was met in the online sales Defendants were engaged in. Thus, even if the federal ruling is correct as to California law—which Plaintiff disputes—Defendants have not shown their sales activities complied.

·       Plaintiff maintains it is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees under CCP §1021.5.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

Section 1021.5 provides the “explicit statutory authority for court-awarded attorney fees under a private attorney general theory” where the lawsuit “has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest regardless of its source – constitutional, statutory or other.” (Woodland Hills Residents Assn., Inc. v. City Council, (1979) 23 Cal.3d 917, 925, quoting Cal. Code Civ. P. 1021.5 (internal quotations and emphasis omitted). Under CCP §1021.5, a plaintiff is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees if four criteria are met: (1) it is the “successful party”; (2) its lawsuit has enforced an important right affecting the public interest; (3) a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and (4) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make an award of fees appropriate. (CCP § 1021.5.)

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

A.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff APRL moves for attorneys’ fees under CCP §1021.5.

 

Section 1021.5 provides the “explicit statutory authority for court-awarded attorney fees under a private attorney general theory” where the lawsuit “has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest regardless of its source – constitutional, statutory or other.” (Woodland Hills Residents Assn., Inc. v. City Council, (1979) 23 Cal.3d 917, 925, quoting Cal. Code Civ. P. 1021.5 (internal quotations and emphasis omitted).) Under CCP §1021.5, a plaintiff is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees if four criteria are met: (1) it is the “successful party”; (2) its lawsuit has enforced an important right affecting the public interest; (3) a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and (4) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make an award of fees appropriate. (CCP § 1021.5.)

 

The moving party bears the burden of establishing each prerequisite to an award of attorney’s fees under section 1021.5. (Ebbetts Pass Forest Watch v. California Dept. of Forestry and Fire Protection (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 376, 381.) If any one of the elements required for an award under section 1021.5 is absent, that alone will suffice to deny a fee request. (Satrap v. Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 72, 81.) The decision to award fees lies within the trial court's discretion and will not be disturbed on appeal absent a prejudicial abuse of discretion. (Baggett v. Gates (1982) 32 Cal.3d 128, 142-43.)

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiff is entitled to attorneys’ fees because it has not established that it was the “successful party.”

 

A plaintiff is a “successful party” entitled to a catalyst fee award if “(1) the lawsuit was a catalyst motivating the defendants to provide the primary relief sought; (2) that the lawsuit had merit and achieved its catalytic effect by threat of victory, not by dint of nuisance and threat of expense . . . and, (3) that the plaintiffs reasonably attempted to settle the litigation prior to filing the lawsuit.” (Tipton Whittingham v. City of Los Angeles (2004) 34 Cal. 4th 604, 608.)

 

To meet the catalyst requirement, a plaintiff need not show that litigation was the sole cause of the defendant’s acquiescence, only that it was a substantial factor contributing to the defendant’s action. (Hogar Dulce Hogar v. Cmty. Dev. Com. of the City of Escondido, (“Hogar”) (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 1358, 1365.) When a defendant has voluntarily provided the relief a plaintiff is seeking, “the chronology of events may raise an inference that the litigation was the catalyst for the relief.” (Id. at 1366.) “The appropriate benchmarks in determining which party prevailed are (a) the situation immediately prior to the commencement of suit, (b) the situation today, and the role, if any, played by the litigation in effecting any changes between the two.” (Id.) Where a plaintiff shows that a defendant’s voluntary changes occurred in the course of litigation, courts often infer the catalytic effect. (Id. at 1366-67.)   

 

Here, Plaintiff relies on the chronology of events to give an inference that the litigation was the catalyst for relief. In its Motion, Plaintiff sets forth the following chronology of events. On July 20, 2020, Plaintiff sent cease and desist letters to Defendants warning them suit would be filed if they continued illegally selling foie gras in California, including to restaurants for resale. (Pease Decl., Exhs. 15-17.) Defendants did not stop their illegal sales, so Plaintiff ultimately filed suit on September 4, 2020. Plaintiffs filed the instant lawsuit to enjoin the sale of foie gras and to enjoin false and misleading statements made by Defendants regarding the foie gras. In August 2022, Plaintiff saw that the false advertising claims had been edited and removed from the D’Artagnan website and sought confirmation from Defendants that they did not seek to re-add these claims. Defendants confirmed. (Pease Decl., Ex. 14.) However, documents produced in discovery showed Defendants continued selling foie gras to California restaurants at least as late as March 2023. (Pease Decl., Ex. 4.) Plaintiff subsequently learned in June 2023 from monitoring Defendants’ website that as of this time, Defendants stopped offering foie gras for sale to California customers, including a statement that such sales are prohibited by law. In November 2023, Plaintiff took the deposition of Mr. Wertheim, who testified there was no particular reason D’Artagnan decided to resume or stop sales to California at various times, and they were simply following the law and advice of counsel at all times. (Pease Decl., Ex. 3.) Defendants never told Plaintiff they had stopped offering foie gras for sale to California and never asked to have this case dismissed on mootness grounds. On September 5, 2024, finally confident this was a permanent change, Plaintiff filed and served a request for dismissal of D’Artagnan Defendants. (Motion, p. 7.)

 

Further, a plaintiff relying on the catalyst theory to be a successful party must reasonably attempt to settle the litigation prior to filing suit. Here, Plaintiff asserts it reasonably attempted to settle the litigation prior to filing the lawsuit because on July 20, 2020, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a cease and desist letter to D’Artagnan. (Pease Decl., Ex. 15, 17.) An automated response showed the message was delivered. (Pease Decl., Ex. 16.)

 

However, the Court does not find Plaintiff’s single cease and desist letter as a reasonable attempt to settle the litigation. As Defendants argue, there is no indication that anyone with decision-making authority ever received this letter — let alone that D’Artagnan, LLC, D’Artagnan, Inc., Andy Wertheim, and Ariane Daguin all received it. (Oppos., p. 9.) Plaintiff had sent its cease and desist letter to a generic D’Artagnan email address (wholesale@dartagnan.com) that is intended for customers to submit wholesale orders — and not an address for feedback or messages for D’Artagnan leadership. (Oppos., p. 9; Pease Decl., Ex. 17.) Thus, Plaintiff received an automated response to its email within one minute, stating “Thank you for placing your order with D'Artagnan.” (Id., Ex. 16.) The letter also demanded D’Artagnan, LLC, make a material change to its business practice, ceasing to sell foie gras in California, by the close of business two days later. (Pease Decl., Ex. 17.) Defendants argue Plaintiff did not give Defendants an opportunity to meet its demands within a reasonable time. Moreover, there is no indication that Plaintiff attempted to follow up or communicate with a decisionmaker at D’Artagnan. (Oppos., p. 9.) While Plaintiff contends that counsel for other Defendants who ultimately represented D’Artagnan wrote back to Plaintiff, this is irrelevant as counsel wrote back only regarding the other Defendants and do not mention D’Artagnan. (Pease Decl., Ex. 18.) Thus, there is no indication that Plaintiff followed up with D’Artagnan, LLC, D’Artagnan, Inc., Andy Wertheim, and Ariane Daguin.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find Plaintiff had reasonably attempted to settle the litigation prior to filing suit. As the court in Carian v. Department of Fish & Wildlife, (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 806, 816 stated, reasonably attempting to settle the litigation is not done simply by notifying the defendant of the grievance and proposed remedies. The court must consider all of the relevant circumstances in determining whether the plaintiff made a reasonable attempt to settle the dispute before filing the lawsuit.  (Id. at 816-17.)

 

Because the Court does not find that Plaintiff is the “successful party,” that alone will suffice to deny a fee request and the Court will not discuss the other elements of section 1021.5. (Satrap v. Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 72, 81.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

II. DISCUSSION

 

Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 372, any settlement of a claim made by a minor or adult with a disability must be approved by the court.¿If the court is satisfied that the settlement is in the best interest of the claimant, then the court shall approve the settlement.¿(See Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

A petition for court approval of a compromise or covenant not to sue under Code of Civil Procedure section 372 must comply with California Rules of Court, Rules 7.950, 7.951, and 7.952. The petition must be verified by the petitioner and contain a full disclosure of all information that has “any bearing upon the reasonableness” of the compromise or the covenant.¿(Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.950.)¿ The person compromising the claim on behalf of the minor or person who lacks capacity, and the represented person, must attend the hearing on compromise of the claim unless the court for good cause dispenses with their personal appearance. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.952.)¿¿¿ 

 

Defendants have offered to settle this matter in the total amount of $250,000. Of the total settlement payment of $250,000, the total settlement amount for Claimant/Lisa Otuonye is $3,000. The petition indicates that the adult Plaintiff, Peace Otuonye, is receiving a higher settlement because the majority of quantifiable damages were directly incurred by the adult Plaintiff, including: 1. All rental payments made for the defective premises; 2. Personal property damage to items purchased solely by Petitioner; 3. Lost wages and economic damages; 4. Out-of-pocket expenses related to the habitability issues. (Petition, p. 11.) Therefore, Petitioner represents that the settlement allocation reflects this, with the minor Claimant’s portion primarily representing compensation for discomfort and annoyance during their occupancy. (Ibid.) The declaration submitted by Claimant’s counsel states that counsel waives all attorney fees for services rendered to the minor and counsel does not request any attorney fees, costs or expenses from minor Claimant. Thus, the total settlement balance for Claimant is $3,000.00. The Court finds that the settlement is in Claimant’s best interests and approves the settlement. 

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise.

 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise for Claimant Lisa Otuonye is GRANTED.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Pro Hac Vice Application of Tessa Mansfield Hirte is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 12, 2024, Plaintiffs Martin Cardona-Alcaraz and Adriana Anahi Ramirez-Luevano filed a Complaint against Defendants Dal-Tile, LLC; Dal-Tile International, Inc.; Dal-Tile Distribution, LLC; Dal-Tile Tennessee, LLC and Mohawk Industries, Inc. (Compl. ¶¶ 83-93.) As a result of working with the stone products, Cardona-Alcaraz alleges he developed silicosis and other related injuries. (Compl. ¶ 94-99.)

 

The Complaint alleges claims for (1) Negligence, (2) Products Liability – Failure to Warn, (3) Products Liability – Design Defect, (4) Fraudulent Concealment; (5) Breach of Implied Warranties; and (6) Loss of Consortium.

 

On November 18, 2024, Defendants Dal-Tile, LLC; Dal-Tile International, Inc.; Dal-Tile Distribution, LLC; Dal-Tile Tennessee, LLC and Mohawk Industries, Inc. filed the instant Pro Hac Vice Application of Tessa Mansfield Hirte.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

CRC Rule 9.40(d) provides that an application to be admitted pro hac vice must state the following: (1) applicant attorney’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant attorney has been admitted and dates of admission; (3) a representation that the attorney applicant is a member in good standing in the courts of admission and is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (4) the title of each court and action in which the applicant attorney has appeared pro hac vice in this State in the preceding two years, if any; and (5) the name, address, and telephone number of the active California State Bar member who is counsel of record in the local action. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion for an order admitting Tessa Mansfield Hirte to appear pro hac vice is GRANTED. 

 

DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Pro Hac Vice Application of Tessa Mansfield Hirte is GRANTED.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.  In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) 

 

“The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) The Court may take judicial notice of its own file. (See Stepan v. Garcia (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 497, 500.)

C.      Allegations in the Complaint

 

On March 1, 2016, Plaintiff Star purchased a 1/3 share in a helicopter for $200,000.  Defendant Fleet owned the remaining 2/3 share.  On March 3, 2016, Plaintiff Star and Defendants Fleet and Wolfe Air Aviation entered into a written agreement for co-ownership of the Helicopter.  (Compl., ¶ 13, Ex. 1.)  Per the co-ownership agreement, Star and Fleet maintained a joint checking account to pay for fixed and ongoing expenses.  The agreement provided that “DBA ‘Six Whisky Alpha’” would manage the helicopter’s operating expenses.  Defendant Fleet controlled 2/3 interest and Plaintiff Star controlled 1/3 interest in DBA Six Whiskey Alpha.  In practice, the expenses were managed by Defendant Fleet’s employee with little or no input from Star.  Defendant Fleet assumed fiduciary duties as a partner to properly manage and account for the funds, expenses, and use of the jointly owned helicopter. 

 

On September 25, 2017, Plaintiff 2010 Leasing purchased from Star its 1/3 ownership share of the helicopter.  Defendant Fleet was notified of this purchase and registered the ownership with the FAA, thereby forming a partnership between Fleet and 2010 Leasing.  Both used or intended to use the helicopter for commercial, for-profit purposes, and both companies had the ability to and did participate in profits and losses and in the management and control of the operation of the helicopter.  Plaintiff Star provides Plaintiff 2010 Leasing with the operations and management of the 1/3 share of the helicopter.  In September 2017, Star obtained the FAA 135 Certification, which requires that Star maintain “Operational Control” of the helicopter and knowledge over all flights involving the helicopter.  Plaintiff 2010 Leasing proposed a new agreement for co-ownership of the helicopter to account for shortcomings in the 2016 agreement and to accommodate requirements of the 135 Certification.  Defendant Fleet refused to enter a new agreement.  Fleet’s president, Dan Wolfe, insisted and acted as if the 2016 agreement and its terms continued to control the partnership between 2010 Leasing and Fleet.   

 

On January 20, 2018, Dan stated in an email that most of the new proposed agreement looked fine and indicated his intention to end the partnership, standing “[i]t is clear to me that we should be engineering a partnership buy-out of some sort.”  Plaintiffs understood that Dan would be terminating the partnership, which would require the sale of the helicopter to a third party if the parties could not agree to buy the other out.  As a result of Dan’s proposal to work out a buy-out relative to the end of the partnership, Plaintiffs took steps to remove the helicopter from the 135 Certificate, which effectively meant they could not operate the helicopter or use it.  The manager of 2010 Leasing, Peter Starflinger, informed Dan that the helicopter should not be used until the partnership disagreements were resolved because the unilateral operation of the helicopter reduced its value, endangered the asset, and imposed on Plaintiffs risks and/or possible liabilities that were unreasonable.  Helicopters have a finite safe-and-useful lifespan.  Each hour of flight diminishes that lifespan.  Defendant Fleet and Dan continued, and continue to, fly the helicopter against 2010 Leasing’s numerous objections.   Defendant Fleet never provided any reports to Plaintiffs regarding the helicopter use, which prevented Plaintiff Star from maintaining Operational Control of the helicopter as required for its FAA Certification.   

 

On February 5, 2018, Dan emailed Starflinger stating the helicopter’s appraised value was $490,000.  Plaintiffs found the appraisal to be artificially low and/or inaccurate.   

 

On March 19, 2018, Defendant’s employee (Ginger Umutayn) sent Plaintiffs an invoice in the amount of $12,236.47 for “current maintenance/annual bill.”  Starflinger requested an accounting of the joint checking account and of the related use and maintenance records.  Umutayn refused.  Plaintiffs reviewed the financial documents that Fleet and Dan had previously provided and found unauthorized diversions from the partnership account without Plaintiffs’’ consent.  Although Plaintiffs requested information and more documentation, none has been provided.   

 

On April 1, 2018, Defendant Dan emailed Starflinger and Keith Harter (managing member of Star) that he was willing to purchase 2010 Leasing’s 1/3 interest for a specific value.  In the same email, Dan affirmed that he would sell Defendant’s 2/3 interest for the same value.  Later that month, Star obtained a fair market appraisal of the helicopter of $572,000.  Although Plaintiffs proposed listing the helicopter for sale and keeping it on the ground, Fleet refused and continued to use the helicopter and diminish its value.   

 

By mid-May 2018, Plaintiffs reviewed the helicopter records and found that the accounts were short at least $50,000 - $60,000.   

 

On May 15, 2018, Starflinger emailed Dan and asked him for a dollar amount.  Dan responded that Fleet would purchase the 1/3 of $490,000 (i.e., $163,334), but was also willing to sell the 2/3 interest for 2/3 of $490,000.  On May 18, 2018, Starflinger accepted the offer to purchase Fleet’s 2/3 interest for $326,666 and that he would have the check ready for Dan on Monday and put it in escrow.  Notwithstanding, Fleet refused to complete the sale.  Defendants continue to use the helicopter and diminish its value.  

  

Plaintiffs seek the following relief: economic damages, non-economic damages, specific performance of the purchase contract, statutory attorney fees and costs, punitive damages, post-judgment legal interest, and other relief deemed appropriate or equitable. 

 

D.     Procedural Defect

 

As an initial matter, Plaintiffs argues Defendants’ Motion must be denied because it was not timely filed.

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 437(c) provides: “Notice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing… The motion shall be heard no later than 30 days before the date of trial, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.  The filing of the motion shall not extend the time within which a party must otherwise file a responsive pleading.”

 

Here, trial in this matter is scheduled for January 21, 2025. The parties do not appear to dispute that based on that trial date, Defendants were required to have served and filed the Motion by October 4, 2024. While the Motion was properly served on Plaintiffs on October 4, 2024, the Motion was not filed until November 6, 2024.

 

The Court finds that Defendants’ Motion was not timely filed in accordance with the notice period for a motion for summary judgment under CCP § 437c(a)(2). The court may not shorten the 75-day notice period without the parties' consent. CCP § 437c(a) gives the court power to shorten time on other summary judgment time requirements, but not on the 75-day notice of hearing. (McMahon v. Sup.Ct. (American Equity Ins. Co.) (2003) 106 CA4th 112, 116; Urshan v. Musicians' Credit Union (2004) 120 CA4th 758, 763.)

 

Defendants assert that the Motion was not timely filed due to Defendants' counsel's inadvertent error in uploading the document. (Jamison Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

The discretionary relief provisions of section 473, subdivision (b) provide in pertinent part: 

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” 

 

As to discretionary relief, “[a]lthough a trial court has discretion to vacate the entry of a default or subsequent judgment, this discretion may be exercised only after the party seeking relief has shown that there is a proper ground for relief, and that the party has raised that ground in a procedurally proper manner, within any applicable time limits.” (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.) Thus, a party requesting relief under C.C.P. §473 must do so in a procedurally proper manner and requires a noticed motion. (C.C.P. §473(b).) Since Defendants have not applied for relief under section 473 in a procedurally proper manner, the Court is not inclined to grant relief as to Defendants’ late filing at this time.

 

Based on the foregoing procedural deficiency, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.
