[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

I.      INTRODUCTION

          On November 30, 2021, plaintiff Yanhong Xing (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Shunjun Zhang (“Zhang”), Meiju Group, LLC (“Meiju Group”), Huiyi Xie (“Xie”) and Queena Wei (“Wei”) for breach of contract, fraud, and unlawful immigration consultant practice.

          Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint on March 9, 2022. On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication, against Zhang, Xie, and Meiju Group. The motion is unopposed.

 

II.     LEGAL STANDARDS

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment must show that there is no defense by proving each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 C4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, it causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subject to its own burden of production to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.)

III.    DISCUSSION

Plaintiff relies on deemed admissions to establish every element of her claims. “[A] deemed admitted order establishes, by judicial fiat, that a nonresponding party has responded to the requests by admitting the truth of all matters contained therein.”  (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979.)  Requests for admissions “differs fundamentally from the other five discovery tools (depositions, interrogatories, inspection demands, medical examinations, and expert witness exchanges) . . . [because] admission requests seek to eliminate the need for proof: ‘The purpose of the admissions procedure . . . is to limit the triable issues and spare the parties the burden and expense of litigating undisputed issues.’  Sometimes, the admissions obtained will even leave the party making them vulnerable to summary judgment.”  [Citations.]  Matters that are admitted or deemed admitted through RFA discovery devices are conclusively established in the litigation and are not subject to being contested through contrary evidence.  [Citation.]”  (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 775.)

          Here, Plaintiff served Zhang and Meiju Group with Requests for Admission (Set Two) (“RFAs”) on November 15, 2023. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2.) On May 7, 2024, the RFAs were deemed admitted. (Ma Decl., Ex. C.) Plaintiff contends that the RFAs establish each element of her three claims for breach of contract, fraud, and unlawful immigration consulting.

          The Court notes that Plaintiff does not cite to any deemed admissions by Xie. Therefore, Plaintiff does not establish any of her causes of action as asserted against Xie. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.410, subd. (b) [any admission made by a party is binding only on that party for the purposes of the pending action].) Accordingly, the motion for summary judgment is DENIED and the motion for summary adjudication is DENIED with respect to Xie.

The Court thus proceed to analyze Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication with respect to Zhang and Meiju Group (collectively, “Defendants”) and Plaintiff’s three causes of action.

          First, for Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim, she must prove “(1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186. The deemed admissions establish the existence of an agreement wherein Defendants offered to assist Plaintiff with her EB-3 petition, offered to assist with Plaintiff’s employment as an officer or manager at Supermarket, and take all steps to file an EB-3 Petition on Plaintiff’s behalf. (UMF Nos. 1-6.) In exchange, Plaintiff would agree to pay $218,000 in addition to attorney fees, with $158,000 to be paid as a deposit which would be invested in the Supermarket so that Plaintiff could be employed there. (UMF No. 7-8.) Plaintiff establishes the element of her performance, breach, and damages because Defendants admit they received the $158,000 deposit but did not have any connections wth the Supermarket and could not employ Plaintiff as promised. (UMF No. 9-11.)

          Accordingly, the unopposed motion for summary adjudication as to the First Cause of Action is GRANTED as to Defendants.

          Second, for Plaintiff’s fraud claim, Plaintiff must establish the following elements: “‘(a) [the defendant’s] misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance [by the plaintiff]; and (e) resulting damage.’” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.)

Defendants have admitted they never intended to help Plaintiff with any of the terms set forth in their agreement and that they did not invest Plaintiff’s money into the Supermarket or take any steps to arrange for Plaintiff’s employment. (UMF No. 18, 29, 30, 31.) However, none of the RFAs are evidence of Plaintiff’s justifiable reliance. (Burch v. Gombos (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 352, 359 [RFA response preclusive only to the extent required by a literal reading of the RFA].) Therefore, Plaintiff does not show that every element of her fraud claim can be established. The motion is DENIED as to the Second Cause of Action.

Last, Plaintiff claims that Defendants unlawfully acted as immigration consultants in violation of Business and Professions Code section 22440, et seq. (Motion, p. 8.) Plaintiff claims that Defendants violated sections 22440, 22441(a)(3), 22441(a)(5), 22442.3, and 22444 because they admit that they were not licensed to act as immigration consultants by the state of California and were engaged in the business of proiding immigration assistance. (UMF Nos. 22-23.) Plaintiff claims that Defendants are subject to treble damages and that Plaintiff is entitled to fees and costs pursuant to section 22446.5. The Court addresses each statute that was allegedly violated below.

First, section 22440 states that it “is unlawful for any person, for compensation, other than persons authorized to practice law or authorized by federal law to represent persons before the Board of Immigration Appeals or the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services, to engage in the business or act in the capacity of an immigration consultant within this state except as provided by this chapter.” Therefore, this statute itself does not give rise to a cause of action.

Second, although Plaintiff alleges that Defendants have admitted that they are not licensed to act as immigration consultants, there is no requirement for a license set forth in the statutory scheme. Instead, it is unlawful for a person to act in the capacity of an immigration consultant unless the person has “on file with the Secretary of State a disclosure statement and a bond.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 22443.3.)

Section 22441 identifies the type of nonlegal aistance or advice that a immigration consultant may provide. Subdivisions (a)(3) and (a)(5) permit an immigration consultant to secure supporting documents or refer people who can “undertake legal represntation activities for a person in an immigration matter.” Therefore, the fact that Defendants performed those activities is not unlawful, without an admission that they are not authorized to do so.

Section 22442.3 states, in relevant part, that “[a]n immigration consultant shall not, with the intent to mislead, literally translate, from English into another language, any words or titles, including, but not limited to, “notary public,” “notary,” “licensed,” “attorney,” or “lawyer,” that imply that the person is an attorney, in any document, including an advertisement, stationery, letterhead, business card, or other comparable written material describing the immigration consultant.” Although Defendants have admitted that the agreement was prepared “with intention of misleading and implying that [Defendants] [were] [attorneys] in violation of Business and Professions Code section 22442.3, the intention to mislead is irrelevant if no such translations were actually made in the agreement.

Section 22444 identifies unlawful acts including making false or misleading statements to a client while providing services to that client. Here, Defendants admit that they told Plaintiff that she would be employed at the Supermarket even though they did not have any connections to the Supermarket, had no ability to fulfill that promise, and never took any steps to arrange for Plaintiff’s employment. Defendants also admit that they told Plaintiff on multiple occasions that her immigration petition was underway, and she had nothing to worry about, even though Defendants never provided any documentation or information to the immigration attorneys that Plaintiff was referred to and retained. Therefore, Plaintiff successfully establishes a violation of section 22444, which imposes a “civil penalty not to exceed $100,000 for each violation” on a person who violates the regulations for immigration consultants set forth in Chapter 19.5. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 22445, subd. (a)(1).) In addition, Plaintiff is entitled to “an amount equal to treble the amount of actual damages or one thousand ($1,000) per violation, whichever is greater,” as well as attorneys’ fees and costs.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 22446.5, subd. (a).)

In assessing the amount of a civil penalty, “the court may consider relevant circumstances presented by the parties to the case, including, but not limited to, the following: the nature and seriousness of the misconduct, the number of violations, the persistence of the misconduct, the length of time over which the misconduct occurred, the willfulness of the defendant's misconduct, and the defendant's assets, liabilities, and net worth.” (Bus.& Prof. Code, § 22445, subd (a)(2).)

Plaintiff requests a civil penalty of the maximum $100,000 but provides no explanation for this amount. Therefore, Plaintiff’s evidence of damages are limited to the $158,000 that she paid to Defendants for their unlawful services. Accordingly, for Plaintiff’s third cause of action against Defendants, Plaintiff is entitled to an award of $632,000, consisting of $158,000 in damages and treble damages of $474,000, as well as reasonable fees and costs.

VI.    CONCLUSION

          Based on the foregoing, the motion for summary judgment is DENIED. Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED in part only as to her First and Third Causes of Action against Zhang and Meiju Group. The motion for summary adjudication is otherwise denied.

I.            INTRODUCTION

Defendant Aurora Las Encinas, LLC (“Defendant”) moves for an order quashing a deposition notice for their person most knowledgeable. This action was filed by plaintiffs Rose Lee Chun Huang, individually and as successor in-interest of David Huang (“Huang”), Michael Huang, and Evelyn Huang (collectively, “Plaintiffs”). The deposition notice at issue requests Defendant produce a person most knowledgeable on 5 categories of testimony and requests Defendant produce 9 categories of documents.  

Defendant moves to preclude questioning and production of documents relating to: (1) Huang’s roommate’s admission to Defendant’s facility, (2) the roommate’s room placement while he was a patient at Defendant’s facility, including but not limited to his room assignments, discipline, monitoring, roommate assessments, and violence interventions; (3) Defendant’s knowledge of the roommate’s history before he was accepted as a patient, and (4) the “financials of the contract(s) between the County of Los Angeles and [Defendant].” (Motion, p. 2.)

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

“Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (a).) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Id., subd. (b).) Such an order may require that the deposition be taken at a different time, that certain matters not be inquired into, that the scope of the examination be limited to certain matters, and that all or some items designated in the deposition notice not be produced. (Ibid.)

III.        DISCUSSION

Defendant argues a protective order is necessary to “prevent disclosure of responsive documents protected by the roommate’s and other patient’s constitutional right to privacy, the healthcare provider-privilege, and sensitive financial information privacy, which rights must be weighed against other countervailing interests.” (Motion, p. 13.)

On July 1, 2024, the Court previously ordered Defendant to provide documents pertaining to the roommate’s physical and verbal altercations, as well as the roommate’s identity. The Court detailed the exception to the psychotherapist-patient privilege where the patient presents a serious danger of violence to others. (Evid. Code, § 1024.) Following that reasoning, as well as acknowledging Plaintiffs’ legitimate interest in determining the circumstances surrounding Huang’s death and Defendant’s potential culpability, the Court does not see an issue with inquiring about Huang’s roommate’s admission, room placement, discipline, monitoring, roommate assessments, and any “violence interventions”, as stated in the deposition notice, insofar as the information is directly related to Defendant’s knowledge of his violent or aggressive behavior. (Motion, Ex. A, pp. 2-3.) The extent of Defendant’s knowledge of any tendency of the roommate to be violent or aggressive is related to their culpability in placing him in the same room as Huang. Therefore, the motion for a protective order is DENIED in part to allow Plaintiffs to question Defendant’s PMK about Huang’s roommate’s admission, room placement, and Defendant’s knowledge about the roommate only as it relates to any violent or aggressive behavior.  

Defendant also wishes to prevent Plaintiffs from inquiring about the contracts between Defendant and the County for programs which place mentally incompetent offenders at Defendants’ facility. Defendant objects on the grounds of third-party privacy, the healthcare provider-patient privilege, and financial privacy.

Plaintiffs argue that good cause exists to pursue these areas of inquiry (along with the associated requests for production) because Defendant has a contractual and financial relationship with the County to take on the County’s most problematic and difficult patients, including those who were seriously disabled, violent, or placed in long-term mental health holds. (Opp., p. 4.) Plaintiffs claim that Defendant was trying to increase its profits by taking on problematic mental health patients from the County which it was ill-equipped to handle due to chronic overstaffing and undertraining. (Opp., pp. 7-8.) Plaintiffs’ claims of staffing and training problems are unsubstantiated by any evidence although Plaintiffs submit an email between Defendant’s CEO and the County in which the County suggests entering into a “special agreement with increased rates” for a patient who is problematic and assaultive after a request for placement was denied. (Opp., Ex. 7.)

Whether Defendant has a pattern or practice of accepting mental health patients with a known history of violent or aggressive tendencies, whether Defendant was or believed it was capable of providing care to those patients, and whether Defendant was financially incentivized to do so, is relevant and/or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence especially as to Defendant’s decision to place Huang in a room with a roommate.

Plaintiffs overreach, however, in demanding all documents and communications relating to Defendant’s contractual relationship with the County. The Court is not inclined to order the production of more patient-specific records. But Plaintiffs should be able to generally inquire about Defendant’s relationship with the County, its acceptance of patients with a history of violent or aggressive behavior, the number of those patients compared to the total number of patients it is accepting from the County, and any differences in the rates of compensation paid to the Defendant by the County for such patients and any differences in the allocation of risk borne by the Defendant and the County for such patients.

Last, Defendant argues that there are 2,000 encrypted emails between it and the County and that Plaintiffs should subpoena such records directly from the County. This is not persuasive. Defendant did not object on the grounds that producing those emails would be unduly burdensome or expensive to un-encrypt them, or that it does not have the means to do so.  Therefore, the request for a protective order based on the availability of communications from the County is denied.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Defendant’s motion for a protective order is DENIED in part to allow Plaintiffs to question Defendant’s PMK on topics relating to Huang’s roommate’s admission, room placement, discipline, monitoring, roommate assessments, and any “violence interventions” as long as the inquiry is related to the roommate’s violent or aggressive behavior. Plaintiffs may also inquire about Defendant’s contractual and financial relationship with the County and require the production of documents and communications, so long as the inquiry is limited to Defendant’s decision to accept patients with a history of violent or aggressive behavior from the County from 2018 to the present.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANT KIA AMERICA, INC.’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

I.            INTRODUCTION

On May 1, 2023, plaintiff Eugene Lee (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Kia America, Inc. (“Defendant”) asserting causes of action or negligence, strict products liability, and breach of express and implied warranty. The action arises from a motor vehicle collision that took place on or about October 7, 2022. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges he suffered injuries to his head, back, and lower extremities when his vehicle rear-ended another vehicle that abruptly stopped on the freeway due to debris. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges these injuries were preventable and that the injuries resulted from his body striking the interior of the vehicle after the airbag failed to deploy. (Compl., ¶¶ 7-8.)

On April 5, 2024, Defendant filed this motion for summary judgment. Defendant argues that the product at issue, a 2016 Kia Optima, did not have a defect that caused Plaintiff’s alleged injuries. (Motion, p. 2.)

Plaintiff filed an opposition brief on August 1, 2024.

Defendant filed a reply brief on August 9, 2024.

On August 16, 2024, the Court continued the hearing to September 20, 2024, so that Defendant could file the supporting declaration that was inadvertently omitted from its filings. Because the declaration had been served on Plaintiff, no additional briefing was permitted.

On September 19, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application for leave to file a declaration from his expert, Felix Lee, whose opinions had previously been submitted in letter format and not as an affidavit or sworn declaration. The Court permitted Defendant to make any evidentiary objections by September 26, 2024, and Defendant filed objections on September 25, 2024.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.) A moving defendant need not conclusively negate an element of plaintiff’s cause of action, but it is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence. (Id. at p. 854; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

III.        EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Defendant’s Objection Nos. 1 and 4 are SUSTAINED. The website page with information about a recall for 2016 Kia Optima vehicles and their frontal air bags is hearsay.

Defendant’s Objection Nos. 2 and 5 are SUSTAINED. The report from Felix Lee is not presented as a sworn affidavit or declaration.

Defendant’s Objection Nos. 3 and 6 are SUSTAINED. The email between counsel is irrelevant and constitutes privilege settlement communications.

Defendant’s Objection Nos. 7 through 15 to Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s separate statement and Plaintiff’s memorandum of points and authorities are not objections to evidence but an objection to argument.  Therefore, the Court does not rule on them.

Defendant’s Objection No. 16 to the Declaration of Felix Lee is OVERRULED.

Defendant’s Objection No. 17 to the Declaration of Felix Lee is SUSTAINED.

IV.        DISCUSSION

The facts regarding the underlying motor vehicle collision are undisputed. On October 7, 2022 Plaintiff was driving his 2016 Kia Optima east on the Glendale Freeway in the #4 lane 700 feet west of the San Fernando Road exit. (Def.’s Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) No. 1.) Dzhuleta Tolmoyan was driving a Land Rover in front of Plaintiff and, prior to the collision, the Land Rover was at a complete stop because traffic was at a standstill. (UMF No. 2.) Prior to the collision, Plaintiff was driving between 40 to 50 miles per hour when he attempted to slow for the traffic ahead. (UMF No. 3.) Plaintiff applied the brakes, but was unable to stop his vehicle in time to avoid crashing into the rear of the Land Rover. (UMF No. 4.)

Defendant premises its motion on two arguments: (1) that no defect exists and (2) even if a defect existed, it did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries.

First, Defendant argues that all of Plaintiff’s causes of action fail because Plaintiff cannot prove that the airbags in the 2016 Kia Optima were defective. (Motion, p. 6.) There are two tests to determine whether a design defect exists: the consumer expectation test and the risk-benefit test. These two tests provide alternative means for a plaintiff to prove a design defect and do not serve as defenses to one another. (McCabe v. American Honda Motor Co. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 1111, 1121.) Therefore, a defendant must show that a design defect does not exist using both tests in order to prevail on summary judgment.

The “consumer expectation test” permits a plaintiff to prove design defect by demonstrating that “the product failed to perform as safely as an ordinary consumer would expect when used in an intended or reasonably foreseeable manner.” (See McCabe v. American Honda Motor Co. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 1111, 1120 [citing Barker v. Lull Engineering Co. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 413, 426-427].) On the other hand, under the risk-benefit test, a product that meets ordinary consumer expectations “nevertheless may be defective if the design embodies an ‘excessive preventable danger.’” (Id., p. 1120-121.) Therefore, to prove a defect under the risk-benefit test, the plaintiff need only demonstrate that the design proximately caused the injuries. “Once proximate cause is demonstrated, the burden shifts to the defendant to establish that the benefits of the challenged design, when balanced against such factors as the feasibility and cost of alternative designs, outweigh its inherent risk of harm.” (Id., p. 1121.)

Here, Defendant argues that the failure to deploy the frontal airbags was not evidence of a defect and submits the expert opinion of Sarah Sharpe, Ph.D (“Dr. Sharpe”). Dr. Sharpe has a doctoral degree in bioengineering and a Bachelor of Science degree in mechanical engineering. (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 1.) Dr. Sharpe has conducted resarch and authored peer-reviewd publications on occupant kinematics and kinetics in planar impacts, and on the effects of frontal airbag deployment in lower-moderate and higher-moderate speed frontal impacts with belted occupants. (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 10.) Dr, Sharpe states that airbag deployment is not appropriate in all frontal or near frontal crashes because deployment of an airbag in lower speed collisions can impart energy to an occupant that might not otherwise have been present and increase the risk of injury. (Sharpe Decl., ¶¶ 16-17.) Dr. Sharpe refers to field accident data which reflects that “for belted occupants in low speed frontal collisions, airbag deployment does not substantially affect injury outcome for the head, spine, and thorax.” (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 19.)

Dr. Sharpe states that here, Plaintiff was involved in a crash with a maximum longitudinal crash delta-V recorded at 12 kph (7.5 mph) and maximum lateral crash delta-V recorded at 1 kph (0.6 mp). (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 14.) Dr. Sharpe states that airbag deployment at this crash severity with a 7.5 to 8 mph delta-V is not required to provide occupant protection. (Sharpe Decl. ¶ 20.) She also states that the risks of deployment in a crash with lower crash severity would  outweigh the benefits and such risks include: “(1) unwanted or inadvertent deployments from pot holes, speed bumps, curbs and road debris, (2) possible injuries from unnecessary deployments, and (3) the airbag being unavailable for a second impact which could be more severe and in which the airbag would be needed.” (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 21.)

 Based on Dr. Sharpe’s opinion, Defendant shows that no triable issue of material fact exists as to the existence of a design defect under the risk-benefit test.

Next, Defendant argues that the Kia Optima was not defective under the consumer expectation test. Defendant appears to argue that an ordinary reasonable consumer’s expectations are shaped by the owner’s manual for the Kia Optima, which states that “[t]here is no single speed at which the air bags will inflate” and that they are “designed to inflate based upon the severity of a collision and its direction.” (Lonnie Decl., Ex. I, p. 2.) The manual further states that a number of factors determine whether airbags will inflate, including, but not limited to, “vehicle speed, angles of impact, and, the density and stiffness of the vehicles or objects which [the] vehicle hits in the collision.” (Lonnie Decl., Ex. I, p. 2.) The manual also identifies low-speed collisions as one of the circumstances in which airbags will not deploy “because they may not provide benefits beyond the protection of the seat belts in such collisions.” (Lonnie Decl., Ex. I, p. 4.)

Accordingly, Defendant shows that no reasonable consumer could expect that an air bag would deploy in a low-speed collision because the owner’s manual identifies low-speed collisions as one circumstance in which air bags would not deploy.  

Last, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot establish that any design defect, if it existed, caused Plaintiff’s injuries. (Stephen v. Ford Motor Co. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1373 (“A product liability case must be based on substantial evidence establishing both the defect and causation (a substantial probability that the design defect, and not something else, caused the plaintiff’s injury . . . “).) Plaintiff testified that he sustained injuries to his neck and did not injure his head or chest. Defendant argues that air bag deployment would not have mitigated or prevented Plaintiff’s soft tissue injuries because the air bas are “intended to mitigate potentially serious to fatal head and chest injuries for belted, properly positined occupants.” (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 16.) Further, the potential for serious injury to belted occupants with or without air bag deployment is similarly small for the head, face, neck, spine, thorax, and abdomen in low speed frontal collisions. (Sharpe Decl., ¶ 19.) According to Dr. Sharpe, field accident data reflects that “seat belt use alone is an effective means of restraint” in lower speed events and that the risk of a serious injury is increased if the air bags are deployed. (Ibid.)

Therefore, Defendant meets its burden to demonstrate that Plaintiff cannot show that the nondeployment of air bags caused his injuries.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Dr. Sharpe’s declaration relies on “case specific information” and facts without foundation or a hearsay exception. Plaintiff never identifies what information or facts he takes specific issue with. The Court also disagrees with Plaintiff’s asssertion that Dr. Sharpe’s opinion is purely conclusory. Dr. Sharpe sets forth numerous factors and identifies multiple studies which she relied on in order to reach her conclusion and Plaintiff does not dispute any of them. Plaintiff also argues that summary judgment should be denied because a reasonable consumer could expect the frontal air bags to deploy.

Plaintiff offers the declaration of his expert, Felix Lee (“Lee”), a forensic mechanical engineer. (Ex Parte, Ex. 2, ¶ 2.) Lee states that based on the “frontal change in velocity of 7.5 to 8 mph”, he calculated that the likelihood of airbag deployment for Plaintiff’s vehicle was approximately 40%” and the collision severity “[fell] within the bounds of other real-world underride collisions where the frontal airbag have [sic] deployed.” (Id., ¶¶ 4-5.) However, Lee does not address any of the risks or benefits of the air bag system’s threshold for deployment in low-speed collisions raised by Dr. Sharpe. Lee also does not create a triable issue of fact regarding the circumstances of the collision because he, like Dr. Sharpe, agree that the crash severity of the collision was about 7.5 to 8 mph.

Plaintiff suggests the existence of a manufacturing defect by including an online printout of a document purporting to identify a recall for 2016 Kia Optima vehicles for frontal air bags. However, even if, as Plaintiff contends, a triable issue of fact exists as to the existence of a defect (either due to an air bag recall or as measured by an ordinary consumer’s reasonable expectations), Plaintiff offers no evidence to create a reasonable inference that his soft tissue injuries could have been mitigated or prevented if the air bags had deployed. Moreover, to the extent that Plaintiff claims he suffered additional injuries other than his soft tissue injuries which Defendant did not mention, Plaintiff offers no evidence of those additional injuries into the record. Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s separate statement refers to a medical report that is not attached as an exhibit. (Plaintiff’s Response to UMF Nos. 15, 18.)

Accordingly, because Plaintiff fails to show that triable issues of material fact exist regarding the element of causation, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

V.     CONCLUSION

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

I.      INTRODUCTION

         On January 23, 2024, plaintiff Antonio Guachiac Carac (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Kenny Wan (“Defendant”), Consolidated Disposal Service, LLC dba Republic Waste Servies of Southern California, LLC (“Republic”), Doe Driver, Tang Lang Shue (“Trustee”) as trustee for the Tang Lang Shue Family Trust (“Trust”), Tse Realty and Management Servies (“Tse Management”), and Timothy Tse (“Tse”).

Plaintiff alleges he was hit by a vehicle owned by Republic and operated by Doe Driver, a Republic employee working in the course and scope of his employment. (Compl., ¶¶ 17-18.) Due to the collision, Plaintiff’s right foot was wounded when it became entangled inside the drain cleaning equipment that he was using. (Compl., ¶ 18.) The wound subsequently became infected and on January 15, 2023, Plaintiff’s right foot was amputated. (Compl., ¶ 18.)

          Plaintiff asserts two causes of action for: (1) vehicular negligence (against Republic and Doe Driver), and (2) violation of Labor Code section 3706 (against Defendant, Trustee, Tse Management, and Tse) for failing to carry workers’ compensation insurance.

          On June 11, 2024, Defendant filed this demurrer to the sole cause of action asserted against him for violation of Labor Code section 3706.

          On September 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief.

          No reply brief is on file with the Court.

II.     LEGAL STANDARDS

A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed. [Citation.]” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

A demurrer may also be brought on the grounds that it is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Where the complaint contains substantial factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty will be overruled or plaintiff will be given leave to amend. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)

III.    DISCUSSION

          As an initial matter, the Court addresses Plaintiff’s claim that Defendant’s demurrer is untimely. A proof of service of the summons and complaint shows that Defendant was served on April 13, 2024. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s demurrer had to be filed by May 13, 2024, since a defendant may demur within the same period of time it has to answer the complaint—i.e., 30 days after service, unless extended by stipulation or court order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40, subd. (a).) This demurrer was filed on June 11, 2024, along with a declaration by defense counsel, Frank Hwu. Defense counsel does not argue that Defendant is entitled to an automatic 30-day extension of the deadline to file a demurrer because he does not state that he made a good faith attempt to meet and confer at least 5 days before the demurrer was due, nor could he, since he was retained on May 17, 2024, four days after the deadline to file a responsive pleading. (Hwu Decl., ¶ 4.) Therefore, it does not appear that the demurrer is timely. Nevertheless, an untimely demurrer may be considered by the court in its discretion. (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 CA4th 742, 750). Here, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the demurrer and proceeds to analyze it on its merits.       

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s second cause of action on the grounds that it fails to state sufficient facts. Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot maintain this lawsuit due to the worker’s compensation exclusivity doctrine since Plaintiff alleged that he was injured “on the job.” (Demurrer, p. 4.) Defendant also argues that Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendant was an “employer” within the meaning of the Labor Code are deficient because there are no allegations describing the “scope and nature of the work Plaintiff was hired to do,” “when [Plaintiff] was hired,” or whether Defendant even compensated Plaintiff. (Demurrer, p. 4.) Defendant contends that although Plaintiff alleges that he did not carry worker’s compensation insurance, Plaintiff’s second cause of action would be defeated if Plaintiff was a licensed contractor or self-insured. (Demurrer, p. 4.)

          Defendant’s demurrer on this ground is OVERRULED. Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient because he details the circumstances of his employment and injury and pleads the ultimate facts necessary to state a cause of action under Labor Code section 3600. (See Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550). Critically, Plaintiff alleges that he was employed by Defendant, Trustee, Tse, and Tse Management and that none of them carried worker’s compensation. (Compl., ¶¶ 27, 29.) Plaintiff alleges he was employed to clean out a drain using mechanized equipment when he was hit by a vehicle which resulted in his injuries. (Compl., ¶ 28.) Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant, along with Trustee, Tse, and Tse Management, were negligent in their supervision of him and negligently instructed him. (Compl., ¶ 31.) Plaintiff also alleges that he was not provided an adequate and safe work environment because he was working in the “zone of danger of vehicles” and was not given “a safe drain cleaning equipment/machine.” (Compl., ¶ 31.)

          As for Defendant’s demurrer on the grounds of uncertainty, it is also OVERRULED. The Complaint is not vague and ambiguous and further, any uncertainty or ambiguities can be clarified through discovery. (See Khoury, supra, 14 Cal.App.4th at p. 616.)

IV.    CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED in its entirety. Defendant is to file an answer within 20 days of the date of this Order.
