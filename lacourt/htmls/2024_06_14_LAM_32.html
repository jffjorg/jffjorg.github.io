DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV09930    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: 32

 

ALEXANDER KHAZAI,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

SAMMY ZABLEN, et al.

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.: 23STCV09930

  Hearing Date: June 14, 2024

 

   [TENTATIVE] order RE:

(1)   Demurrer to first amended complaint

(2)   Motion to strike portions of the first amended complaint

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 3, 2023, Plaintiff Alexander Khazai filed this action against numerous Defendants, asserting 16 causes of action.

On January 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (FAC) against Sammy Zablen, and Sammy’s Warehouse (“Sammy’s”; collectively with Zablen, “Zablen Parties”), among others, alleging four causes of action for: (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) fraudulent inducement and promissory fraud, and (4) accounting.

The FAC alleges that using his connections, Plaintiff provided services to Defendants as a broker, partner and facilitator in the purchase and sale of an estimated $500 million worth of masks during the COVID-19 outbreak of 2020. (FAC, ¶ 1.)

 

The FAC further alleges that the Zablen Parties convinced Plaintiff to invest his life savings in their joint enterprise, and that Plaintiff provided $100,000 in seed capital to purchase the initial supply of masks. (Id., ¶¶ 4-5.)

Plaintiff claims that the Zablen Parties orally promised to pay Plaintiff commissions on mask sales to Plaintiff’s contacts and customers and any sales worked on by Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 7.) However, Plaintiff alleges that after many months of work by Plaintiff, the Zablen Parties only paid Plaintiff $2,000, notwithstanding Plaintiff’s $100,000 investment and all of the deals that were consummated using Plaintiff’s contacts and services. (Id., ¶ 8.)

 

In addition, the FAC alleges that the Zablen Parties agreed in writing that they would not circumvent Plaintiff in any deals Plaintiff worked on or consummated.  (Id., ¶ 11; Exh. A.) The Zablen Parties intentionally circumvented Plaintiff by contacting and transacting business with Plaintiff’s customers without the knowledge of Plaintiff and cutting him out of financial rewards he earned. (Id., ¶ 12.) Further, the FAC alleges that the Zablen Parties interfered with Plaintiff’s business relationships by telling Sammy’s Warehouse salespeople not to discuss with Plaintiff the deals they were working on. (Id.)

 

On May 13, 2024, Defendant Sammy’s Warehouse filed a demurrer with motion to strike. On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On June 7, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

 

DEMURRER

A.    Legal Standard

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not on the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.) 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s first through fourth causes of action, arguing they fail to state sufficient facts.

 

1.     First Cause of Action (Breach of Written Contract)

      The elements for a breach of contract cause of action are: (1) the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) resulting damages.¿ (Reichert v. General Ins. Co.¿(1968) 68 Cal.2d 822, 830;¿Hale v. Sharp Healthcare¿(2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1373, 1387.) Although a written contract is usually pleaded by alleging its making and attaching a copy which is incorporated by reference, a written contract can also be pleaded by alleging the making and the substance of the relevant terms. (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199; Perry v. Robertson (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 333, 341.)

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiff references the Non-Disclosure/Non-Circumvention Agreement as the “valid and binding contract” upon which the claim is based, but he does not specifically allege the material terms of that agreement that address Defendant’s alleged obligation to pay commissions to Plaintiff.

 

However, the FAC alleges that Defendant and Plaintiff agreed in writing that Defendant would not circumvent Plaintiff in any deals Plaintiff worked on or consummated.  (FAC., ¶ 11; Exh. A.) The written contract is not based upon the alleged failure to pay commissions. Further, Defendant contends that Plaintiff has not pled the key terms of the contract, but Plaintiff has attached the contract to the FAC. (FAC, Exh. A.)

 

Next, Plaintiff has pled his own performance by alleging he “did all of the material things he was required to do including, but not limited to, providing Sammy’s Warehouse/Zablen Parties with $100,000 in seed money.” (FAC, ¶ 37.)

 

Plaintiff has also pled that Defendant breached the contract by circumventing Plaintiff by contacting and transacting business with Plaintiff’s customers without the knowledge of Plaintiff and cutting him out of financial rewards he earned. (Id., ¶ 12.) Further, the FAC alleges that the Zablen Parties interfered with Plaintiff’s business relationships by telling Sammy’s Warehouse salespeople not to discuss with Plaintiff the deals they were working on. (Id.)

 

Lastly, the FAC sufficiently alleges that Defendant’s breach caused Plaintiff damages in that he had initially invested his life savings of $100,000 for only a fraction of what was promised to him orally, due to Defendant’s circumventing Plaintiff in deals so that they did not have to pay him commissions. (FAC, ¶¶ 4, 7, 8, 12.)

 

As a result, the demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of written contract is overruled.

2. Second Cause of Action (Breach of Oral Contract and De Facto Partnership)

The elements of a breach of oral contract claim are the same as those for a breach of written contract: (1) a contract; (2) its performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) breach; and (4) damages.  (Stockton Mortgage, Inc. v. Tope (2014) 233 Cal.App.4th 437, 453.) An oral contract may be pleaded generally as to its effect, because alleging the exact words is rarely possible. (Scolinos v Kolts (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 635, 640-641.) 

 

Plaintiff sufficiently alleged that the Zablen Parties orally promised to pay Plaintiff commissions on mask sales to Plaintiff’s contacts and customers and any sales worked on by Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 7.) As noted above, Plaintiff has sufficiently pled his own performance by alleging he “did all of the material things he was required to do including, but not limited to, providing Sammy’s Warehouse/Zablen Parties with $100,000 in seed money.” (FAC, ¶ 37.)

 

As for breach, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges that after many months of work by Plaintiff, the Zablen Parties only paid Plaintiff $2,000, notwithstanding Plaintiff’s $100,000 investment and all of the deals that were consummated using Plaintiff’s contacts and services. (Id., ¶ 8.)

 

The demurrer to the second cause of action is therefore overruled.

3. Third Cause of Action (Fraudulent Inducement and Promissory Fraud)

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.) “Fraud must be pleaded with specificity rather than with ‘“‘general and conclusory allegations.’”’ (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) The specificity requirement means a plaintiff must allege facts showing how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were made, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, the plaintiff must allege the names of the persons who made the representations, their authority to speak on behalf of the corporation, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when the representation was made. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645; West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 793.) 

 “The facts essential to the statement of a cause of action in fraud or deceit based on a promise made without any intention of performing it are: (1) a promise made regarding a material fact without any intention of performing it; (2) the existence of the intent at the time of making the promise; (3) the promise was made with intent to deceive or with intent to induce the party to whom it was made to enter into the transaction; (4) the promise was relied on by the party to whom it was made; (5) the party making the promise did not perform; (6) the party to whom the promise was made was injured.” (Regus v. Schartkoff (1957) 156 Cal.App.2d 382, 389.) 

The court agrees with Defendant that the allegations are not specifically pled.

Plaintiff only alleges that Zablen promised (i) that he would pay Plaintiff commissions; (ii) that he would not circumvent plaintiff and cut him out of deals he participated in; (iii) that he would not transact business with the individuals and or business entities introduced by Plaintiff or worked on by Plaintiff or any deals whose Plaintiff’s seed money was used without paying a commission; and (iv) that commissions would be paid.” (FAC, ¶ 50.)

The FAC further alleges that “when Zablen made these promises to Plaintiff, he had no intention of performing them. Instead, Zablen intended and planned to trick Plaintiff into introducing his contacts (individuals and businesses) to Zablen and then intentionally cut him out and developed a customer base and thereafter directly contacted and transacted business with these customers and without paying Plaintiff commissions. At a meeting in 2020, shortly after their initial launch of their joint enterprise, Zablen denied the need to put the oral agreements about the commissions in writing, and reassured Plaintiff, “Don’t worry, I’ve got your back.” (Id., ¶ 51.)

Plaintiff has failed to allege how these alleged false promises were made, when, where, and by what means. The allegations as to each element of fraud are conclusory and general and therefore are not sufficient to withstand demurrer.

As a result, the demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained.

The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) Plaintiff seeks leave to amend but has failed to show how the complaint can be amended successfully. As such, leave to amend is denied.

4. Fourth Cause of Action (Accounting)

“A cause of action for an accounting requires a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.” (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179.)

The alleged arrangement between Plaintiff and Defendant, discussed above, constitutes a relationship involving a balance owed which requires an accounting to determine. Therefore, the FAC properly pleads a claim for accounting.

C.     Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED in part and

OVERRULED in part. It is overruled as to the first, second, and fourth causes of action, but sustained without leave to amend as to the third cause of action.

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole or any part thereof – i.e., even single words or phrases. (Code. Civ. Proc. § 435(b)(1); Warren v. Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe Ry. Co. (1971) 19 Cal App.3d 24, 40.) A motion to strike lies either: (1) to strike any “irrelevant, false or improper matter inserted in any pleading”; and/or (2) to strike any pleading or part thereof “not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule or order of court.” (Code. Civ. Proc. § 436; Ferraro v. Camarlinghi (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 528.) As with demurrers, the grounds for a motion to strike must appear on the face of the pleading, or from any matter which the court may judicially notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.) 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Defendant also moves to strike allegations relating to punitive damages and the claim under the Independent Wholesale Sales Representatives Contractual Relations Act from the FAC.

1. Punitive Damages

To support a claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege facts and circumstances showing conduct constituting malice, fraud or oppression.¿(Grieves v. Superior Court¿(1984) 157 Cal. App. 3d 159, 166.) The predicate acts to support a claim for punitive damages must be intended to cause injury or must constitute “malicious conduct,” defined as “despicable conduct” carried on by Defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of others. (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(a).) Oppressive conduct is defined as “despicable conduct” that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of a person’s rights. (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(c).)¿¿Plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others." (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 90.) Moreover, a claim for punitive damages cannot be pleaded generally and allegations that a defendant acted "with oppression, fraud and malice" toward plaintiff are insufficient legal conclusions to show that the plaintiff is entitled to an award of punitive damages. (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) Rather, specific factual allegations are required to support a claim for punitive damages. (Id.)

 

 Further, in order to state a claim for punitive damages against an employer based upon the acts of the employees, the plaintiff must plead that “the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”  (Civ. Code § 3294(b).)

 

Because Plaintiff has failed to plead fraudulent conduct, the Court concludes that the complaint fails to state a claim for punitive damages. There are no other allegations that would show malice or fraud.

 

Therefore, the motion to strike is granted as to punitive damages. As discussed above, Plaintiff has not shown how the pleading can be successfully amended and thus, leave to amend is denied.

            2. Independent Wholesale Sales Representatives Contractual Relations Act

“A manufacturer, jobber, or distributor who willfully fails to enter into a written contract as required by this chapter or willfully fails to pay commissions as provided in the written contract shall be liable to the sales representative in a civil action for treble the damages proved at trial.” (Civ. Code, § 1738.15.) 

 

Civil Code section 1738.12 provides express definitions for “manufacturer,” “jobber,” and “distributor.” The commonality among all three definitions is that manufacturers, jobbers, and distributors all deal in “products” “intended for resale to, or use by, the consumers of this state.” The “wholesale sales representative” is the person who contracts with these manufacturers, jobbers, or distributors for the purpose of soliciting wholesale orders, “but shall not include one who places orders or purchases exclusively for his own account for resale and shall not include one who sells or takes orders for the direct sale of products to the ultimate consumer.” (Civ. Code, § 1738.12, subd. (e).) 

The allegations in the FAC are sufficient to show that Plaintiff solicited wholesale orders of masks and did not sell to the ultimate consumer. (FAC, ¶¶ 2, 7, 25, 31.)

Next, the allegations in the FAC are sufficient to show that Defendant Sammy’s qualifies as a “jobber.” A jobber, at minimum, purchases the products intended for resale. (Civ. Code, § 1738.12, subd. (b).) The FAC alleges that Sammy’s Warehouse was involved in “the business of procuring [masks] and the sale of other goods.” (FAC, ¶ 15.)

Defendant contends, among other things, that there are no specific allegations that Plaintiff contracted with Defendant for the purpose of soliciting wholesale orders. However, the applicability of this claim is supported by the allegations in the FAC. When reading the allegations in the FAC as a whole and in context, the reference to this claim is not irrelevant, improper matter, or not drawn in conformity with California law. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63 [“judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth”].)  Whether Plaintiff contracted for the purpose of soliciting wholesale orders is a factual question that cannot be resolved at this early stage of the proceedings.

B. Conclusion

Defendant’s motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part. It is granted as to punitive damages without leave to amend, but denied as to the claim under the Independent Wholesale Sales Representatives Contractual Relations Act.

FERNANDO BARAJAS-SALOMON,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

101 GRANITE COUNTERTOP LLC, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV01615

  Hearing Date:  June 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant caMBRIA COMPANY LLC’s demurrer and motion to strike (CRS# 4376)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff Fernando Barajas-Salomon filed this product liability action against various defendants. Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint on March 18, 2024, asserting (1) negligence, (2) products liability – failure to warn, (3) products liability – design defect, (4) fraudulent concealment, (5) breach of implied warranties, and (6) loss of consortium.

            According to the complaint, from 2005 to 2023, Plaintiff worked as a cutter, fabricator and/or installer of Defendants’ stone products. (FAC ¶¶172-175.) As a result of this work, Plaintiff came into contact with “inherently hazardous stone products manufactured, imported, supplied, distributed, contracted, and/or brokered, by the named Defendants and Does 1-100.” (Id., ¶177.) Specifically, Plaintiff was “exposed to and inhaled stone dust containing silica and other toxins and carcinogens.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff allegedly developed lung disease and is at increased risk of developing other silica-related diseases. (Id., ¶178.) Plaintiff alleges that he “has had to receive substantial medical treatment, including hospitalizations and surgeries, and will require lung transplantation.” (Id., ¶ 179.)

            On May 8, 2024, Defendant Cambria Company LLC filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike against the FAC. On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff Baraja-Salomon filed an opposition. On June 7, 2024, Defendant Cambria Company LLC filed a reply.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part of that pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b).) The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike (1) any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading and (2) all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.)

 

 

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Tynan Decl., ¶¶4-8.)

DISCUSSION

I. Demurrer

            a. Fraudulent Concealment

                        1. Specificity

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to plead specific, individualized facts to support a legal duty as required. However, the rule of specificity “is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations.” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.) “[I]t is harder to apply this rule to a case of simple nondisclosure. How does one show ‘how’ and ‘by what means’ something didn't happen, or ‘when’ it never happened, or ‘where’ it never happened?” (Ibid.) Plaintiff cannot provide specific details on nonexistent statements. For pleading purposes, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged that Defendants had a duty to disclose the hazardous nature of their stone products but failed to do so, thereby inducing Plaintiff to purchase and/or use Defendants’ products, leading to his injuries at issue in this case. (FAC ¶¶1219-1227.)

2. Special Relationship

“There are four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 311, internal citations omitted.) In the absence of a fiduciary relationship, the latter three circumstances still “presuppose the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant.” (Ibid.) “[S]uch a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties . . . from direct dealings between the plaintiff and the defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large.” (Id. at pp. 311-312.)    

A fraudulent concealment claim is actionable in toxic exposure cases. (See Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198-1200.) However, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to allege the requisite special relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose. The court in Bigler-Engler acknowledged that a duty to disclose may arise in relationships such as “seller and buyer.” (Bigler-Engler, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at p. 311.) Here, Plaintiff alleges that he “purchased and/or received toxic stone products from Defendants.” (FAC ¶1226.) For pleading purposes, this supports a transactional relationship between Plaintiff and Defendant. (See also Arnold v. Dow Chemical Co. (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 698, 720-21 [privity may exist between the seller of a hazardous product and the intended ultimate user, even if that user did not directly purchase the product].) Plaintiff is not merely a member of the general public. Rather, Plaintiff was exposed to silica as a result of his occupation, which specifically involved the purchase and/or usage of silica-contaminated stone from Defendant. Therefore, the FAC sufficiently alleges a transactional relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose.

In sum, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action for fraudulent concealment is OVERRULED.

 

 

II. Motion to Strike

            a. Punitive Damages

            “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id., subd. (c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud is “intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Id., subd. (c)(3).)

            As discussed above, Plaintiff has adequately pled the fraudulent concealment claim. Fraud is an independent basis for punitive damages. Additionally, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant knew of the hazardous nature of its products for an extended period of time yet concealed the nature and extent of such hazards. (See, e.g., FAC ¶¶1175-1176, 1192-1194, 1213-1249, 1266-1270.) Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant made false and misleading statements in its written materials regarding the nature and extent of such hazards. (Id.) Even if this did not constitute fraud, it would constitute a conscious disregard for Plaintiff’s rights and safety. Therefore, the complaint adequately pleads punitive damages.

            b. Additional Products to be Identified in Discovery

            “In the ordinary personal injury lawsuit, in which the complaint's factual recitations show plainly the connection between cause and effect, it suffices to plead causation succinctly and generally.” (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78.) “But when, by contrast, the pleaded facts of negligence and injury do not naturally give rise to an inference of causation, the plaintiff must plead specific facts affording an inference the one caused the others.” (Ibid.) The facts must show that “the defective products supplied by the defendant were a substantial factor in bringing about his or her injury.” (Id. at p. 79.) In order for a complaint to satisfy the substantial factor test in a toxic exposure case, the plaintiff must plead the following:

(1) he was exposed to each of the toxic materials claimed to have caused a specific illness;

(2) he must identify each product that allegedly caused the injury;

(3) he must allege that as a result of the exposure, the toxins entered his body;

(4) he must allege that he suffers from a specific illness, and that each toxin that entered his body was a substantial factor in bringing about, prolonging, or aggravating that illness; and

(5) he must allege that each toxin he absorbed was manufactured or supplied by a named defendant.

(Id. at p. 80.) These facts must be pled “in ordinary and concise language” and may be pled “in a conclusory fashion if [the plaintiff’s] knowledge of the precise cause of injury is limited.” (Ibid.)

Defendant seeks to strike the phrase “and other silica-containing stone products to be identified during the course of discovery.” (FAC ¶171 at 56:26.) Defendant argues that this is insufficient to meet the standard for toxic tort pleading set forth in Bockrath. The Court agrees. The heightened standard under Bockrath requires Plaintiff to “identify each product that allegedly caused the injury.” (Bockrath, supra, 21 Cal.4th at p. 80.) As such, the catchall allegation set forth in the FAC would allow Plaintiff to base liability on numerous unidentified products, thereby contravening Bockrath. Plaintiff does not address this contention in his opposition. Therefore, the phrase is stricken.

 

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Cambria Company LLC’s demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED as to the phrase “and other silica-containing stone products to be identified during the course of discovery” (FAC ¶171 at 56:26) and DENIED as to punitive damages.

  Case No.:  24STCV01615

  Hearing Date:  June 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

SPECIALLY APPEARING defendant GUIDONI USA INC.’s MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS

(CRS# 8192)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff Fernando Barajas-Salomon filed this product liability action against various defendants. Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint on March 18, 2024, asserting (1) negligence, (2) products liability – failure to warn, (3) products liability – design defect, (4) fraudulent concealment, (5) breach of implied warranties, and (6) loss of consortium.

            According to the complaint, from 2005 to 2023, Plaintiff worked as a cutter, fabricator and/or installer of Defendants’ stone products. (FAC ¶¶172-175.) As a result of this work, Plaintiff came into contact with “inherently hazardous stone products manufactured, imported, supplied, distributed, contracted, and/or brokered, by the named Defendants and Does 1-100.” (Id., ¶177.) Specifically, Plaintiff was “exposed to and inhaled stone dust containing silica and other toxins and carcinogens.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff allegedly developed lung disease and is at increased risk of developing other silica-related diseases. (Id., ¶178.) Plaintiff alleges that he “has had to receive substantial medical treatment, including hospitalizations and surgeries, and will require lung transplantation.” (Id., ¶179.)

            On May 16, 2024, Defendant Guidoni USA Inc.’s filed the instant motion to quash service of summons. On Plaintiff’s FAC for lack of personal jurisdiction. On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff Baraja-Salomon filed an opposition. No reply has been filed.

LEGAL STANDARD

Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10, a defendant, on or before the last day of their time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction over them. (Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10(a)(1).)

California law permits a Court to exercise either “general” or “special” jurisdiction over a foreign entity. (Pavlovich v. Superior Court (2002) 29 Cal.4th 262, 268-269.)

“[A] court may assert general jurisdiction over foreign (sister-state or foreign-country) corporations to hear any and all claims against them when their affiliations with the State are so ‘continuous and systematic’ as to render them essentially at home in the forum State.” (Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 127 [internal citations omitted], emphasis added.)

In the absence of general jurisdiction, a court might still exercise specific jurisdiction over a defendant in a given cause of action, depending on “the ‘ “relationship among the defendant, the forum, and the litigation.” ’ ” (Helicopteros Nacionales de Colombia v. Hall (1984) 466 U.S. 408, 414.) A court may exercise special jurisdiction when “(1) the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits; (2) the controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant’s contacts with the forum; and (3) the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice.” (Pavlovich v. Superior Court, supra, 29 Cal. 4th 262, 269, emphasis added.)

“When a nonresident defendant challenges personal jurisdiction the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that all necessary jurisdictional criteria are met. [Citation.] This burden must be met by competent evidence in affidavits and authenticated documentary evidence. An unverified complaint may not be considered as an affidavit supplying necessary facts.” (Jewish Defense Organization, Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1045, 1054-55.)

DISCUSSION

I. Motion to Quash Service of Summons

Defendant Guidoni USA Inc. (“Guidoni”) argues this Court lacks personal jurisdiction over it because does not conduct business in California including acquiring any stone products from California, doing installation or other work in California, and marketing its sales in California. Furthermore, Guidoni contends neither maintains a physical presence in California nor own or possess property in California. Similarly, Guidoni contends it does not have an agent for service of process in California. As such, Guidoni asserts there are insufficient contacts between it and California to support a finding of general or specific personal jurisdiction over it. Kallie Still, who is the General Manager and Assistant Secretary of Guidoni, attests Guidoni is incorporated in the State of Georgia and has its principal place of business located there. (Still Decl., ¶5.) Guidoni is only licensed to conduct business in Georgia and does not have any employees in California. (Id. at ¶¶6-7.) Moreover, Guidoni imports ornamental rocks such as granite and quartz from Latin America, then processes them at their factory in Georgia before selling the products to distributors in Florida, Georgia, Illinois, Indiana, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin, and Utah. (Id. at ¶11.)

The burden shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that Guidoni has sufficient contacts with California. Plaintiff responds that Guidoni conducted business in California by shipping at least 34 containers of its artificial stone products directly into the Port of Long Beach, as recently as October 27, 2022. (Metzger Decl., ¶6, Ex. A.) Guidoni provides no response to rebut this contention. Nevertheless, Plaintiff fails to show Guidoni’s shipment of the 34 containers to Long Beach constitute continuous and systematic contacts necessary for general personal jurisdiction because it is evidence of only one day. Likewise, Plaintiffs fails to show that Guidoni purposefully availed itself of California’s benefits. As discussed above, one shipment of 34 containers does not establish intentionality on part of Guidoni to avail itself to the benefits of California but appears to be the result of “random, fortuitous, or attenuated contacts.” (Pavlovich v. Superior Court (2002) 29 Cal.4th 262, 269 [internal quotations omitted].) Furthermore, Plaintiff’s contention that Guidoni was properly served with process does not support exercising personal jurisdiction over Guidoni because his agent for service of process was not served while present in California. (Opp. at 6:12-16.) Additionally, Plaintiff fails to present any evidence showing that his injuries in this action are related to or arise out the October 27, 2022 shipment. Lastly, Plaintiff submits no evidence to support asserting personal jurisdiction over Guidoni on the basis of one shipment would comport with fair play and substantial justice.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Guidoni USA Inc.’s motion to quash service of summons is GRANTED.
